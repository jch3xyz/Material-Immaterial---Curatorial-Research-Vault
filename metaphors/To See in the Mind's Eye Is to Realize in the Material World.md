# To See in the Mind's Eye Is to Realize in the Material World

## The Metaphor

The instrument of vision is at the same time the instrument of fabrication. Ascott's figure is the scanning tunnelling microscope (STM), which does not merely *show* matter at the atomic scale but lets one *manipulate* it, atom by atom, building "atomic structures of our own choosing from the bottom up." From this Ascott draws the keystone line: **"the prosthesis of vision can be at one and the same time instrumental in constructing what is envisioned. To see in the mind's eye is to realize in the material world."** Seeing and making, conception and construction, the imagined and the real, fuse into a single operation — the act of looking is already an act of building. (`Directly stated`)

This is the keystone material/immaterial-collapse metaphor of chapter 30 ("Weaving the Shamantic Web"): the figure through which Ascott declares that "the immaterial and material lose their categorical distinction." (`Directly stated`)

The mapping turns a classical assumption inside out. Vision is normally cast as *receptive* — the eye takes in a world that is already there. Ascott's STM figure makes vision *productive*: to envision is to instantiate. The mental image (the "mind's eye") and the material outcome (realization "in the material world") are no longer two stages of a process but one event. (`Strongly implied`)

## What It Reveals

- **The material/immaterial distinction is an artefact of weak technology, not an ontological fact.** Ascott grounds the old separation historically: "our command of the material world has been such that we have had little option but to keep the worlds of our double consciousness in separate and distinct categories." It is the *advent* of the artificial life sciences and nanotechnology — "both dry (pixel) and moist (molecular)" — that begins "eroding the boundaries between states of mind, between conception and construction." The collapse the metaphor names is thus a technological achievement, and the STM is its proof. (`Directly stated`) This is the chapter's specific contribution to the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension.
- **Seeing is a mode of making.** The prosthesis is not a neutral window. By instrumenting vision down to the atomic level, technology converts perception into fabrication, so that the "tools of seeing and building" become a single repertoire — the "techno-qualia" Ascott asks artists, philosophers, and neuroscientists to attend to. (`Directly stated`)
- **It supplies the materialist underwriting for [[Cyberception]] and the [[The Double Gaze|double gaze]].** The metaphor is what licenses Ascott to say the two fields of [[Double Consciousness]] "become less distinctly separate": if vision builds, then the visionary (immaterial) and the constructed (material) are continuous. (`Strongly implied`)
- **It reframes art's vocation.** If to envision is to realize, the artist's interior image is no longer separated from the work by a gap of skill or matter — which is why Ascott can speak of art as "an enactment of mind" rather than the making of an object. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It universalizes from a single, exquisitely engineered instrument.** The STM manipulates atoms only under cryogenic, ultra-high-vacuum laboratory conditions, on conductive surfaces, at scales and speeds irrelevant to everyday making. "To see in the mind's eye is to realize in the material world" generalizes a rare, costly capability into a new human condition, eliding the enormous material infrastructure, energy, and labour that the metaphor's frictionlessness conceals. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **It treats the loss of "categorical distinction" as liberation, muting the question of construction-as-control.** Ascott himself, earlier in the chapter, worries about "the way in which specific protocols and conditions control or construct a given reality." A metaphor that fuses seeing and building can equally describe a regime in which what is *seen* is already what has been *imposed* — the constructive power cuts both ways, and the figure foregrounds the emancipatory reading. (`Speculative connection`)
- **It risks idealism dressed as materialism.** "To see in the mind's eye is to realize" can collapse into the claim that thinking something makes it so. The hard asymmetry — that constructing matter remains laborious, irreversible, and resistant in ways that envisioning is not — is exactly what the elegant chiasmus smooths over. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **The "prosthesis of vision" naturalizes a prosthesis.** By calling the instrument an extension of *the* eye, the metaphor folds a contingent technical apparatus into the body's own perception, hiding the fact that this "vision" is mediated, encoded, and rendered — not seen. (`Speculative connection`)
