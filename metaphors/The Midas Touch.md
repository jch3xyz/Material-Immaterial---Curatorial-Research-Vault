# The Midas Touch

## The Metaphor

"The Midas touch" — King Midas's curse of translating everything he touched into gold — is McLuhan's image for the way every medium remakes the field around it in its own image. Coined in the chapter on money but explicitly extended to "all technology" and even to language itself, it is a compressed restatement of [[The Medium Is the Message]]: what a medium *does* to its environment matters more than what it carries.

## What It Compares

Target → source-domain mapping:

- **Midas** → any community that develops "some extension of itself" — a medium or technology.
- **Midas's touch** → the medium's formative action on everything around it.
- **Everything turned to gold** → all other social functions "altered to accommodate that form."
- **The *curse*** → that the transformation is involuntary and indiscriminate: Midas cannot eat or embrace without converting; a medium cannot operate without bending the rest of the culture to its grain.

McLuhan applies the figure first to money (a "vast social metaphor, bridge, or translator" that "separates work from the other social functions") and then generalizes it to language, writing, the alphabet, and "all technology whatever." `Directly stated`

## What It Reveals

The metaphor makes visible the *systemic* and *self-amplifying* character of media. A new extension does not simply add a capacity; it reorganizes the whole around itself, so that pre-existing functions are restructured to serve the new form. Money, "like writing, has the power to specialize and to rechannel human energies and to separate functions, just as it translates and reduces one kind of work to another." The Midas image names this totalizing reach: the medium's form propagates outward until it has remade its environment in its own terms. `Directly stated`

It also reveals McLuhan's claim that this is true of language too — that the "magic aspect of all extensions of human sense and body" is precisely this transmuting power, so the analysis of one medium illuminates all the others. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

The gold of Midas is uniform: everything he touches becomes the same substance. The metaphor thus carries McLuhan's own warning — language "Is, in addition, a reducer and a distorter of experience." Read as a self-description, the Midas figure flags that any medium (including the metaphor itself) homogenizes difference: it reduces "multiple things to some common denominator" and translates the qualitatively distinct into a single currency. McLuhan frames this loss as easily "overridden" by the advantages of transmission across time and space, but the curse is exactly that the transformation is indiscriminate — gold cannot be eaten. `Strongly implied`

What the cheerful "the Midas touch" phrasing can obscure is the violence of the curse in the original myth (Midas nearly starves; in some versions transmutes his daughter). McLuhan keeps the *amplification* but softens the *catastrophe*, treating universal transmutation as the neutral "character of any medium" rather than as a danger. `Interpretive synthesis`
