# The Salamander (Regeneration)

## The Metaphor

The salamander regrowing a lost limb is Haraway's closing figure for cyborg reconstitution. Where "organismic, holistic politics depend on metaphors of rebirth and invariably call on the resources of reproductive sex," Haraway proposes that "cyborgs have more to do with regeneration and are suspicious of the reproductive matrix and of most birthing." The salamander's regenerative biology — "regrowth of structure and restoration of function with the constant possibility of twinning or other odd topographical productions at the site of former injury" — supplies the image: a body remade after wounding, but not restored to a prior, flawless original. "We have all been injured, profoundly. We require regeneration, not rebirth."

The mapping turns a fact of amphibian biology into a political-mythic figure: healing that produces something new and irregular rather than recovering something lost. (`Directly stated` — Haraway draws the salamander analogy explicitly to figure cyborg reconstitution.)

## What It Reveals

The metaphor lets Haraway think survival and repair *without* the apparatus of origin, purity, and return that she has criticized throughout the essay. Rebirth, in her account, belongs to "every story that begins with original innocence and privileges the return to wholeness" — a "reproductive politics" of "rebirth without flaw, perfection, abstraction." The salamander severs healing from this nostalgia. Regeneration is reconstitution *forward*: the regrown limb may be "monstrous, duplicated, potent," and that monstrousness is not a failure of repair but its political promise. The figure thus carries [[The Refusal of Innocence|the refusal of innocence]] into the register of the body — "a cyborg body is not innocent; it was not born in a garden" — and makes possible "the possibilities for our reconstitution," up to "the utopian dream of the hope for a monstrous world without gender." (`Directly stated` for the quoted phrases; `Interpretive synthesis` for reading the salamander as the bodily form of the essay's broader anti-origin argument.)

It is also the figure in which the [[Regeneration vs Rebirth]] opposition is made concrete: it gives the abstract contrast between regenerative and reproductive politics a single, biological image to stand on.

## What It Hides or Distorts

As a biological analogy it risks naturalizing what Haraway elsewhere insists is constructed: the salamander's regeneration is automatic and species-given, whereas cyborg reconstitution is, in her own terms, a matter of seizing tools, retelling stories, and political struggle, not an organic given. The figure can also soften the violence it names — "we have all been injured, profoundly" — by framing damage as the precondition for potent regrowth, which can read as making a virtue of wounding. And the celebratory "potent" risks obscuring that "twinning" and monstrous duplication are, in the amphibian case, abnormalities; the metaphor reclaims them as desirable, which is rhetorically powerful but elides the difference between a defect and a chosen political form. (`Interpretive synthesis` — these are limits of the analogy, not claims Haraway makes against herself.)
