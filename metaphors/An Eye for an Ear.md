# An Eye for an Ear

## The Metaphor

"Only the phonetic alphabet makes such a sharp division in experience, giving to its user an eye for an ear, and freeing him from the tribal trance of resonating word magic and the web of kinship." The title figure of Chapter 9 compresses McLuhan's whole thesis about literacy into a single barter: to acquire alphabetic literacy is to *trade in* an organ. The user hands over the ear — the auditory, resonant, all-involving sense that organizes tribal experience — and receives in exchange the eye, the "cool and neutral" sense of detachment, sequence, and uniform visual space. The phrase plays on the lex talionis formula "an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth," recasting it not as retribution but as the founding transaction of "civilized man." `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the structural effect of [[The Phonetic Alphabet]] on perception — the "sudden breach between the auditory and the visual experience of man" that splits a previously fused sensorium into a dominant visual function and a repressed acoustic one.
- **Source domain:** a *trade of organs* — "an eye for an ear" — surrendering one sense to gain another, set inside the talionic idiom of measured, equivalent exchange.

The mapping: as a debt is settled by handing over an equivalent body part, so the user of the alphabet settles into literacy by handing over the ear. But the trade is not symmetrical or just — McLuhan calls the division "both crude and ruthless, culturally speaking," because "the phonetically written word sacrifices worlds of meaning and perception that were secured by forms like the hieroglyph and the Chinese ideogram." One does not merely add the eye; one *loses* the ear, and with it "the tribal trance of resonating word magic and the web of kinship." The barter is the price of detribalization. `Directly stated`

This figure sits beside the chapter's two other compact emblems of the same breach: the Greek myth in which Cadmus "sowed the dragon's teeth, and they sprang up arm[ed] men" (the alphabet as the agent of "aggressive order and precision," empire, and "control of military structures at a distance"); and Prince Modupe's account of letters as "trapped words" that "anyone could learn to decipher" and "turn loose again into speech." Where those figures dramatize the alphabet's *power* and its *capture of the voice*, "an eye for an ear" names the *sensory cost* — the organ given up to gain that power. `Interpretive synthesis`

## What It Reveals

- **Literacy is a reorganization of the senses, not just a skill.** The metaphor makes vivid that the alphabet does not add information to a stable mind; it re-weights the [[Sense Ratios|ratio among the senses]]. "As an intensification and extension of the visual function, the phonetic alphabet diminishes the role of the other senses of sound and touch and taste in any literate culture." The eye is gained at the expense of the ear — a literal trade in perceptual emphasis. `Directly stated`
- **The effect is formal, independent of content.** What the breach accomplishes "has nothing to do with the content of the alphabetized words; it is the result of the sudden breach between the auditory and the visual experience of man." This makes "an eye for an ear" a precise emblem of [[The Medium Is the Message]]: it is the *form* of the alphabet — its splitting of sight from sound — not what is written, that detribalizes. `Directly stated`
- **It names the birth of the detached individual.** Trading the ear for the eye, literate man is "freed" from "the web of kinship" and becomes "a civilized individual, a man of visual organization who has uniform attitudes, habits, and rights with all other civilized individuals." The metaphor underwrites McLuhan's strongest claim, that "the phonetic alphabet, alone, is the technology that has been the means of creating 'civilized man' — the separate individuals equal before a written code of law." It is the originating moment of [[Detribalization and Retribalization|detribalization]] and of [[Detachment vs Participation|detachment over participation]]. `Directly stated`
- **The two worlds are sensorily characterized.** The figure presupposes McLuhan's contrast between the eye and the ear as *kinds* of world: "the auditory sense, unlike the cool and neutral eye, is hyper-esthetic and delicate and all-inclusive. Oral cultures act and react at the same time." The trade is therefore a trade between an all-inclusive, simultaneous, participatory world and a neutral, sequential, detached one. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It frames an irreversible mutilation as a fair exchange.** The talionic idiom "an eye for an ear" implies measured equivalence — one organ for another, debt settled. But McLuhan's surrounding text insists the trade is a net *sacrifice*: "worlds of meaning and perception" are lost, and the division is "crude and ruthless." The tidy barter form can make a profound impoverishment of the sensorium ("a rich store of inclusive perception in depth of experience that tends to become eroded") sound like a wash. `Directly stated`
- **It can read as endorsement when McLuhan claims neutrality.** The eye is the "cool and neutral" sense of detachment and the "peculiar advantage of Western literate man" — "to act without reacting, without involvement." Cast as an even trade, the metaphor risks valorizing that detachment as gain, even as McLuhan protests "we are not here concerned with the question of values, but with the configurations of societies." The figure's coolness smuggles a preference. `Strongly implied`
- **It implies a clean, total swap.** "An eye *for* an ear" suggests the ear is wholly given up and the eye wholly received — a complete substitution. But McLuhan elsewhere treats the senses as a shifting *ratio*, never one cleanly traded out for another, and treats the electric age as poised to reverse the alphabet's visual bias. The exchange metaphor freezes as a one-time transaction what is in fact a re-proportioning open to later reversal. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It abstracts the trade from its violence and politics.** Bracketed beside the dragon's-teeth figure, "an eye for an ear" presents detribalization as a quiet perceptual swap, when the same chapter ties the alphabet to military empire, "the transfer of power from the priestly to the military class," and "the secret of Western power over man and nature alike." The exchange idiom domesticates a technology McLuhan calls an "agent of aggressive order." `Strongly implied`
