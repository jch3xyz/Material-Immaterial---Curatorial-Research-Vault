# Procession vs Processor

## The Metaphor

Han stages a contrast between two figures that share a Latin root: the *procession* — a scenic, ritual, narrative movement forward — and the *processor* — a calculative device whose forward motion is mere addition. Both derive from *procedere* ("to step forward"), but only one steps within a story. The wordplay extends to German: the procession *tells* [*erzählt*], the processor only *counts* [*zählt*]. This single etymological pivot becomes Han's anchor image for the broader opposition between [[Narration vs Addition|narrative]] and additive temporality under the regime of [[The Transparency Society|transparency]].

## What It Compares

- Target: the transformation of contemporary social, cognitive, and temporal processes — from ritual, narrative, and meaningful direction toward operational, additive, and accelerable functions.
- Source-domain: the etymological doubling of *procedere* into two divergent figures — the religious/scenic *procession* (with choreography, tact, and a "there" toward which it moves) and the *processor* (purely additive, image-less, transparent, infinitely accelerable).
- The metaphor maps:
  - *Procession* → [[Ritual]], [[Pilgrimage]], narrative event, scenography, meaningful direction, temporality that cannot be accelerated.
  - *Processor* → calculation, [[Acceleration|hyperacceleration]], operationalization, naked numbers, the [[Obscenity (Han)|obscene]] absence of scene.

## What It Reveals

- That *transparency* and *narrativity* are mutually exclusive temporal regimes. "Addition is more transparent than narration. Only a process that is additive and not narrative admits acceleration" (Han, Ch. 5). The processor's transparency is the price paid for the loss of story.
- That direction is not the same as speed. The procession has "a strong sense of direction"; the processor merely advances. Hyperacceleration "no longer really moves [*bewegen*] anything or anywhere" — it spills past its *Wohin* ("where-to") (see [[Acceleration]]).
- That the etymological doubling — *erzählen* / *zählen*, telling / counting — is not a pun but a diagnosis. To count is to step forward without scene; to tell is to step forward through a scene. The contemporary social body, Han argues, has migrated from the second to the first.
- That [[Memory vs Storage|memory]], [[Pilgrimage|pilgrimage]], [[Ritual|ritual]], and even the experience of [[Threshold|thresholds]] all belong to the procession side of the split. Their disappearance under transparency is not incidental but structural: they "do not admit operationalization" and so are abolished.
- That the conclusion or ending requires narrative form. The processor "knows no narration; therefore, it proves incapable of reaching a conclusion." Without narrative, endings become "absolute loss, absolute lack" — a breaking-off rather than a completion. (Interpretive synthesis: this connects the metaphor to Han's broader account of mourning and the loss of [[Negativity]].)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- The metaphor is structured as a clean binary, but actual social processes mix narrativity and addition. A pilgrimage can be quantified (steps, days, miles); a database can be storied (the historian's archive). Han's sharp split risks idealizing pre-modern ritual as untouched by calculation.
- By rooting the contrast in etymology, Han implies a deep, almost ontological difference between two ways of stepping forward. This rhetorical move can naturalize a contingent historical contrast as a metaphysical one. (Speculative connection: a [[Michel Foucault|Foucauldian]] reading would ask whether "procession" was ever as transparent-to-itself as Han's nostalgic register suggests.)
- The figure of the *processor* may overstate the innocence of "mere counting." Counting is itself a culturally embedded practice with its own scenography (the ledger, the dashboard, the metric) — the processor too has images, even if Han calls its activity image-less.
- The metaphor obscures the question of *who* operates the processor and who marches in the procession. It is a phenomenology of process, not a politics of agents. The economic and institutional drivers of additive acceleration ([[Neoliberalism]], platform capitalism) recede behind the etymological figure.
- The German wordplay *erzählen / zählen* does not travel cleanly into English; the metaphor's force depends on a linguistic accident that the translation can only gesture at. (Directly stated by Han via the bracketed German.)
