# Growbag Culture

## The Metaphor

The city is not built but grown. Ascott calls for "a growbag culture ... in which seeding replaces designing" — architectural practice that finds its guiding metaphors in **horticulture** rather than in **warfare**. The city becomes a living, cultivated system; buildings are to be "planted and 'grown'"; the artist becomes a sower of seeds, and the vocabulary of the new urbanism is pollination and grafting rather than streets, fortifications, and master plans.

The metaphor maps the *making* of the city onto the *cultivation* of a living thing: a growbag is a sealed bag of compost in which plants are seeded and left to grow, and Ascott takes it as the figure for a built environment that is nurtured into being rather than fabricated to a blueprint. (Directly stated — Ascott names the growbag, seeding, pollination, and grafting explicitly.)

## What It Reveals

- **The city as a grown, not designed, living system.** The horticultural image makes the city thinkable as biology rather than physics — Ascott's complaint that "there is no biology of building, simply the physics of space" is answered by a practice that treats the built environment as something cultivated. This connects the metaphor to [[The City as Zone of Transformation]], the cybernetic, feedback-driven, interactive city it is meant to produce. (Directly stated / Strongly implied.)
- **Bottom-up over top-down.** Seeding cedes control to growth: the gardener sets conditions and tends, but cannot dictate the final form. The metaphor thus carries Ascott's preference for cultivated emergence over imposed master-planning — the same bottom-up logic that animates [[The Planetary Collegium]], conceived as an organism cultivated from the ground up rather than an institution designed from the top down. (Interpretive synthesis — the metaphor names horticulture vs warfare; the bottom-up/top-down framing is the synthesis across the cultivated-organism passages. Bottom-up vs top-down is rendered as plain text, with no dedicated note.)
- **The artist as cultivator.** By naming artists as "the sowers of these seeds," the metaphor assigns the creative agent a horticultural rather than authorial role — the new city grows through tending, chance, and feedback, not through signature design. (Directly stated.)
- **A peaceable urbanism.** Choosing horticulture *against* warfare reframes the city away from the military-strategic vocabulary (fortification, command, the battle zone) toward growth, nurture, and reciprocity. (Directly stated.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It naturalizes design decisions.** Casting urban form as "grown" can obscure the human, political, and economic choices that still seed, fund, and prune the city — growth metaphors can launder authorship and power into the appearance of organic inevitability. (Speculative connection.)
- **Gardens are still controlled.** A growbag is an engineered, bounded, fertilized container; "letting it grow" is itself a managed regime. The metaphor's promise of release from designing may understate how much cultivation remains a form of control — selecting, grafting, weeding. (Interpretive synthesis.)
- **It downplays the material and the durable.** Buildings are slow, heavy, and long-lived in ways plants are not; the horticultural image, congenial to Ascott's stress on fluidity and apparition, can distort the intractable materiality the same chapter elsewhere acknowledges ("dense and intractable materiality"), feeding the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension Ascott's cyberception runs through. (Interpretive synthesis.)
