# The Bug Was a Feature

## The Metaphor

"The bug was a feature." A surveillance practice exposed as overreach — Facebook's "Like" button silently tracking users who never clicked it, planting cookies in every browser, profiling non-members across the web — is first denied as an accidental defect (a "bug," a "glitch," a "misstep," an "error") and is then, once habituation has done its work, openly avowed as the intended design (a "feature"). The metaphor inverts the software vocabulary of accident and intention: what the corporation called a malfunction was the function all along.

The phrase lands as the punch line of an entire sequence. Across 2010–2014 Facebook denied tracking, called Arnold Roosendaal's discovery a "bug," promised to fix "the glitch," and characterized its privacy violations as "missteps" — and then in 2014 announced that it would track users across the internet using the very "Like" button it had disavowed, "reversing every assertion since April 2010 with a few lines inserted into a dense and lengthy terms-of-service agreement." Zuboff's verdict: *the truth was finally out: the bug was a feature.* (Directly stated.)

## What It Compares

| Target (surveillance practice) | Source domain (software development) |
|---|---|
| Intended data-extraction capability | A designed software *feature* |
| The public alibi during contestation | A *bug* / *glitch* / *error* — an unintended defect |
| The eventual avowal once normalized | Shipping the feature openly |
| The "fix" that is never really a fix | A patch that quietly preserves the behavior |

The metaphor maps the moral binary of *accident vs. intention* onto the technical binary of *bug vs. feature*. To call surveillance a "bug" is to claim it was never meant to happen; to admit it is a "feature" is to confess it was the point. Zuboff's reversal of the cliché ("it's not a bug, it's a feature") names the gap between the two as a deliberate communications strategy, not a sequence of honest errors. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## What It Reveals

- **Denial is structural, not incidental.** The "bug" alibi is a recurring move in the [[The Dispossession Cycle]] — specifically the *incursion* and *habituation* stages, when the firm denies a practice while waiting for the public to grow accustomed to it. Facebook "secured a solid year and a half with which to habituate the world to its 'Like' button" precisely by "denying every accusation." The metaphor exposes denial as a function of the extraction strategy. (Strongly implied.)
- **The defect-framing buys time for normalization.** Naming surveillance a "bug" or "glitch" presents it as fixable and exceptional, deferring the moment of avowal until resistance has been worn down — "the sharp yelp of pain when a Novocain needle first pierces the flesh, before numbness sets in." (Directly stated.)
- **Intent precedes the alibi.** The patent Facebook filed three days before a researcher exposed cross-domain tracking — for the very techniques it was denying — shows the "feature" was engineered before the "bug" was confessed. The metaphor reveals the denial as cover for a capability already built. (Strongly implied.)
- **It belongs to a euphemistic vocabulary of power.** "Bug," "glitch," "misstep," and "comprehensive privacy program" are of a piece — see [[Euphemism and secrecy are structural to surveillance capitalism not incidental]]. The metaphor is the moment that vocabulary collapses and the designed intent becomes legible. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It can flatter the firms with a hacker's wit.** "It's not a bug, it's a feature" is an in-joke of programmer culture, a knowing wink. Adopting it risks lending the dispossession an air of cleverness rather than coercion, aestheticizing what is in fact the systematic transfer of rights "from the many to the few." Zuboff weaponizes the cliché against the firms, but the phrase's native charm can soften the harm it names. (Speculative connection.)
- **It localizes blame in a "rogue" decision.** The bug/feature frame can imply a single design choice by a single actor — Zuckerberg "stuck to the script" — when Zuboff insists the driver is impersonal: the [[The Extraction Imperative]] of a "new market form with distinct and intractable imperatives whose mandates can be fulfilled only at the expense of user privacy." The metaphor risks personalizing a structural logic. (Strongly implied.)
- **It implies a fixable system.** The vocabulary of bugs presupposes a baseline of correct behavior to which the system could be patched back. But if surveillance is the *feature*, there is no clean version to restore; the framing can mislead regulators into chasing defects within a model whose defaults are the problem. (Interpretive synthesis.)
