# A Thing That Has Us

## The Metaphor

"This convergence signals the metamorphosis of the digital infrastructure from a thing that we have to a thing that has us." The keystone figure of the apparatus's reversal of control. Zuboff takes the ordinary grammar of *possession* — we *have* a phone, a thermostat, a watch, an internet of things — and inverts its subject and object. The thing we own becomes the thing that owns us. The grammatical pivot is the whole argument: the same word, *have*, swings its arrow around, and in that turn the human moves from possessor to possessed, from user to used. In Chapter 8 the figure recurs and names its engine: "The prediction imperative transforms the things that we have into things that have us."

## What It Compares

| Source domain (possession / ownership) | Target (the digital apparatus under surveillance capitalism) |
|---|---|
| A thing we *have* — a tool, a device we own and command | The digital infrastructure as instrument: "a thing that we have" |
| The thing turning to *have* us — to possess, grip, hold | The same infrastructure as "an actuating machine," an apparatus that knows and acts upon us |
| Owner → owned: the reversal of who holds whom | Subject → object: humans rendered as "behaving objects for its calculations" |
| Metamorphosis — one creature transformed into another kind | The "metamorphosis of the digital infrastructure" from instrument to agent |

The figure leans on a verb, not a noun, and that is its precision. There is no new object on the scene; the very thing in our pocket simply changes which way the relation runs. Zuboff doubles the inversion with the chapter's closing line: the animal-tracking science that taught surveillance its methods has been turned on us, so that "now we are the animals" — the watchers' instruments now have *us* in their sights.

## What It Reveals

- **The inversion of agency at the heart of the apparatus.** The metaphor is the controlling image for the claim that the [[The Prediction Imperative|prediction imperative]] does not merely watch but actuates: "ubiquitous computing is not just a knowing machine; it is an actuating machine designed to produce more certainty about us and for them." The thing that has us is a thing that acts on us — the [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|means of behavioral modification]]. (`Directly stated`)
- **Material infrastructure as the condition of the logic.** The reversal is staged precisely where two histories converge — "the early ideals of ubiquitous computing and the economic imperatives of surveillance capitalism." The apparatus is "the material expression of the prediction imperative"; the immaterial logic can only have us by way of a "pervasive real-world material 'knowing and doing' presence." The figure carries the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Object vs System]] spine into the surveillance cluster: the object (device) is subsumed by the system (the imperative). (`Directly stated`)
- **The human re-rendered as object.** In Chapter 8 the metaphor names its purpose: the things that have us exist "in order that it might render the range and richness of our world, our homes, and our bodies as behaving objects." [[Rendition]] is the operation by which the inversion is accomplished — experience translated into [[The Body as Data|data]], persons into behaving objects. The figure thus delivers the move tracked in [[Subject vs Object]]. (`Directly stated`)
- **A reversal accomplished without consent.** The thing comes to have us "out of ignorance and the dictatorship of no alternatives" — "involuntary rendition has become an inescapable fact." We did not hand ourselves over; the grip closed while "the actors perform the stylized lullabies of disclosure and agreement for the public." The metaphor registers a dispossession that feels like ordinary having. (`Directly stated`)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **The thing has no will of its own.** "A thing that has us" personifies the apparatus, lending it the appetite of a possessor. But Zuboff is emphatic elsewhere that the technology is the puppet, not the master — the agent is the economic logic that commands it (see [[The Puppet Master Not the Puppet]]). Taken alone, the figure can invite a search for a malevolent machine rather than for the [[Surveillance Capitalism|economic imperatives]] that animate it; the device "has" us only because [[The Prediction Imperative|the prediction imperative]] requires it to. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **The clean before-and-after.** "Metamorphosis from a thing we have to a thing that has us" implies a once-innocent instrument that later turned. But Zuboff's own history — the Galapagos tortoise, the elk herds, "the vision of individuals and groups as so many objects to be continuously tracked" — shows the surveilling intent was present at the design stage. The figure's tidy reversal can soften how early the having-of-us was built in. (`Strongly implied`)
- **Possession as the only relation.** Framing the stakes as *who has whom* keeps the analysis inside the grammar of ownership, where the remedy might seem to be re-taking possession — owning our data, our devices. But the deeper harm Zuboff names is the abolition of the very ground on which "having" or "deciding" is possible: "We are left with few rights to know, or to decide who knows, or to decide who decides." The metaphor of possession can under-describe a loss that is epistemic and political, not merely proprietary. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
