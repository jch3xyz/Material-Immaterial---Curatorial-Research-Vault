# Joey the Mechanical Boy

## The Metaphor

The human as voluntary machine. Where the long history of [[Automata]] runs on the dream of a machine that comes alive — [[The Robot]], the android, the [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically|Faustian]] re-creation of life nonbiologically — Joey is the figure that runs the other way: the living child who experiences his own humanity as unbearable and so becomes a machine to be rid of it. Burnham takes the case from Bruno Bettelheim's essay "Joey, the Mechanical Boy" and names it the most ghastly of all his automata: not the machine turned human, but the human turned automaton. *Directly stated.*

## What It Compares

The target is the alienated body of "our time and culture" — the human who finds security and self-protection in becoming a robot. The source domain is the documented behavior of a nine-year-old who, in Burnham's report of Bettelheim, did not imitate a machine but *was* one to those around him:

- His body's autonomic functions are recast as mechanical operations — he "plugged himself in to run his eating apparatus."
- His sleep depends on external apparatus — "'machinery' made of wire, masking tape, cardboard, and other bits of material had to be reassembled next to his bed so that it could 'live' him, keeping him running during sleep."
- His emotional states are read as machine states — idling, shifting gears, "crash, crash!", "the final breakdown of the machine."
- His self-worth is staked on machine superiority over the body — hurting himself to prove that machines "don't break; they're much harder and stronger."

The mapping is total: every human function and interest "guided by practical necessities of maintaining the machine." *Directly stated.*

## What It Reveals

It reveals the dark underside of the sociology of automata that runs through Burnham's Chapter 5. The fascination with [[The Robot]] is usually told as the human wish to animate the inanimate; Joey inverts it into the wish to deanimate the self. Burnham makes the case diagnostic rather than merely pathological: he stresses that "probably only in our time and culture could such a situation have arisen" — as machines "become the accepted surrogate for human attention and affection," the machine becomes a genuine escape route "for the unprotected." Joey is what it looks like when a culture saturated with [[Automata]] and machine values offers the alienated body a place to hide. *Directly stated.*

For the vault, Joey is a hinge between [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Embodiment]]: the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] fantasies of leaving the body behind (uploading, patternism, the body as data) reappear here in their most literal and saddest form — not a transcendence of flesh but a child treating his flesh as defective hardware, asserting that the machine is "much harder and stronger." It is the embodied, clinical limit case of [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality|the collapse of the human–machine boundary]]. *Interpretive synthesis.*

## What It Hides or Distorts

As Burnham himself half-admits by routing the figure through Bettelheim, the metaphor risks aestheticizing a child's suffering: read too quickly as a cultural symbol, Joey's actual condition — "an extreme reaction to parental rejection," whose cure was "regaining the ability to trust people around him" — disappears into Burnham's larger argument about art and automata. The clinical fact is that Joey is *not* a machine and his "recovery meant" re-entering "the human world," which cuts against any reading that treats becoming-machine as a stable or chosen identity. The metaphor also flattens the difference between a psychotic delusion and the broader cultural drift Burnham invokes; the chapter's leap from one disturbed child to "our time and culture" is suggestive but, on its own terms, asserted rather than demonstrated. *Strongly implied.*
