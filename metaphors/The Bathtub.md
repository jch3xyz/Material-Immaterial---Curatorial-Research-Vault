# The Bathtub

## The Metaphor

The bathtub is Donella Meadows's teaching emblem for stock-and-flow dynamics. The water in the tub is a **stock**; the faucet and the drain are the **flows** that raise and lower it. The claim is that intuition already outruns theory here: *"if you have had much experience with a bathtub, you understand the dynamics of stocks and flows."* No mathematics is required to feel what an accumulation is and how its inflow and outflow drive its behavior over time. `Directly stated`.

## What It Compares

| Bathtub (source domain) | System dynamics (target) |
|---|---|
| Water level in the tub | The **stock** — a store, quantity, or accumulation ([[Stocks and Flows]]) |
| The faucet | The **inflow** (filling, births, deposits, growth) |
| The drain | The **outflow** (draining, deaths, withdrawals, decay) |
| Turning the faucet up or down | Changing the inflow rate |
| Opening or plugging the drain | Changing the outflow rate |
| A full, plugged, faucet-off tub | An "unchanging, undynamic, boring system" — no flows, no change |
| Inflow set equal to outflow at half-full | **Dynamic equilibrium** — the level holds though water still runs through ([[Homeostasis]]) |

The bathtub is "a very simple system with just one stock, one inflow, and one outflow" — the minimal unit from which Meadows deduces principles that "extend to more complicated systems." `Directly stated`.

## What It Reveals

- **A stock is the memory of its flows.** Read the water level and you are reading the integrated record of every inflow and outflow that has acted on the tub — "the present memory of the history of changing flows within the system." `Directly stated`.
- **The direction of change is a race between the two flows.** As long as inflows exceed outflows the level rises; as long as outflows exceed inflows it falls; when they are equal the level holds. `Directly stated`.
- **The outflow is a lever, not just the faucet.** The metaphor's payoff is a correction to a cognitive bias. Because "the human mind seems to focus more easily on stocks than on flows," and when it does attend to flows attends more readily to *in*flows, we miss that a stock "can be increased by decreasing its outflow rate as well as by increasing its inflow rate." Meadows's slogan: *"There's more than one way to fill a bathtub!"* Burning less oil raises the stock of available oil exactly as discovering a new field does; a company can grow its workforce by hiring more *or* by reducing quitting and firing. `Directly stated`.
- **Slowness and delay.** Because you cannot change a stock instantly — only bleed or fill it through its flows — stocks introduce lags and buffers into a system, which is why the same tub logic reappears in the thermostat and inventory models of the [[The Systems Zoo|Systems Zoo]] ([[Delays]]). `Strongly implied`.

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It is deliberately a simplification.** Meadows brackets a real outflow — "I have assumed that evaporation from the tub is insignificant, so I have not included that outflow" — and flags the general caveat: "All models, whether mental models or mathematical models, are simplifications of the real world." The tidiness that makes the bathtub teachable is bought by ignoring flows that matter in fuller systems. `Directly stated`.
- **One stock, no feedback.** The bathtub has a single stock and flows that *you* set by hand; it hides the feedback loops, coupled stocks, and nonlinearities that produce the surprising behavior of real systems ([[Feedback Loops]]). In the Systems Zoo the same tub becomes a car-inventory "bathtub" governed by competing balancing loops — behavior the lone tub cannot show. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It flatters our intuition, then exploits its blind spot.** The very familiarity the metaphor trades on carries the inflow-biased habit of mind it must then correct — so the image both grants and quietly misleads the reader, which is why Meadows has to spell out the outflow lever explicitly. `Speculative connection`.
