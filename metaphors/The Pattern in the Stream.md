# The Pattern in the Stream

## The Metaphor

The self is *the pattern that water makes in a stream as it rushes past the rocks*. The water molecules are replaced every instant, yet the standing shape — the eddy, the whirlpool — endures. So too, on this figure, the cells, atoms, and neurons of the body are always changing, but the *pattern* that organizes them is the person, and that pattern is what survives.

[[Ray Kurzweil|Kurzweil]] gives the image its canonical form: "I am rather like the pattern that water makes in a stream as it rushes past the rocks in its path… The actual molecules of water change every millisecond, but the pattern persists for hours or even years." This is the founding figure of what Kurzweil calls **patternism** — the doctrine that consciousness is "a pattern of information, a biological configuration of energy and matter that persists over time," residing not in the hardware of the brain but in its organization. As O'Gieblyn glosses it, patternism is a functionalist account of mind dressed in "a more organic metaphor."

## What It Compares

| Target (the self) | Source domain (the stream) |
|---|---|
| Cells, atoms, neurons (matter) | Water molecules |
| Consciousness / identity | The standing pattern / whirlpool |
| Continual metabolic turnover | The river ever-flowing past the rocks |
| Persistence of selfhood through change | The pattern that "persists for hours or even years" |

The crucial move is to locate the person in the *form* rather than the *matter* — to treat identity as substrate-independent. This is precisely the [[Pattern and Randomness]] dialectic that, in Hayles's account, displaces presence/absence as the ground of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] subject, and the same logic by which [[Information Lost Its Body|information lost its body]]: if the self is a pattern, it is computational, and "a pattern, after all, is essentially computational, which means it can, at least in theory, be transferred onto a computer." The figure is therefore the connective tissue between the [[Mind as Machine]] metaphor and the dream of mind-uploading.

### The lineage: same image, opposite meaning

O'Gieblyn's argument turns on tracing the figure's descent and showing that its meaning was *inverted* in transit.

- **[[Heraclitus]]** — the pre-Socratic source: it is impossible to step into the same river twice. The figure names the *transience* of all things.
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]**, *The Human Use of Human Beings* (1954): "we are but whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water. We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves." O'Gieblyn reads Wiener as "likely alluding to" Heraclitus and, "like Heraclitus, emphasizing the transience of identity, the fact that nature is made of fluid patterns that are always changing." (This same Wiener line is quoted by Hayles — see [[The Body as Data]] — where it anchors the cybernetic reconstruction of the human as informational pattern.)
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** inherits Wiener's whirlpool but reverses its valence: "for Kurzweil, patternism was precisely what made possible the most decisive form of permanence: immortality."

The figure thus migrates from a memento mori (you are flux, you do not abide) to a promise of eternal life (because you are only a pattern, you can be copied, transferred, resurrected). The metaphor stays fixed while the metaphysics flips.

## What It Reveals

- **The substrate-independence of identity.** The image makes vivid the genuine fact that the matter of the body is not continuous over time — Origen of Alexandria made the same observation in the third century ("the material substratum is never the same… a river is not a bad name for the body"), naming the self an *eidos*, "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter." The stream figure illuminates that whatever we are, we are not our particular atoms.
- **The deep historical continuity of the problem.** By recovering the Heraclitus → Wiener → Kurzweil descent — and pairing it with Origen and the Resurrection debates — O'Gieblyn shows that the computational self is not a clean modern invention but the latest carrier of an ancient question about how identity survives the turnover of the body. The [[Mind as Machine]] metaphor, "expressly designed to avoid the notion of a metaphysical soul," has "returned to us these ancient religious ideas about physical transcendence and the disembodied spirit."
- **Why the metaphor is so consoling.** For O'Gieblyn personally, conceiving selfhood as a pattern "overturned the most depressing conclusions of materialism" — that the mind does not exist, that identity is finite — while remaining "rooted in material reality." The figure does real existential work; it is the materialist's permitted substitute for the soul.

## What It Hides or Distorts

This is where O'Gieblyn's critique bites: the stream metaphor is *taken too literally*, and in being literalized it conceals what it cannot represent.

- **Embodiment.** The image treats the water (the body) as inert, interchangeable filler for the pattern — exactly the disembodiment Hayles diagnoses, in which information comes to seem "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates." But "transcendence depends on the notion that information can be liberated from the material constraints of the physical world," and O'Gieblyn (via Hayles) names this an ideology in which "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form." The figure hides that a pattern is not actually separable from its instantiation. See [[Embodiment]], [[The Materiality of Informatics]].
- **Qualia / subjective experience.** A whirlpool has a shape but no inside. The metaphor cannot register *what it is like* to be the pattern. Susan Schneider's objection is that mind-uploading "may indeed produce a digital copy… But the new person will be a zombie with no subjective experience"; the most it achieves is "functional similarity to the original." O'Gieblyn underscores the darker irony: "our new, digital selves will entirely lack subjective experience… Our resurrected forms might behave much like our current selves, but nothing will be going on inside." Kurzweil's own resolution — that the uploaded copy "will claim to be conscious… We'll believe him. He'll get mad if we don't" — concedes that the figure dissolves consciousness into convincing behavior.
- **Meaning.** The figure is a child of [[Claude Shannon|Shannon]]'s information theory, which "stripped information of meaning." Treating the self as a transferable pattern reiterates "the fundamental problem of our disenchanted age: the inability to account for the mind." It also smuggles in a downgrade: while it "claims to offer humanity a portal into a higher realm," its logic is "actually nudging us down to a lower ontological status," reducing humans to "computation" and, in Lanier's phrase, to "transient fragments" while "bits are presented as if they were alive."
- **The continuity of *this* self.** Because a pattern can be copied, it can exist "in two places at once" — which means the figure cannot guarantee that *you* survive the transfer rather than a twin who merely claims to. The stream image, smooth and singular, hides the branching that uploading actually entails.
