# Prometheus and the Eagle

## The Metaphor

The bound Prometheus and the eagle that eternally consumes his ever-regrowing liver, read — after Kafka — as a single intrapsychic scene: the achievement-subject as both the chained sufferer and the bird that feeds on him. `Directly stated`: "Viewed in this way, the relation between Prometheus and the eagle represents a relation of self-exploitation."

## What It Compares

Han takes Kafka's terse retelling of the Greek legend ("The gods grew weary, the eagles grew weary, the wound closed wearily") and, in his words, subjects it "to further revision and turn it into an intrapsychic scene: the contemporary achievement-subject inflicting violence on, and waging war with, itself."

The mapping (target → source-domain):

- **Achievement-subject → bound Prometheus.** "Today's achievement-subject deems itself free when in fact it is bound like Prometheus." The myth's gift of fire — Prometheus "also brought work to mankind" — maps onto the achievement-subject's work, which it performs not under command but for the supposed profits of enjoyment.
- **The subject's own alter ego → the eagle.** "The eagle that consumes an ever-regrowing liver can be interpreted as the subject's alter ego." Predator and prey are therefore the same body — perpetrator and victim collapsed into one. `Directly stated`.
- **Endless self-exploitation → the eternal, regenerating wound.** The liver perpetually regrows so that the devouring never ends; this models the inconclusiveness of the achievement-subject's labor, which "works into the open" without closure. `Strongly implied` (Han makes the liver-as-self-consumption mapping explicit; the link to inconclusiveness is drawn from the same chapter's account of the absent forms of closure).
- **Burnout / fatigue → the pain of the liver.** "Pain of the liver, an organ that cannot actually experience pain, is said to be tiredness. Prometheus, the subject of self-exploitation, has been seized by overwhelming fatigue." `Directly stated`.

This is the mythic figure for the chapter's thesis that "the exploiter is simultaneously the exploited" — the structure named by [[Auto-Exploitation]] and lived by the [[Achievement Society]]. The same chapter restates it without the myth: "On the basis of the paradoxical freedom it holds, the achievement-subject is simultaneously perpetrator and victim, master and slave." `Directly stated`.

## What It Reveals

The image makes thinkable a violence that has no external aggressor. By housing eagle and Prometheus in one psyche, it shows that domination need not come from a "commanding Other": exploitation can be **self-administered yet total**. It renders visible why this self-exploitation is so efficient and so hard to resist — "a deceptive feeling of freedom accompanies it." `Directly stated`. It also dignifies burnout as a structural outcome rather than personal failure: "burnout represents the pathological consequence of voluntary self-exploitation." `Directly stated`. And it supplies a face for the otherwise abstract claim that "achievement society is the society of self-exploitation. The achievement-subject exploits itself until it burns out." `Directly stated`.

## What It Hides or Distorts

By relocating the whole scene "intrapsychic[ally]," the metaphor risks obscuring what Han himself insists must not be lost — the economic ground of the suffering. He warns against exactly this when criticizing Ehrenberg, who "considers depression exclusively in terms of psychology and the pathology of the self. In so doing, he fails to consider economic context." The Prometheus image, taken alone, can read as a purely psychological drama; Han offsets this in the surrounding argument by tying auto-exploitation to "capitalist relations of production" — "the capitalist system is switching from allo-exploitation to auto-exploitation in order to accelerate." `Interpretive synthesis` (the chapter both deploys the intrapsychic figure and repeatedly returns it to systemic/economic causes; the metaphor's limit is what the prose around it is at pains to supply). The figure can also flatten the difference between the eagle as punisher (the original myth's externally imposed torment) and the eagle as alter ego (Han's reading), smoothing over the fact that the subject now volunteers for its own torture.
