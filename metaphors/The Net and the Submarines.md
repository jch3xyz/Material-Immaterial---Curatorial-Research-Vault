# The Net and the Submarines

## The Metaphor

"The net is cast wide over the waters of daily life, but there are also submarines exploring the depths." Shoshana Zuboff uses a single maritime image to picture the two-part structure of [[Economies of Scope]] — the second logic of surveillance capitalism's supply imperatives, after economies of scale. The **net** figures *extension*: the horizontal spread of [[Rendition]] across the surface of ordinary life, seizing every utterance, gesture, and location it can reach. The **submarines** figure *depth*: the vertical descent beneath that surface in search of the rarest, most predictive raw material — "your personality, emotions, and endorphins." The image bridges the two chapters that anatomize scope: Chapter 8 ("Rendition: From Experience to Data") works the extension dimension; the submarines anticipate Chapter 9 ("Rendition from the Depths"). (`Directly stated`)

The two craft are not alternatives but complements: the net and the submarines work the same waters simultaneously, the breadth campaign and the depth campaign jointly serving the [[The Prediction Imperative|prediction imperative]]. (`Directly stated` for the net/submarines and surface/depth mapping; `Interpretive synthesis` for the table's allocation of catch and treasure.)

## What It Reveals

The image makes legible that scope is **two-dimensional** — that surveillance capitalism does not merely want *more* data of the kind it already has (that would be scale alone) but data of *new varieties and new locations*. The net pictures the relentless lateral push of extension: "Extension wants every corner and crevice... All that is moist and alive must hand over its facts. There can be no shadow, no darkness." The submarines picture the qualitatively different, harder, more secretive pursuit of interior states — the personality, emotion, and neurochemistry that yield the most certain predictions. (`Directly stated`)

It also reveals the **stealth** proper to the depth dimension. A net is visible on the surface; a submarine is built to operate unseen, below the waterline. The figure thus encodes the "subterfuge" Zuboff names in the same passage — the way the hunt for the deepest surplus proceeds covertly, beneath awareness, exactly as the wearables literature insists devices be "unobtrusive" and operate "outside the awareness" of their subjects. The submarine is the figure of [[Digital Dispossession]] conducted in the dark. (`Strongly implied`)

Finally, the maritime register reveals the **inexhaustibility** the operation imagines for itself: the sea has both a vast surface and unplumbed depths, so there is always more to catch and always deeper to dive. The image naturalizes the open-endedness of the [[The Extraction Imperative|extraction imperative]] — experience treated as an ocean that can be fished and sounded without limit. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## What It Hides or Distorts

A net and submarines are instruments of **harvest from a natural commons** — the sea belongs to no one and the catch is simply *taken*. This is precisely the naturalizing move Zuboff elsewhere exposes as [[Digital Dispossession]]: the image of fishing open water occludes that "the waters of daily life" are not an ownerless resource but *human experience belonging to persons*, and that the "catch" is seized "without asking" on a path Zuboff explicitly names "dispossession." The romance of the deep-sea expedition can dignify what is in fact unilateral taking. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

The figure also pictures a **clean separation** of fish (data) from water (life), as though experience could be netted without remainder and the living world left intact behind it. But Zuboff's own gloss — "All that is moist and alive must hand over its facts" — concedes that rendition does not skim a separable substance; it reaches into what is "moist and alive." The tidy image of a catch hauled from the sea understates the violence of converting lived, embodied experience into [[Behavioral Surplus]]. (`Strongly implied`)
