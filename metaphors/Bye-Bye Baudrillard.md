# Bye-Bye Baudrillard

## The Metaphor

"Bye-bye Baudrillard" — Ascott's polemical farewell, in the closing of the "Moist Manifesto" (ch. 31, 2000), to the nihilism and despair he ascribes to late postmodernism. The phrase casts an entire intellectual mood — the simulacral terminal decline that, for him, "characterised art at the end of the preceding millennium" — as a guest to be shown to the door. Baudrillard, the theorist of the simulacrum and of the disappearance of the real, stands in by name for that whole mood; saying goodbye to him is saying goodbye to it. `Directly stated` (the phrase is verbatim Ascott's).

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the structure of feeling Ascott calls "the nihilism and despair of late postmodernism" — art's "sense of terminal decline" at the end of the twentieth century.
- **Source domain:** a casual, even cheerful, leave-taking — the idiom of a farewell ("bye-bye"), turned on a proper name. The dismissal is light, almost dismissive of its own gravity, which is the rhetorical point.
- **Mapping:** Baudrillard (the named figure) → the totality of postmodern terminal pessimism; the act of waving goodbye → [[Visionary Pragmatism]]'s "cornering" and reversal of that pessimism; the door one walks out of → the threshold into the [[The Post-Biological Era|post-biological culture]] and its [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)|radical constructivism]].

In Ascott's argument the reversal is performed by visionary pragmatism, which "corners the nihilism and despair of late postmodernism… and springs forth into the post-biological culture with a radical constructivism." The metaphor is the affect-laden punchline of that movement: terminal decline does not collapse of its own accord — it is cornered, dismissed, and walked past into [[Moistmedia|moist]] optimism. `Directly stated`.

## What It Reveals

- **That late-postmodern despair is a position one can simply leave, not a fate.** By rendering Baudrillard a departing guest rather than a diagnosis, Ascott reframes terminal decline as contingent and refusable — the keystone move of his optimism over nihilism. `Strongly implied`.
- **The constructivist ground of the optimism.** The farewell is not naïve cheer; it is underwritten by [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)|radical constructivism]] — the conviction that reality and the self are continually built (through language, code, the body, simulation), so the future is something artists make in the present rather than a decline they suffer. The metaphor compresses Ascott's whole "working with the future in the present" stance into a wave. `Directly stated` (constructivism), `Interpretive synthesis` (the compression).
- **The networked affect that replaces paranoia.** The reversal is of a piece with [[Telenoia|telenoia]] — "universal connectivity and transparency" displacing the "industrial age of paranoia" — and with the [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]] of a nature whose "sentience" technology now reveals. Bye-bye Baudrillard is the emotional signature of that connective, re-enchanted, post-biological turn. `Strongly implied`.

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It caricatures Baudrillard to make its exit.** The phrase enrolls a complex body of thought (simulation, hyperreality, the precession of simulacra) as a single mood — "nihilism and despair" — that can be waved away. Whether Baudrillard's analysis of the simulacrum is actually *refuted* by the moist turn, or merely *declined*, is left unexamined; the metaphor performs a mood-shift, not an argument against the theory. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It treats optimism as a choice rather than a claim that could be wrong.** The cheerful leave-taking obscures the possibility that "terminal decline" might be a diagnosis rather than a defeatism — that the post-biological, re-materialised culture Ascott welcomes could itself be read (by a [[Byung-Chul Han|Han]], for instance, who reads the same "transparency" Ascott celebrates as a neoliberal dispositive of control) as the very condition Baudrillard described, not its overcoming. The farewell hides the disagreement it depends on. `Speculative connection`.
- **It immunizes the program from its own dark register.** By exiling despair with Baudrillard, the metaphor leaves little room inside Ascott's frame for the loss, surveillance, or homogenisation that other authors locate precisely in ubiquitous computing and the post-biological body. The door closes on the critique as well as the mood. `Interpretive synthesis`.
