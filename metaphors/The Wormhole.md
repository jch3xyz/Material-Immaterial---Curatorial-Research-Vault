# The Wormhole

## The Metaphor

The wormhole — borrowed, Roy Ascott is explicit, from quantum physics and from Kip Thorne's description of wormholes as "handles in the topology of space, connecting two widely separated locations in our universe" — figures the instantaneous tunnelling between otherwise separate realities, real and virtual, natural and "paranatural." Where a planner speaks of "streets, alleyways, avenues and boulevards," Ascott says "we need to think of wormholes . . . tunnelling between separate realities, real and virtual, at many levels, through many layers" (ch.27). The figure migrates across three registers that Ascott binds into one image: the *shamanic* (the shaman who "could shift at will across the barriers of space and time, a kind of wormhole in consciousness"), the *telematic-present* ("the telematic links and hypermedia of the Net allow us to wormhole effortlessly between sites, nodes, places, people, images, and texts"), and the *cosmological-future* (literal wormholes "tunnelling in quantum foam"). The recurring claim is architectural and institutional: "The museum must be riddled with wormholes, a hyperstructure that links us seamlessly between the nodes of experience . . . The museum must become a wormhole to facilitate our passage between worlds" (ch.29). `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the cognitive and networked passage between distinct, ordinarily non-adjacent realities — between real and virtual space, between one `[[Telepresence|telepresence]]` and another, "from one mind to another"; and, at the institutional scale, the museum (and city architecture) reconceived as the structure that makes such passage possible — a `[[The Mind of the Museum|mind-of-the-museum]]` rather than a container of objects.
- **Source domain:** the physicist's wormhole — a "handle in the topology of space" that folds two far-apart points into immediate contact, "affording the rapid transit of particles and people from one layer of reality to another, from one galaxy to another."

The mapping is point-to-point. The two mouths of the wormhole are the two realities (real/virtual, natural/paranatural, this node/that node); the throat is the tunnel of "cognitive tunnelling through what might be called 'data foam' on the Web"; the topological economy of the figure — distance abolished by folding rather than by traversal — is exactly the economy of `[[Cyberception|cyberception]]` and `[[Telepresence|telepresence]]`, where the far is made present without the body crossing the intervening space. `Strongly implied`

## What It Reveals

- **Connection by folding, not by crossing.** A wormhole's whole point is that you do not traverse the gap — the gap is folded out of existence. The figure makes vivid Ascott's claim that the Net does not *transmit you across* distance so much as *abolish* the betweenness of separated realities, "linking us seamlessly between the nodes of experience." This is the spatial logic underwriting `[[Inter-Reality]]` (the fuzzy zone between virtual and real) and `[[Telepresence]]`. `Directly stated`
- **It re-purposes the museum from storage to passage.** By demanding the museum be "riddled with wormholes," the figure reframes the institution as a `[[The Mind of the Museum|hyperstructure]]` of transit between experiences rather than a repository of material artefacts — the keystone of Ascott's case for `[[New Media Art]]` and `[[Art as Experiential Technology]]`, where the artwork is a node of passage, not an object on a base. `Directly stated`
- **It unifies the archaic and the futuristic under one image.** The shaman's "wormhole in consciousness," the present-day hyperlink, and the cosmologist's quantum tunnel are made *the same gesture* — tunnelling between layers of reality. The figure thereby smuggles Ascott's technoetic thesis (that ancient consciousness-travel and networked tele-travel are continuous) into a single, scientifically respectable picture. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It is an immaterial figure for an immaterial art.** A wormhole has no mass, no facade, no interior to walk through — it is pure topological relation. The figure thus carries Ascott's side of `[[Material vs Immaterial]]`: the museum-as-wormhole is "a rift in space/time, a hole in space," not a building, fitting an art of telematic relation rather than of objects. `Strongly implied`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It launders a speculative physics into an accomplished fact.** Traversable wormholes remain theoretical (Ascott himself concedes the cosmological case is "distantly theoretical"); importing the term lends the everyday hyperlink the borrowed glamour and apparent inevitability of frontier physics. The "seamless" passage between worlds is asserted as already real ("a very present reality") when much of it is program. `Strongly implied`
- **"Seamless" hides the seams.** Real networked passage is full of interfaces, latency, mistranslation, exclusion, and infrastructural friction; the topological figure of an instantaneous fold occludes the labour, hardware, and bandwidth that actually carry the "transit of . . . people from one layer of reality to another." The frictionlessness is an aesthetic claim, not a technical one. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It pictures pure connection and so suppresses control.** A wormhole connects but does not *govern*; the image of effortless tunnelling "from one mind to another" elides who builds the throat, who routes the traffic, and who is admitted — the surveillance and gatekeeping dimensions of the very networks Ascott celebrates. The figure's beauty is in its innocence of power. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **Ascott half-concedes the figure's fragility.** He grants that "whether or not the metaphor of the wormhole will stick, will be useful, or will quickly die is of no great issue" — an unusual authorial signal that the image is offered as a provocation to "transform our thinking," not as a settled description. The monumental solidity the word *museum* implies sits awkwardly against a figure for a thing that is, by definition, "a hole in space." `Directly stated`
