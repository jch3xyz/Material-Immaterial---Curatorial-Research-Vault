# The Golem

## The Metaphor

The golem is the pre-modern template for artificial intelligence: a humanoid figure sculpted from clay and animated not by biological life but by language — God's name written on a slip of paper placed in its mouth, or, in some tellings, by "mathematical permutations" of the Hebrew alphabet. O'Gieblyn invokes it, by way of the theologian Anne Foerst, to argue that the AI laboratory is the direct heir of the Kabbalist's workshop. The roboticist who "animate[s] these inanimate machines by getting strings of symbols just right" is doing, in a secular register, exactly what the medieval mystic did: summoning life from dead matter through the manipulation of symbols. The metaphor is doubly load-bearing because the golem was never *only* a tool. It was also a theological experiment — an attempt "to understand God and God's creation" by repeating, at human scale, the divine act of creation. `Directly stated`.

The crucial hinge is symbolic animation. Where the Kabbalist permuted letters, the computer scientist permutes bits; both proceed on the assumption — central to [[The Computational Universe]] and the [[Mind as Machine]] metaphor — that the right arrangement of symbols can call mind into being. This is why the golem is not a loose poetic flourish but a genealogy: it locates the [[Information]]-first metaphysics of computing inside a much older mystical tradition that "preceded matter." `Interpretive synthesis`.

## What It Reveals

- **The continuity between religion and AI.** The golem makes visible what a secular self-image of the field conceals: that the dream of building a mind is a theological dream, "both a form of worship and 'a repetition of God's act of creating us.'" Foerst's harmonization — and Sussman's quip that "we computer scientists are really the Kabbalists of today" — exposes the field's eschatological undercurrent. The golem set to "awaken at the end of time" by a kabbalistic rhyme prefigures the transhumanist hope of resurrection and the [[Transhumanism|transhumanist]] end-time; O'Gieblyn even notes that the first big computer built in Israel was named *Golem*. `Directly stated` (the awakening myth and the *Golem* computer); `Interpretive synthesis` (the transhumanist resonance).
- **The informational metaphysics underneath computing.** By tracing AI to the *Sefer Yetzirah*, the metaphor surfaces the buried premise that "the very fabric of reality is informational" — the same premise Hayles names in [[The Computational Universe]] and that O'Gieblyn elsewhere calls [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]]. The golem reveals that treating code as more real than matter is an ancient mystical move, not a neutral engineering fact. `Strongly implied`.
- **AI as inquiry, not just instrument.** The golem was simultaneously "tool" and theology — an attempt "to understand God." The metaphor reveals that building artificial minds has always been a way of asking what a mind, a soul, and a creator *are*. `Directly stated`.

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **Embodiment.** The golem story flatters the conviction that life is conferred by an immaterial something — a name, a formula, a "divine breath" of "abstract mathematical patterns" — descending into otherwise dead clay. This is the dualism George Dyson names ("Computers arose from the mud, and code fell from the sky"). It hides what O'Gieblyn, with Hayles, insists on: that mind is not a symbol-string poured into inert matter but a property of a living, sensing body. The golem metaphor distorts by making [[Embodiment]] look like a mere vessel — "mansions for the souls," in Alan Turing's phrase — rather than the condition of mind itself. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **The hard problem.** The animating "incantation" papers over exactly what computing cannot supply: consciousness, qualia, an interior. As O'Gieblyn argues, "digital technologies were built on the assumption that consciousness played no role in the process of intelligence," so the hope that mind will *emerge* from the right symbols repeats the golem's wishful thinking while disguising it as engineering. The metaphor lets [[Emergence]] stand in for the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]] it never solves. `Strongly implied`.
- **Hubris and its disavowal.** Most golem tales end in catastrophe — the golem "turned violent," even killing the rabbi — encoding "the theme of hubris that appears in so many myths about the human attempt to create life." Borrowing the golem's mystique while discarding its cautionary ending lets the field claim the romance of creation without the warning. `Directly stated` (the hubris theme); `Speculative connection` (the selective borrowing).
