# The Turing Test as a Magic Trick

## The Metaphor

In the Prologue to *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*, Hayles names her governing figure for the whole book in one sentence:

> Think of the Turing test as a magic trick.

The test is a piece of stage magic, and the reader is the mark. As with any good trick, the spectacle the audience is told to watch — *which is the man, which the woman, which the machine?* — is not where the work is done. The real move happens earlier, off the line of attention, when assumptions are quietly accepted that will govern everything seen afterward. By the time the spectator reaches the moment of choice, the outcome is already fixed. The figure is rhetorical and reflexive: it describes not the test's internal mechanics (for that, see the definition [[Turing Test (Hayles)]]) but the way the test operates *on the person reading and performing it*. *(Directly stated)*

## What It Reveals

The figure makes visible that the Turing test's most consequential effect is **temporal and prior**, not evidentiary. The standard question — does the machine pass? — fixes attention on the verdict. Hayles's magic-trick framing relocates the action to the setup, where the participant is already reconstituted before any guess:

> Rather, the important intervention comes much earlier, when the test puts you into a cybernetic circuit that splices your will, desire, and perception into a distributed cognitive system in which represented bodies are joined with enacted bodies through mutating and flexible machine interfaces. As you gaze at the flickering signifiers scrolling down the computer screens, no matter what identifications you assign to the embodied entities that you cannot see, you have already become posthuman.

Read this way, the trick does three things at once. It shows that becoming [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] is not a future event awaiting better machines but an *already-accomplished* condition of sitting at the terminal — the splice precedes the choice. It exposes the participant, not the hidden entity, as the figure transformed: *you* are the one who has become [[The Cyborg|cyborg]], joined to your own represented body "through the technology that connects them." And it diagnoses the test rhetorically: like a magician's patter, the test's framing manufactures the consent it needs, so that the dismantling of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] feels like a neutral experiment about machines rather than an operation performed on the human. The figure is thus a reading instruction for the entire book — a warning that foundational scenes of computation secure assent to disembodiment before argument begins. See [[Embodiment]], [[Embodiment vs Information]]. *(Strongly implied)*

## What It Hides or Distorts

A magic trick, by its own logic, has a *trickster* and a known method — someone who set it up to deceive, and a deception that could in principle be exposed and undone. Carried too far, the figure can imply that the erasure of embodiment is a contained illusion (a sleight that, once seen through, simply dissolves), when Hayles's larger claim is that the splice into the [[Distributed Cognition|distributed cognitive system]] is a real material reconfiguration of the subject, not a mere misperception to be corrected by looking harder. The "trick" language risks suggesting a single author of the deception — Turing, or the AI tradition — whereas Hayles locates the move in a diffuse, agentless apparatus: the [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] redefinition of [[Information]] as substrate-independent pattern that "could flow between different substrates without loss." It also flattens the test's *productive* ambivalence: in the same Prologue Hayles argues the test, by including Turing's man/woman example, can "transgress" subject-boundaries as much as reinforce them, making gender and thought alike "a contingent production" — a generativity the word "trick," with its connotation of pure dupery, tends to occlude. Finally, the theater metaphor keeps the body offstage: the figure's punch depends on insisting that something material has *happened* to the embodied reader, even as "magic trick" invites the audience to treat the whole scene as immaterial show. See [[Material vs Immaterial]]. *(Interpretive synthesis)*
