# The Resilience Plateau

## The Metaphor

Resilience as a **plateau** — a flat playing field, walled by gentle, elastic edges, over which a system can wander and play in safety. A resilient system has a *big* plateau, a wide space to move around in; a brittle one has a narrow ledge. This is Donella Meadows's governing image for the otherwise nearly invisible property she calls [[Resilience]]: "I think of resilience as a plateau upon which the system can play, performing its normal functions in safety. A resilient system has a big plateau, a lot of space over which it can wander, with gentle, elastic walls that will bounce it back, if it comes near a dangerous edge" (`Directly stated`).

The figure comes with a companion image of erosion and a companion image of blindness — the *play* and the *playing space*. As resilience is lost, the plateau shrinks and its once-elastic walls stiffen; the system ends up "operating on a knifeedge, likely to fall off in one direction or another whenever it makes a move." And the danger of this shrinkage is that no one is watching the ground: "the system usually is paying much more attention to its play than to its playing space. One day it does something it has done a hundred times before and crashes" (`Directly stated`).

## What It Compares

- **Target** → the abstract, structural property of resilience: a system's capacity to survive, persist, and recover across shocks, drawn from a rich redundant web of [[Feedback Loops]].
- **Source domain** → a piece of *terrain* — a raised, level plateau ringed by soft elastic walls — over which a body can move around as if at play.

The mapping runs term for term. The **plateau surface** is the safe operating region, the range of states in which the system can perform "its normal functions in safety." Its **width** is the quantity of resilience: a big plateau means "a lot of space over which it can wander," a shrunken plateau means brittleness. The **elastic walls** are the restorative [[Feedback Loops]] — several of them, redundant, "gentle" so they nudge rather than snap — that "bounce it back, if it comes near a dangerous edge." Their stiffening into "lower and more rigid" walls is the loss of that redundancy; the **knife-edge** the plateau collapses into is the limiting brittle case where a single move tips the system off. The system's **play** is its day-to-day activity; the **playing space** it ignores is the resilience — the plateau — that quietly permits the play. Because resilience "may be very hard to see, unless you exceed its limits," the metaphor externalizes an invisible property as a visible landscape, and locates the crash not in the last action but in the eroded ground beneath it.

## What It Reveals

The plateau makes **resilience spatial, and therefore intuitable**. An abstract, redundancy-based property — hard to define and, in Meadows's own words, "something that may be very hard to see" — becomes a place with an area you can imagine shrinking. This does three things at once. It renders resilience as a *range* rather than a point, so it can be distinguished from static stability (a plateau is a whole region to wander in, not a single balance point held constant). It makes *degradation continuous and legible*: you can picture the walls creeping inward and losing their spring long before anything breaks, which is exactly the whole-system foresight Meadows wants. And through the play / playing-space pairing it names a specific cognitive failure — **attending to activity while ignoring the conditions that permit it** — that explains why loss of resilience "can come as a surprise" and why a system "does something it has done a hundred times before and crashes." The catastrophe is reframed as the end-point of an unwatched erosion, not a bolt from the blue. It also grades naturally toward [[Self-Organization]]: a system that can rebuild its own walls, or grow its own plateau, is resilient at a higher level.

## What It Hides or Distorts

Read as a picture rather than a pointer, the plateau can mislead. It suggests a **fixed, mappable safe zone** with a clean edge — as if resilience had a knowable boundary you could survey and stay inside — when Meadows's whole point is that the limits are *not* visible in advance ("unless you exceed its limits, overwhelm and damage the balancing loops"); the crash reveals the edge that no map showed. The image of a solid surface underfoot also connotes **stasis and constancy**, the very thing resilience is *not*: Meadows insists "resilient systems can be very dynamic," that oscillation, outbreak, and cycles of "succession, climax, and collapse" may be the normal condition resilience acts to restore — a truth the calm, level plateau under-dramatizes. It can imply resilience is a **single scalar** ("bigger plateau = more") when the source is a *rich, redundant structure of many feedback loops* "operating through different mechanisms, at different time scales" — texture the flat field flattens. The **passive, playful** verbs ("play," "wander") soften the stakes and can make erosion feel benign, obscuring how much of it, per the [[Stability vs Resilience]] tension, is *actively bought* — resilience deliberately traded away for productivity, efficiency, or a legible, constant-looking output. And a plateau is a *given* landscape one merely occupies, which underplays that a system can, at its strongest, remake or enlarge its own ground — the self-organizing capacity the metaphor gestures at but the terrain-image cannot really hold.

## Related Symbols and Concepts

- [[Resilience]] — the concept this metaphor is the governing image *of*; the plateau is Meadows's picture of survival-under-variation, and this note is its dedicated figure.
- [[Self-Organization]] — the strongest form of resilience; the plateau grades upward into a system that can rebuild its own elastic walls and even enlarge its own ground.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the "gentle, elastic walls": resilience is provided by a rich, redundant web of restorative loops, and their stiffening is the plateau's shrinkage.
- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — the single restorative loop; one such loop is only one wall, whereas the plateau's safety comes from several operating redundantly.
- [[Stability vs Resilience]] — the tension the metaphor's blind spot dramatizes: the "play" the system watches (visible stability, productivity) versus the "playing space" it ignores (invisible resilience) until it crashes.
