# A Scribble in the Air

## The Metaphor

"A scribble in the air" — Herbert Read's figure, quoted by Jack Burnham, for what postwar sculpture has become: a thin, dynamic line traced in empty space rather than a solid body occupying it. Where sculpture was once defined by mass, palpable form, and spatial occupancy, it has dwindled — on Read's reading — to a gesture so attenuated it barely registers as matter at all. `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

The target is the historical trajectory of post-World War II sculpture; the source domain is the image of a scribble drawn in the air.

- **mass → line.** The solid, free-standing body gives way to a thin filament of form.
- **containment → extension.** Volume that encloses and holds space yields to gesture that reaches out into it.
- **stability → dynamicism.** The inert, grounded object yields to movement and energy.
- **occupancy → trace.** Sculpture no longer *fills* space; it merely *marks* it, weightlessly.

Burnham notes the polarity Read draws: "extension rather than containment, dynamicism instead of mass, have become the values carrying modern sculpture further away from its original stability." `Directly stated` This is the same axis the vault tracks as [[Material vs Immaterial]].

## What It Reveals

The figure makes the *loss of substance* vivid and felt. For Read it diagnoses a decline — a falling-away from sculpture's twenty-five-century "obsession for the free-standing human form" and its "prime attribute of palpable form." Burnham preserves the keenness of that diagnosis (he calls it "this mien of disappointment, amounting almost to a gap between the generations") while re-reading it: what Read mourns as the *end of an era*, Burnham reframes as a *transition*. `Strongly implied`

In Burnham's hands the scribble is not exhaustion but a symptom of [[Dematerialization]] driven by the "psychic and intellectual authority increasingly invested in science and technology" — sculpture moving "from idealism ... to materialism" and from object toward system. The metaphor is thus the keystone bridge from Read's diagnosis of decline to Lucy Lippard's later, affirmative account of the dematerialized art object: the same reduction-to-a-line, revalued. `Interpretive synthesis`

## What It Hides or Distorts

As Read deploys it, "a scribble in the air" carries a built-in verdict — it casts dematerialization as *reduction*, even degradation (a "tortuous" loss), measuring the new work against the lost standard of mass and "original stability ... a quality which he views as its only salvation." The image therefore hides what Burnham wants to make visible: that the move off the pedestal and out of solid mass is not a falling-short but a directed metamorphosis toward life-simulating systems. `Strongly implied`

The figure also flattens difference. A "scribble" implies aimlessness or triviality; it occludes the "coherent metamorphic forces" Burnham insists are at work, and it cannot distinguish a Constructivist line from a kinetic system or a cybernetic organism — all of which Burnham reads as distinct stages, not one undifferentiated thinning-out. `Interpretive synthesis`
