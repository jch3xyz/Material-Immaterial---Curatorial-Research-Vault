# Fragrance of Time

## General Orientation

Two authors converge on the figure of *time as scent*, and this note keeps their senses distinct. **Byung-Chul Han** (below, and across the rest of this note) is the metaphor's home: time has *fragrance* when it is thick, narrative, ritual, layered, and is *odorless* when it is transparent, additive, accelerated. **Marshall McLuhan** supplies a near-identical olfactory image two decades earlier — the incense-clock by which "the Chinese and Japanese had for thousands of years measured time by graduations of incense" — and grounds it in a sensory thesis (smell as "the most iconic" sense). The McLuhan material is gathered in its own clearly-marked section and given its own metaphor note, [[The Scent of Time (McLuhan)]]; the historical-influence relation between the two is labelled below. The rest of this note remains Han's.

## The Metaphor

**Time has fragrance.** More precisely: certain forms of time — thick, narrative, ritual, layered — *exude* a scent, while the transparent, additive, accelerated time of the present is odorless. "Compulsive transparency annihilates the fragrance of things, the fragrance of time. Transparency has no fragrance" (Han, *The Transparency Society*, Ch. 5). The image, which Han carries forward into The Scent of Time, pairs with Phosphorescence of Time and the Atoms vs Fragrant Molecules figure as a single olfactory-aesthetic register for narrative duration.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** temporality itself — and specifically the difference between [[Narrativity|narrated time]] (rituals, ceremonies, pilgrimages, memory, ripened experience) and [[Transparency|transparent time]] (additive, processor-like, atomized, accelerated).
- **Source domain:** *fragrance* — a slow exhalation released only by complex, ripened, compound material. Scent is delayed (it lingers and bloooms), compositional (it requires bound molecules, not bare atoms), and resists acceleration (you cannot rush a perfume's development). The German *verströmen* — "to stream out, exude" — emphasizes that fragrance flows *from* a thickened body of substance.

The mapping reads: narrative time : transparent time :: fragrant molecule : naked atom. Just as scent presupposes molecular complexity, the fragrance of time presupposes a "figurative attraction, a narrative gravity" binding instants into stories.

## What It Reveals

- **Time has *qualities*, not only durations.** Han reframes the critique of [[Acceleration]] as a critique not of speed but of the *aesthetic-temporal register* that speed destroys: "The crisis of our times is not acceleration, but rather the scattering and dissociation of temporality." Fragrance names what is lost when time is "emptied of all narrativity." `Directly stated`
- **Beauty is constitutively belated.** The metaphor binds the temporality of beauty to slow exhalation: "Only belatedly do things reveal their fragrant essence of beauty. This essence consists of temporal layers and deposits that phosphoresce." Beauty is "a pupil [Zögling], a late bloomer" — see Beauty as Late Bloomer and Phosphorescence of Time. `Directly stated`
- **Composition over accumulation.** The atoms-vs-molecules image distinguishes mere storage / addition (which can only "accumulate") from narrative formation, which binds events through "figurative attraction." Only the bound form smells of anything. `Directly stated`
- **A diagnostic for transparency.** Because transparency works by leveling thresholds, dissolving negativity, and converting narration into addition, it is structurally incapable of producing scent. The olfactory test — *does it smell of time?* — becomes a way of detecting the difference between Procession and Processor, Pilgrim and Tourist, Erfahrung and Erlebnis.
- **Proustian inheritance.** Han routes the figure through Proust: "immediate enjoyment" is incapable of beauty; the fragrance arrives only "much later," as a reminiscence. The metaphor is therefore not merely descriptive but allied with a whole aesthetic of involuntary memory and ripening. `Strongly implied`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It aestheticizes a structural critique.** By placing the loss in the register of *perfume*, the metaphor risks softening Han's argument into nostalgic connoisseurship — as if the problem with the [[The Transparency Society|Transparency Society]] were chiefly a thinning of ambience rather than a political-economic restructuring of attention, labor, and visibility. Readers can mistake "lack of fragrance" for a sigh of taste rather than a diagnosis.
- **It naturalizes "thickness" as good.** Fragrance is appetitive; odorlessness is uncanny. The metaphor smuggles in a normative preference for *layered, ritualized, slow* time without arguing for it on its own terms. What about toxic fragrances, oppressive rituals, suffocating narratives? The image does not allow for *bad* scents — only the polarity *scented / odorless*.
- **It treats narrativity as compositional in a literally molecular way.** The atoms-to-molecules figure imports a chemistry of "attraction" and "gravity" that may not survive scrutiny: not every sequence of events can or should be bound into a single fragrant whole, and the demand for "narrative gravity" risks rejecting fragmentary, atonal, or anti-narrative forms (montage, glitch, certain modernisms) as merely odorless. See [[Narration vs Addition]].
- **It privileges the nose over other senses.** Olfaction is famously hard to share, archive, or argue about — which makes it an excellent figure for what resists transparency, but also a figure that exempts itself from public verification. The metaphor performs the very mystique it describes.
- **It risks conflating distinct losses.** The "fragrance of things" (objects ripened in use) and the "fragrance of time" (narrative duration) are folded into one image. Whether the two losses share a single cause, or simply rhyme, the metaphor does not say.

## ## Marshall McLuhan (Understanding Media)

Two decades before Han, McLuhan gives the olfactory-temporal figure its likely originating form. In *Understanding Media* (1964), Ch. 15 — titled "Clocks: The Scent of Time" — he contrasts the West's "feeling for time as duration," produced by clocks that turn time into "uniform seconds, minutes, and hours on an assembly-line pattern," with a wholly different way of telling time: "the Chinese and Japanese had for thousands of years measured time by graduations of incense. Not only the hours and days, but the seasons and zodiacal signs were simultaneously indicated by a succession of carefully ordered scents." Time, here, is inhaled rather than read. McLuhan calls this "the most integral and involving time sense imaginable" and grounds the choice of *smell* in a thesis about the senses: "The sense of smell is not only the most subtle and delicate of the human senses; it is, also, the most iconic in that it involves the entire human sensorium more fully than any other sense." `Directly stated`

The resonance with Han is close enough to be more than coincidence: both oppose a *scented*, integral, communal, non-fragmented temporality to an *odorless*, abstract, atomized one, and both make smell the privileged figure for what resists segmentation. McLuhan even anticipates Han's diagnostic edge — "highly literate societies take steps to reduce or eliminate odors from the environment" — pairing the loss of scent with the visual, uniform fragmentation of experience, much as Han pairs the loss of the "fragrance of time" with [[Transparency]] and [[Acceleration]]. For McLuhan the engine of the odorless regime is the phonetic alphabet, print, and the mechanical clock (the [[Acoustic vs Visual Space|shift from acoustic to visual space]], a re-tuning of [[Sense Ratios]]); for Han it is the transparency dispositive. The two figures share a structure while differing in cause. `Strongly implied`

That McLuhan's Ch. 15 is the *historical source* behind Han's "fragrance / scent of time" is plausible but unproven: Han is nowhere shown to cite McLuhan, and the image of scent as memory and ripening also reaches Han through [[Marcel Proust]]. The claim of direct influence is therefore an `Interpretive synthesis` (Han is not shown to cite McLuhan), not a documented lineage. McLuhan's version is given its own note, [[The Scent of Time (McLuhan)]], to keep the primary text intact and to avoid folding it into the Han metaphor; this section records only the cross-author resonance.
