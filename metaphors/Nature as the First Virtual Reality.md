# Nature as the First Virtual Reality

## The Metaphor

"Nature" — the supposedly pristine, god-given ground against which culture, the city, and technology have always defined themselves — is itself the *first virtual reality*: a construct, "pure data" given form by human language, fear, and desire. Ascott inverts the ordinary intuition that virtual reality is the artificial latecomer to a prior, real nature. On his account the order of priority runs the other way: nature was always the original VR, and it is a metaphor whose "sell by" date has now passed (`Directly stated`).

## What It Compares

- **Target:** "nature" — the good, the pure, the unadulterated, the whole; the innocent, wild, unspoilt, instinctive given that we imagine technology has estranged us from.
- **Source domain:** a *virtual reality* — an "undifferentiated wholeness" of "pure data" that is "programmed, shaped, and categorised according to our language, fears, and desires."

The mapping makes nature a rendered environment rather than a substrate: an "undifferentiated wholeness" (raw data) is run through the human apparatus of language and projection (the program) to yield the experienced category "nature" (the simulation). Ascott prefaces the claim by conceding that nature "has never as such existed, or that it has existed in different ways for different societies" — a constructivist premise that links the metaphor directly to his [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]]: there is no observer-independent nature to recover, only versions we author (`Strongly implied`).

## What It Reveals

- **Nature is authored, not found.** Treating nature as the first VR exposes "nature" as a coding of reality — continuous with the coded environments of telematics rather than their opposite. This dissolves the binaries that the old metaphor rested on: Ascott notes the "binary opposition of town and country... is disappearing," and the line between natural and artificial systems is becoming "meaningless" (`Directly stated`). The metaphor is the semantic hinge of his whole "back to nature" thesis: if nature was always virtual, then working in electronic, immaterial space does not lead us away from nature but toward a *new* one — [[Nature II]], "second-order nature, emergent nature" (`Directly stated`).
- **Metaphors expire.** By giving nature a "sell by" date, Ascott frames worldviews as perishable software due for replacement, clearing the ground for new metaphors "to house the complex interacting systems of biological, technological, and social life that we are developing" (`Directly stated`).
- **It reframes the [[Material vs Immaterial]] arc.** If the "real" pole (nature, materiality) was already a virtuality, the supposed gulf between material nature and immaterial information narrows — preparing Ascott's counter-move on that tension: not one-way [[Dematerialization]] but a "marriage of the immaterial and the material," the "reinstatement... of a natural materiality" (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **A re-enchantment register.** Calling nature a programmed wholeness recodes it as something we participate in authoring rather than worship from outside — of a piece with Ascott's late claim that art mediates "between human and post-human consciousness," cross-linking to [[Re-enchantment]] and [[Posthumanism]] (`Speculative connection`).

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It can flatten the nonhuman into "data."** Reducing nature to "pure data" programmed by "our language, fears, and desires" risks an anthropocentric idealism in which there is nothing but our coding — eliding the recalcitrant, agentive, more-than-human world that exists whether or not we render it. The metaphor's debt to constructivism and to the information ontology of [[The Computational Universe]] / [[Information]] is also its blind spot: it presupposes that "wholeness" is *data* at all (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **It can license technological triumphalism.** If nature was always a virtual reality, then replacing it with engineered "second-order" nature can seem to cost nothing — the destruction or substitution of the given is reframed as merely upgrading an expired metaphor. Ascott himself concedes nature has not "retreated" or been "destroyed"; only that "the metaphors that it has long supported no longer hold" — but the figure invites readers to slide from *the metaphor is exhausted* to *the referent was never there* (`Strongly implied`).
- **"First" smuggles in a sequence.** Naming nature the *first* VR quietly installs a developmental ladder (first nature → telematic VR → Nature II) that naturalizes the very technological succession it claims merely to describe (`Speculative connection`).
