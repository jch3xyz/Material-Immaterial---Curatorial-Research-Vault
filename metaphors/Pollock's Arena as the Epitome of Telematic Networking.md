# Pollock's Arena as the Epitome of Telematic Networking

## The Metaphor

Jackson Pollock's drip canvas is for Ascott the art-historical anchor of the network: "the very epitome of telematic networking." Pollock paints not pictures-on-a-wall but "fields" of "intertwining, interweaving, branching, joining, crossing, linking lines of energy," and the resulting "alloverness" and "all-at-onceness" pre-figure, in paint, what the computerised network will make "technologically explicit." The painting is the *figure*; telematic networking is what it is a figure *of* — the metaphor runs from the horizontal arena (source domain) to the dispersed, decentralised electronic meeting place (target). `Directly stated`.

## What It Compares

Ascott maps a transitional twentieth-century painting onto the structure of distributed, computer-mediated art-making:

- **Pollock's "fields" of branching, linking lines of energy → the topology of the network.** The web of poured lines is read as the web of "planetary inputs" and "lines of communication." `Directly stated`.
- **"Alloverness" / "all-at-onceness" → the asynchronous, non-localised simultaneity of telematic discourse.** Ascott reaches the figure through Leo Steinberg's account of the tilt of the picture plane from the vertical to the horizontal — the "flatbed picture plane" that "no longer simulate[s] vertical fields, but opaque, flatbed horizontals" — and through McLuhan's "instantaneous world of electric information media [that] involves all of us, all at once." The flatbed and the arena are folded into this single figure rather than minted as separate notes. `Directly stated` (Steinberg/McLuhan quoted by Ascott to build the figure).
- **The "arena" → the electronic meeting place.** "Pollock's painting surface was an 'arena,' a meeting place, of behaviour, myth, and idea. The computerised network is another kind of meeting place, an electronic arena for creative work." `Directly stated`.
- **"Anonymity of authorship that embraces the viewer" → the collapse of sender/receiver into a unitary "user."** The painting's "inclusive and inviting" space, "providing for a kind of anonymity of authorship," is the precursor of the network's [[Distributed Authorship]], where "the dichotomy of artist/viewer or sender/receiver of the earlier era is resolved into a unitary 'user' of the creative system." `Directly stated`.

This is the decisive painting-to-network bridge in *Telematic Embrace*: it grounds [[Telematic Art]] and [[Network Consciousness]] in a canonical modernist painting, and it is the load-bearing metaphor for the [[Horizontal vs Vertical]] tension (the horizontal arena/table-top as inclusive matrix vs. the vertical picture/mirror as fixed, isolating viewpoint). It also carries Ascott's realisation of the long-deferred art/new-media stubs: the network as "an electronic arena for creative work" is precisely [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] in their experiential, participatory mode, and the resolution of sender/receiver into a co-creating user is an instance of [[Reciprocal Communication]].

## What It Reveals

- **It makes the network *art-historical*, not merely technological.** By locating "the most powerful visual metaphor of telematic interconnectedness" inside Pollock, Ascott argues that telematics "carry forward an aesthetic of participation and interconnectedness implicit in the developing strands of art practice during this century" — networking is the fulfilment of a tendency already visible in paint, not a rupture imposed from outside art. `Directly stated`.
- **It reframes meaning as produced *in the field*, not dispatched.** The viewer is "embrace[d]… in the creation of meanings"; meaning is "negotiated, distributed, transformed, and layered in multiple exchanges" rather than "asserted and consumed in one-way linearity." The arena dissolves the spectator into a participant — the same move Ascott makes in [[The Artwork as Matrix and Catalyst]] and that underwrites [[Reciprocal Communication]]. `Directly stated`.
- **It names the dissolution of the author.** The painting's "anonymity of authorship" lets Ascott present [[Distributed Authorship]] as something painting already gestured toward: the "metaphysical promise" of Pollock is what telematic systems "make… technologically explicit." `Directly stated`.
- **It supplies the visual key to the [[Horizontal vs Vertical]] tension.** The horizontal arena is the holistic, all-round, transactional table-top; verticality is "the analogue of a world perceived from an upright position," which Ascott links (via Lévi-Strauss) to "the aggressive, alienating stance of a society." `Strongly implied` (Ascott raises "Is there some connection between the insistent verticality of viewing in art and the aggressive, alienating stance of a society?" as a question, not an assertion).

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It reads a solitary act of painting as a model of collective, distributed making.** Pollock's drip paintings were produced by one artist; Ascott himself concedes the figure is a "metaphysical promise" only "made technologically explicit" later. Treating an individual's gestural field as already a network risks projecting the telematic "we" back onto a body of work whose authorship was, historically, singular and heroic — the very "individual genius" Ascott elsewhere calls "the invention of an era." `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It aestheticises a power-laden infrastructure.** The arena figure foregrounds embrace, conviviality, and inclusion while Ascott acknowledges, in the same chapter, that "most timesharing systems are in the hands of the multinational corporations and the military" and that "we can expect a growing and close interest in telematics on the part of the controlling institutions." The painterly metaphor can soften the institutional and surveillant materiality of the network it stands for — a tension the vault tracks under [[Material vs Immaterial]] and the surveillance-side critiques (e.g. the [[Digital Panopticon]]). `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It compresses a contested art-historical lineage into a single trajectory.** Folding Steinberg's flatbed plane, Duchamp's tilted picture plane (Ascott names [[Marcel Duchamp]] as "the most vital force… in this radical shift"), and McLuhan's electric simultaneity into one continuous "shift to the horizontal" smooths over the differences between these accounts, presenting a teleology toward the network where the historical record is more discontinuous. `Interpretive synthesis`.
