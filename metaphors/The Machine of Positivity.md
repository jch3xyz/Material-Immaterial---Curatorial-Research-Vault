# The Machine of Positivity

## The Metaphor

The computer is a *Positivmaschine* — a machine of positivity. Han names the calculator as the purest figure of a mind from which all negativity has been removed: it computes at superhuman speed precisely *because* it is "free of all Otherness." Generalized to the whole social field, the figure expands into a second image — human beings and society as "autistic performance-machines." The metaphor and its symbolic embodiment (the computer; its human double, the idiot savant) name one and the same condition: pure positivity is calculation without negativity, and therefore without thought.

The term *Positivmaschine* already appears verbatim in [[Positivity]] (where it was cited secondhand under "Healing as Killing"); this note is its primary-source home.

## What It Compares

Target → source-domain mapping:

- **The positivized mind → the computer / calculator.** A mind stripped of negativity behaves like a processor: fast, capacious, but unable to pause, delay, or turn toward the Other.
- **Speed and capacity → freedom from Otherness.** The machine's superiority at calculation is mapped onto its *lack* — it is fast because nothing interrupts it. "The computer calculates more quickly than the human brain and takes on inordinate quantities of data without difficulty because it is free of all Otherness" (`Directly stated`).
- **The thinking subject → the idiot savant.** The human who calculates like a machine is the one in whom negativity is absent — "Because of autistic self-referentiality, because negativity is absent, an idiot savant can perform what otherwise only a calculator can do" (`Directly stated`).
- **Society → an aggregate of autistic performance-machines.** The figure scales from the individual processor to the social whole: "the general positivization of the world means that both human beings and society are transforming into autistic performance-machines" (`Directly stated`).

## What It Reveals

The metaphor makes visible Han's central equation: **pure positivity = calculation; calculation = thought without negativity, hence not thought at all.** Where the everyday metaphor of [[Mind as Machine]] (O'Gieblyn) treats the brain-as-computer as a flattering or neutral analogy, Han inverts it — to be like the computer is to have lost the very capacity that distinguishes thinking from computing.

- The machine's strength is exposed as a *deficit*: it "lacks the ability to delay," and "Machines cannot pause." Calculation is fast because it carries no [[Negativity]] — no interrupting *no*, no pause (*Innehalten*), no opening onto "the Other [das Andere]" (`Directly stated`).
- It links thinking to immune negativity. Han cites the figure of thinking as "a 'network of antibodies and natural immune defenses'" and draws the consequence: "the absence of negativity would transform it into calculation" (`Directly stated`). The machine is what thought becomes once its immune-defensive negativity is gone.
- It diagnoses contemporary subjectivity. Under the general positivization of the world ([[Positivity]]), the human does not merely *use* machines — the human *becomes* one, an "autistic performance-machine," tying the metaphor to the broader pole opposition [[Positivity vs Negativity]].

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It risks naturalizing the machine as the destiny of the mind.** Read carelessly, "human beings... transforming into autistic performance-machines" sounds like an accomplished fact rather than a tendency Han resists. His own argument is conditional and counter-factual: "If man were a being of negativity [*Negativitätswesen*], the total positivization of the world would prove more than a little dangerous" — the danger is precisely that we *forfeit* a negativity that keeps existence alive (`Strongly implied`).
- **It can flatten "calculation" into a synonym for mere stupidity.** Han is careful that the computer is "stupid" only "insofar as it lacks the ability to delay" — i.e., its deficit is specifically the absence of negative potency, not a lack of capacity. The metaphor, taken loosely, can obscure that distinction (`Directly stated`).
- **It under-states the activity the figure opposes.** The machine-image is set against contemplation, which Han insists is "anything but passivity" — an "extremely active process." The metaphor's clean opposition of fast-machine vs. slow-human can hide that the negativity Han defends is itself a *sovereign action* (the power to say no), not idleness (`Strongly implied`).
