# The Artwork as Matrix and Catalyst

## The Metaphor

For Roy Ascott the behaviourist artifact is not an end-object but a double figure: **matrix** and **catalyst**. As *matrix* it is "the substance between two sets of behaviours" — the artist's and the spectator's — a connective tissue that "exists neither for itself nor by itself." As *catalyst* it "triggers changes in the spectator's total behaviour" — an agent that initiates transformation without being the terminus of it. The work is a pivot, not a product. `Directly stated` (ch.07).

The matrix image is borrowed from chemistry and biology — the surrounding medium *in which* something is generated or held — and from religion, where, as Ascott writes of the table, it is "the matrix of transformation and transfer, of change and exchange, between two poles, two worlds." The catalyst image is borrowed from chemistry — the substance that initiates a reaction and emerges unconsumed. Together they ground the anti-object ontology of `Behaviourist Art`: the artwork is the *between*, and its job is to *set in motion*. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## What It Compares

| Target (the artwork) | Source domain |
|---|---|
| The work sits between artist and spectator | The matrix — the substance *between* two poles |
| The work has no value for-itself or by-itself | The matrix exists only as medium, not as end |
| The work provokes change in the viewer | The catalyst — triggers a reaction |
| The work survives and persists across encounters | The catalyst is not consumed by the reaction it provokes |
| The work is a field for many meanings | The matrix as "field of psychic interplay" / "field of psychic probability" |

The mapping is *functional*, not descriptive: it names what the artifact **does** in a system, not what it is made of. This is the hinge of Ascott's shift from the object to the system — the same `Object vs System` turn Jack Burnham theorized for sculpture, here recast as a relational behaviourist process. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## What It Reveals

- **It dissolves the autonomous art object.** If the work "exists neither for itself nor by itself," then it cannot be the self-sufficient, finished artifact of formalist modernism. Its being is *relational* — it is constituted only in the loop between maker and viewer. This makes the metaphor the connective-pivot image that grounds the anti-object ontology of `Behaviourist Art`. `Directly stated` (ch.05, ch.07).
- **It makes feedback the basic principle.** Because the artifact is a catalyst whose reaction feeds back into its own structure, "the basic principle is feedback": the spectator's response becomes an input that introduces more variety into the system — a `Feedback Loops` ontology of art rather than a one-way transmission. `Directly stated` (ch.07).
- **It relocates meaning in the viewer.** As a matrix "between two sets of behaviours … providing for a field of psychic interplay that can be generative of multiple meanings," the final responsibility for meaning "lies with the viewer." The work is generative, not declarative. `Directly stated` (ch.13).
- **It scales up into an institution.** Ascott extends the same figure from the single artifact to a social framework — the **Cybernetic Art Matrix (CAM)** — "a framework within which creative behaviour is generated and developed," and even describes his proposal itself "not [as] a paradigm but a catalyst to action and change." The matrix/catalyst pair is thus both the micro-ontology of the artwork and the macro-design of `Cybernetic Art Matrix`. `Directly stated` (ch.07).
- **It is a primary-source anchor for the vault's art-as-system cluster.** It gives `New Media Art` and `Art as Experiential Technology` (the committed stubs Burnham realized) their Ascottian behaviourist core: art as a triggering, connective substance rather than an object on a base. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **A catalyst is, strictly, unchanged by the reaction it enables — but Ascott's artwork is not.** He insists the artifact's "structure must be adaptive, implicitly or physically, to accommodate the spectator's responses," and concedes "the artwork is not usually at present homeostatic." The chemical catalyst metaphor undersells the *mutual* transformation Ascott actually wants: in a true self-organising system the work changes too. The figure smuggles in a one-directional residue (the work acts *on* the passive-ish viewer) that his own feedback principle is meant to abolish. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **"The substance between" can sound neutral or inert.** A matrix is a medium, and a medium can read as a mere channel — yet Ascott's whole point against `One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication` is that the work is *not* a transmission channel for "finite messages." The matrix image risks collapsing back into the one-way "transmission of alternatives" it opposes unless the catalyst half is kept active. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It can erase the artifact's persisting material specificity.** By defining the work functionally — as pure between-ness and pure triggering — the metaphor brackets the question of *what the work is made of* and *whether its particular form matters*. The table essay quietly reintroduces this ("an oval, polished mahogany table" vs "a square vinyl-topped table" generate different behaviours), but the headline matrix/catalyst figure tends to abstract the object away, which is the same risk the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` tension tracks. `Interpretive synthesis`.
