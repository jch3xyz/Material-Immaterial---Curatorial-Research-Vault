# Hypnosis vs Hallucination

## The Metaphor

The closing figure of Chapter 2 ("Media Cold and Hot") names the two altered states of consciousness that correspond to the two media temperatures: *hypnosis* for the hot medium that heats up a single sense, *hallucination* for the cool, low-definition medium that cools all the senses at once. McLuhan compresses the whole hot/cool typology into one psychophysical sentence: "So the hotting-up of one sense tends to effect hypnosis, and the cooling of all senses tends to result in hallucination." (`Directly stated`)

The figure is the sensory correlate of the hot/cool axis. Where [[Media Hot and Cold]] sorts media by "definition" and inverse participation, this metaphor states what those temperatures *do to the perceiver* — it reads media effects off the body's sensorium rather than off content.

## What It Compares

- **Target** → the two opposite effects of media temperature on the human sensorium. Heating one sense to "dominant command of a situation" produces one effect; withdrawing all sensation produces the other.
- **Source domain** → two recognizable altered states. *Hypnosis*: the trance of a perceiver locked onto a single, over-amplified stimulus — McLuhan's earlier-in-chapter Narcissus/Newton figures, the "hypnotized Narcissus types" who fall into "psychic rigor mortis, or of somnambulism." (`Directly stated`) *Hallucination*: the brain's "furious fill-in or completion of senses that is sheer hallucination" when "all outer sensation is withdrawn" — the sensory-deprivation experiment as the limit case of the cool, high-participation medium. (`Directly stated`)

The mapping is therefore: hot medium → one sense over-filled → low participation → hypnosis; cool medium → all senses under-filled → maximal participation/completion → hallucination. The audience "fill-in" that defines a cool medium throughout the chapter (the eye acting "as hand in filling in and completing the image," the reader as "co-author" of the detective story) is, pushed to its extreme, the same fill-in that produces hallucination. (`Strongly implied` — McLuhan uses the identical "fill-in" language for cool-media participation and for hallucinatory completion, but does not label the two explicitly as one mechanism.)

## What It Reveals

- **It locates media effect in the [[Sense Ratios|sense ratios]], not in content.** Hypnosis and hallucination are states of the *whole* sensorium — one sense raised to "dominant command," or every sense lowered together. The metaphor makes vivid McLuhan's claim that a medium acts by re-proportioning the senses beneath opinion and message; the perceiver's state of consciousness is an index of the prevailing sense ratio. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **It turns participation into pathology, both ways.** Neither pole is neutral or safe. The hot medium does not merely "exclude" the audience; it can numb and entrance ("a lifelong state of psychic rigor mortis"). The cool medium does not merely "include"; pushed far enough, completion becomes confabulation. The figure refuses the easy reading in which cool, high-participation media are simply the healthier kind. (`Strongly implied`)
- **It seeds the narcosis theme.** Hypnosis here is the Chapter 2 anticipation of the fuller account McLuhan gives in Chapter 4, "The Gadget Lover: Narcissus as Narcosis," where extension becomes self-amputation and numbing: "Any invention or technology is an extension or self-amputation of our physical bodies, and such extension also demands new ratios or new equilibriums among the other organs and extensions of the body." The Narcissus who is hypnotized by his own extended image, and the perceiver hypnotized by the over-heated single sense, are the same numbed figure — the narcosis that protects the nervous system by anaesthetizing it. (`Interpretive synthesis`; the explicit narcosis/self-amputation account is in Ch4, cross-linked via [[Sense Ratios]] and [[Media as Extensions of Man]].)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **The clinical terms are analogies, not diagnoses.** McLuhan borrows "hypnosis" and "hallucination" as vivid psychophysical shorthand; he does not establish that media literally induce these states, nor cite the perceptual science behind the sensory-deprivation result he invokes. The figure persuades by aphoristic symmetry — "hotting-up of one sense" against "cooling of all senses" — and the neatness of the chiasmus can stand in for an argument. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **The neat one-vs-all opposition flattens real perception.** Few media heat exactly one sense in isolation or cool literally "all" senses; the binary is an idealization. McLuhan's own chapter concedes that "it makes all the difference whether a hot medium is used in a hot or a cool culture," so the effect is never purely a function of the medium's temperature — culture, prior sense ratio, and historical "reversal" all intervene. The hypnosis/hallucination figure abstracts from exactly those mediating conditions. (`Strongly implied`)
- **It risks reading agency out of the perceiver.** Cast as hypnosis or hallucination, the audience appears as something done-to by media temperature — entranced or made to confabulate — which understates the deliberate, skilled "fill-in" (the seminar over the lecture, "dig"-ging the message) that McLuhan elsewhere in the chapter prizes as participation and insight. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
