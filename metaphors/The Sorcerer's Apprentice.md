# The Sorcerer's Apprentice

## The Metaphor

Instrumentarian power as Goethe's sorcerer's apprentice. In the master's absence the apprentice enchants a broom to do his work, but with no authority to guide or check it the broom transforms into "a demonic force of pure unrelenting power" that floods the room. Zuboff makes the apprentice's broom the figure for [[Instrumentarian Power]]: it has "flourished in the sorcerer's absence with little authority to check its action," and like the broom that fetches faster and faster, surveillance capital's "appetite for totality has grown with this success."

## What It Compares

Target → source-domain mapping:

- the apprentice's enchanted broom → [[Instrumentarian Power]] / [[Big Other]], an artifice set in motion to serve, now running unchecked
- the absent sorcerer → the missing authority (in Zuboff's catalogue: "democratic institutions; laws; regulations; rights and obligations… civil society; the political authority of the people; and the moral authority of individual human beings") that alone could "guide and check the action"
- the broom that "runs and fetches faster" with "ever new… torrents" → the self-amplifying drive whose "appetite for totality has grown with this success"
- the flood that pours "a hundred currents… upon my head" → the totalizing reach of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] as it "deletes all boundaries and overwhelms every source of friction"

## What It Reveals

The metaphor makes legible the structural condition Zuboff calls "the sorcerer's absence." Her claim is not that surveillance capitalists are uniquely malevolent but that "all power yearns toward totality, and only authority stands in the way" — and that authority has been missing, so nothing has called the broom to a halt. (Directly stated.) It locates the danger in an *absence of friction* rather than in any single villain: the broom is dangerous because there is no master's word to stop it, just as instrumentarian power is dangerous because the institutions that could check it have stood aside. (Strongly implied.)

It also captures the perverse feedback by which **success breeds appetite**: each conquest enlarges the ambition for the next, so the drive toward totality is self-accelerating, not self-limiting — the broom's currents multiply. This ties the figure to [[A Utopia of Certainty]] and the argument that [[Instrumentarian Power]] is "a new species of power" whose logic is expansion without satiety. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

A fable casts the apprentice as a naïve dabbler who *loses control* of his magic — an accident of inexperience. But Zuboff's surveillance capitalists do not lose control; they pursue totality deliberately and profitably, conforming to "the classic profile of the most ambitious modern utopianists." The broom image can thus understate **intentionality and economic compulsion**: the flood is not a mishap but the operation of [[The Extraction Imperative]] and [[The Prediction Imperative]] working exactly as designed. (Interpretive synthesis.)

The fable also promises a tidy ending — the returning master speaks the word and "makes the broom a broom once more." That restoration is precisely what is in doubt in Zuboff's account, where the authority to utter the word may no longer exist. The metaphor's reassuring narrative arc can soften the book's harder warning that there may be **no sorcerer left to return**. (Strongly implied.)
