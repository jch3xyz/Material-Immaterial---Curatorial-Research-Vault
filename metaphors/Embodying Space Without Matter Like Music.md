# Embodying Space Without Matter Like Music

## The Metaphor

Sculpture's deepest unrealized aspiration is to embody space *like music* — to produce a pure spatial expression that organizes and inhabits space without the burden of material substance, the way sound fills a room without being a thing in it. Burnham names this "the desire to embody space without matter-s-like music" and reads it not as a quirk of one art but as a figure for a much larger drive: a "profound striving of Western technology" to "produce effect without the hindrance or fallibility of objects." `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

- **Target**: pure spatial expression in sculpture — space "undisturbed by visible material form," shaped and felt without the mediation of mass, weight, or fabricated object.
- **Source domain**: music — an art that demonstrably structures and "embodies" space (intervals, volumes, durations, presence) while having no material body of its own; effect without object.
- **Mapping**: as music achieves expressive form without a persisting material artifact, so the ideal sculpture would achieve spatial form without matter. The analogy carries an implied second mapping that Burnham makes explicit — sculpture's longing : Western technology's striving — equating the dream of immaterial art with the engineering dream of effect divorced from the recalcitrance of physical objects. `Directly stated`

## What It Reveals

The metaphor names the immaterial pole of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis at its purest: the wish for an art (and a technology) whose results are felt but whose substrate has disappeared. By routing that wish through music — an art everyone grants is "immaterial" yet undeniably real and present — Burnham makes the aspiration intelligible rather than mystical: dematerialization is not nothing, it is effect-without-object. This is why the figure reaches past sculpture into "a profound striving of Western technology": the same logic that wants spatial expression without matter wants machinery whose effects arrive without "the hindrance or fallibility of objects." It thereby links the history of modern sculpture to the broader cultural movement toward [[Dematerialization]] and toward the [[Object and System|system]] as the locus of art, where what matters is the relation or effect, not the discrete material thing. `Strongly implied`

## What It Hides or Distorts

Burnham himself frames the longing as "unrequited": "Pure spatial expression is one of the unrequited longings of our age." `Directly stated` The metaphor flatters the immaterial ideal while concealing that, for sculpture, it remains unfulfillable — sculpture "remains bound by Newton's rectilinear coordinate system... and a prisoner of this earthly continuum." The music analogy hides the difference between an art native to time and immateriality (music) and an art historically rooted in matter and mass (sculpture); it smuggles in the assumption that escaping the object is a triumph rather than an evasion. The phrase "without the hindrance or fallibility of objects" also quietly recodes material resistance as mere defect — as obstacle and unreliability — when that resistance is precisely what gives the object its presence and limits. The figure thus risks naturalizing the technological wish to be rid of matter as if matter were only friction to be engineered away. `Interpretive synthesis`
