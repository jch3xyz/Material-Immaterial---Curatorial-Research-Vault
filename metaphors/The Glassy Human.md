# The Glassy Human

## The Metaphor

"Transparency makes the human being glassy." Han's compact figure for the subject as it is rendered under the [[The Transparency Society|transparency regime]]: a person no longer opaque, no longer harboring an interior, but a body and psyche made see-through — material for inspection, communication, and exploitation. The glassy human is the anthropological correlate of total transparency, and a recurring visual register that, on the planner's note, thickens later in the book into the vocabulary of "utter illumination" and *Durchleuchtung* (Ch. 9). Within the [[03 Preface|Preface]] itself the figure is stated in its barest form: glassiness as violence.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the human being as subject of the digital, neoliberal [[Society of Control]] — its psyche, its private and intimate sphere, its capacity for [[The Other|Otherness]] and [[Secrecy]].
- **Source domain:** *glass*. A material that is at once transparent (light passes through), hard yet brittle (it shatters), and surface-only (it has no interior to hide). The metaphor maps the subject onto a substance whose defining property is the absence of opacity.

The mapping: as glass to light, so the human being to information. What in an opaque subject would remain inward — secrecy, foreignness, otherness — is, in the glassy subject, forced "inward in order to transform it into information." The interior is not abolished; it is reformatted as throughput.

## What It Reveals

- **Transparency as violence, not openness.** Han's sentence — "Therein lies its violence" — refuses the liberal framing of transparency as emancipation. Glassiness is not a freedom but an injury: a subject made viewable is a subject made vulnerable to the gaze. `Directly stated` in the [[03 Preface|Preface]].
- **The continuity between communication and control.** Glassiness names the point at which "unrestricted freedom and communication switch into total control and surveillance." The metaphor compresses the book's thesis that freedom of information and the [[Digital Panopticon]] are not opposites but two faces of the same material. `Directly stated`.
- **The neoliberal subject as productive surface.** Because "transparency is a [[Neoliberal Dispositive|neoliberal dispositive]]" that "forces everything inward in order to transform it into information," the glassy human is legible as the worker of "[[Immaterial Relations of Production|immaterial relations of production]]" — a being whose interiority has been refactored into exploitable signal. `Strongly implied` from the surrounding paragraph.
- **Self-illumination as collaboration.** Glassiness, unlike the opacity broken open by the classical panopticon, is *self-produced*: inhabitants of the digital panopticon "engage in lively communication and bare themselves of their own free will." The metaphor reveals that the violence is internalized — the subject becomes its own glazier. `Directly stated`.
- **A visual register that scales.** Even within the Preface, the figure travels: from glassiness, to "digital panoptica," to "voluntary self-illumination and self-exposure." Planner note: this visual lexicon thickens later into *Durchleuchtung* / "utter illumination" — see [[Utter Illumination (Durchleuchtung)]]. `Interpretive synthesis` regarding the trajectory beyond the Preface.

## What It Hides or Distorts

Metaphors always occlude. The glass figure carries its own blind spots.

- **It implies a prior opacity that may be idealized.** To say the human is *made* glassy presupposes a once-opaque subject — a bounded interiority that transparency then violates. This risks naturalizing a particular (modern, bourgeois) model of the private subject as the human baseline, and treating its loss as the only intelligible harm. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It aestheticizes a political-economic process.** "Glassy" is a beautiful, almost crystalline image; it can soften what Han elsewhere names as exploitation under "immaterial relations of production." The visual register can crowd out the labor register. `Speculative connection`.
- **It can suggest passivity where Han insists on activity.** Glass is acted upon; it does not act. But Han's subjects "actively collaborate," "bare themselves of their own free will," communicate "out of inner need." The metaphor of being-made-glassy understates the volitional, even libidinal investment in self-exposure that the same Preface foregrounds. A tension internal to the figure. `Strongly implied`.
- **It collapses different opacities.** Secrecy, foreignness, and [[The Other|Otherness]] are grouped together in the Preface as "obstacles for communication without borders." The glass metaphor treats them as a single property — non-transparency — and risks losing the distinctions between political dissent, ethical alterity, and mere private reserve. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **Glass shatters; information does not.** The brittleness of glass suggests a breaking point, a limit at which violence becomes visible. But Han's argument is that transparency works precisely by *not* shattering the subject — by smoothing, leveling, synchronizing. The figure's most vivid physical property (fragility) is the one the analysis least uses. `Speculative connection`.
