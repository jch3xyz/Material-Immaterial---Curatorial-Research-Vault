# Man as the Sex Organs of the Machine World

## The Metaphor

McLuhan figures the human-machine relation through biological reproduction: "Man becomes, as it were, the sex organs of the machine world, as the bee of the plant world." Just as the bee, in seeking nectar, unwittingly serves the plant's reproduction — carrying pollen so that new flowers can grow — so the human, in inventing, using, and embracing technology, serves the machine world's propagation and evolution. The relation is reciprocal: the machine world "reciprocates man's love" by "expediting his wishes and desires," chiefly by "providing him with wealth." `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

| Target (the technological relation) | Source domain (biological reproduction) |
|---|---|
| Human being inventing and using machines | The bee visiting the flower |
| The machine world / technology | The plant world |
| Technological proliferation and the evolution of new machine forms | Fecundation and the evolution of new plant forms |
| Wealth provided to humans by technology | Nectar / reward the bee receives from the plant |

The mapping inverts the commonsense hierarchy. The agent who *seems* to be in charge (the human inventor, as the bee seems merely to be feeding) is recast as the reproductive *instrument* of the system being served. Technology, not the human, is the entity that "evolves ever new forms." `Strongly implied`

## What It Reveals

- **Technology has its own evolutionary momentum.** By placing the human in the role of the bee — a vector, not the origin — McLuhan makes thinkable the idea that technical forms reproduce and develop through a quasi-autonomous logic, using humans as their means. This is continuous with his argument across the chapter that any invention is "an extension or self-amputation of our physical bodies." `Strongly implied`
- **The relation is one of mutual servitude, not mastery.** The metaphor pairs tightly with the servomechanism figure in the same passage: "By continuously embracing technologies, we relate ourselves to them as servo-mechanisms... we must, to use them at all, serve these objects, these extensions of ourselves, as gods or minor religions." The human serves technology and is rewarded by it — a closed loop of love and reciprocation. See `Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions`. `Directly stated`
- **A durable cyborg/posthuman image.** The figure of the human fused into, and functionally subordinate to, a self-propagating technical system anticipates later accounts of the human-machine hybrid, where "the machine is us, an aspect of our embodiment" (Haraway) and where the boundary between organism and machine has broken down. See `The Cyborg`. `Interpretive synthesis`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It naturalizes technological proliferation as benign symbiosis.** The bee-and-flower image is mutualist and reproductive — life-giving — which casts the machine world's growth as a natural, even loving, partnership rather than something driven by markets, war, or domination. The chapter itself partly undercuts this elsewhere, noting that "war and the fear of war have always been considered the main incentives to technological extension of our bodies." `Strongly implied`
- **It obscures who profits.** Reducing technology's reward to "providing him with wealth" flattens the question of *which* humans accumulate that wealth and which are merely instruments — the metaphor's universal "man" hides distributional and political difference. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It erases human intention while smuggling it back as "love."** Humans are figured as blind reproductive organs (the bee acts without grasping its role), yet the machine reciprocates "man's love" — attributing desire to the very agency the metaphor has just demoted. The figure both denies and affirms human will. `Speculative connection`
