# The Kybernetes as the New Prime Mover

## The Metaphor

The closing figure of Burnham's Introduction to *Beyond Modern Sculpture*: as an age that "sought vitality in latent visual metaphor" ends, "the 'elan vital' will be looked upon as the old prime mover, while the Greek 'kybernetes' (derived from steersman and the basis of the word cybernetics) becomes the expression of a new and even more effective prime mover" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). Two sources of motion and life are set side by side and one supersedes the other: the *élan vital* — Bergson's "vital impulse," the inner life-force that vitalist sculpture tried to render in stone — is demoted to the *old* prime mover, while the *kybernetes*, the steersman whose name gives [[Cybernetics]] its root, is installed as the *new* and "more effective" one. This is the book's pivot from the biotic metaphor to the cybernetic system, condensed into a single succession of animating principles.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the *prime mover* — the source of motion, animation, and "life" in art and, by extension, in a technological culture; the principle that brings inert matter to life.
- **Source domain (old):** the **élan vital**, the vitalist life-force that biomorphic and organicist sculpture treated as the animating essence to be imitated ("the so-called biomorphic idiom relied on vitalism," [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]).
- **Source domain (new):** the **kybernetes**, the *steersman* of the ship — the figure of governance, control, and feedback-steering from which Norbert Wiener derived the word "cybernetics," now made the more effective animator via [[Cybernetics]], [[Feedback Loops|feedback]], and [[Homeostasis]].

The mapping is a succession, not a substitution of like for like: a *biological/metaphysical* source of life (the vital impulse) is replaced by a *governing/operational* one (the steersman who controls a system). The phrase "prime mover" carries its own buried theology — Aristotle's and the schoolmen's *primum movens*, the unmoved mover that is the first cause of all motion — so Burnham is naming the kybernetes the new first cause of artistic life. "More effective" is the load-bearing word: the steersman does not merely *symbolize* life as vitalist sculpture did; through cybernetic control it actually produces lifelike behavior ([[Cyborg Art]], "the art of cybernetic organisms").

## What It Reveals

- **The book's structural hinge.** The figure makes legible Burnham's entire object→system thesis ([[Object and System]]): Part I treats [[Vitalism]] and [[Organicism]] as the "biotic sources" — sculpture under the *old* prime mover, trying to imbue mass with the life-force; Part II follows the drift into [[Automata]], [[Kinetic Art|kineticism]], light, and finally [[Cyborg Art]] — sculpture under the *new* prime mover, animated by control and communication. The metaphor is the seam between the two halves. *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis.*
- **Why vitalism had to be superseded.** It dramatizes Burnham's claim that vitalism "lasted as long as it remained tenable as a scientific philosophy" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). Once life is reconceived as "physicochemical conditions rather than spiritual energies," the *élan vital* loses its scientific warrant and the steersman — control, feedback, self-regulation — takes over the work of animation. This is sculpture's [[Reification]] of the prevailing scientific world-picture. *Strongly implied.*
- **The cybernetic redefinition of life as governance.** By rooting the new prime mover in *kybernetes* (steersman) rather than in any organic essence, the figure reveals the cybernetic premise the vault tracks across [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] and [[Donna Haraway|Haraway]]: that life is not a substance but a pattern of control and communication, equally realizable in "the animal and the machine." It anticipates the [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] and the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] by relocating vitality from biology into system. *Interpretive synthesis.*

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It smuggles theology back in while claiming to retire it.** "Prime mover" is the language of the unmoved mover, a first cause — exactly the metaphysical apparatus the move from vitalism to cybernetics is supposed to dissolve. Calling the kybernetes a "more effective prime mover" does not escape the demiurgic frame; it re-enthrones it in cybernetic dress. The "scientific demiurge" Burnham elsewhere invokes is a god by another name. *Interpretive synthesis.*
- **It naturalizes a succession that is really a substitution of values.** Framing the *élan vital* as merely the "old" mover implies an orderly, almost evolutionary handoff, masking that vitalism and cybernetics are *incompatible accounts of life* (one essentialist and inward, one operational and relational) — the genuine opposition the vault files as [[Mechanism vs Vitalism]]. The smooth "old → new" rhetoric softens a conceptual rupture into a generational changing of the guard. *Interpretive synthesis.*
- **It overstates "more effective."** The claim that the steersman is the *more effective* animator presumes cybernetic systems can in fact bring "inert matter to life" — a wager the book itself hedges as "art fiction" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). The metaphor's confidence ("even more effective") outruns the evidence it rests on, treating an anticipated future as an accomplished succession. *Strongly implied.*
