# We Are the Native Peoples Now

## The Metaphor

"We are the native peoples now." Zuboff casts the user of surveillance capitalism as a colonized indigenous people — a population whose lands (here, the inner territory of lived experience) are claimed, mapped, and exploited by an external power, and whose own claims to that territory have been quietly erased from the official record. The human subject is simultaneously "human natural resources" (the resource to be extracted) and an exile, "exiles from our own behavior," dispossessed of the very experience that surveillance capital harvests as `[[Behavioral Surplus]]`.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** human beings whose behavior is rendered into data, predicted, and sold under `[[Surveillance Capitalism]]`.
- **Source domain:** native peoples under colonial conquest — populations declared a frontier, their land treated as virgin and unclaimed, their tacit ownership written out of the colonizers' maps.

The mapping is precise and runs through the whole passage:
- human **experience** ↔ colonized **land/territory** ("human experience could be Google's virgin wood")
- **digital dispossession** ↔ colonial **primitive accumulation / accumulation by dispossession** (Polanyi's "commodity fictions," Marx's "original sin of simple robbery," Harvey's "accumulation by dispossession," all named by Zuboff in the same section)
- the user as **"human natural resources"** ↔ the colonized as **extractable resource**
- our experience as **"virgin and blameless as nature's once-plentiful meadows and forests"** ↔ the colonial fiction of **terra nullius**, empty land free for the taking
- our **"tacit claims to self-determination"** that **"have vanished from the maps of our own experience"** ↔ indigenous land claims **erased from colonial cartography**

The map figure is the metaphor's hinge: dispossession is accomplished not only by force but by representation — by drawing a map on which the prior inhabitants' claims simply do not appear. `Directly stated` — Zuboff writes "we are the native peoples now," "exiles from our own behavior," and "human natural resources" verbatim; the colonial-accumulation lineage (Polanyi/Marx/Arendt/Harvey) is laid out explicitly in the same section.

## What It Reveals

- **Dispossession, not exchange.** The figure reframes data extraction as *taking*, not *trading*. We are not customers in a fair bargain but a frontier population whose territory is being annexed; "we are not essential to this market action." This is the affective and political core of `[[Digital Dispossession]]`. `Directly stated`.
- **The continuity with capitalism's deep history.** By placing the user inside the long colonial cycle of "primitive accumulation" — Arendt's "recurring phase in a repeating cycle," Harvey's drive into "new, undefended territories" — the metaphor denies surveillance capitalism the alibi of novelty or inevitability. It is the oldest move in capital's repertoire, "not a wave but the tide itself." `Directly stated`.
- **Exile as condition.** Casting us as "exiles from our own behavior" names a specific harm: not the loss of a possession but estrangement from one's own life, "denied access to or control over knowledge derived from its dispossession." This is the experiential ground of the `[[Home vs Exile]]` tension — the self made homeless in its own experience. `Strongly implied` (the exile language is direct; reading it as a *home/exile* structure is interpretive synthesis with the tension note).
- **The violence of the map.** That tacit claims "vanished from the maps of our own experience" exposes representation itself as an instrument of conquest: what cannot be seen on the map cannot be defended. Surveillance capitalism's power lies partly in being unmappable to those it maps. `Strongly implied`.

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It risks appropriating a real historical trauma.** The colonial-indigenous figure borrows the moral weight of actual genocidal dispossession to describe relatively privileged digital subjects. The analogy can flatten the difference between losing ancestral land and lives and losing control over one's clickstream, and it speaks *for* "native peoples" while the people it actually describes are largely the colonizers' historical heirs. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It can imply a lost prior innocence.** The image of experience as "virgin and blameless" meadows before the market suggests a pristine pre-digital self that was whole and self-possessed — a romanticized "home" that may never have existed, since experience was already shaped by older media, markets, and power. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It positions us as passive victims.** A conquered native people is acted upon; the metaphor can occlude the everyday participation, convenience-seeking, and consent (however manufactured) through which users supply the surplus — the dimension Zuboff elsewhere theorizes as the demand-side "collision." The figure dramatizes the taking and underplays the giving. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It naturalizes the extractor.** If we are the natives, the surveillance capitalist is the settler/sovereign — which can subtly grant the conqueror an air of historical inevitability ("the tide itself"), the very fatalism Zuboff's larger argument fights against. `Speculative connection`.
