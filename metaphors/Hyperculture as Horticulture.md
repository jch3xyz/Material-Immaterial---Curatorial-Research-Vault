# Hyperculture as Horticulture

## The Metaphor

The artist does not fabricate the future; the artist plants it. Ascott names the figure directly: "hyperculture seen as horticulture, the moist synthesis of artificial and natural systems." Making becomes growing. In a moist culture — Ascott's "moist domain" where "dry pixels and wet molecules converge" — the creative act is recast as cultivation: the artist seeds concepts, plants images, tends, and lets emerge. The horticultural register governs an entire vocabulary of "moist design": **seeding** replaces **designing**, **growth** replaces **construction**, and the work's increase "depends on cybersynthesis rather than photosynthesis." This is the same root metaphor Ascott applies to the city in [[Growbag Culture]], here generalised from architecture to the whole [[Moistmedia|moist]] synthesis of the artificial and the natural.

## What It Compares

|---|---|

| The finished, fabricated object | The living, grown system |

The metaphor maps the *production* of culture onto the *cultivation* of living things. Where the engineering register imagines the artist building, assembling, or programming, the horticultural register imagines the artist setting conditions and tending growth — sowing seeds whose final form cannot be fully dictated. Ascott extends "the wise application of plant technology, in the technoetic context, to the creative employment of horticultural metaphor in envisioning outcomes at the material level of construction." (Directly stated — Ascott names hyperculture-as-horticulture, seeding, planting, pollination, grafting, and cybersynthesis explicitly.)

## What It Reveals

- **Synthesis, not replacement, of artificial and natural.** The phrase "the moist synthesis of artificial and natural systems" is the metaphor's payload: the digital ("dry") and the biological ("wet") are not opposed but combined in a "moist" third domain. Horticulture is the apt image precisely because a garden is already a synthesis — engineered conditions hosting living growth. This makes [[Moistmedia]] ("bits, atoms, neurons, and genes") thinkable as something cultivated rather than manufactured. (Directly stated / Strongly implied.)
- **Bottom-up emergence over top-down imposition.** "MOIST DESIGN is bottom-up, seeded, and emergent," and Ascott's ch.32 entry sets "Design" — "always a top-down affair with blueprints, master plans, and models" — against "Biohaus," which calls for "a biology of building." Seeding cedes control to growth: the cultivator tends but cannot pre-specify the outcome, the same logic that lets [[The Planetary Collegium]] "plan for itself." (Directly stated.)
- **The artist as cultivator and an ethics of care.** It is "in large measure the artist's responsibility to plant, grow, and cultivate" the "self-organising, self-aware, planetary environment." The metaphor reassigns the creative agent from author to gardener — nurturing, reseeding, and tending a living system — extending the practice-redefinition the vault tracks under [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]. (Directly stated.)
- **A continuity with Burnham's object→system shift.** Casting art-making as cultivating living systems rather than producing objects is the horticultural face of [[Systems Esthetics]] — the move "from object to system" — and threads the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension: the seeded work is immaterial in its informational growth yet "re-materialises" in molecules, atoms, and genes. (Interpretive synthesis — Burnham is the vault's systems-esthetics anchor; Ascott does not cite him here.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It naturalises authored, funded, governed choices.** Calling culture "grown" can launder the human, political, and economic decisions that still seed, fund, prune, and patent it into the appearance of organic inevitability — a risk sharpened by Ascott's own gesture toward "the new transnational marketplace of e-commerce" and "private investment" for the Collegium. (Speculative connection.)
- **Gardens are managed regimes.** A growbag is an engineered, bounded, fertilised container; "letting it grow" is itself a regime of selecting, grafting, and weeding. The promise of release from "designing" may understate how much cultivation remains a form of control. (Interpretive synthesis.)
- **Photosynthesis is not cybersynthesis.** The analogy borrows the *image* of organic growth while quietly substituting a different engine — "cybersynthesis rather than photosynthesis." Network-driven proliferation is not metabolism; the figure can smuggle the felt naturalness of a plant onto processes that are computational, recombinant, and potentially viral. (Interpretive synthesis.)
- **It softens the spiritual-shamanic claims.** Ascott's horticulture runs through "cyberbotany" and the psychoactive plant (ayahuasca), folding [[Technoetics]] and the "spiritually numinous" into the metaphor. The agrarian innocence of "seeding" can obscure how strong — and contestable — those claims about consciousness and the "moist mind" are. (Speculative connection.)
