# Render Unto Caesar

## The Metaphor

"Rendering unto surveillance capitalism" its "continuous tithe of raw-material supplies." Zuboff plays the theological figure of *Render unto Caesar* — the Gospel injunction of obligatory tribute to a sovereign power — onto the second, *sur-render* sense of the verb *render*. Where the concept [[Rendition]] names the active extraction by which "something is formed out of something else" (oil from fat, behavior from experience), *Render Unto Caesar* names the **other side of the equation**: the way the thing that is changed "gives itself over to this process: it sur-renders." Every encounter with a digital interface is recast as an act of obligatory tribute — a tithe paid, not by choice, to a power that has assumed the position of Caesar.

## What It Compares

| Source domain (tribute / tithe) | Target (datafication) |
|---|---|
| Caesar — the sovereign owed tribute | Surveillance capitalism — the power to which experience is rendered |
| The tithe — a recurring, obligatory payment | The "continuous" supply of behavioral surplus rendered at each interface |
| Rendering unto Caesar — handing over, acknowledging dependency or obligation | "Rendering unto surveillance capitalism" — making experience available to datafication |

Zuboff explicitly grounds the figure in the etymology of *render/rendre*, which by the fourteenth century "incorporated the idea of handing over, delivering, or acknowledging dependency or obligation, as in 'Render unto Caesar.'" *(Directly stated.)*

## What It Reveals

- **The double meaning of rendition.** The metaphor makes visible that surveillance capitalism "must work both sides of the equation": on one side experience is rendered *from* us as oil from fat (extraction, outside awareness); on the other, we render experience *unto* the apparatus (sur-render, the tithe). The single verb *render* holds both the active dispossession and the structural yielding. *(Directly stated.)*
- **The obligatory, sovereign character of the relation.** Casting datafication as tribute to a Caesar exposes it as a relation of dependency and obligation, not exchange. Zuboff stresses that "individuals do not render their experience out of choice or obligation but rather out of ignorance and the dictatorship of no alternatives" — the metaphor's theological weight underscores that the tithe is exacted, "unauthorized, unilateral, gluttonous, secret, and brazen." *(Directly stated.)*
- **Continuity with [[Capital as Religion]].** The theological register — tithe, tribute, a power occupying the throne of Caesar — converges with the vault's existing diagnosis (Han, Harari) of capital as a religion that admits no transcendence and no redemption, only perpetual obligation. *(Interpretive synthesis.)*

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **The voluntary appearance of the tithe.** A tribute to Caesar is *recognized* as a payment to a sovereign; the digital tithe is paid without recognition. The phrase "rendering unto surveillance capitalism" lends a clarity of obligation that the actual operation lacks — rendition "typically occurs outside of our awareness, let alone our consent." The biblical idiom risks making the surrender sound like an acknowledged duty rather than an "involuntary rendition" that has "become an inescapable fact." *(Strongly implied.)*
- **The asymmetry of return.** Caesar, in the original, renders protection and order in exchange for tribute; the rendering subject gets a coin's worth of governance. The surveillance-capitalist tithe returns nothing comparable — "we are forced to purchase products that we can never own while our payments fund our own surveillance and coercion." The metaphor's symmetry of obligation can mask a one-directional dispossession. *(Strongly implied.)*
- **Caesar's visibility.** Caesar is a known, located sovereign; surveillance capital "impose[s] their will backstage," its rendition "implemented by invisible methods." The figure of a nameable Caesar may over-personalize a diffuse, "ubiquitous apparatus." *(Interpretive synthesis.)*
