# The Communication of the Incommunicable

## The Metaphor

In thesis 192 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*, [[Guy Debord]] names spectacular culture "the communication of the incommunicable." The figure works by paradox: the spectacle presents itself as a vast apparatus of communication — museums, media, the wholesale circulation of all past art — yet what it actually transmits is the fact that real communication is absent. The spectacle "gives overt expression in its cultural sector to what it implicitly is in its totality: the communication of the incommunicable" (thesis 192). The cultural sphere becomes the place where the spectacle *says out loud* what it *is* everywhere: a message whose content is the impossibility of a message. `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

- **Target**: the spectacle's cultural sector — the "spectacular consumption that preserves past culture in congealed form" (thesis 192); the "age of museums in which artistic communication is no longer possible" (thesis 189), where "all the previous expressions of art can be accepted equally" precisely because they are eclipsed by "all the present-day obstacles to communication in general."
- **Source domain**: a communicative act — a transmission, an utterance, a message — whose delivered content is *the incommunicable itself*: the announcement that "all communication has been smugly declared absent" (thesis 192).

The mapping folds the sender's function against its content. Where an ordinary message carries something across, this one carries across the news of its own emptiness — communication reduced to a signal that broadcasts the death of communication.

## What It Reveals

The metaphor exposes the mechanism of [[Recuperation]]. Because the spectacle communicates only incommunicability, even the negative — art and language turned against themselves — can be absorbed as confirmation. "The most extreme destruction of language can be officially welcomed as a positive development because it amounts to yet one more way of flaunting one's acceptance of a status quo where all communication has been smugly declared absent" (thesis 192). Dada and Surrealism, the "modern decomposition and formal destruction of all art," are not defeated but *consumed*: revolt survives only as a spectacle of revolt. The figure reveals that the spectacle's relation to its own critique is not incidental co-optation but its essential strategy — "to use culture to bury all historical memory" (thesis 192), turning the negation of communication into content that reinforces the spectacle's own thesis. `Directly stated`

It also reveals the metaphor's kinship with [[Reification]]: culture is consumed as a "dead object for spectacular contemplation" (thesis 184), congealed and contemplatable precisely because it no longer lives as communication between people. The living relation is preserved only in the form of its corpse — and the display of the corpse is what passes for culture. `Interpretive synthesis`

## What It Hides or Distorts

Taken as a totalizing verdict, the figure risks flattening every cultural act into pure spectacle, foreclosing the possibility of genuine communication it means to mourn. Debord himself guards against this: the "critical truth" of art's self-destruction — "the real life of modern poetry and art" — is "concealed" by the spectacle but not annihilated (thesis 192), and he insists that the "critical concept of 'the spectacle'" can *itself* be "turned into one more hollow formula of sociologico-political rhetoric" (thesis 203). The danger the metaphor hides is that it can be recuperated in turn — deployed as a knowing, elegant diagnosis that "flaunts one's acceptance of a status quo," performing the very incommunicability it names rather than breaking it. The image of communication-as-death can console instead of provoke, letting "the death of communication" acquire "a sufficient beauty of its own" (thesis 192). `Strongly implied`
