# Form as a Waterfall Not a Crystal

## The Metaphor

An organic form is "more comparable to that of a waterfall than a crystal." The figure is the biologist Edmund Sinnott's, quoted by Jack Burnham in his account of the biotic sources of modern sculpture and applied to the concretions of Jean Arp. A crystal is a fixed lattice: its form is identical with its static material arrangement. A waterfall, by contrast, keeps its shape while the water that constitutes it is wholly replaced from moment to moment — form persists precisely because matter passes through. The living thing, and the vitalist sculpture that images it, is a waterfall, not a crystal.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the organism (and, by extension, Arp's biomorphic sculpture) as a form whose persistence does not depend on the persistence of its matter.
- **Source domain:** a waterfall — a stable visible shape produced by, and only by, continuous material throughput — opposed to a crystal, the paradigm of inorganic form whose shape is its static substance.

The mapping turns on what holds form steady. In the crystal, form is held by the rigid arrangement of fixed parts. In the waterfall, form is held by the *rate and order of flow*: "Materials are constantly entering it and leaving it." Form here is "the result of progress" — an equilibrium that is "dynamic, not static" (Directly stated by Sinnott via Burnham). Sinnott extends the same intuition to time: an organism "has been called a slice of spacetime, since it changes in respect to both." Form is a process caught in cross-section, not a thing.

## What It Reveals

The metaphor makes thinkable a form that is identical with a flux rather than with a substance — and so underwrites the whole vitalist program Burnham is tracing. Arp's "concretions" stand still in plaster or bronze, yet through "signs of growth, mitosis, or metamorphic activity" they coax "a beholder is induced to sense life encased in some rock-hard material" (Directly stated). The waterfall figure licenses this: a sculpture can be "an extremely fluid, tenuous system frozen in plaster or bronze," alluding to "laws of dynamics which apply to systems suspended in another atmospheric density, namely water" (Directly stated). The dynamic-equilibrium image — matter cycling through a self-maintaining boundary — is the biotic precursor of the cybernetic notion of [[Homeostasis]] that later organism-as-system thinking would formalize (Interpretive synthesis). Read against the vault's running material/immaterial question, the metaphor relocates identity from the stuff to the pattern that organizes its turnover, a move close to the informatic reframings of [[Embodiment]] elsewhere in the corpus (Speculative connection).

## What It Hides or Distorts

The figure smooths over how much it imports. A waterfall is shaped by gravity and an external supply of water; it has no boundary it maintains, no "specific and orderly development," and no inheritance — it does not grow, differentiate, or reproduce. Sinnott's own qualifications pull against the image even as he offers it: the organism's changes "produce a specific and orderly development," its form "the visible expression of an organized living system" with "growth correlations among different parts" (Directly stated) — exactly the directed, internally regulated order a waterfall lacks. The metaphor's elegance can therefore overstate the case, suggesting mere flux where the biology insists on regulated, patterned flux. Applied to sculpture it distorts in the opposite direction: Arp's object is not in flux at all but "frozen," so the waterfall lives only as allusion — and Burnham is careful that "a viewer is never left to doubt that a vitalist sculpture is a man-made product rather than the result of natural forces" (Directly stated). The image risks naturalizing what is, finally, "metaphor."
