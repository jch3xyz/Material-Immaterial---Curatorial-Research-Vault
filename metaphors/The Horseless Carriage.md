# The Horseless Carriage

## The Metaphor

"The new as the old, minus one familiar part." Confronted with the automobile — a genuinely new kind of thing — people reached for the nearest available category and called it a *horseless carriage*: a carriage, only without the horse. The name registers what has been removed but cannot register what is unprecedented about what remains. For Zuboff this is the recurring figure for why [[Surveillance Capitalism]] and [[Instrumentarian Power]] elude recognition and so elude contest: "When we encounter something unprecedented, we automatically interpret it through the lenses of familiar categories, thereby rendering invisible precisely that which is unprecedented. A classic example is the notion of the 'horseless carriage' to which people reverted when confronted with the unprecedented facts of the automobile." (`Directly stated`)

The metaphor is Zuboff's master figure for [[The Unprecedented]] — the claim that surveillance capitalism's "many triumphs" rest above all on the fact that "it is unprecedented" and that "the unprecedented is necessarily unrecognizable." (`Directly stated`)

## What It Compares

- **Target:** our cognitive encounter with surveillance capitalism / instrumentarian power — a "conspicuously twenty-first-century conjuring of power for which the past offers no adequate compass."
- **Source domain:** the encounter with the automobile, named the "horseless carriage" — the new thing grasped only as the old thing with one element subtracted.

The mapping: *carriage → existing economic and political categories (the market, the corporation, monopoly, privacy, totalitarianism); the absent horse → the one obvious novelty we fixate on; the engine and everything the automobile actually changes → the unprecedented logic we fail to see.* Zuboff stretches the same figure across the book: in Chapter 1 it explains why we cannot see surveillance capitalism at all; in Chapter 12 it explains why, even when we sense danger, we misread it — "We are back to the syndrome of the horseless carriage, where we attach our new sense of peril to old, familiar facts, unaware that the conclusions to which they lead us are necessarily incorrect." (`Directly stated`)

## What It Reveals

- **The unprecedented is structurally invisible, not merely overlooked.** The failure is built into perception itself: "existing lenses illuminate the familiar, thus obscuring the original by turning the unprecedented into an extension of the past." Naming-by-subtraction feels like understanding while guaranteeing misunderstanding. (`Directly stated`)
- **It diagnoses the misfire of resistance.** Even alarm is captured by old categories. Attaching "our new sense of peril to old, familiar facts" leads to conclusions that are "necessarily incorrect" — e.g. reading [[Instrumentarian Power]] through the lens of [[Totalitarianism]], when totalitarianism sought "the reconstruction of the human species" through "genocide and the 'engineering of the soul,'" whereas "Surveillance capitalists have no interest in murder or the reformation of our souls." Reaching for the familiar tyranny disarms us against the unfamiliar one. (`Directly stated`)
- **It explains "the normalization of the abnormal."** Folding the new into the old "contributes to the normalization of the abnormal, which makes fighting the unprecedented even more of an uphill climb" — the metaphor names a mechanism, not just an error. (`Directly stated`)
- **It licenses Zuboff's whole vocabulary.** Because old words mislead, "the work of naming a strange form of power unprecedented in the human experience must begin anew" — justifying her coinages (surveillance capitalism, instrumentarianism, behavioral surplus). (`Directly stated`)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It can overstate discontinuity.** A figure built to insist nothing in the past is an "adequate compass" risks discounting real continuities — surveillance, market enclosure, behaviorism, advertising — that *do* illuminate the present. The automobile, after all, *was* a carriage in important respects; the "horseless carriage" was partly right. The metaphor's rhetorical force pushes toward treating the new as wholly sui generis. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **It is asymmetric to Harari's horse.** The vault's other automobile-and-horse figure, [[Retiring the Horses]], runs the comparison the opposite way: there the human is the *horse* made obsolete by a superior technology. Zuboff's horse is what we wrongly keep looking for (the missing familiar element); Harari's horse is what *we ourselves become* (the discarded obsolete element). Read together, the two metaphors share an image but invert its moral — a productive resonance that the single phrase "horseless carriage" can obscure. (`Speculative connection`)
- **It places the difficulty in perception more than in power.** By framing the problem as one of categories and lenses, the metaphor can underweight the active work surveillance capitalists do to *keep* their operations unprecedented and unnameable — secrecy, complexity, and what Zuboff elsewhere calls dispossession by encroachment. Invisibility is partly manufactured, not only cognitive. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
