# Mind as Machine

## The Metaphor

The mind is a machine — and not just any machine, but whatever happens to be the most sophisticated apparatus of its age. O'Gieblyn traces a single figure across centuries of changing hardware: "To be human was to be a mill, a clock, an organ, a telegraph machine." Bergson "likened the mind to a radio that might be tuned into a cosmic channel"; La Mettrie called the brain "the mainspring of the whole machine." In each era the comparison borrows the period's apex technology. Today that technology is the digital computer, and the figure has hardened into its current canonical form: "The brain is often described today as the hardware that 'runs' the software of the mind. Cognitive systems are spoken of as algorithms: vision is an algorithm, and so are attention, language acquisition, and memory."

O'Gieblyn's definitive contribution is to show that this is not a stable analogy but one that *easily slides into literalism*. A metaphor, she notes, "can also die when it becomes so common that we forget it is a metaphor. It no longer functions as a figure of speech; its meaning is taken to be literal." This is precisely what happened to the computational theory of mind: terms once "couched in quotation marks when applied to machines — 'behavior,' 'memory,' 'thinking' — are now taken as straightforward descriptions of their functions." When the figure is taken literally in both directions, "as we increasingly come to speak of our minds as computers, computers are now granted the status of minds."

This note figures the mind/brain specifically. It is distinct from [[Technology as Metaphor]] (O'Gieblyn's *historicizing method* — the recognition that every age describes the mind through its dominant machine, and that "metaphors are two-way streets") and from [[The Computational Universe]] (the same computer figure scaled up to the *cosmos*, where "the computer metaphor, which has proven so durable in describing the human mind, can also serve as an analogy for the cosmos"). The mind-as-machine is the figure; Technology as Metaphor is the method that historicizes it.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the human mind and brain — perception, cognition, memory, attention, language, the experiencing self.
- **Source domain:** the dominant machine of the era — successively the clock, mill, loom, organ, telegraph, and radio, and now the digital computer (hardware/software, input/output device, algorithm).
- **The mapping (computer version):**
  - Brain → hardware / "the physical substrate — much like a computer's hard drive — where all the brute mechanical work happens"
  - Mind → software / "a pattern of information — an algorithm, or a set of instructions — that supervenes on the hardware"
  - Cognition → algorithm / "vision is an algorithm, and so are attention, language acquisition, and memory"
  - Thinking → information processing / "If thinking was just information processing, computers could be said to 'learn,' 'reason,' and 'understand'"
  - Perception → input/output / a "machine that receives information through the senses, processes this information through neuronal operations, and generates plans of action through motor system outputs"

The mapping has a specific genealogy O'Gieblyn names: Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts "came up with the computational theory of mind," arguing the mind "functioned, on the neural level, much like a Turing machine," while [[Claude Shannon]]'s information theory "redefined information so as to exclude the need for a conscious subject" — so that thought "could be accounted for in purely abstract, mathematical terms." (See [[Cybernetics]], [[Information]].) (`Directly stated`, Ch1.)

## What It Reveals

- **A scientific, third-person mind.** The computational metaphor "offered a way to conceive of the psyche — long banished from the laboratory — in more rigorous, scientific terms, as a mechanism that obeyed the laws of classical physics." It lets cognition be modeled, predicted, and built — McCulloch sought a metaphor "so general" that "every circuit built by God or man must exemplify it in some form." (`Directly stated`, Ch1.)
- **A way around the soul.** "While the computer metaphor was invented to get around the metaphysical notion of a soul and the long, inelegant history of mind-body dualism" — it promised, in Hilary Putnam's words, "an autonomous mental life" with "no mysteries, no ghostly agents, no élan vital." (`Directly stated`, Ch2.)
- **The historical pattern itself.** Because the figure recurs in each age's idiom, it reveals that our self-image is technological: "To discover truth, it is necessary to work within the metaphors of our own time, which are for the most part technological." McCulloch's contemporaries already knew "computing was [unlikely to be] the final word." (`Directly stated`, Ch1; see [[Technology as Metaphor]].)
- **A reflexive turn — the mind black-boxed.** O'Gieblyn's sharpest revelation is that the metaphor runs in reverse: once human consciousness is treated as opaque computation, our own minds become as inscrutable as the algorithm. "The human brain is also a black box," and "the logical endpoint of 'black boxing' human consciousness is that it now appears just as reasonable to trust the opaque logic of an algorithm as it is to trust our own minds." (`Directly stated`, Ch11.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **Qualia / the hard problem.** The metaphor cannot account for first-person experience — "the ability to see, to feel, to have the sensation of self-awareness." McCulloch's theory "was particularly vague when it came to the question of how computation gave way to the phenomenon of interior experience." A computer "can perform their most impressive functions without interiority," which is exactly why David Chalmers's "hard problem" — "Why should physical processing give rise to a rich inner life at all?" — survives the metaphor untouched. (`Directly stated`, Ch1.)
- **Meaning / semantics.** The figure inherits Shannon's deliberate excision of content: information became "purely mathematical — defined by patterns and probabilities," with "semantic aspects of communication" deemed "irrelevant to the engineering problem." The machine is "merely manipulating symbols, blindly following instructions without understanding the content." "Understanding," like meaning, "is an anthropocentric concept — one that information technologies were deliberately built to elide." (`Directly stated`, Ch1, Ch11; see [[Information]], [[Pattern and Randomness]].)
- **Embodiment.** As cybernetics generalized, "context began to seem like a hindrance," and (citing [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*) "information came to take precedence over materiality," seeming "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates." The metaphor presumes the mind is "multiply realizable," instantiable in "any medium, biological or mechanical" — erasing the flesh. Searle: "a computer simulation of consciousness isn't thereby conscious." (`Directly stated`, Ch4, Ch2; see [[Embodiment]], [[The Materiality of Informatics]], [[The Body as Data]].)
- **It smuggles dualism back in.** The supposedly anti-metaphysical figure secretly restores Descartes: "The popular notion that the mind is software running on the brain's hardware is itself a form of dualism." It "reiterates the Cartesian assumption that the mind is something above and beyond the physical" — "the soul keeps slipping back in." (`Directly stated`, Ch2; see [[René Descartes]].)
- **It dissolves the self into a black box.** Taken to its terminus the figure turns the person into a determined mechanism. In O'Gieblyn's Calvinist register: "To concede that one's mind is controlled by God is to become a machine. It is to grant that the heart is also a black box, full of hidden desires and shadowy motivations whose true causes remain hidden from the conscious mind." The mind-as-machine thus undermines [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the autonomous liberal subject]] it was meant to preserve. (`Directly stated`, Ch12; `Interpretive synthesis` for the link to liberal humanism.)
- **It does not solve the old problems — it relocates them.** As Seymour Papert noted, the analogy only shows that the philosophers' and theologians' problems "come up in equivalent form in the new context." "The metaphor has not solved our most pressing existential problems; it has merely transferred them to a new substrate." (`Directly stated`, Ch2.)
