# We Wear All Mankind as Our Skin

## The Metaphor

"In the electric age we wear all mankind as our skin." The figure closes Chapter 4 and names the terminal point of McLuhan's [[Media as Extensions of Man|extension thesis]]: when electric technology externalizes the central nervous system itself, the prior extensions of single organs (wheel = foot, print = eye) give way to a single extension of the whole sensorium. At that scale, extension stops adding a tool to the body and instead makes the entire human collective into the body's nearest, most intimate layer — its skin. `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the condition of total electric interconnection — every other human being now continuously present to the self through the externalized nervous system. McLuhan's name for this elsewhere is [[The Global Village]].
- **Source domain:** *skin* — the body's outermost organ, the membrane of contact, the limit at which self ends and world begins; also, in the verb "wear," a garment one puts on.

The mapping: as skin is the body's boundary and its primary surface of contact, so under electric media all of mankind becomes the surface at which the self touches the world. The collective is not *out there* as environment but *worn* — as close as one's own skin, no longer separable from the self. This inverts the chapter's earlier extensions, which pushed functions *outward* and *away* by "autoamputation"; here the externalized nervous system pulls the whole species *in*, against the skin. `Strongly implied`

## What It Reveals

- **Extension reaches its limit as collective integration.** The chapter builds from single-organ extensions ("the wheel as an extension of the toot [foot]") to "a live model of the central nervous system itself." Once the *coordinating* organ is externalized, what is extended is no longer a part but the whole — and the whole reaches everyone at once. "We wear all mankind as our skin" is the apex of that escalation. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **Intimacy at planetary scale.** Skin connotes closeness, contact, the erotic and the tactile. The figure insists that electric interconnection is not abstract or remote but immediate and bodily — mankind is felt, not merely known. This grounds the chapter's closing turn to "total social involvement instead of the bourgeois spirit of individual separateness or points of view." `Directly stated`
- **The self is reconstituted as relational.** Existentialism's "philosophy of structures, rather than categories" is offered as the philosophy fit for this condition: the individuated viewpoint dissolves into a field of involvement. The skin-of-mankind figure makes that dissolution sensory. `Strongly implied`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It romanticizes contact as harmony.** "Skin" suggests warmth, belonging, organic wholeness — but the same chapter frames the externalized nervous system as "a development that suggests a desperate and suicidal autoamputation" and warns that "we have to numb our central nervous system when it is extended and exposed, or we will die." The garment of mankind is also a wound; the metaphor's tactile intimacy can mask the [[Narcissus as Narcosis|numbness]] that protects the overexposed nervous system. `Directly stated`
- **It elides who is integrated and on whose terms.** "All mankind" is grammatically total and politically empty: it names no asymmetry of power, no center and periphery, in the worn collective. The figure presents global integration as a uniform membrane rather than a structured, unequal field.
- **It naturalizes a technological condition as a body.** By rendering electric infrastructure as *skin* — given, organic, one's own — the metaphor makes a contingent media regime feel like anatomy, occluding that the "nervous system" in question is owned, built, and (in McLuhan's own later phrasing) leased. See [[Leasing the Central Nervous System]]. `Speculative connection`
