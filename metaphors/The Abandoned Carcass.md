# The Abandoned Carcass

## The Metaphor

"You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass." Zuboff's visceral correction of the popular slogan *if it's free, you are the product*. Under surveillance capitalism the person is not the thing sold but the slaughtered animal from which the saleable thing is taken: like an elephant poached for ivory, you are poached for [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral surplus]] and your remains — your meaning, your body, your interior life — are left behind as so much carrion. The product is not you; it is the surplus *ripped from* you.

## What It Compares

|---|---|
| The elephant, "most majestic of all mammals" | The human person, the experiencing self |
| Ivory — the valuable part poached and traded | [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the extractable raw material |
| The poacher / the slaughter for ivory | [[Big Other]], which "poaches our behavior for surplus" |
| The carcass left to rot | The body, brain, and "beating heart" with all the *meaning* lodged in them — discarded |

| Ivory refined into a marketable good elsewhere | The "product" (prediction products) derived from the surplus, sold in markets you never enter |

The figure literalizes the [[The Extraction Imperative|extraction imperative]]: value is *carved out of* the living being and carried off, and the being itself is neither customer nor product but residue.

## What It Reveals

- **The cliché understates the violence.** "You are the product" implies you are at least *something* in the transaction — a finished, traded good. Zuboff's correction insists the truth is more brutal: you are the *discarded remainder* of an extraction, and the actual product is made from what was torn out of you. (`Directly stated` — "Forget the cliché... You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass.")
- **It names the seat of [[Radical Indifference]].** A poacher does not hate the elephant; the elephant's inner life is simply irrelevant to the ivory. So too Big Other "does not care what we think, feel, or do" so long as it can "render, datafy, and instrumentalize" the surplus. The carcass image makes indifference *felt* rather than merely defined. (`Strongly implied` — the metaphor sits inside the chapter's definition of radical indifference and "observation without witness.")
- **It separates surplus from the self.** The surplus is *ripped from* your life; the meaning stays "lodged in our bodies, our brains, and our beating hearts." The metaphor draws the line between the datafied behavioral exhaust that is harvested and the inner experience that is left, untouched and unvalued. This is the same cut named by [[Instrumentarian Power|instrumentarianism]] as the severing of "our insides from our outsides." (`Strongly implied`.)
- **It re-grounds [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|behavioral modification]] in dispossession.** The carcass is what remains after the means of behavioral modification has taken its harvest — power identified no longer with ownership of the means of production but with "ownership of the means of behavioral modification that is Big Other." (`Interpretive synthesis` — joining the carcass image to the chapter's closing redefinition of power.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **A carcass is fully dead and inert; the surveilled human is not.** The metaphor's pathos depends on total finality, but surveillance capitalism needs the "animal" *alive and behaving* in order to keep generating surplus — it farms more than it poaches. Read too literally, "carcass" obscures the cyclical, ongoing nature of extraction (the [[The Behavioral Value Reinvestment Cycle|reinvestment cycle]], [[Rendition|rendition]] from ever-new depths). (`Speculative connection`.)
- **It frames the person as wholly passive victim.** The elephant cannot consent or resist; framing the user as a carcass can erase the (limited, manufactured, but real) participation, "free" services accepted, and self-exposure that the surveillance apparatus depends on. The metaphor's moral clarity comes at the cost of agency. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
- **Poaching is illegal and universally condemned; surveillance extraction is normalized, even celebrated.** The ivory analogy imports a settled moral verdict ("monstrous slaughter") that surveillance capitalism has precisely *not* incurred in public consciousness — the very [[Surveillance Exceptionalism|exceptionalism]] Zuboff diagnoses. The metaphor is rhetorically borrowing a condemnation the phenomenon has not yet earned. (`Strongly implied` — Zuboff deploys it as polemic for exactly this reason.)
