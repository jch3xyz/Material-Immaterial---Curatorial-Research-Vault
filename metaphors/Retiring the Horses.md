# Retiring the Horses

## The Metaphor

Humanity as the horse rendered redundant by the car. When a superior technology displaces an older one, the obsolete instrument is not improved — it is taken out of service. Harari turns this on Homo sapiens itself: "When the car replaced the horse-drawn carriage, we didn't upgrade the horses – we retired them. Perhaps it is time to do the same with Homo sapiens." (`Directly stated`)

This is the sharpest figure in *Homo Deus* for the dethroning of the human. Where humanism made the human the source of all meaning, [[Dataism]] reduces the human to one more data-processing system — and one already being outperformed.

## What It Compares

| Source domain (the horse) | Target (Homo sapiens) |
|---|---|
| Horse-drawn carriage | The human as the most efficient data-processing algorithm "for the last 70,000 years or so" |
| The car (superior technology) | Electronic algorithms / the [[The Internet-of-All-Things]] |
| We did not *upgrade* the horse | Techno-humanist attempts to "upgrade the human data-processing system" will not be enough |
| We *retired* the horse | Homo sapiens is released from function — superseded, not enhanced |

The carriage-to-car transition is not a transfer of work to a faster horse; it is the elimination of the horse from the productive system altogether. Harari maps this exactly onto the human: the issue is not that humans get better tools, but that humans themselves become the tool that gets replaced. The horse becomes the [[The Useless Class]] writ as species. (`Strongly implied` — Harari makes the functional logic explicit; the species-scale generalization is his framing in the same and adjacent passages.)

## What It Reveals

- **Obsolescence is structural, not moral.** The horse is not retired because it is bad or unloved, but because it no longer serves a function the system needs. Harari's surrounding argument is strictly functional: "if we develop an algorithm that fulfils the same function better, human experiences will lose their value." The metaphor strips away sentiment and exposes the cold logic of [[Dataism]]. (`Directly stated`)
- **The end-state of treating organisms as algorithms.** If "Homo sapiens is an obsolete algorithm," then retirement is not a catastrophe but a routine technological upgrade — the same move humans have always made with their tools. The metaphor makes species-level redundancy *thinkable* by analogizing it to something mundane and already accomplished. (`Directly stated`)
- **A species-scale reversal of agency.** It crystallizes Harari's claim that "Dataism thereby threatens to do to Homo sapiens what Homo sapiens has done to all other animals" — once humans "lose their functional importance to the network," they "join the mammoths and the Chinese river dolphins in oblivion." The horse is one creature among the herd of the displaced. (`Directly stated`)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It naturalizes a contestable dogma.** The figure works only if one already accepts that humans *are* algorithms whose sole value is data-processing function. Harari himself flags this as "current scientific dogma" rather than settled truth, and devotes "A Ripple in the Data Flow" to noting "we have no idea how or why data flows could produce consciousness." The crisp horse/car analogy smuggles the disputed premise in as common sense. (`Strongly implied`)
- **It erases consciousness and the subject.** A horse is property with no claim on its own continuation; "retiring" it raises no ethical remainder. Mapping this onto humans quietly brackets exactly what the chapter elsewhere admits is unresolved — subjective experience, the question of "what, if anything, would be lost by replacing conscious intelligence with superior non-conscious algorithms." The metaphor's tidiness depends on that erasure. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **It frames passively what would be a decision.** "We retired them" presents obsolescence as something that simply happens, like weather. Who is the "we" that retires Homo sapiens, and on whose authority? The carriage analogy disguises a political and economic project (see [[The Useless Class]], [[Upgrading Inequality]]) as a neutral technological succession. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
