# Computers Arose from the Mud and Code Fell from the Sky

## The Metaphor

George Dyson's two-creation-myths figure, which O'Gieblyn adopts as the organizing image of Chapter 6: "There are two types of creation myths," Dyson writes, "those where life arises out of the mud, and those where life falls from the sky." Modern computing, he argues, draws on both at once — "Computers arose from the mud, and code fell from the sky." O'Gieblyn reads the figure as naming the *dualism* at the heart of [[Emergence]]: matter (the mud — hardware, material parts) waiting to be inhabited by an abstract informational pattern (the sky — code, "that divine breath"). It is the golem rewritten in silicon. "Just as the golems were sculpted out of mud and animated with a magical incantation, so the hope persists that robots built from material parts will become inhabited by that divine breath—the abstract mathematical patterns that make up so much of our world and have the power to make systems come to life." (`Directly stated`.)

The figure stages, in mythic shorthand, the same structure O'Gieblyn anatomizes in [[Mind as Machine]]: a material substrate plus a substrate-independent pattern that "supervenes" on it. Here the pattern is given its theological name — an *informational [[Imago Dei|imago dei]]*. The kabbalistic frame O'Gieblyn supplies in the same chapter makes the lineage explicit: in the Sefer Yetzirah "the totality of the universe is undergirded by mystical combinations of the letters of the Hebrew alphabet, a kind of cosmic code that preceded matter and is inherent in every worldly thing. The very fabric of reality is informational." The "code from the sky" is the modern, secularized descendant of that cosmic code (see [[The Computational Universe]]). (`Directly stated`; `Interpretive synthesis` for the kabbalah→informationalism continuity, which O'Gieblyn herself draws.)

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the project of summoning conscious, emergent intelligence from machines — the hope that a system "built from material parts" will "come to life" and develop an interior.
- **Source domain:** the fused creation myth — life *from the mud* (matter shaped by hand) and life *from the sky* (an animating word that descends into it), with the golem as the figure that joins them: "androids out of clay" brought to life by "magical incantations… specifically identified as mathematical permutations."
- **The mapping:**
  - The mud / clay → the robot's hardware, "material parts," the physical machine
  - The divine breath / incantation → "the abstract mathematical patterns," the code, the informational pattern that animates (see [[Information]], [[Pattern and Randomness]])
  - The golem-maker (the Kabbalist, made "in the image of God") → the AI engineer; "we computer scientists are really the Kabbalists of today. We animate these inanimate machines by getting strings of symbols just right"
  - Animation by God's name in the golem's mouth → [[Emergence]]: consciousness presumed to descend into, or arise from, a sufficiently complex material system

The mapping is not O'Gieblyn's invention but the self-understanding of the field she reports: the computer scientist Mitchell P. Marcus, she notes, "sees the informational all around me, and I see the informational as very separate from the physical" — the sky cleanly partitioned from the mud. (`Directly stated`.)

## What It Reveals

- **The hidden dualism inside emergence.** The figure exposes that emergentism, for all its naturalist surface, often runs on a two-substance picture — inert matter and an abstract pattern that inhabits it. The "hope" O'Gieblyn names is precisely the hope that material parts "will become inhabited by that divine breath." This is the *informational imago dei*: the conviction that mind is a pattern that can descend into any sufficiently arranged matter. (`Directly stated` for the dualism; `Strongly implied` for "informational imago dei" as the name of the hope.)
- **The theological genealogy of computing.** By tracing the figure to golem lore and the practical Kabbalah, O'Gieblyn shows that AI "was both a form of worship and 'a repetition of God's act of creating us.'" The drive to "create life from ordinary matter—to make our own stones come to life" is the old prohibited magic in technical dress, which is why even rationalists like Marvin Minsky carry the golem-rhyme of their bar mitzvah into "the cradle of AI." (`Directly stated`.)
- **Why the [[Mind as Machine|computational metaphor]] keeps its sacred residue.** The figure makes legible why machine learning can feel "fundamentally religious": the code-from-the-sky is functionally a soul descending into a body. The "divine breath" is the pneuma renamed as pattern. (`Strongly implied`; cross-link [[Mind as Machine]].)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It conceals that the machines were built to exclude mind.** O'Gieblyn's central objection: the hope of consciousness emerging is "a form of wishful thinking, if only because digital technologies were built on the assumption that consciousness played no role in the process of intelligence." You cannot expect the divine breath to descend into a vessel designed from the outset to need no breath. "It is difficult to believe that technologies designed specifically to elide the notion of the conscious subject could possibly come to develop an interior life." The "sky" half of the myth promises an animating interiority the engineering deliberately omitted. (`Directly stated`.)
- **The "sky" is actually reductive materialism in disguise.** Scratch the mystical surface, O'Gieblyn argues, "and it becomes clear that emergentism is often not so different from the most reductive forms of materialism." The pattern that supposedly *descends* turns out, at bottom, to be "agents that 'cannot think at all'": Minsky's mind is "a sort of tangled-up bureaucracy" with "no place… for what we typically think of as interior experience, or the self." The metaphor's heavenly half evaporates into pure mud. As Jessica Riskin puts it, emergent theories "end up dismissing the very thing they are supposed to explain." This is the hard problem the figure smuggles past (see [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]], [[Mind as Machine]]). (`Directly stated`.)
- **It hides embodiment and the materiality of the substrate.** By splitting "the informational" cleanly from "the physical," the figure reproduces the disembodiment O'Gieblyn (following [[N. Katherine Hayles]]) critiques throughout the book: pattern treated as freely transferable "divine breath," matter treated as mere inert clay. It cannot register that the code is only ever instantiated *in* a medium — the counter-claim of [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — nor that interiority might be inseparable from a living body (see [[Embodiment]]). (`Interpretive synthesis`; the mud/sky split is O'Gieblyn's, the embodiment critique is the book's through-line.)
- **It dissolves meaning into symbol-manipulation.** Marcus's creed — "Creating and animating by the manipulation of symbols, which is what computer scientists both do and study" — inherits Shannon's content-free [[Information|information]]: the "incantation" works by "getting strings of symbols just right," with no understanding required. The "code from the sky" carries no semantics, only [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]]. The figure makes a soul out of syntax. (`Directly stated` for the symbol-manipulation creed; `Interpretive synthesis` for the meaning critique.)
