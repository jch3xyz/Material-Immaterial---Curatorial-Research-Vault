# The Dragon's Teeth (Cadmus)

## The Metaphor

The letters of the phonetic alphabet are the dragon's teeth that Cadmus sowed, springing up as armed men. McLuhan takes the Greek myth — in which Cadmus, "reputedly the king who introduced the phonetic letters into Greece, sowed the dragon's teeth, and they sprang up arm[ed] men" — and reads it as a compressed account of what literacy does. The teeth that become soldiers are letters that become empires, armies, and military bureaucracies. Through Elias Canetti's *Crowds and Power*, McLuhan grounds the figure further: the teeth are "an obvious agent of power in man," so it is "natural and fitting" that the power of letters "as agents of aggressive order and precision should be expressed as extensions of the dragon's teeth."

The myth, "[l]ike any other myth," compresses a long historical transformation into a single image. McLuhan reads it as recording the moment the easy alphabet and "the light, cheap, transportable papyrus together effected the transfer of power from the priestly to the military class."

## What It Reveals

- **The alphabet is a weapon, not a neutral notation.** The metaphor makes visible that "[t]he alphabet meant power and authority and control of military structures at a distance" — its martial character is intrinsic, not incidental. `Directly stated`
- **The shared root of Western industrial and military uniformity.** Because the alphabet breaks "every kind of experience into uniform units," McLuhan can claim "our Western industrial programs have quite involuntarily been so militant, and our military programs have been so industrial. Both are shaped by the alphabet." The teeth-into-soldiers image figures this fusion of the factory and the army in a single technology. `Directly stated`
- **Letters resemble teeth in form, not only in effect.** "Teeth are emphatically visual in their lineal order. Letters are not only like teeth visually, but their power to put teeth into the business of empire-building is manifest in our Western history." The metaphor binds the alphabet's *visual lineality* to its *aggressive precision* — the same trait does both jobs. `Directly stated`
- **Detribalization as a martial event.** The armed men sprung from the teeth are the "civilized" individuals the alphabet produces — "separate individuals equal before a written code of law" — released "from the tribal web." The metaphor connects literate individuation to military mobility: "the mobility of armed groups and of ambitious individuals" in a "homogeneous and malleable milieu." This anchors `[[Detribalization and Retribalization]]`. `Strongly implied`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It foregrounds aggression and order while McLuhan elsewhere insists "we are not here concerned with the question of values."** The teeth-as-weapon image carries an evaluative charge — order, precision, militancy as conquest — that sits in tension with his stated descriptive aim of mapping "the configurations of societies." `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It naturalizes a contingent technology.** By rooting the alphabet's power in something as bodily and "natural and fitting" as teeth (an "agent of power in man, and especially in many animals"), the metaphor risks presenting the phonetic alphabet's effects as organic and inevitable, when McLuhan's own argument stresses that "[o]nly the phonetic alphabet makes such a sharp division in experience" — it is "a unique technology," historically singular, not a natural endowment. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It privileges the visual-martial line and occludes what is sacrificed.** The image of armed precision says nothing of the loss McLuhan also names: "[t]he phonetically written word sacrifices worlds of meaning and perception that were secured by forms like the hieroglyph and the Chinese ideogram," and "diminishes the role of the other senses." The teeth metaphor lights up power and hides the "inclusive gestalt" the alphabet destroys. `Strongly implied`
