# The Scent of Time (McLuhan)

## The Metaphor

**Time can be smelled.** Against the West's "feeling for time as duration" — time hacked by mechanical clocks into "uniform seconds, minutes, and hours on an assembly-line pattern" — McLuhan sets a wholly different way of telling time: for "thousands of years" the Chinese and Japanese "measured time by graduations of incense," so that "the hours and days, but the seasons and zodiacal signs were simultaneously indicated by a succession of carefully ordered scents." Time here is not seen or counted but inhaled. It is the chapter's titular image — *the scent of time* — and McLuhan grounds it in a claim about the senses: smell is "the most iconic" sense "in that it involves the entire human sensorium more fully than any other sense."

This note names the *McLuhan* metaphor specifically — the incense-clock figure from *Understanding Media* (1964). It is qualified `(McLuhan)` to avoid colliding with Han's vault metaphor [[Fragrance of Time]] (drawn from *The Transparency Society* / *The Scent of Time*), which it strongly resembles and very probably precedes as a source. See *Cross-reference to Han* below.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** temporality itself — specifically the contrast between *integral, involving, communal* time (the "most integral and involving time sense imaginable," which McLuhan locates in "the Chinese and Japanese cultures") and *abstract, visual, uniform* clock-time (time "separated from the rhythms of human experience").
- **Source domain:** *incense* — a clock made of scent, in which the passage of hours, days, and seasons is registered as "a succession of carefully ordered scents." Scent is sequential without being segmented; it is sensed by the whole body rather than read off a dial; it diffuses and lingers rather than ticking into discrete units. Where the mechanical clock is "a machine that produces uniform seconds" on "an assembly-line pattern," the incense clock measures time as a continuous olfactory unfolding.

The mapping reads: communal/integral time : clock-time :: a burning thread of ordered scent : a row of uniform, visualized, fragmented units. The scent-clock belongs to what McLuhan calls the *audile-tactile* world; the mechanical clock belongs to the *visual* world of phonetic literacy and print.

## What It Reveals

- **Smell is the "most iconic" sense — it engages the whole sensorium.** McLuhan's case for the metaphor is sensory, not merely picturesque: "The sense of smell is not only the most subtle and delicate of the human senses; it is, also, the most iconic in that it involves the entire human sensorium more fully than any other sense." A scent-clock therefore does not isolate a single sense (as the *visual* dial does) but keeps perception unified. This connects the metaphor directly to McLuhan's theory of [[Sense Ratios]]: a medium that tells time by smell preserves a very different sensory balance than one that tells time by sight. `Directly stated`
- **Time-sense is technologically conditioned, not natural.** The metaphor's polemical point is that "our Western feeling for time as duration" comes "from our division of time into uniform, visualizable units" — it is "unknown among non literate cultures." The incense clock is offered as the counter-case proving the rule: a different time-medium produces a different lived time. `Directly stated`
- **Scent-measured time makes for cohesive, change-resistant cultures.** McLuhan ties the sensory form to social form: "Societies that measured time by scents would tend to be so cohesive and so profoundly unified as to resist every kind of change." The metaphor thus carries a thesis about the relation between perceptual medium and social integration. `Directly stated` (NB: the chapter text reads "Societies that measured time scents" — apparently a typographic slip in the raw EPUB for "by scents"; the verbatim quote in *Sources* preserves the source exactly.)
- **Smell is "the root of memory and the unifying basis of individuality."** McLuhan folds in the Penfield brain-surgery experiments — memories "dominated and unified by unique scents and odors" — to argue that olfaction binds experience into a unity. The scent of time is, by the same logic, a time bound into a remembered, lived whole rather than scattered into countable instants. `Directly stated`
- **Literate societies suppress odor — and so suppress this kind of time.** "It is not surprising, therefore, that highly literate societies take steps to reduce or eliminate odors from the environment. B.O. ... is a bad word in literate societies. It is far too involving for our habits of detachment and specialist attention." The metaphor doubles as a diagnosis: the deodorized visual culture cannot inhabit scent-time. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It romanticizes "non literate" and Asian cultures as a sensory foil.** The incense clock functions in McLuhan's argument as an emblem of the "integral and involving," set against Western fragmentation. This flattens vast, internally literate traditions (China and Japan had highly developed writing and print long before "the coming of the missionaries in the seventeenth century") into a single "audile-tactile," change-resistant type. The metaphor's charm depends on an Orientalized and primitivist contrast.
- **Sensory determinism.** The figure leans on McLuhan's strong claim that a time-*medium* (scent vs. dial) determines a time-*sense* and even a society's capacity for change. As an image it is suggestive; as causation it overreaches, treating perceptual form as the master variable and downplaying economy, politics, and belief.
- **It treats odorlessness as loss without weighing the gains.** Like Han's [[Fragrance of Time]], the metaphor codes scented time as rich and deodorized time as impoverished. McLuhan is more ambivalent than Han (he grants that the visual clock "leaves some gaps and some islands free from the unremitting acoustic pressure"), but the scent-of-time image still trades on a nostalgia for cohesive, "profoundly unified" cultures whose very virtue — resisting "every kind of change" — could equally be read as a virtue's opposite.
- **It privileges the least shareable sense.** Olfaction is notoriously hard to record, transmit, or verify — which makes it a vivid figure for an "involving," non-detached, non-specialist mode, but also one that resists the public scrutiny McLuhan's own argument would invite. The metaphor performs the very immediacy it praises.

## Cross-reference to Han

This chapter is almost certainly the *source* McLuhan text standing behind Han's vault metaphor [[Fragrance of Time]] and the title of Han's book *The Scent of Time*. Both authors build a critique of abstract, fragmented, accelerated modern time around the same olfactory contrast — scented, integral, lived time vs. odorless, uniform, atomized time — and both treat smell as the sense of memory, depth, and unity. The proximity is striking enough to record as a cross-author bridge.

The historical-influence claim — that Han *drew* this image from McLuhan — is `Interpretive synthesis`: it is not asserted by either source in the material ingested here, and should be upgraded to `Directly stated` only if Han is found to cite McLuhan explicitly. What *can* be stated directly is the conceptual parallel: independent or not, the two metaphors map the same target onto the same source domain. See [[Fragrance of Time]] for Han's development of the figure.
