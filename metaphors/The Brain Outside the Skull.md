# The Brain Outside the Skull

## The Metaphor

The human being under electric conditions is imagined as **an organism that wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide**. The interior of the body — the central nervous system and the brain, the seats of sensation and cognition — is figured as having been turned inside out and hung on the world as technology. What was anatomically enclosed (brain in skull, nerves under skin) is now worn externally, the way one wears a garment.

## What It Compares

| Target (the human under electric technology) | Source domain (the externalized organism) |
|---|---|
| The central nervous system extended by electric media | Nerves worn "outside its hide" — exposed, no longer beneath the skin |
| The brain / consciousness mediated by electromagnetic networks | A brain "outside its skull" — carried on the outside of the body |
| The body placed inside its own technological extensions | An organism whose interior has become its exterior |

The figure completes a move McLuhan builds across the chapter: "By putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems, by means of electric media" (`Directly stated`). The metaphor inverts the ordinary topology of the body — what was inside is now outside, and the body itself is placed inside what it has externalized.

## What It Reveals

- It makes vivid the **exteriorization of the interior**: under electromagnetic technology the most intimate, enclosed biological systems (brain, nerves) become public, distributed, technological. This is a strong material/immaterial figure — flesh translated into circuitry and information, the body translated into "the form of information" (`Strongly implied`; the chapter states "In this electric age we see ourselves being translated more and more into the form of information").
- It dramatizes the **posthuman condition** McLuhan diagnoses: the human is no longer a bounded organism but a creature whose sensory and cognitive apparatus has been turned outward and networked, "moving toward the technological extension of consciousness" (`Directly stated`).
- It clarifies the **stakes of docility**: because the brain and nerves are now worn externally as technology, McLuhan ties the image to "utter human docility and quiescence of meditation," casting the externalized organism as one that must serve its electric technology "with the same servo-mechanistic fidelity" it gave earlier tools (`Directly stated`).

## What It Hides or Distorts

- The image is **anatomically literalizing in a way the situation is not**: nothing is actually removed from the skull. The metaphor's force ("brain outside its skull") risks suggesting a clean externalization, when McLuhan's own argument is about *extension* and translation, not surgical removal — the body is put *inside* its extensions, not emptied of its organs.
- It can **understate agency and resistance**. The figure pairs the externalized organism with "docility" and "servo-mechanistic fidelity," foregrounding submission; it occludes the possibility of the externalized nervous system being contested, programmed otherwise, or refused. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- It **flattens the difference between sensation and information**. By making the brain and nerves wearable technology, the metaphor invites the equation of nervous experience with retrievable, storable data ("possible to store and to translate everything"), which can obscure what is lost when "immediate sense experience" is translated into "vocal symbols" and then into information systems. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
