# The Schmoo

## The Metaphor

**Automation as the world of the Schmoo.** McLuhan borrows Al Capp's cartoon creature — the Schmoo, which on being looked at and longed for would "ecstatically / transform itself into the object of desire" — as the figure for what electric automation does to production. Where the mechanical age had to set up, tool, and run a line to stamp out identical goods in quantity, the automated, information-saturated machine can be reprogrammed at will to yield whatever is named: "We have now only to name and program a process or a product in order for it to be accomplished." Hence McLuhan's verdict: "Automation brings us into the world of the Schmoo. The custom-built supplants the mass-produced." `Directly stated`

| Information / a program directing matter | Desire alone summoning the thing |

The mapping turns on the collapse of effort. In the Schmoo, no labor stands between wanting a thing and having it — the longing *is* the production. McLuhan reads automation the same way: because "energy and information can be applied in a great variety of ways" and "as information levels rise, almost any sort of material can be adapted to any sort of use," the machine no longer locks one apparatus to one product. The example he gives is literal: a single tailpipe machine on which "it is possible to make eighty different kinds of tailpipe in succession, as rapidly, as easily, and as cheaply as it is to make eighty of the same kind." `Directly stated`

## What It Reveals

- **The mechanical-to-electric reversal of production.** The figure makes vivid the core claim of the chapter's [[Mechanical vs Electric]] arc: standardized mass production was a property of the *mechanical* age of fragmentation, where "the power and the work done were always in direct relation," and where a machine was tooled for one line. Electric automation severs that relation — power and information become "indifferently and quickly" applicable to many tasks — so the custom-built can supplant the mass-produced. The Schmoo names the endpoint of that reversal. `Directly stated`
- **Production as information, not as matter handled.** The Schmoo turns desire directly into the thing, with no intervening manufacture. McLuhan's automation is similarly an affair of pattern, not stuff: "Automation is information"; "Wealth and work become information factors." What the program specifies, the machine delivers — fabrication is reduced to naming. This grounds the metaphor in [[Information]] and the [[Feedback Loops]] (the "information loop or circuit") that make the automatic machine reprogrammable. `Directly stated`
- **Abundance and the end of toil.** The Schmoo is a creature of effortless plenty. McLuhan frames automation in the same Edenic register just before invoking it — "As unfallen Adam in the Garden of Eden was appointed the task of the contemplation and naming of creatures, so with automation" — so the figure carries the promise that "the very toil of man now becomes a kind of enlightenment," that fabrication will be as easy as wishing. This is the optimistic face of [[Automation as Learning a Living]]. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It erases labor, cost, and the program's author.** In Al Capp's strip the Schmoo gives without anyone working, paying, or designing — desire alone suffices. The figure thereby occludes the engineering, the programming, the capital, and the displaced "present work force" that automation actually involves; McLuhan himself names "the specter of joblessness and propertylessness in the electric age" elsewhere in the same chapter, which the Schmoo's effortless bounty papers over. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It overstates desire's sovereignty over supply.** The Schmoo answers any wish instantly; real automated production still depends on materials, programs written in advance, and "complete knowledge of ultimate effects" ("electric speed-up requires complete knowledge of ultimate effects"). The metaphor's magic — want it and it appears — flattens the determinations and constraints that remain. `Strongly implied`
- **Its wish-fulfillment register naturalizes the regime as benign abundance.** A Schmoo is lovable, generous, and uncoerced; framing automation as "the world of the Schmoo" presents a sweeping reorganization of work, wealth, and society as a gift rather than a power formation. The vault's surveillance and political-economy thread reads the same information-driven production very differently — as capture and control ([[Digital Panopticon]]) rather than as the cartoon's free pork chops and caviar. `Interpretive synthesis`
