# The Web of Meaning

## The Metaphor

Meaning as a **web** — a fabric of shared stories spun "thread by thread" by a civilisation, in which the believers are caught "like flies." Harari's master image for [[Intersubjective Reality]]: the self-perpetuating tissue of laws, gods, forces, entities and places that "exist purely in their common imagination," and that — like a spider's web — is woven, reinforced through mutual belief, and eventually unravelled and re-spun over historical time. The animating sentence is: "Sapiens rule the world because only they can weave an intersubjective web of meaning." `Directly stated`

Keep this note distinct from [[Intersubjective Reality]]: that is the *ontological category* (Harari's third level of reality, between the objective and the subjective); **this is the historian's metaphor** for how that level is spun, tightened and torn across the ages. The two are companion notes, deliberately not collapsed.

## What It Compares

|---|---|---|
| Shared stories — gods, nations, money, crusades | → | Threads of silk |
| Each act of mutual belief / confirmation | → | One more thread spun, tightening the web |
| The believer (Harari's crusader "John") embedded in a civilisation's myths | → | The fly caught and held fast |
| A culture's collective myth-making | → | The spider (the weaver — "they can weave") |
| The rise and fall of worldviews over centuries | → | The web unravelling and "a new web spun in its place" |
| The historian's vantage | → | The observer watching "the spinning and unravelling of these webs" |

Harari layers a second, adjacent image onto the first — the **web of stories** ("Humans think they make history, but history actually revolves around the web of stories"). The spider's-web figure (spun, trapping like flies) and the textile/storytelling figure (weaving, threads) are the same metaphor seen from two angles: the web as snare and the web as fabric. `Directly stated` (both phrasings are Harari's); `Interpretive synthesis` (treating them as one metaphor).

## What It Reveals

- **Meaning is woven, not found.** The metaphor makes thinkable Harari's central claim that significance is manufactured collectively rather than discovered in the cosmos: "Meaning is created when many people weave together a common network of stories." Belief is not a private state but a thread in a shared textile. `Directly stated`
- **Self-reinforcing structure.** A web tightens as more threads cross. So with meaning: "Each round of mutual confirmation tightens the web of meaning further, until you have little choice but to believe what everyone else believes." The image captures *why* fictions feel inescapable from the inside — not because they are true, but because everyone around you holds the same thread. `Directly stated`
- **Captivity without coercion.** "Trapping John and his contemporaries like flies" reveals how a worldview can be total and invisible at once: the crusader cannot conceive that the stories are "just figments of the imagination," because the whole social fabric — parents, minstrels, priest, Pope, even the enemy Muslims — corroborates it. The web holds by ubiquity, not force. `Directly stated`
- **Historicity — webs come and go.** Unlike a static "worldview," a web can be unravelled. "Over decades and centuries the web of meaning unravels and a new web is spun in its place. To study history means to watch the spinning and unravelling of these webs." This is what licenses Harari's deflationary historical method: today's certainties will look, in hindsight, "like utter madness." This is the engine of the vault's [[Narrativity]] / [[Narration vs Addition]] conversation — meaning as a story that can be told and then forgotten. `Directly stated`
- **Why Sapiens rule.** The metaphor grounds Harari's answer to "the human spark": dominance comes not from soul, intelligence or toolmaking but from the unique capacity to *weave* — "only they can weave an intersubjective web of meaning." The web is the medium of flexible mass cooperation. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **The fly has no part in the weaving.** The snare image casts the believer as passive prey — caught, immobilised, deceived. Yet Harari elsewhere insists the believer is also a *weaver*: meaning is created when people "weave together" and "constantly reinforce each other's beliefs." The "like flies" figure occludes this agency, tilting the metaphor toward a story of dupes rather than co-authors. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It flattens the difference between benign and pathological fictions.** A web is a web; the metaphor offers no internal way to distinguish a life-giving order from a murderous one. Harari has to supply that criterion from *outside* the image — "Can it suffer?" — reminding us that "the cause of war is fictional, but the suffering is 100 per cent real." The web figure, taken alone, risks treating all meaning as equally arbitrary entrapment. `Interpretive synthesis` (the suffering criterion is `Directly stated` in *The Storytellers*).
- **It implies an outside.** A fly is trapped *in* a web that an observer sees *from without*; the metaphor positions the historian as someone who has escaped the silk. But if all meaning is intersubjective, the historian too writes from within some web — modern beliefs in "democracy and human rights might look equally incomprehensible to our descendants." The image smuggles in a view from nowhere it cannot actually deliver. `Strongly implied`
- **Spun "thread by thread" understates the material base.** The textile metaphor suggests meaning is spun out of nothing but belief, whereas Harari's own argument is that webs scale only on a *material* foundation — the Agricultural Revolution "provided the necessary material base for enlarging and strengthening the intersubjective networks." The pure-fabric image can hide the granaries, writing and money that let the web grow, a point that bears on [[Material vs Immaterial]]. `Strongly implied`
