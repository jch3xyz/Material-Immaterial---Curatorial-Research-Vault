# The Technological Trojan Horse

## The Metaphor

Big Other is the technological Trojan horse of surveillance capitalism's *coup de gens*. The ubiquitous, sensate, networked apparatus is the wheeled wooden horse that the people themselves draw inside the city walls — welcomed as a gift of connection, convenience, and "personalization" — while what it actually carries, concealed within its frame, is the overthrow of their own sovereignty.

Zuboff deploys the figure in a single, deliberate clause: surveillance capitalism is "not a coup d'état in the classic sense but rather a *coup de gens*: an overthrow of the people concealed as the technological Trojan horse that is Big Other" (`Directly stated`). The horse is the apparatus; the concealment is the gift's friendly face; the army inside is the coup itself.

## What It Reveals

The metaphor's whole work is to relocate the *moment of seizure*. A classical coup has a visible event — troops at the palace, tanks in the square. The Trojan horse has none: the breach happens because the besieged voluntarily admit the very thing that defeats them. This is exactly Zuboff's point about surveillance capitalism — there is no palace stormed, "not an overthrow of the state but rather an overthrow of the people's sovereignty." The horse figure makes thinkable a conquest with no battle, a tyranny "that feeds on people but is not of the people" (`Directly stated`).

It also names the *deception built into the form*. The horse is not merely a weapon; it is a weapon disguised as a gift, and its power depends on that disguise. So too Big Other: its instruments are "Big Other's reassuring messages and emoticons," the sensored shirt, the listening TV, "the book that reads you" — the apparatus must be wanted, drawn inside, in order to do its work. The metaphor thus binds together the apparatus's *material* welcome (devices in the home and on the body) and its *political* payload (the expropriation of self-governance), revealing them as one object seen from two sides. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

Finally, the horse is *hollow* — a shell whose meaning is what it hides. This rhymes with Zuboff's claim that Big Other, "like the ancient tyrant, exists out of mankind while paradoxically assuming human shape" (`Directly stated`): the human-shaped, human-friendly surface is precisely the carapace concealing an inhuman power.

## What It Hides or Distorts

A Trojan horse implies a *deceiving intelligence* — Odysseus, the Greeks — that built the trap and a discrete night of betrayal. Surveillance capitalism has no single Odysseus and no single night: it is, on Zuboff's own account elsewhere, "a logic not a technology," an order that "must expand toward totality" by economic imperative rather than by a plotter's design. The horse can suggest a conspiracy where Zuboff more often describes a *structural drive*, risking the very "horseless-carriage" error she warns against — fitting an unprecedented power to a familiar story. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

The figure also implies a clean *inside/outside*: a wall, a city, a population that could in principle have refused the gift. But the apparatus is now the infrastructure of ordinary life; there is no wall left to keep it out and, for most, no living "home" outside it (the dilemma of "home or exile in the digital future"). The horse misleads if it suggests the people had a free, informed choice at the gate — Zuboff insists "consent is coercion," that the gift was never legible as a weapon. (`Strongly implied`)

And a horse is *inert wood*, a one-time delivery vehicle emptied once the army is out. Big Other is the opposite: a sensate, *continuously operating* apparatus whose payload is never fully "delivered" but perpetually renewed — it renders, monitors, computes, and modifies without end. The metaphor captures the deception of entry but understates the permanence of occupation. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
