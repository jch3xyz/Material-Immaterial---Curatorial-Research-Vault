# Information Lost Its Body

## The Metaphor

"How information lost its body" is the governing figure of the first of the three stories Hayles tells in *How We Became Posthuman*. Information is personified as something that *had* a body and then *lost* it — that "came to be conceptualized as an entity separate from the material forms in which it is thought to be embedded." The metaphor's hinge is a single image of weightless mobility: once stripped of its body, information becomes "a kind of bodiless fluid that could flow between different substrates without loss of meaning or form."

The figure is not Hayles's own conviction but the cultural construction she sets out to historicize and contest. She narrates *how* information lost its body precisely in order to argue it never should have, and to recover "the flesh that continues to be erased." The whole metaphor is capped by her signature rejoinder: "Just because information has lost its body does not mean that humans and the world have lost theirs." This counter-clause is the load-bearing part of the figure — it keeps "lost its body" as a description of a *conceptual move*, not a fact about the world.

This note figures the dematerialization of *information itself*. It is distinct from [[The Body as Data]], which figures the inverse move — the *body* recoded as information ("data made flesh"). The two metaphors are the two faces of the same [[Material vs Immaterial|materiality/information separation]].

## What It Compares

- **Target:** information as redefined in cybernetics and information theory after World War II — a "probability function with no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning," "a pattern, not a presence" (see [[Information (Hayles)]]).
- **Source domain:** an embodied creature that can be parted from its body — and, in the paired counter-figure, a *fluid* (the "bodiless fluid") that moves between containers without spilling or changing.
- **The mapping:**
  - Body / flesh → material instantiation, the medium, the substrate
  - Losing the body → abstracting information from any particular material base
  - Bodiless fluid → information as pattern, "free to travel across time and space"
  - Flowing "between different substrates without loss" → the claim that information "can circulate unchanged among different material substrates" — carbon to silicon, brain to disk, here to "a remote location"
  - The dream of disembodied immortality → "if we can become the information we have constructed, we can achieve effective immortality"

The metaphor underwrites the era's defining belief, which Hayles dates to Wiener's 1948 dictum: "Information is information, not matter or energy. No materialism which does not admit this can survive at the present day." (`Directly stated` in Ch1 / Ch3; the "bodiless fluid" and "immaterial fluid" phrasings are `Directly stated` in Ch3 and Ch11 respectively.)

## What It Reveals

- **The historicity of a "natural" fact.** By personifying information as something that *lost* a body, Hayles makes visible that information *had* one — that its bodilessness is an achievement, not a given. The figure lets her ask "what had to be elided, suppressed, and forgotten to make information lose its body," reframing a technical definition as a contingent cultural [[Seriation|seriated]] construction rather than an inevitability.
- **The equation of humans and machines.** "When information loses its body, equating humans and computers is especially easy, for the materiality in which the thinking mind is instantiated appears incidental to its essential nature." The metaphor names the precise hinge by which [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal subject]] slides into the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] — and into the [[The Cyborg|cyborg]], whose prosthetic pathways presuppose "a conception of information as a (disembodied) entity that can flow between carbon-based organic components and silicon-based electronic components."
- **The dream of immortality and unconstrained flow.** "The great dream and promise of information is that it can be free from the material constraints that govern the mortal world." The figure exposes the desire driving Moravec's downloaded consciousness and Minsky's memories ported "intact and unchanged, to computer disks" — the fantasy that "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form."
- **The ideology of the [[The Computational Universe|computational universe]].** In Ch11 the metaphor lets Hayles diagnose Artificial Life's "strong claim" that codes are alive: it rests on "a conceptualization that sees information and materiality as distinct entities," allowing "information as a kind of immaterial fluid that circulates effortlessly around the globe while still retaining the solidity of a reified concept." The fluid figure thus reveals the hidden Platonism — "form is privileged over matter; form defines life, whereas the material basis merely instantiates life."

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It hides that information always requires a medium.** This is Hayles's central counter-claim: "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium." The metaphor of a body that can be shed makes thinkable a thing that, on Hayles's view, cannot exist — "conceiving of information as a thing separate from the medium instantiating it is a prior imaginary act." The figure's very intelligibility depends on forgetting [[The Materiality of Informatics|the materiality of informatics]]. (`Directly stated`.)
- **It slides from "humans" to "the world."** The metaphor's danger is its overreach — the leap from "information has lost its body" to "*we* can do away with the body." Hayles's rejoinder ("does not mean that humans and the world have lost theirs") marks exactly the equivocation the figure smuggles in: a claim about a concept is mistaken for a claim about lived [[Embodiment]]. (`Directly stated`, Ch11.)
- **It erases bodily difference and the lifeworld.** Because "loss of body" is presented as loss of an inessential accident, the metaphor licenses treating "embodiment in a biological substrate" as "an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life." What drops out is "the variegated leaves, fractal branchings, and particular bark textures" — the "world's multiplicity" and "the embodied processes constituting the lifeworld of human beings." (`Strongly implied`.)
- **The fluid image launders a hierarchy as neutrality.** A "bodiless fluid" sounds frictionless and value-free, but the figure installs a ranking — it "allows the construction of a hierarchy in which information is given the dominant position and materiality runs a distant second." Calling information a fluid hides the fact that this is, in Hayles's terms, the "Platonic backhand and forehand" at work, privileging "the abstract as the Real." (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
- **The agentless "loss" hides who did the losing.** "Lost its body" has no subject — as if information misplaced something by itself. Hayles's whole method is to restore the agents: the Macy participants, Shannon's quantification choices, the "historically specific negotiations" through which "the disembodiment of information was not inevitable." The passive, agentless figure conceals the contest [[Pattern and Randomness|over what information would mean]]. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
