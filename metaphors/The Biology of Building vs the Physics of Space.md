# The Biology of Building vs the Physics of Space

## The Metaphor

In "The Architecture of Cyberception" (1994), Roy Ascott diagnoses Western architecture through a single contrasting pair: it has a *physics of space* but no *biology of building*. "The problem with Western architecture is that it is too much concerned with surfaces and structures and too little concerned with living systems. There is no biology of building, simply the physics of space" (Directly stated). The figure casts the building not as a static object whose surfaces and volumes are to be composed (physics — bodies, forces, geometry, inert space), but as something that ought to be alive, grown, and metabolically embedded in networks (biology — organisms, systems, growth). It is the figure that carries the [[Object and System|object→system]] thesis into the built environment.

## What It Compares

| Target (architecture / the city) | Source domain | What the mapping asserts |
|---|---|---|
| Surfaces, façades, "Edificial images" | the *physics* of space — geometry, volume, inert matter | the building treated as an object: appearance, structure, "the edificial look is all" |
| Networks, interfaces, feedback, growth | the *biology* of building — living systems, organisms, metabolism | the building treated as a system: responsive, transactional, "planted and 'grown'" |
| Designing / composing form | engineering inert matter | imposing a fixed structure on dead space |
| Seeding, pollination, grafting, horticulture | cultivating living things | letting the built environment grow and transform |

Architecture's proper object is mapped from *physical body* to *living system*; the architect's proper act is mapped from *designing* to *gardening*. Ascott makes the horticultural pole explicit as a replacement metaphor: "It's a growbag culture that is needed, in which seeding replaces designing. Architectural practice should find its guiding metaphors in horticulture, rather than in warfare. Ultimately, we can perhaps talk about pollination and grafting" (Directly stated).

## What It Reveals

- **The city as a living system, not a collection of objects.** The metaphor lets Ascott re-see the city "not just a pretty façade" but "a zone of negotiation made up of a multitude of networks and systems" — the move that grounds [[The City as Zone of Transformation]] (Directly stated). What is needed are "designers of such spaces who can provide forms of access" working in "systems interfaces and network nodes" rather than "traditional architectural discourse."
- **It names the missing dimension as time and life.** "Physics of space" diagnoses architecture as spatial and static; "biology of building" supplies the missing axes of growth, change, and metabolism — "notions of unfolding, transformation, and coming into being." Buildings should "behave in ways consistent with their announced function," and "must be planted and 'grown'."
- **It is the object→system thesis applied to the built environment.** The figure carries [[Jack Burnham]]'s [[Object and System]] / [[Systems Esthetics]] logic out of sculpture and into architecture and urbanism: the building-as-surface-object gives way to the city-as-living-system (Interpretive synthesis). It is a strong [[Material vs Immaterial]] anchor for Ascott — the city of "dense and intractable materiality" is to be replaced by "the environmental fluidity of faster-than-light pathways, intelligent surfaces and structures, and transformable habitations."
- **It indicts the present as inert.** The contemporary city is "a battle zone in which this or that architectural genre or idiomatic impulse fights to survive... a matter of relative inertia," where "No one is interested in radical change or intimations of the future" — the horticultural pole is offered precisely against this "warfare" of styles (Directly stated).

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It risks naturalizing the technological.** Calling networked, sensor-laden, telematic infrastructure "biological" and "grown" lends an organic innocence to what is in fact engineered, owned, and surveillant. The metaphor's warmth ("seeding," "pollination," "fruitful reality") can occlude the [[Liberation vs Control (Telematics)|control]] dimension of pervasive networked space (Interpretive synthesis).
- **It overdraws the opposition.** Surfaces and structures are not opposed to living systems so much as their substrate; organisms have surfaces and structures. The clean binary (physics *vs.* biology) is rhetorical — Ascott himself elsewhere asks the architect "to fuse together material structures and cyberspace organisms into a new continuum," which is a synthesis, not a victory of biology over physics (Strongly implied).
- **It can read as anti-material.** Pressed toward "visible immateriality" and the dematerialized city, the figure may understate the irreducibly physical, durable, and bodily demands of the built environment — the very "intractable materiality" it wants to dissolve still houses bodies (Interpretive synthesis).
- **"Growth" is not self-evidently benign.** The horticultural pole imports the assumption that organic growth is good; growth, in cities, also names sprawl, gentrification, and the unplanned. The metaphor borrows biology's positive valence without inheriting its ambivalences (Speculative connection).
