# The Internal Work Camp

## The Metaphor

The carceral institution carried within: in the late-modern society of compulsion, the achievement-subject does not labor inside an external camp policed by a separate guard — it *is* the camp. Han states that "everyone carries a work camp inside," a labor camp whose defining feature is that "one is simultaneously prisoner and guard, victim and perpetrator." (`Directly stated`)

## What It Compares

- **Target:** The structure of neoliberal [[Auto-Exploitation]] in the [[Achievement Society]] — a regime of compulsion in which the subject coerces, surveils, and burns out itself.
- **Source domain:** The labor camp (work camp), with its paired roles of prisoner and guard, victim and perpetrator, and its apparatus of confinement and forced labor.
- **Mapping:** The two opposed institutional roles of the camp — the confined laborer and the enforcing authority — are collapsed into a single subject. The subject is at once the one compelled to produce and the one issuing the compulsion. Confinement is interior; the wall, the guard, and the prisoner are the same person.

## What It Reveals

The figure makes thinkable Han's central claim that "exploitation is possible even without domination" (`Directly stated`). By siting the camp *inside* the subject, the metaphor shows why the achievement-subject's suffering cannot be traced to an external master or class antagonist: the master "has become a laboring slave," and "the dialectic of master and slave does not yield a society where everyone is free" (`Directly stated`). It dramatizes the fusion of prisoner and guard that distinguishes self-exploitation from classical exploitation — coercion that feels like freedom because it issues from the self. It also gives the burnout diagnosis its carceral weight: Han continues the camp imagery directly, comparing the apathy of depression, bipolar disorder, and burnout to the symptoms of the Muselmänner, the emaciated prisoners of the concentration camps (`Directly stated`).

## What It Hides or Distorts

By analogizing the freely participating achievement-subject to the inmate of an actual labor or concentration camp — and by extending the figure to the Muselmann (`Strongly implied` as a deliberate provocation) — the metaphor risks flattening the difference between voluntary self-compulsion and the historical violence of literal incarceration and genocide. Han's own logic notes this asymmetry elsewhere in the chapter: the late-modern subject's bare life "cannot be killed absolutely" and is "undead," whereas the camp inmate was "absolutely expendable" (`Directly stated`). The interior camp has no gate, no liberation, and no perpetrator who can be named, tried, or overthrown — which is precisely Han's point, but it can also obscure that real external structures (capital, the labor market, the dispositive of achievement) condition the self-compulsion the metaphor locates wholly within the individual.
