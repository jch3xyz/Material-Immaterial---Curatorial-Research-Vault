# The Mirror Always Lies

## The Metaphor

"The mirror always lies." Ascott's terse figure for the fraudulence of representation: the mimetic image — the reflected look of things — does not deliver reality but counterfeits it. The mirror is the oldest instrument of Western art, the flat reflecting surface that promises to give back the world as it is; Ascott declares it a liar. "Representation in art was always essentially mendacious, illusory, and counterfeit. The mirror always lies." `Directly stated`

The figure is the polemical pivot of his "appearance to apparition" argument. An art of *appearance* keeps faith with the surface, "the look of things," "the outward and visible look of things"; it treats the world as a finished, [[Apparition|ready-made]] reality to be reflected. Ascott folds the optical mirror into the epistemological one — the eye that merely reflects, the retinal gaze that takes the surface for the truth — and brands both fraudulent: "appearance is a fraud." The metaphor thus compresses an entire indictment of mimesis into one image that lies by its very nature. `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

- **Target** (representation / the mimetic, appearance-based paradigm of Western art) → **source** (the mirror — a reflecting surface).
- **Target** (the retinal gaze and the surface image it trusts) → **source** (the mirror's returned reflection, taken for the real).

The mapping is an indictment, feature by feature:

| The mirror (representation) | What Ascott asserts of it |
|---|---|
| returns an image of the world | the image is "mendacious, illusory, and counterfeit" |
| claims to show reality | "appearance is a fraud" |
| reads the surface | "the surface of the world hides more than it discloses" |
| trusts the eye | "the retinal gaze can penetrate very little of the material state and almost nothing of the spiritual state of things" |

The mirror is not a neutral instrument that *can* lie; it is one that *always* lies, because reflection of a surface is, structurally, all it does. The lie is not error but the medium's condition.

## What It Reveals

It reveals that representation is not a transparent window onto reality but an ideological surface. Ascott binds the art of appearance to "classical science" and to "ideologies of both church and state," an art that looks "no further into things than their outward forms allow, making of the world a clockwork machine of parts" and "seeing man as little more than a material object." The mirror-lie names how a culture of appearances "bases itself on certainties, a definitive description of reality" — fixed, dogmatic, finished. `Directly stated`

By exposing the surface as a fraud, the figure clears the ground for [[Apparition]] and [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)|radical constructivism]]: if the mirror lies, then art's task is not to reflect a prior reality but to *construct* one. "We now realise that an art dedicated to appearance simply gives the lie to whatever is the case, since the retinal gaze can penetrate very little of the material state and almost nothing of the spiritual state of things. The surface of the world hides more than it discloses." The mirror-lie is therefore the negative ground on which the [[The Screen of Transformations vs the Mirror of Reflections|screen of transformations]] is built — the counter-figure to the mirror, where the network transforms rather than reflects. The shift it licenses is from object to process, from appearance to apparition, from contemplation to participation (the [[The Window vs the Doorway|window to the doorway]]). `Strongly implied`

## What It Hides or Distorts

The figure is itself a rhetorical mirror, and its absolutism is its blind spot. "Always" forecloses the possibility that representation might *also* construct — that mimesis was ever a passive return rather than a made, conventional, world-disclosing act. Ascott concedes part of this when he exempts Kandinsky, Duchamp, and Pollock as artists who "attempt[ed] to reveal the invisible, and construct their separate realities," which suggests the line between the lying mirror and the constructing screen runs *through* the history of painting, not cleanly between an old order and a new one. The clean break the metaphor performs is, to that degree, overstated. `Interpretive synthesis`

It also smuggles a metaphysics of depth past the very surface it condemns: to say "the surface of the world hides more than it discloses" presumes a true interior — "the spiritual state of things," "the forces, fields, and relationships underlying 'our' visual world" — that apparition will disclose where appearance failed. The figure may thus replace one claim to access reality (the mirror's) with another (the network's), rather than abandoning the claim as such. And by making the lie a property of the instrument ("the mirror *always* lies"), it can dismiss representational art wholesale as fraud rather than reading particular works on their terms — a polemic's economy purchased at the cost of discrimination. `Speculative connection`
