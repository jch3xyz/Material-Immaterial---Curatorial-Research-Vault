# Consciousness as Mental Pollution

## The Metaphor

In *[[Homo Deus]]*, Harari relays the most radical (epiphenomenalist) theory of consciousness as an image: subjective experience is a by-product with no causal work to do — "a kind of mental pollution produced by the firing of complex neural networks. It doesn't do anything. It is just there" (`Directly stated`, as a theory Harari reports rather than endorses). The figure is doubled by two supporting images of useless exhaust: the noise of a jet engine, which "roar[s] loudly, but the noise doesn't propel the aeroplane forward," and the carbon dioxide that "each and every breath fills the air with" though "humans don't need" it. Consciousness, on this view, is to the brain what roar is to the engine and CO₂ is to breathing — a real, unmistakable emission that accompanies the work without performing any of it.

## What It Compares

- **Target**: consciousness / the stream of subjective experience (pain, pleasure, anger, love) generated by the brain's electrochemical activity.
- **Source domain**: pollution and waste exhaust — engine noise, exhaled carbon dioxide, environmental run-off — the unwanted but inevitable side-effects of a productive physical process.
- **Mapping**: the firing of "complex neural networks" → the working machine (jet engine, lungs, brain-as-data-processor); the felt experience that accompanies that firing → the by-product (roar, CO₂, "mental pollution"). The crucial inherited entailment is *causal inertness*: as the roar does not move the plane, consciousness "doesn't do anything." It is produced by the process but feeds nothing back into it. (`Directly stated` for the mapping; the engine/CO₂ pairing is Harari's own gloss on the same passage.)

This image is the standalone figure attached to the epiphenomenon argument that underwrites [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]]: if consciousness is mere exhaust, then intelligence never needed it, and the two can be prised apart and outsourced to non-conscious algorithms. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## What It Reveals

By recasting consciousness as a *by-product*, the metaphor makes vivid the central lacuna Harari has been building toward: that science can find no evolutionary function for subjective experience. "The better we understand the brain, the more redundant the mind seems" — and the pollution image gives that redundancy a concrete shape. It lets us *think* a mind that is fully real yet fully inert: something that, like engine noise, you can be "100 per cent certain" you are experiencing while it changes nothing downstream. It thereby exposes the explanatory gap at the heart of [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — if every electron "moves because another electron moved earlier," there is no causal slot left for feeling to occupy — and it dramatises the premise (that mind contributes no work) on which the economic obsolescence of conscious humans, and the rise of [[Dataism]], are later built. (`Strongly implied`.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

The metaphor smuggles in its own conclusion. "Pollution" is by definition a useless residue, so to *name* consciousness pollution is already to have decided the question — whether subjective experience does any work — that the science Harari reports cannot actually settle ("we have no clue," "we have absolutely no idea"). The image's tidiness conceals how contested and unproven the epiphenomenalist position is; Harari himself flags it as merely "the best theory of consciousness that contemporary science has to offer," a thought "worth thinking, even if it isn't true."

It also risks an inference Harari explicitly blocks. Calling pain and pleasure "mental pollution" suggests they are worthless, yet Harari insists the opposite: consciousness is "a direct and very tangible reality," it "may actually have great moral and political value," and "the whole edifice of modern politics and ethics is built upon subjective experiences." The pollution figure speaks only to *function and causal efficacy*; it says nothing about *reality* or *value*, and read carelessly it distorts both — flattening "all the pain and pleasure experienced by billions of creatures for millions of years" into mere waste. Finally, like the [[Mind as Machine]] analogy Harari elsewhere distrusts (the steam engine, then the computer), the waste-exhaust image imports the assumptions of a particular technology — the brain as a data-processing system whose output is dispensable noise — assumptions Harari himself warns "may turn out to be just as naïve." (`Strongly implied`; the value/reality boundary is `Directly stated` in the chapter.)

## Related Symbols and Concepts

- [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]] — the argument this metaphor serves: if consciousness is functionless exhaust, intelligence never required it and can be separated from it.
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — the gap the image dramatises; "mental pollution" is one (deflationary) response to the question of why physical processing is accompanied by any experience at all.
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the substrate premise behind the figure: if the brain is a data-processing machine, felt experience is the algorithm's idle exhaust, not its engine.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the family of mechanical/computational analogies (steam engine, computer) within which the jet-engine image sits, and which Harari treats with suspicion.
- [[Dataism]] — the techno-religion the epiphenomenon thesis licenses: once consciousness "doesn't do anything," value migrates to data-processing efficiency.
- [[Can It Suffer (The Reality Test)]] — the counter-emphasis: even if consciousness does no causal work, its capacity for suffering is what Harari reserves as the test of moral reality.
