# A Game About a Game

## The Metaphor

The augmented-reality game *Pokémon Go* is **a game about a game**: the visible game you play (a treasure hunt for virtual creatures) is, unbeknownst to its players, merely the board of a second, higher-order game whose real contestants are the paying merchants who bid for guaranteed foot traffic, and whose real prize is the actuated behavior of the players themselves. Zuboff: "The genius of Pokémon Go was to transform the game you see into a higher-order game of surveillance capitalism, a game about a game" (`Directly stated`).

This is the keystone figure of [[Economies of Action]] in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* — the point at which surveillance capitalism stops merely *predicting* behavior and begins *producing* it, and the demonstration case for [[The Reality Business]]: surveillance capitalism operating in physical space exactly as it operates online.

## What It Compares

| Source domain (the nested game) | Target (surveillance capitalism's economies of action) |
|---|---|
| The visible game — catching Pokémon, earning XP, joining gyms | The consumer experience: the "free" service that secures voluntary, rapturous engagement |
| The players who "took the city as their board" | Human subjects whose movements are the raw material; they become "a wholly different kind of human game board" for the second game (`Directly stated`) |
| The hidden second game | Surveillance capitalism — a market for behavioral futures conducted just beyond the rim of player awareness |
| The real players (invisible to the enthusiasts on the lawn) | The paying merchants — pizzerias, McDonald's, Starbucks, Sprint — who buy "sponsored locations" to "drive foot traffic" |
| The game's rules, lures, and rewards | [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]] — the protocols of [[Telestimulation]] that "prod and herd people across real-world terrains" (`Directly stated`) |
| Winning the visible game (catch 'em all) | "Guaranteed outcomes" — the conversion of probable behavior into near-certain commercial result |

The structural inversion is the metaphor's whole point: "running game players through the real world, but not for the sake of the game they think they are playing" (`Directly stated`). The visible game is sincere bait; the consequential game is concealed beneath it. Zuboff names this the unmasking of "the Greeks secreted deep in the belly of the Trojan horse: the economic orientation obscured behind the veil of the digital" (`Directly stated`).

## What It Reveals

- **Gamification is actuation, not just engagement.** The literature treats [[Gamification]] as a motivational technique (Han reads it as auto-exploitation; Werbach's dynamics/mechanics/components). Zuboff's metaphor reveals its surveillance-capitalist destiny: a game is a *means of behavior modification* whose rules can "automatically condition and herd collective behavior" toward externally-owned market objectives (`Directly stated`). The figure makes visible that the player's pleasure and the controller's profit live on two different boards.
- **The asymmetry of knowledge is structural, not incidental.** The real players "could not be found in the clot of enthusiasts waving their phones at the edge of David's lawn" (`Directly stated`). The metaphor names a designed invisibility: one set of contestants cannot even perceive the game in which they are the pieces. This is the lived form of the one-way mirror — [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] operating below awareness.
- **It concretizes the move off the screen.** *Pokémon Go* "proved that surveillance capitalism could operate in the real world much as it does in the virtual one" — the demonstration of [[The Reality Business]]. The metaphor stages how [[Behavioral Surplus]] mined online migrates into "fleshand-blood behavioral futures markets" ([[Behavioral Futures Markets]]) in pizzerias and malls (`Directly stated`).
- **It exposes the two-level service.** Niantic's services "operate on two levels: game services for players and prediction services for Niantic's customers" (`Directly stated`). The metaphor is the popular shorthand for this concealed bifurcation between the user-facing product and the customer-facing one.

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **"Game" can romanticize coercion as play.** The framing of "play," "adventure," and "natural selection" aestheticizes what Zuboff insists is a unilateral imposition: a "for-profit declaration of eminent domain in which reality is recast as an unbounded expanse of blank spaces to be sweated for others' enrichment" (`Directly stated`). Calling it a game risks softening the dispossession — the metaphor must be held against David, the homeowner whose yard and "boundaries of civility" were annexed without consent.
- **The two-board image can imply a clean separation that obscures continuity.** The danger is reading the "second game" as a discrete hidden room rather than as the *same* extractive logic of [[Behavioral Surplus]] and the [[The Prediction Imperative]] simply extended into space. The metaphor's tidy nesting can understate that the players' bodies, locations, and social dynamics are the surplus — there is no clean wall between the boards.
- **It can flatter the controller's agency into omnipotence.** "Writing the music" and letting "the music make them dance" frames herding as near-total. Zuboff elsewhere stresses friction, resistance, and the contingency of these outcomes; the game metaphor, taken too far, can present guaranteed outcomes as already achieved rather than as the asymptote surveillance capital is racing toward.
- **"A game about a game" can sound clever and self-contained**, masking that the *real* players are not abstract — they are named merchants (McDonald's, Starbucks, Sprint, Disney) bidding on a "cost per visit" basis. The wit of the figure can distract from the banality of the business model it exposes.
