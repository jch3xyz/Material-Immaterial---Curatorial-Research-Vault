# The Hooded Machine

## The Metaphor

Modern sculpture is a **hooded machine**: a smooth, sealed metal body whose outer covering symbolizes psychic inaccessibility, and which promises "minor miracles" only on the unrealizable condition that its "outer canopy could be lifted to reveal an inner sense of life force." Burnham names the structure of belief this image encodes directly: "Such a rationalization is vitalism applied to the machine." `Directly stated`

The metaphor is the explicit sibling of an epistemic claim Burnham makes in the immediately preceding sentences: "Like the human organism, the functional explanation of the modern machine is internalized; it remains a black box to all but the most initiated." The hooded machine is what the black box looks like once it is recast as sculpture — the same sealed interiority, now read not as functional opacity but as the promise of a withheld vital essence. `Directly stated`

## What It Reveals

- It exposes formalist faith in the autonomous art object as a **vitalism applied to the machine** — a residual animism that survives industrialization by relocating "life" from the organism into the sealed machine-form. The smooth modern object keeps the old expectation of an inner animating presence even as its body has become a manufactured, opaque casing. `Directly stated`
- It links sculpture's fate to a broader cultural condition: the "acute transition from classical physics to modern atomic theory," in which "matter had lost its supremacy in human conception" — so that the machine, like matter itself, has withdrawn from readable external form into internalized, black-boxed function. `Strongly implied` (Burnham frames the technology passage as one of several parallel pressures — physics, technology — bearing on "the shifting horizon of the sculptor's attitude.")
- It diagnoses the smoothness of the modern object as **a mask, not a transparency**: the protective metal skin advertises an interior it will never disclose, so the promised miracle is structurally deferred. `Interpretive synthesis`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- The metaphor names a **rationalization** — Burnham's own word — so its danger is that it dignifies an article of faith (a hidden "life force") as if it were a property of the object. The canopy can never actually be lifted; there is no inner force, only the expectation of one. `Directly stated`
- By promising "minor miracles," it sustains the cult of the autonomous, hermetic sculptural object at the very moment Burnham argues that object is losing its foundations — distracting from his counter-thesis that art is migrating from discrete objects toward "a unified network above and beyond natural order." `Strongly implied`
- It mistakes psychic inaccessibility (an effect of the machine's *covering*) for inner depth, collapsing the difference between a sealed surface and a meaningful interior. `Interpretive synthesis`
