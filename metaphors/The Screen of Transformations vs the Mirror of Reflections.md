# The Screen of Transformations vs the Mirror of Reflections

## The Metaphor

Two optical instruments stand for two paradigms of art and meaning. The **mirror of reflections** is the older, mimetic instrument: a flat surface that returns an image, the metaphysics of representation in which the artwork *reflects* a prior reality back to a passive contemplator. The **screen of transformations** is its telematic successor: not a reflecting plane but a field of "branching, interacting pathways" through which inputs collide, conjoin, and are remade. "In telematic networks, the screen of transformations replaces the mirror of reflections," Ascott writes — the network does not give back what is put before it but transforms it, becoming "a planetary field for the dance of data." `Directly stated`

The figure compresses Ascott's central claim that the network is a medium of *process*, not of *picturing*. Where a mirror has senders and receivers, an original and its reflection, the screen has only "users" whose intervention is "more or less creative depending on the degree of their participation in the system." The image thus carries the whole telematic argument: from object to system, from contemplation to participation, from fixed meaning to [[Negotiated Meaning|negotiated meaning]]. `Strongly implied`

## What It Compares

- **Target** (telematic network / post-aesthetic practice) → **source** (a screen with branching, interacting pathways).
- **Target** (the mimetic/representational paradigm Ascott supersedes) → **source** (a flat mirror returning a reflection).

The mapping runs feature by feature:

| Mirror of reflections | Screen of transformations |
|---|---|
| Returns a faithful image | Transforms inputs into "creative collisions and conjunctions" |
| Single plane, single viewpoint | "Branching, interacting pathways" / a "field" |
| Senders and receivers (information theory) | Only "users," graded by participation |
| Representation, fixed signified | Process, "flux of meanings," no fixed location for truth |
| Passive contemplation | Active intervention, the "dance of data" |

The mirror belongs to the perspectival, Cartesian order Ascott elsewhere attacks — "a reality composed only of discrete objects set in a strict causality"; the screen belongs to the quantum/cybernetic order in which "particles are not isolated grains of matter, but are probability patterns." The two instruments thus also stand in for the [[Object vs System]] divide and feed the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension — the screen's outputs live "within the virtual 'immateriality' of the networks." `Interpretive synthesis`

## What It Reveals

- **Meaning is generated, not transmitted.** By swapping the mirror for the screen, Ascott makes vivid that the telematic artwork has no message to reflect back; meaning "is not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined." The figure makes thinkable an art whose content is its own transformative process — the position elaborated in [[Telematic Discourse]] and [[Negotiated Meaning]]. `Directly stated`
- **Participation replaces contemplation.** The screen has "branching, interacting pathways"; one acts *through* it rather than looking *at* it. This is the decisive rupture Ascott locates in Duchamp — "from the passive pleasure of the contemplation of the beautiful" to a creative act completed by the spectator — and it grounds [[Reciprocal Communication]] and the participatory program of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]. `Strongly implied`
- **The network is a cosmology, not just a tool.** The screen as "a planetary field for the dance of data" links the art-historical claim to quantum physics' "cosmic web" and to [[Network Consciousness]] — the image scales from the gallery to "mind at large." `Strongly implied`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It can romanticize the medium's neutrality of access.** The clean opposition screen-good / mirror-bad suppresses Ascott's own warning that "advanced technology is not innocent or value-free" and that the network's "creative potential will only be realised to the extent that artists resist the monopolisation and colonisation of electronic space by multinational business and the military." The transformations a screen performs can be capture as easily as creativity. `Strongly implied`
- **It overstates the death of representation.** Casting the mirror as merely passive caricatures mimetic art, which was never only reflection; and a "screen" still displays — it has not abolished the image so much as set it in motion. The figure's tidiness risks hiding the persistence of representation inside the transformation. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **"Transformation" obscures who is transformed.** By dissolving "senders and receivers" into "users," the metaphor can flatten real asymmetries of authorship, skill, and power within the network — the very inequalities Ascott elsewhere flags (the "Third World" brought into the "information embrace"). The screen names process without naming whose process. `Speculative connection`
