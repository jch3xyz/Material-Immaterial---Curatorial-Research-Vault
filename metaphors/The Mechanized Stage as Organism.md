# The Mechanized Stage as Organism

## The Metaphor

The mechanized stage as a living organism. In Jack Burnham's reconstruction of Oskar Schlemmer's Bauhaus theory, the "absolute visual stage" is figured not as a flat picture-plane to be looked *at* but as a giant body to be looked *from within* — a "mechanistic organism" with bowels and a sensory metabolism, run from a central nervous switchboard by a single hidden operator. The human spectator is no longer seated outside the proscenium; he is *nested in the organism's viscera*, getting his "sensory fill" while the machine does the seeing for him. Burnham reads this as Schlemmer envisioning "his optimum stage as a sort of giant organism."

The metaphor performs a reversal: the human is moved from *audience* to *organ*, and the apparatus is promoted from *instrument* to *body*. `Strongly implied` — Burnham's own gloss ("a sort of giant organism, with man, the spectator nested in its bowels") makes the mapping explicit, though the tabular schematization is interpretive.

## What It Reveals

- **It names the immersive, programmed, kinetic environment decades before the term exists.** Burnham presents Schlemmer as articulating "the theatre as an organism (or complete mechanism) capable of engulfing the audience, thereby becoming the means for the total preoccupation of man's sensory equipment." This is the earliest articulation in the book of the *enveloping experiential environment* — the precursor of [[Art as Experiential Technology]], where the artwork is no longer an object but a tunable field of stimuli surrounding the viewer. `Directly stated` (that Schlemmer's stage engulfs and totally preoccupies the senses); `Interpretive synthesis` (that this prefigures the experiential-technology paradigm).
- **It relocates the artwork from object to system.** Once the stage is an organism with an operator at a switchboard, the relevant unit of art is no longer a discrete made thing but a *running system* of energy, light, and feedback — exactly the shift the vault tracks as [[Object and System]]. The sculptor becomes a programmer; the static object dissolves into a controlled flow. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It makes the operator a hidden engineer rather than an artist-author.** Schlemmer's man is "banned from view" and stands as "the perfect engineer," aligning the figure of the artist with the control-room technician — a control/programming model of authorship continuous with Burnham's broader interest in [[Automata]] and self-acting machines.

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **The organism is in fact a mechanism with no life of its own.** The "organism" metaphor lends warmth, autonomy, and vitality to what Burnham is careful to call a "complete mechanism." The figure of a *living* body conceals that nothing on the stage is alive — the apparent vitality is wholly authored at the switchboard, a parody of life rather than life. (Burnham notes Schlemmer's awareness of mechanical motion "as an instrument of human parody.")
- **It naturalizes the spectator's passivity.** To be "nested in its bowels, getting his sensory fill" sounds like nourishment, but it casts the viewer as something *fed by* and *enclosed within* the machine — a digesting organ rather than an agent. The biological image makes total sensory capture feel like satiety rather than subjection.
- **It folds two incompatible positions of "man" into one body.** Man is simultaneously *banned from view inside* the organism (the absorbed spectator in the bowels) and *standing outside* it at the switchboard (the perfect engineer "directing the feast"). The organism metaphor papers over the gap between the controlled and the controller — the question of who is the body and who is the brain. `Strongly implied` — the doubling is visible in Burnham's two sentences ("banned from view in this mechanistic organism… at the central switch-board" vs. "nested in its bowels… while standing outside directing the feast").
- **It treats sensory saturation as the telos of art.** Framing the stage as an organism whose function is "the total preoccupation of man's sensory equipment" smuggles in the assumption that *more total absorption is the goal*, occluding the older theatre of critical distance and reflection.
