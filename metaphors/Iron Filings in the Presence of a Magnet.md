# Iron Filings in the Presence of a Magnet

## The Metaphor

Good intentions as **iron filings in the presence of a magnet** — well-meant tools and research, however benign in conception, are drawn helplessly toward an invisible market force whose pull they cannot resist. Zuboff applies the figure to MIT Media Lab professor Rosalind Picard's field of "affective computing," which began as a good-hearted project for "your own use" and was bent into a supply operation for emotional surplus once the **prediction imperative** exerted its demand.

The magnet is not any individual's malice but the structural **force field** of surveillance capitalism: a market gravity that reorients any data-producing technology toward the production of prediction. The filings — Picard's intentions — are "innocent," which is precisely the point. Innocence offers no protection against a field that acts on the material's composition rather than its purpose.

The mapping is governed by *material susceptibility*: iron filings respond to a magnet because of what they are made of, not what they were for. So too, any technology that renders human experience as data is, by composition, susceptible to the prediction imperative — `Directly stated` for the affective-computing case, `Strongly implied` for the generalization.

## What It Reveals

- **Capture is structural, not moral.** The metaphor relocates the cause of the corruption from the individual (Picard is not villainized — she "did not foresee the market forces") to the field. This is the core of the argument [[Benign technology is captured by surveillance capitalism's force field]]: good intentions are not a defense because the force does not negotiate with intent. `Directly stated`
- **The pull is impersonal and lawful-seeming.** Like magnetism, the prediction imperative operates as a quasi-physical regularity ("latent demand summons suppliers and supplies"), making the drift toward surveillance look natural and inevitable rather than chosen. `Strongly implied`
- **Innocence is the bait, not the shield.** The filings are "innocent," yet that innocence is exactly what makes them frictionless raw material. Benign framing ("do-good" applications, an "affective mirror" for the user) accelerates, rather than prevents, conscription into [[Rendition]] of the emotional depths. `Directly stated`
- **It diagnoses a recurring fate.** Zuboff calls Affectiva's "transformation from doing good to doing surveillance capitalism" a "metaphor for the fate of the larger undertaking" — the iron-filings image names a general law of capture, not a single misfortune. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It risks excusing agency.** Magnetism is deterministic; iron filings have no choice. The figure can understate the real decisions made by firms, sponsors, and the Media Lab that encouraged the "spin off," and by Picard's later collaborators who "went totally off the original mission." Zuboff partly corrects this elsewhere (Picard was "pushed out"; the CEO "absolutely not comfortable with the medical space"), but the metaphor itself flattens these choices into physics. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It naturalizes a contingent market.** Presenting the prediction imperative as a *field* — a feature of nature — obscures that surveillance capitalism is, on Zuboff's own account, [[Surveillance Capitalism|a logic not a technology]]: a historically specific, contestable economic order, not a law of physics. The metaphor's persuasive force comes from the very inevitability Zuboff's larger project insists is an illusion. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It implies a single, fixed pole.** A magnet has one field; the image can suggest capture is total and uni-directional, downplaying the possibility of resistance, regulation, or alternative arrangements of the same technology (Picard's original vision of data "for you, not merely about you"). `Speculative connection`
