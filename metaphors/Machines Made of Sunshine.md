# Machines Made of Sunshine

## The Metaphor

"Our best machines are made of sunshine." [[Donna Haraway]]'s signature figure for the seeming immateriality of the late-twentieth-century microelectronic machine: the silicon-chip device imagined as weightless, clean, and bodiless because it is "nothing but signals, electromagnetic waves, a section of a spectrum." The machine is sunshine — light, radiation, "a section of a spectrum" — and so is exempted, in appearance, from the heaviness, opacity, and locality of matter. Where the cyborg's body should be there is only ether: "Cyborgs are ether, quintessence." The figure is the sharpest single image in *A Cyborg Manifesto* of the Material vs Immaterial axis, and it is doubled-edged from the first sentence — the same machines that are "all light and clean" are "a matter of immense human pain in Detroit and Singapore." (`Directly stated`.)

The image is built on a contrast Haraway states flatly in the same breath: "People are nowhere near so fluid, being both material and opaque." The machine's apparent immateriality is defined *against* the human body's stubborn materiality and opacity. Sunshine is fluid, transparent, placeless; the body is heavy, opaque, situated. The metaphor thus stages the [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy at its starkest — the chip as pure pattern/signal, the body as resistant matter — and is the founding image of what Haraway names a page earlier as the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]]: a regime of power that operates through "floating signifiers," coding, and "rates of flow across boundaries" rather than through the integrity of solid objects. (`Directly stated` for the contrast; `Strongly implied` for the link to the informatics regime, which Haraway develops in the surrounding section.)

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the modern microelectronic machine — "quintessentially microelectronic devices: they are everywhere and they are invisible," the silicon chip and the miniaturized devices (TV wristbands, hand-sized cameras, cruise missiles) it makes possible.
- **Source domain:** sunshine, light, electromagnetic radiation — "signals, electromagnetic waves, a section of a spectrum"; and, at the limit, the alchemical/metaphysical terms "ether, quintessence" (the fifth element, the heavenly substance above the four earthly ones).
- **The mapping:**
  - Sunshine / light → the machine's medium: signal, not stuff; spectrum, not mass
  - Cleanliness and weightlessness of light → the machine's portability, mobility, invisibility ("eminently portable, mobile"; "everywhere and... invisible")
  - Ether / quintessence → the cyborg's apparent escape from material substrate altogether ("Cyborgs are ether, quintessence")
  - The opacity and fluidity-deficit of bodies → the human as the residual material term ("People are nowhere near so fluid, being both material and opaque")

The figure belongs to the third of Haraway's three "boundary breakdowns" — the dissolving of "the boundary between physical and nonphysical," which "is very imprecise for us." Sunshine is the image that carries that breakdown: a machine made of light has crossed from the physical into the nonphysical, or appears to. (`Directly stated`.)

## What It Reveals

- **The dream of the immaterial machine.** The figure makes vivid the cultural conviction that the information machine has shed its body — that it is "all light and clean" precisely because it is "nothing but signals." It names, in a single image, the fantasy that the chip is pure pattern and the substrate has dropped away. This is the [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy in its most seductive form, and Haraway gives it its most quotable expression. (`Directly stated`.)
- **Why these machines are politically invisible.** Because they are made of "sunshine" — light, signal, ether — the new machines "are as hard to see politically as materially." Their very immateriality is a mode of power: "The ubiquity and invisibility of cyborgs are precisely why these Sunshine Belt machines are so deadly." The figure reveals that weightlessness is not innocence but camouflage; the "floating signifiers moving in pickup trucks across Europe" are harder to oppose than the heavy machinery of "older masculinist politics." (`Directly stated`.)
- **The theology smuggled into the chip.** "Ether, quintessence," "pure spirit," "an irreverent upstart god": Haraway's idiom exposes that the immaterial machine inherits the attributes of the divine — ubiquity, invisibility, spirituality. "Modern machinery is an irreverent upstart god, mocking the Father's ubiquity and spirituality." The machine made of sunshine is a secular pneuma, and its engineers are "sun-worshippers." (`Directly stated`; the reading of this as an informational theology is `Interpretive synthesis`.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **The immense human pain the "clean" machine costs.** The figure's irony is internal and Haraway plants it inside the sentence: the machines "all light and clean" are "a matter of immense human pain in Detroit and Singapore." What sunshine hides is labor and the body — "the nimble fingers of 'Oriental' women," "the unnatural cyborg women making chips in Asia," the deindustrialized workers of Detroit. The metaphor's cleanliness conceals a global division of material labor; the light machine runs on opaque bodies. (`Directly stated`.)
- **That the machine never actually leaves its substrate.** "Sunshine" presents the chip as nothing but signal, but the silicon chip is "a surface for writing... etched in molecular scales disturbed only by atomic noise." The immaterial machine is still inscribed in matter — which is exactly the counter-claim the vault registers as [[The Materiality of Informatics]]: information must always be instantiated in a medium. The figure distorts by letting the signal seem to float free of the spectrum-carrying material that bears it. (`Directly stated` for the chip-as-etched-surface; the link to the materiality-of-informatics counter-claim is `Interpretive synthesis`.)
- **The body it leaves behind.** By making fluidity and weightlessness the machine's virtue, the figure demotes the body's materiality and opacity to a defect — "People are nowhere near so fluid." It thereby reproduces, even as Haraway ironizes it, the disembodiment that treats [[Embodiment|embodied]] matter as the inferior term. The "diseases evoked by these clean machines" — "the minuscule coding changes of an antigen in the immune system," "the experience of stress" — are precisely the bodily costs the sunshine image renders invisible. (`Strongly implied`; Haraway names the diseases directly, the disembodiment critique is the figure's through-line.)
