# The Crucifix Made of Machine Parts

## The Metaphor

On a Chicago street she had walked daily for a year, O'Gieblyn comes upon what she takes for a church and discovers is only its shell: "it was not in fact a church but the façade of a church—just the front wall, which had a bell tower and a small wooden door." The building had burned forty years earlier and been "repurposed as an art space." Then the image that becomes the master symbol of the "Paradox" chapter: "Behind the façade, where the sanctuary should have been, was a fenced-in garden, above which hung, from wire, a crucifix made of machine parts."

The object is the book's thesis condensed into a single found image. Where the sanctuary should be — the holy interior, the place of presence — there is instead a garden and, suspended on wire, the mechanical Christ. Faith has not vanished; it has been *fabricated out of mechanism*. The crucifix made of machine parts is the literal picture of religious longing migrated into engineering: theology, evacuated from its old institutional shell, reassembled from the components of the machine age. (`Interpretive synthesis` — O'Gieblyn presents the image without exegesis, but its placement as the chapter's recurring "doubling" and her surrounding argument make the figural reading clear.)

The whole chapter has prepared this reading. O'Gieblyn's presentation in Sweden argued "that for all its supposed 'naturalism,' the simulation hypothesis was ultimately an argument from design" belonging to "a long lineage of creationist rhetoric that invoked human technologies to argue that the universe could not have come about without the conscious intention of a designer." The crucifix on the wire is that lineage made object: the divine reconstituted from technology. It is the visual rhyme of [[The Simulation Hypothesis]] — the programmer-god of [[The Computational Universe]] — standing where the altar used to be.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** theology, faith, the sacred — the religious impulse and its objects (God, providence, resurrection, the design of the cosmos) in a secular, technological age.
- **Source domain:** a crucifix physically constructed from machine parts, hung from wire in the gutted interior of a church that is now an art space — the sacred image manufactured out of mechanism, in the place vacated by the sanctuary.
- **The mapping:**
  - The burned church / surviving façade → institutional religion, hollowed out, its interior of belief gone, only the public face remaining
  - The empty sanctuary, now a garden → the vacated place of presence and the holy, where meaning once resided
  - The crucifix → faith, the sacred, the figure of God
  - *Made of machine parts* → theology reconstituted from technology; the religious migrated into engineering, computation, and the metaphors of the machine
  - Hung *from wire* → the sacred now suspended by mechanical means, dependent on the apparatus rather than on the building that once housed it

This is the same structural move O'Gieblyn diagnoses across the book under [[Technology as Metaphor]]: each age describes its deepest things in the idiom of its dominant machine, and in the computer age the machine's idiom reaches even God. The crucifix of machine parts is to theology what [[Mind as Machine]] is to the psyche and [[The Computational Universe]] is to the cosmos — the same figure (the sacred remade as mechanism) operating on a different target. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## What It Reveals

- **That theology has migrated into engineering.** The image makes visible the chapter's central claim: technological projects like the simulation hypothesis are religious projects in disguise. Bostrom himself conceded "some loose analogies" between his theory and "traditional religious concepts" — the programmers "would be like gods," describable "as omnipotent" and "omniscient," able to "'resurrect' individual people from the simulation," to "reward or punish us based on our actions," even to produce "its own brand of naturalistic theology." The crucifix of machine parts is that naturalistic theology given a body. (`Directly stated` for the analogies; `Strongly implied` for the image as their emblem.)
- **The persistence of the design argument under [[Disenchantment]].** O'Gieblyn shows the argument from design was itself "an outgrowth of the mechanistic philosophy of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries" — a "disenchanted cosmology" of "order," "patterns," and "predictability" that led thinkers "to argue that such a world could not function without a divine Engineer." The crucifix assembled from gears and parts is the divine Engineer's signature: the machine that, by its very order, points back to a maker. It is [[Re-enchantment]] arriving through the back door of mechanism. (`Directly stated` for the genealogy.)
- **The literalizing slide.** The chapter's deepest warning is about taking metaphors at face value: "we run into trouble when we forget that these are metaphors and take them at face value—when we insist, for example, that because there are similarities between physics and information technologies, the universe is in fact an enormous computer. This brand of scientific fundamentalism can lead, ironically, back to the premises of religion." A crucifix that is *actually made of* machine parts is the literalized metaphor incarnate — the figure no longer held as a figure, but built. (`Directly stated` for the slide; `Strongly implied` for the image as its emblem.)
- **A mechanical [[Theodicy]].** Reading the cosmos as buggy software lets O'Gieblyn reimagine evil as error: "sin and suffering were simply errors in the code that could not be corrected without significantly disrupting the system." The crucifix of machine parts presides over exactly this engineering theology — Christ, the figure of suffering, rebuilt from the very mechanism whose glitches the theodicy must explain. (`Directly stated` for the code-theodicy.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It conceals that the sanctuary is empty.** The most disquieting feature of the image is what is *not* there: behind the façade "where the sanctuary should have been" is only a fenced garden. The mechanical Christ presides over an absence. The metaphor of faith-as-engineering supplies a sacred object while quietly evacuating the interior — presence, the holy, the living congregation — that the object was supposed to mediate. It offers the form of the sacred while concealing the loss of its substance. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
- **It distorts faith into mechanism — and thereby loses what is not mechanizable.** To rebuild the crucifix from machine parts is to assume the sacred is the kind of thing that *can* be assembled — that meaning, presence, and grace are reducible to components and code. This is the same elision O'Gieblyn tracks in [[Mind as Machine]]: the machine "merely manipulating symbols," "without understanding the content," information "stripped of meaning." What the mechanical crucifix cannot hold is precisely what the church interior was for. (`Interpretive synthesis`; the meaning-stripping claim is `Directly stated` of information.)
- **It launders an old hubris as new humility.** O'Gieblyn argues that technological cosmologies which claim to dethrone the human in fact re-center it: the appeal of Bostrom's argument "was that it was anthropocentric," letting "us believe once again that we were at the center of things, and that our lives had purpose and meaning in the larger scheme of the universe." As Lisa Randall put it, it takes "a lot of hubris to think we would be what ended up being simulated." A crucifix of machine parts, posing as secular art, smuggles the old anthropocentric theology back in under cover of the machine. (`Directly stated` for the anthropocentrism.)
- **It pretends to explain origins while only displacing them.** Even granting the simulation, "the question about origins remained unchanged, only pushed back one degree. Where did the universe originally come from?" The crucifix of machine parts likewise looks like an answer — the sacred, rebuilt and accounted for — but it only relocates the mystery into the mechanism, exactly as "the metaphor has not solved our most pressing existential problems; it has merely transferred them to a new substrate." (`Directly stated` for the displacement of origins.)
