# The Project as Projectile

## The Metaphor

The project is a projectile. In *The Burnout Society*'s title chapter, Han turns the achievement-subject's apparent act of freedom — liberating itself *into a project* — into an image of intrapsychic warfare: "The project turns out to be a projectile that the achievement-subject is aiming at itself." The German pun (*Projekt* / *Projektil*) is the figure's hinge: the same gesture of self-projection that frees the subject from external command becomes a launched weapon whose target is the launcher. `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

- **Target (psychic event):** the achievement-subject's transition from `subject` to `project` — its self-positivizing "liberation" into limitless self-realization against an unattainable ego ideal.
- **Source domain (a projectile):** an object launched outward as force; here, fired not at an enemy but back at the self that launched it.

The mapping turns one verb into two outcomes. To *project oneself* (Han: "The achievement-subject projects itself [*entwirft sich*] onto the ego ideal") reads as an act of freedom. But the projected thing returns as a *projectile*: "In view of the ego ideal, the real ego appears as a loser buried in self-reproach. The ego wages war with itself." Self-projection and self-bombardment are the same motion seen from launcher and target. `Directly stated`

## What It Reveals

- **Freedom and violence coincide.** The figure makes legible Han's central claim that "the change from subject to project does not make power or violence disappear. Auto-compulsion, which presents itself as freedom, takes the place of allo-compulsion." The projectile is what auto-compulsion looks like from inside. See [[Subject vs Project]], [[Auto-Exploitation]], [[Allo-Exploitation]]. `Directly stated`
- **The wound is self-inflicted and therefore unseen.** Because the subject launched the projectile, it experiences the impact as its own freedom: "the victim of such violence considers itself free." The metaphor names why `Achievement Society` produces `burnout` and depression as *auto-aggressive* maladies — "Exogenous violence is replaced by self-generated violence." `Directly stated`
- **The gap, not the goal, is the wound.** The projectile flies in the space "when the ego gets caught in an unattainable ego ideal" — "The gap between the real ego and the ego ideal then brings forth auto-aggression." The image localizes the injury in the distance between real ego and ego ideal. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It can read as a discrete event.** A projectile is launched once and lands; Han's auto-aggression is continuous and recursive — the achievement-subject "grinds itself down," runs "a rat race it runs against itself," and "succumbs to the destructive compulsion to outdo itself over and over." The ballistic image risks making episodic what Han describes as endless self-exploitation "until it burns out." `Strongly implied`
- **It implies an external launcher and target.** The force of the figure is that launcher and target are *the same* — "The exploiter is simultaneously the exploited." A projectile metaphor smuggles in the spatial separation (shooter / struck) that Han's collapse of `subject` into `project` is precisely meant to dissolve. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It foregrounds aggression over emptiness.** Alongside war, Han diagnoses a hollowing — the self "locks its jaws on itself; paradoxically, this leads the self to hollow and empty out." The violence of a projectile is additive impact; it under-describes the depressive *evacuation* and worldlessness that Han pairs with auto-aggression. `Interpretive synthesis`
