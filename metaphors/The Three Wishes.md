# The Three Wishes

## The Metaphor

A system is like the genie of the traditional three-wishes fairy tale: it grants your wish exactly as stated, not as intended. Ask badly and you get precisely — and only — what you literally asked for, which is rarely what you wanted. In [[Donella Meadows]]'s *Thinking in Systems*, this is the warning behind [[Seeking the Wrong Goal]]: "Systems, like the three wishes in the traditional fairy tale, have a terrible tendency to produce exactly and only what you ask them to produce. Be careful what you ask them to produce."

## What It Compares

- **Target** → the way a system behaves once a goal or indicator is set for it: it drives faithfully toward that goal, whatever it happens to be.
- **Source domain** → the fairy-tale wish, granted by a genie who takes your words with pitiless literalness — the classic tale where each wish rebounds because the wisher named the wrong thing.

The mapping: *your wish* is the system's **goal** — what Meadows elsewhere calls "the direction-setter of the system, the definer of discrepancies that require action, the indicator of compliance, failure, or success toward which balancing feedback loops work." *The genie's literal granting* is the system's [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing feedback]], which relentlessly closes the gap between the current state and the stated goal. *The rebounding wish* is the trap: "If the goal is defined badly, if it doesn't measure what it's supposed to measure, if it doesn't reflect the real welfare of the system, then the system can't possibly produce a desirable result." Wish for military spending and you get military spending — not necessarily national security. Wish for a per-student budget and you get a per-student budget — not necessarily good education.

## What It Reveals

The metaphor makes vivid that a system's failures are often failures of *specification*, not of *operation*. The genie is not malicious and not broken; it is exactly obedient. So too the system: in [[Seeking the Wrong Goal]] "the system obediently follows the rule and produces its specified result—which is not necessarily what anyone actually wants." The perversity is fidelity, not error. This reframes "the system went wrong" as "we asked for the wrong thing," and it locates the remedy upstream, at the moment of asking — which is precisely why the goal sits so high on Meadows's ranked list of [[Leverage Points]] (leverage point #3). Change the wish and the whole system below it — its rules, information flows, and [[Feedback Loops]] — twists to serve the new wish. The fairy tale also carries a built-in ethic of humility: *be careful what you ask for*, because you will get it, whole and unamended.

## What It Hides or Distorts

The genie is a single agent who grants a discrete, one-shot wish; a system is a standing structure of stocks, flows, and loops with no intent at all, granting the "wish" continuously for as long as the goal stands. Reading the trap through the fairy tale can smuggle in the sense that someone *chose* the wrong wish, when Meadows's harder point is structural: once a proxy indicator like GNP is institutionalized as the goal, "governments around the world respond to a signal of faltering GNP by taking numerous actions to keep it growing," whether or not anyone still wants it to rise. The image of three clean wishes also understates the mundane mechanism of the trap — that its usual form is not exotic irony but the ordinary confusion of a measurable proxy with the thing itself: "These examples confuse effort with result, one of the most common mistakes in designing systems around the wrong goal." And a fairy tale has a moral and an ending; a badly wished system just keeps producing, indefinitely, "a result that is not really intended or wanted."
