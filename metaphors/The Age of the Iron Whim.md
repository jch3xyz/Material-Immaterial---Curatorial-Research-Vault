# The Age of the Iron Whim

## The Metaphor

"The age of the iron whim had begun." McLuhan names a whole historical period after the typewriter by fusing two incongruous registers in a single phrase: *iron* — the hard, uniform, mass-produced metal of the machine and of mechanical industry — and *whim* — a caprice, a sudden act of will, the wholly personal and ungovernable impulse of a self deciding its own course. The machine becomes the instrument of a woman's self-determination; the most rigid of materials carries the most intimate of motives. The figure compresses the chapter's social-revolution argument into an emblem: the cheap typewriter put a means of vocation, wage, and "private independence" into the hands of the "career woman," and her "whim" — to leave the doll's house, to take a job, to set a style — is now backed by *iron*. [`Directly stated`]

The metaphor arrives by way of two smaller images that feed it. First, the "twelve-pound look" — "British women were reputed to have developed a 'twelve-pound look' when typewriters became available for sixty dollars or so" — the bearing of a woman who can now command an income and so look at the world differently. Second, "the Viking gesture of Ibsen's Nora Helmer, who slammed the door of her doll's house and set off on a quest of vocation and soul-testing." The slammed door is the whim; the typewriter is the iron that makes the gesture more than theatre. [`Directly stated`]

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the social transformation set off by the typewriter once it became cheap enough to own — the entry of women into the business office, "careers for the Nora Helmers of the West," "the creation of much private independence," and, simultaneously, the homogenizing of dress and commerce.
- **Source domain:** an *age* (a named historical epoch, like an iron age or a machine age) governed by an *iron whim* — a phrase that yokes the machine's hard, mass-produced materiality (iron) to the free, personal, almost capricious will of the individual who wields it (whim).

The mapping turns on a paradox the chapter states outright: the typewriter is at once an agent of *uniformity* and an agent of *independence*. On the uniform side, "the uniform ranks of fashionable lady typists made possible a revolution in the garment industry," and "AS much as the typewriter, the typist brought into business a new dimension of the uniform, the homogeneous, and the continuous." On the independence side, the same machine "has also caused an integration of functions and the creation of much private independence." The iron *whim* holds both: iron (the homogeneous machine, mass fashion, the typewriter "indispensable to every aspect of mechanical industry") and whim (the personal vocation, the slammed door, the self-made style). This is the very "homogenization-vs-independence" double movement the chapter dramatizes, and it sits squarely on the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis — the iron machine on one side, the immaterial wage-vocation and "private independence" it confers on the other. [`Directly stated`] (the both-at-once reading is `Strongly implied`)

## What It Reveals

The figure makes thinkable McLuhan's claim that a *machine* — not a movement, a manifesto, or a law — was the lever of a social revolution, and specifically of a change in the situation of women. By calling it a *whim* he lodges agency and desire inside the apparatus: the typist "was a popular figure of enterprise and skill. She was a style-maker who was also eager to follow styles," at once the author of a style and its follower, the source of homogeneity and the figure of independence. The metaphor also reveals the typewriter as a *fusing* technology of the kind the chapter repeatedly describes — it "fuses composition and publication," it "fuses the functions of the pen and sword" — here fusing the worker and her means, so that the woman who "refused to be dictated to" could "go out and become a stenographer," an "integration of functions" that hands her the "private independence" of a wage. Read alongside [[The Authority of Knowledge]] and the chapter's coming "electric age," the iron whim is an early, mechanical-age intimation that the principal work is becoming "the movement of information," where the typist's clerical labour anticipates the [[Immaterial Labour]] of later regimes. [`Strongly implied`]

## What It Hides or Distorts

A *whim* is light, free, and self-authored — and the word flatters the new independence in just the way G. K. Chesterton mocks within the chapter itself: "women refused to be dictated to and went out and became stenographers." The freedom to choose the office may be a smaller freedom than it looks; the iron may dictate more than the whim chooses. The metaphor's romance — the Viking gesture, the slammed door, the quest of "vocation and soul-testing" — is quietly retracted by the chapter's last line: "The typewriter, with its promise of careers for the Nora Helmers of the West, has really turned out to be an elusive pumpkin coach, after all." The coach turns back into a pumpkin; the emancipation is a Cinderella spell that expires. The figure also tilts toward independence and away from the homogenizing it names in the same breath — the "uniform ranks," the mass garment industry, the typewriter that makes the office "uniform, homogeneous, and continuous" and that an army needs "more typewriters than medium and light artillery pieces." That standardizing pressure is the explosive, [[Specialism as Counter-Irritant|fragmenting]] face of print technology (the chapter calls the typewriter's reach "explosive," even as it "acted in some respects as an implosion, rather than an explosion" — see [[Explosion vs Implosion]]). The whim is iron, and iron is uniform: the metaphor's charm conceals how much of the "revolution" was conscription into the homogeneous. [`Interpretive synthesis`]
