# The Brothel-without-Walls

## The Metaphor

McLuhan names the photographic age "the world since photography as a Brothel-without-Walls." The photograph reproduces and multiplies the human image into purchasable merchandise, turning people into things; society becomes a wall-less brothel that, like the institution in Jean Genet's *The Balcony*, "remains firm and permanent amidst the most furious changes." The figure is borrowed openly from Genet's play and echoes André Malraux's "museum without walls."

`Directly stated` — McLuhan coins the phrase as the chapter's title figure and attributes the theme to Genet.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the condition of human images under photographic mass-reproduction — movie stars, matinee idols, and ordinary faces extended and multiplied into mass-produced goods.
- **Source domain:** the brothel, where bodies are made available for purchase, made wall-less because the photograph removes every barrier of place and access, so the merchandise is everywhere at once.

The mapping runs: the photograph (like the monocle) fixes and objectifies people → the human image becomes mass-produced merchandise → those images become "dreams that money can buy," more easily had than living prostitutes → society at large becomes the brothel, with no walls to contain or limit it.

`Directly stated` — the monocle/camera objectification and the "dreams that money can buy" mapping are explicit in the text.

## What It Reveals

The metaphor makes visible the **reification** at the heart of mechanical image-reproduction: that to photograph a person is to convert them into a thing, a circulating commodity. By calling the result a *brothel* it foregrounds the eroticized, transactional character of this circulation — the image is not merely seen but bought, "hugged and thumbed." By making the brothel *wall-less*, McLuhan registers the loss of any boundary between private body and public merchandise, and the durability of the arrangement: it stays "firm and permanent" through revolution and upheaval.

`Strongly implied` — McLuhan does not use the word "reification," but the turning of people into things and into purchasable merchandise is stated directly.

## What It Hides or Distorts

Cast as titillation and commerce, the figure can obscure questions of consent, labor, and gendered exploitation that the literal brothel raises; it aestheticizes prostitution into a tidy analogy for media circulation. It also leans on the photograph's *commodity* aspect while McLuhan elsewhere in the same chapter insists the photograph's deeper effect is sensory and cognitive (the abolition of time, the turn from "outer matching to inner making") — so the brothel image, vivid as it is, risks reducing the photograph to merchandising and missing the perceptual revolution he otherwise stresses.

`Interpretive synthesis` — drawn from the tension between the brothel passage and McLuhan's larger argument in the chapter about photography and perception.
