# Language Does for Intelligence What the Wheel Does for the Feet

## The Metaphor

In Chapter 8, McLuhan reports Henri Bergson's analogy (from *Creative Evolution*) for understanding language as a human technology: "Language does for intelligence what the wheel does for the feet and the body. It enables them to move from thing to thing with greater ease and speed and ever less involvement." Just as the wheel lifts the body off direct contact with the ground and lets it travel faster while touching less, language lifts intelligence out of immersion in its objects and lets the intellect move between things quickly, easily, and with "ever less involvement." Language is thus figured not as a transparent vehicle of thought but as a wheel-like prosthesis — an [[Media as Extensions of Man|extension of man]] that, like every extension, both amplifies a power and detaches the user from what it acts upon.

## What It Compares

| Source domain (the wheel) | Target (language / speech) |
|---|---|
| The feet and the body | Intelligence, the intellect |
| Rolling locomotion that lifts the body off direct ground contact | Speech that detaches the intellect from "the vastly wider reality" |
| Movement "from thing to thing with greater ease and speed" | Intelligence moving from object to object, no longer "totally involved in the objects of its attention" |
| "Ever less involvement" of the body with the terrain it crosses | Reduced involvement of consciousness with the cosmic / collective unconscious |
| A technology that extends and amplifies physical power | "Language extends and amplifies man but it also divides his faculties" |

The analogy belongs to McLuhan's wider wheel-as-extension trope: the wheel recurs across *Understanding Media* as the paradigm extension of the foot. Here the wheel becomes the source image for explaining how an immaterial technology — speech — performs the same accelerating, detaching work for the mind that a material technology performs for the body. (`Directly stated`: the wheel/feet pairing and the "ease and speed and ever less involvement" gloss are verbatim from the chapter.)

## What It Reveals

- **Language is a technology, not a neutral medium.** The metaphor reframes speech as equipment on the same footing as the wheel — an [[Extensions of Man (McLuhan)|extension of man]]. This anchors McLuhan's broader thesis that media are extensions of the body and senses, and that the spoken word is "an extension or uttering (outering) of all our senses at once." (`Directly stated`)
- **Extension is also amplification and division.** The wheel's "greater ease and speed" exposes the double action McLuhan attributes to every extension: language "extends and amplifies man but it also divides his faculties." This is the same logic as McLuhan's autoamputation thesis — that [[Every extension of man is an autoamputation that numbs perception|every extension numbs the faculty it extends]]. (`Strongly implied`: McLuhan places the Bergson analogy inside his extension-and-amputation framework, though the word "autoamputation" is not used in this passage.)
- **Detachment is the price of mobility.** Just as the wheel separates the foot from the terrain, language separates the intellect from immersion in its objects and "man iron man, and mankind from the cosmic unconscious." The metaphor makes the dimming of the collective unconscious legible as a cost of an acquired technical mobility. (`Directly stated`)
- **It grounds an evolutionary-cosmic reading of media.** By routing the claim through Bergson's *Creative Evolution*, McLuhan links language and even consciousness to a [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness|technical extension of consciousness]] that "dims the bliss of union in the collective unconscious," and looks forward to an electric/computer "Pentecostal condition of universal understanding" that might by-pass language entirely. (`Directly stated`)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It naturalizes a loss as mere efficiency.** "Greater ease and speed and ever less involvement" sounds like pure gain; the metaphor's brightness ("ease," "speed") can obscure that, for McLuhan via Bergson, language "impaired and diminished the values of the collective unconscious." The wheel image smuggles a verdict (diminishment) inside a vocabulary of convenience.
- **It treats language as locomotion between fixed "things."** The wheel rolls between pre-given destinations; the metaphor implies intelligence likewise moves "from thing to thing" among objects that exist prior to language. This understates language's constitutive, world-shaping role that McLuhan himself names elsewhere in the chapter — "Each mother tongue teaches its users a way of seeing and feeling the world." (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **It is a borrowed, secondhand image.** McLuhan attributes the analogy to Bergson and the surrounding argument to "a tradition of thought" Bergson worked within; the wheel/feet figure is reported, not original to McLuhan, and is folded into his own extension thesis. Reading it as straightforwardly McLuhan's own can flatten that provenance. (`Directly stated`: the chapter explicitly credits Bergson and *Creative Evolution*.)
- **The body/mind, material/immaterial split it relies on is itself contestable.** The metaphor leans on a clean separation — wheel:body :: language:intelligence — that brackets the embodied, sensuous, "tactile" dimension of speech the same chapter foregrounds (worry beads, the spoken word that "involves all of the senses dramatically").
