# Pygmalion and Galatea

## The Metaphor

The sculptor Pygmalion, disgusted with female carnality, carves an ideal woman from ivory and falls in love with the figure; through the blessing of Venus the statue, Galatea, miraculously comes alive. For Jack Burnham this Ovidian tale is not a one-off love story but the founding myth of all image-making: it "foretells the mystical equation of all art: that with hard work and faith in divine results a creation may magically become more than the sum of the artist's energies." `Directly stated`

It is the originating figure of [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] — the desire to "bring to life" a human-shaped entity not through natural biological creation but through art, magic, mimesis, or (in the case of automata) literal movement.

## What It Reveals

It names the surplus at the heart of creation: the sense that a finished work possesses a vitality the artist did not consciously put there — a "more than the sum" that registers as the magical or the divine. For Burnham this is the *aesthetic core* of the Western drive he calls Faustian: art and "a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically" converge in the maker who wants the figure not merely to resemble life but to *live*. `Strongly implied`

The myth also exposes the psychology beneath fetishes, idols, dolls, and automata alike: "the ultimate concern of the maker of anthropomorphic images is to 'bring to life' the human-shaped entity, though not through natural biological creation." Pygmalion is the literary distillation of that concern. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

Burnham is careful to mark what the happy ending conceals. Ovid himself notes the bargain is not "an altogether happy exchange": "elephant bone into lovely female flesh" buys mortality — "Deterioration and death through decay are the ultimate ends even after successful love-making." The myth's promise of life through art is shadowed by the fact that what is animated is thereby made *transitory*, whereas "static being is eternal security." `Directly stated`

The metaphor's optimism — creation crowned by Venus — also flatters the maker. Burnham elsewhere insists that the moving image (the automaton, the successor to Pygmalion's wish) is in fact "doomed to failure, not only by physical disintegration, but in the mind of the beholder, by a final sense of inconviction." The Pygmalion fantasy of the work that truly lives hides the pathos that animated creations never fully convince. `Interpretive synthesis`
