# The Inchworm in the Future Tense

## The Metaphor

"I am an inchworm moving with determination and purpose across the distance between now and later." Zuboff casts the willing self as an inchworm inching across the gap between the present and an imagined future, where every increment of distance it crosses is *annexed* — each step turns a patch of the unknown into known, settled ground. The image makes the will visible as labour: the slow, purposive, bodily work by which a free being closes the gap "between making promises and keeping them" and so reaches forward into the future tense.

This is the affirmative counter-image at the centre of chapter 11. Against surveillance capitalism's promise of *guaranteed outcomes* — a future delivered in advance through behavioral prediction and modification — Zuboff offers the inchworm: a creature that does not *receive* its future but *makes* it, transforming uncertainty into fact through its own determined effort. The metaphor is autobiographical in the text (Zuboff describes her own promise to finish the book as "my flag planted in the future tense") and is meant to model what every human being does whenever they will, promise, and act. `Directly stated`.

The inchworm's defining trait — that it must measure out the distance with its *own* body, span by span, with nothing carrying it — maps onto Zuboff's claim that the future is not given but *willed* into being. "This future will not exist without my capacity first to imagine its facts and then to will them into being." The annexation image is doing precise work: the worm does not abolish the unknown ahead of it; it *converts* what it has already crossed into the known, leaving the uncertainty of the present tense intact as "the necessary habitat of the present tense." `Directly stated`.

## What It Reveals

The metaphor makes thinkable a positive account of freedom that does not depend on certainty or control. The inchworm asserts "the right to the future tense" without claiming "total authority over the future, of course, only over my piece of it." Freedom here is not omniscience or mastery; it is the modest, embodied capacity to influence one's own piece of the future by making and keeping promises. This is the heart of `The Will to Will` and `The Right to the Future Tense`: the promise is "an anchor that girds me against the vagaries of my moods" and "a compass that steers my course toward a desired future that is not yet real." `Directly stated`.

It also reveals what surveillance capitalism *takes*. By framing the future as something a subject must *annex through effort*, the metaphor exposes the alternative — `The Uncontract` and the regime of guaranteed outcomes — as a usurpation of that effort. The right to the future tense "is not extinguished, but rather it is usurped: commandeered and accumulated by surveillance capital's exclusive claims on our futures." Where the inchworm wills its piece of the future, surveillance capital sells a future already settled, eliminating the very uncertainty in which willing occurs. `Directly stated`.

Finally, the image preserves a distinction Zuboff treats as foundational: in the absence of the freedom to will, "the future collapses into an infinite present of mere behavior, in which there can be no subjects and no projects: only objects." The inchworm is a *subject* precisely because it must will its way forward; a creature whose future is guaranteed would be reduced to an *object* of others' prediction — the stake of `Subject vs Object`. `Directly stated`.

## What It Hides or Distorts

Read too cheerfully, the inchworm risks overstating the sovereignty of the individual will. Zuboff is careful to hedge it: "Events may originate in energy sources outside my will and abruptly alter my course in ways that I can neither predict nor control. Indeed, they have already done so." The worm crawls in a world of weather, accident, and other wills; the metaphor's tidy image of territory steadily annexed can mask how much of the terrain is never the worm's to claim. The annexation framing may also carry a faint colonial overtone — a *conquering* of the unknown — that sits uneasily beside Zuboff's own insistence that "uncertainty is not chaos but rather the necessary habitat of the present tense," something to be inhabited rather than conquered. `Strongly implied`.

It can also individualize what Zuboff elsewhere treats as social. The chapter ties the right to the future tense not only to a solitary will but to "the joining of wills in mutual commitment to dialogue, problem solving, and empathy." A lone inchworm crossing its own distance can obscure that promises are *shared*, that the future tense is asserted "in mutual commitment," and that freedom is exercised among others rather than by an isolated self annexing private ground. `Strongly implied`.
