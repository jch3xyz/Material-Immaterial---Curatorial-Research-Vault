# The Music-Box Automata

## The Metaphor

The little figures that dance on top of a music box stand for all prior free-standing sculpture — and even contemporary Kinetic Art. They move, but their motion is a pattern "set in advance"; a message reaches them from the machinery, but it travels one way only and stops at the figures. They are "blind, deaf, and dumb." Burnham takes Norbert Wiener's illustration as the epigraph that opens Chapter Eight ("Robot and Cyborg Art") and makes it the keystone image of his argument that all sculpture before the cybernetic changeover was a one-way message — a "dead soul made alive through 'art' and prearranged mechanized motion."

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the entire Western tradition of free-standing sculpture, the eighteenth-century automaton, and twentieth-century Kinetic Art — objects that receive (or transmit) but cannot reciprocate.
- **Source domain:** Wiener's dancing music-box figures — animated by a mechanism, performing a "conventionalized pattern," cut off from any communication with the outer world except the single one-way channel from the box.

The mapping turns on the **direction of the message**. The music box sends; the figures only receive. By analogy, the statue, the mobile, the motorized kinetic work all deliver "one-way stimulation for the human party involved" but never enter into the "kind of interaction of two men talking." The metaphor sets up Burnham's contrast with the **cyborg** (the cybernetic organism as an art form) and its **reciprocated message** — the two-way, feedback-coupled relationship he forecasts as "the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture." `Directly stated`

## What It Reveals

It reframes the whole history of sculpture as a *communications* problem rather than a problem of form, material, or durability. Once the work is seen as a system that exchanges messages with its environment, its defect becomes legible in a single stroke: it cannot answer. Burnham generalizes Wiener's figures to "all free-standing sculpture beginning with the paleolithic age," diagnosing an "inherent defect, psychical and physical, in sculpture and automata — and even today's Kinetic Art — [that] they do not respond to man in any intelligent fashion." The image makes thinkable a criterion by which the celebrated kinetic "liveliness" of a Calder mobile or a Tinguely machine is exposed as still one-way — "this sculpture no more 'acts' than does a ball of yarn when it is batted and chased by a kitten." `Directly stated`

It also names the **yearning** that the whole tradition has carried: "to break down the psychic and physical barriers between art and living reality ... to furnish images capable of intelligent intercourse with their creators." The music-box figure is the measure of that unmet desire. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

By making *reciprocity* the sole criterion of life, the figure quietly devalues every non-responsive art form as a "dead soul" — a "blind, deaf, and dumb" carrier of a merely prearranged pattern. This privileges feedback and behavior over the affective, contemplative, or symbolic charge a static object can hold; the "message" of "the texture of a Henry Moore bronze" is granted, but only as a lesser, one-way kind. The metaphor's force depends on accepting Wiener's cybernetic equation of *living* with *two-way communicating*, so that the immense expressive history of unresponsive sculpture is recast as a long prehistory of a problem only the cyborg will solve. It also flattens the difference between a stone statue, a programmed automaton, and a motorized kinetic work — all collapsed into the same one-way category — which serves Burnham's teleology toward cybernetic art but obscures what each form does on its own terms. `Interpretive synthesis`
