# The Burglar's Meat

## The Metaphor

McLuhan's figure for content as decoy: "the 'content' of a medium is like the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind" (Ch. 1). The content of a medium is not what the medium *is about* but a lure tossed to occupy our critical attention — "the watchdog of the mind" — so that the medium itself can break in and do its restructuring work unseen. The image is the signature figure of the corollary [[The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium]]: because content is always another, more familiar medium, it makes an especially appetizing distraction (`Directly stated`).

## What It Compares

- **Target** → the "content" of any medium (a movie's novel or play, print's speech, the brand name spelled out in electric light) — always, in McLuhan's analysis, *another medium*.
- **Source domain** → the piece of meat a burglar carries to pacify a guard dog while he robs the house.
- **Mapping**: the watchdog → "the watchdog of the mind," our critical, evaluative attention; the burglar → the medium (the new technology entering "within the gates"); the meat → the content we fixate on debating as good or bad; the burglary → the medium's silent alteration of "the scale and form of human association and action." Crucially, the figure inverts the ordinary intuition about distraction: the meat does not merely *accompany* the theft but *enables* it — "The effect of the medium is made strong and intense just because it is given another medium as 'content'" (`Directly stated`).

## What It Reveals

It dramatizes McLuhan's master claim — [[The Medium Is the Message]] — as a scene of misdirection. Attention is a finite resource and a defensive one (the "watchdog"); whatever holds it cannot also be examining the thing that holds it. By naming our "conventional response to all media, namely that it is how they are used that counts," as "the numb stance of the technological idiot," McLuhan recasts the whole content-versus-use debate ([[The Content Obsession]]) as exactly the bone the burglar wants us gnawing (`Directly stated`). The metaphor also makes concealment *productive* rather than incidental: the medium is intensified, not merely accompanied, by the content it carries — so the more engrossing the program, the deeper and less resisted the formal restructuring it smuggles in. This is why [[Media as Extensions of Man|the electric light]], being "pure information" with no content, "escapes attention as a communication medium just because it has no 'content'" — no meat, no distraction, and so it is the limit case that exposes the rule (`Strongly implied`).

## What It Hides or Distorts

The figure casts the medium as a *thief* and the viewer as a duped *householder*, which loads the analysis with intentional malice and victimhood that McLuhan's larger argument does not actually claim — for him the medium's action is impersonal, subliminal, and pattern-level ("alter sense ratios or patterns of perception steadily and without any resistance"), not a planned heist. The image of a guard merely *distracted* also implies that the attention could, with vigilance, simply be redirected to catch the burglar — yet McLuhan elsewhere insists the spell "can occur immediately upon contact" and that media operate beneath opinion, so the watchdog is less distracted than constitutionally unable to perceive form at all. Finally, by treating content purely as a lure, the metaphor underwrites McLuhan's most contested move — relegating what a medium *carries* to something "as diverse as [it is] ineffectual" — which brackets out the politics and ethics of content that later information theorists in the vault ([[Information]]) decline to dismiss (`Interpretive synthesis`).
