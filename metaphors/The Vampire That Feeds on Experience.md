# The Vampire That Feeds on Experience

## The Metaphor

Surveillance capitalism is **a vampire that feeds on human experience**. Zuboff borrows the figure directly from Marx — who imaged industrial capital as undead, dead labor reanimated to drain the living worker — and gives it "an unexpected turn." The old vampire fed on *labor*; this one feeds on *experience itself*: "every aspect of every human's experience." The image names the [[Surveillance Capitalism|extraction imperative]] in a single, charged figure — the parasitic appetite at the core of the new economic logic. `Directly stated`

## What It Compares

| Source domain (the vampire) | Target (surveillance capitalism) |
|---|---|
| The undead — animate but not alive | A "parasitic and self-referential" economic logic, alive only by drawing on its host |
| Feeds on the blood of the living | Feeds on the data exhaust of lived human experience |
| Marx's original: feeds on **labor** (the worker's expended life-force) | Zuboff's twist: feeds on **experience** — not just the working hours but "every aspect of every human's experience" |
| Drains the host to sustain itself | Claims human experience "as free raw material" for translation into [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral data]] |

The decisive move is the substitution at the point of the bite. Marx's vampire is bounded by the wage relation: it takes the labor you sell it. Zuboff's takes what was never offered for sale — the whole of waking and unwaking life as raw material. `Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis`

## What It Reveals

- **The appetite is the point.** Calling the logic a vampire foregrounds the [[Surveillance Capitalism|extraction imperative]] — surveillance capitalism does not merely *use* data, it *needs* an ever-widening supply, the way the undead needs blood. This frames the book's later arc (the drive from the online world into "the one we call 'real'") as a feeding compulsion, not a strategy. `Strongly implied`
- **Continuity with the critique of capital.** By reviving *Marx's* image, Zuboff places surveillance capitalism inside the lineage of capitalist accumulation rather than treating it as a novel-but-benign tech phenomenon. It is a *mutation* of capital, still recognizably capital. `Directly stated`
- **The scope of the dispossession.** "Labor" → "experience" widens the field of what is taken from a portion of life (work) to the totality of it. The metaphor makes vivid that nothing is exempt — the [[Quantified Self|rendering of lived experience into data]] is the supply that keeps the creature fed. `Strongly implied`
- **Parasitism, not partnership.** The host gets nothing the vampire needs it to have; "digital connection is now a means to others' commercial ends." The figure strips away the rhetoric of connection-as-community. `Directly stated`

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **Agency and consent vanish into pure predation.** The vampire image makes extraction look like an attack on a passive victim, which can obscure the *voluntary self-exposure* that Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] foregrounds — the host that bares its own throat. Zuboff's economic account and Han's psychological one each supply what the other's master-image elides. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **It can over-naturalize the appetite.** A vampire feeds *because that is its nature*; but Zuboff insists surveillance capitalism is *historically conditioned* — made possible by the digital milieu and the neoliberal habitat, not metaphysically destined. The figure's air of inevitability sits in tension with her own claim that this logic "is not technologically inevitable." `Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis`
- **"Experience" is figured as substance.** Blood is a material thing drained from a body; "experience" is not straightforwardly a substance one can lose by the pint. The metaphor materializes what is actually rendered as an immaterial data-image — the very ambiguity the vault tracks under [[Material vs Immaterial]]. Is the host depleted, or only copied? `Interpretive synthesis`
