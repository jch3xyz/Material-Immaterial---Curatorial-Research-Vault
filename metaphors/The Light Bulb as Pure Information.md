# The Light Bulb as Pure Information

## The Metaphor

A glowing light bulb is pure information. Burnham closes his chapter on light as a sculpture medium by recalling Marshall McLuhan, who "pointed to a glowing light bulb and remarked that it radiated pure information—at least to those who understood its signal" (`Directly stated`). Burnham pairs this with Otto Piene's claim that "light, as Otto Piene has pointed out, is the incarnation of visible energy" (`Directly stated`), and draws from the two a single closing image: that art is reducing to its barest essences, where "increasingly pure energy and information seem to be the essences of art; all else is being dropped methodically by the wayside" (`Directly stated`).

The metaphor folds two figures into one. From McLuhan, the bulb is *information* — a radiated signal legible to those who can read it. From Piene, light is *energy made visible*. Burnham reads the convergence of the two as the destination of Light Art and, beyond it, of the art object as such: the work thinned to a glowing emission of energy and information with nothing material left over.

## What It Compares

- **Target:** the glowing light bulb — and, by extension, the work of art at the far end of its technological "ephemeralization."
- **Source domain:** *pure information* (a contentless signal radiated to a competent receiver) and *the incarnation of visible energy* (light as energy made perceptible).

The mapping runs: the bulb radiates → it transmits a signal; the signal is *pure* → it carries no material object, only information and energy; the receiver must *understand its signal* → meaning is a matter of decoding, not of handling a thing. Where a traditional sculpture is a mass occupying space, the glowing bulb is a flux — Piene's "visible energy" — that the viewer reads rather than confronts as matter. This is a direct convergence with the vault's existing McLuhan symbol [[The Electric Light]], where the same bulb is "pure information... a medium without a message" because it has no content of its own. Burnham, hearing McLuhan lecture, transplants that figure into the history of sculpture: the bulb is the emblem of the art object dissolving into [[Information]] and energy.

## What It Reveals

- **The trajectory toward immateriality.** The metaphor names what Burnham calls "the cultural trend toward intangibility." Light as a sculpture medium is "a further step in the 'ephemeralization' of the art object"; the bulb-as-information is that ephemeralization taken to its limit, where the object survives only as a radiated signal. It is the technological sibling of Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] — the same period's reading of the art object thinning toward the idea — and it sits squarely on the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
- **Energy and information as the residue of art.** By Burnham's reckoning the bulb shows what is left when everything inessential is stripped away: "pure energy and information seem to be the essences of art; all else is being dropped methodically by the wayside." The metaphor reveals the art object's matter as the dispensable part. (`Directly stated`.)
- **Signal requires a competent reader.** McLuhan's qualifier — "at least to those who understood its signal" — makes information relational: the bulb radiates meaning only to a receiver equipped to decode it. The metaphor reveals that "pure information" is not self-evident content but a signal awaiting interpretation. (`Directly stated`.)
- **The convergence of the immaterialist's two lineages.** Burnham's pairing of McLuhan (information) and Piene (visible energy) shows that the Light artist's program — which Burnham elsewhere in the chapter calls a recurrent "desire for immateriality" and a wish "toward an art of total immateriality" — and McLuhan's media theory arrive independently at the same image. (`Strongly implied`.)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **The bulb is not immaterial.** As the rest of the chapter insists, light in art "is a relatively inflexible and fragile medium... still wedded to glass tubing and cumbersome electrical equipment," "a system of variable life expectancy." The metaphor's "purity" hides the filament, the transformer, the power grid, and the electrical codes (Billy Apple's run-in with the City of New York inspector) — the obstinate materiality of "light and its modern mother, electricity." Calling the bulb "pure information" subtracts the apparatus that makes the signal possible. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
- **"Pure" smooths over the loss of the Other and of resistance.** The vault's Han cluster warns that information radiated without negativity becomes a [[Lightless Radiation]] and a [[Hyperinformation]] that "brings no light into darkness." The Burnham–McLuhan figure celebrates exactly the frictionless transmission Han indicts; the metaphor's optimism hides what is occluded when art becomes signal — the difficulty, opacity, and material weight that resist instant legibility. (`Speculative connection`.)
- **It collapses energy and information into one word.** Piene's "visible energy" and McLuhan's "information" are not the same thing; the metaphor fuses a thermodynamic figure (light as radiated energy) with a communicational one (light as signal) and treats the seam as seamless. The reduction is rhetorically powerful but elides the difference between *radiating power* and *carrying a message*. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
