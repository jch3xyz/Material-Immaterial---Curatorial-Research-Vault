# The Word as Virus

## The Metaphor

"The word is now a virus." In William Burroughs's *The Ticket That Exploded*, as read by N. Katherine Hayles in [[The Materiality of Informatics]], language is figured not as something that resembles a virus but as one literally: a parasitic organism that replicates through the human host, appropriates the body's vocal apparatus, and erupts into flesh as visible infestation. The interior monologue — the story the self tells itself to assure itself that it exists — is the symptom of this infection; the tape-recorder is both the diagnostic instrument that externalizes it and the metaphor for the body that carries it. The figure's whole force lies in Burroughs's "insistent pressure to take metaphors literally," collapsing the distance between word and thing on which ordinary metaphor depends.

## What It Compares

The metaphor maps the source domain of *viral / parasitic infection* onto the target of *language and discourse*:

- **word → virus / parasite**: "the word" is the body's "Other Half," "a separate organism attached to your nervous system on an air line of words," replicating through the host the way a virus replicates through a cell.
- **the body → host organism / tape-recorder**: the corporeal frame is the medium the word infests and, by metonymic extension, a magnetic-tape machine "regulated by the principles that govern magnetic tape in its reproduction, erasure, and reconfiguration."
- **social conditioning → "pre-recordings"**: ideology, upbringing, and sexual frustration are figured as taped "pre-recordings" entwined into the flesh, "parasites ready to take over the organism."
- **infection's symptom → the interior monologue**: the compulsion to speak ("Try to achieve even ten seconds of inner silence") is read as proof that an alien organism — the word — has colonized the self.

Crucially, Hayles uses Burroughs to *invert* the postmodern direction of causation. Against the orthodoxy that the body is "primarily, if not entirely, a linguistic and discursive construction" — discourse dematerializing the body — Burroughs has "the body materialize discourse." The word does not dissolve the flesh into signs; it *becomes* flesh, "visible as green fish in the flesh and crab parasites tearing at the base of the spine and brain."

## What It Reveals

- **The materiality of informatics.** The figure is the centerpiece of Hayles's demonstration that information and discourse are never disembodied. By making "language erupt directly into the body," Burroughs forces the reader to see that words have material effects, that signification is carried in a corporeal medium, and that an ideology of dematerialization (Baudrillard, the Krokers, Hardison) "depends in complex and highly specific ways on the embodied circumstances" it would obscure. The metaphor is thus a counter-figure to the dream of [[The Body as Data|the body disappearing into information]] and a corporeal complement to the [[Flickering Signifier]].
- **Voice severed from presence.** Situated in the "high technology (for its time)" of magnetic audiotape, the virus figure reveals how a medium can cut "voice" loose from "presence." Where telephone and radio still "happened in the present," tape made voice "at once permanent and mutable," so that a speaker could be made to "hear himself say the opposite of what he knows he said." Once "the body became a tape-recorder," voice is no longer "a naturalized union of voice and presence but... a mechanical production with the frightening ability to appropriate the body's vocal apparatus and use it for ends alien to the self."
- **Inscription merging with incorporation.** The figure stages exactly the dynamic interplay Hayles theorizes in [[Inscription vs Incorporation]]: in the "Exhibition" inside the tape-recorder, "inscriptions fall from the walls to become corporeal 'word dust,'" and "incorporations are transformed into inscriptions through video- and audio-recording devices." The virus is what passes back and forth across that membrane — a "cyborg configuration of explosive potential."
- **A harbinger of the posthuman.** The "mutating and metamorphosing bodies" the virus produces are "a harbinger of the posthuman body." The word-virus reconfigures the tape-recorder "as a cybernetic technology capable of radically transforming bodies and subjectivities," dissolving the boundary between observer and system that underwrote the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]]. (See [[Posthumanism]].)

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It universalizes by collapsing the metaphor.** Taken literally, "word is virus" risks erasing the very specificity of [[Embodiment]] that Hayles elsewhere insists on — that "there is no body as such; there are only bodies." The figure operates on a generic, fetal "divide line" common to "human flesh" as such; the heterogeneity of bodies "fissuring along lines of class, gender, race, and privilege" can fade into a single infected organism, repeating in another key the abstraction Hayles criticizes in Foucault's universalized Panoptic body.
- **It is self-infecting and can become what it fights.** Hayles stresses that the metaphor distorts by its own contagion: Burroughs "is acutely aware of the danger that he might, through his words, spread the viral infection he is trying to combat." "The reifying and infective power of words can be defused only through other words, which can always turn against their master and become infectious in turn." The cure ("vaccine") and the disease share a single mechanism — "Making the word flesh is both how the virus infects and how the vaccine disinfects" — so the figure cannot cleanly separate liberation from co-optation.
- **It tilts toward subversion over creation.** Because any positive content can only be voiced in words, and words reinfect, "the emphasis remains on subversion and disruption rather than creative rearticulation." The figure illuminates how conditioning is broken but distorts the prospects of building a new subjectivity: the one hopeful image, the "fish boy," can be represented "only in words, which would infect and destroy exactly the transformation they were attempting to describe."
- **It pathologizes signification as such.** By rendering the ordinary fact of inner speech as parasitic invasion ("Modern man has lost the option of silence"), the metaphor can overstate language as alien occupation rather than as a constitutive, enculturating practice — flattening the more nuanced point that "the body produces culture at the same time that culture produces the body."
