# Now We Are the Animals

## The Metaphor

"All that has changed is that now we are the animals." Zuboff opens the reality business with a scene of origin: a 1964 Galapagos expedition in which "a giant tortoise stirred from her torpor to swallow a succulent chunk of cactus into which a dedicated scientist had wedged a small machine." The science of biological telemetry — devised to surveil free-roaming creatures in their natural habitat, with sensors so compact they "disappear into the body without triggering the animal's awareness" — becomes the genealogical template for surveillance capitalism's instrumentation of human life. The human subject is figured as the elk, the sea turtle, the goose: an "unrestrained" creature rendered as information even while it "believed itself to be free, wandering and resting, unaware of the incursion."

## What It Compares

- **Target:** human beings whose behavior is continuously instrumented, datafied, and predicted under `[[Surveillance Capitalism]]` — "individuals and groups as so many objects to be continuously tracked, wholly known, and shunted this way or that for some purpose of which they are unaware."
- **Source domain:** wild animals studied by biomedical telemetry — elk herds, sea turtles, geese, the Galapagos tortoise and marine iguana — surveilled in the field because caging them "would only eliminate the very behavior that scientists wanted to study."

The mapping is exact, and Zuboff draws it explicitly:
- the wedged, swallowed **machine** that disappears into the tortoise's body ↔ the ubiquitous sensors of the **"apparatus"** woven into car, kitchen, bed, body ("Today those regions include the front seat of your car, your kitchen, and your kidneys")
- the scientist's principle that telemetry "operated **outside an animal's awareness**" ↔ surveillance capitalism's operation "outside the driver's awareness while she still thinks that she is free"
- the rendering of **"unrestrained animals"** as data in "inaccessible regions" ↔ `[[Rendition]]` of human experience from "previously inaccessible regions"
- telemetry's **"reverse process" of telestimulation** ↔ the `[[Economies of Action]]` that "automatically stimulate behavior"
- MacKay's dream of **"whole populations of connected data-emitting individuals"** ↔ the population-scale correlation that feeds prediction

The hinge of the figure is *captured freedom*: the animal is not penned but left to roam — its natural, unforced behavior is precisely the prize, because only un-coerced behavior yields true knowledge. `Directly stated` — Zuboff writes "now we are the animals" verbatim and lays out the MacKay genealogy as the literal origin of the surveillance-capitalist method.

## What It Reveals

- **The continuity of method.** The figure denies surveillance capitalism the alibi of novelty: its core technique was perfected on wildlife sixty years ago. "The science of animal tracking... became the template for surveillance capitalism's next phase of evolution." What looks like inevitable twenty-first-century life is a "refurbished" solution once "concocted by scholars of elk herds, sea turtles, and geese." `Directly stated`.
- **Why freedom must be preserved to be harvested.** Caging the animal destroys the behavior worth studying; therefore the subject must be left feeling free. The metaphor exposes the perverse structure by which surveillance capitalism *protects* a sensation of liberty precisely in order to extract authentic, un-coerced behavior — "outside the driver's awareness while she still thinks that she is free." `Directly stated`.
- **The demotion of the human to specimen.** To be "the animals" is to be reclassified out of the category of agent, citizen, or end-in-oneself and into the category of *object of study* — a wild population to be known, managed, and culled for purposes never disclosed to it. This is the affective core of Zuboff's larger claim that rendition strips world, self, and body of moral standing, reducing the body to "a set of coordinates in time and space." `Strongly implied`.
- **Surveillance as the price of knowledge.** Zuboff's narration of the scientists who "concluded that surveillance was the necessary price of knowledge" reveals the epistemic justification that surveillance capital inherits and weaponizes: total knowledge requires total observation. `Directly stated`.

## What It Hides or Distorts

- **It naturalizes domination as care.** MacKay is shown "poised tenderly" beside the tortoise, holding the iguana "gently"; he "viewed telemetry as a means of enhancing and protecting the well-being of animal populations." The benevolent-scientist origin can soften the figure — implying that those who instrument us mean us well — when Zuboff's point is that the *method* migrated to ends that "heighten the predictability of behavior," not to "save the human herd from catastrophe." `Directly stated` (the tenderness is in the text; reading it as a softening risk is `Interpretive synthesis`).
- **It can flatten human agency into animal passivity.** Wild animals cannot read a privacy policy, consent, organize, or refuse. Casting us as "the animals" dramatizes the *taking* but underplays the human capacities for awareness, contestation, and political resistance that Zuboff's book is ultimately written to mobilize — the very "human will as the author of the future" her argument defends. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **It implies the herd is unified and interchangeable.** Telemetry studies populations as statistical aggregates; the figure can erase the social, moral, and historical particularity of human lives — exactly what `[[Rendition]]` accomplishes when "each rendered bit is liberated from its life in the social." The metaphor risks reproducing the flattening it diagnoses. `Strongly implied`.
- **It grants the method an air of scientific neutrality.** Originating the technique in field biology lends it the authority of disinterested science, which can obscure that its contemporary form serves "hidden economic objectives," not knowledge for its own sake. `Speculative connection`.
