# Byung-Chul Han

Korean-German philosopher and cultural critic working out of the German philosophical tradition. Han writes short, aphoristic books that diagnose the affective and informational pathologies of late neoliberal capitalism by appropriating and re-tuning a fixed canon of interlocutors — Hegel on the negative, Heidegger on dwelling and *Erfahrung*, Benjamin on aura and storytelling, Baudrillard on the obscene and the end of the panoptic gaze, Foucault on discipline and biopolitics, Sennett on opacity and autonomy, Nietzsche on suffering and the will to ignorance. Within the vault he is represented by [[The Transparency Society]] and [[Psychopolitics]]; the `raw/` folder also contains [[The Burnout Society]], which has not yet been processed. The two ingested books are continuous: *Psychopolitics* carries the [[Positivity]]/[[Negativity]], [[Digital Panopticon]], [[Transparency]], and [[Auto-Exploitation]] vocabulary of *The Transparency Society* into an explicit theory of neoliberal power — the regime that no longer disciplines the body but steers the [[Psychopolitics|psyche]].

## Central Concerns

Han's diagnostic targets in *The Transparency Society* — and, by his own cross-referencing, across his work — are the disappearance of [[Negativity]] under a regime of compulsive [[Positivity]]; the conversion of [[Discipline|disciplinary society]] into an [[Achievement Society|achievement society (Leistungsgesellschaft)]] of self-exploiting subjects; and the mutation of [[The Panopticon|Bentham's panopticon]] into a [[Digital Panopticon|digital, aperspectival panopticon]] in which the watched freely illuminate themselves. He treats [[Transparency]] not as a virtue but as "a neoliberal dispositive" that "forces everything inward in order to transform it into information" (`Directly stated`, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/03 Preface|Preface]]).

## Major Concepts

## Disagreements or Tensions

- [[Transparency vs Trust]] — Han argues the two are antithetical: trust depends on "a state between knowing and not knowing" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/12 Ch9 - Control|Ch9]]). This positions him against the "new dogma" that "transparency creates trust" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/03 Preface|Preface]]). Polemic target: contemporary public discourse generally; David Brin's "transparent society" specifically.
- [[Discipline vs Achievement|Disciplinary Society vs Achievement Society]] — Han revises Foucault: the panopticon has not been abolished but interiorized. The achievement-subject "exploits itself" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/12 Ch9 - Control|Ch9]]).
- [[Panopticon Continuity vs End]] — Han disagrees with Baudrillard's 1978 obituary for the panoptic system: "we are not experiencing the end of the panopticon, but rather the beginning of an entirely new, aperspectival panopticon" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/12 Ch9 - Control|Ch9]]).
- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the underlying structural tension of Han's diagnosis ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/04 Ch1 - Positivity|Ch1]]).
- [[Aphoristic Appropriation as Method]] — `Interpretive synthesis`. Han's polemical method is to quote a canonical figure in a single block and then bend the citation toward his own diagnosis (Hegel on the negative becoming a critique of "surfing"; Baudrillard's end-of-panopticon becoming a beginning; Schmitt's *arcanum* becoming a defense of opacity). Not asserted by Han, but visible across the chapters cited.

## Psychopolitics (2026-06-02 ingest)

*Psychopolitics: Neoliberalism and New Technologies of Power* (trans. Erik Butler) extends the *Transparency Society* corpus into an explicit theory of power. Its thesis: neoliberalism does not repress freedom but **exploits** it; power has migrated from the disciplinary **body** ([[Biopolitics]]) to the neoliberal **soul** ([[Psychopolitics]]), governing through motivation, emotion, debt, and prediction rather than commandment and prohibition. The book is at once a diagnosis (Ch. 1–11) and a search for a counter-figure — the de-subjectivated, silent, immanent **idiot** (Ch. 12–13). `Directly stated`, [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/03 The Mole and the Snake.md|Ch3]]: "the disciplinary regime organizes itself as a 'body'. It is a biopolitical regime. The neoliberal regime, in contrast, seems like a 'soul'. As such, psychopolitics is its form of government".

### Central concerns this book adds

The crisis of freedom (freedom itself now "bringing forth compulsion and constraint"); the conversion of [[Discipline|disciplinary power]] into [[Smart Power|smart, friendly, permissive power]]; the colonization of the [[The Psyche as Productive Force|psyche as a productive force]]; the exploitation of [[Emotional Capitalism|emotion]] and [[Gamification|play]]; the rise of [[Big Data]] / [[Dataism]] as a [[The Second Enlightenment|second Enlightenment]] that predicts and pre-empts free will; and, against all of this, [[Idiotism]] and a [[Beyond the Subject|life beyond the subject]] as a [[De-psychologization|praxis of freedom]].

### Major concepts introduced

- [[Psychopolitics]] — the book's title concept: neoliberal power that "exploits the psyche above all," steering and pre-empting the will. "Today, we are entering the age of digital psychopolitics. It means passing from passive surveillance to active steering" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/01 The Crisis of Freedom.md|Ch1]]). `Directly stated`.
- [[Biopolitics]] — realized here as a Han-anchored note (Foucauldian provenance): "the governmental technology of disciplinary power," bounded by its limit — it "has no access to the psychic realm" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/04 Biopolitics.md|Ch4]]). Defined against psychopolitics.
- [[Smart Power]] — permissive, seductive, invisible power that "cosies up to the psyche rather than disciplining it"; "Neoliberalism is the capitalism of 'Like'" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/02 Smart Power.md|Ch2]]). `Directly stated`.
- [[Big Data]] / [[Dataism]] — the psychopolitical instrument that draws a "psychogram of the unconscious" and "has announced the end of the person who possesses free will" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/01 The Crisis of Freedom.md|Ch1]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/11 Big Data.md|Ch11]]).
- [[The End of Theory]] / [[Spirit (Geist)]] — Han's Hegelian rebuttal of Anderson: correlation lacks the Concept, so "totalized data-knowledge amounts to absolute ignorance: the absolute zero of Spirit" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/11 Big Data.md|Ch11]]). `Directly stated`.
- [[The Digital Unconscious]] / [[Quantified Self]] / [[Micro-targeting]] / [[The Ban-opticon]] — the apparatus of digital psychopolitics: "the digitalized, networked subject is a panopticon of itself" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/11 Big Data.md|Ch11]]).
- [[Emotional Capitalism]] / [[Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect (Han)]] — capitalism exploits "the performativity of emotion"; "today we do not consume things so much as emotions" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/09 Emotional Capitalism.md|Ch9]]).
- [[Gamification]] / [[Human Capital]] — play captured as a productivity engine ("gamification is destroying play's potential to set free"); free time recaptured so that "human beings… are now transforming themselves into Capital" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/10 Gamification.md|Ch10]]).
- [[Self-Optimization]] / [[The Age of Exhaustion]] — the therapeutic imperative as total self-exploitation, inaugurating the "age of exhaustion" of depression and burnout ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/05 Foucault's Dilemma.md|Ch5]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/06 Healing as Killing.md|Ch6]]).
- [[The Project (Han)]] / [[Allo-Exploitation]] / [[Obedience-Subject]] — the self-fashioning "project" that re-subjugates itself; the obedience-subject of sovereign/disciplinary *allo-exploitation*, antonym of the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]]'s [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploitation]] ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/01 The Crisis of Freedom.md|Ch1]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/04 Biopolitics.md|Ch4]]).
- [[Capital as Religion]] / [[Dictatorship of Capital]] — Capital as "a new God, making us guilty and debt-ridden"; debt (*Schuld*) as an instrument of domination ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/01 The Crisis of Freedom.md|Ch1]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/03 The Mole and the Snake.md|Ch3]]).
- [[Immunological Age]] — shock as a *disciplinary*, negative, immunological technique, mis-taken by Klein for the logic of neoliberalism ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/07 Shock.md|Ch7]]).
- [[Idiotism]] / [[The Idiot (Han)]] / [[Singularity (Han)]] / [[Heresy as Choice]] / [[Politics of Silence]] — the closing counter-figure: "the idiot is unallied, un-networked, and uninformed," a "practice of freedom" against the "violence of consensus" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/13 Idiotism.md|Ch13]]).
- [[Beyond the Subject]] / [[De-psychologization]] / [[The Event]] / [[The Art of Living]] / [[Immanence and A Life]] — the praxis of freedom as "the art of killing psychology," opening "a mode of existence that still has no name" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/12 Beyond the Subject.md|Ch12]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/13 Idiotism.md|Ch13]]).

- [[Antonio Negri]] — disagreement: the "Multitude" "does not describe the contemporary mode of production" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/01 The Crisis of Freedom.md|Ch1]]). See [[Byung-Chul Han and Antonio Negri]].
- [[Jeremy Bentham]] — the optical, perspectival panopticon Big Data supersedes ("Cameron's hospital was a panopticon far crueller than Bentham's"; the digital panopticon is *aperspectival*) ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/04 Biopolitics.md|Ch4]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/07 Shock.md|Ch7]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/11 Big Data.md|Ch11]]). See [[Byung-Chul Han and Jeremy Bentham]].

### Disagreements or tensions introduced

- [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] — power over the body/population vs. power over the psyche; "Demography is not the same thing as psych-ography" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/04 Biopolitics.md|Ch4]]).
- [[Subject vs Project]] / [[Allo-Exploitation vs Auto-Exploitation]] / [[Can vs Should]] — the periodizing oppositions of the neoliberal turn ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/01 The Crisis of Freedom.md|Ch1]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/03 The Mole and the Snake.md|Ch3]]).
- [[Repression vs Seduction]] / [[Visibility vs Invisibility of Power]] — disciplinary "no" vs. smart power's "yes"; "the greater power is, the more quietly it works" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/02 Smart Power.md|Ch2]]).
- [[Rationality vs Emotionality]] — the medium of disciplinary society vs. the "dictatorship of emotion" of emotional capitalism ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/09 Emotional Capitalism.md|Ch9]]).
- [[Average vs Event]] / [[Correlation vs Causation]] — statistical sameness vs. the singular event; probability vs. the Concept ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/11 Big Data.md|Ch11]]).
- [[Immanence vs Transcendence]] / [[Intelligence vs Idiotism]] / [[Silence vs Communication]] — the book's affirmative poles: "A Life" against Capital-as-transcendence; the idiot's vertical outside against system-immanent intelligence ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/10 Gamification.md|Ch10]], [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/13 Idiotism.md|Ch13]]).
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — extended with a Han/labour inflection: "What gets produced are not material objects, but immaterial ones - for instance, information and programs" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/05 Foucault's Dilemma.md|Ch5]]).
- [[Freedom vs Control]] / [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the book's governing axes, now applied to *power* rather than information: neoliberal psychopolitics "is dominated by positivity" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/07 Shock.md|Ch7]]).
