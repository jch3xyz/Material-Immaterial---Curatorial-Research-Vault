# Roy Ascott

Roy Ascott is the British artist, theorist, and art-educator whose collected essays — written across four decades and gathered as *[[Telematic Embrace]]: Visionary Theories of Art, Technology, and Consciousness* (ed. Edward A. Shanken; Berkeley: University of California Press, 2003) — trace a single sustained migration: from the cybernetic, participatory "behaviourist" art of the 1960s, through the network art and "telematic culture" of the 1980s and 1990s, to the "moistmedia" and technoetics of the new century. This note covers Ascott as he appears in *Telematic Embrace*. The book is an *edited anthology* of his own writings; quotations attributed to Ascott are taken verbatim from his essays. The editor's framing apparatus (chapter 04, Shanken's long introduction) is **not** Ascott's voice and is not quoted here as such.

## Central Concerns

Ascott is preoccupied with the dissolution of the art *object* into the art *behaviour* and then into the art *network*. From his earliest manifesto-essays he holds that "the vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour, and its function has become less descriptive and more purposive," and that "process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure." His name for the integrative science this art must adopt is cybernetics: "to fully orientate himself in the modern world, the artist must turn to science as a tool and reference. I recommend that he turn to cybernetics." The participatory consequence runs through every phase: the artwork is "a matrix, the substance between" artist and spectator, and "the effort to establish a final resolution must come from the observer," so that the spectator becomes "a decision maker" who is "responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning."

His later concern is the [[The Telematic Embrace|telematic embrace]] — the convergence of "the two powerful technologies of computing and telecommunications... into one field of operations" — and what it does to consciousness, authorship, and the self. In [[Telematic Art|telematic art]], "meaning is not created by the artist, distributed through the network, and received by the observer. Meaning is the product of interaction between the observer and the system." From this Ascott builds toward a [[Network Consciousness|network consciousness]] in which "a telematic network is more than the sum of its parts," toward [[Cyberception (Ascott)|cyberception]] as "a whole new faculty," and finally toward [[Moistmedia (Ascott)|moistmedia]] and [[Technoetics (Ascott)|technoetics]] — "techne and gnōsis combined into a new knowledge of the world" — at the convergence of "art, technology, and consciousness." The throughline is reversible: art's whole tendency is the immaterialization of its object into "pure electronic différance," tested "within the immaterial, virtual hyper realities of data space."

## Major Concepts

- [[Behaviourist Art]] — Ascott's name for the art whose subject is behaviour rather than the object: "We recognise process at the human level as behaviour, and we are beginning to understand art now as being essentially behaviourist." "Change is what we are all about... That is the essence of behaviourist art, and generating change is the aim of the behaviourist artist." See [[Behaviourist Art (Ascott)]]. (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Cybernetic Vision]] — the unifying lens of his 1966–67 essay: art "characterised by an evolving behavioural tendency" that "evidences our present transition from the old deterministic culture to a future shaped by a cybernetic vision." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Telematic Art]] — art residing "in a cultural communications system rather than in the art object as a fixed semantic configuration"; it "encompasses a wide array of media: hypermedia, videotex, telefacsimile, interactive video, computer animation and simulation, teleconferencing, text exchange, image transfer." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the converged field of computing and telecommunications, named in the question "Is there love in the telematic embrace?"; "the telematic embrace releases feelings of solidarity and co-operation." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the planetary mind emerging from the net: "a telematic network is more than the sum of its parts... The new order of perception it constitutes can be called 'global vision.'" (`Directly stated`)
- [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — CAM, "a technological and social framework for the furtherance of this vision"; "essentially a futurible — anticipatory and speculative." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Futuribles]] — the alternative futures art generates: "we are very much concerned with generating futuribles — maybe that's because the more we can dream up alternative futures, the more changeable the present can become." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Psychotechnology]] — the carrier of the post-industrial future: "In the post-industrial society, it is not technology that will carry us through so much as psychotechnology." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Cybernated Society]] — the social horizon of his early work: "Cybernation is bringing about a total industrial revolution, which will have far reaching social consequences." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Distributed Authorship]] — "The emerging new order of art is that of interactivity, of 'dispersed authorship'... Creativity is shared, authorship is distributed, but not in a way that denies the individual her authenticity." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Participator]] — the upgraded spectator: "the observer in an interactive telematic system is by definition a participator"; "the viewer/observer must be a participator and is of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Change Painting]] — Ascott's own early works: "structures in which the relationships of parts are not fixed and may be changed by the intervention of the spectator," intended "to function as analogues of ideas — structures that are subject to change and human intervention in the way that ideas themselves are." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Groundcourse]] — his radical art pedagogy at Ealing and the OCA: "a flexible structure within which everything can find its place, every individual his way... an art that does not eschew science," where students "acquir[e] and act[] out for a limited period... a totally new personality." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Artwork as Matrix and Catalyst]] — "The work of art occupies a pivotal point between two sets of behaviour, the artist's and the spectator's. It is essentially a matrix, the substance between"; art is "a catalyst that sets up patterns of behaviour." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Thermodynamics of an Artwork]] — his hot/cool reworking of McLuhan: "An artwork is hot when it is densely stacked with information bits, highly organised, and rigidly determined... Call it cool when the information bits are loosely stacked, of uncertain order... Then the system allows the observer to participate." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Cyberception (Ascott)]] — "the postbiological faculty of 'cyberception'... a convergence of conceptual and perceptual processes in which the connectivity of telematic networks plays a formative role." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Moistmedia (Ascott)]] — "Moistmedia (comprising bits, atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination) will constitute the substrate of the art of our new century." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Technoetics (Ascott)]] — "technoetic: techne and gnōsis combined into a new knowledge of the world, a connective mind that is spawning new realities and new definitions of life and human identity." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Telematics (Ascott)]] · [[Cyberspace (Ascott)]] · [[Cybernetics (Ascott)]] · [[Behaviourist Art (Ascott)]] — the definition notes anchoring Ascott's key terms.
- [[Connective Criticism]] · [[A Field Theory of Art]] · [[Network as Artwork]] · [[Psibernetics]] — the critical-and-theoretical apparatus he proposes around the work.
- [[New Media Art]] · [[Art as Experiential Technology]] · [[Cyborg Art]] · [[Systems Esthetics]] · [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the cross-author / committed-stub notes (realized by Burnham) that Ascott's telematic and behaviourist art **extends** from the object→system spine into the network.

## Major Arguments

- [[Art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour]] — "The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour, and its function has become less descriptive and more purposive." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Process replaces product just as system supersedes structure]] — "When art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware in the creative sphere. Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The artist must turn to cybernetics as the integrative science for art]] — "to fully orientate himself in the modern world, the artist must turn to science as a tool and reference. I recommend that he turn to cybernetics... Cybernetics, however, is essentially integrative, drawing many disparate sciences together... It is a coordinator of science, as art is the coordinator of experience." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The spectator completes the artwork in a feedback loop]] — "A feedback loop is established, so that the evolution of the artwork/experience is governed by the intimate involvement of the spectator"; "this loop... makes an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork/observer." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Telematic networks can create a consciousness greater than the sum of its parts]] — "A telematic network is more than the sum of its parts, more than a computer communications web." Quoting Peter Russell: "the billions of information exchanges... can create coherence in the global brain, similar to those found in the human brain." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The decentralized structure of telematic networks is subversive]] — the holomatic "Planetary Network" project "had the effect of pulling the exhibition from its rather elite, centralised, and exclusive domain in Venice and stretching it out over the face of the globe"; in telematic culture "the polarities of maker and user become destabilized." (`Strongly implied`)
- [[The post-industrial future will be carried by psychotechnology]] — "In the post-industrial society, it is not technology that will carry us through so much as psychotechnology. That may take us beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering into the shadow lands, the futuribles." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object]] — "Object hustlers! Reduce your anxiety! Process culture and behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object, as long as it means the beginning of new values for art." (`Directly stated`)

## Characteristic Metaphors

- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the governing figure of the later work: the convergence of computing and telecommunications imagined as an embrace, even as love — "the metaphor of love in the telematic embrace may not be entirely misplaced." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The Thermodynamics of an Artwork]] — heat and cooling as a measure of a work's openness to feedback: an overheated work "switches to a very cool state and feedback of a high order is possible." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Psibernetics]] — the "rainbow arch" bridging cybernetics and parapsychology: "an arch bridging two apparently opposed spheres: cybernetics and parapsychology... technology and telepathy; provision and prevision; cyb and psi." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Trance and Automatism]] — his account of making at "the level of trance," "that kind of open, unconstrained but intense process... induced by random behaviour," the board "the arena for any kind of force, just as the ouija board seems to elicit information from a deep psychic level." (`Directly stated`)
- *The semantic sea / electronic différance* — meaning in telematic art figured as "a semantic sea, endlessly ebbing and flowing, of meaning constantly in flux," content "pure electronic différance, until it has been reconstituted at the interface." (Quoted under [[Telematic Art]].) (`Directly stated`)
- *The noosphere / subtle body* — the net as "a psychic envelope for the planet, a telematic 'noosphere'" — Ascott's adoption of [[Teilhard de Chardin]] and [[Gregory Bateson|Bateson's]] "mind at large." (Quoted under [[Network Consciousness]].) (`Directly stated`)

## Related Authors

- [[Jack Burnham]] — *Interpretive synthesis*: the vault's keystone Ascott pairing. Burnham's 1968 [[Object and System|object→system]] thesis and his "Cyborg Art (the art of cybernetic organisms)" are the immediate precursor to Ascott's behaviourist-then-telematic art; Ascott's [[Behaviourist Art|behaviourist art]] *extends* the committed stubs [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], [[Cyborg Art]], [[Reciprocal Communication]] and the field of [[Systems Esthetics]] that Burnham realized. Both turn the art object into [[Material vs Immaterial|immaterial]] system; Burnham tracks sculpture's obliteration into the system, Ascott carries the system out into the network. See [[Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham]].
- [[Marshall McLuhan]] — Ascott explicitly takes up McLuhan: he reworks hot/cool media into [[The Thermodynamics of an Artwork|the thermodynamics of an artwork]] and writes "as with TV, so with ESP, the medium is the message." The cybernet "redefines our individual body, just as it connects all our bodies into a planetary whole" — McLuhan's "extension of the central nervous system" carried into [[Network Consciousness]]. See [[Roy Ascott and Marshall McLuhan]].
- [[N. ## Disagreements or Tensions

- [[Process vs Product]] — the organizing opposition of the early manifestos: the provisional, behaviour-generating process against the finished, "rigidly determined" art object — "Process replaces product in importance."
- [[Connection vs Narcissism]] — telematic connectivity and "dispersed authorship" set against the vertical, mirror-like, self-regarding tradition Ascott opposes ("the painting on the wall looks back only at us"); the horizontal, all-embracing "telenoia of universal connectivity" against "the industrial age of paranoia."
- [[Liberation vs Control (Telematics)]] — the network read as emancipatory ("telematic culture amplifies the individual's capacity for creative thought and action") against the fear "of the machine coming to dominate the human will and of a technological formalism erasing human content and values"; Ascott embraces a "telematic politics, embodying the features of feedback, self-determination, interaction."
- [[Formalist Modernism (Ascott)]] · [[Postmodernist Art (Ascott)]] — Ascott's polemic against "the formalist modernist aesthetic": formalist modernism "was not connective, inclusive, transactional... it ignored autobiographical data," "explicitly a one-way street, stylistically irreversible," while postmodernist art "presents a connective paradigm, which in turn demands a connective criticism."
- [[Cyb vs Psi]] — the two sides of the "psibernetic arch": "cybernetics and parapsychology... cyb and psi" — "ESP is instant, but sporadic. Cybernetics is universal, but entropic" — which Ascott seeks to unite rather than oppose.
