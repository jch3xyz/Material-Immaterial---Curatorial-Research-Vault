# Lucy Lippard

Lucy R. Lippard (b. 1937) is the critic, curator, and editor who compiled *[[Six Years]]: The Dematerialization of the Art Object from 1966 to 1972*. She coined (with [[John Chandler]]) the term "dematerialization" in the 1968 essay "The Dematerialization of Art," and her annotated chronicle of conceptual art's emergence is the central primary-source document of the period. This note covers her work as it appears in *Six Years*; her later, sustained feminist and political activism and writing fall largely outside this book.

## Books in the Vault

- [[Six Years]]

## Central Concerns

Lippard is preoccupied with the moment when, between 1966 and 1972, the art object thinned out — when "the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized.'" She is concerned with conceptual art as a set of escape attempts from the "frame-and-pedestal syndrome" and from commodity status, and with whether such art could escape the market at all (her own answer, by 1973, is that it could not). Across the book she is also a chronicler of her own politicization: a trip to Argentina in 1968 "belatedly radicalized" her, and she increasingly reads the art-and-politics relationship as inescapable — "everything, even art, exists in a political situation." She is explicit that her account is partial and personal: "My own version is inevitably tempered by my feminist and left politics."

Her method is doubled. She is both **narrator** (the framing essays — "Escape Attempts," the Preface, the Postface) and **chronicler** (the year-by-year annotated bibliography of 1966–1972). She calls the result "an occasionally critical memoir," distrusts hindsight, and so "quote[s] myself a lot," reprinting her own period statements unrevised so that "things look[ed] then." This makes *Six Years* an avowedly biased, memoir-based, anti-individualistic compilation rather than a neutral history.

## Major Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Lippard's "dematerialization" is the art-historical instance of this tension: a "deemphasis on material aspects (uniqueness, permanence, decorative)" rather than a literal disappearance of matter. She concedes the term is "inaccurate" — "a piece of paper or a photograph is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead" — but keeps it "for lack of a better term."
- [[New Media Art]] — the "inexpensive, ephemeral, unintimidating" conceptual mediums (video, performance, photography, narrative, text, actions) she catalogues are forerunners of later media-based art practice.
- [[Information]] — for the conceptualists Lippard tracks, "information and systems replaced traditional formal concerns of composition, color, technique, and physical presence"; she frames criticism itself as the accurate transmission of information about art.
- [[Systems Theory]] — she quotes [[Hans Haacke]]'s "working premise is to think in terms of systems," and notes McShine's 1970 MoMA "Information" show was "born of an art-oriented interest in systems and information theory."
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the art-as-life / life-as-art works she documents (Ukeles's "Maintenance Art," Piper's Catalysis, Acconci's pieces) treat ordinary experience and behavior as artistic material.

## Major Arguments

- **The recuperation verdict.** By the 1973 Postface, Lippard concludes the escape failed: "Hopes that 'conceptual art' would be able to avoid the general commercialization ... were for the most part unfounded," and "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries." In "Escape Attempts" she restates it as: "The escape was temporary. Art was recaptured and sent back to its white cell, but parole is always a possibility." (`Directly stated`)
- **The political turn.** Lippard argues that the *form* of conceptual art carried its politics — "it was usually the form rather than the content of Conceptual art that carried a political message. The frame was there to be broken out of" — and that "everything, even art, exists in a political situation." (`Directly stated`)
- **The feminist correction of the record.** Her fourth index-card show, "c. 7,500," was "an international women's Conceptual show," compiled "by way of an exasperated reply ... to those who say 'there are no women making conceptual art.'" (`Directly stated`)
- **Resistance to category-making.** Even as she narrated the field, Lippard refused to canonize it: when "557,087" originated, "'concept art' had not yet crystallized, and ... I was particularly concerned not to be providing a new category in which disparate artists would be lumped." (`Directly stated`)

## Characteristic Methods and Practices

- **The curator-as-author / organizer practice.** Lippard organized a series of four index-card-catalogue exhibitions whose titles were the host cities' populations: "557,087" (Seattle, 1969), "955,000" (Vancouver, 1970), "2,972,453" (Buenos Aires, 1970), and "c. 7,500" (the 1973 women's show). The catalogues were "randomly arranged packs of index cards" mixing her own aphorisms unsequentially with the artists' cards; with helpers she "executed ... most of the outdoor works myself." A reviewer suggested "Lucy Lippard is in fact the artist and that her medium is other artists" — a curator-as-author claim she half-embraces ("a critic's medium is always ... critics are the original appropriators"). (`Directly stated`)
- **The memoir method.** She frames the book as "an occasionally critical memoir," distrusts memory ("I was there, but I don't trust my memory"), and reprints her own 1969 statements unrevised.

## Related Authors

- [[Sol LeWitt]] — "a close friend" and "my major intellectual influence at the time"; his premise "that the concept or idea was more important than the visual results" anchors her account, and his "Sentences on Conceptual Art" open "Escape Attempts." (forward-stub)
- [[Seth Siegelaub]] — the dealer-as-"distributor extraordinaire" whose strategy of "bypassing the art world with exhibitions ... united in publications that were art rather than merely about art" "dovetailed with" her own dematerialization notions. (forward-stub)
- [[Hans Haacke]] — the systems-and-information artist whose Guggenheim show censorship she treats as the bridge "between Conceptualism, activism, and postmodernism." (forward-stub)
- [[Joseph Kosuth]] — recurrent interlocutor, "very much concerned with Art, with retaining a ... coherency," and an Art Workers Coalition collaborator. (forward-stub)
- [[John Chandler]] — co-author of the 1968 "The Dematerialization of Art" essay. (forward-stub)
- [[N. Katherine Hayles]] — *Interpretive synthesis*: a cross-author parallel, not a relationship in the text. Lippard's art-historical "dematerialization" and Hayles's account of how "[[The Materiality of Informatics|information lost its body]]" both interrogate the idea that meaning or value can be detached from material substrate; both turn on the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension and on the role of [[Information]] and [[Virtuality]]. The link is interpretive, drawn by this vault, not asserted by either author.

## Disagreements or Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Lippard's own term names the tension and she openly concedes its inaccuracy; the "dematerialized" work is still made of paper, photographs, and bodies.
- Critic vs. artist — Lippard worked in the gap between criticism and art-making ("If art could be anything at all ... then so could criticism be whatever the writer chose to do"), provoking the charge that she "had become an artist."
- Communication vs. accessibility — she notes the contradiction at the heart of the work: "Communication (but not community) and distribution (but not accessibility) were inherent in Conceptual art."
