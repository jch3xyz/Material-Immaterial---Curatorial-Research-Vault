# N. Katherine Hayles

## Books in the Vault

- [[How We Became Posthuman]]

Other works by Hayles are not currently in the vault; this note represents her as she appears through *How We Became Posthuman: Virtual Bodies in Cybernetics, Literature, and Informatics*.

## Central Concerns

Hayles is preoccupied with how a "historically specific construction called the human is giving way to a different construction called the posthuman," and with how, along the way, "information lost its body." Her abiding target is the cultural fantasy that information is an immaterial pattern that can "circulate unchanged among different material substrates" — a fantasy she traces from Norbert Wiener through Claude Shannon's [[Information]] theory to Hans Moravec's dream of downloading consciousness. Against this, she insists on recovering [[Embodiment]]: the claim that "human being is first of all embodied being," that information "must always be instantiated in a medium," and that the [[Material vs Immaterial]] separation is itself a constructed "imaginary act" to be contested rather than presupposed. Her method reads literature and science together as mutually constitutive discourses, treating cybernetic theory and contemporary fiction as two registers of the same cultural transformation.

## Method

Hayles works as a literary scholar inside the history of science. Three commitments distinguish her approach:

- **Reading literature and science together.** She refuses the one-way "influence" model in which science flows into literature. Instead she "entangle[s] abstract form and material particularity" so that scientific texts reveal "the foundational assumptions" while literary texts reveal "the complex cultural, social, and representational issues tied up with conceptual shifts." Cybernetics is read alongside Bernard Wolfe, [[Philip K. Dick]], William Gibson, Italo Calvino, and Don DeLillo.
- **Seriation against Kuhn and Foucault.** She rejects "a Kuhnian model of incommensurable paradigms" and "a Foucauldian model of sharp epistemic breaks." Borrowing "seriation" from archaeological anthropology, she charts conceptual change as "overlapping replication and innovation," tracing how homeostasis, reflexivity, and emergence each persist as "skeuomorphs" that smooth the transition between the three waves of [[Cybernetics]].
- **The materiality of informatics.** Following [[Donna Haraway]], she uses *informatics* to mean "the technologies of information as well as the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes that initiate, accompany, and complicate their development." Material interfaces (the typewriter, the computer screen, print versus magnetic encoding) are not neutral; they instantiate "new models of signification."

## Major Concepts

- [[Posthumanism]] — the posthuman as "a point of view" that privileges "informational pattern over material instantiation" and articulates the human "seamlessly" with intelligent machines
- [[Embodiment]] — the recovered ground of being; "human being is first of all embodied being"
- [[Cybernetics]] — read across three waves (homeostasis, reflexivity, virtuality)
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the flow that "deconstruct[s] the liberal humanist subject" and entangles subject and environment
- [[Systems Theory]] — the "analytically driven" discourse she sets against the embodied form of narrative
- [[The Body as Data]] — Gibson's "data made flesh," the figure of the body recoded as informational pattern
- The flickering signifier — the signifier under informatics, "a flexible chain of markers" whose internal play of difference succeeds Lacan's floating signifier
- Virtuality — "the cultural perception that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns"
- Seriation and skeuomorphs — her historiographic tools for tracing conceptual change without sharp breaks

## Major Arguments

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — that the separation of information from materiality is a constructed "imaginary act," not a fact about the world, and that it is "this materiality/information separation that I want to contest"
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — that "the disembodiment of information was not inevitable," and that embodiment "can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated"
- That narrative, with its "located actions, and personified agents," is "a more embodied form of discourse than is analytically driven [[Systems Theory|systems theory]]," and so can be used to resist the "teleology of disembodiment"
- That the posthuman is dangerous not in itself but in "the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self"
- That because the meaning of the posthuman is still being decided, "the best possible time to contest for what the posthuman means is now"

## Characteristic Metaphors

- The Platonic backhand and forehand — the two complementary moves by which "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form"
- "Data made flesh" / [[The Body as Data]] — Gibson's phrase for the body recoded as pattern
- The flickering signifier — language as "data made flesh," signifiers that flicker rather than float
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the body imagined as the "original prosthesis," extended or replaced as a continuation of a process

## Related Authors

- [[Norbert Wiener]] — founder of cybernetics; for Hayles a "visionary" who "did not intend to dismantle the liberal humanist subject" yet whose paradigm "worked to undermine" it
- [[Claude Shannon]] — defined [[Information]] as "a probability function with no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning"; the formalization that let information "lose its body"
- [[Alan Turing]] — invoked through reflexivity and the "Catholic Turing Test" skeuomorph; a touchstone for the machine/human boundary
- [[Hans Moravec]] — the roboticist whose "dream" of downloading consciousness "struck me as a nightmare"; Hayles's chief antagonist on disembodiment
- [[Warren McCulloch]] — Macy cyberneticist whose model of neurons as information processors helped forge the paradigm; later voice of posthuman pessimism
- [[Humberto Maturana]] and [[Francisco Varela]] — authors of autopoiesis, the second-wave epistemology that "sees the world as a set of informationally closed systems"; Varela also a resource for reading randomness as creative
- [[Donna Haraway]] — source of Hayles's sense of *informatics* and an ally in the feminist critique of mastery and "an objectivist account of science"
- [[Philip K. Dick]] — novelist of simulation whose epistemological obsessions "cross the scientific theory of autopoiesis"

## Disagreements or Tensions

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — her central disagreement is with the fantasy of disembodied information shared by [[Norbert Wiener]], [[Claude Shannon]], and above all [[Hans Moravec]]: the belief that "information can circulate unchanged among different material substrates." She contests not the science but the leap "from embodied reality to abstract information."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — against the hierarchy in which "information is given the dominant position and materiality runs a distant second," she argues that "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium."
- Against Kuhn and Foucault — she rejects incommensurable paradigms and sharp epistemic breaks in favor of [[seriation|seriated]] change.
- Against the liberal humanist subject — she does "not mourn the passing of a concept so deeply entwined with projects of domination and oppression," yet refuses to let "disembodiment" be "rewritten, once again, into prevailing concepts of subjectivity."
