# Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Books in the Vault

- [[God Human Animal Machine]]

Other works by O'Gieblyn are not currently in the vault; this note represents her as she appears through *God, Human, Animal, Machine: Technology, Metaphor, and the Search for Meaning*. Her own books are the only O'Gieblyn material in `raw/`.

## Central Concerns

O'Gieblyn is an American essayist and ex-evangelical — a former fundamentalist Bible-college student who "believed in the soul longer, and more literally, than most people do," lost her faith, and now reads contemporary technology "through the lens of" the theological tradition she left. Her governing claim is that the questions modernity assigned to engineers are old religious questions in disguise: "All the eternal questions have become engineering problems." She is preoccupied with disenchantment (after [[Max Weber]], "the mechanism of a world robbed of gods"), with the persistence of the soul as a "dead metaphor," with consciousness and the mind-body problem, and with [[Transhumanism]] as a technological reprise of Christian eschatology. Her recurring memoir thread — predestination, Calvinist theodicy, the Resurrection, the loss of "an eternal order" in which "each and every object has purpose and telos" — is not decoration but method: it is the interpretive instrument she turns on AI, mind-uploading, and the [[Algorithm|algorithm]].

## Method

O'Gieblyn's signature procedure is **technology as metaphor**: she treats the computational vocabulary of mind not as literal description but as a figure of speech whose origins we have forgotten. "To discover truth," she writes, "it is necessary to work within the metaphors of our own time, which are for the most part technological." Three commitments distinguish her approach:

- **The dead metaphor as historical evidence.** A metaphor "can also die when it becomes so common that we forget it is a metaphor"; the [[Mind as Machine]] computer metaphor is for her exactly this — a figure now "taken to be literal." She tracks how words like "memory," "learning," and "understanding," once placed in quotation marks to mark them as metaphors, lost their scare quotes and became "straightforward descriptions" of what machines do.
- **Reading the present through the theology of the past.** Following [[Max Weber]] and Hans Blumenberg's *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age*, she argues that "many 'new' ideas are merely attempts to answer questions that we have inherited from earlier periods of history." The "boundless alien deity" of the [[Algorithm|algorithm]] is, she concludes, "the God of Calvin and Luther — a thoroughly modern entity."
- **Memoir as analytic frame.** Her ex-evangelical biography is the lens. Her unease at algorithmic opacity "owes a great deal — maybe everything" to Calvinist predestination; her attraction to [[Pattern and Randomness|patternism]] satisfied "a much deeper, existential longing" inherited from a faith in the immortal soul.

## Major Concepts

- [[Disenchantment]] — after [[Max Weber]], the modern condition in which "everything in modern life, from our minds to the rotation of the planets, can be reduced to the causal mechanism of physical laws"; its true trauma is that the world "is devoid of intrinsic meaning"
- [[Transhumanism]] — the movement, embodied in [[Ray Kurzweil]], that "promises to restore through science the transcendent — and essentially religious — hopes that science itself obliterated"
- [[Pattern and Randomness|Patternism]] — Kurzweil's view, traced back through [[Norbert Wiener]] to Heraclitus, that "consciousness is a pattern of information" that "does not reside in the hardware of our brains" but can in principle be transferred onto a computer
- The hard problem of consciousness — [[David Chalmers]]'s name for the question "why brain processes are accompanied by first-person experience," which O'Gieblyn reads as the latest iteration of the mind-body problem "typically blamed on [[René Descartes|Descartes]]"
- The soul as dead metaphor — "one of those words that survive in language long after a culture has lost faith in the concept"
- The [[Algorithm|algorithm]] as sovereign — "black box" deep-learning systems that demand "blind submission," placing the modern subject "in a position much like Job's"

## Major Arguments

- That [[Transhumanism]] is Christian eschatology in materialist guise: "Like the biblical prophets, Kurzweil believed that the dead would rise, that the earth would be transformed, that humans would become immortal."
- That to find meaning in the world she "came to see myself more or less as a machine" — accepting the premises of disenchantment "all too well, such that I can no longer understand matter as anything but passive and inert."
- That the computational theory of mind rests on a circularity at the origin of [[Cybernetics]]: Claude Shannon "removed the thinking mind from the concept of [[Information|information]]" while [[Warren McCulloch]] "applied the logic of information processing to the mind itself," so that "the limits of the metaphor began to dissolve."
- That the AI we have built resembles "the God of Calvin and Luther" — the nominalist deity who is "radically other," whose justice is "entirely alien to ourselves," and whose verdicts we accept "as pure revelation."
- That transhumanist transcendence depends, as she notes by citing Hayles by name, on the idea that "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form."

## Characteristic Metaphors

- [[Mind as Machine]] — the computer metaphor she diagnoses as a dead metaphor: the brain as "the hardware that 'runs' the software of the mind"
- The soul as "an empty carapace that remains intact years after its animating organism has died"
- Identity as the pattern water makes in a stream — [[Ray Kurzweil|Kurzweil]]'s and [[Norbert Wiener|Wiener]]'s image, "whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water," echoing Origen's *eidos*, "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter"
- The algorithm as the whirlwind God of the Book of Job, before whom we are "forced to accept the decisions of our algorithms blindly, like Job accepting his punishment"
- The enchanted world as "a great enchanted garden" ([[Max Weber|Weber]]) versus modernity as "the mechanism of a world robbed of gods"

## Related Authors

- [[N. Katherine Hayles]] — O'Gieblyn's deepest and partly unnamed interlocutor; she cites Hayles **by name** for the claim that within transhumanist thought "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form." Both read the dream of substrate-independent information as the load-bearing fantasy of [[Posthumanism]]; O'Gieblyn supplies the theological genealogy ([[Disenchantment]], the Resurrection) where Hayles supplies the cybernetic one (the [[The Body as Data|body as data]], [[Embodiment]], the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]]).
- [[Ray Kurzweil]] — the "academic prophet" of the information age; *The Age of Spiritual Machines* was the book that began her obsession with technology, read as "a secret gospel"
- [[Hans Moravec]] — invoked across transhumanist mind-uploading as a figure of "total physical transcendence — the mind as pure information, pure spirit"
- [[Norbert Wiener]] — "the grandfather of cybernetics," whose *The Human Use of Human Beings* furnished the patternist image of selves as "patterns that perpetuate themselves," and whose *God & Golem, Inc.* framed machine learning as an "implicitly theological question"
- [[Warren McCulloch]] — co-author of the computational theory of mind, who "applied the logic of information processing to the mind itself" and sought a metaphor "so general" that "every circuit built by God or man must exemplify it"
- [[René Descartes]] — "the serpent in the garden," who divided *res extensa* from *res cogitans* and, in privileging the soul, "helped facilitate its disappearance"
- [[Max Weber]] — the source of [[Disenchantment]] and of her insistence that science "gives no answer to our question, the only question important for us: 'What shall we do and how shall we live?'"
- [[David Chalmers]] — who named "the hard problem" of consciousness, the dilemma O'Gieblyn keeps returning to against both neuroscience and AI

## Disagreements or Tensions

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — she shares [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s suspicion of disembodied information, but where Hayles contests the materiality/information separation as a constructed "imaginary act," O'Gieblyn diagnoses it from the inside as a *theological* inheritance: the patternist longing for "some spark that would remain unspoiled even as my body aged" is the immortal soul under a new name.
- Against [[Transhumanism]]'s self-understanding — she rejects the movement's claim, advanced by Nick Bostrom, to approach existential questions "in a sober, disinterested way," arguing instead that it smuggles in "the transcendent — and essentially religious — hopes that science itself obliterated."
- Against algorithmic governance — she refuses the "black box" defense that "the human brain is also a black box," insisting that machines "reach conclusions in a way that is totally unlike human reason," that "That's not a human move."
- Internal tension (disenchantment vs. dualism) — she names her own predicament: is her resistance to the [[Mind as Machine|machine model of mind]] "a holdover of my religious past," or has she "come to accept the premises of disenchantment all too well"?
