# Jack Burnham

Jack Burnham is the critic, theorist, and sometime sculptor who wrote *[[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]: The Effects of Science and Technology on the Sculpture of This Century* (New York: George Braziller, 1968). The book is a single sustained argument that twentieth-century sculpture is governed not by stylistic cycles but by "a set of coherent metamorphic forces: the impetus generated by the psychic and intellectual authority increasingly invested in science and technology." This note covers Burnham as he appears in *Beyond Modern Sculpture*; his closely related "Systems Esthetics" position (the *Artforum* essays of the same period) is treated here as the conceptual horizon the book moves toward — the migration from the art object to the art system — rather than as a separate text in the vault.

- [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]

## Central Concerns

Burnham is preoccupied with the effect of science and technology — which he compresses into the single word [[Technics]] — on the archaic activity of making sculpture. He argues that "both physically and spiritually technics are responsible for sculpture's impending rupture with the past," and that almost all of Western culture is "to some degree ramifications of technics," the older humanistic forces having been "systematically undermined and subsumed." His master concept is [[Reification]] — Marx's "thingification" (*Verdinglichung*) redirected from economics to form-making: "reification is the means by which the world is made anew, through sculpture or other artifacts, in a scientific culture."

His central thesis inverts the conventional obituary for modern sculpture. Sculpture is not dying of [[Form Exhaustion]]; it is reaching its oldest goal. He sees "modern sculpture as a preparatory stage representing steps toward the simulation of biological life," and holds that the sculptor "forsake[s] naturalism and the human figure" not from a wish to destroy the figure but from "the realization that if he was serious in his quest to bring inert matter to life anthropomorphism would not accomplish it." The driving migration of the book is from [[Object and System]]: the traditional sculpted [[Object (Burnham)|object]] gives way to the [[System (Burnham)|system]] — "an interacting assembly of varying complexity" that "may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life." That migration carries Burnham from [[Vitalism]] and [[Organicism]] through kinetic, light, and ultimately cybernetic and [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] art, ending where "the 'elan vital' will be looked upon as the old prime mover, while the Greek 'kybernetes'... becomes the expression of a new and even more effective prime mover."

## Major Concepts

- [[Technics]] — Burnham's name for science and technology "link[ed]... into a single word." Technics is the coercive, "destructive-creative duality" working on sculpture; he endorses "Friedrich Junger['s]... conce[ssion]... that technology is the metaphysics of our century."
- [[Reification]] — Marx's "reification or 'thingification' (*Verdinglichung*)" appropriated from economics to art: "the constant resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory." See [[Reification (Burnham)]].
- [[Object and System]] — the book's two-part architecture: "The object denotes sculpture in its traditional physical form, whereas the system... is the means by which sculpture gradually departs from its object state and assumes some measure of lifelike activity." See [[Object (Burnham)]], [[System (Burnham)]], [[Object vs System]].
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — the destination of the object→system migration: art "increasingly with men enmeshed with and within purposeful responsive systems," "dropping the term 'sculpture'."
- [[Scientific Idealism]] — "Much of the most original formalist and geometric sculpture of the past half century owes its existence to what could be called 'scientific idealism'" — sculpture that "view[s] essential reality through the theories and models put forth provisionally by scientists."
- [[The Scientific Demiurge]] — Burnham's figure for technics as a directional cosmic force: "art becomes a psychical manifestation of the scientific demiurge, which moves ceaselessly in a single irreversible direction."
- [[The Sculpture Base]] · [[The Vanishing Base]] — the base read as a symptom of reification: "the base is the sculptor's convention for rooting his art to surrounding reality"; its "destruction or withering away... is very much a part of the 'secularization' of sculpture." See [[Base Pedestal Plinth (Burnham)]].
- [[The Readymade]] — Duchamp's "more imposing threat to the idealism of the sculpted object," "a sort of Goedel's Theorem of aesthetics, which has proved the provisional quality of art."
- [[Object Art]] — Minimal/Object sculpture as "art which has been cut free of all previous iconic influences; it is sculpture de-idealized" — yet "quite likely not the last step in the process toward absolute 'thingification.'"
- [[Vitalism]] — "a preeminently poetic view of life, a celebration of the natural condition," embodied for Burnham in Bergson's [[Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)|élan vital]] and the sculpture that "seemed not to be carved but to grow from an inner direction." See [[Mechanism (Burnham)]], [[Mechanism vs Vitalism]].
- [[Organicism]] — "a beautiful view of the utilitarian evolution of systems, both biological and non biological"; "the awareness of the interrelation between systems and their components within larger systems." See [[Vitalism vs Organicism]], [[Organic vs Geometric]].
- [[Form Exhaustion]] — the apparent "death through formal exhaustion" of modern sculpture, which Burnham reinterprets as "time extinction through the attainment of goals as old as sculpture itself."
- [[Reification (Burnham)]] · [[Object (Burnham)]] · [[System (Burnham)]] · [[Technique (Ellul via Burnham)]] · [[Entropy (Burnham)]] · [[Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)]] · [[Mechanism (Burnham)]] · [[Base Pedestal Plinth (Burnham)]] — the definition notes anchoring Burnham's key terms.

## Major Arguments

- [[Modern sculpture is not dying of formal exhaustion but achieving its oldest goal the simulation of life]] — "Modern sculpture has shown evidence for some time of its fate: not death through formal exhaustion as has appeared imminent, but time extinction through the attainment of goals as old as sculpture itself." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Only the machine not anthropomorphism can bring inert matter to life]] — the sculptor abandoned the figure because "if he was serious in his quest to bring inert matter to life anthropomorphism would not accomplish it. The machine... remained the only means by which man would eventually reconstruct intelligent life." (`Directly stated`)
- [[In an industrial society abstraction is psychic preparation for the re-creation of life]] — departing from Worringer, Burnham holds that "in an industrial society the role of artistic abstraction is nothing less than psychic preparation for the entire re-creation of society, including remaking the biological composition of its inhabitants." (`Directly stated`)
- [[A base-less sculpture ceases to be an object and becomes a physical system of forces]] — the dissolving base marks sculpture's shift from inert object toward field, mobility, and system; "two trends have dominated the modern base: the first incorporates the base into the sculpture itself... the second attempts to free sculpture from all earthly contact." (`Strongly implied`)
- [[Constructivist sculpture dematerialized its support from the conceptual models of science]] — "Constructivism found its impetus in the evanescences of modern structural engineering, mathematics, and physics" — a [[Scientific Idealism]] dematerialization of mass and support. (`Directly stated`)
- [[Vitalism is the transitional step from the sculptural object to the system]] — "formalist and vitalist sculpture represent two preparatory tendencies which symbolically anticipate the re-creation of life through nonbiological means, that is, through technology." (`Directly stated`)
- [[The mechanist-vitalist dispute was settled by sensibilities not facts]] — "the 'vitalist versus mechanist' argument was essentially one, not of the right facts, but of the right sensibilities toward all that makes life bearable as a state of consciousness." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Biology machine theory and sculpture converge on understanding living matter through its creation]] — "the meaning of organicism for all three disciplines has already begun to converge toward a single end result-the understanding of living matter through its creation." (`Directly stated`)

## Characteristic Metaphors and Symbols

- [[The Kybernetes as the New Prime Mover]] — Burnham's closing figure: "the 'elan vital' will be looked upon as the old prime mover, while the Greek 'kybernetes' (derived from steersman and the basis of the word cybernetics) becomes the expression of a new and even more effective prime mover."
- [[The Universe as a Spendthrift Artist]] — Boulding's image, which Burnham adopts for the place of [[Entropy (Burnham)|entropy]] in art: "The universe then is seen to be like a man, who is spending his capital so that his total capital in the form of potential continually diminishes, but who continually builds up the diminishing capital into ever more elaborate works of art."
- [[Form as a Waterfall Not a Crystal]] — the shift Burnham tracks from static, crystalline, Euclidean form toward "the Faustian dynamicism of the mathematical function," from form-as-object to form-as-process and system. (`Interpretive synthesis` — names the object→system / élan-vital→kybernetes trajectory; not a single verbatim Burnham phrase.)
- [[The Scientific Model as Icon]] — "the contemporary icon par excellence becomes the scientific model which interprets principles believed to be operative in the real world."
- [[The Automobile as Leased System]] — Burnham's emblem of the object dissolving into systems: "When we buy an automobile we no longer buy an object in the old sense of the word, but instead we purchase a three- to five-year lease for participation in the state-recognized private transportation system."
- [[A Scribble in the Air]] — Herbert Read's phrase, which opens the book's argument: Read "characterizes... the tortuous dematerialization of post-World War II sculpture as a reduction to a 'scribble in the air.'"

## Related Authors

- [[Jacques Ellul]] — Burnham's primary theorist of the system: "A systems-oriented world is a milieu completely given over to what Jacques Ellul calls technique. Science and technology for Ellul are simply the most efficient forms of technique." He adopts Ellul's diagnosis ("technique... is self-aggrandizing and, most alarming, irreversible") while rejecting its terminal pessimism. See [[Technique (Ellul via Burnham)]]. (forward-stub)
- [[Herbert Read]] — the elder historian of modern sculpture whose "mien of disappointment" and "scribble in the air" Burnham uses as his point of departure; "this book explains that premonition." (forward-stub)
- [[Thomas Kuhn]] — "As Thomas Kuhn points out in his study *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962), scientific paradigms do not follow one another in close procession"; Burnham maps "major" and "minor reifications" onto Kuhn's normal-science/paradigm-shift model. (forward-stub)
- [[Kenneth Boulding]] — the economist whose entropy-and-art image ("ever more elaborate works of art... at the cost of disorganization") Burnham folds into his account of systems. (forward-stub)
- [[Friedrich Jünger]] — the essayist who "conceded over forty years ago that technology is the metaphysics of our century"; Burnham takes this as the book's metaphysical premise. (forward-stub)
- [[Lucy Lippard]] — *Interpretive synthesis*: a vault-internal pairing, not a textual relationship. Burnham's "thingification"-driven move from [[Object and System|object to system]] and Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] of the art object are the same period's two readings of the thinning art object; both turn on [[Material vs Immaterial]]. Lippard tracks the conceptualist route (idea over matter); Burnham tracks the technological route (object into lifelike system). The link is drawn by this vault.
- [[Donna Haraway]] — *Interpretive synthesis*: Burnham's [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] art — "the cybernetic organism as an art form," "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism" — is the 1968 aesthetic precursor to Haraway's 1985 [[Cyborg (Haraway)|cyborg]] as political ontology; both dissolve the organism/machine boundary. The convergence is interpretive.
- [[N. Katherine Hayles]] — *Interpretive synthesis*: Burnham's program of "the simulation of life through technology," of "understanding... living matter through its creation," anticipates the [[Artificial Life]] / [[Embodiment]] questions Hayles later interrogates; his object→system migration parallels her account of [[The Materiality of Informatics|information losing its body]].
- [[Marshall McLuhan]] — *Interpretive synthesis*: Burnham's [[Leasing the Central Nervous System|leased]]-system reading of the automobile and his cyborg "man-machine system" echo McLuhan's [[Media as Extensions of Man]]; both read technology as an extension and reorganization of the organism. Drawn by this vault.

## Disagreements or Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — the book's organizing opposition: the durable, place-occupying sculpted object versus the provisional, lifelike, "evanescent" system that succeeds it.
- [[Mechanism vs Vitalism]] — the long dispute over whether life has "a physical basis, usually electrochemical" or "an 'entelechy'... a nonmaterial center of being"; Burnham judges it "settled" by sensibility, and on the side of mechanism for science but vitalism for the poetic life of sculpture.
- [[Vitalism vs Organicism]] — Burnham holds vitalism and organicism "in polar opposition" even as they "influenced each other": the poetic celebration of life force versus the utilitarian evolution of systems.
- [[Organic vs Geometric]] — the abstract idiom's pull between organic biomorphism and geometric/constructive form, both of which Burnham reads as proto-mechanical anticipations of re-created life.
- [[Gravity vs Mobility]] — read off the base: traditional sculpture is "virtually life that cannot move," "given nowhere to go"; modern sculpture works "to disturb this traditional biological immobility," making sculpture "gravity defying."
