## Central Concerns

Haraway writes to build "an ironic political myth faithful to feminism, socialism, and materialism" — a myth whose central figure, the cyborg, is at once "a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction." Her abiding preoccupation is the late-twentieth-century collapse of the boundaries that organized the Western world picture — human/animal, organism/machine, physical/nonphysical — and the new "system of world order" she names the informatics of domination, in which "communications sciences and modern biologies are constructed by a common move—the translation of the world into a problem of coding." Against the feminist and progressive traditions that "recalled us to an imagined organic body," she refuses innocence, origin, and organic wholeness, insisting on the partial, the constructed, and the impure: "no construction is whole." Hers is a politics of *affinity, not identity* — coalition "related not by blood but by choice" — and a method of *double vision* that holds domination and possibility together without resolving them. She is the originating source for the vault's [[The Cyborg]] and for the term *informatics* that [[N. Katherine Hayles]] later borrowed.

## Method

- **Irony and blasphemy as political method.** Haraway writes in the mode of irony — "contradictions that do not resolve into larger wholes, even dialectically" — and of blasphemy, "more faithful as blasphemy is faithful, than as reverent worship and identification." The refusal to synthesize is itself the argument.
- **Myth and tool as mutually constituting.** She treats the cyborg as simultaneously a political myth and a material reality — "a condensed image of both imagination and material reality" — and insists "myth and tool mutually constitute each other," so that science fiction (Russ, Delany, Tiptree, Butler) is read as theory and theory as fiction.
- **The social relations of science and technology.** Against technological determinism, she locates power in "a historical system depending on structured relations among people," not in machines as such; the homework economy, she is careful to say, "is made possible by (not caused by) the new technologies."

## Major Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — "a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism"; the ironic, postgender, blasphemous political myth and ontology of the late twentieth century. Haraway is the originating source the vault's existing note (Hayles-anchored) points back to.
- [[The Informatics of Domination]] — her coinage for the historic shift from "the comfortable old hierarchical dominations" to "the scary new networks" of coded command-control-communication; the term [[Informatics]] descends from this.
- [[The Three Breached Boundaries]] — human/animal, organism/machine, physical/nonphysical: the three "boundary breakdowns" that make cyborg analysis possible.
- [[The World as a Problem of Coding]] — the common move of communications sciences and modern biologies that dissolves "all resistance to instrumental control."
- [[Fractured Identities]] — gender, race, and class as "contradictory, partial, and strategic," incapable of grounding an "essential unity"; "woman disintegrates into women."
- [[Affinity Not Identity]] — political coalition "related not by blood but by choice," built on "conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship" rather than natural identification.
- [[Women in the Integrated Circuit]] — women's dispersed, networked positions in computerized, militarized, multinational capitalism, where "there is no 'place' for women in these networks, only geometries of difference and contradiction."
- [[The Homework Economy]] — the worldwide restructuring of work as "literally female and feminized, whether performed by men or women" (term borrowed from economist Richard Gordon).
- [[Cyborg Writing]] — "the power to survive, not on the basis of original innocence, but on the basis of seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other."
- [[The Refusal of Innocence]] — the rejection of origin myths, organic wholeness, and "victimhood as the only ground for insight."
- [[Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity]] — "a potent subjectivity synthesized from fusions of 'outsider' identities," theorized through Sandoval's oppositional consciousness, Sister Outsider, and Malinche.

## Major Arguments

- [[The cyborg is our ontology and our politics]] — because "we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism," the cyborg is not optional metaphor but our actual condition, and a politics follows from it.
- [[The world has shifted from organic domination to the informatics of domination]] — a periodizing claim: "a movement from an organic, industrial society to a polymorphous, information system," comparable in scope to the rise of industrial capitalism.
- [[The cyborg is not subject to biopolitics it simulates politics]] — the pointed displacement of Foucault; cyborg politics operate on "boundary conditions and interfaces, on rates of flow across boundaries—and not on the integrity of natural objects."
- [[Political unity must be built through affinity not natural identity]] — since "there is nothing about being 'female' that naturally binds women," effective unity is "an achievement, not a natural fact."
- [[Totalizing feminisms leave no structural room for race]] — both Marxist/socialist-feminism (grounded in labor) and MacKinnon's radical feminism (grounded in sexual appropriation) demand the status of a totality and append "race, by addition."
- [[Cyborg politics refuses innocence and organic wholeness]] — "we do not want any more natural matrix of unity and that no construction is whole."
- [[The homework economy is made possible by not caused by the new technologies]] — her careful refusal of technological determinism.

## Characteristic Metaphors

- [[Machines Made of Sunshine]] — "Our best machines are made of sunshine... they are nothing but signals... Cyborgs are ether, quintessence" — the material/immaterial entanglement at the heart of the cyborg.
- [[The Salamander (Regeneration)]] — the regrown limb, "monstrous, duplicated, potent," figuring cyborg reconstitution against the reproductive demand for flawless rebirth.
- The refused Garden of Eden / Frankenstein's monster — the origin myths the cyborg refuses; it "would not recognize the Garden of Eden" and "does not expect its father to save it through a restoration of the garden."
- Floating signifiers / "an irreverent upstart god" — cruise missiles as "floating signifiers moving in pickup trucks"; modern machinery as a blasphemous deity "mocking the Father's ubiquity."

## Symbols

- [[I Would Rather Be a Cyborg Than a Goddess]] — the manifesto's signature closing slogan; the ironic, technological, partial cyborg chosen over the organic, holistic, reproductive goddess.
- [[Blasphemy]] — the stance of the essay: "Blasphemy protects one from the moral majority within, while still insisting on the need for community. Blasphemy is not apostasy."
- C3I (command-control-communication-intelligence) — the militarized acronym Haraway takes as "the meta phor" condensing the whole informatics of domination.

## Related Authors

- [[N. Katherine Hayles]] — historical influence (now reciprocal): Hayles took the term *informatics* and her reading of the cyborg's entity/metaphor doubleness from this manifesto. See [[N. Katherine Hayles and Donna Haraway]].
- [[Michel Foucault]] — disagreement / supersession: "Michel Foucault's biopolitics is a flaccid premonition of cyborg politics, a very open field" (ch.01); "the cyborg simulates politics, a much more potent field of operations" (ch.03). A 1985 demotion that converges with Han's later claim that Foucault "failed to make the turn to psychopolitics."
- [[Karl Marx]] — shared tradition (socialist-feminism) with internal critique: cyborg exchange "transcends the universal translation effected by capitalist markets that Marx analyzed so well," and "most Marxisms see domination best" but read complicity only as false consciousness. Chela Sandoval, Katie King, Catharine MacKinnon, Julia Kristeva, Audre Lorde, and Cherríe Moraga are cited within the argument but have no books in the vault and are rendered as plain text.

## Disagreements or Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the originating feminist-cyborg articulation of the vault-wide tension: the cyborg as "a condensed image of both imagination and material reality," set against machines that are "nothing but signals" and people who are "both material and opaque"; bodies "textualized... as code problems on the grid of C3I."
- [[Cyborg vs Goddess]] — the manifesto's defining opposition: technological/ironic/partial cyborg vs organic/holistic/reproductive goddess. "Both are bound in the spiral dance," but Haraway chooses the cyborg.
- [[Identity vs Affinity]] — essentialist natural identity vs constructed coalitional affinity.
- [[Totality vs Partiality]] — the persistent value of the partial, contradictory, "permanently unclosed" against the totalizing whole: "We do not need a totality in order to work well."
- [[Regeneration vs Rebirth]] — reconstitution after injury (monstrous, partial, potent) vs the reproductive matrix's demand for return to wholeness.

Haraway's cyborg is the originating source for the vault's [[The Cyborg]], grounds the [[Posthumanism|posthumanist]] dissolution of "all the signs of Man," and supplies an early, explicitly feminist-critical statement of [[The Body as Data|the body as data]] ("a high-tech view of the body as a biotic component or cybernetic communications system") and of the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]]'s undoing ("In the 'Western' sense, the end of man is at stake").
