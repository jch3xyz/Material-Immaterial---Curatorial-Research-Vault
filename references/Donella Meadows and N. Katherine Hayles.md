# Donella Meadows ↔ N. Katherine Hayles

## Relationship Type

Shared tradition — **interpretive synthesis**. Meadows and Hayles stand on opposite sides of one discipline: cybernetics. In *How We Became Posthuman* Hayles is the **historian** of second-wave cybernetics — the mid-century science of [[Feedback Loops|feedback]], [[Homeostasis|homeostasis]], [[Reflexivity|reflexivity]], and self-organization — tracing how [[Information|information]] came to be conceived as a pattern that could be abstracted from any material substrate. In *Thinking in Systems* Meadows is a **practitioner** of that same science: she inherits its core apparatus not as an object of historical study but as a working toolkit for reading real systems — economies, ecosystems, populations, thermostats. Meadows does **not** cite Hayles (the two never reference each other); this note is a vault-internal cross-reading. The bridge is that Meadows *does* the systems science Hayles *narrates* — she is the applied downstream of the cybernetic tradition Hayles historicizes.

## Explanation

The load-bearing overlap is precise, term for term. **Feedback** is the "basic operating unit of a system" for Meadows and the founding concept of the Macy-era cybernetics Hayles chronicles: for Meadows a loop forms "when changes in a stock affect the flows into or out of that same stock" (Ch. 1) — the closed circuit of self-monitoring control that Wiener and company built the field around. **Homeostasis**, the second-wave keyword Hayles centers, is Meadows's balancing loop — the loop "that should keep the system state at an acceptable level" (Ch. 5), which can be overwhelmed by a reinforcing loop and drift, exactly the failure mode of a homeostatic goal-seeking mechanism. **The system that causes its own behavior** — Meadows's central heresy, "the system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior!" (Introduction) — is the operational face of reflexivity and operational closure: behavior is generated endogenously by the system's own loop structure, not imposed from outside. And **[[Information|information]] holds systems together** — Meadows's claim that "many of the interconnections in systems operate through the flow of information" and that "information holds systems together" (Ch. 1) — is the working-scientist's version of the very thesis Hayles reconstructs and interrogates: that information is the substance that binds and organizes a system. Where Hayles asks how information "lost its body," Meadows simply uses information-as-organizing-pattern as a given of the trade. Finally, **self-organization** — for Meadows "the strongest form of system resilience," a system that "can evolve … can survive almost any change, by changing itself" (Ch. 6) — is the second-wave endpoint (and third-wave threshold) Hayles narrates, the capacity that shades from homeostasis toward [[Emergence|emergence]] and [[Autopoiesis|autopoiesis]]. The reading is therefore an [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]]-grounding one: Meadows's book is the native applied ground of the science Hayles reads as intellectual history. `Interpretive synthesis` — Meadows nowhere invokes Hayles or the historiography of cybernetics; the parallel is constructed by placing the practitioner's manual beside the historian's account.

Two cautions keep the synthesis honest. First, the directions of interest are opposite: Hayles is critical and genealogical (how did we come to erase [[Embodiment|the body]] from information?), while Meadows is constructive and pragmatic (how do I find the leverage point?). Second, `Self-Organization` is kept **distinct** from [[Emergence]] and [[Autopoiesis]] — Meadows's self-organization is a designer's category (adding new structure, loops, or rules to a system), cross-linked to but not collapsed into them.

## Source Passages

Meadows's cybernetic apparatus, in her own words: the feedback loop as the system's operating unit ("A feedback loop is formed when changes in a stock affect the flows into or out of that same stock"); information as the binding medium ("Information holds systems together"); endogenous behavior ("The system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior!"); the balancing (homeostatic) loop and its collapse ("The balancing feedback loop that should keep the system state at an acceptable level is overwhelmed by a reinforcing feedback loop heading downhill"); and self-organization as resilience ("The ability to self-organize is the strongest form of system resilience"). Each is a term Hayles treats as an object of history; here it is a working tool.
