# Roy Ascott ↔ Marshall McLuhan

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation + conceptual parallel.** Of the vault's interpretive Ascott bridges, this is the most strongly grounded in the primary text: Ascott names [[Marshall McLuhan]] repeatedly across the corpus, quotes him by published reference (the [[The Global Village|global village]], the "rear-view mirror" content-of-a-new-medium thesis, the "no detachment of frame" of electric information media), and his editor Edward Shanken stages McLuhan as Ascott's explicit prophetic frame. McLuhan does not cite Ascott (*Understanding Media* is 1964; Ascott's telematic essays run 1980s–1990s), so the influence is one-directional — McLuhan as predecessor, Ascott as the artist-theorist who tries to *make art out of* the global village. Two further passages (the 1964 machine that "extends his perception," the hot/cool "thermodynamics of an artwork") are **interpretive parallels** where the McLuhanian logic is unmistakable but McLuhan is not named.

## Explanation

The spine of the relationship is McLuhan's [[The Global Village]] → Ascott's [[Network Consciousness]]. In ch.07 Ascott summarizes *The Gutenberg Galaxy* and *Understanding Media* by name, taking over McLuhan's thesis that "electric media" and the "electric extensions of the central nervous system" ([[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]]) produce a global village of "social interdependence and deep involvement." Where McLuhan describes this condition, Ascott proposes to *build* it: [[Telematic Art]] and [[Distributed Authorship]] are attempts to turn the global village into a participatory artwork, a planetary "thinking system" he ultimately calls network consciousness. This is the predecessor-to-medium logic McLuhan himself supplies — and Ascott cites it explicitly in ch.28: "following Marshal McLuhan's idea that the content of a new medium is the medium that preceded it, the rear-view mirror effect," telematic concerns (telepresence, connectivity, distributed authorship) will be realized by the molecular/nanotech society to come. *(Directly stated.)*

The convergence is sharpest on participation and the collapse of the detached spectator. In ch.15 Ascott quotes McLuhan directly — "The instantaneous world of electric information media involves all of us, all at once. No detachment of frame is possible" — and reads it straight into telematic discourse, where meaning is "negotiated, distributed, transformed, and layered in multiple exchanges" and the "authorial role is decentralised." McLuhan's end of the framed Renaissance viewpoint becomes Ascott's [[Reciprocal Communication]] and the decentred author. Ch.09 carries the same DNA: "as with TV, so with ESP, the medium is the message" — a verbatim borrowing of [[The Medium Is the Message]], here extended (in Ascott's psybernetic register) from broadcast media to telepathy. *(Directly stated.)*

Shanken's editor's introduction (ch.04) makes the bridge explicit and also marks its limit. He names *The Gutenberg Galaxy* and *Understanding Media* as Ascott's frame, and he **stages McLuhan against [[Jean Baudrillard]]**: against "McLuhan's proposition that telecommunications is creating an interconnected global village," Baudrillard's "Requiem for the Media" insists that the form of media is "what always prevents response," undermining any liberatory potential. Ascott sits on McLuhan's side of this split — his telematics is utopian and connective where Baudrillard's is pessimistic and one-way — which is precisely why the McLuhan inheritance matters: it is the optimistic media theory Ascott chooses. *(Directly stated, of Shanken's framing.)*

Two earlier passages are McLuhanian without naming him. Ascott's 1964 cybernetic-vision account — "The machine, largely self-regulating and highly adaptive, stands between man and his world. It extends his perception into furthest / space and deep into the finest particles of matter" — restates [[Media as Extensions of Man]] in cybernetic dress. And the ch.08 "thermodynamics of an artwork" (an artwork is "hot when it is densely stacked with information bits, highly organised, and rigidly determined… one-way," "cool" when "loosely stacked… ambiguous, entropic," admitting feedback and participation) is a direct transposition of McLuhan's [[Media Hot and Cold]] from media onto artworks, complete with the [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium|overheating-and-reversal]] mechanism ("when an artwork overheats… the system switches to a very cool state"). These are **interpretive synthesis** — the parallel is strong and structural, but McLuhan is unnamed in those passages.

Where they diverge: McLuhan's global village is an *acoustic, retribalizing* reorganization of the sensorium ([[Detribalization and Retribalization]]); Ascott reroutes the same network through cybernetics, second-order observer-as-participant theory, and a frankly mystical telos (the noosphere, "Total telepathy," "Let the mediums give the message"). McLuhan diagnoses; Ascott prophesies and prescribes. Both belong to the cluster the vault reads through [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott's "superconnectivity… planetary culture, a holistic world art" is McLuhan's information-without-content pushed to its immaterial, networked conclusion.

## Source Passages

The ch.07, ch.28, ch.15, ch.09 passages name or quote McLuhan and are **directly stated** evidence of citation; the ch.04 passage is Shanken's editorial framing (also directly stated, but Shanken's prose, not Ascott's); the ch.05 and ch.08 passages are **interpretive synthesis** (McLuhan unnamed).

The keystone direct citation — McLuhan's electric media and global village as Ascott's frame:

> He claims, moreover, that the extensive development of “electric media,” of instant and simultaneous communication (TV and electric extensions of the central nervous system) produces a “global village” of social interdependence and deep involvement.

Ascott citing McLuhan's rear-view-mirror / content-of-a-new-medium thesis to license telematic optimism (ch.28):

> But following Marshal McLuhan’s idea that the content of a new medium is the medium that preceded it, the rear-view mirror effect, we telematic artists can be optimistic that the molecular society of tomorrow will realise with ease the ideas of telepresence, connectivity, distributed authorship, and interactivity that we are working with today.

Ascott quoting McLuhan on the end of the detached frame, read straight into telematic participation (ch.15):

> The instantaneous world of electric information media involves all of us, all at once. No detachment of frame is possible

Shanken staging McLuhan against Baudrillard — the global village as the optimistic media theory Ascott chooses (ch.04):

> In contrast to McLuhan’s proposition that telecommunications is creating an interconnected global village, Baudrillard insists that the formal qualities and institutional conditions of media embody “what always prevents response”

The interpretive parallel: the 1964 machine that extends perception (McLuhan unnamed, ch.05):

> The machine, largely self-regulating and highly adaptive, stands between man and his world. It extends his perception into furthest

The interpretive parallel: the hot/cool "thermodynamics of an artwork" transposing McLuhan's hot/cool media onto the artwork (ch.08):

> An artwork is hot when it is densely stacked with information bits, highly organised, and rigidly determined.

## Confidence

**High.** Ascott names and quotes McLuhan by published title across multiple chapters (07, 15, 28; the medium-is-the-message borrowing in 09), and his editor names McLuhan's global village as the explicit frame and stages it against Baudrillard (04). The direct-citation half of the relationship is unambiguous and primary-text grounded — hence high rather than the medium typical of the vault's purely interpretive bridges. The ch.05 (machine extends perception) and ch.08 (hot/cool artwork) mappings are labelled interpretive synthesis and do not carry the rating; the citation passages do.
