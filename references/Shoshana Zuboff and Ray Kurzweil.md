# Shoshana Zuboff ↔ Ray Kurzweil

## Relationship Type

Conceptual parallel — **interpretive synthesis**. Zuboff (2019) does not cite Kurzweil, and *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* nowhere engages *The Singularity Is Near* (2005); Kurzweil could not have cited Zuboff. The connection is a construction of this vault, not a historical influence or direct citation. This is the standout pro/contra pairing of the surveillance cluster: the **same technical substrate** — pervasive, sensate, networked, computational infrastructure — read as transcendence by the proponent and as a coup from above by the critic. Every bridge below is labeled accordingly.

## Explanation

Kurzweil and Zuboff describe one and the same emerging infrastructure — ubiquitous distributed sensors, the instrumented body, smart dust, pervasive computing, continuous data flow — from opposite directions and with opposite valuations. For Kurzweil this sensate environment is the precondition of human transcendence: the body version 2.0 wired with [[Smart Dust]] and nanobots, [[Decentralization]] as a democratizing force, [[Full-Immersion Virtual Reality]] dissolving the boundary between flesh and information. For Zuboff the identical apparatus is [[Big Other]] — "the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior" — and she diagnoses it as the **materialization** of [[Instrumentarian Power|instrumentarian power]] and the operational core of [[Surveillance Capitalism]].

The decisive move is Zuboff's insistence that this infrastructure is not technology but a *logic that commands technology into action*. Where Kurzweil sees the ubiquitous sensorium as a neutral substrate whose risks are governance problems — he names the [[Privacy vs Security]] tension directly and defers it — Zuboff reads the very ubiquity Kurzweil celebrates as the means of behavioral modification: "under the direction of surveillance capitalism the global reach of computer mediation is repurposed as an extraction architecture." Kurzweil's "knowing machine" is, in Zuboff's terms, "not just a knowing machine; it is an actuating machine designed to produce more certainty about us and for them." The same word — *ubiquitous computing* — is the banner of liberation in one text and the name of domination in the other.

The sharpest convergence is over a single shared figure: **Mark Weiser, the visionary of ubiquitous computing**. Kurzweil's project is Weiserian — pervasive, sub-perceptual, ambient computation woven into every surface and into the body itself. Zuboff quotes Weiser to the opposite end: "hundreds of computers in every room, all capable of sensing people near them and linked by high-speed networks have the potential to make totalitarianism up to now seem like sheerest anarchy." The technology Kurzweil hails for its "ultra-unobtrusive ubiquity" — Zuboff's "smart skin ... as inconspicuous as a 'decal sticker'" — is for her precisely what makes instrumentarian power dangerous: it "accomplishes its aims quietly and persistently, using methods that intentionally bypass our awareness, disappearing into the background of all things." Eric Schmidt's "The internet will disappear" becomes, in Zuboff's reading, "The internet will disappear into Big Other." Disappearance is Kurzweil's design goal and Zuboff's indictment.

This bridge also connects to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis from the surveillance side: Kurzweil dissolves the material body into information infrastructure ([[The Body as Data]], [[Transcending Biology]]); Zuboff reads that same dissolution — experience rendered into immaterial "behavioral data" — as the condition of possibility for [[The Coup from Above|a coup from above]]. It is the surveillance-economy complement of the existing [[Ray Kurzweil and Byung-Chul Han]] note (Han names the affective regime; Zuboff names the economic logic and its material apparatus) and of [[Yuval Noah Harari and Byung-Chul Han]] / Harari's [[Dataism]] (the worldview side). Among these, Kurzweil is the lone *advocate*: he wants the infrastructure the others diagnose.

## Source Passages

### Passage 1 — Big Other names Kurzweil's ubiquitous sensorium as domination

`Directly stated`

> "Part III examines the rise of instrumentarian power; its expression in a ubiquitous sensate, networked, computational infrastructure that I call Big Other; and the novel and deeply antidemocratic vision of society and social relations that these produce."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/03 Chapter 1 - Home or Exile in the Digital Future]].

The phrase "ubiquitous sensate, networked, computational infrastructure" is, almost word for word, the environment Kurzweil forecasts and celebrates across *The Singularity Is Near* (pervasive sensors, smart dust, the networked instrumented body). Zuboff gives the identical substrate a name — [[Big Other]] — and a verdict: "deeply antidemocratic." This is the single tightest point of contact between the two visions. `Interpretive synthesis` for the Kurzweil bridge.

### Passage 2 — The infrastructure as extraction architecture, not neutral substrate

`Directly stated`

> "Indeed, under the direction of surveillance capitalism the global reach of computer mediation is repurposed as an extraction architecture."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/07 Chapter 5 - The Elaboration of Surveillance Capitalism - Kidnap, Corner, Compete]].

Kurzweil treats "the global reach of computer mediation" as an intrinsically democratizing force (the fax-machine-and-Internet argument against totalitarian relinquishment in *The Singularity Is Near*, Ch. 8). Zuboff's verb is "repurposed": the same global mediation Kurzweil reads as liberation is, under surveillance capitalism's logic, turned into an [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|architecture of extraction]]. The disagreement is not about the technology but about what its logic commands it to do. `Interpretive synthesis` for the bridge.

### Passage 3 — From knowing machine to actuating machine

`Directly stated`

> "Under this regime, ubiquitous computing is not just a knowing machine; it is an actuating machine designed to produce more certainty about us and for them."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/09 Chapter 7 - The Reality Business]].

Kurzweil's account of ubiquitous computing is an account of a *knowing* machine — sensors that report, model, optimize, enhance. Zuboff's claim is that the same ubiquity inevitably becomes an *actuating* machine: it does not merely sense us, it modifies us ("the goal now is to automate us"). This is the precise hinge of [[A Utopia of Certainty]] and of the asymmetry the two authors share but evaluate oppositely. `Interpretive synthesis` for the bridge.

### Passage 4 — "Ultra-unobtrusive ubiquity" as the danger, not the achievement

`Directly stated`

> "Initially valued for its ability to monitor and diagnosis health conditions from Parkinson’s disease to sleep disorders, smart skin is now hailed for its promise of ultra-unobtrusive ubiquity."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/10 Chapter 8 - Rendition - From Experience to Data]].

"Smart skin" — wireless, batteryless, "as inconspicuous as a 'decal sticker'" — is the literal realization of Kurzweil's [[Human Body Version 2.0]] and the body-instrumented-by-sensors he forecasts. For Kurzweil the body that "monitors" itself and transmits "dynamic information" is enhancement. Zuboff records the same artifact under the chapter title "Rendition — From Experience to Data": the very *unobtrusiveness* Kurzweil prizes is, for her, what allows experience to be rendered into [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral surplus]] without consent. `Interpretive synthesis` for the bridge.

### Passage 5 — "The internet will disappear into Big Other"

`Directly stated`

> "Recall that Alphabet/Google’s Eric Schmidt provoked uproar in 2015 when in response to a question on the future of the web, he said, “The internet will disappear.” What he really meant was that “The internet will disappear into Big Other.”"

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/15 Chapter 13 - Big Other and the Rise of Instrumentarian Power]].

Disappearance — computation receding into the background of all things — is Kurzweil's explicit *design goal* (the Weiserian dream of ambient, pervasive, sub-perceptual computing that pervades *The Singularity Is Near*'s account of the instrumented environment). Zuboff completes the sentence with her indictment: the internet does not vanish; it dissolves into [[Big Other]], the apparatus that "accomplishes its aims quietly and persistently, using methods that intentionally bypass our awareness." What Kurzweil celebrates as seamlessness, Zuboff names as evasion of awareness. `Interpretive synthesis` for the bridge.

### Passage 6 — Weiser's ubiquitous computing and the line to totalitarianism

`Directly stated`

> "Although he did not name it, the visionary of ubiquitous computing, Mark Weiser, foresaw the immensity of instrumentarian power as a totalizing societal project... “hundreds of computers in every room, all capable of sensing people near them and linked by high-speed networks have the potential to make totalitarianism up to now seem like sheerest anarchy.”"

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/16 Chapter 14 - A Utopia of Certainty]].

Mark Weiser's "ubiquitous computing" is the explicit lineage of Kurzweil's pervasive-sensor future. Zuboff invokes the very same Weiserian vision — "hundreds of computers in every room ... sensing people" — to argue it could "make totalitarianism up to now seem like sheerest anarchy." The shared technical genealogy (Weiser) and the opposite political conclusion (transcendence vs. a power beyond totalitarianism) make this the clinching passage of the pairing. Zuboff is careful to add that this infrastructure is *not* digital totalitarianism but "an unprecedented power" — which is precisely her [[Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism|distinction]] Kurzweil's framework lacks the categories to register. `Interpretive synthesis` for the bridge.

## Confidence

**Medium.** The parallel is structurally exact: both authors describe the same Weiser-descended infrastructure of ubiquitous sensate computing, both foreground its disappearance into the background of everyday life, and the asymmetry of valuation (transcendence vs. coup) is sharp and fully documentable from Zuboff's primary text. The Kurzweil side is sourced in his own concept and argument notes ([[Smart Dust]], [[Human Body Version 2.0]], [[Transcending Biology]], [[Decentralization]], [[Privacy vs Security]]) and is not re-quoted here, since this note's purpose is to document Zuboff's side of the contrast. The confidence label reflects provenance, not validity: Zuboff does not name Kurzweil and Kurzweil does not name Big Other; this is vault synthesis, not authorial dialogue. The naming asymmetry is itself the point — Zuboff supplies the critical vocabulary (Big Other, instrumentarian power, extraction architecture, rendition) that Kurzweil's celebratory frame structurally cannot produce.
