# Yuval Noah Harari ↔ Alan Turing

## Relationship Type

Historical influence. Harari positions Turing's theoretical work — the concept of the Turing Machine and the behaviourist framing of the Turing Test — as one of the two founding waves that together produce [[Dataism]], the other being Darwinian biology. Turing's contribution is structural: the demonstration that algorithmic calculation is substrate-independent underwrites Harari's claim that biological and electronic algorithms are governed by identical mathematical laws. The Turing Test figures separately as the operational emblem of Harari's argument about the [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]].

## Explanation

Harari traces [[Dataism]] to the convergence of two scientific traditions: the life sciences' embrace of organisms-as-algorithms (rooted in Darwin) and the computer-science tradition that begins with Turing's formalization of the algorithm as a universal substrate. Because algorithmic calculation is indifferent to the material in which it is instantiated — carbon or silicon — the barrier between biological and electronic computation collapses, and [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] becomes the master premise of a new worldview. `Directly stated`

The Turing Test enters Harari's argument in a different register. In *The Great Decoupling*, Harari's key claim is that intelligence and consciousness, historically coupled, are now separating: non-conscious systems can match or surpass human performance on every measurable task. The behaviourist logic of the Turing Test — which evaluates intelligence purely from the outside, by observable outputs, without reference to inner experience — is the conceptual prototype for this move. A system that passes the test need have no inner life; it need only produce indistinguishable behaviour. Harari draws the political and economic consequence: for armies and corporations, consciousness becomes optional while intelligence remains mandatory. See also [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] and [[The Turing Test as a Magic Trick]] (Hayles's parallel treatment of what the test forecloses). `Strongly implied`

## Source Passages

### Thread 1 — The Turing Machine as founding wave of Dataism

From *Homo Deus*, Chapter 11 "The Data Religion":

> Dataism was born from the explosive confluence of two scientific tidal waves. In the 150 years since Charles Darwin published On the Origin of Species, the life sciences have come to see organisms as biochemical algorithms. Simultaneously, in the eight decades since Alan Turing formulated the idea of a Turing Machine, computer scientists have learned to engineer increasingly sophisticated electronic algorithms. Dataism puts the two together, pointing out that exactly the same mathematical laws apply to both biochemical and electronic algorithms. Dataism thereby collapses the barrier between animals and machines, and expects electronic algorithms to eventually decipher and outperform biochemical algorithms.

Label: `Directly stated`. This is Harari's explicit genealogy of [[Dataism]]: Turing is named as the founding figure of one of the two constituent waves.

### Thread 2 — The decoupling of intelligence from consciousness

From *Homo Deus*, Chapter 9 "The Great Decoupling":

> Humans are in danger of losing their value, because intelligence is decoupling from consciousness.

> Until today, high intelligence always went hand in hand with a developed consciousness. Only conscious beings could perform tasks that required a lot of intelligence, such as playing chess, driving cars, diagnosing diseases or identifying terrorists. However, we are now developing new types of non-conscious intelligence that can perform such tasks far better than humans. For all these tasks are based on pattern recognition, and non-conscious algorithms may soon excel human consciousness in recognising patterns. This raises a novel question: which of the two is really important, intelligence or consciousness? As long as they went hand in hand, debating their relative value was just a pastime for philosophers. But in the twenty-first century, this is becoming an urgent political and economic issue. And it is sobering to realise that, at least for armies and corporations, the answer is straightforward: intelligence is mandatory but consciousness is optional.

Label: `Directly stated`. The behaviourist premise of the Turing Test — that intelligent behaviour can be verified from the outside without access to inner states — is the logical precondition for this claim. The test defines intelligence as pattern-matching performance; once that definition is accepted, the question of whether the system is conscious becomes, as Harari says, optional.

### Thread 3 — The substrate-independence argument (algorithmic calculation in carbon or silicon)

From *Homo Deus*, Chapter 9 "The Great Decoupling":

> 1. Organisms are algorithms. Every animal – including Homo sapiens – is an assemblage of organic algorithms shaped by natural selection over millions of years of evolution.
> 2. Algorithmic calculations are not affected by the materials from which you build the calculator. Whether you build an abacus from wood, iron or plastic, two beads plus two beads equals four beads.
> 3. Hence there is no reason to think that organic algorithms can do things that non-organic algorithms will never be able to replicate or surpass. As long as the calculations remain valid, what does it matter whether the algorithms are manifested in carbon or silicon?

Label: `Directly stated`. This three-part syllogism is the philosophical core of the [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] argument. Premise 2 is the Turing inheritance: the universality of algorithmic computation regardless of physical substrate is exactly what the abstract Turing Machine demonstrates.

### Thread 4 — The grand summary: intelligence decouples, consciousness becomes a question

From *Homo Deus*, Chapter 11 "The Data Religion" (closing summary):

> 1. Science is converging on an all-encompassing dogma, which says that organisms are algorithms, and life is data processing.
> 2. Intelligence is decoupling from consciousness.
> 3. Non-conscious but highly intelligent algorithms may soon know us better than we know ourselves.

Label: `Directly stated`. Harari distils the book's argument into three numbered theses. Turing is the historical anchor for the first (the algorithmic substrate) and the conceptual precondition for the second (behaviourist intelligence independent of inner experience).

## Confidence

**High.** Harari names Turing and the Turing Machine explicitly in Chapter 11 as one of the two founding waves of [[Dataism]]. The connection to the Turing Test in the decoupling argument is strongly implied rather than named — Harari never uses the phrase "Turing Test" in these chapters — but the behaviourist logic is the same, and Hayles's parallel treatment in [[The Turing Test as a Magic Trick]] confirms that this is a durable cross-author connection. The substrate-independence argument (carbon vs. silicon) and the decoupling thesis are both stated directly and at length.
