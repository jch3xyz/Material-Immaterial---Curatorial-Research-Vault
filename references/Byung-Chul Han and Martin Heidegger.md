# Byung-Chul Han and Martin Heidegger

## Relationship Type

Direct citation of [[Martin Heidegger]] in *[[The Transparency Society]]* (Chapters 2 and 7) combined with shared tradition / historical influence. [[Byung-Chul Han]] wrote his doctoral dissertation on Heidegger and is broadly Heideggerian in vocabulary and orientation; this book in particular mobilizes Heideggerian terminology — *Wohnen* (dwelling), *Ge-Stell* (Framing), *Ent-fernung* (de-distantiation), *aletheia* / *entbergen* (unveiling) — both in explicit citation and as silent armature. Confidence is high for the direct-citation reading and high-to-medium for the broader shared-tradition reading.

## Explanation

Heidegger is named directly in the **Exhibition** chapter (Ch. 2) and the **Information** chapter (Ch. 7). In Exhibition, Han retrieves Heidegger's notion of *[[Dwelling|Wohnen]]* to diagnose what the compulsion to display destroys ("Dwelling originally meant 'to be at peace, to be brought to peace, to remain in peace'"), invokes Heidegger's "thing" as that which "cannot be exhibited, for it consists solely of [[Cult Value|cult value]]," and turns to Heidegger again on distance — quoting "pure nearness which sustains the distance [reine die Ferne aushaltende Nähe]" and the "pain of the nearness of the distant" — to argue that transparency abolishes proximity through *Ent-fernung*. Heidegger's vocabulary is here used affirmatively, as a measure against which the [[Exhibition Value|society of exhibition]] is found wanting.

In the Information chapter, the engagement turns critical-revisionary. Han names Heidegger to identify [[Information]] with what "Heidegger would call ... a language of 'Framing' [Ge-Stell]," and quotes Heidegger directly: "speaking is challenged to correspond in every respect to Framing in which all present beings can be commandeered. Within Framing, speaking turns into information." But Han then argues that Heidegger's framework "does not encompass the forms of positioning that are characteristic of today." Where Heidegger reads *Ge-Stell* through domination (*Bestellen*, *Vorstellen*, *Herstellen*), Han argues that contemporary positioning — *Aus-Stellen* (exhibiting), *Zur-Schau-Stellen* (putting-on-display) — is driven not by power but by attention: "The motivating factor is not polemos but porno." Han neologizes *Ge-Menge* (accumulation) as a counter to *Ge-Stell*, suggesting that today's media images are "simulacra that no longer represent 'beings'" and thereby exceed Heidegger's "picture" theory.

The Heideggerian background is also unmistakable beyond direct citation. The Ch. 8 title — **"The Society of Unveiling"** — alludes to Heidegger's reading of truth as *aletheia* / *entbergen* (unconcealment), even where Heidegger is not named: Han argues that "digital transparency is not cardiographic but pornographic," a society of total illumination [*Ausleuchtung*] in which the unveiling has lost its metaphysical depth. Likewise the Ch. 5 **Acceleration** elaborates the loss of contemplative dwelling-time that Heidegger associates with *Verweilen* — Han argues that "compulsive transparency annihilates the [[Fragrance of Time|fragrance of things, the fragrance of time]]," and that what is lost is the kind of "tarrying" or thresholded temporality Heidegger had associated with thinking and dwelling. The book thus deploys Heidegger as both explicit interlocutor (cited, quoted, revised) and as background ontology of the very critique transparency calls forth.

## Source Passages

From Ch. 2 ("The Society of Exhibition"), Han retrieves Heidegger on dwelling and the thing:

> The imperative to display destroys dwelling itself. When the world becomes a display room, dwelling proves impossible. Dwelling yields to solicitation [Werben], which serves to heighten the capital of attention [Aufmerksamkeitskapital]. Dwelling originally meant "to be at peace, to be brought to peace, to remain in peace [zufrieden sein, zum Frieden gebracht, in ihm bleiben]." Unrelenting compulsion to exhibit and perform threatens this peace. It cannot be exhibited. The thing, as Heidegger defines it, also vanishes entirely. It cannot be exhibited, for it consists solely of cult value.

Later in the same chapter, on distance:

> In this sense, Heidegger speaks of a "pure nearness which sustains the distance [reine die Ferne aushaltende Nähe]." However, the "pain of the nearness of the distant" counts as negativity to be eliminated. Transparency re-moves [ent-fernt] everything into uniform de-distantiation that stands neither near nor far.

From Ch. 7 ("The Society of Information"), Han identifies information with Heidegger's *Ge-Stell* and then revises him:

> The society of transparency is the society of information. Information is a phenomenon as such insofar as it lacks all negativity. It amounts to positivized, operationalized language. Heidegger would call it a language of "Framing" [Ge-Stell ]: "speaking is challenged to correspond in every respect to Framing in which all present beings can be commandeered. Within Framing, speaking turns into information." Information positions [stellt] human language. Heidegger conceives "Framing" in terms of domination. Accordingly, figures of order such as commanding [Bestellen], imagining [Vorstellen], and producing [Herstellen] signify power and rule.

The revision follows immediately:

> However, Heidegger's Framing does not encompass the forms of positioning that are characteristic of today. Exhibiting [Aus-Stellen] or putting-on-display [Zur-Schau-Stellen] do not primarily serve the acquisition of power. Power is not the aim so much as attention [Aufmerksamkeit]. The motivating factor is not polemos but porno.

And on the picture / simulacrum:

> This theory of the picture does not explain today's media images, for they are simulacra that no longer represent "beings." They do not serve the purpose of "positioning," being "before oneself," and "constantly having it in this way." As simulacra without reference, they lead an independent existence, so to speak. They also proliferate beyond power and dominion. They are, as it were, fuller with being and life than what simply "is." Today's multimediated mass of information and communication presents things more as an accumulation [Ge-Menge] than as a "framing."

## Confidence

**High** for the direct-citation relationship: Heidegger is named four times across Ch. 2 and Ch. 7, his German terms (*Ge-Stell*, *Ent-fernung*, *Wohnen*, *Bestellen*, *Vorstellen*, *Herstellen*) are quoted in the original, and a passage from Heidegger is reproduced verbatim in Ch. 7. **High** for the revisionary dimension: Han explicitly states that Heidegger's framework "does not encompass" today's positioning and counters *Ge-Stell* with his neologism *Ge-Menge*. **Medium-to-high** for the broader shared-tradition reading: the Ch. 8 title "Unveiling" (the German *entbergen* is the Heideggerian rendering of *aletheia*) and the Ch. 5 critique of speed-as-loss-of-tarrying are unmistakably Heideggerian in idiom but Heidegger is not named in those chapters.
