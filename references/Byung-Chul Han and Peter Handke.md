# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Peter Handke

## General Orientation

This note tracks [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s direct, sustained citation of [[Peter Handke]]'s *Essay on Tiredness* (*Versuch über die Müdigkeit*) in the closing chapter of *The Burnout Society*, "The Society of Tiredness." It is the most load-bearing single source in the book's *constructive* movement: where the preceding chapters diagnose the [[Achievement Society]] as a [[Positivity vs Negativity|positivity-saturated]] regime of self-exploitation and exhaustion, the final chapter borrows Handke's typology of tiredness wholesale to imagine a counter-society. Han adopts Handke's distinction between [[I-Tiredness vs We-Tiredness|"I-tiredness" and "we-tiredness"]], his [[Fundamental Tiredness|"fundamental tiredness"]] (*tiefe Müdigkeit*) "that trusts in the world," his figure of the [[Community of Singularities]] held together by a "friendly *and*," and his images of the New Orpheus, the [[The Pentecostal Company|Pentecostal company]], and the "all in one" of the Dutch still life. Handke is a literary, not theoretical, source; Han reads the poet's essay as already containing the philosophy of a "society to come." (Label: `Directly stated` that the chapter is built on Handke; the framing of Handke as the constructive backbone of the book is `Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation** — and beyond citation, structural appropriation. Across the chapter Han quotes Handke's *Essay on Tiredness* repeatedly, with page references in parentheses (the source's pagination of Handke), and lets Handke's vocabulary — "divisive tiredness," "we-tiredness," "fundamental tiredness," "the Pentecostal company," "the new Orpheus," "the all in one" — carry the entire positive proposal of the book. Han supplies the diagnostic apparatus (achievement society, excess positivity, the [[Burnout]] of the over-active ego); Handke supplies the redemptive counter-image. The poet is not corrected or critiqued but elaborated philosophically (Han threads Heidegger's *Gelassenheit*, the Sabbath, and the *not-to* through Handke's images).

## Explanation

The chapter turns on Handke's typology, which Han imports almost intact and reads as a diagnosis of the achievement society itself:

1. **Divisive tiredness / [[I-Tiredness vs We-Tiredness|I-tiredness]].** Han identifies the exhaustion of the [[Achievement Society]] with what Handke calls "divisive tiredness" — a "solitary tiredness" that "has a separating and isolating effect," strikes one "mute and blind," and "destroys all that is common or shared, all proximity, and even language itself." This is the tiredness of the over-active, depleted ego, in which "[t]he isolated I [das Ich] fills the field of vision entirely." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

2. **[[Fundamental Tiredness|Fundamental tiredness]] / "tiredness that trusts in the world."** Against divisive tiredness Han sets Handke's "eloquent, seeing, reconciliatory tiredness," a "fundamental tiredness" (*tiefe Müdigkeit*) that "opens" the I and "makes room" for the world: "Less I means more world." It is "tiredness that trusts in the world" [*weltvertrauende Müdigkeit*], a *capacity* rather than an incapacity — it "inspires," "allows spirit/intellect [Geist] to emerge," and restores the duality (seeing and being seen, touching and being touched) that solitary tiredness destroys. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

3. **"Tired *with* you," not "tired *of* you" — [[I-Tiredness vs We-Tiredness|we-tiredness]].** The hinge of Han's appropriation: Handke's tiredness "is not 'I-tiredness'… He calls it 'we-tiredness'." Han quotes the formula that grounds the whole counter-society — "I am not tired 'of you,' as he puts it, but rather I am tired 'with you'." This converts tiredness from a symptom of isolation into a medium of community. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

4. **The [[Community of Singularities]] of the "friendly *and*."** Han reads Handke's fundamental tiredness as founding "a community that requires neither belonging nor relation [Verwandtschaft]," in which "[h]uman beings and things show themselves to be connected through a friendly *and*." This "singular community, this community of singularities" is, in Handke's words, "prefigured in a Dutch still life" — the "all in one" in which "a beetle, a snail, a bee, or a butterfly" sit together "at the moment, my moment." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

5. **The New Orpheus and the [[The Pentecostal Company|Pentecostal company]].** Han closes by adopting Handke's two communal images. Handke's "certain tired man can be seen as a new Orpheus," around whom "the wildest beasts gather… and are at last able to join in his tiredness." And his "Pentecostal company that received the Holy Ghost," conceived as "tired to a man," whose "inspiration of tiredness tells them not so much what they should, as what they need not, be." Han proposes that "[i]f 'Pentecostal company' offered a synonym for the society of the future, the society to come might also be called a society of tiredness." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

A sixth, interpretive point: the entire valuation in the chapter is governed by Han's own [[Positivity vs Negativity]] axis — the tiredness of exhaustion is "the tiredness of positive potency" (the *can*, making one "incapable of doing something"); the tiredness that inspires is "tiredness of negative potency, namely of *not-to*." Handke does not work in this vocabulary; Han maps Handke's two tirednesses onto his positive/negative potency distinction and onto Heidegger's *Gelassenheit*, the Sabbath, and the *not-to*. So the citation is exact but the conceptual framing (potency, [[Negativity]], the Sabbath of *not-to*) is Han's. (Label: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Source Passages

From "The Society of Tiredness":

> Peter Handke, in "Essay on Tiredness,"[1] calls it "divisive tiredness": "already the two . . . were irresistibly recoiling, each into . . . private tiredness, not ours, but mine over here and yours over there" (8).

> Handke sets eloquent, seeing, reconciliatory tiredness in opposition to speechless, sightless, divisive tiredness.

> It is "tiredness that trusts in the world" [weltvertrauende Müdigkeit] (33),[5] whereas I-tiredness—"solitary tiredness" (5)—is worldless, world-destroying tiredness. The trusting tiredness "opens" the I and "makes room" (34) for the world.

> Less I means more world: "Now tiredness was my friend. I was back in the world again" (28). Such "fundamental tiredness" (37) brings together all the forms of existence and coexistence that vanish in the course of absolutized activity.

> So let's have a Pindaric ode, not to a victor but to a tired man. I conceive of the Pentecostal company that received the Holy Ghost as tired to a man. The inspiration of tiredness tells them not so much what they should, as what they need not, be. (41)

> Human beings and things show themselves to be connected through a friendly and. Handke sees this singular community, this community of singularities, prefigured in a Dutch still life:

> I have an image for the "all in one": those seventeenth-century, for the most part Dutch floral, still lifes, in which a beetle, a snail, a bee, or a butterfly sits true to life, in the flowers, and although none of these may suspect the presence of others, they are all there together at the moment, my moment. (38)

> Handke's tiredness is not "I-tiredness"; it is not the tiredness of an exhausted ego. He calls it "we-tiredness" (15). I am not tired "of you," as he puts it, but rather I am tired "with you" (26)

> A certain tired man can be seen as a new Orpheus; the wildest beasts gather around him and are at last able to join in his tiredness. Tiredness gives dispersed individuals the keynote" (41).

> If "Pentecostal company" offered a synonym for the society of the future, the society to come might also be called a society of tiredness.
