# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Alain Ehrenberg

## Relationship Type

Disagreement (a running polemic with partial agreement). Across two chapters of *The Burnout Society*, Han engages Alain Ehrenberg's *The Weariness of the Self* (*La Fatigue d'être soi*) as the most important rival diagnosis of late-modern depression. He **accepts** Ehrenberg's historical placement of depression — that it rises in the passage from the disciplinary model to a model of personal initiative — but **rejects** Ehrenberg's "economy of the self" explanation and, in ch. 9, his continued allegiance to the psychoanalytic "negativity scheme." Ehrenberg is a forward-stub author; the named target work is *The Weariness of the Self*.

## Explanation

Han concedes Ehrenberg's core periodization: depression "began its ascent" when the disciplinary "rules of authority and observance of taboos" broke against norms enjoining us "to be ourselves." But he charges Ehrenberg with two faults. First, a wrong *cause*: Ehrenberg "considers depression only from the perspective of the economy of the self" — the imperative only to belong to oneself — whereas for Han the cause is the **pressure to achieve** (the new commandment of late-modern labor society), **impoverished attachment** [*Bindungsarmut*], and the **systemic violence** of achievement society. Second, a wrong *frame*: in ch. 9 Han argues that Ehrenberg "still holds to" the negativity scheme — hidden conflict, the unconscious, the irreducible — and so "attempts to rescue psychoanalysis even though its conditions have disappeared." Finally, Han contests Ehrenberg's reading of Nietzsche: where Ehrenberg equates the late-modern individual with Nietzsche's "sovereign man," Han insists the real figure is the **last man** (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Friedrich Nietzsche]]).

This makes the reference a load-bearing hinge of the [[Achievement Society]] argument: Ehrenberg supplies the depression-history Han adopts and the diagnosis he overturns in favor of [[Auto-Exploitation]], the failure of [[Positivity]] (not [[Negativity]]) to be theorized, and [[Burnout]]. (`Interpretive synthesis` — Han's own verbatim claims, organized.)

## Source Passages

### Agreement on periodization (disciplinary → achievement)

Han quotes Ehrenberg approvingly on where depression comes from:

> Depression began its ascent when the disciplinary model for behaviors, the rules of authority and observance of taboos that gave social classes as well as both sexes a specific destiny, broke against norms that invited us to undertake personal initiative by enjoining us to be ourselves. . . . The depressed individual is unable to measure up; he is tired of having to become himself.

(`Directly stated` that Han adopts the placement: "Alain Ehrenberg locates depression in the transition from disciplinary society to achievement society" and treats the quoted thesis as the shared starting point.)

### Rejection of the "economy of the self"

> Problematically, however, Ehrenberg considers depression only from the perspective of the economy of the self: the social imperative only to belong to oneself makes one depressive.

Han's counter-cause, stated immediately after: "It is not the imperative only to belong to oneself, but the pressure to achieve that causes exhaustive depression." And the two factors Ehrenberg omits: "depression also follows from impoverished attachment [Bindungsarmut]," and "He also overlooks the systemic violence inhabiting achievement society, which provokes psychic infarctions." (`Directly stated`.)

### Rejection of the negativity scheme (ch. 9)

> Ehrenberg's main thesis reads: 'the success of depression lies in the decline of conflict as a reference point upon which the nineteenth-century notion of the self was founded.'

Han's verdict on the conflict/psychoanalysis frame: "Although Ehrenberg recognizes that depression is characterized by an absent relation to conflict, he still holds to the conflict model. . . . In fact, depression defies the conflict model—that is, depression eludes psychoanalysis. Ehrenberg attempts to rescue psychoanalysis even though its conditions have disappeared." (`Directly stated`.)

### The Nietzsche dispute (sovereign man vs. last man)

Ehrenberg, as quoted by Han: "Nietzsche's sovereign man, his own man, was becoming a mass phenomenon." Han's reply: "In fact, Nietzsche would say that that human type in the process of becoming reality en masse is no sovereign superman but 'the last man,' who does nothing but work." (`Directly stated`; developed in [[Byung-Chul Han and Friedrich Nietzsche]].)
