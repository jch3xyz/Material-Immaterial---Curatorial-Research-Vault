# Meghan O'Gieblyn ↔ Hans Moravec

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel / disagreement — routed through Hayles, not direct citation.** [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] never names [[Hans Moravec]] in the two chapters cited here (or, by search, anywhere in *[[God Human Animal Machine|God, Human, Animal, Machine]]*). She names [[Ray Kurzweil]], Nick Bostrom, Stewart Brand, Elon Musk, and Jaron Lanier; the machine-supremacist and substrate-independent positions she anatomizes she attributes to "transhumanism" as a movement. **Moravec is the figure who carries those exact positions *by name* in [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*** — the book O'Gieblyn cites directly in Ch04. The relationship is therefore real but mediated: O'Gieblyn's anonymous "transhumanist" is Hayles's named Moravec, and the bridge is the Hayles text both authors share. This note exists to let the vault read the same download fantasy theologically (O'Gieblyn: [[Transhumanism|transhumanism]] as Christian eschatology) and cybernetically (Hayles/Moravec: disembodied [[Information|information]]) at once. It realizes the forward-stub [[Hans Moravec]] from the O'Gieblyn side.

## Explanation

Two positions O'Gieblyn ascribes to transhumanism are, in the Hayles-Moravec literature, Moravec's signature claims — and reading them side by side makes O'Gieblyn's chapters and the existing [[N. Katherine Hayles and Hans Moravec]] note converge on one object.

**1. Machine-supremacism (the gospel of self-supersession).** O'Gieblyn's Ch04 names the "darker irony" of transhumanism: its "rather depressing gospel message insists that we are inevitably going to be superseded by machines, and that the only way we can survive the Singularity is to become machines ourselves—objects that we for centuries regarded as lower than plants and animals." This is, almost word for word, the position Hayles attributes to Moravec in her conclusion: he is named first among "researchers ... [who] believe" humans will be "displaced as the dominant form of life on the planet by intelligent machines." O'Gieblyn supplies the theological reading of that displacement — it is an *inversion* of the Great Chain of Being, a fall to "a lower ontological status" dressed as ascent — that Hayles, working cybernetically, does not. The two descriptions name the same eschatology of the machine from opposite sides (`Interpretive synthesis`).

**2. Substrate-independence (the mind that survives its medium).** O'Gieblyn's Ch04 traces the genealogy of mind-uploading precisely as Hayles does, because she is reading Hayles: she cites *How We Became Posthuman* by name, quotes Hayles that at the Macy Conferences information "became decontextualized" into "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates," and notes the "functionalist theories of consciousness, which insist that mental states can be 'multiply realizable,' instantiated by any medium, biological or mechanical." That decontextualized, substrate-free information is, in Hayles's own epigram, exactly the abstraction "Moravec decontextualizes ... [into the dream of] the information contained in a brain being downloaded into a computer." So when O'Gieblyn writes that "proponents of mind-uploading ... [argue] that the patterns of our minds are not only abstract and irreducible but potentially immortal," she is describing — through Hayles's frame — Moravec's download fantasy. This is the [[The Body as Data|body-as-data]] / [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] thesis carried to its literal extreme, and the precise hinge of [[Embodiment vs Information]] (`Strongly implied`).

**The disagreement.** Both O'Gieblyn and Hayles refuse the fantasy, but on different grounds, and the difference is the payload of this note. Hayles's refusal is cybernetic and ontological: "human being is first of all embodied being," and "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium," so the download rests on an impossible separation of [[The Materiality of Informatics|materiality]] from [[Information|information]]. O'Gieblyn's refusal is theological and phenomenological: even granting the engineering, mind-uploading may yield "digital selves [that] will entirely lack subjective experience"— "Our resurrected forms might behave much like our current selves, but nothing will be going on inside" — so transhumanism is "merely another attempt to argue that humans are nothing more than computation." Where Hayles diagnoses the download as the lethal *graft* of the posthuman onto [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] (autonomy and mastery extended into "the ultimate privilege of immortality"), O'Gieblyn diagnoses it as the lethal graft of transhumanism onto *Christian eschatology* — "an outgrowth of Christian eschatology" whose Singularity is "an obvious precursor" in Teilhard's Omega Point. The two critiques are complementary readings of one fantasy: Hayles secularizing it as disembodied [[Information|information]], O'Gieblyn re-enchanting it as a [[Disenchantment|disenchanted]] age's botched [[Resurrection]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).

**Ch08 (the cybernetic-theological convergence in another key).** The Ch08 source does not name Moravec either; its named figure is Bostrom. But it supplies the second half of the bridge the planner asks for: O'Gieblyn there restates the cybernetic objection in Hayles's own terms — "information cannot be 'ungrounded,' without a material instantiation," "Claude Shannon ... insisted that information had to exist in some kind of physical medium" — and shows the *same* substrate-independence dream (information "taken to be freefloating and immaterial") underwriting both mind-uploading (Ch04) and the simulation hypothesis (Ch08). Read with Ch04, Ch08 confirms that O'Gieblyn's quarrel is with the identical decoupling of [[Information|information]] from matter that, in Hayles, Moravec personifies — which is why this note's two source chapters together let the download fantasy be read as [[The Computational Universe|computational-universe]] eschatology and as disembodied-information [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] simultaneously (`Strongly implied`).

## Source Passages

The load-bearing O'Gieblyn passages: (1) Ch04's "gospel message" of supersession — humans must "become machines ourselves—objects that we for centuries regarded as lower than plants and animals" — the machine-supremacism Hayles attributes to Moravec by name; (2) Ch04's Hayles citation establishing the substrate-independent information that is, in Hayles's epigram, exactly what "Moravec decontextualizes"; (3) Ch04's "nothing will be going on inside," O'Gieblyn's phenomenological refusal of the download; (4) Ch08's Shannon/"material instantiation" passage, the cybernetic objection she shares with Hayles against the immaterial-information dream. Moravec himself is quoted only in the companion note [[N. Katherine Hayles and Hans Moravec]]; here the Moravec side is supplied through Hayles, whom O'Gieblyn cites.

## Confidence

**Medium.** The two positions are genuinely shared and the textual chain is solid: O'Gieblyn *cites Hayles by name* in Ch04, and Hayles is the source in which Moravec is the named carrier of both machine-supremacism and substrate-independent information — so the parallel is not invented, it runs along a citation O'Gieblyn herself makes. The reason it is not "high" is the planner's own caution, now verified: **Moravec is not named in O'Gieblyn's text** (search of Ch04, Ch08, and the bibliography returns nothing). Every claim that O'Gieblyn's "transhumanist" *is* Moravec is therefore this vault's `Interpretive synthesis`, licensed by the shared Hayles frame, not O'Gieblyn's assertion. The verbatim quotations below are O'Gieblyn's; Moravec's own words live in [[N. Katherine Hayles and Hans Moravec]].
