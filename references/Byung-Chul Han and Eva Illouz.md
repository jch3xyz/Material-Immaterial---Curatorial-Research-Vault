# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Eva Illouz

## Relationship Type

Disagreement / adoption-with-correction: Han takes over Illouz's term "emotional capitalism" from *Cold Intimacies: The Making of Emotional Capitalism* and then rejects her genealogy of it. Illouz grounds emotional capitalism in the ascetic, Protestant-inflected capitalism analyzed by [[Max Weber]]; Han argues this is a mislocation — for him the productive deployment of emotion is specific to *neoliberal* psychopolitics and only becomes possible with a switch to immaterial production. He takes the label but reroutes the history.

## Explanation

[[Eva Illouz]] names and theorizes [[Emotional Capitalism]] in *Cold Intimacies: The Making of Emotional Capitalism* (2007). [[Byung-Chul Han]] adopts the term in chapter 9 of *Psychopolitics* but mounts a sustained critique of how Illouz deploys it.

Han's objections are threefold. First, Illouz collapses conceptual distinctions he considers fundamental — she "equates feelings and emotions without drawing any conceptual distinction at all," and her roster of sociological affects ("indifference," "guilt") mixes genuine feelings with emotions and affects as though the categories were interchangeable. Han's own tripartite scheme (feeling / emotion / affect — see [[Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect (Han)]]) is what makes it possible to identify *emotion* specifically as the exploitable register under neoliberalism.

Second, and most important, Illouz mislocates the phenomenon historically. She traces emotional capitalism back to the anxiety at the heart of the Weberian, Protestant-ascetic entrepreneur — but Han argues that what Weber analyzes is "the ascetic capitalism of accumulation, which obeys rational logic more than it follows emotional logic." That form of capitalism neither generates nor requires the productive mobilization of emotion; it precedes the conditions that make emotion economically valuable. Consumer capitalism — in which "not use value but emotive or cultic value plays a constitutive role" — is a different formation entirely.

Third, Illouz "fails to account for the fact that emotion comes to possess value for capitalism only when a switch to immaterial production occurs." The emotional boom is not a perennial feature of capitalism but a historically specific one, arising in Han's own times, when communicative and affective labor displace material production and make emotions into "raw material" for productivity. Han's counterproposal is that [[Emotional Capitalism]] names the affective dimension of neoliberal [[Psychopolitics]]: emotion is exploitable precisely because it is "dynamic, situative and performative" — and neoliberal psychopolitics "seizes on emotion in order to influence actions on this pre-reflexive level."

Despite these disagreements, Han relies on Illouz's evidence. A quotation from *Cold Intimacies* appears in note 4 of the chapter, where Han borrows Illouz's citation of a Hewlett-Packard manager's description of corporate culture as "an affective relationship" — using her fieldwork to illustrate his own, differently framed, thesis about emotion as immaterial raw material.

## Byung-Chul Han (The Agony of Eros)

*The Agony of Eros* (2012) is a second, deeper site of Han's quarrel with Illouz — this time on the *crisis of love* rather than emotional labor — and it engages three different Illouz books across three chapters. The disagreement keeps the same shape as in *Psychopolitics*: Han borrows Illouz's material while rejecting her framing. But the stakes shift from the sociology of the workplace to the fate of [[Eros]] and [[The Other]].

**The erosion of the Other, not an excess of freedom (ch. 2, "Melancholia").** Han opens the book by granting that "the end of love has been announced many times" — that love is said to be "foundering because of endless freedom of choice, the overabundance of options, and the compulsion for perfection." He names Illouz as the author of this diagnosis: "Eva Illouz has traced this state of affairs back to the rationalization of love and expanding technologies of choice." He then relocates the cause entirely. Illouz's "sociological theory," he argues, "fails to recognize that another influence is now underway, which is corroding love far more than endless freedom or unlimited possibilities." The crisis is not quantitative but ontological: "The crisis of love does not derive from too many others so much as from the erosion of the Other." Where Illouz sees the modern lover paralyzed by *too many* others to choose among, Han sees the vanishing of *the* Other as such — the placeless, atopic alterity that alone can sustain eros — and its corollary in "the mounting narcissification of the Self." (Directly stated.)

**Positivization, not "feminization" (ch. 4, "Bare Life").** Han turns to Illouz's *Consuming the Romantic Utopia*, where she claims that "love is now becoming 'feminized'" — the vocabulary of romance ("nice," "intimate," "quiet," "peaceful," "comfortable," "sweet," "gentle") drawing men and women alike into "a female sphere of sentiment." Han rejects the diagnosis while accepting the phenomenon: "But counter to Illouz's diagnosis, love is not simply being 'feminized.' Rather, in the course of a positivization of all spheres of life, it is being domesticated into a consumer formula devoid of risk and daring, without excess or madness." What Illouz reads as a gendering of love, Han reads as its subjection to [[Positivity]] — the elimination of all [[Negativity]], "all negative feeling," so that love degrades "into an object of consumption, a matter of hedonistic calculation" and "the desire for the Other is giving way to the comfort of the Same" (see [[Hell of the Same]]). (Directly stated.)

**Desire, fantasy, and expectation (ch. 6, "Fantasy").** The book's most sustained engagement takes on Illouz's *Why Love Hurts: A Sociological Explanation*. Where Illouz argues that increasing choice *rationalizes* desire, Han inverts the claim: "Counter to what Illouz assumes, desire is not 'rationalized' today by increasing opportunities for, and criteria of, choice. Instead, unchecked freedom of choice is threatening to bring about the end of desire. Desire is always desire for the Other." Illouz, he charges, "does not take the libido-economical particularity of desire into account" — she cannot distinguish desire (for the Other, nourished by "the negativity of privation and absence") from the wants and needs consumer culture manufactures. He presses the same wedge against her sociology of disappointment: "It is not heightened fantasy, but—if anything—higher expectations that are responsible for the mounting disappointment experienced in contemporary society. Problematically, however, Illouz does not distinguish between fantasy and expectation." Han's counter-thesis is that today's high-information, hypervisible image regime "stifles fantasy" — that "pornography—which maximizes visual information, as it were—destroys erotic fantasy" (see [[Pornography (Han)]]). As with the earlier chapters, he even repurposes Illouz's literary evidence: her socioeconomic reading of Emma Bovary he rereads, via Bataille, as a "logic of expenditure and loss." (Directly stated.)

Across all three, the pattern set in *Psychopolitics* holds: Han adopts Illouz's terrain and her examples while rerouting the theory. The workplace critique argued that emotion becomes a means of production only under neoliberal immaterial labor; the love critique argues that eros dies not from a surfeit of choice but from the disappearance of the [[The Other|Other]] into the [[Hell of the Same|Same]].

## Source Passages

### Illouz named and critiqued (Weber-mislocation)

> Eva Illouz's *Cold Intimacies: The Making of Emotional Capitalism* offers no answer to the question of why it is that feelings experience a boom under conditions of capitalism, in particular. What is more, the book equates feelings and emotions without drawing any conceptual distinction at all. Nor is it very useful to locate the question of feelings under capitalism at its inaugural stages: 'Weber's Protestant ethic contains at its core a thesis about the role of emotions in economic action, for it is the anxiety provoked by an inscrutable divinity which is at the heart of the capitalist entrepreneur's frantic activity.' It is mistaken to understand anxiety in terms of emotion. Anxiety is a feeling. Its corresponding temporality proves incompatible with affect. Affect is not a constant state. As such, it lacks the constancy that defines feeling. It is the constant feeling of anxiety that would entail frantic entrepreneurial activity. But what Weber analyses is the ascetic capitalism of accumulation, which obeys rational logic more than it follows emotional logic. Accordingly, capitalism of this sort does not feed into consumer capitalism, which derives its profits from emotions. Moreover, consumer capitalism operates through the selling and consumption of meanings and emotions. It is not use value but emotive or cultic value that plays a constitutive role in the economy of consumption. By the same token, Illouz fails to account for the fact that emotion comes to possess value for capitalism only when a switch to immaterial production occurs.

> Emotions have become a means of production only in our own times.

### Illouz's conceptual roster criticized

> All of these references to various sociological theories of emotion do nothing at all to explain the boom of emotion today. This corresponds to Illouz's neglect of conceptual distinctions between feeling, emotion and affect. After all, 'indifference' and 'guilt' are neither affects nor emotions. It is only meaningful to speak of the feeling of guilt.

## Confidence

High. In *Psychopolitics* Han names Illouz and her book explicitly, quotes her directly (the Weber passage is cited to *Cold Intimacies*, p. 1, in the chapter's notes), and his critique is a sustained engagement across the whole of ch. 9. In *The Agony of Eros* he names Illouz and three distinct books (*Cold Intimacies* by lineage, and by title *Consuming the Romantic Utopia* and *Why Love Hurts*), quotes each directly, and rejects her framing across chapters 2, 4, and 6. All substantive claims above are directly stated in the raw chapters.
