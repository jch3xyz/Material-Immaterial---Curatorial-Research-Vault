# Lucy Lippard ↔ N. Katherine Hayles

## Relationship Type

Conceptual parallel / possible resonance. Neither author cites the other; Lippard's *Six Years* (1973) predates Hayles's *How We Became Posthuman* (1999) by a quarter century, and they work in different fields (art criticism, literary/media theory). Every cross-author claim below is therefore an **Interpretive synthesis** or **Speculative connection** made by this vault, not an asserted historical link.

## Explanation

Lippard's "dematerialization of the art object" and Hayles's "disembodiment of information" are structural twins: both name a postwar *becoming-immaterial* — a dream of escaping the material, economic, and bodily substrate — and both authors document, in remarkably parallel ways, how that escape fails. This is the single most load-bearing cross-author link in the vault because it lets the art-historical episode of [[Conceptual Art]] and the cybernetics-to-posthumanism episode read as two instances of the same [[Material vs Immaterial]] fantasy and its same defeat.

The fantasy: Lippard hoped "idea-art" would be "forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status," a weapon to "transform the art world into a democratic institution"; Hayles names the cybernetic dream of [[Information]] as "a thing separate from the medium instantiating it." The defeat is symmetrical. For Hayles the constraint is ontological — [[The Materiality of Informatics|materiality]] is non-optional: "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium." For Lippard the constraint is economic and documentary — the dematerialized work always leaves a residue (a xerox, a catalogue, a photograph, a mailed piece) and is promptly re-commodified: three years on "the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums" and "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries." Read together, [[Dematerialization]] is the art-economic register of what Hayles diagnoses as a metaphysical impossibility (`Interpretive synthesis`).

Most precisely, the *internal dissents* mirror each other. Inside Conceptual art the Art-Language group rebutted Lippard's own term: dematerialized works "are nevertheless matter in one of its forms, either solid-state, gas-state, liquid-state"; and Douglas Huebler-era Lippard concedes "there is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality. To deny it is to descend to irony." These map almost exactly onto Hayles's thesis that [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]]. The Art-Language "matter-state" rebuttal and the "burden of physicality" are, in this reading, the art world independently arriving at Hayles's slogan — the same correction, from inside the same dream, decades earlier (`Speculative connection`). This is why the link also touches [[Embodiment vs Information]] and [[Virtuality]]: the art object's "[[Dematerialization]]" is a regional dialect of "information lost its body."

## Source Passages

Quoted passages establishing the parallel. The Lippard side is drawn from *Six Years*; note that two of the most pointed statements are **artist statements quoted by Lippard**, not Lippard's own prose. The Hayles side is supplied for the parallel but its canonical home is the Hayles ingest.

> for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium

(Hayles, *How We Became Posthuman*, "Toward Embodied Virtuality" — supplied here for the parallel; canonical in the Hayles ingest.)

> They may not be an art-object as we know it in its traditional matter-state, but they are nevertheless matter in one of its forms, either solid-state, gas-state, liquid-state.

(The Art-Language group, in a 1968 letter-essay to Lippard and Chandler, quoted in *Six Years*.)

> There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality. To deny it is to descend to irony.

(Lippard, *Six Years*, in the 1970 section.)

> whatever minor revolutions in communication have been achieved by the process of dematerializing the object (easily mailed work, catalogues and magazine pieces, primarily art that can be shown inexpensively and unobtrusively in infinite locations at one time), art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries.

(Lippard, *Six Years*, "Postface.")

## Confidence

Medium. The textual evidence on the Lippard side is strong and verbatim, and the thematic isomorphism (becoming-immaterial → non-optional substrate → internal dissent) is tight and non-trivial. Confidence is held to medium — not high — precisely because the link is *constructed by this vault*: there is no citation in either direction, the authors are a generation and a discipline apart, and "dematerialization" (an economic/documentary claim about the art object) and "disembodiment of information" (an ontological claim about information) are not identical claims. The synthesis is offered as the most valuable interpretive bridge in the vault, explicitly labeled `Interpretive synthesis` / `Speculative connection`, not as historical influence.
