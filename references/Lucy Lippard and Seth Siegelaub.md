# Lucy Lippard ↔ Seth Siegelaub

## Relationship Type

**Shared tradition and operative collaboration — strategy dovetailing with theory.** [[Seth Siegelaub]] is not an author Lippard cites at a distance but the dealer-organizer whose practical models she chronicles as the working embodiment of her own idea of a dematerialized art. Lippard supplies the term ([[Dematerialization]]); Siegelaub supplies the distribution apparatus — the catalogue-as-exhibition, the magazine-exhibition, the worldwide free mailing — that makes a commodity-free art operational. She states the convergence directly: his strategy "dovetailed with my own notions of a dematerialized art that would be free of art-world commodity status." He is the load-bearing figure for the concept [[Exhibition as Medium]]. (Note: Siegelaub is **not** named in the 1966, 1967, or 1971 chronicle sections of *Six Years*; this note draws only from "Escape Attempts" and the 1969 chronicle, where he and his exhibitions appear.)

## Explanation

Lippard frames Siegelaub as the man who "reinvent[ed] the role of the 'art dealer' as distributor extraordinaire" — a reconception of the dealer's function away from selling objects and toward circulating information. The hinge of his model is the inversion of catalogue and exhibition. Where a conventional catalogue documents a show, Siegelaub's catalogue *is* the show: his January 1969 exhibition was captioned "The exhibition consists of (the ideas communicated in) the catalog; the physical presence (of the work) is supplementary to the catalog," and his *One Month* (March 1969) is recorded by Lippard as the "First exhibition to exist in catalogue form alone; distributed free worldwide." The publication ceases to be *about* art and becomes art.

The theoretical key that licenses this inversion is Siegelaub's primary-vs-secondary-information distinction, which he gives in his interview with the critic Ursula Meyer. A reproduction of a physical painting is "secondhand," "bastardized and distorted," because the painting "depends upon its physical presence, in terms of color, scale, material and context." But dematerialized work, "when it has become an abstraction," is "not distorted and altered by its representation in books and catalogues. It becomes primary information." For such work, print is not a lossy channel but the native register — so, as he puts it, "the catalogue can become the exhibition." This is exactly the [[Material vs Immaterial]] logic transposed into the art world: only work whose existence does not "depend upon its physical presence" can travel undistorted as [[Information]], and that immateriality is the precondition for escaping the object's commodity status.

The convergence with Lippard is mutual and explicit. Her own curatorial practice runs on the same principle — her "557,087"/"955,000" exhibitions used randomly arranged packs of index cards as catalogues — and *Six Years* is itself an exhibition-medium rather than a report on one. Siegelaub's 1970 *Studio International* "magazine exhibition," for which he gave six critic-curators (including Lippard) eight pages each, is the clearest joint instance: artist, dealer, and critic all treating the periodical page as primary exhibition space. Lippard reads his work as a practical realization of a politics she shared: a "decommodified 'idea-art'" that could not be "bought and sold." Both knew the escape was provisional — by 1973 the major conceptualists were "selling work for substantial sums" — but the model of distribution-native, commodity-resistant art that Siegelaub built is, for Lippard, the period's most durable structural invention, and it prefigures later [[New Media Art]] and network-native practice.
