# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Hans Moravec

## Relationship Type

**Disagreement — and founding provocation.** [[Hans Moravec]] is the recurring antagonist of *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*: not an interlocutor [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] argues *with* so much as the figure whose fantasy she argues *against* from the book's first sentence to its last pages. The roboticist's claim in *Mind Children: The Future of Robot and Human Intelligence* that "it will soon be possible to download human consciousness into a computer" is, for Hayles, the dream that "struck me as a nightmare" — and the provocation that set the whole six-year project in motion. Moravec is a **forward-stub author** (no work in `raw/`); he appears in the vault only as Hayles reads him.

## Explanation

Moravec functions in three escalating registers across the book, and each of the three source chapters supplies one.

1. **Origin / the nightmare (chapter 1, [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality|Toward Embodied Virtuality]]).** Hayles tells the reader that the book *began* with Moravec. Reading *Mind Children* and "enjoying the ingenious variety of his robots," she hits the download passage — Moravec's fantasy of a robot surgeon who purees the brain layer by layer, reading off the information and transferring it to a computer, so that the patient "wakens to find his consciousness exactly the same as it was before." Her reaction is incredulity at a category error: "How, I asked myself, was it possible for someone of Moravec's obvious intelligence to believe that mind could be separated from body?" This is the generative wound of the book. From it she traces a whole genealogy of disembodiment — Wiener's proposal "to telegraph a human being," Minsky's memories "imported, intact and unchanged, to computer disks," *Star Trek*'s "Beam me up, Scotty" — all sharing "the belief that information can circulate unchanged among different material substrates." Moravec is the cleanest, most literal instance of that belief and the one she names first.

2. **The logical mechanism / decontextualization (chapter 3, [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/05 Contesting for the Body of Information - The Macy Conferences on Cybernetics|Contesting for the Body of Information]]).** Here Hayles diagnoses *how* the nightmare is built. Shannon and Wiener divorced [[Information]] from meaning so it would "be calculated as the same value regardless of the contexts in which it was embedded." In context this was "an appropriate and sensible decision"; taken out of context it "allowed information to be conceptualized as if it were an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates, as when Moravec envisions the information contained in a brain being downloaded into a computer." Her epigrammatic formulation pins the relationship exactly: **"The theory decontextualizes information; Moravec decontextualizes the theory."** Moravec is thus the endpoint of a double abstraction — he takes a local engineering convenience and inflates it into an ontology in which "a reified concept of information is treated as if it were fully commensurate with the complexities of human thought." This is the precise hinge of [[Embodiment vs Information]] and of [[The Body as Data]]: the download fantasy is the substrate-independence thesis carried to its literal extreme.

3. **The political payload / the lethal graft (chapter 11, [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/13 Conclusion - What Does It Mean to be Posthuman?|Conclusion]]).** The conclusion shows that Moravec's posthuman is not the *abolition* of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] but its expansion. "What is lethal is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self." Moravec is her worked example: "When Moravec imagines 'you' choosing to download yourself into a computer, thereby obtaining through technological mastery the ultimate privilege of immortality, he is not abandoning the autonomous liberal subject but is expanding its perogatives into the realm of the posthuman." This is the crux the planner flags: the download appears to dissolve the bounded human, but it actually preserves and extends the liberal subject's deepest assumptions — autonomy, mastery, the location of the self in a disembodied cognition — now equipped with "effective immortality." Moravec also voices the *apocalyptic* register Hayles works to defuse: he is named first among "researchers ... [who] believe" humans will be "displaced as the dominant form of life on the planet by intelligent machines," a view that "echoes the deeply pessimistic sentiments of [[Warren McCulloch]] in his old age."

Against all three registers stands Hayles's counter-commitment: "human being is first of all embodied being," and "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium." The download is impossible (and undesirable) not because the science is wrong but because it rests on a prior "imaginary act" — the separation of [[Material vs Immaterial|materiality from information]] — that the book exists to contest. Crucially, Moravec is the foil that lets Hayles distinguish her **own** posthuman from his: hers is "grounded in embodied actuality rather than disembodied information," and "the best possible time to contest for what the posthuman means is now." The disagreement is therefore not symmetrical debate but a sustained refusal in which Moravec supplies the position to be refused.

## Source Passages

The three load-bearing moments map onto the three registers: (1) the opening of chapter 1, where the *Mind Children* download passage is named as "a roboticist's dream that struck me as a nightmare" and the book's origin; (2) chapter 3's epigram "The theory decontextualizes information; Moravec decontextualizes the theory," which locates Moravec as the logical endpoint of substrate-independent information; (3) the conclusion's diagnosis that Moravec's download "is not abandoning the autonomous liberal subject but is expanding its perogatives into the realm of the posthuman," paired with "what is lethal ... is the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self."

## Confidence

**High.** Moravec is named, quoted, and structurally positioned across all three chapters; Hayles explicitly identifies him as the book's point of origin and returns to him as her exhibit in the conclusion. The one interpretive layer — reading the download as an *expansion* rather than an abandonment of the liberal subject — is Hayles's own stated claim, not a synthesis added here. The only quotations *of Moravec himself* are mediated through Hayles's paraphrase (his exact words from *Mind Children* are not in `raw/`); the verbatim passages below are Hayles's, so the relationship is documented from her side.
