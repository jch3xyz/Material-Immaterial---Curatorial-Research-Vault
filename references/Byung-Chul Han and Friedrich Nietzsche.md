# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Friedrich Nietzsche

## Relationship Type

Direct citation and conceptual grounding. Across *Psychopolitics* Han quotes Nietzsche verbatim — in quotation marks, with chapter-and-volume notes — at three load-bearing junctures, deploying him both as an ally against the regime of [[Positivity]] and as the diagnostician who named, avant la lettre, the statistical sameness at the root of [[Big Data]].

## Explanation

Nietzsche functions for Han less as a passing reference than as a recurring witness summoned to underwrite the book's central oppositions. The citation works in three registers. (1) In "Healing as Killing" (ch. 6), Han quotes Nietzsche on suffering as the source of the soul's "profundity, secret, mask, spirit" in order to ground his claim that [[Negativity]] — here, pain — is constitutive of [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)|experience (Erfahrung)]] and of the soul's "defining tautness and depth," against the neoliberal imperative of self-optimization and total [[Positivity]] (`Directly stated`). (2) In "Big Data" (ch. 11), Han quotes Nietzsche's invective against statistical reason — statistics proving "how vulgar and disgustingly uniform the masses are" — and Nietzsche's formula *die Menschen zunehmen im Gleichwerden* (people grow equal / grow fat as they become the same), which Han reads as the very root of the sameness that [[Big Data]] and [[Dataism]] amplify: dataism is "augmenting Zunehmen im Gleichwerden, or hypertrophied sameness." This grafts Nietzsche directly onto Han's own lexicon of [[Gleichschaltung]] and the [[Hell of the Same]], and onto the argument that [[Big Data]] is "wholly blind to the event" because it calculates only averages (`Directly stated`). (3) In "Beyond the Subject" (ch. 12), Han opens with Nietzsche's notion of the "naturification" (*Vernatürlichung*) of the human being, which includes "readiness for what is absolutely sudden and thwarting," to ground his concept of the Event as that which "annuls what has held until now" and "breaks the subject open and wrests it from subjection" (`Directly stated`). The same chapter then routes Nietzsche through [[Michel Foucault]], who "following Nietzsche's lead" develops the conception of history "that deals with events in terms of their most unique characteristics" — so Nietzsche is also the upstream source for the Han–Foucault account of the event and of experience as that which wrenches the subject from itself (`Strongly implied`). The relationship is therefore one of sustained direct citation in the service of diagnosis: Nietzsche supplies the affirmation of suffering, the abhorrence of statistical uniformity, and the readiness for the sudden that together stand against neoliberal psychopolitics.

## Source Passages

From "Healing as Killing" (ch. 6), Nietzsche on suffering as the source of the soul's depth:

> That tension of the soul in unhappiness which cultivates its strength . . . its inventiveness and courage in enduring, persevering, interpreting, and exploiting suffering, and whatever has been granted to it of profundity, secret, mask, spirit, cunning, greatness - was it not granted through suffering, through the discipline of great suffering?

Han's framing of the quotation:

> Pain is constitutive for experience (Erfahrung). Life that consists wholly of positive emotions and the sensation of 'flow' is not human at all. The human soul owes its defining tautness and depth precisely to negativity

From "Big Data" (ch. 11), Nietzsche against statistical reason:

> Statistics proves that there are laws in history. Yes, it proves how vulgar and disgustingly uniform the masses are. You should have kept statistics in Athens! Then you would have sensed the difference! The more inferior and un-individual the masses are, the more rigorous the statistical law.

And, on the *Gleichwerden* of the masses, with Han's gloss:

> For Nietzsche, the figures of statistics prove only that man is a herd animal. He observes that *die Menschen zunehmen im Gleichwerden*. This means both that 'people are increasingly growing equal' and that 'people batten [i.e., grow fat] as they become the same'. Such Gleichschaltung also characterizes our contemporary society of transparency and information.

From "Beyond the Subject" (ch. 12), Nietzsche on naturification and the sudden:

> What Nietzsche refers to as the 'naturificatiori' (Vernatürlichung) of the human being includes 'readiness for what is absolutely sudden and thwarting (das absolut Plötzliche und Durchkreuzende)'

## Confidence

High. Han names Nietzsche explicitly at all three sites, places the quoted material in quotation marks, and supplies bibliographic notes (the *Basic Writings*; the *Unpublished Writings from the Period of Unfashionable Observations*; the *Nachgelassene Fragmente 1880–1882*). The three deployments — suffering/depth, statistical sameness, and the readiness for the sudden — are each directly stated. The only interpretive step is reading Nietzsche as the upstream source of the Han–Foucault account of the event, which Han himself marks ("Following Nietzsche's lead, Foucault also advances a conception of history . . .").
