# Shoshana Zuboff ↔ Karl Marx

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel grounded in the text's own deliberate invocation.** Unlike the vault's purely interpretive Zuboff bridges (e.g. [[Shoshana Zuboff and Byung-Chul Han]], [[Shoshana Zuboff and Yuval Noah Harari]]), this one is anchored in language Zuboff *chose* to echo: her keystone category, [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]], is a deliberate structural pun on Marx's "means of production," and she states the analogy explicitly — "Just as industrial capitalism was driven to the continuous intensification of the means of production, so surveillance capitalists are now locked in a cycle of continuous intensification of the means of behavioral modification." Marx is a **forward-stub author** in this vault (no book in `raw/`; he already carries reference notes from [[Byung-Chul Han and Karl Marx]], [[Donna Haraway and Karl Marx]], [[Marshall McLuhan and Karl Marx]], [[Yuval Noah Harari and Karl Marx]]). The relationship here is best read as **Zuboff thinking with Marx beyond Marx**: she adopts the Marxian grammar of a *means* of value-creation, the rising organic composition / intensification of that means, the subordination of older forms to it, and the diagnosis of a "fictional commodity," while relocating the whole apparatus from production to *behavioral prediction and modification*. Where the constituent claims rest on Zuboff's explicit naming of Marx the label is `Directly stated`; where the note ties them into one coherent parallel the layer is `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Explanation

Zuboff builds [[Surveillance Capitalism]] on a borrowed skeleton. Marx's *Capital* is organized around the **means of production** — the instruments, machinery, and material conditions whose private ownership defines class power and whose competitive intensification drives the system forward. Zuboff transposes every joint of that skeleton onto a new substrate. The defining asset is no longer the means of production but [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]]; the engine of the system is no longer the intensification of production but "a cycle of continuous intensification of the means of behavioral modification"; and the older Marxian asset is not abolished but *demoted* — "Under surveillance capitalism, the 'means of production' serves the 'means of behavioral modification.'" The Marxian locus of power is rewritten in the same breath: "Power was once identified with the ownership of the means of production, but it is now identified with ownership of the means of behavioral modification that is [[Big Other]]." This is not loose analogy; it is a one-to-one re-mapping that depends on the reader hearing Marx underneath.

The parallel deepens at three further points. First, **the subordination of production to extraction**: in [[A Coup from Above|ch.18]] Zuboff describes surveillance capitalism as "a profit-seeking form in which production is subordinated to extraction," restaging the Marxian primacy of production as a primacy of *raw-material seizure* (her [[Behavioral Surplus]]). Second, **the fictional commodity**: invoking Karl Polanyi rather than Marx by name, she names behavior the "fourth 'fictional commodity'" after land, labor, and money — extending the Marxian-Polanyian critique of capitalism's tendency to commodify what was never produced for sale, now applied to human experience itself. Third, and most pointed, Zuboff stages a **disagreement with Marx as theorist of revolution**: "Marx grasped the world with his thickly articulated theory, but with only the power of his ideas, he could not implement his vision." The surveillance capitalists, she argues, are "sui generis utopianists" who, unlike Marx, "seize the world in practice" — their theories thin, their power "monumental and largely unimpeded." The Marxian dream of a planned order returns, inverted, as a corporate one: [[The Instrumentarian Collective|"Instrumentarian society is a planned society, produced through total control of the means of behavioral modification."]]

Two real divergences keep this from collapsing into pastiche. (1) **Where surplus comes from.** Marxian surplus value is extracted from labor inside the production process; Zuboff's [[Behavioral Surplus]] is extracted from *lived experience itself*, "from the depths" — outside any wage relation, from people who are neither workers nor, in the usual sense, customers. The exploited is no longer the laborer but the population rendered as behavioral data. (2) **Who acts.** Marx kept faith in the dialectic and the revolutionary subject; Zuboff's whole point in the ch.14 passage is that *theory without practical power changes nothing* and that the surveillance capitalists have outflanked the Marxian script by acquiring the practical means Marx lacked — concentrations of computation, capital, and the asymmetry of knowledge ([[Instrumentarian Power]]) that produce "false consciousness... no longer produced by the hidden facts of class and their relation to production but rather by the hidden facts of instrumentarian power's command over the division of learning in society." Class is displaced by the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning]]. Read together, Zuboff retains Marx's *form* of critique — a named means of value-creation, owned, intensified, and productive of domination — and replaces its *content*, moving the scene of exploitation from the factory floor to the rendition of behavior, and the locus of inequality from class to knowledge. This anchors the pair on [[Material vs Immaterial]] (production of things → extraction of behavioral data) and [[Freedom vs Control]].

## Source Passages

### Passage 1 — the explicit Marx echo: intensification of the means

`Directly stated` (the analogy is Zuboff's own) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the structural reading)

> "Just as industrial capitalism was driven to the continuous intensification of the means of production, so surveillance capitalists are now locked in a cycle of continuous intensification of the means of behavioral modification."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/13 Chapter 11 - The Right to the Future Tense]].

This is the load-bearing sentence of the entire reference. Zuboff does not merely allude to Marx; she builds an explicit "just as... so..." parallel between the *means of production* and her own coinage, [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]]. The same chapter establishes the demotion that the parallel implies — "the means of production are subordinated to an elaborate new means of behavioral modification" — restating Marx's central category only to subordinate it.

### Passage 2 — the means of production *serves* the means of behavioral modification

`Directly stated`

> "Under surveillance capitalism, the “means of production” serves the “means of behavioral modification.”"

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/14 Chapter 12 - Two Species of Power]].

Zuboff puts both Marxian-and-coined phrases in scare quotes in the same sentence, making the borrowing unmistakable and stating its inversion: the Marxian master-category is now the *servant* of hers. Machine processes "replace human relationships so that certainty can replace trust" — the relations of production give way to the relations of [[A Utopia of Certainty|certainty]].

### Passage 3 — the locus of power: ownership relocated from production to modification

`Directly stated`

> "Power was once identified with the ownership of the means of production, but it is now identified with ownership of the means of behavioral modification that is Big Other."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/15 Chapter 13 - Big Other and the Rise of Instrumentarian Power]].

The Marxian thesis that power follows ownership of the means of production is preserved as a *grammar* and rewritten in its *object*. The same passage performs the displacement of class: "False consciousness is no longer produced by the hidden facts of class and their relation to production but rather by the hidden facts of [[Instrumentarian Power|instrumentarian power]]'s command over the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning in society]]."

### Passage 4 — disagreement: Marx the theorist vs the practitioners of utopia

`Directly stated` (Zuboff's explicit contrast)

> "Marx grasped the world with his thickly articulated theory, but with only the power of his ideas, he could not implement his vision. Long after the publication of Marx's theories, men such as Lenin, Stalin, and Mao applied them to real life."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/16 Chapter 14 - A Utopia of Certainty]].

Here Zuboff names Marx directly and turns the parallel into a *disagreement*: the surveillance capitalists are "sui generis utopianists" who, unlike Marx, "seize the world in practice." The Marxian failure — theory without the practical power to implement it — is precisely the gap the surveillance capitalists have closed with computation and capital. This is the note's strongest single warrant for naming Marx an explicit, not merely interpretive, interlocutor.

### Passage 5 — the planned society returns as the instrumentarian collective

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the Marx parallel)

> "Instrumentarian society is a planned society, produced through total control of the means of behavioral modification."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/17 Chapter 15 - The Instrumentarian Collective]].

The Marxian-socialist dream of a rationally *planned* social order reappears, inverted, as a corporate-behavioral one. "Total control of the means of behavioral modification" is the means-of-production grammar carried to its planning-society conclusion — but the planners are the [[The Instrumentarian Collective|surveillance capitalists]] and their behavioral scientists (Skinner, Pentland), not a revolutionary class.

### Passage 6 — production subordinated to extraction

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the Marx parallel)

> "It is a profit-seeking form in which production is subordinated to extraction as surveillance capitalists unilaterally claim control over human, societal, and political territories"

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/20 Chapter 18 - A Coup from Above]].

The Marxian primacy of *production* is explicitly demoted in favor of *extraction* — the seizure of [[Behavioral Surplus]] from lived experience. The continuation of the passage extends the Marxian-Polanyian critique of the "fictional commodity": behavior becomes "the fourth 'fictional commodity'" after Polanyi's land, labor, and money, none of which were produced for sale.

### Passage 7 — surplus extracted from the depths, not from labor (the divergence)

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the divergence from Marx)

> "From the point of view of the prediction imperative, personalization is a means of “individualizing” supply operations in order to secure a continuous flow of behavioral surplus from the depths."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/11 Chapter 9 - Rendition from the Depths]].

This marks the **key divergence**. Marxian surplus value is wrung from labor power inside the production process; Zuboff's [[Behavioral Surplus]] is a "continuous flow... from the depths" — personality, emotion, intention, "your most intimate core" — secured outside any wage relation. The exploited is no longer the worker but the rendered population. The Marxian *form* (a surplus seized to produce value) survives; its *site* migrates from the factory to the interior of experience, re-inflecting the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis.

## Confidence

**High.** This is the most text-grounded of the vault's Zuboff–to–unfingested-author bridges. Zuboff names Marx directly (ch.14), puts "means of production" in scare quotes beside her own "means of behavioral modification" (ch.12), builds an explicit "just as... so..." analogy (ch.11), and reproduces the Marxian grammar of power-follows-ownership (ch.13) and of the planned society (ch.15). The parallel is therefore *authorial*, not merely a vault construction. The single interpretive layer is the synthesis that gathers these scattered, deliberate echoes into one "with Marx beyond Marx" relationship — labelled `Interpretive synthesis` where it appears — together with the two documented divergences (surplus from experience rather than labor; the displacement of class by the division of learning), each of which is itself anchored in directly-stated passages. The `high` label reflects that the relationship rests on Zuboff's chosen language rather than on inference, even though Marx remains a forward-stub author with no book in `raw/`.
