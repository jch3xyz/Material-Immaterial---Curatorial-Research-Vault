# Yuval Noah Harari ↔ Friedrich Nietzsche

## Relationship Type

Historical influence. Harari invokes Nietzsche's declaration that "God is dead" as the philosophical hinge of the Humanist Revolution: the moment authority and meaning were relocated from a divine cosmic order to the inner experience of human beings. Nietzsche stands behind two of Harari's three humanist branches — liberal humanism's centering of individual feeling, and [[Evolutionary Humanism]]'s drive to transcend the present human toward a superior type.

## Explanation

Harari's account of [[Humanism (Harari)]] is built on a transfer of sovereignty: where medieval culture placed meaning and authority in a superhuman source (God, natural law, scripture), the modern humanist revolution relocated both squarely inside human experience. Harari cites Nietzsche's declaration that God is dead as the canonical statement of this shift, but immediately reverses the expected consequence — social collapse did not follow. The reason, Harari argues, is that humanism supplied a replacement authority: feelings, desires, and inner experiences now legislate what is good, right, and beautiful. Nietzsche's shadow extends further into Harari's taxonomy of humanist sects: [[Evolutionary Humanism]] — whose most extreme advocates were the Nazis — draws explicitly on Nietzsche's valorisation of war, the will to power, and the imperative to transcend the merely human toward the superman. Harari also notes the Nietzschean lineage of [[Techno-Humanism]], which inherits the evolutionary humanist aspiration to upgrade humanity, now via biotechnology and artificial intelligence rather than natural selection. The overall relationship is one of historical influence and diagnostic reuse: Harari appropriates Nietzsche's diagnosis of modernity (the death of God, the will to power, the overcoming of the human) as evidence for his own argument about [[Humanism (Harari)]]'s internal tensions and its likely supersession by post-humanist technologies.

See also the parallel: [[Byung-Chul Han and Friedrich Nietzsche]], where Nietzsche's concept of the pathos of distance and the will to power is read against Han's critique of the transparency society and the achievement subject.

## Source Passages

### 1. Nietzsche cited as the philosopher of the death of God and the relocation of authority

> When Nietzsche declared that God is dead, this is what he meant. At least in the West, God has become an abstract idea that some accept and others reject, but it makes little difference either way. In the Middle Ages, without a god I had no source of political, moral and aesthetic authority. I could not tell what was right, good or beautiful. Who could live like that? Today, in contrast, it is very easy not to believe in God, because I pay no price for my unbelief. I can be a complete atheist, and still draw a very rich mix of political, moral and aesthetical values from my inner experience.

Confidence: `Directly stated` — Harari explicitly attributes to Nietzsche the diagnosis that the death of God marks the relocation of authority from the divine to human inner experience.

### 2. Evolutionary humanism and the Nietzschean valuation of war as the school of life

> Evolutionary humanism has a different solution to the problem of conflicting human experiences. Rooting itself in the firm ground of Darwinian evolutionary theory, it says that conflict is something to applaud rather than lament. Conflict is the raw material of natural selection, which pushes evolution forward. Some humans are simply superior to others, and when human experiences collide, the fittest humans should steamroll everyone else.

Confidence: `Directly stated` — Harari locates the Nietzschean inheritance of [[Evolutionary Humanism]] here.

### 3. Nietzsche quoted directly on war as the school of life

> Nietzsche summed it up by saying that war is 'the school of life' and that 'what does not kill me makes me stronger'.

Confidence: `Directly stated` — verbatim Nietzsche quotation cited by Harari as the philosophical banner of evolutionary humanism's embrace of war and natural selection.

### 4. The humanist revolution as the transition from divine to human sources of meaning

> As the source of meaning and authority was relocated from the sky to human feelings, the nature of the entire cosmos changed. The exterior universe – hitherto teeming with gods, muses, fairies and ghouls – became empty space. The interior world – hitherto an insignificant enclave of crude passions – became deep and rich beyond measure. Angels and demons were transformed from real entities roaming the forests and deserts of the world into inner forces within our own psyche. Heaven and hell too ceased to be real places somewhere above the clouds and below the volcanoes, and were instead interpreted as internal mental states.

Confidence: `Strongly implied` — this passage articulates the cosmological consequence of the death of God that Nietzsche diagnosed; Harari presents it as the structural transformation underwriting the humanist revolution.

### 5. God's death pronounced again, now in relation to radical Islam

> More than a century after Nietzsche pronounced Him dead, God seems to be making a comeback. But this is a mirage. God is dead – it just takes a while to get rid of the body.

Confidence: `Directly stated` — Harari repeats and endorses Nietzsche's formula as an accurate description of modernity's religious situation.

### 6. The humanism that supersedes God as the engine behind Homo Deus aspirations

> True, it has won the humanist wars of religion, and as of 2016 it has no viable alternative. But its very success may contain the seeds of its ruin. The triumphant liberal ideals are now pushing humankind to reach for immortality, bliss and divinity.

Confidence: `Strongly implied` — the trajectory from the death of God to the aspiration for human divinity is the Nietzschean arc that Harari's entire chapter on the Humanist Revolution traces; the coming of [[Homo Deus]] is the endpoint of the humanist revolution that Nietzsche's diagnosis set in motion.
