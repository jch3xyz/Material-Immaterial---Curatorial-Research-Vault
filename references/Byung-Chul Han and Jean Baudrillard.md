# Byung-Chul Han and Jean Baudrillard

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation with partial disagreement.** Han repeatedly and explicitly quotes [[Jean Baudrillard]] across *The Transparency Society* — leaning on Baudrillard's vocabulary of [[Obscenity (Baudrillard)|obscenity]], the [[More Visible than Visible|more-visible-than-visible]], [[Promiscuity of the Look]], and [[Seduction (Baudrillard)|seduction]] — while in [[Society of Control]] (Ch. 9) explicitly *correcting* Baudrillard's 1978 diagnosis of the end of the panopticon. The relationship is therefore best characterized as heavy lexical inheritance plus one named, dated disagreement.

## Explanation

Baudrillard supplies Han with the conceptual armature for the entire critique of [[Transparency]]. The key Baudrillardian terms — *obscenity* as the collapse of the scene into total visibility, the *more visible than visible*, *promiscuity*, *prostitution* of the look, and *seduction* as the play of the secret — are quoted verbatim or near-verbatim in chapters on [[Exhibition]], [[Pornography (Han)]], and [[Acceleration]]. Han's account of the [[Exhibition Value|Society of Exhibition]] and the [[Pornography (Han)|Society of Pornography]] is unthinkable without this borrowed apparatus.

The disagreement is sharply localized. In Ch. 9, Han cites Baudrillard's 1978 claim that we are witnessing the end of "the perspectival truth of the panoptic system" and the despotic gaze of TV — and then explicitly reverses it for the digital era: what is ending is only the *perspectival* panopticon; an [[Aperspectival Panopticon (Han)|aperspectival]], networked one is just beginning. This is not a repudiation but a temporal update: Baudrillard could not have known about digital networking, so his diagnosis must be inverted on its own terms.

A related, unstated tension runs underneath: Han's diagnosis depends on Baudrillard's seduction/secret as a normative remainder (the [[Erotic]], the veil, [[Beauty]] as that which conceals) — even as Han rejects Baudrillard's earlier announcement that perspectival visibility has ended. Han keeps the Baudrillardian critique of obscene transparency while denying Baudrillard's claim that surveillance has lost its center.

## Source Passages

### Lexical borrowing — "more visible than visible" / obscenity (Ch. 2)

In the chapter on [[Exhibition]], Han cites Baudrillard to characterize how things in the [[Exhibition Value|Society of Exhibition]] do not vanish into darkness but into overexposure:

> More generally things visible do not come to an end in obscurity and silence—instead they fade into the more visible than visible: obscenity.

This is then carried into Han's claim that the [[Exhibition Value|Society of Exhibition]] *is* a society of pornography. See [[More Visible than Visible]] and [[Obscenity (Baudrillard)]].

### Lexical borrowing — "promiscuity of the look" / prostitution (Ch. 2)

At the close of Ch. 2 Han names [[Transparency]] itself by quoting Baudrillard:

> Transparency ultimately proves to be "the total promiscuity of the look with what it sees," namely "prostitution."

Han then glosses tactile, distanceless perception as "epidermal contiguity of eye and image" — also Baudrillard's phrase. See [[Promiscuity of the Look]], [[Aesthetic Distance]].

### Lexical borrowing — seduction and the secret (Ch. 4)

In the chapter on [[Pornography (Han)]], Han uses Baudrillard explicitly to defend the [[Erotic]] against [[Giorgio Agamben]]'s reading of nudity:

> According to Baudrillard, the erotic power of seduction plays with the "intuition of something in the other that remains forever secret for him, something that I can never know directly about him but which nevertheless exercises a fascination upon me from behind its veil of secrecy."

This is decisive: Han mobilizes Baudrillard's [[Seduction (Baudrillard)|seduction]] as the conceptual antonym of pornographic transparency. See [[Secrecy|The Secret]], [[The Veil|Veil]].

### Lexical borrowing — obscenity as scenelessness (Ch. 5)

In the chapter on [[Acceleration]], Han again quotes Baudrillard to define obscenity in social-temporal terms:

> Society becomes obscene "when there is no longer a scene, when everything becomes inexorably transparent."

Han also borrows Baudrillard's gloss on movement collapsing into "the more mobile than movement" — a structural parallel to "more visible than visible." See [[Scene (Baudrillard)]], [[Hyperacceleration]].

### Explicit disagreement — the end of the panopticon (Ch. 9)

The disagreement is named, dated, and quoted. Han opens [[Society of Control]] with Baudrillard's 1978 claim:

> We are experiencing the end of "the perspectival truth of the panoptic system," Baudrillard remarked in 1978.

And quotes the supporting passage on TV: "The eye of TV is no longer the source of an absolute gaze, and the ideal of control is no longer that of transparency. This still presupposes an objective space (that of the Renaissance) and the omnipotence of the despotic gaze."

Han then explicitly reverses the diagnosis for the digital era:

> at the moment, we are not experiencing the end of the panopticon, but rather the beginning of an entirely new, aperspectival panopticon.

The reversal is generous, not hostile: "When he wrote these words, Baudrillard could not know about digital networking, of course." See [[Aperspectival Panopticon (Han)|Aperspectival Panopticon]], [[Digital Panopticon]], [[The Panopticon|Panopticon]].

## Confidence

**High.** The relationship is *directly stated* on every count: Han names Baudrillard, dates him (1978), and quotes him verbatim five times across four chapters. The lexical inheritance (obscenity, more-visible-than-visible, promiscuity, prostitution, seduction, scene) is textually undeniable, and the Ch. 9 disagreement is explicit and bounded. The only interpretive layer is the framing of the relationship as inheritance-plus-update rather than rupture — but Han's own "of course he could not know" sentence licenses that reading.
