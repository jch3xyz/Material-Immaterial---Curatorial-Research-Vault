# Meghan O'Gieblyn ↔ René Descartes

## Relationship Type

Historical influence, shading into disagreement. [[René Descartes]] is the recurring origin-figure of *God, Human, Animal, Machine* — the philosopher whose seventeenth-century partition O'Gieblyn names as the unacknowledged source of the book's central problems. She does not cite Descartes as an ally but treats his metaphysics as a fault line modernity has never closed: an inheritance that the [[Mind as Machine|computer metaphor]] reproduces precisely while claiming to have escaped it.

## Explanation

O'Gieblyn returns to Descartes at four distinct depths. (1) The **res extensa / res cogitans split** — animals as machines, the body as inert matter, the soul as the one immaterial exception — is for her the move that "introduced into philosophy" the very distinctions later thinkers cannot dislodge. (2) The **mind-body problem** that haunts neuroscience and AI is "typically blamed on Descartes," and [[David Chalmers]]'s [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]] is "merely the most recent iteration" of it. (3) The **cogito** — *I think, therefore I am* — is the foundation [[Bernardo Kastrup]] returns to (Ch. 10) on the road to [[Idealism]]. (4) The **disenchantment** narrative casts Descartes as "the serpent in the garden," even as O'Gieblyn concedes that "modern science would not have been possible" without his division, which underwrites Nagel's "view from nowhere." Her critical thesis: the [[Mind as Machine|computer metaphor]], invented to bypass the soul, "has not yet managed to completely eradicate the distinctions Descartes introduced into philosophy." The metaphor does not solve the Cartesian problem; it inherits it.

## Source Passages

The book's hinge claim is that the soul "keeps slipping back in": the [[Mind as Machine|mind-as-software metaphor]] is "itself a form of dualism," "appealing because it reiterates the Cartesian assumption that the mind is something above and beyond the physical." Daniel Dennett's derisive "Cartesian theater" (Ch. 2) and the [[Eliminativism|eliminativist]] "Great Denial" are themselves, on O'Gieblyn's reading, captive to "these seventeenth-century assumptions": "To say that consciousness is an illusion is to place it outside the material world, deeming it something—much like Descartes's soul—that does not exist within time or space." See **Sources** below for the verbatim passages.
