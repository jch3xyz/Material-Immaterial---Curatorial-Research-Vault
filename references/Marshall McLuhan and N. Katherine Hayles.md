# Marshall McLuhan ↔ N. Katherine Hayles

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel — no citation in either direction — best described as *precursor and critique*, and explicitly an interpretive synthesis the vault constructs rather than a citation either author made.** [[Marshall McLuhan|McLuhan]] (*[[Understanding Media]]*, 1964) does not cite [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]], who was a child when he wrote; Hayles (*[[How We Became Posthuman]]*, 1999) is not establishing a relationship to McLuhan in the chapters the vault has ingested. There is no `direct_citation` and no documented `historical_influence` to point to here. What binds them is that they perform the *same operation on the same object* across thirty-five years: both treat the **body and the senses as the material substrate that media/technology reorganize**, and both watch the [[Embodiment|embodied]] human be progressively translated into an information system. McLuhan supplies the **mid-century announcement and the celebration** of this translation — the "extensions of man," the central nervous system thrown into "a global embrace" — where Hayles supplies the **late-century genealogy and the warning** that the same translation, taken to its limit, becomes the fantasy of [[Virtuality|disembodiment]] she spends a book contesting. The relationship is `conceptual_parallel` with a sharp asymmetry of *stance*. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Explanation

The two converge on a premise and split on a verdict. **Premise (shared):** the human sensorium is not a fixed given but the malleable ground that technology continually re-proportions; every medium is "an extension of ourselves" that alters our "sense ratios," and in the electric age the thing extended is no longer a limb but the nervous system and consciousness itself. **Verdict (divergent):** McLuhan reads the extension of the central nervous system "in a global embrace" as a movement toward "the technological extension of consciousness" — planetary, integrating, the human "translated more and more into the form of information" — and meets it with an ambivalent but largely celebratory wonder. Hayles reads the *same* translation, "information lost its body," as the contingent and resistible production of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] as **disembodiment** — and writes [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment|against it]]. McLuhan is thus a precursor to Hayles in two precise senses, and her antagonist in a third.

1. **The body and the senses as substrate, not essence.** McLuhan's foundational move is to treat the senses themselves as a kind of technology — a register of "fixed charges" that media draw upon and reorganize. His exact formulation: "That our human senses, of which all media are extensions, are also fixed charges on our personal energies, and that they also configure the awareness and experience of each one of us." The sensorium is a budget that each new medium taxes; perception is re-proportioned, never neutral. This is structurally the position Hayles arrives at from the other direction — that there is no disembodied, substrate-free human, only specific [[Embodiment|embodiments]] that information technologies rework. Where McLuhan says any extension "affects the whole psychic and social complex," Hayles says embodiment is always *materially instantiated* and so cannot be subtracted without remainder. They share the substrate; McLuhan's "fixed charges" are an early, physiological version of Hayles's insistence on the materiality of the medium. (`Directly stated` on McLuhan; `Interpretive synthesis` on the bridge to Hayles.)

2. **The translation of the human into information — McLuhan as precursor.** McLuhan's most Hayles-anticipating sentences describe the electric age as a recoding of the person into data: "In this electric age we see ourselves being translated more and more into the form of information, moving toward the technological extension of consciousness," and, more graphically, "By putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems, by means of electric media, we set up a dynamic by which all previous technologies that are mere extensions of hands and feet and teeth and bodily heat-controls—all such extensions of our bodies, including cities—will be translated into information systems." This *is* the genealogy Hayles tells — the [[The Body as Data|body recoded as data]], the human rearticulated as informational pattern — narrated by McLuhan a generation early and as a fait accompli he greets with curiosity rather than alarm. McLuhan even reaches McLuhan's own version of the cybernetic premise the vault tracks under [[The Body as Data]]: he reports that "it is also common now for biologists like Hans Selye to think of the physical organism as a communication network." The organism-as-network and the body-translated-into-information are, in 1964, already in place; what is missing is Hayles's critical question of *what is lost* in the translation. (`Directly stated`.)

3. **Planetary extension vs disembodiment — the point of divergence.** The Introduction's keynote is the line where McLuhan most clearly becomes both Hayles's precursor and her antagonist: "During the mechanical ages we had extended our bodies in space. Today, after more than a century of electric technology, we have extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace, abolishing both space and time as far as our planet is concerned." For McLuhan this abolition of space and time is an enlargement — consciousness extended to "the whole of human society," the body still present and re-proportioned ("In the electric age we wear all mankind as our skin"). For Hayles, the very same gesture — pattern lifted off matter, the self distributed beyond the skin, space and time "abolished" — is the threshold at which the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]] is recoded as free-floating information and embodiment quietly drops out. This is the [[Embodiment vs Information]] / [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis, and it is exactly where the two are closest in *content* and furthest apart in *valuation*: McLuhan celebrates the planetary extension that Hayles warns becomes disembodiment. McLuhan does register a cost — the "numbness," the [[Posthumanism|servomechanism]] relation, the Narcissus-narcosis by which "we have to numb our central nervous system when it is extended and exposed, or we will die" — but he frames it as the price of an enlargement, not, as Hayles does, as the symptom of an erasure to be resisted. (`Directly stated` on each text; `Interpretive synthesis` on the opposition.)

The net relationship: **McLuhan announces and celebrates the translation of the embodied human into a planetary information system; Hayles genealogizes and contests the same translation as disembodiment.** Both hold the senses/body to be the reworkable substrate of technological change ([[The Body as Data]], [[Embodiment]]); both watch the electric/cybernetic age recode the human as information. Where McLuhan thrills to "the technological extension of consciousness," Hayles insists ([[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]], [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]]) that information never loses its body without violence and that the outcome was never inevitable. Read together they bracket the postwar recoding of the human: McLuhan as its mid-century herald, Hayles as its end-of-century critic. (See the author notes [[Marshall McLuhan]] / [[N. Katherine Hayles]] for the fuller positions.)

## Understanding Media chs. 8-13

These two chapters of *Understanding Media* (Ch. 10, "Roads and Paper Routes"; Ch. 11, "Number: Profile of the Crowd") sharpen the same materiality/dematerialization axis the rest of this note traces, and they do so on McLuhan's own terms — one chapter narrating the **detachment of information from matter**, the other insisting that even the most abstract sign remains **grounded in touch and the body**. Read against [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]], the pair stages the two halves of her argument in a single author thirty-five years early: the disembodiment-of-information narrative she contests, and the recovery of embodiment she affirms.

**Dematerialization (Ch. 10) — precursor to Hayles's disembodiment narrative.** McLuhan dates the moment information comes loose from its physical carrier: "It is only since the telegraph that information has detached itself from such solid commodities as stone and papyrus, much as money had earlier detached itself from hides, bullion, and metals, and has ended as paper." The analogy is precise and load-bearing — information sheds matter exactly as money sheds bullion, each "detaching itself" from the substance that once carried it. This is, in 1964, the very event Hayles narrates as the historical condition of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]: the moment [[Information Lost Its Body|information could be imagined as lost its body]], pattern lifted off the "solid commodities" of stone and papyrus. McLuhan reports the detachment matter-of-factly, as the defining "character of the electric age" ("the transition of the idea from transport to information by means of electricity"); Hayles ([[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]]) supplies the genealogy and the warning that the detachment was never as clean or as inevitable as it looks. Same event — information detaching from solid commodities — opposite stance, the [[Material vs Immaterial]] / [[Embodiment vs Information]] axis again. (`Directly stated` on McLuhan; `Interpretive synthesis` on the bridge to Hayles.)

**Embodiment (Ch. 11) — convergence with Hayles's recovery of the body.** Where Ch. 10 watches information come loose from matter, Ch. 11 pulls the most disembodied-seeming abstraction — number — back into the body. McLuhan's thesis: "Just as writing is an extension and separation of our most neutral and objective sense, the sense of sight, number is an extension and separation of our most intimate and interrelating activity, our sense of touch." Number, "seen as an extension of our physical bodies, ... becomes quite intelligible"; it "originates in the sense of touch, of which it is an extension." Even the founding abstraction of "the language of science" is, for McLuhan, [[The Body as Data|grounded in the tactile body]] — its "iconic power," its "tactile dimension," traced to the haptic hand. This converges directly with Hayles's project: against the fantasy that information (or number, or pattern) floats free of matter, McLuhan insists the abstraction is always an *extension of a sensing, touching body* and cannot be subtracted from it without the "disintegration" he names when "our mechanical technologies for extending and separating the functions of our physical beings have brought us near to a state of disintegration by putting us out of touch with ourselves." That is McLuhan's version of [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment|Hayles's claim that there is no disembodiment, only different embodiment]] — the recovery of [[Embodiment|embodiment]] beneath the abstraction. (`Directly stated` on McLuhan; `Interpretive synthesis` on the convergence with Hayles.)

Taken together the two chapters double the note's thesis. Ch. 10 gives the **dematerialization** McLuhan shares as precursor with the disembodiment narrative Hayles contests; Ch. 11 gives the **re-grounding in the body** that converges with Hayles's recovery of embodiment. McLuhan himself holds both at once — information detaches from its solid commodities, yet number never detaches from the touching hand — which is exactly the unresolved tension Hayles makes the explicit subject of her book. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Confidence

**Medium.** The McLuhan passages are verbatim and load-bearing, and each maps onto a securely documented Hayles position, so the *content* of the parallel — both treat the body/senses as the substrate technology reorganizes, and both watch the human be translated into information — is well supported (`Directly stated` on the McLuhan side taken alone). Confidence is held to medium, not high, for two reasons. First, there is **no citation in either direction**: the linkage is a juxtaposition the vault constructs across a thirty-five-year gap, so the relationship itself is `Interpretive synthesis`, not a claim either author asserts. Second, the **valuations diverge sharply** — McLuhan celebrates the planetary extension of the nervous system; Hayles writes against the disembodiment it becomes — so the parallel is genuine at the level of the shared object (the body as technological substrate, the human recoded as information) but must not be read as agreement. The "precursor vs critique" framing is the vault's interpretive scaffolding.
