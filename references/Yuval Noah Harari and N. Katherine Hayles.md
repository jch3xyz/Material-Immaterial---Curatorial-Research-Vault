# Yuval Noah Harari ↔ N. Katherine Hayles

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel — no citation in either direction — best described as *diagnosis and completion*.** [[Yuval Noah Harari|Harari]] does not cite [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] in the chapters ingested here, and Hayles (writing in 1999) predates *[[Homo Deus]]* by seventeen years and so cannot cite him. There is no `direct_citation` and no documented `historical_influence`. What binds them is that they perform the *same operation on the same object* from opposite ends of the same period: the dismantling of the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]] — the indivisible, free, self-present individual — and its replacement by a view of the human (and the cosmos) as a substrate-independent, information-processing system. Hayles supplies the **genealogy and the critique** of how this construction was assembled ([[Cybernetics]], the Macy decoupling of [[Information|information]] from matter, the [[Flickering Signifier|flickering signifier]]); Harari narrates its **technological completion** as accomplished fact — the "[[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness|decoupling of intelligence from consciousness]]," the dissolution of the individual into "'dividuals'," and the [[The Computational Universe|computational]] reading of the species as a "single data-processing system." The relationship is therefore `conceptual_parallel` with a sharp asymmetry of *stance*: Hayles writes *against* the disembodiment she traces; Harari mostly declines to mourn it. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Explanation

The two authors converge on a single thesis and split on what to do about it. Both hold that the bounded, sovereign, indivisible self of liberal humanism is a historically specific construction now coming apart, and that what replaces it is the human recoded as information-processing. They diverge on register and verdict: Hayles is a cultural-materialist critic recovering [[Embodiment|embodiment]] against the fantasy of disembodied information; Harari is a popular historian reporting the same recoding as the near-inevitable trajectory of twenty-first-century science and technology. The parallel runs across three registers.

1. **The dismantling of the indivisible subject — "dividuals" vs the recoded posthuman.** The liberal subject is, etymologically and ideologically, the *in-dividual*: that which cannot be divided, the "single and indivisible self." Harari attacks this directly: "Liberals believe that we have a single and indivisible self. To be an individual means that I am in-dividual," and then dissolves it — "Humans aren't individuals. They are 'dividuals'." The unitary self splits, in his account, into the [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self|experiencing self and the narrating self]], a "cacophony of conflicting voices, none of which is 'my true self'." Hayles reaches the same place by a different road. Her whole book tracks how "a historically specific construction called the human is giving way to a different construction called the posthuman," in which the subject is rearticulated as an informational pattern — the [[Flickering Signifier|flickering signifier]] succeeding the stable sign, the self distributed across [[Distributed Cognition|cognitive systems]] that exceed the skin. Where Hayles names the *construction* of this division (and warns against it), Harari announces its *completion* as the verdict of the life sciences. (`Directly stated` on each side individually; `Interpretive synthesis` on the convergence.)

2. **Consciousness as epiphenomenon, intelligence as substrate-independent.** Both authors detach the prized capacities of the human from the body that was supposed to ground them. Harari's hinge claim is that "intelligence is decoupling from consciousness" — that non-conscious algorithms now out-perform conscious humans at problem-solving, so that "for armies and corporations… intelligence is mandatory but consciousness is optional" (see [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]] and the [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] tension). This rests on [[Organisms Are Algorithms|the premise that organisms are algorithms]] and that "algorithmic calculations are not affected by the materials from which you build the calculator." That last sentence is, almost word for word, the fantasy Hayles spent a book contesting: the belief that "information can circulate unchanged among different material substrates," that pattern can be lifted off matter without loss. The convergence is exact at the level of the *claim* — mind reduces to substrate-independent information-processing — and total at the level of *valuation*: Hayles insists "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium" and that "embodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated," while Harari treats the body as, potentially, "an obsolete algorithm." This is the [[Embodiment vs Information]] / [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis, and it is where the two are closest in content and furthest apart in commitment. (`Directly stated`.)

3. **The species (and the universe) as a data-processing system — the computational completion.** Hayles ends *How We Became Posthuman* by naming "[[The Computational Universe|the computational universe]]" as the worldview her material is "edging" toward: the doctrine that "the essential function of the universe as a whole is processing information," that "we are the programs it runs" — which she treats as a *contingent, socially constructed ideology* "dangerous when it goes from being a useful heuristic to an ideology that privileges information over everything else." Harari's [[Dataism]] is that ideology arrived and embraced: "From a Dataist perspective, we may interpret the entire human species as a single data-processing system, with individual humans serving as its chips." The individual becomes "a tiny chip inside a giant system that nobody really understands"; humanity becomes "just a ripple within the cosmic data flow." What Hayles flags as the totalizing metaphysics to be resisted, Harari narrates as the religion of the coming century. They share the object — the cosmos as information processor, the human as its component — and split on function: ideology to be demystified (Hayles) versus historical destiny to be diagnosed with cool ambivalence (Harari). (`Directly stated` on each text; `Interpretive synthesis` on the bridge.)

The net relationship: **Hayles diagnoses the construction; Harari narrates its technological completion.** She writes in the contestatory present tense — "the best possible time to contest for what the posthuman means is now" — precisely to forestall the future Harari then reports as largely settled. Read together, they bracket the same transformation: Hayles asks how the human came to be recodable as information and refuses the recoding's erasure of the body; Harari asks what happens once the recoding is operationalized by Google, Watson, and the [[The Internet-of-All-Things|Internet-of-All-Things]], and largely accepts the answer. Their deepest shared commitment — and the one place a reader should not let the parallel collapse them — is that *both* hold the liberal subject's dissolution to be real; where Hayles fights to keep [[Embodiment]] from being subtracted in the process, Harari is willing to let it go. (See [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]], [[Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman]], and the author notes [[Yuval Noah Harari]] / [[N. Katherine Hayles]] for the fuller positions.)
