# Roy Ascott ↔ Hans Moravec

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation — endorsement.** This is a citation Ascott actually makes, not an interpretive synthesis. In *[[Telematic Embrace]]* [[Roy Ascott|Ascott]] twice invokes the computer scientist [[Hans Moravec]] by name and embraces him as a positive resource: Moravec's vision of downloading personality from wetware to hardware is offered as a liberation, and his account of advanced robotics is cited as evidence of the dissolving boundary between the natural and the artificial. [[Hans Moravec]] is a **forward-stub author** in the vault (no work in `raw/`); he appears here only as Ascott reads him.

This relationship is the inverse mirror of the vault's other Moravec references. Where [[N. Katherine Hayles and Hans Moravec|Hayles makes Moravec her antagonist]] — the "roboticist's dream that struck me as a nightmare," the man who "decontextualizes the theory" of [[Information]] and grafts the posthuman onto [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] — Ascott reaches for the *same* download fantasy as **promise**, not threat. The disembodiment Hayles diagnoses as a lethal erasure of [[Embodiment]], Ascott welcomes as release from "history and the chains of trauma."

## Explanation

Two distinct citations, one in each chapter.

1. **Telenoia (ch. 23): Moravec against Freud.** Arguing that the telematic condition has shattered the unitary self into "the multiple, distributed presences of a set of many selves," Ascott declares that "psychology is unprepared to deal with the exigencies of telepresence in a telematic society." Against this unpreparedness he sets Moravec explicitly *over* Freud: "the computer scientist Hans Moravec has more to offer us than Freud; his vision of a consummate downloading of personality from wetware to hardware offers us more promise of liberating ourselves from history and the chains of trauma than Freud's closet consciousness could ever hope to do." The download here is therapeutic and emancipatory — a technological exit from the psychoanalytic interior ("closet consciousness"), a way to slip the determinations of personal history. This is precisely the [[Mind Uploading]] scenario (the migration of the self from "wetware to hardware"), valorized rather than contested. (`Directly stated`)

2. **Back to Nature II (ch. 28): Moravec and the post-human descendants.** Ascott cites Moravec a second time within his "systems metaphor," in which "the line between natural and artificial systems" becomes "meaningless" and "we are all becoming, to a greater or lesser extent, bionic." He pairs "Drexler's account of the consequences of nanotechnology and Hans Moravec's vision of advanced robotics" as twin attestations of "this rapidly and radically evolving interdependency and connectedness of the natural and artificial." The citation culminates in a verbatim quotation *of* Moravec — the prospect of consciousness evolving "into something transcending everything we know—in whom we can take pride when they refer to themselves as our descendants" [Moravec 1988] — which Ascott then assigns to art: "Art may come to constitute a form of mediation between human and post-human consciousness." (`Directly stated`)

Read together, the two citations show Ascott using Moravec to underwrite a single trajectory: the immaterialization of the self (uploading from wetware) and the [[Posthumanism|post-human]] succession (silicon-based descendants), with art as the mediator across the threshold. This places the reference squarely on the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] spine and aligns Ascott's optimism with the [[Transhumanism|transhumanist]] / [[Patternism|patternist]] lineage that O'Gieblyn and Kurzweil document — but as an *artist's* embrace rather than an engineer's program.

## Source Passages

The two load-bearing moments: (1) ch. 23's elevation of Moravec over Freud — the download "from wetware to hardware" as liberation "from history and the chains of trauma"; (2) ch. 28's pairing of Moravec (with Drexler) as evidence of a dissolving natural/artificial boundary, climaxing in the quoted Moravec passage on consciousness "transcending everything we know" in our silicon "descendants," with art positioned as mediator between human and post-human.
