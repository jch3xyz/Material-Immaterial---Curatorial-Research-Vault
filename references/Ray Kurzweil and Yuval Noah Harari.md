# Ray Kurzweil ↔ Yuval Noah Harari

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel (no direct citation; the bridge is the vault's).** [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s *The Singularity Is Near* (2005) and [[Yuval Noah Harari]]'s *[[Homo Deus]]* (2017) do not cite each other, and neither claims the other as a source. What they share is a *stance*: both are the vault's two **affirmative cosmologists of information** — thinkers who scale data-processing up from the organism to the entire universe, and who end the story in a god-like informational telos that humans merge into. This note exists to isolate that shared affirmative cosmology as a single object, because it is precisely the position that the vault's two critics — [[N. Katherine Hayles]] and [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] — write *against*. Kurzweil is a **forward-stub author** (his book *The Singularity Is Near* is in `raw/` and being ingested; he appears elsewhere in the vault only as O'Gieblyn reads and corresponds with him — see [[Meghan O'Gieblyn and Ray Kurzweil]]). Harari is a realized author. The relationship is `conceptual_parallel` rather than `direct_citation` or `shared_tradition`: there is no textual line of influence, only an independent convergence on the same cosmology, arrived at from a futurist-engineer's side (Kurzweil) and a historian's side (Harari).

## Explanation

The parallel is unusually exact, and runs along four registers. The point is not that the two men agree on details — their tempers differ sharply (Kurzweil is a celebrant; Harari is a diagnostician who calls his own object a "religion" with some irony) — but that they project the *same* arc: data-processing climbs from matter to organism to civilization to cosmos, and culminates in an intelligence that "would be like God" and that humans dissolve into.

1. **Information is the more fundamental reality.** Kurzweil's metaphysics is explicitly an information-first metaphysics: glossing Edward Fredkin, he writes that "information rather than matter and energy may be regarded as the more fundamental reality" (Ch2). Matter and energy are, for him, substrate; the real story is "the evolution of patterns of information" (Ch1). Harari's [[Dataism]] makes the identical inversion as a worldview — "if life is the movement of information" then the supreme value is "information flow," and "we should extend, deepen and spread the flow of information in the universe" (Ch14). Both treat the universe as, at bottom, a [[The Computational Universe|computational universe]] in which the carrier (mass, energy, biochemistry) is secondary to the pattern it bears. This is the metaphysical hinge that [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] names and resists — the dream of "[[Information]] ... liberated from the material constraints of the physical world," against [[Embodiment]].

2. **Data-processing scales from organism to cosmos.** Both authors run the *same ladder*, treating the organism, the society, and the universe as successive data-processing systems of increasing scale. Harari is explicit: "we may interpret the entire human species as a single data-processing system, with individual humans serving as its chips," and the whole of history as "improving the efficiency of this system" (Ch14) — the move captured in [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] and [[Society as Algorithm]]. Kurzweil's [[The Six Epochs|Six Epochs]] are the same ladder drawn out across cosmic time: physics/chemistry → biology/DNA → brains → technology → the human–machine merger → "The Universe Wakes Up," each epoch "using the information-processing methods of the previous epoch to create the next" (Ch1). For both, there is no qualitative break between a cell, a person, a civilization, and a galaxy — only data-processing systems nested at larger scales.

3. **The endpoint is a god-like informational telos humans merge into.** This is the sharpest convergence. Kurzweil's sixth epoch: "the 'dumb' matter and mechanisms of the universe will be transformed into exquisitely sublime forms of intelligence, which will constitute the sixth epoch in the evolution of patterns of information. This is the ultimate destiny of the Singularity and of the universe" (Ch1) — intelligence "saturating the matter and energy in its midst" and spreading outward until "the entire universe will become saturated with our intelligence." Harari's Dataism reaches the same terminus: the [[The Internet-of-All-Things]] "may eventually spread out from planet Earth to cover the whole galaxy and even the whole universe. This cosmic data-processing system would be like God. It will be everywhere and will control everything, and humans are destined to merge into it" (Ch14). Both cosmologies are eschatological in structure (the vault marks this as [[Techno-Religion]]): a single, all-encompassing informational intelligence as the destiny of the cosmos, and the human as the thing that *merges into* it rather than survives alongside it. Kurzweil supplies even the physical mechanism — "with full-scale nanotechnology, computing resources can be made self-replicating and thus can rapidly convert mass and energy into an intelligent form" (Ch3) — which is the engineering literalization of Harari's "convert everything to the system."

4. **The human is a pattern / an algorithm.** Underwriting all of the above is a shared ontology of the human. For Harari, organisms (humans included) are algorithms, and the self is among the data the system processes — "individual humans serving as its chips," eventually "reduced from engineers to chips, then to data" (Ch14). Kurzweil's parallel is [[Patternism]]: consciousness is a pattern of information that "persists over time," substrate-independent and in principle transferable to a non-biological medium — the position O'Gieblyn dissects in [[Meghan O'Gieblyn and Ray Kurzweil]] ("he calls himself a 'patternist'"). Treating the human as pattern/algorithm is what *licenses* the merger in register 3: if the person is just a portable pattern, then dissolving into a larger computational intelligence is continuity, not death. This is the precise claim Hayles historicizes (the contingent "disembodiment of information") and O'Gieblyn theologizes (the pattern as the soul recoded) — the affirmative move both critics exist to interrupt.

**What distinguishes this note from each author's own page.** Harari's own notes ([[Dataism]], [[The Internet-of-All-Things]], [[Organisms Are Algorithms]]) and Kurzweil's ([[The Six Epochs]], [[The Singularity]], [[The Law of Accelerating Returns]], [[Patternism]]) each develop one side in full. This reference does something neither page does: it holds the two *together* as one shared **affirmative-cosmologist stance**, so that the critical cluster ([[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]], [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]], the tensions [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Embodiment vs Information]]) has a single, named target to push against. The asymmetry worth flagging: Kurzweil *celebrates* the telos as humanity's "ultimate destiny," while Harari narrates it with a diagnostician's distance ("Dataism too began as a neutral scientific theory, but is now mutating into a religion") — but the cosmology they describe is the same one, and that sameness is the point.
