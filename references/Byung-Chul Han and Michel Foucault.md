# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Michel Foucault

## General Orientation

This note collects Han's engagement with Foucault across more than one book, and the two books pull the relationship in different directions.

In *The Transparency Society*, Foucault is overwhelmingly a **scaffold-and-foil for the panopticon**: Han periodizes us *out of* Foucault's [[Disciplinary Society]] into a [[Digital Panopticon]] / [[Society of Control]] / [[Achievement Society]], while enlisting the Foucault of "strategic games" against symmetrical [[Transparency]] (see [[#Explanation|the panopticon material below]]).

In *Psychopolitics*, the engagement is **far richer and double-edged**, and the axis shifts from discipline/visibility to the *psyche*. Here Foucault is at once (1) a **periodized past** — his [[Biopolitics]] and the docile body are declared "unsuited to the neoliberal regime," which exploits the soul rather than the body — *and* (2) the **source of Han's positive program** — the [[#Psychopolitics (Han) — Foucault's Dilemma and the art of living|care of the self, the art of living, and the event]] are taken over from late Foucault as the practice of freedom that disarms [[Psychopolitics]]. The productive tension is that Han claims to *supersede* Foucauldian [[Biopolitics]] yet routes his whole emancipatory exit through Foucault.

In *The Burnout Society* (2010), the engagement is the **purest periodization** of the three, and this book is the **substantive [[Disciplinary Society|disciplinary-society]] anchor** for Han's supersession thesis. Here Foucault is named once and almost entirely as the *prior* epoch: the explicit claim is that "Foucault's analysis of power cannot account for the psychic and topological changes that occurred as disciplinary society transformed into achievement society" (ch. 3). The argument is carried by the modal shift from the disciplinary, prohibitive *Should* to the affirmative, limitless *Can* ([[Negativity]] → [[Positivity]]) and is realized as the argument [[The achievement society supersedes the disciplinary society]]. Foucault here is a **forward-stub author** — cited, not yet given a book — and the Foucauldian [[Disciplinary Society]] is worked out in two extended examples that this note records below: Melville's "Bartleby" as a *still-disciplinary* architecture of walls and the Tombs (ch. 7), and the Freud/Kant disciplinary apparatus of [[Negativity]] in the title chapter (ch. 9).

The sections below are organized by book: the panopticon/periodization material is the *Transparency Society* layer; "Foucault's Dilemma" and the "art of living" are the *Psychopolitics* layer; the discipline-superseded-by-achievement material is the *Burnout Society* layer.

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation** combined with **conceptual revision**. Foucault is *The Transparency Society*'s most pervasive interlocutor — sometimes named, often unnamed. Han quotes him verbatim once (Ch. 3, on power as strategic play) and otherwise inherits, extends, and revises the Foucauldian apparatus of the [[The Panopticon|Panopticon]], [[Disciplinary Society]], and biopolitics. The dominant move is a periodization: Han argues we have passed *out of* Foucault's [[Disciplinary Society]] into a [[Digital Panopticon]] / [[Society of Control]] / [[Achievement Society]] whose subjects illuminate themselves voluntarily rather than being illuminated from a central tower.

## Explanation

Han uses Foucault as both scaffold and foil. The scaffold: the panoptic schema, the link between visibility and power, and a non-moralized account of productive power are taken over almost without argument. The foil: Foucault's [[The Panopticon|Panopticon]] is "a phenomenon of disciplinary society" (Ch. 9, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/12 Ch9 - Control|Control]]) — perspectival, centred, asymmetrical, isolating. Han claims the twenty-first century inhabits a successor structure — an "aperspectival" panopticon in which subjects "actively collaborate in its construction and maintenance by putting themselves on display and baring themselves." The Foucauldian inmate who could not speak gives way to the Hanian inmate who will not stop communicating. This is the core revision: surveillance has been internalised as [[Auto-Exploitation|Self-Exploitation]], visibility as [[Freedom vs Control|Freedom as Control]], and power as the [[Achievement Society|Achievement-Subject]]'s own desire.

A second, quieter inheritance runs through Ch. 3 ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/06 Ch3 - Evidence|Evidence]]) and the chapters on revelation and intimacy: the [[Confessional Society|confessional]] / truth-as-avowal motif. Han's recurring claim that subjects "bare themselves of their own free will" and that "the society of control achieves perfection when its inhabitants ... communicate ... out of inner need" rhymes with Foucault's history of confession as a productive technology of the self, though Han does not cite *The History of Sexuality* by name in these chapters. This is best read as a **strongly implied** Foucauldian undertone rather than a direct citation.

The one explicit quotation — Foucault on power as "strategic games" that are "not an evil" — is doing structural work for Han's broader argument against [[Transparency]]. Han enlists Foucault to defend asymmetry, opacity, and "intransparency and incalculability" as conditions of pleasure, politics, and freedom. The Foucault of strategic games is mobilised *against* the moralised, symmetrical, contractual transparency Han diagnoses as a [[Neoliberal Dispositive]].

## Psychopolitics (Han) — Foucault's Dilemma and the art of living

*Psychopolitics* (2014) makes Foucault its central case of a thinker who diagnosed the right object but stopped one step short. Han's verdict is that Foucault "stuck to the concepts of population and biopolitics" even after turning to neoliberalism in the 1978–9 lectures, so that "he failed to do what the circumstances actually called for: to make the turn to psychopolitics" (Ch. 5, [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/05 Foucault's Dilemma|Foucault's Dilemma]]). The argument hinges on a material periodization carried by the [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] tension: [[Biopolitics]] "fundamentally concerns the biological and the physical … a politics of the body in the fullest sense" (Ch. 4, [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/04 Biopolitics|Biopolitics]]), but neoliberalism "has discovered the psyche as a productive force," so that "Foucault's 'docile body' has no place in this production process." Where Foucault's panoptic [[Disciplinary Society]] disciplined bodies through external [[Negativity]], [[Psychopolitics]] optimizes minds from within. Crucially, Han turns Foucault's own late work against him: the neoliberal regime "utterly claims the technology of the self for its own purposes," so that "perpetual [[Self-Optimization|self-optimization]] — as the exemplary neoliberal technology of the self — represents nothing so much as a highly efficient mode of domination and exploitation." The Foucauldian "entrepreneur of himself" and the "self-as-a-work-of-art" are not, for Han, escapes from power but its most efficient capture: "Self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, fall into one." This is the productive tension in its sharpest form — Han supersedes Foucauldian [[Biopolitics]] precisely by showing that Foucault's emancipatory category (the care of the self) has been weaponized into [[Auto-Exploitation]] by the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]]. `Directly stated` (the "dilemma," biopolitics-as-bodily, the technology-of-the-self capture are all explicit).

Yet Han does not abandon Foucault; he routes the exit *through* him. In the *Big Data* chapter (Ch. 11, [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/11 Big Data|Big Data]]) he recovers the ancient **care of the self** against [[Dataism|dataist]] self-tracking, opposing Tertullian's *publicatio sui* — "committing to the search for truth," where "writing was also important in the culture of taking care of oneself" — to the "Quantified Self," which "empties the self of any and all meaning." The contrast is the [[Counting vs Recounting]] tension: "Counting is not recounting … A sense of self derives from giving an account." The same digital regime that Foucault could not foresee turns the subject into "a panopticon of itself," the "self-illuminating, self-surveilling subject" who "carries around its own labour camp." `Directly stated`. Then, in the closing chapter (Ch. 12, [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/12 Beyond the Subject|Beyond the Subject]]), Foucault becomes the *name of freedom itself*: "Following Foucault, the art of living may be understood as a practice of freedom, bringing forth an entirely different mode of existence." This art of living "unfolds as **de-psychologization**" — Han quotes Foucault: "The art of living is the art of killing psychology, of creating with oneself and with others unnamed individualities, beings, relations, qualities." Against the [[Psychopolitics|psychopolitical]] subject that emotion and experiencing ([[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)|Erlebnis]]) only "ensnare … deeper and deeper in the state of subjection," Han mobilizes the Foucauldian–Nietzschean **event** — [[The Event|the event]] as "the reversal of a relationship of forces" that "breaks the subject open and wrests it from subjection" ([[Subject vs Project]]). So the book's emancipatory program is wholly Foucauldian in vocabulary even as its central polemic declares Foucault's master-concept obsolete. `Directly stated` for the quotations; `Interpretive synthesis` for the framing that Han both periodizes-past and depends-upon Foucault.

## The Burnout Society (Han) — discipline superseded by achievement

*The Burnout Society* (2010) is the vault's substantive anchor for Han's supersession of Foucault's [[Disciplinary Society]]. The thesis is stated flatly and early: "Today's society is no longer Foucault's disciplinary world of hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories," and "Foucault's analysis of power cannot account for the psychic and topological changes that occurred as disciplinary society transformed into achievement society" (ch. 3, [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/03 Beyond Disciplinary Society|Beyond Disciplinary Society]]). The periodization is carried not by a new object (the panopticon, the psyche) but by a change of *modality*: "Disciplinary society is a society of [[Negativity|negativity]]. It is defined by the negativity of prohibition. The negative modal verb that governs it is May Not," whereas "Unlimited Can is the positive modal verb of achievement society." Foucault's inmate — the "obedience-subject" — gives way to the "achievement-subject" who is an "entrepreneur of [itself]"; "the walls of disciplinary institutions, which separate the normal from the abnormal, have come to seem archaic." Crucially, Han marks a *continuity within* the break: "the Can does not revoke the Should. The obedience-subject remains disciplined. It has now completed the disciplinary stage." This is the cleanest formulation in the vault of the argument [[The achievement society supersedes the disciplinary society]], and it is where Foucault is most explicitly periodized as PRIOR. `Directly stated`.

The Bartleby chapter (ch. 7, [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/07 The Bartleby Case|The Bartleby Case]]) supplies the *worked Foucauldian example*. Han reads Melville's "Story of Wall-Street" as a still-disciplinary world and reads its architecture literally as Foucauldian: "The society that Melville describes is still a disciplinary society. Walls and partitions, the elements of disciplinary architecture, traverse the entire narrative." The "dead wall," the screen, the recurrent "thick-walled prison called the 'Tombs'" — these are the spatial signature of confinement, and Bartleby himself "still represents an obedience-subject." Han's point is periodizing: Bartleby "does not develop symptoms of depression, which is a hallmark of late-modern achievement society"; "he does not face the imperative to be himself that characterizes late-modern achievement society." Because Bartleby belongs to the prior, disciplinary epoch of [[Negativity|negativity]], Han argues that Agamben's reading "fails to take note of the change of mental structure [psychischer Strukturwandel] in the present day." Bartleby is thus deployed as a *negative* benchmark — the disciplinary obedience-subject against whom the self-exploiting [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] is measured. `Directly stated`.

The title chapter (ch. 9, [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/09 Burnout Society|Burnout Society]]) widens the periodization from Foucault's institutions to the *psyche itself*, folding Freud and Kant into the disciplinary order. "The ego, as Freud defines it, is a well-known disciplinary subject … Like disciplinary society, the psychic apparatus sets up walls, thresholds, borders, and guards"; "Freudian psychoanalysis is only possible in repressive societies that found their organization on the negativity of prohibitions and commandments." The Kantian conscience — the "internal judge" of the "doubled self" — is the same disciplinary [[Negativity|negativity]] in moral dress: the obedience-subject "subjects itself [sich unterwift] to the superego," whereas "the achievement-subject projects itself [entwirft sich] onto the ego ideal" ([[Subject vs Project]]). Because "the modal verb that determines achievement society is not the Freudian Should, but Can," the late-modern subject "would no longer have an unconscious … a post-Freudian ego." This is the supersession-of-Foucault thesis carried into depth psychology: where Foucault's [[Disciplinary Society]] disciplined bodies through external [[Negativity]], the achievement society exploits the soul through [[Positivity]], so that "exogenous violence is replaced by self-generated violence … inasmuch as the victim of such violence considers itself free" — the [[Auto-Exploitation]] that the rest of Han's corpus diagnoses. Foucault is not named in ch. 9, but the chapter is built on the ch. 3 periodization, so the Foucault link is `Strongly implied` here; the disciplinary-negativity-as-prior reading of Freud and Kant is `Directly stated`.

## Source Passages

The three load-bearing moments are: (1) the Preface's reworking of the panopticon as a *digital* panopticon of voluntary self-exposure; (2) Ch. 3's verbatim Foucault citation on power as strategic play, used to defend [[Power as Productive|productive asymmetry]]; (3) Ch. 9's explicit naming of "Bentham's panopticon" as "a phenomenon of disciplinary society" — the cleanest formulation of the periodization away from Foucault.
