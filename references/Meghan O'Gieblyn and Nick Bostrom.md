# Meghan O'Gieblyn ↔ Nick Bostrom

## Relationship Type

Disagreement. Bostrom is O'Gieblyn's named antagonist-interlocutor across the two "Paradox" chapters: she takes his "simulation hypothesis" — the thesis of his 2003 *Philosophical Quarterly* paper "Are You Living in a Computer Simulation?" — as the occasion and target of a sustained critique. The disagreement is not about the hypothesis's internal logic, which she rehearses faithfully, but about its self-presentation. Bostrom, "a prominent transhumanist," advances the theory as a piece of sober probabilistic naturalism; O'Gieblyn reads it from the outside as crypto-creationist — "ultimately an argument from design" and, in her earlier formulation, "a modern creation myth." The edge runs O'Gieblyn → Bostrom (critic on subject); Bostrom has no book in `raw/` and is a forward-stub.

## Explanation

The quarrel is a quarrel about disguise. Bostrom builds the simulation hypothesis as a statistical argument: assume many generations of [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] civilizations capable of running ancestor-simulations, accept that minds can be "enacted on silicon processors," and it follows that we are "almost certainly living in a simulation." For Bostrom this is continuous with his transhumanist program — "humanity is in the process of becoming posthuman as we merge our bodies with technology" — and he frames it, per O'Gieblyn's wider reading of the movement, as approaching existential questions "in a sober, disinterested way." O'Gieblyn's presentation in Sweden was built to puncture exactly that framing: "for all its supposed 'naturalism,'" she argued, "the simulation hypothesis was ultimately an argument from design," belonging "to a long lineage of creationist rhetoric that invoked human technologies to argue that the universe could not have come about without the conscious intention of a designer."

This is the same move she makes against [[Ray Kurzweil]] and [[Transhumanism]] generally — reading a "naturalist" futurism as theology that has forgotten its own origins — but routed here through the eighteenth-century design argument rather than through eschatology. She historicizes Bostrom's intuition by way of the [[The Computational Universe|computer-as-cosmos]] metaphor: if the world's fine-tuned "patterns and mathematical regularities" are baffling under pure contingency, they "suddenly made sense" once "the cosmos was in fact an enormous computer that was intentionally designed." The simulation's programmers are, on Bostrom's own admission, "like gods" — "omnipotent" and "omniscient," capable of "resurrect[ing]" inhabitants — so that "thinking deeply about this system" could "produce its own brand of naturalistic theology." O'Gieblyn's verdict is that the theory thereby reproduces, under a [[Information|computational]] vocabulary, the very anthropocentrism it claims to have escaped: "the larger appeal of Bostrom's argument was that it was anthropocentric," letting "us believe once again that we were at the center of things."

The disagreement nests inside O'Gieblyn's larger suspicion of the [[The Computational Universe|informational universe]]. She concedes that Bostrom's hypothesis is more than a debating point — it "could, at least in theory, explain some of the gaps," supplying the missing material "instantiation" that the disembodied, free-floating concept of [[Information|information]] otherwise lacks (the objection, drawn from Claude Shannon, that information "cannot be 'ungrounded'"). But she finally judges that, taken to its conclusion, the theory "didn't really explain anything about the universe or its origins": "the question about origins remained unchanged, only pushed back one degree." The simulation hypothesis is, for her, the "twenty-first century's favored variation on Descartes's skeptical thought experiment" — and, like [[René Descartes|Descartes]]'s evil genius, it resolves only into another regress.
