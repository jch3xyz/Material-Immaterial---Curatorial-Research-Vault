# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Bernard Stiegler

## Relationship Type

Direct citation / shared tradition + disagreement. Han adopts Stiegler's diagnosis that Foucauldian biopower has been superseded by a psychotechnological form of power, but explicitly corrects Stiegler for being too television-centric and for missing the panoptic structure of digital networks — the very structure that makes neoliberal [[Psychopolitics]] possible.

## Explanation

Han cites Stiegler approvingly on the central claim: Foucault's biopower is no longer operative, and in its place stands what Stiegler calls "psychotechnological psychopower." Both thinkers agree that the locus of contemporary domination has shifted from the biological body to the psyche, and that this shift requires a new analytic framework beyond [[Biopolitics]]. However, Han breaks from Stiegler on the diagnosis of the dominant psychotechnical medium. Stiegler focuses overwhelmingly on television and the "programming industry" — a mass-broadcast, one-to-many model that reduces subjects to impulsive consumers. For Han, this is a superannuated framework of cultural critique that cannot account for the digital revolution. Stiegler "hardly examines truly digital technology such as the internet and social media" and "barely pays attention to the panoptic structure of digital networks." Because Stiegler misses the [[Digital Panopticon]], he misses the specifically neoliberal mechanics of [[Smart Power]]: a power that does not operate through external compulsion or passive reception but recruits the free, self-optimizing subject into willing, even passionate self-exploitation. This is the correction that ties Han's critique of Stiegler directly to his account of [[Auto-Exploitation]] and the [[Exploitation of Freedom]]. See also [[Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault]] for the broader argument that Foucault's framework requires abandonment, not mere extension.

## Source Passages

### Stiegler's thesis — adopted by Han

> Bernard Stiegler has rightly observed that Foucault's concept of biopower is not suited to the times: 'Foucault's biopower, which he himself describes (and so powerfully) historically and geographically by localizing it in Europe, is no longer the force behind our age.'

`Directly stated` — Han explicitly endorses ("rightly observed") Stiegler's rejection of biopower as the operative framework.

> According to Stiegler, biopower has been replaced by 'psychotechnological psychopower'. Above all, he has in mind the 'programming industry', which is 'telecratic': by reducing (entmündigen) us to impulsive (triebgesteuert) creatures of consumption, it induces mass regression. Such psychotechnics contrasts with the technology of writing and reading. For Stiegler, the medium of literacy means enlightenment. Accordingly, he invokes Kant: 'Kant's thought of maturity as humanity's sovereignty' is based on the 'apparatus of reading and writing'.

`Directly stated` — Han summarizes Stiegler's positive thesis on psychopower and its telos of Kantian enlightenment-through-literacy.

### Han's correction of Stiegler

> However, Stiegler's overemphasis on television is problematic. For him, it represents the psychotechnical apparatus tout court: 'Now, at the beginning of the twenty-first century, cell phones, and . . . many other devices . . . catalyze . . . synaptic circuitry,' yet it is 'principally . . . television' that governs the influx of information. In fact, the opposition between literacy and television affords a superannuated scheme of cultural critique, which fails to do justice to the digital revolution. Strangely, Stiegler hardly examines truly digital technology such as the internet and social media - which prove fundamentally different from the mass media of the past. What is more, he barely pays attention to the panoptic structure of digital networks. As a result, he completely misses the workings of neoliberal psychopolitics, which employs digital technology on a massive scale.

`Directly stated` — Han's explicit, named critique of Stiegler on three counts: television-centrism, neglect of digital and social media, and blindness to the [[Digital Panopticon]].
