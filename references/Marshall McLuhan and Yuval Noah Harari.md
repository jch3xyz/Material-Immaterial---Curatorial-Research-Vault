# Marshall McLuhan ↔ Yuval Noah Harari

## Relationship Type

Conceptual parallel / historical anticipation — read here as **interpretive synthesis**, not a citation either author made. McLuhan's *Understanding Media* (1964) cannot cite Harari; Harari's *Homo Deus* (2015) does not cite McLuhan in the chapters ingested in this vault. The bridge is the vault's own. The claim is that McLuhan's mid-century vision of life being "translated more and more into the form of information" — all employment becoming "paid learning," all wealth flowing from "the movement of information," and the globe resolving into "a single consciousness" — is an early, technologically-grounded statement of the worldview Harari would later name [[Dataism]]: the cosmos as a single data-processing system in which value migrates wholly to the flow of information. McLuhan supplies the prophecy in a key of arrival; Harari supplies the codified religion, half a century on. This is **interpretive synthesis**, not a debt acknowledged by either party.

## Explanation

McLuhan's *Media as Translators* (ch. 6) makes the founding move: under electric technology the human being itself is converted into information. "In this electric age we see ourselves being translated more and more into the form of information, moving toward the technological extension of consciousness." This is the seed of the vault's [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]] concept, and it points directly at the data-centric ontology Harari systematizes. Where McLuhan says we are translated *into* the form of information, Harari's [[Dataism]] says the human always *was* a data-processing system — an [[Organisms Are Algorithms|algorithm]] — and that information flow is the supreme value. The two meet on the proposition that the world is most truly described as information in motion. (`Interpretive synthesis` — McLuhan describes a translation underway; Harari asserts an ontology; the vault reads the first as anticipating the second.)

The convergence is sharpest in McLuhan's economics. In ch. 6 he draws the consequence for the household-economy: "Under electric technology the entire business of man becomes learning and knowing," so that "all forms of employment become 'paid learning,' and all forms of wealth result from the movement of information." *Automation: Learning a Living* (ch. 33) repeats and hardens the claim — "Paid learning is already becoming both the dominant employment and the source of new wealth in our society" — and dissolves the older categories of work into information: "Wealth and work become information factors." Read against Harari, this is the information economy stated in advance: the worldview in which, as Harari's Dataism holds, the value of any phenomenon is determined by its contribution to data processing, and economic life is recast as the production, movement, and refinement of information. McLuhan's "the movement of information" is, structurally, Harari's [[Freedom of Information|freedom of information]] elevated to first principle. The vault also reads McLuhan's "paid learning" — labour turned into the continuous intake and processing of information — as a precursor to the feminized, dispersed, always-on [[Immaterial Labour]] that the vault tracks from Haraway's homework economy through Han; McLuhan's worker who no longer holds a "job" but is a "nomadic gatherer of knowledge" is an immaterial labourer avant la lettre. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

The cosmological pitch is shared too. McLuhan ends ch. 6 by asking whether "our current translation of our entire lives into the spiritual form of information" might "make of the entire globe, and of the human family, a single consciousness?" — and in *Weapons* (ch. 32) generalizes the premise that grounds it: "we now see all technology, including language, as a means of processing experience, a means of storing and speeding information." This is McLuhan's version of the move Harari makes when Dataism dissolves the boundary between human, animal, and machine into one [[The Internet-of-All-Things|Internet-of-All-Things]] — a single planetary information-processing system, the universe "waking up" into data flow. Both imagine the globe resolving into one consciousness or one processor. The reversal of *affect* that organizes the vault's [[Marshall McLuhan and Byung-Chul Han]] note recurs here in a milder form: McLuhan greets the becoming-information of life as a golden-age "metamorphosis," the toil of man become "a kind of enlightenment," whereas Harari narrates the same trajectory as the possible obsolescence of the human — the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal subject]] superseded once the [[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves|algorithms know us better than we know ourselves]]. The mechanism is one; McLuhan reads it as fulfillment, Harari as a question with an ominous edge. (`Interpretive synthesis` for the mapping; the [[Homo-Centric vs Data-Centric]] tension is where the two valuations sit.)

The upshot for the vault: McLuhan furnishes the early, medium-theoretic statement that life is being "translated into the form of information" and that wealth and work follow the information rather than the matter — a 1964 prophecy of the information economy and the data-centric cosmos. Harari furnishes the named worldview, [[Dataism]], that makes this translation total and gives it the status of a [[Techno-Religion|techno-religion]]. They are best read as one diagnosis stated twice: McLuhan in the optative mood of the electric age's promise, Harari in the indicative mood of an ontology already installed.

## Understanding Media chs. 8-13

McLuhan's later chapters push the same translation past the threshold of language itself, and it is here that the bridge to Harari's [[Dataism]] hardens. *The Spoken Word: Flower of Evil?* (ch. 8) ends on the claim that electric technology supersedes the word as it supersedes the digit — "Electric technology does not need words any more than the digital computer needs numbers" — and then issues its most radical projection: "Electricity points the way to an extension of the process of consciousness itself, ona world scale, and_ without any verbalization whatever." (The raw OCR runs "ona" for "on a" and inserts "and_"; quoted verbatim per provenance rules.) This is the strong, post-verbal form of the move the existing [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]] concept tracks: not merely the human translated *into* the form of information (ch. 6), but consciousness itself extended planet-wide as a process that bypasses language — "a general cosmic consciousness," a "Pentecostal condition of universal understanding and unity." Read against Harari, this preverbal, world-scale processing of awareness is the medium-theoretic anticipation of the data-centric cosmos: where McLuhan says the next step is "not to translate, but to by-pass languages," Harari's Dataism says the supreme value is the free flow of information through the single global system of [[The Internet-of-All-Things]], with the human reconceived as one [[Organisms Are Algorithms|biochemical algorithm]] among others. (`Interpretive synthesis` for the bridge; McLuhan's two quoted passages are `Directly stated`.)

*Number: Profile of the Crowd* (ch. 11) supplies the political-economic underside that Harari's worldview also carries. McLuhan treats statistical aggregation as a genuine technology of knowledge and power: "In every sense, the amassing of numbers statistically gives man a new influx of primitive intuition and magically subconscious awareness, whether of public taste or feeling," and at the chapter's sharpest he names the use to which the statistical profile is put — "At the extreme of the graph profiles of the statisticians there is the frankly expressed object of manipulation of population for varieties of power purposes." His "Mr. Odd Lots" example, where a profile of the small buyer's reliable error lets "the big operators" be "about 80 per cent right," is statistical population-management stated as ordinary fact. The vault reads this "manipulation of population" through the statistical crowd-profile as a precursor to the [[Big Data]] regime that grounds Harari's Dataism (and Han's [[Dataism|psychopolitical]] use of the same word): the population rendered as quantified flow, then steered. Here too McLuhan greets the convergence as a homecoming — "the electric age brings number back into unity with visual and auditory experience," the globe moving toward a "world-wide consensus" — where Harari hears the same statistical instrument as the engine by which authority migrates from the human to the [[Society as Algorithm|algorithmic system]]. The mechanism is shared; only the mood differs. (`Interpretive synthesis`. McLuhan's quoted passages are `Directly stated`; neither author cites the other.)
