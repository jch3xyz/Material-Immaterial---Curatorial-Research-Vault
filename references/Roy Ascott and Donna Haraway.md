# Roy Ascott ↔ Donna Haraway

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation — and the only one in this Ascott cluster where the cross-author link is a claim Ascott actually makes rather than a parallel the vault constructs.** In the essay "Telenoia" [[Roy Ascott|Ascott]] names [[Donna Haraway|Haraway]] by name, names her book *Simians, Cyborgs and Women*, and singles out the "[[A Cyborg Manifesto|Cyborg Manifesto]]" — crediting her with "more wisdom concerning the telematic condition of our species" than he has found "in most of the many (male-dominated) conferences and congresses on technoculture over the past ten years." This is a `direct_citation` with an unusually warm, partisan tone: Ascott is not merely acknowledging a source but enlisting Haraway as the authority who has read the networked condition more truly than the male-dominated technoculture around him. The conceptual bond underwriting the citation — Ascott's networked, [[The Post-Biological Body|post-biological]], re-materialised body meeting Haraway's [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] — is itself well grounded in both texts; that part is `Strongly implied` / `Interpretive synthesis`, but the citation event is `Directly stated`.

## Explanation

Ascott's [[Telenoia]] is his name for the networked, connected consciousness of global telematics — the affirmative inverse of the "anxious, alienated, secretive, and neurotically private paranoia of the old industrial culture." Inside that essay's survey of writers who grasp the new human-machine condition, Haraway is the one he raises above the conference circuit. The reason the citation is more than ceremonial is that Ascott and Haraway are working the *same* boundary: the breakdown of the line between organism and machine, the human and the artificial. Haraway's cyborg is precisely a creature of [[The Breached Boundaries|breached boundaries]] — human/animal, organism/machine, physical/nonphysical — and a figure to be inhabited rather than mourned. Ascott's [[The Post-Biological Body|post-biological body]] is the artist's parallel construction: "we are leaving the old, classical, earthly body for another… not in order to dematerialise but to inhabit a new corporeality, which is almost totally artificial, bionic, prosthetic." Both refuse the choice between nature and machine; both treat the merger as the ground of a new identity rather than a loss.

Three points anchor the relationship.

1. **The citation itself, and its gendered framing.** Ascott does not cite Haraway in passing — he ranks her above "most of the many (male-dominated) conferences and congresses on technoculture over the past ten years," in the same breath as his praise for Marge Piercy's fiction as more telematically aware than William Gibson's "unidimensional punks." The framing matters: Ascott is explicitly aligning his telematic project with a feminist account of technoculture against a male-dominated one, and naming the [[A Cyborg Manifesto|Cyborg Manifesto]] as the source of that wisdom. This is the only place in the cluster where Ascott reaches outside his own coinages to borrow another thinker's figure to characterise "the telematic condition of our species." (`Directly stated`.)

2. **The cyborg meets the post-biological body.** Haraway's cyborg is a hybrid of organism and machine, a refusal of the organic/technological boundary that takes "pleasure in the confusion of boundaries." Ascott's post-biological body is the artist's realisation of the same hybrid: a "new corporeality, which is almost totally artificial, bionic, prosthetic," explicitly framed as "the creation of a cyborg culture" preparing us "for connectivity with artificial life." Crucially, Ascott shares Haraway's anti-disembodiment instinct — the move out of the old body is "not in order to [[Dematerialization|dematerialise]] but to inhabit a new corporeality" — so the cyborg they share is a *re-materialised*, not a disembodied, body. This is the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis stated, like Haraway's cyborg, as "a condensed image of both imagination and material reality." (`Strongly implied` on the convergence; `Directly stated` on each author's own term.)

3. **The decentred, distributed, "schizophrenic" self — Haraway's fractured identity in Ascott's glossary.** Ascott's "Technoetic Aesthetics" glossary defines the *cyberself* as: "We are each made up of many selves: de-centred, distributed, and constructively schizophrenic. We are the embodiment of technoetic relativity." A nearby entry (*phreno-fractals*) repeats it — "Freud is dead and the myth of the unified individual has been destroyed. We are each made up of many selves: de-centred, distributed, and tele-schizophrenic." This decentred, multiple, non-unitary self is the structural cousin of Haraway's cyborg subject — the [[Fractured Identities|fractured identities]] and [[Affinity Not Identity|affinity-not-identity]] she sets against the myth of the unified (and the naturally-grounded) self. Ascott arrives at the partial, multiple self through the practice of telematic art and a borrowed Haraway-style refusal of the unified individual; Haraway argues it as feminist politics. The cyborg is the shared figure of a self that is constitutively many. (`Directly stated` on Ascott; `Interpretive synthesis` on the bridge to Haraway's fractured identities.)

The net relationship: **Ascott directly and admiringly cites Haraway's *Simians, Cyborgs and Women* and her [[A Cyborg Manifesto|Cyborg Manifesto]] as the wisest available account of the telematic condition, and his own post-biological, re-materialised, decentred [[The Post-Biological Body|networked body]] is the artist's construction of exactly Haraway's [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] — a [[The Breached Boundaries|boundary-breaching]] hybrid of organism and machine, a [[Fractured Identities|fractured, distributed self]], a [[Material vs Immaterial|material-and-imaginary]] creature.** Where they differ is register and stakes: Haraway's cyborg is an "ironic political myth" tuned to socialist-feminist [[The Informatics of Domination|domination and its possibilities]]; Ascott's cyborg culture is an artist's celebratory, often mystical [[Telenoia|telenoia]], lighter on the politics of domination. But the citation is real, the figure is shared, and Ascott's anti-dematerialisation reframing of the move-beyond-the-body lands him close to Haraway's own refusal of disembodiment. (See [[Roy Ascott]] / [[Donna Haraway]] for the fuller positions, and [[Roy Ascott and N. Katherine Hayles]] for the cognate Ascott↔Hayles parallel on distributed mind and embodiment.)
