## Explanation

McLuhan's central thesis is that every medium is a prosthesis: "Any extension, whether of skin, hand, or foot, affects the whole psychic and social complex." The technology does not merely serve a pre-given body; it reorganizes the whole sensorium and social order around itself. The motorcar becomes "the carapace, the protective and aggressive shell, of urban and suburban man" — a shell grown over the body, a second skin that refashions the very spaces that "unite and spearate [sic] men." This is already a posthuman move toward [[Embodiment]] as technologically mediated rather than naturally given.

But McLuhan still presupposes a **"Man"** whose body is the stable thing being extended — the medium is an *extension of* an antecedent organic subject who remains the owner and center of his prostheses. Haraway dissolves exactly that boundary. Where McLuhan keeps the carapace external to the man who wears it, Haraway's cyborg makes the machine internal to what we are: "The machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment" ([[The machine is us an aspect of our embodiment]]). The cyborg is one of [[The Breached Boundaries]] between organism and machine; there is no prior "Man" left over to be extended, only the hybrid. McLuhan's extension is thus legible as a **precursor** to the cyborg — the moment before the boundary it presupposes is breached. Read forward through [[The Cyborg]] and [[Posthumanism]], the "extensions of man" become not appendages of a humanist subject but constituents of a being that was never bounded in the first place. (Confidence: Interpretive synthesis — the developmental ordering and the "still presupposes a Man" reading are the vault's, not stated by either author.)

This places both authors inside the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension: the carapace is a material shell whose effect is the reorganization of the immaterial "psychic and social complex," just as the cyborg condenses imagination and material reality.
