# Roy Ascott ↔ Jack Burnham

The keystone vault-internal bridge of the 1960s cybernetic-art moment: the two poles of the same object→system turn. [[Roy Ascott]] (1964–67) stakes out the **participatory / behavioural pole** — the artwork as a feedback loop located in the artist–work–participant triad and *completed* by the spectator — earlier than, and complementary to, [[Jack Burnham]]'s (1968, *Beyond Modern Sculpture*) **object→system pole**, the drift of sculpture from inert object toward responsive, lifelike system. The two never cite one another (Ascott's manifestos *predate* Burnham's book), so the parallel is **interpretive synthesis**. The one direct, cited link runs through the volume's editor: in ch. 04 (Shanken's introduction) Burnham is explicitly named — his vitalist history in *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, his *Software* exhibition (1970), and his account of US cybernetic art lagging Europe.

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel** (the core relationship — interpretive synthesis, not mutual citation) plus a **direct citation** within ch. 04 (Shanken, the editor, naming Burnham). The two artists converge on a single thesis — that aesthetic value has migrated from the styled object to the structured system and process — and arrive from opposite directions: Ascott from participatory cybernetic *pedagogy* and the feedback triad; Burnham from a vitalist/Bergsonian *history of sculpture* turning into the responsive system.

## Explanation

Where Burnham reads the history of modern sculpture as a slow drift from the discrete object toward the autonomous, lifelike, responsive **system** (`[[Systems Esthetics]]`, `[[Object and System]]`), Ascott reads the whole of modern art as a drift from a "deterministic vision" toward a **behavioural tendency** in which the artwork is no longer a finished message but a feedback loop the spectator completes (`[[Behaviourist Art]]`). Burnham's keyword is *system*; Ascott's keyword is *behaviour* — and the two interlock: "When art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware… Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure" (ch. 08, 1967). This is the cleanest statement of the shared move: behaviour → process → **system supersedes structure**, the art-side restatement of the vault's `[[Object vs System]]` and `[[Material vs Immaterial]]` tensions.

Chronology matters and is the reason the parallel is labelled interpretive: Ascott's "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" (*Cybernetica*, 1966–67) and "Behaviourables and Futuribles" (1967) **predate** *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) by one to two years. Ascott is therefore the *earlier* and *participatory* pole, Burnham the slightly later *object-historical* pole — together the participatory and the object-genealogical halves of one systems turn. The only cited contact is editorial: Shanken's introduction (ch. 04) repeatedly invokes Burnham to situate Ascott's career (the *Software* show, the vitalist genealogy, the US-lags-Europe note), and the two share `[[Reciprocal Communication]]`, `[[Cybernetics]]`, `[[Feedback Loops]]`, and the realized art/new-media cluster (`[[New Media Art]]`, `[[Art as Experiential Technology]]`, `[[Cyborg Art]]`).

## Source Passages

**Ascott's behaviour→system thesis (the parallel to Burnham's object→system spine) — Ascott's own words:**

> When art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware in the creative sphere. Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure.

> a shift, in short, towards an art of system and process. This implies that our appreciation of the beautiful... resides in our perception and awareness of a system rather than an object.

> Art is all process, all system.

- The **direct-citation** component (ch. 04) is `Directly stated` and high-confidence: Shanken (the editor) names Burnham and *Beyond Modern Sculpture* verbatim. This is the editor's framing of Ascott's context, **not** a citation Ascott himself made.
- The **conceptual-parallel** component (the two-poles-of-one-systems-turn reading) is high-confidence `Interpretive synthesis`: it rests on the dense textual convergence between Ascott's behaviour→system statements and Burnham's object→system thesis, but the two artists do **not** cite each other, and Ascott's manifestos predate Burnham's book.
