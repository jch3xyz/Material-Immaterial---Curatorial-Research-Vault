# Shoshana Zuboff ↔ George Orwell

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation and historical influence.** [[Shoshana Zuboff]] closes *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* by enlisting George Orwell — not the Orwell of *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (whose Big Brother she has spent the book arguing is the *wrong* model for [[Instrumentarian Power]]), but the Orwell of a 1946 review of the political theorist James Burnham's bestseller *The Managerial Revolution*. Orwell's evisceration of Burnham's "cowardly attachment to power" and his diagnosis of inevitabilism as "a major mental disease" rooted in "cowardice" and "the worship of power" become Zuboff's authority for rejecting the message of surveillance capitalism's invincibility (see [[Inevitabilism]]). Orwell's named courage grounds her closing call to resist — to "bend ourselves toward friction" (see [[Be the Friction]]). This is documented direct citation, not vault synthesis. Orwell remains a **forward-stub** author (no book of his is in `raw/`); per policy his author page is not realized here. He is already an interlocutor of Byung-Chul Han in the vault (see [[Byung-Chul Han and George Orwell]]) — there too as the foil whose dystopia the present has *inverted* rather than fulfilled.

> NOTE: the **James Burnham** of Orwell's 1946 review is the political theorist (author of *The Managerial Revolution*, 1940), NOT [[Jack Burnham]], the art critic of *Beyond Modern Sculpture* ingested into this vault. They are unrelated figures who happen to share a surname.

## Explanation

**Orwell against Burnham's "inevitability."** In the final pages of her closing chapter [[The Coup from Above]], Zuboff turns to Orwell's review of James Burnham, whose 1940 book prophesied that "capitalism, democracy, and socialism would not survive World War II" and would be replaced by "a new planned centralized society modeled on totalitarianism" run by a "managerial" class. The detail Orwell — and Zuboff — fasten on is that Burnham kept "predicting a continuation of the thing that is happening": in 1940 he prophesied a German victory and the managed society to follow, then, as the Red Army advanced, "asserted with equal certainty that Russia would dominate the world." For Zuboff this serial submission to "the conqueror of the moment" is the precise pathology of [[Inevitabilism]] — "the message of inevitability that is power's velvet-gloved right hand," the conviction that the existing trend must be "accept[ed] … as irreversible." (`Directly stated`)

**Inevitabilism as a "mental disease" of cowardice and power-worship.** The line Zuboff quotes at greatest length is Orwell's verdict on the *habit* of predicting the continuation of the present: "It is a major mental disease, and its roots lie partly in cowardice and partly in the worship of power, which is not fully separable from cowardice." This reframes the whole rhetorical apparatus of surveillance capitalism's inevitability — the smooth narrative that resistance is futile, that "there is no alternative" — not as sober realism but as moral failure (see [[Smoothness]], the "smooth flows of coercive confluence" Zuboff names a few lines later). Orwell's "instinct to bow down before the conqueror of the moment, to accept the existing trend as irreversible" is, for Zuboff, exactly the enthrallment surveillance capitalists cultivate in their subjects. (`Directly stated`)

**Orwell's courage as the ground of resistance.** Zuboff makes the cautionary tale explicit: "Burnham's cowardice is a cautionary tale. We are living in a moment when surveillance capitalism and its instrumentarian power appear to be invincible." Against that appearance she sets "Orwell's courage," which "demands that we refuse to cede the future to illegitimate power" and "break the spell of enthrallment, helplessness, resignation, and numbing." This is the hinge where the diagnostic citation becomes a normative program: "We answer his call when we bend ourselves toward friction, rejecting the smooth flows of coercive confluence." That sentence is the textual root of the vault concept [[Be the Friction]] — friction as the deliberate refusal of frictionless inevitability. Orwell thus supplies the courage; Hannah Arendt, quoted immediately after, supplies the faculty of beginning (see [[Shoshana Zuboff and Hannah Arendt]]) — the two figures jointly close the book against fatalism. (`Directly stated`)

**Continuity with the Orwell of the rest of the book.** This closing use of Orwell is pointed because Zuboff has spent the book distinguishing [[Instrumentarian Power]] from Orwell's *Nineteen Eighty-Four*: instrumentarianism is "the precise antithesis of Orwell's Big Brother," operating through certainty and behavioral modification "without terror" rather than through the boot and the telescreen. So she does not invoke Orwell the dystopian prophet — she invokes Orwell the *critic of inevitabilist cowardice*, the moralist who insisted the future is not foreordained. The choice underlines her central methodological claim that the present is *unprecedented* and therefore not legible through old dystopias, while still being answerable to the old courage. (`Strongly implied` — Zuboff makes the Big Brother / instrumentarianism distinction explicitly elsewhere in the book; the inference that her Ch. 18 Orwell is deliberately the *non*-dystopian Orwell is the synthesis.)

**High.** Orwell is named and quoted verbatim across the closing pages of Ch. 18, and Zuboff explicitly builds her rejection of [[Inevitabilism]] and her call to [[Be the Friction]] on Orwell's 1946 review of James Burnham — "Burnham's cowardice is a cautionary tale … Orwell's courage demands that we refuse to cede the future." This is documented direct citation and acknowledged intellectual debt, hence the high confidence and the `direct_citation` label. The one interpretive element is the observation that this closing Orwell is deliberately the critic-of-inevitabilism rather than the Big-Brother dystopian whom Zuboff rejects as a model for [[Instrumentarian Power]] (`Strongly implied`).
