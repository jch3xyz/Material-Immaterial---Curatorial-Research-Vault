# Donna Haraway ↔ Karl Marx

## Relationship Type

Shared tradition with disagreement. Haraway writes *from within* socialist-feminism and "faithful filiation" to Marxism, yet her cyborg myth is built precisely against Marxism's foundational moves: the plot of original unity, the ontology of labor, the "preeminently Western self," and the reflex of reading domination as false consciousness. The debt is real and the critique is internal — she is a heretic, not an apostate.

## Donna Haraway (A Cyborg Manifesto)

In *A Cyborg Manifesto* Haraway names her project as "an effort to build an ironic political myth faithful to feminism, socialism, and materialism," but faithful in the manner of blasphemy rather than reverent worship — an inheritor who refuses the inheritance's deepest premises. Her quarrel with Marxism runs along four lines, all of them affirming the tradition while breaking with it.

**1. Against the plot of original unity.** Haraway's cyborg "has no origin story in the Western sense." The humanist origin story "depends on the myth of original unity, fullness, bliss and terror," and she names Marxism and psychoanalysis as "the twin potent myths" that inscribe it. Citing Hilary Klein, she holds that "both Marxism and psychoanalysis, in their concepts of labor and of individuation and gender formation, depend on the plot of original unity out of which difference must be produced." Where Marxism narrates alienation as separation from an original wholeness to be recovered (unalienated labor, restored species-being), the cyborg "skips the step of original unity" — it has "no truck with... unalienated labor, or other seductions to organic wholeness." (`Directly stated`)

**2. Against the ontology of labor and the "preeminently Western self."** Haraway gives a precise reconstruction of the Marxian apparatus she inherits: "Labor is the preeminently privileged category enabling the Marxist to overcome illusion... Labor is the humanizing activity that makes man; labor is an ontological category permitting the knowledge of a subject." Socialist-feminism "advanced by allying itself with the basic analytic strategies of Marxism," expanding labor by analogy to include women's household and reproductive activity, so that "the unity of women here rests on an epistemology based on the ontological structure of 'labor.'" Her objection is exact: "The inheritance of Marxian humanism, with its preeminently Western self, is the difficulty for me." The essentializing move, she argues, is not in naturalizing unity but "in the ontological structure of labor or of its analogue, women's activity." (`Directly stated`)

**3. Against domination read as false consciousness.** Haraway credits Marxism with seeing power clearly while faulting it for misreading complicity: "For excellent reasons, most Marxisms see domination best and have trouble understanding what can only look like false consciousness and people's complicity in their own domination in late capitalism." Against sorting experience into "clear-sighted critique grounding a solid political epistemology" versus "manipulated false consciousness," she calls for "subtle understanding of emerging pleasures, experiences, and powers." Marxist analysis is good at domination but blind to the ambivalent, partial complicities of life in "the integrated circuit." (`Directly stated`)

**4. Marx as the analyst of an order now superseded.** In "The Informatics of Domination," Haraway uses Marx as the high-water mark of an analysis whose object has mutated. Under the regime of coding, "Exchange in this world transcends the universal translation effected by capitalist markets that Marx analyzed so well." The phrase is double-edged: it honors Marx's analysis of the market's "universal translation" of value while claiming that the new "translation of the world into a problem in coding" — information as the universal equivalent — exceeds the frame of *Capital*. Domination has shifted from the commodity form to the informatics of command-control-communication-intelligence. (`Directly stated` for the quotation; `Interpretive synthesis` for the reading that information supersedes the commodity as universal equivalent.)

Even Haraway's rejection of "dialectics" is staged within the tradition: irony is "about contradictions that do not resolve into larger wholes, even dialectically," and "dialectics too is a dream language, longing to resolve contradiction." The cyborg refuses the dialectical promise of synthesis that Marxism shares with the humanism it opposes.

`Directly stated` — Haraway reconstructs and then names her precise difficulty with the Marxian ontology of labor.

Exchange in this world transcends the universal translation effected by capitalist markets that Marx analyzed so well.
