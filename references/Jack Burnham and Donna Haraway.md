# Jack Burnham ↔ Donna Haraway

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel — vault-internal interpretive synthesis, NOT mutual citation.** Burnham and Haraway never cite each other, and there is no evidence either read the other on this point. This note draws a bridge the vault editor finds load-bearing: Burnham's 1968 chapter "Robot and Cyborg Art" deploys the *cybernetic organism* as an art-world category seventeen years before Haraway's 1985 "[[A Cyborg Manifesto]]" turns it into a political ontology. Both seize on the cyborg as the figure that dissolves the boundary between [[The Breached Boundaries|organism and machine]]; the convergence is real, but the projects diverge sharply, and everything below is labeled `Interpretive synthesis` or `Speculative connection` accordingly. Burnham makes no claim Haraway made; Haraway makes no claim Burnham made.

## Explanation

The two writers reach for the same word — *cyborg*, "cybernetic organism" — from opposite ends of a seventeen-year gap and toward opposite ends.

**The shared move: erasing the organism/machine line.** Burnham's working definition is bluntly continuist: "A cyborg, then, is any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism." He grounds it in a flat ontology of matter — "there are no qualitative physical differences between living and nonliving matter; both groupings represent, simply, an ascending scale of complexity in the organization of matter" — and concludes that "organisms artificially created may possess consciousness." Haraway performs the identical boundary-breach but names it as one of [[The Breached Boundaries|three boundary breakdowns]]: "the boundary between human and animal is thoroughly breached," then "the leaky distinction is between animal–human (organism) and machine," then "physical and nonphysical." Both treat the organism-machine seam as the constitutive site of the cyborg, and both route it through [[Cybernetics]] and [[Feedback Loops]] (Burnham's "reciprocal feedback," Norbert Wiener's "control and communication in the animal and the machine"). This much is a genuine [[Material vs Immaterial]] parallel. (Label: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

**The divergence: teleology of art vs. ironic political ontology.** Burnham's cyborg is the endpoint of an *art-historical teleology* — sculpture's slow, "preordained" march toward "the simulation of biological life." He frames it as the "aesthetic core of the Faustian urge": "Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically." Cyborg Art "heralds the beginnings of the sculpture of the future"; it is the medium through which "the scientific demiurge … moves ceaselessly in a single irreversible direction." This is exactly the posture Haraway *refuses*. Her cyborg is "[[The cyborg is our ontology and it gives us our politics|our ontology]]," an "ironic political myth," a creature with "no origin story in the Western sense" and no salvation narrative — "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess." Where Burnham's cyborg fulfills a destiny and re-enchants the Faustian dream of man-as-creator, Haraway's blasphemously cancels the dream of wholeness, mastery, and teleology altogether. Burnham's is a celebratory engineer's eschatology; Haraway's is an "[[The Refusal of Innocence|ironic faith]]" weaponized against precisely such totalizing stories. (Label: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

**Domination, latent vs. named.** Burnham's chapter does register the cyborg's darker continuum — RHIC "thought control," the CAM "walking truck" drafted by the U.S. Army, the cyborg as "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism" including military and disciplinary ones — but he treats these as neutral milestones in technique's "irreversible" advance. Haraway makes the politics the whole point: the cyborg "is the awful apocalyptic telos of the 'West's' escalating dominations," "the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism," and her [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]] names the regime Burnham can only describe and admire. The same object (the feedback-coupled organism-machine) is, for Burnham, the consummation of art's destiny, and for Haraway, a structure of domination to be turned against itself. (Label: `Speculative connection` — Burnham nowhere thematizes domination as Haraway does; the contrast is the editor's.)

In sum: Burnham anticipates Haraway's *figure* without any of her *irony*. He is the art-world's early, earnest deployment of the cybernetic organism — proof that the cyborg was circulating as a serious aesthetic-ontological category in 1968 — while Haraway, in 1985, inherits a culture saturated with exactly Burnham's kind of teleological technophilia and writes against it.

## Source Passages

From Burnham's Introduction — the first naming of Cyborg Art as art's future:

> Nothing more spectacular heralds the beginnings of the sculpture of the future than the slow emergence of what is called "Cyborg Art" (the art of cybernetic organisms).

From "Sculpture and Automata" — the Faustian teleology of re-creating life:

> Here the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge shows itself; Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically.

From "Robot and Cyborg Art" — Burnham's flat definition of the cyborg:

> A cyborg, then, is any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism.

Against which stands Haraway's own definition, already in the vault under [[The Cyborg]] and [[A Cyborg Manifesto]]: "A cyborg is a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction" — a *political* and *ironic* hybrid, not an art-teleological one.

## Confidence

**Medium.** The textual basis on Burnham's side is direct and verbatim: he uses the exact phrase "cybernetic organism," defines the cyborg, and dates the term ("Less than a decade ago the term cyborg … was created"). The Haraway side is independently grounded in the already-ingested *[[A Cyborg Manifesto]]*. What is *not* in either text is any link between them — neither author cites the other, and there is no documented influence. The parallel (shared organism/machine breach) and the contrast (teleology vs. ironic ontology; technophilia vs. critique of domination) are the vault editor's interpretive synthesis, not a claim made by Burnham or Haraway. Hence the relationship is `conceptual_parallel`, explicitly flagged as a vault-internal reading rather than a citation. The strongest, lowest-risk claim is simply chronological: the cyborg was a working art-world category in 1968, seventeen years before the manifesto.
