# Jack Burnham ↔ N. Katherine Hayles

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel — no citation in either direction — best described as *precursor*, and explicitly an interpretive synthesis the vault constructs rather than a citation either author made.** [[Jack Burnham|Burnham]] (*[[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]*, 1968) does not cite [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]], who was an undergraduate when he wrote; Hayles (*[[How We Became Posthuman]]*, 1999) does not cite Burnham in the chapters the vault has ingested. There is no `direct_citation` and no documented `historical_influence` to assert. What binds them is that, working from opposite disciplines three decades apart, both read the postwar moment through **first-wave [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]]** — the same source literature (Wiener, Ashby, Shannon, the negative-feedback machine, the organism-as-information-system) — and both watch the boundary between the living and the made dissolve into a single category of **[[Systems Theory|system]]**. Burnham supplies the **late-1960s art-historical announcement** that sculpture is on its way to producing "[[Artificial Life|life-bearing artifacts]]" and ushering in a "**post-human species**"; Hayles supplies the **late-1990s genealogy and critique** of how cybernetics produced exactly that posthuman by recoding the body as [[Information|information]]. The relationship is `conceptual_parallel` with a near-perfect convergence of *source material* and a sharp asymmetry of *stance*: Burnham greets the dissolution with prophetic wonder, Hayles with a critical genealogy. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Explanation

Burnham and Hayles are reading the same intellectual event — the cybernetic redefinition of "life" as a property of systems rather than of a vital essence — and they even use the same vocabulary, because they are downstream of the same sources. Three registers of convergence, all of them `Interpretive synthesis` on the cross-author bridge and `Directly stated` on the Burnham side taken alone:

1. **The system, not the organism, becomes the unit of analysis — life as a list of functional properties.** Burnham's foundational move in the Introduction is to dissolve the difference between the living and the artificial by defining a *system* as anything that "may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life: self-organization, growth, internal or external mobility, irritability or sensitivity, input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death." Life is no longer an essence but a checklist of behaviors — "input and output," "kinetically sustained equilibrium" (i.e. [[Homeostasis|homeostasis]]) — that a non-living assembly can exhibit. In the Cyborg-Art chapter he generalizes the move explicitly: "the system becomes the unit by which all levels of fluid organization are analyzed up through and including living organisms." This is precisely the first-wave cybernetic operation Hayles genealogizes ([[The Three Waves of Cybernetics]]): the redefinition of the human and the machine as instances of one informational category, so that "[[The Body as Data|body]]" and "machine" become interchangeable substrates for the same [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] dynamics. Burnham, writing in 1968, is *inside* the worldview Hayles later names and historicizes. (`Directly stated` on Burnham; `Interpretive synthesis` on the bridge to Hayles.)

2. **Negative feedback as the bridge from nineteenth-century biology to twentieth-century machines.** Burnham locates, with unusual historical precision, the exact mechanism by which the organism became machine-readable: in the biotic-sources chapter he credits Claude Bernard, whose "disclosure of the various regulatory mechanisms (systems utilizing negative feedback circuits)" reconceived the body "as a vast web of balanced processes," and concludes — in a sentence that could be an epigraph for Hayles's first chapter — that "Bernard's nineteenth-century biological explanations became the control concepts for machines in the twentieth century." This is the genealogy of [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] in miniature: the regulatory, self-correcting body of nineteenth-century physiology supplying the [[Homeostasis|homeostatic]] [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] concept that Wiener's wartime machines would borrow back. Hayles tells the same story from the cybernetics side — homeostasis as the organizing principle of the first wave, the [[The Observer|observer]] and [[Reflexivity|reflexivity]] complicating it in the second — and the [[Homeostasis vs Reflexivity]] tension is the axis on which she does it. Burnham hands the vault the *biological prehistory* of the homeostatic machine; Hayles hands it the *cybernetic afterlife*. (`Directly stated` on Burnham; `Interpretive synthesis` on the convergence.)

3. **The "post-human" horizon — Burnham's prophecy, Hayles's critique.** Burnham's Introduction frames modern sculpture as "a preparatory stage representing steps toward the simulation of biological life," driving toward "the only means by which man would eventually reconstruct intelligent life, or what might be called life-bearing artifacts." In the final chapter he names the destination outright: "one might have a few premonitions of the part being played by sculpture in shaping our destination as a post-human species." This is, thirty-one years early and in an art-historical key, the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] Hayles makes her subject — but the stances split exactly where the McLuhan and Kurzweil bridges also split. Burnham greets the supersession of "organic life" by "very sophisticated forms of synthetic life" as possibly "part of an unseen plan," a prophetic wonder; Hayles ([[The disappearance of the body is the symptom of a new subjectivity]], [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]]) reads the same supersession as the contingent and resistible erasure of [[Embodiment|embodiment]]. The two are closest in *content* (the artifact that lives; the human surpassed) and furthest apart in *valuation*. (`Directly stated` on Burnham; `Interpretive synthesis` on the opposition.)

A fourth, more oblique convergence sharpens register 3. Burnham's Introduction makes [[Reification|reification]] — the "thingification" by which scientific culture remakes the world — the engine of modern sculpture, and pushes it past the object toward "remaking the biological composition of its inhabitants": abstraction becomes "psychic preparation for the entire re-creation of society, including remaking the biological composition of its inhabitants." That is the project Hayles tracks under the body recoded as [[Information|information]] and the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal subject]] reengineered as pattern — and it is the same impulse her Kurzweil-side notes ([[Human Body Version 2.0]], [[Reverse-Engineering the Human Brain]], [[Transcending Biology]]) treat as the [[The Computational Universe|computational]] dream of a substrate-independent human. Burnham frames the remaking of the biological body as the *cultural aim* of formalist art in a scientific age; Hayles frames it as the ideology of disembodiment to be contested ([[Material vs Immaterial]], [[Embodiment vs Information]]).

The net relationship: **Burnham announces, from within art history and on the basis of first-wave cybernetics, that the boundary between the living and the made is collapsing into the category of the system and that art is preparing a "post-human species"; Hayles, three decades later, genealogizes and contests the same cybernetic collapse as the production of the posthuman through the disembodiment of information.** Both treat life as a set of system-properties rather than a vital essence (Burnham explicitly retiring the Bergsonian "élan vital" for the cybernetic "kybernetes"); both route the body-as-information through Bernard's negative feedback and Wiener's [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]]; both arrive at the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]. Where Burnham thrills to the [[Artificial Life|life-bearing artifact]], Hayles insists the recoding of the human as information was [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment|never a real subtraction of the body]]. Read together they bracket the cybernetic posthuman: Burnham as its 1968 art-world herald, Hayles as its end-of-century critic. (See the author notes [[Jack Burnham]] / [[N. Katherine Hayles]] for the fuller positions.)

## Source Passages

The Burnham side of the parallel is carried by verbatim passages from *Beyond Modern Sculpture*. The system-as-life-properties claim is the Introduction's catalogue — "self-organization, growth, internal or external mobility, irritability or sensitivity, input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death" — generalized in the Cyborg-Art chapter to "the system becomes the unit by which all levels of fluid organization are analyzed up through and including living organisms." The negative-feedback genealogy is the biotic-sources chapter's Bernard passage, ending "Bernard's nineteenth-century biological explanations became the control concepts for machines in the twentieth century." The cybernetic-organism claim is the automata chapter's account of Grey Walter, Ashby, and Shannon's machines as "working models, generally analogues of various aspects of the nervous system found in simple organisms." The post-human horizon is the Cyborg-Art chapter's closing "premonitions of the part being played by sculpture in shaping our destination as a post-human species." A supporting passage from the formalism chapter — "to produce effect without the hindrance or fallibility of objects" — names the dematerializing impulse (effect without object) that converges with the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis Hayles makes her subject. The Hayles half of this note is documented by cross-reference to her already-ingested notes ([[N. Katherine Hayles]], [[Cybernetics]], [[The Three Waves of Cybernetics]], [[Homeostasis]], [[The Body as Data]], [[Posthumanism]], the disembodiment arguments) rather than by new quotation of her text; wherever a Burnham passage is paired with a Hayles commitment, the cross-author claim is labelled `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Confidence

**Medium.** The Burnham passages are verbatim and load-bearing, and each maps onto a securely documented Hayles position, so the *content* of the parallel — both read the cybernetic dissolution of the living/made boundary, both route the body-as-information through Bernard's negative feedback and first-wave cybernetics, both arrive at the posthuman — is well supported (`Directly stated` on the Burnham side taken alone). The convergence is unusually strong at the level of *shared source literature*: Burnham and Hayles are both downstream of Wiener, Ashby, Shannon, and the negative-feedback machine, which is why their vocabularies coincide ("input and output," "negative feedback circuits," "post-human"). Confidence is held to medium, not high, for two reasons. First, there is **no citation in either direction**: the linkage is a juxtaposition the vault constructs across a thirty-one-year gap, so the relationship itself is `Interpretive synthesis`, not a claim either author asserts. Second, the **valuations diverge sharply** — Burnham greets the life-bearing artifact and the "post-human species" with prophetic wonder; Hayles writes against the disembodiment the posthuman becomes — so the parallel is genuine at the level of the shared object and shared sources but must not be read as agreement. The "precursor vs critique" framing is the vault's interpretive scaffolding.
