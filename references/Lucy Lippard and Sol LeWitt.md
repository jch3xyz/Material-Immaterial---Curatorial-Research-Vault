# Lucy Lippard ↔ Sol LeWitt

## Relationship Type

**Historical influence — and personal/intellectual interlocution.** Sol LeWitt is the single figure [[Lucy Lippard]] names as "my major intellectual influence at the time" of [[Conceptual Art]]'s emergence. The relationship is not citation-as-critique (as Hayles reads Wiener) but the reverse: Lippard builds her account of the field *out of* LeWitt's friendship, work, and writings. He supplies the two canonical texts that name and program the genre — "Paragraphs on Conceptual Art" (1967) and "Sentences on Conceptual Art" (1969) — and the small-c / capital-C distinction that calibrates Lippard's own working definition. LeWitt is therefore the site at which the productive idea-vs-object tension within [[Dematerialization]] is first articulated.

## Explanation

For Lippard, LeWitt is not one artist among many but the hub of the studio community and the personal source of her entry into the movement. "Sol LeWitt was a close friend of ours, and my major intellectual influence at the time," she writes; her "own history of Conceptual art is particularly entwined with that studio community, and with LeWitt's work and writings" — and it was "through him, around 1965–66," that she met or saw the work of Graham, Smithson, Darboven, Art & Language, Kosuth, and Bochner. (Label: `Directly stated`.) The friendship is the conduit; the influence is documentary as well as intellectual, since *Six Years* reproduces LeWitt's two foundational statements in full.

Three of LeWitt's contributions structure Lippard's definition of the field:

1. **"Paragraphs on Conceptual Art" (1967) names the field and locates the work in the idea.** LeWitt's essay opens, "I will refer to the kind of art in which I am involved as conceptual art. In concept art the idea or concept is the most important aspect of the work," and proceeds: "all of the planning and decisions are made beforehand and the execution is a perfunctory affair. The idea becomes a machine that makes the art." Lippard quotes the slogan directly — "In 1967, LeWitt said 'The idea becomes a machine that makes the art'" — and her own definition (idea paramount, material secondary) is the critical generalization of LeWitt's programmatic claim. (Label: `Directly stated`, LeWitt.)

2. **"Sentences on Conceptual Art" (1969) pushes the idea past the requirement of physical form.** LeWitt's aphorism — "Ideas alone can be works of art; they are in a chain of development that may eventually find some form. All ideas need not be made physical" — is the strongest statement of dematerialization in *Six Years*, and Lippard places it as an epigraph at the head of "Escape Attempts" (slightly varied there as "they are a chain of development"). It is also the book's literal first words: "Conceptual artists are mystics rather than rationalists. They leap to conclusions that logic cannot reach." (Label: `Directly stated`, LeWitt.)

3. **The small-c / capital-C distinction qualifies Lippard's own definition.** LeWitt "distinguished between conceptual art 'with a small c' (e.g. his own work, in which the material forms were often conventional, although generated by a paramount idea) and Conceptual art 'with a capital C.'" Lippard adopts the distinction explicitly: her "Conceptual art, for me, means work in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary," is the capital-C usage, while LeWitt's own practice — material forms "often conventional, although generated by a paramount idea" — is the small-c case. This is the precise hinge of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension within the movement: even where the object remains conventional and durable, it is the *idea* that generates it. The idea is paramount; the material is not necessarily abolished, only subordinated. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The relationship is thus one of **constitutive influence**: where Hayles reads against her source, Lippard reads *with* hers. LeWitt furnishes the vocabulary ("conceptual art," "the idea becomes a machine that makes the art," "all ideas need not be made physical"), and Lippard's role is to extend, historicize, and bound it — narrowing his "concept art" into a documented field while preserving his careful qualification that conventional material forms can still be "conceptual" if a paramount idea generates them. This qualification is what keeps Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] from collapsing into a simple claim that Conceptual art has no objects; the object may persist, demoted, as the idea's residue. The same tension surfaces in the systems-and-information strand of the genre that LeWitt's permutational work (his serial projects, the "machine" that makes the art) shares with [[Systems Theory]]. (Label: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Confidence

**High.** The relationship is anchored in Lippard's explicit self-description ("my major intellectual influence at the time") and in two LeWitt texts she reproduces verbatim within *Six Years*. The three substantive contributions — field-naming, the idea-as-machine, ideas-need-not-be-physical — and the small-c/capital-C distinction are all directly stated, the latter in Lippard's own words adopting LeWitt's terms. Only the synthesizing frame (LeWitt as the site of the idea-vs-object tension, and the link of his permutational practice to [[Systems Theory]]) is interpretive; the textual dependence of Lippard's definition on LeWitt's writings is explicit.
