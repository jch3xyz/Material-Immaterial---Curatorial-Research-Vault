# Donella Meadows ↔ Roy Ascott

## Relationship Type

Shared tradition (no mutual citation) — a conceptual parallel routed through a common second-wave cybernetic lineage. Meadows and Ascott never cite one another, and their projects sit in different registers: Meadows writes a discipline of system dynamics (populations, economies, thermostats), Ascott a theory and practice of cybernetic and [[Telematic Art]]. What they hold in common is inherited rather than exchanged — both take the [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]], and specifically the *shifting balance among competing loops*, as the engine of a system's changing behavior. This note is an **interpretive synthesis**, not a documented influence.

## Explanation

Meadows and Ascott descend from the same [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] tradition — the control-communication-information synthesis of Wiener, Ashby, and Pask, and its later second-order, self-organizing turn. For Meadows, a system's behavior over time is set not by any single loop but by which loop currently *dominates* the stock, and behavior changes when that dominance shifts (see [[Shifting Dominance]]): the same fixed structure yields growth, equilibrium, or decline depending on the relative strength of a reinforcing and a balancing loop. Ascott takes the same primitive — the feedback loop that closes "an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork/observer" — and reads the whole trajectory of modern art as a shift from a "deterministic vision" toward a participatory, self-regulating system the spectator completes, scaling it in his later work toward [[Network Consciousness]] and [[Cyberception]]. (Interpretive synthesis.)

The bridge is Meadows's own generalization that "systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors, even if the outward appearance of these systems is completely dissimilar." Read across the vault, this licenses the parallel: an Ascottian participatory artwork and a Meadowsian population are outwardly nothing alike, yet both are stocks under coupled feedback loops whose *changing dominance* is what makes their behavior evolve. Where Meadows names shifting dominance as a *diagnostic* — why systems surprise us — Ascott treats the loop's openness as a *generative* and even utopian principle (the "perfectibility of systems… the perfectibility of man"). The shared premise is that behavior is a property of loop structure and its shifting balance, not of a system's material surface. (Interpretive synthesis; the two authors do not cite each other.)

The divergence is as telling as the convergence. Meadows keeps feedback rigorously value-neutral and constraint-bound — a reinforcing loop of expansion is eventually overtaken by a balancing loop imposed by limits, and no amount of participation removes the balancing loop's eventual dominance. Ascott's cybernetics is normative and expansive, reading the feedback loop as the vehicle of consciousness, connectivity, and "world brain" telematic union, with far less attention to the balancing loops and delays that, for Meadows, make such reinforcing dynamics overshoot and collapse. The tradition is shared; the ethic drawn from it is not. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## Source Passages

Meadows's statement of the loop-structure-determines-behavior principle — the hinge that makes the cross-reading possible:

> One of the central insights of systems theory, as central as the observation that systems largely cause their own behavior, is that systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors, even if the outward appearance of these systems is completely dissimilar.

Ascott's feedback-loop primitive is documented in [[Cybernetics]] and [[Feedback Loops]]: cybernetics as "a matter of 'stance'" whose operative principle is the loop that makes "an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork/observer," concerned "with the procurement of effective action by means of self-organising systems." The two are not quoted together in either author; the alignment is the interpretive claim of this note.
