# Yuval Noah Harari ↔ Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation (one-directional: O'Gieblyn → Harari) resting on a deep conceptual parallel.** The two authors are not symmetrically related. [[Yuval Noah Harari|Harari]]'s *[[Homo Deus]]* (2017) predates [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]'s *[[God Human Animal Machine]]* (2021), does not and cannot cite it, and reaches its conclusions about [[Dataism]], the [[The Algorithm as Providence|algorithm-as-providence]], [[Algorithmic Determinism|algorithmic determinism]] and the dissolution of the liberal subject independently. O'Gieblyn, downstream, **names Harari and quotes *Homo Deus* verbatim at least twice** (and lists the book in her bibliography), using him as a primary witness for two of her own theses: (1) that the inscrutable [[The Black Box|master algorithm]] is the [[Disenchantment|disenchanted]] age's substitute for the Christian God whose plan we cannot grasp, and (2) that [[Dataism]] marks "the end of [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)|liberal humanism]]." The relationship is therefore classed `direct_citation`, but the citations are load-bearing precisely *because* the two books already converge on a single problem-set; O'Gieblyn cites Harari to ratify a parallel, not to argue against him. Underneath the citation sits a `shared_tradition`: both read the [[Mind as Machine|mind-as-machine]] metaphor (steam engine, then computer) and the rise of [[Society as Algorithm|society-as-algorithm]] as a quasi-religious shift, and both stake their final reservation on the same unsolved riddle — [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|the hard problem of consciousness]].

## Explanation

The shared problem-set is unusually tight, which is why O'Gieblyn can quote Harari almost as a co-author. Four overlapping registers organize the relationship.

1. **The algorithm as providence — and O'Gieblyn's verbatim citation of it (Ch11 direct citation).** Harari's most theologically charged claim in *[[Homo Deus]]* is that the master algorithm occupies the structural position of God: "Just as according to Christianity we humans cannot understand God and His plan, so Dataism says the human brain cannot embrace the new master algorithms." This is the core of the vault concept [[The Algorithm as Providence]] and of [[The Black Box]] — the opacity of machine-learning systems re-enchanted as divine inscrutability. O'Gieblyn lifts exactly this sentence into her own Ch11 argument that "it was difficult to read an article on these technologies without coming across a religious metaphor," writing: "In his book *Homo Deus*, Yuval Noah Harari makes virtually the same analogy." (Note a small but real textual fact: O'Gieblyn's rendering — "Dataism *declares* that the human brain cannot *fathom* the new master algorithms" — slightly alters Harari's actual wording, "Dataism *says* the human brain cannot *embrace* the new master algorithms." Each is quoted verbatim below *from its own source*; the discrepancy is O'Gieblyn's, not the vault's.)

2. **Dataism as the end of liberal humanism — O'Gieblyn routing the subject's dissolution through Harari (Ch12 direct citation).** O'Gieblyn's chapter on prediction and [[Algorithmic Determinism]] turns explicitly to Harari to name its stakes: "Harari argues that this would officially mark the end of liberal humanism, which depends on the assumption that an individual knows what is best for herself and can make rational decisions about her best interests." She then quotes *Homo Deus* directly: "Whereas humanism commanded: 'Listen to your feelings!' ... Dataism now commands: 'Listen to the algorithms! They know how you feel.'" This is the precise passage in Harari's Ch14 ("The Data Religion") and it is the hinge by which O'Gieblyn imports Harari's diagnosis of [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)|liberal humanism]]'s collapse into her own. It is also where the two books touch [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] from opposite ends: Harari dissolves the liberal individual from *inside* science (free will and the unitary self are "imaginary stories"; see Ch8, "The Time Bomb in the Laboratory"), while O'Gieblyn reads the same dissolution as the disenchanted endpoint of a longer theological story.

3. **The mind as machine — steam engine, then computer (shared tradition).** Both authors make the identical genealogical move: the dominant technology of each era supplies the reigning metaphor for mind. Harari (Ch3/Ch4, "The Human Spark"): "In the nineteenth century, scientists described brains and minds as if they were steam engines ... Today we know of a far more sophisticated technology – the computer – so we explain the human psyche as if it were a computer processing data rather than a steam engine regulating pressure. But this new analogy may turn out to be just as naïve." This is exactly the argument of [[Technology as Metaphor]] and the [[Mind as Machine]] metaphor as O'Gieblyn develops them — the warning that [[Organisms Are Algorithms|"organisms are algorithms"]] is a metaphor mistaken for a literal truth. Neither cites the other here; the convergence is independent and structural, which is what makes it a `shared_tradition` rather than a citation.

4. **The hard problem as the shared reservation (shared tradition / conceptual parallel).** Both authors, having pushed the computational picture to its limit, halt at the same wall. Harari (Ch3/Ch4): "nobody has any idea how a congeries of biochemical reactions and electrical currents in the brain creates the subjective experience of pain, anger or love ... as of 2016, we have absolutely no idea." This is [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] stated in Harari's terms, and it is the same lacuna O'Gieblyn organizes her book around (via [[David Chalmers]]). Both also pass through [[René Descartes]]: Harari names Descartes as the origin of the animals-as-automata view ("only humans feel and crave, whereas all other animals are mindless automata, akin to a robot or a vending machine"), the [[The Mind-Body Problem|mind-body split]] O'Gieblyn likewise treats as the founding wound of the disenchanted self. The net: where the computational dogma is strongest, both authors locate consciousness as the thing it cannot explain — and both note that intelligence is "decoupling from consciousness" without either's account of how subjective experience arises.

The asymmetry is the whole point. Harari is the *source*; O'Gieblyn is the *reader* who uses him to corroborate a reading he does not himself frame theologically. Harari treats [[Dataism]] as a "religion" sociologically and somewhat mischievously ("Dataism too began as a neutral scientific theory, but is now mutating into a religion"); O'Gieblyn takes that same observation and presses it into a sustained genealogy of [[Disenchantment]] and re-enchantment, reading the opaque algorithm as the returned God of a secular age. Where they fully converge is the refusal to take "organisms are algorithms" as settled fact; where the registers diverge is that Harari's is a historian's provocation and O'Gieblyn's is a theological-genealogical argument.

## Source Passages

Two verbatim O'Gieblyn citations of *Homo Deus* carry the `direct_citation` classification: the Ch11 quotation of Harari's God/master-algorithm analogy ("makes virtually the same analogy"), and the Ch12 quotation of "Listen to the algorithms! They know how you feel," framed by O'Gieblyn's gloss that Dataism "would officially mark the end of liberal humanism." Both Harari originals are reproduced below from his own raw chapters (Ch14, "The Data Religion") alongside the O'Gieblyn passages that cite them, so the slight wording drift in O'Gieblyn's rendering is visible and attributable. The `shared_tradition` registers — the steam-engine-then-computer metaphor (Harari Ch3/Ch4) and the hard problem (Harari Ch3/Ch4; the black box and free-will dissolution in Ch8) — are documented from Harari's side; their counterparts in O'Gieblyn are already captured in [[Technology as Metaphor]], [[Mind as Machine]], [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]], and [[The Algorithm as Providence]].
