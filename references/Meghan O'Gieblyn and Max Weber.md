# Meghan O'Gieblyn ↔ Max Weber

## Relationship Type

Historical influence / shared tradition. Weber is not O'Gieblyn's contemporary interlocutor but the supplier of her book's master interpretive frame. She inherits his diagnosis of **disenchantment** as the founding condition of modernity, names him explicitly as its author, and then redeploys two of his more specific theses — the warning against "academic prophecy" and the analysis of Protestant salvation-anxiety in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* — as live critical tools against, respectively, [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s singularitarianism and contemporary surveillance technologies. The relationship runs in one direction (Weber → O'Gieblyn) and is best read as a twentieth-century sociologist's frame being turned, a century later, on the artifacts of the information age.

## Explanation

O'Gieblyn treats Weber as the canonical theorist of secular modernity's central loss. She glosses disenchantment through his phrases — the world that was once "a great enchanted garden," now reduced to "the mechanism of a world robbed of gods" — and, crucially, corrects the popular reading: for Weber the true trauma is not ontological hollowing-out but the discovery that the world "is devoid of intrinsic meaning." This frame structures her recurring problem of [[Embodiment]], soul, and the [[Mind as Machine]]: once the [[The Computational Universe|mechanistic]] picture banishes final causes, "matter" can only be "passive and inert," and the question of mind becomes the question of whether anything irreducible survives reduction.

The book's sharpest use of Weber is polemical. In the [[Pattern and Randomness|Pattern]] chapter she takes Weber's warning against "academic prophecy" — science presenting itself "as a new form of revelation" — and turns it directly on [[Ray Kurzweil]], naming him "the information age['s]…academic prophet" whose Singularity narrative promises "nothing short of total reenchantment." Here Weber supplies the diagnostic vocabulary by which O'Gieblyn reads transhumanist [[Posthumanism|posthumanism]] (and its [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] inheritance, via [[Norbert Wiener]] and the [[The Computational Universe|universal metaphor]] of [[Information|information]]) as disguised eschatology. In the Algorithm chapter she invokes a different Weber text: *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, whose account of salvation-anxiety — the doubt that drives the elect to "go above and beyond what is required" — she argues digital surveillance unwittingly reproduces in the self-censoring subject of predictive analytics.

A note on scope: the disenchantment frame pervades *God, Human, Animal, Machine*, but it frequently appears unnamed or routed through other thinkers (Bruno Latour, Hans Blumenberg, [[René Descartes]] as "the serpent in the garden"). This reference realizes the edge **only where Weber is named**, per the source passages below.

> The word is often attributed to Max Weber, who argued that before the Enlightenment and Western secularization, the world was "a great enchanted garden," a place much like the illuminated world described by Hopkins. In the enchanted world, faith was not opposed to knowledge, nor myth to reason. The realms of spirit and matter were porous and not easily distinguishable from one another. Then came the dawn of modern science, which turned the world into a subject of investigation. Nature was no longer a source of wonder but a force to be mastered, a system to be figured out. At its root, disenchantment describes the fact that everything in modern life, from our minds to the rotation of the planets, can be reduced to the causal mechanism of physical laws. In place of the pneuma, the spirit-force that once infused and unified all living things, we are now left with an empty carapace of gears and levers—or, as Weber put it, "the mechanism of a world robbed of gods."

Relevant passage:

> Weber was not arguing that the sciences should return to the business of values and spirituality. On the contrary, he was wary of the fact that so many intellectuals of his day were attracted to "antique religious ideas" that were cleverly dressed up in new, materialist guises. The worst thing that science could do was to take up the mantle of reenchantment, presenting itself as a new form of revelation, or what he called "academic prophecy." In the lecture rooms and the laboratory, the only value that should hold is intellectual integrity. In fact, when it came to the modern hunger for meaning, Weber found a retreat to traditional religions less objectionable than the impulse to find telos or purpose in empiricism. "In my eyes, such religious return stands higher than the academic prophecy," he wrote. In other words, if you wanted some kind of spiritual experience, you should just go to church.

Supports: Establishes the Weberian "academic prophecy" warning that O'Gieblyn turns, in the next section, on Kurzweil ("If the information age can be said to have its own 'academic prophet,' Kurzweil is the most probable candidate").
