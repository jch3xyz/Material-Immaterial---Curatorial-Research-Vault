# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Norbert Wiener

## Relationship Type

**Historical influence — and sustained critical reading.** [[Norbert Wiener]] is the single thinker [[N. Katherine Hayles]] engages most closely in *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*; she devotes an entire chapter ("Liberal Subjectivity Imperiled") to him and treats him as the threshold figure of first-wave [[Cybernetics]]. The relationship is not citation-as-endorsement but a symptomatic reading: Hayles reconstructs Wiener's worldview — his probabilistic universe, his construction of [[Information]], his trope of analogy, his entropy cosmology — in order to show how he founded the cybernetic dissolution of the subject's boundaries and then recoiled from it to protect [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]].

## Explanation

For Hayles, Wiener is the man who built "the mirror of the cyborg" and then made "a horrified withdrawal" when he glanced over his shoulder and saw himself reflected in it. She identifies him as the figure in whom the deep contradiction of [[Posthumanism]]'s origin is most visible: he envisioned humans and machines as equivalent control-communication systems joined by [[Feedback Loops]], yet "spoke up strongly for liberal humanist values" and tried to make cybernetics *extend* the autonomous self rather than dissolve it. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

Four moves anchor Hayles's reading of Wiener:

1. **Information as "word for world."** Wiener built [[Information]] on his prior commitment to a probabilistic universe, defining it as the selection of one message from a field of possible messages — taking the statistical cosmology of Willard Gibbs and substituting "word for world." This is the construction by which information becomes "a probability function with no dimensions, no materiality," the engineering decontextualization that Hayles tracks across the book as the condition for [[The Body as Data]] and for the fantasy that information can circulate unchanged across substrates. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

2. **Analogy as the trope that erases embodiment.** Hayles reads *analogy* as Wiener's most important rhetorical figure — "for Wiener, analogy was communication, and communication was analogy" — and argues that it is precisely analogy that constructs the cyborg by moving "easily and quickly to an abstract level" of pattern while erasing "the very real differences in embodied materiality." Wiener's own estrangement from the flesh (his clumsiness in the lab, his abandonment of biology) is, for Hayles, "not entirely irrelevant" to this elision. This is the genealogical root of her central thesis on [[Embodiment vs Information]]. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

3. **The entropy / negentropy cosmology.** Wiener allied information with structure and entropy with "randomness, dissipation, and death," casting life as "an island of negentropy amid a sea of disorder" and staging a "cosmological drama" in which good (flexible) cybernetic machines and humans stand "shoulder to shoulder" against noise and the entropic tide. Hayles notes that this moral valuation was a *choice*: Claude Shannon took the opposite sign and identified information *with* entropy. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

4. **The historical failure to contain cybernetics within liberal humanism.** Hayles's verdict is that "Wiener was not successful in containing cybernetics within the circle of liberal humanist assumptions." The link between liberal humanism and self-regulation, "forged in the eighteenth century," was "stretched thin" by the 1960s and "largely broken" by the 1980s. The cyborg propagated through "promiscuous couplings" that no founder could authorize. This is the historical hinge of [[Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman]]. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The relationship is therefore one of **critical inheritance**: Hayles writes within the cybernetic tradition Wiener founded — adopting its vocabulary of [[Feedback Loops]], [[Homeostasis]], [[Pattern and Randomness]], and [[The Observer]] — while turning that tradition against its founder's stated aim, recovering the embodiment his analogies abstracted away. (Label: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Source Passages

On information as a probabilistic construction (chapter "Liberal Subjectivity Imperiled"):

> In a sense, he took Gibbs's idea and substituted word for world. Instead of one world coming into being from among a galaxy of possible worlds, one message comes into being from a cacophony of possible messages.

On Wiener as definer of immaterial information (chapter "Toward Embodied Virtuality"):

> Information is information, not matter or energy. No materialism which does not admit this can survive at the present day.

On the historical aftermath (chapter "Liberal Subjectivity Imperiled"):

> The voices that speak the cyborg do not speak as one, and the stories they tell are very different from the narratives that Wiener struggled to authorize.
