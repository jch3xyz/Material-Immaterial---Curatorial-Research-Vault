# Byung-Chul Han and Alain Badiou

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation and adoption, framed by an explicit, named disagreement.** Alain Badiou is not merely a source Han quotes — he wrote the Foreword to *The Agony of Eros* ("The Reinvention of Love"), so this reference is unusually two-sided for a forward-stub author: Badiou speaks in his own voice about Han, and Han speaks in his own voice about Badiou. Han **adopts** Badiou's signature thesis that "love is a 'Two scene'" as the very matrix of his politics of [[Eros|eros]], and quotes Badiou's *In Praise of Love* and *Ethics* to anchor his account of the [[The Event|event]] and of fidelity. Badiou in turn credits Han with "a strong reading of my own thesis" — and then, from inside the same tradition, files a measured **objection** to Han's demand for total self-abolition. The relationship is therefore adoption-with-dissent between two thinkers who share a conception of love as an encounter with [[The Other|the Other]] but part ways over how much of the self that encounter must consume. *Alain Badiou has no book in the library*; his positions are known here through his own Foreword and through the passages Han quotes and paraphrases.

## Explanation

Three strands hold this relationship together.

**(1) The Two scene as political matrix — Han's adoption of Badiou (Ch. 7, "The Politics of Eros").** Han takes over Badiou's thesis that love is a dual scene and makes it the hinge of his own politics: "Love is a 'Two scene,' a theater meant for a duo, to paraphrase Badiou. Interrupting the isolated perspective of the One, it makes the world arise anew from the vantage point of the Other, or Difference." Against a [[Achievement Society|burnout society]] of "isolated, self-alienated achievement-subjects" in which "a we—now proves impossible," Badiou's Two furnishes the form of a possible collective. Han grants that Badiou "rejects a direct link between politics and love" yet borrows Badiou's own hedge — the "kind of secret resonance" between a life lived under a political idea and the intensity of love, "like two musical instruments that are completely different in tone and volume, but which mysteriously converge when unified by a great musician in the same work." Han also imports Badiou's coupling of love to the [[The Event|event]] and to fidelity: the loving encounter demands that "I must completely rework my ordinary way of 'living' [habiter] my situation," so that eros, like politics and art, commands "fidelity" to the event. This is the [[Negativity|negativity]] of rupture that "interrupts the Same in favor of the Other." (`Directly stated` — Ch. 7)

**(2) Badiou's own thesis, and his crediting of Han (Foreword).** In the Foreword Badiou names the borrowing explicitly and approvingly: "The author offers a strong reading of my own thesis that 'love is a Two scene'—a dual perspective—and, by virtue of this fact, represents a kind of basic political matrix." So the adoption in (1) is authorized from Badiou's side: the "Two scene" is Badiou's own coinage, and he reads Han's use of it as a genuine political extension rather than a distortion. (`Directly stated` — Foreword, in Badiou's voice)

**(3) The disagreement — self-abolition versus a world of the Two (Foreword).** Having praised the book, Badiou "open[s] just one avenue of discussion" that is a real dissent from Han's central demand. Han's minimum condition for love is "possessing sufficient courage to accept self-negation for the sake of discovering the Other" — an "absolute negativity" mustered "to abolish the self, on a sublime and all-but-impossible scale, in order to encounter the Other." Badiou questions exactly this: "Is it absolutely certain that the only way to oppose a consumerist and contractual conception of alterity is to abolish the self, on a sublime and all-but-impossible scale, in order to encounter the Other?" He locates Han's self-abolition in the long mystical lineage of "amorous oblativity—the vanishing of the self in the Other" (Saint John of the Cross) and asks whether, "after the death of God," that road need still be traveled. In its place he proposes a positive alternative: "Another road may stretch ahead, along the lines of a world beginning with the Two of love. This world—whose foundations lie in 'we two,' singular—would belong neither to me nor to the Other but would exist for all." His diagnostic barb is that absolutizing the negative risks a covert extremism: "Isn't there, metaphorically, a kind of ultra-leftism hiding in the unlimited and absolute assumption of the negative and alterity?" Where Han makes love the surrender of the self to the atopic Other, Badiou makes it the construction of a shared, universalizable reality — "two combined instances of forgetting that come to work together—in a way that admits universal validation—for the sake of a shared reality." (`Directly stated` — Foreword, in Badiou's voice)

**(4) Fidelity and eternity-in-time (Ch. 4, "Bare Life").** The disagreement is bounded by a real convergence, visible where Han closes the "[[Bare Life]]" chapter by quoting Badiou's *In Praise of Love*. Against the additive, accumulative economy of capitalist survival, Han reads Senta's *Liebestod* as a decision and conclusion (*Schlussform*) that "brings forth a duration... in time," and glosses it with Badiou: "But love, the essence of which is fidelity..., demonstrates how eternity can exist within the time span of life itself. Happiness, in a word! Yes, happiness is the proof that time can accommodate eternity." For both, love's fidelity opens an eternity inside time and stands opposed to the "economy of survival" — the shared ground on which the quarrel of strand (3) is possible at all. (`Directly stated` — Ch. 4, Han quoting Badiou)

**Attribution note.** All Foreword quotations belong to **Alain Badiou** — several are Badiou quoting Han (e.g. "self-negation for the sake of discovering the Other," "burnout society") or quoting himself ("love is a Two scene"). The Ch. 7 and Ch. 4 quotations are Han's, framing or quoting Badiou; the *In Praise of Love* and *Ethics* lines within them are Badiou's, as Han's own endnotes record (*In Praise of Love*, 38, 48, 75; *Ethics*, 42).

## Source Passages

> The author offers a strong reading of my own thesis that 'love is a Two scene'—a dual perspective—and, by virtue of this fact, represents a kind of basic political matrix.

Han's adoption of the Two scene as the interruption of the One (Ch. 7, Han's voice, paraphrasing Badiou):

> Love is a "Two scene," a theater meant for a duo, to paraphrase Badiou. Interrupting the isolated perspective of the One, it makes the world arise anew from the vantage point of the Other, or Difference.

Han importing Badiou's "secret resonance" between politics and love (Ch. 7, Han quoting Badiou's *In Praise of Love*):

> Even though Alain Badiou rejects a direct link between politics and love, he presumes a "kind of secret resonance" that arises between life lived wholly under the sign of a political idea and the intensity of love. They are "like two musical instruments that are completely different in tone and volume, but which mysteriously converge when unified by a great musician in the same work."

Badiou's fidelity/eternity-in-time thesis, quoted by Han (Ch. 4, Han quoting Badiou's *In Praise of Love*):

> But love, the essence of which is fidelity …, demonstrates how eternity can exist within the time span of life itself. Happiness, in a word! Yes, happiness is the proof that time can accommodate eternity.

## Confidence

**High** for the adoption, the crediting, and the disagreement alike. Badiou is named repeatedly and quoted verbatim by Han in Ch. 7, and Han quotes Badiou's *In Praise of Love* directly in Ch. 4 (endnote 27); the borrowing of the "Two scene," the "secret resonance," and the fidelity/eternity thesis are `Directly stated`. The disagreement is exceptionally well-grounded because Badiou states it in his own voice in the Foreword he wrote for the book — his questioning of self-abolition, his "world... [that] would exist for all," and his "ultra-leftism hiding in the... absolute assumption of the negative" are `Directly stated`, not inferred. **Medium** only for any claim about Badiou's fuller position beyond these three works: because *Alain Badiou has no book in the library*, his philosophy of the event, of fidelity, and of the amorous Two is known here through his Foreword and through Han's citations of *In Praise of Love* and *Ethics*, not through a primary ingest — so this note records the Han–Badiou exchange as it appears in *The Agony of Eros*, awaiting a future [[Eros]] / [[The Other]] extension or a Badiou author-section to adjudicate whether Han's self-abolition truly parts ways with Badiou's Two, or merely names its negative face. **Forward-stub preserved per policy:** Alain Badiou (author, no book in `raw/`) is referenced as plain text, not wiki-linked, and no `authors/Alain Badiou.md` file is created.
