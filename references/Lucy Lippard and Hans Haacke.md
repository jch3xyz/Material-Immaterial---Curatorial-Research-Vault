# Lucy Lippard ↔ Hans Haacke

## Relationship Type

Direct citation and curatorial-critical advocacy. [[Hans Haacke]] is not a peer theorist Lippard reads but an artist whose statements she chronicles and reprints across *[[Six Years]]*, and whose 1971 censorship at the Guggenheim she documents at length as a flashpoint. He is the book's clearest junction between **systems aesthetics** and Lippard's **art-and-politics turn**: his work supplies the systems thread that runs through her account of [[Dematerialization]], and his Guggenheim affair dramatizes the institution's political stake in supposedly immaterial art. (Note: the critic Jack Burnham, whose *Beyond Modern Sculpture* sits in `raw/` un-ingested, is the contemporary theorist of "systems aesthetics" standing behind this material; he is a forward-stub here.)

## Explanation

Haacke gives Lippard two things at once. First, a vocabulary of **systems**: his "'sculpture' that physically reacts to its environment is no longer to be regarded as an object" but "a 'system' of interdependent processes," and his flat insistence that "A system is not imagined, it is real," let Lippard frame conceptual practice as a turn from the discrete art object toward [[Information]], process, and [[Systems Theory]] — the substrate of her [[Material vs Immaterial]] thesis. Second, a demonstration that this dematerialized, systems-based art is not politically inert. When Haacke's "real time social system" works — the Shapolsky real-estate documentation — were to enter the Guggenheim in 1971, director Thomas Messer cancelled the show, curator Edward Fry was fired for siding with the artist, and the Art Workers' Coalition demonstrated inside the museum. Haacke's own gloss — "Real-time systems are double agents. They might run under the heading 'art,' but this culturization does not prevent them from operating as normal" — names exactly the doubleness Lippard is tracking: work that is "art" and an operating social system simultaneously. In the 1973 Postface, where Lippard concludes that conceptual art mostly failed to break "the real barriers between the art context and those external disciplines," Haacke is the first of the few named exceptions whose work genuinely engages them.
