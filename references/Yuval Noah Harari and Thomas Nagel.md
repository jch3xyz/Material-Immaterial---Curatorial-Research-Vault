# Yuval Noah Harari ↔ Thomas Nagel

## Relationship Type

Conceptual parallel — direct citation. Harari explicitly invokes Nagel's 1974 argument ("What Is It Like to Be a Bat?") to establish the philosophical boundary his data-religion chapter runs against: algorithms can map every measurable parameter of a mind from outside, but they cannot capture what it is *like* to be that mind from the inside. Nagel's question names the limit; Harari's [[The Ocean of Consciousness]] is the figure for everything that lies beyond it.

## Explanation

In *Homo Deus* Chapter 10, Harari argues that humans occupy a "tiny island of consciousness within a giant ocean of alien mental states." To anchor that claim philosophically, he cites Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" directly and by name, identifying it as "one of the most important articles about the philosophy of mind." Nagel's point — that no amount of third-person, algorithmic description of bat echolocation can yield the first-person felt quality of the experience — becomes the conceptual wall against which Harari's broader argument about [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]] presses. If intelligence is decoupling from consciousness, and if Nagel is right that the subjective interior is in principle inaccessible from the outside, then the data-religion Harari describes in the next chapter acquires a structural blind spot: it can process every signal from the ocean but cannot feel the water. [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] is the philosophical name for that gap; [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] is the tension Harari locates at the center of the techno-humanist project.

## Source Passages

All quotations below are verbatim from the raw file.

> "One of the most important articles about the philosophy of mind is titled 'What Is It Like to Be a Bat?' In this 1974 article, the philosopher Thomas Nagel points out that a Sapiens mind cannot fathom the subjective world of a bat. We can write all the algorithms we want about the bat body, about bat echolocation systems and about bat neurons, but it won't tell us how it feels to be a bat. How does it feel to echolocate a moth flapping its wings? Is it similar to seeing it, or is it something completely different?"

`Directly stated` — Harari names Nagel, names the article title, gives its date, and paraphrases its core argument.

> "We are akin to the inhabitants of a small isolated island who have just invented the first boat, and are about to set sail without a map or even a destination. Indeed, we are in a somewhat worse condition. The inhabitants of our imaginary island at least know that they occupy just a small space within a large and mysterious sea. We fail to appreciate that we are living on a tiny island of consciousness within a giant ocean of alien mental states."

`Strongly implied` — this is the passage that gives the chapter (and the concept note [[The Ocean of Consciousness]]) its name; it extends Nagel's argument from the bat case to a general epistemology of mind.

> "Just as Sapiens cannot understand what it's like to be a bat, we have similar difficulties understanding how it feels to be a whale, a tiger or a pelican. It certainly feels like something; but we don't know like what."

`Directly stated` — Harari applies Nagel's "what it is like" formulation explicitly and extends it across species.

> "Since intelligence is decoupling from consciousness, and since non-conscious intelligence is developing at breakneck speed, humans must actively upgrade their minds if they want to stay in the game."

`Strongly implied` — this sentence from the chapter's opening frames the Nagel problem within Harari's techno-humanist thesis: a decoupled, non-conscious intelligence cannot answer the Nagelian question, and that absence is the stakes.
