# Shoshana Zuboff ↔ Max Weber

## Relationship Type

Direct citation. Unlike most of the vault's Zuboff edges, this is **not** an interpretive synthesis: Zuboff names Max Weber explicitly, quotes him verbatim, and deploys his concepts as load-bearing premises of her argument. Weber is `author_b` here as a forward-stub figure (no book of his in `raw/`) whom Zuboff cites across chapters 10, 11, and 12. The relationship runs one way (Weber → Zuboff) and is one of historical influence realized through direct quotation: Weber supplies two of the analytic hinges of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* — the thesis that economic action is determined by **"economic orientation"** rather than by technology, and the warning that **contractual freedom can be turned into "the achievement of power over others."**

## Explanation

Weber's first and most consequential function in Zuboff's argument is to **decouple power from devices**. Closing the chapter on [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|behavior modification]] ([[Economies of Action]]), she states the thesis flatly: "In the end, it's not the devices; it's Max Weber's 'economic orientation,' now determined by surveillance capitalism." The point is that the same fitness tracker, car, or refrigerator could be turned to the owner's ends; what makes it an instrument of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] is not its engineering but the economic logic that orients it. This is Zuboff's standing answer to technological determinism — her argument that [[Surveillance capitalism is a logic not a technology]], an imperative that captures the device rather than a property of the device itself, and she borrows Weber's vocabulary to name the orienting force. `Directly stated`.

Weber's second function is to ground Zuboff's critique of **contract** in [[The Right to the Future Tense|the right to the future tense]]. Surveillance capitalism, she argues, substitutes "uncontract" — guaranteed outcomes engineered below the threshold of will — for the mutual promising on which contract depends; but she is careful to note that contract itself has always been corruptible, "twisted and abused in every age, from the Requirimiento to the 'slave contract.'" Here she enlists Weber as a witness: "Max Weber warned that the great achievements of contractual freedom create opportunities to exploit property ownership as a means to 'the achievement of power over others.'" Weber's warning supplies the historical depth to her claim that the freedom of contract can be inverted into domination — the same inversion she sees consummated when surveillance capital replaces promises with [[A Utopia of Certainty|certainty]]. `Directly stated`.

Weber appears a third time, more glancingly, as one of the **"early-century interpreters of industrial society"** who could not foresee what was coming. Setting up the unprecedentedness of [[Instrumentarian Power]] in chapter 12, "Two Species of Power," Zuboff pairs Weber with Durkheim: "Not even the most farsighted of the early-century interpreters of industrial society, thinkers such as Durkheim and Weber, had anticipated this murderous turn." Here Weber functions not as a tool but as a marker of the limits of classical sociology — the canonical analyst whose foresight the twentieth century's totalitarian rupture, and now the twenty-first's instrumentarian one, exceeded. (The same sentence grounds the companion edge [[Shoshana Zuboff and Émile Durkheim]].) `Directly stated`.

Across the three citations Weber is doing characteristically Zuboffian work: he licenses her economic — rather than technological — explanation of power, he historicizes the corruption of contractual freedom, and he names the horizon of classical social theory that surveillance capitalism overruns. The first two are active analytic borrowings; the third is a gesture of intellectual lineage.
