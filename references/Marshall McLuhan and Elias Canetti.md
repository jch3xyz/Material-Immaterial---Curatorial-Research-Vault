# Marshall McLuhan ↔ Elias Canetti

## Relationship Type

Direct citation. Across two adjacent Part II case studies — Chapter 11 ("Number: Profile of the Crowd") and Chapter 14 ("Money: The Poor Man's Credit Card") — McLuhan names Elias Canetti and draws on *Crowds and Power* twice: once for the figure of the trader as an arboreal ape (financial grasping as the climbing-and-swinging movement pattern of the greater apes), and once for the crowd dynamic of "rapid and unlimited growth," which McLuhan transfers from crowds to "large concentrations of wealth or treasure." The citation is explicit in McLuhan's text. Canetti's own argument is reconstructed here as McLuhan reports it (Canetti's *Crowds and Power* is not in `raw/`; he remains a forward-stub author), so the *reading* of Canetti is interpretive synthesis set beside citations McLuhan actually made.

## Explanation

McLuhan recruits Canetti as a witness for his governing thesis that every medium is a bodily extension — the master frame of [[Media as Extensions of Man]]. In the **Money** chapter, McLuhan reads currency itself as an extension of the hand's reach: "Trading by currency is based on the principle of grasping and letting go in an oscillating cycle," the two hands working "somewhat in the manner of a trapeze artist exchanging one bar for another." He then cites Canetti directly: "In fact, Elias Canetti in Crowds and Power argues that the trader is Involved in one of the most ancient of all pastimes, namely that of climbing trees and swinging from limb to limb." The "primitive grasping, calculating, and timing of the greater arboreal apes" Canetti "sees as a translation into financial terms of one of the oldest movement patterns." For McLuhan this is perfect corroboration: the financier's "enthralling abstract activities" are literally "extensions of the avid climbing and mobility of the greater apes" — finance as the tactile, grasping hand externalized. (`Directly stated` that McLuhan cites Canetti for the arboreal-ape figure; the gloss on what this corroborates is `Interpretive synthesis`.)

The second citation closes the chapter and hinges Money to the adjacent **Number** chapter. McLuhan observes that "the money hoard or collection has much in common with the crowd," and that "the psychological patterns of the crowd and those associated with accumulations of wealth are very close." He then attributes the key dynamic to Canetti: "Elias Canetti stresses that the dynamic which is basic to crowds is the urge to rapid and unlimited growth. The same power dynamic is characteristic of large concentrations of wealth or treasure." McLuhan extends the point — the modern "unit of treasure" is the million, "always associated" with "the idea that it can be reached by a rapid speculative scramble" — and notes that for Canetti "the ambition to see numbers mounting up was typical of Hitler's speeches." Growth has a shadow: crowds and "piles of money" also "breed uneasiness about the possibility of disintegration and deflation," a "two-way movement of expansion and deflation." McLuhan reports that "Canetti spends a good deal of analysis on the psychic effects of the German inflation after the First World War," where "the depreciation of the citizen went along with that of the German mark" — "the personal and monetary units became confused." (`Directly stated` for all attributions; the inflation reading is McLuhan's report of Canetti, not Canetti quoted directly.)

This same Canetti material surfaces in the **Number** chapter, where it does its heaviest conceptual work for McLuhan and is captured in the concept note [[Number as the Extension of Touch]]. There McLuhan argues that "the mysterious need of crowds to grow and to reach out, equally characteristic of large accumulations of wealth, can be understood if money and numbers are, indeed, technologies that extend the power of touch and the grasp of the hand." Number is "an extension and separation of our most intimate and interrelating activity, our sense of touch," and the growth-drive of crowds and capital is that grasping, incorporating hand made into a medium. The Canetti citation is thus the connective tissue between McLuhan's two chapters on quantity and value: the crowd's urge to grow, the hoard's urge to grow, and the trader's grasp are one tactile movement pattern, extended through [[Number as the Extension of Touch]] and the money-medium alike. This is also why the cross-chapter Canetti thread is consolidated **here** rather than duplicated across the Ch11 and Ch14 concept notes.

The encounter is asymmetric and one-directional: McLuhan reads and enlists Canetti, but the relationship is McLuhan citing a prior source, not a dialogue. Crucially, McLuhan **repurposes** Canetti. Canetti's *Crowds and Power* (1960) is a phenomenology of the crowd and the wielding of power; McLuhan annexes its crowd dynamics and its arboreal-ape image to a media thesis Canetti did not hold — that money and number are *extensions of the body's senses*, specifically of touch. The crowd's growth-urge, for Canetti a feature of crowds as such, becomes for McLuhan evidence that quantity (people, digits, units of money) shares "the same factual magic for seizing and incorporating," because it externalizes the haptic, grasping hand. That re-framing — turning Canetti's crowd psychology into a corollary of [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — is the interpretive move this note records. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
