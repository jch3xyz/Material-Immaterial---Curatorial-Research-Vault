# Donna Haraway ↔ Michel Foucault

## Relationship Type

**Disagreement** by way of **periodization and supersession**. Haraway names Foucault directly — twice — and in both moments she demotes him. *A Cyborg Manifesto* does not extend Foucauldian [[Biopolitics]]; it declares it obsolete, a "flaccid premonition" of a politics that operates on a different ontological terrain. The cyborg, Haraway says, "is not subject to Foucault's biopolitics." This is the same structural move Han makes in *Psychopolitics* (see [[#Cross-book note|the cross-book note below]]), but Haraway's ground for the supersession is informatic rather than psychic: where biopower governs the integrity of natural bodies, the **informatics of domination** governs coding, simulation, and "rates of flow across boundaries."

## Explanation

Foucault is, for Haraway, a thinker of the *organic* regime — of bodies, populations, hygiene, and the disciplining of natural objects. Her central chart of "transitions from the comfortable old hierarchical dominations to the scary new networks" maps exactly this passage out of his domain: from "biology as clinical practice → biology as inscription," "physiology → communications engineering," "reproduction → replication," "eugenics → population control." Biopolitics belonged to the left-hand column — the "Organics of Domination." The cyborg lives in the right-hand column, the "Informatics of Domination," where "the objects … cannot be coded as 'natural'" and where one must think "not in terms of essential properties, but in terms of design, boundary constraints, rates of flows, systems logics." `Directly stated` (the chart and the informatic/organic split are explicit); `Interpretive synthesis` (locating biopolitics in the organic column).

The decisive sentence comes in *The Informatics of Domination*: "One should expect control strategies to concentrate on boundary conditions and interfaces, on rates of flow across boundaries—and not on the integrity of natural objects." This is the precise pivot away from biopower. Foucault's biopolitics regulates *the body* — its life, fertility, health, normality. Haraway's informatics is indifferent to the body's integrity; it treats "human beings, like any other component or subsystem" as something to be "localized in a system architecture whose basic modes of operation are probabilistic, statistical." Hence: "The cyborg is not subject to Foucault's biopolitics; the cyborg simulates politics, a much more potent field of operations." The cyborg outruns biopower not by escaping power but by inhabiting a regime — **simulation** — for which the natural body is no longer the object of government. `Directly stated`.

The earlier, blunter demotion in *An Ironic Dream of a Common Language* sets the tone: "Michel Foucault's biopolitics is a flaccid premonition of cyborg politics, a very open field." "Premonition" concedes that Foucault saw something coming; "flaccid" insists he could not grasp its potency. The disagreement is therefore not a rejection of Foucault's project but a claim to have periodized past it — biopolitics is a *forerunner* whose object (the natural body) has been dissolved into coding by the postwar sciences of communication and biotechnology. `Directly stated`.

## Cross-book note

There is a strong parallel here to Han's verdict that "Foucault failed to make the turn to psychopolitics" (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault]] and the argument [[Foucault failed to make the turn to psychopolitics]]). Both Haraway and Han argue that Foucauldian biopolitics, anchored to the body and the population, is outrun by a successor regime — for Han the *psyche* (Big Data, [[Psychopolitics]]); for Haraway *information* (coding, simulation, the informatics of domination). The convergence is striking and vault-worthy, but the two diagnoses differ in object and idiom, and merging the notes would flatten that difference. **Flagged as a separate review item** (a future Haraway↔Han reference, or an essay on "the obsolescence of biopolitics"), not merged here. `Speculative connection`.
