# Shoshana Zuboff ↔ Byung-Chul Han

## Relationship Type

Conceptual parallel — **vault-internal interpretive synthesis**. Zuboff does **not** cite Han anywhere in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, and Han does not cite Zuboff. They write in different idioms (Zuboff: American social psychology and political economy; Han: Continental philosophy of culture) and the bridge below is a construction of this vault, not a documented authorial dialogue or historical influence. Every cross-reading is labeled `Interpretive synthesis`; the verbatim Zuboff passages that ground it are `Directly stated`. This is the keystone bridge of the surveillance cluster, pairing Zuboff's [[Surveillance Capitalism]] / [[Big Other]] / [[Instrumentarian Power]] with Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] / [[Psychopolitics]] / [[Auto-Exploitation]].

## Explanation

Zuboff and Han offer two halves of one diagnosis: a power that rules not by terror but by our own ostensibly free participation. Both locate its hinge in the **contingent collision of self-determination with neoliberalism**. Zuboff states it almost as a thesis — the success of surveillance capitalism owes "to the destructive collision of two opposing historical forces," the "centuries-long societal shift from the mass to the individual" running into "the neoliberal economic paradigm," whose aim is "to reverse, subdue, impede, and even destroy the individual urge toward psychological self-determination and moral agency." Han's [[Psychopolitics]] makes the same collision its founding move: neoliberalism does not repress freedom but *exploits* it, turning the achievement-subject's own drive to self-realize into the engine of [[Auto-Exploitation]] — "[[Freedom itself is producing coercion]]." The shared object is psychopolitical control: domination that operates through, not against, the will to be free.

The convergence is sharpest at the figure of **voluntary subjection**. Zuboff's most quotable lines — "we now pay for our own domination" and surveillance capitalism's normalization "leaves us singing in our chains" — are almost a paraphrase of Han's [[The digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure|argument that the digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure]]: its inhabitants "actively communicate, baring themselves of their own free will." Both insist the new power is *post-Orwellian*. Zuboff: instrumentarian power is "the precise antithesis of Orwell's Big Brother"; "There is no brother here of any kind, big or little." Han: the digital panopticon's keeper is a "[[Friendly Big Brother|friendly Big Brother]]," and [[Smart Power]] "ingratiates itself" rather than commanding. Where Bentham's tower and Orwell's screen presumed coercion and a watcher, both authors describe a frictionless, affectively soft apparatus that secures compliance by feeling like freedom — Zuboff's "certainty without terror," Han's smart power that "doesn't issue commands."

But the bridge also marks a real **divergence in where the engine sits**. For Han, the subject is the *agent* of its own subjection: it auto-exploits in apparent freedom, the perpetrator and victim coincide, and there is, in his late formulation, no external exploiter to point to — power has migrated inside the psyche. For Zuboff, the subject is the *raw material* of an unambiguously external extraction operation: "You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass," "the objects of a technologically advanced and increasingly inescapable raw-material-extraction operation." Han's panopticon has, in a sense, dissolved its owner into the network; Zuboff insists on naming "the puppet master, not the puppet" — there *is* a [[Behavioral Surplus|surplus]] being "ripped from your life," there *are* [[Behavioral Futures Markets|behavioral futures markets]] and [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|owners of the means of behavioral modification]] who profit. Han psychologizes and universalizes the domination until it has no outside; Zuboff economizes and localizes it until it has a balance sheet. Read together, Han supplies the phenomenology of the subject who experiences extraction as freedom, and Zuboff supplies the political economy of the firm that monetizes that experience.

This bridge also re-inflects the vault's broader axis. The rendition of "lived experience as behavioral data" is Zuboff's economic-power version of Han's reduction of the world to [[Dataism|data]] and "behavioral data"; the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning in society]] — "Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?" — is the asymmetry of knowledge that Han names with the [[Digital Panopticon|aperspectival panopticon]]. Both treat the dissolution of the embodied subject into informational surplus as the condition of the new power, connecting them on [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Freedom vs Control]].

## Source Passages

### Passage 1 — "we now pay for our own domination"

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the bridge)

> "Whether it's a 'smart' home device, what the insurance companies call 'behavioral underwriting,' or any one of thousands of other transactions, we now pay for our own domination."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/03 Chapter 1 - Home or Exile in the Digital Future]].

This is the line in which Zuboff most exactly converges with Han's [[Auto-Exploitation]] and the [[The digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure|voluntary-self-exposure argument]]: the subject does not merely tolerate but *finances* its own subjection. Han's version is internal (the achievement-subject exploits itself); Zuboff's is transactional (the consumer pays a surveillance capitalist) — the same paradox located on opposite sides of the firm boundary.

### Passage 2 — "singing in our chains"

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the bridge)

> "In this way, surveillance capitalism imposes a fundamentally illegitimate choice that twenty-first-century individuals should not have to make, and its normalization leaves us singing in our chains."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/03 Chapter 1 - Home or Exile in the Digital Future]].

The figure of *willing* captivity — chains accepted, even sung in — is Zuboff's image for the same condition Han calls voluntary self-exposure in the [[Digital Panopticon]]. The compliance is affectively positive (singing), not coerced; this is precisely Han's claim that the new power is one of [[Positivity]] rather than negativity/prohibition.

### Passage 3 — the neoliberal aim against self-determination

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the bridge)

> "its aim to reverse, subdue, impede, and even destroy the individual urge toward psychological self-determination and moral agency."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/04 Chapter 2 - August 9, 2011 - Setting the Stage for Surveillance Capitalism]] (describing the neoliberal economic paradigm in the chapter's account of the [[The Collision|collision]] of two historical forces).

Zuboff names the *contingent collision* of the drive toward self-determination with neoliberal power — the shared diagnosis at the root of both projects. Han builds [[Psychopolitics]] on the same collision, but reverses the verb: where Zuboff says neoliberalism aims to *destroy* self-determination, Han says it *captures and exploits* it, leaving the urge intact and weaponizing it as [[Auto-Exploitation]]. The divergence in passage 6 follows from this difference.

### Passage 4 — the one-way mirror without consent

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the bridge)

> "an automated architecture that operates as a one-way mirror irrespective of a person's awareness, knowledge, and consent, thus enabling privileged secret access to behavioral data."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/05 Chapter 3 - The Discovery of Behavioral Surplus]].

The one-way mirror is Zuboff's image for the *asymmetry of knowledge* at the heart of [[Behavioral Surplus]] extraction — they see us, we cannot see them. It is the economic-extraction counterpart to Han's [[Digital Panopticon]]: both describe total visibility of the subject paired with the opacity of the watching apparatus. Note the structural divergence already visible here — Zuboff's mirror operates "irrespective of consent" (external, non-consensual capture), whereas Han stresses that the digital panopticon's subjects expose themselves "of their own free will."

### Passage 5 — "smart" as rendition

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the bridge)

> "in this context, 'smart' is a euphemism for rendition: intelligence that is designed to render some tiny corner of lived experience as behavioral data."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/10 Chapter 8 - Rendition - From Experience to Data]].

Zuboff's rendition of lived experience into behavioral data is the extraction-side analogue of Han's [[Dataism]] and the reduction of the world to information. Both treat the conversion of embodied, lived reality into immaterial data as the founding operation of the new regime — the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis read as a mechanism of power.

### Passage 6 — the abandoned carcass: subject as raw material (the divergence)

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the bridge)

> "Forget the cliché that if it's free, 'You are the product.' You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass. The 'product' derives from the surplus that is ripped from your life."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/15 Chapter 13 - Big Other and the Rise of Instrumentarian Power]].

This passage marks the **key divergence** from Han. For Zuboff the subject is the *external raw material* of an extraction operation run by an identifiable owner — there is a [[Behavioral Surplus|surplus]], a market, a "puppet master." For Han the achievement-subject is the *agent* of its own [[Auto-Exploitation]]: perpetrator and victim coincide, and the exploiter has dissolved into the subject's own freedom. Han internalizes and universalizes the domination; Zuboff externalizes and monetizes it. The two accounts are complementary rather than identical — Han's phenomenology of self-exploitation, Zuboff's political economy of extraction.

### Passage 7 — no brother here: the post-Orwellian power

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the bridge)

> "There is no brother here of any kind, big or little, evil or good; there are no family ties, however grim."

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/15 Chapter 13 - Big Other and the Rise of Instrumentarian Power]].

Zuboff's [[Big Other]] is defined *against* Orwell's Big Brother (see also her "precise antithesis of Orwell's Big Brother" in [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/14 Chapter 12 - Two Species of Power]]). Han performs the same inversion from the affective side with the [[Friendly Big Brother|friendly Big Brother]] of [[Smart Power]] — a power that "ingratiates itself" and "doesn't issue commands." Both reject the totalitarian-surveillance frame: the new power is not a watching tyrant but a frictionless, ownerless-feeling apparatus. (Zuboff insists there is still an owner; Han is more willing to let the keeper vanish.)

### Passage 8 — frictionless confluence: certainty without terror

`Directly stated` (Zuboff) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the bridge)

> "less a society than a population that ebbs and flows in perfect frictionless confluence, shaped by the means of behavioral modification that elude our awareness"

Source: [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/16 Chapter 14 - A Utopia of Certainty]].

Zuboff's [[A Utopia of Certainty|utopia of certainty]] — "guaranteed outcomes," compliance secured below the threshold of awareness by [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — is the political-economic counterpart to Han's [[Smoothness|smooth]], frictionless [[Positivity|positive society]]. Both describe a power that achieves order not through prohibition and pain but through smooth, pre-emptive shaping: Zuboff's "certainty without terror," Han's society that has shed all [[Negativity]]. This is where instrumentarian power and psychopolitics name the same affective texture of control.

## Confidence

**Medium.** The parallel is unusually tight at the level of theses and even phrasing: "we now pay for our own domination" / "singing in our chains" against Han's voluntary self-exposure and auto-exploitation; both authors' explicit anti-Orwell move; both grounding the new power in the neoliberal collision with self-determination; both naming a frictionless, terror-free mode of control. The divergence (subject-as-agent in Han vs subject-as-raw-material in Zuboff) is equally documentable and is what keeps the two distinct rather than redundant. The medium label reflects provenance, not doubt about the comparison: Zuboff and Han never cite each other, use different vocabularies, and pursue different stakes (an economic-order critique vs a philosophy of subjectivity). This is vault synthesis.
