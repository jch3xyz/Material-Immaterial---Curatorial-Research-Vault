# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Karl Marx

## Relationship Type

Direct citation and disagreement. Throughout *Psychopolitics*, Han cites Marx by name and by source (the *Grundrisse*, *Capital*, *The German Ideology*) and thinks **with Marx against Marx** — adopting Marx's analysis of capital and freedom while rejecting Marx's prognosis of revolution and the dialectical resolution of capitalism's contradictions.

## Explanation

Han keeps Marx's diagnostic apparatus — the critique of "free competition," the analysis of forces vs. relations of production, the account of free time and the "general intellect" — but breaks decisively with Marx's eschatology. Where Marx expected the dialectic of production to issue in communism and a "realm of freedom," Han argues the contradiction admits "no dialectical Aufhebung," so capitalism merely mutates (into neoliberalism, immaterial production, auto-exploitation) rather than being overcome. The result is a critique that explicitly proposes to "think with Marx beyond Marx": Marx's faith in labour and in the liberatory dialectic is itself, for Han, what keeps freedom captured by Capital. This note is distinct from, and cross-links, [[Byung-Chul Han and Antonio Negri]], where Han extends the same disagreement to Negri's post-Marxist "Multitude."

## Source Passages

### 1. Individual freedom as a ruse of Capital — free competition sets Capital, not the individual, free

Han endorses Marx's relational definition of freedom, then cites Marx's *Grundrisse* to argue that "individual freedom" is a trick by which Capital reproduces itself. This grounds Han's central thesis that neoliberalism is an "intelligent system for exploiting freedom" (see [[Exploitation of Freedom]], [[Auto-Exploitation]]).

> For Marx, individual freedom represents a ruse - a trick of capital. ‘Free competition’, which is based on the idea of individual freedom, simply amounts to the ‘relation of capital to itself as another capital, i.e., the real conduct of capital as capital’.

> The freedom of Capital achieves self-realization by way of individual freedom. In the process, individuals degrade into the genital organs of Capital.

Marx's own formulation, as Han quotes it:

> ‘It is not the individuals who are set free by free competition; it is, rather, capital which is set free.’

Label: `Directly stated` — Han attributes the position to Marx and cites the *Grundrisse* (note 2–3).

### 2. The contradiction admits no dialectical Aufhebung — capitalism mutates rather than becoming communism

Against Marx's expectation that the proletariat's struggle resolves the contradiction between forces and relations of production into a communist order, Han argues the contradiction is permanent, so capitalism escapes into the future by mutating. This is the structural core of the disagreement.

> But counter to what Marx assumed, communist revolution cannot resolve the contradiction between forces of production and relations of production. The contradiction admits no dialectical Aufhebung. Capitalism can always escape into the future precisely because it harbours permanent and inherent contradiction. Accordingly, industrial capitalism has now mutated into neoliberalism and financial capitalism, which are implementing a post-industrial, immaterial mode of production - instead of turning into communism.

Han adds that the neoliberal regime of [[Auto-Exploitation]] dissolves the very class antagonism Marx's revolution presupposed:

> Such classless self-exploitation - which was something utterly unknown to Marx - renders impossible any social revolution based on the difference between the exploiters, on the one hand, and the exploited, on the other.

Label: `Directly stated` — explicit, attributed disagreement with Marx's prognosis.

### 3. Thinking with Marx beyond Marx — free time, the general intellect, and the colonization of the realm of freedom

In "Gamification," Han argues that Marx, despite imagining a "realm of freedom," ultimately "adhered to the primacy of labour," so that on Marx's own account free time is fed back into the production of capital. Han therefore proposes to move past Marx.

> Yet despite highly vigorous forces of production, we have not witnessed the advent of the 'realm of freedom' (Reich der Freiheit) where 'labour determined by necessity and external expediency ends'. Ultimately, Marx himself adhered to the primacy of labour: the 'increase of free time', he contends, 'reacts back upon the power of labour as itself the greatest productive power'. But with that, the realm of necessity comes to colonize the realm of freedom.

On the general intellect and human capital:

> Even though Marx speaks of 'capital fixe being man himself', human beings, endowed with 'general intellect', are now transforming themselves into Capital.

The explicit programmatic statement of the relationship:

> Contrary to Marx's assumptions, the dialectic between forces of production and relations of production is not leading to freedom. More than anything else, it is entangling us in exploitation along new lines. We would have to think with Marx beyond Marx in order to make freedom - indeed, time that is free - our own.

Label: `Directly stated` — Han names Marx, cites *Capital* Vol. 3 and the *Grundrisse* (notes 2–4), and explicitly frames his move as "with Marx beyond Marx."
