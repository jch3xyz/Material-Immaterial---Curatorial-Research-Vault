# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Gilles Deleuze

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation** combined with **adoption and redirection**. This is Deleuze's first *named*, directly cited appearance in the vault. In *The Transparency Society* the [[Society of Control]] was used without attribution; in *Psychopolitics* Han names Deleuze, quotes his ‘Postscript on the Societies of Control’ three times in Ch. 3, and in Ch. 13 takes over Deleuze's late metaphysics — Pure Immanence / "A LIFE" and the "politics of silence" — as the positive ground of resistance to neoliberal psychopolitics. Two load-bearing moments: (1) the mole/snake periodization of body→soul and control-as-intensification; (2) the elevation of immanence and silence into a metaphysics and ethics of the [[idiot|idiot]].

## Explanation

Han uses Deleuze in two opposite registers. In Ch. 3 ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/03 The Mole and the Snake|The Mole and the Snake]]) Deleuze is the **diagnostic** source. Han borrows the whole architecture of the ‘Postscript on the Societies of Control’: the crisis of the milieus of confinement, the figural pair of the burrowing mole (the animal of [[Disciplinary Society]]) and the gliding snake (the animal of the [[Society of Control]]), and Deleuze's distinction between the disciplinary regime that "organizes itself as a ‘body’" and the control regime that "seems like a ‘soul’." Han's signature reading is that the passage from mole to snake — "from subject to project" — is "not setting out for an entirely new way of life" but "a mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same." Deleuze supplies the periodization; Han adds the neoliberal twist that control operates as [[Auto-Exploitation|self-exploitation]], guilt and debt — i.e. as *psychopolitics*, the regime of the soul rather than the body.

In Ch. 13 ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/13 Idiotism|Idiotism]]) Deleuze is the **resistant** source. Here Han enlists late Deleuze against the very control society Deleuze diagnosed. From *Pure Immanence: Essays on a Life* he takes the formula "We will say of pure immanence that it is A LIFE, and nothing else," reading immanence as a plane on which "no order of domination can be erected" — Capital being precisely the transcendence that "estranges life from itself." From the *Negotiations* interview "Mediators" he takes a "politics of silence": "Repressive forces don't stop people from expressing themselves but rather force them to express themselves." Han fuses these into his figure of the [[idiot|idiot]] — un-networked, un-informed, idiosyncratic — whose refusal to communicate is a "practice of freedom" against "total communication and total surveillance." The idiot's idiosyncrasy is an immunological defence of [[The Other|otherness]] against the frictionless circulation of the Same — Han's own [[Hell of the Same]] — making Deleuze the philosophical underwriter of [[Negativity]] as resistance.

A third, indirect link runs through Ch. 8 ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/08 Friendly Big Brother|Friendly Big Brother]]): the [[Digital Panopticon]] of "voluntary self-exposure" in which "communication and control have become one" and "everyone is his or her own panopticon" is Han's elaboration of the Deleuzian control society — coercion replaced by the friendly compulsion to express, consume, and disclose. Deleuze is not named in Ch. 8, but it is the chapter where the Deleuzian thesis (control through freedom, not confinement) is dramatized most fully.

The load-bearing moments are: (1) Ch. 3's naming and triple citation of the ‘Postscript on the Societies of Control’ — the mole/snake, body/soul, and control-as-intensification; (2) Ch. 13's adoption of "A LIFE" / pure immanence as the metaphysics of resistance and Deleuze's "politics of silence" as its ethics; (3) Ch. 8's unnamed dramatization of the control society as the [[Digital Panopticon]] of voluntary self-exposure.

## Byung-Chul Han (The Agony of Eros)

**Relationship: affirmative-adoption.** Han's fourth book in the vault cites Deleuze a fourth time — now not the Deleuze of the control society but the Deleuze (with Félix Guattari) of *What Is Philosophy?*, deployed as a positive resource for a philosophy of thinking. The final chapter, ‘The End of Theory’ ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/08 The End of Theory|The End of Theory]]), extends Han's [[Eros|eros]] thesis from love and politics into thought itself: "Thinking, in the strong sense, begins with eros. To be able to think, one must first have been a friend, a lover." Against the Dataist fantasy that "the numbers speak for themselves," Han argues that genuine thinking requires the negativity and resistance of [[The Other|the atopic Other]] — and he recruits Deleuze and Guattari as the modern witnesses to this ancient claim, following the same Platonic lineage (Eros as *philosophos*, the "friend of wisdom") that runs through the chapter.

Here Deleuze and Guattari supply the crucial formula that the friend/lover is not "an empirical circumstance" but "a presence that is intrinsic to thought, a condition of possibility of thought itself." Han glosses their position directly: "In *What Is Philosophy?* Deleuze and Guattari extol eros as the transcendental condition for the possibility of thinking." The mode is pure affirmation — where *Psychopolitics* both borrowed and *redirected* Deleuze (accepting the mole→snake periodization while insisting control is capitalism's intensification, not its supersession), here Han adopts Deleuze and Guattari *without qualification*: their claim that "the friend reintroduce[s] into thought a vital relationship with the Other that was supposed to have been excluded from pure thought" is precisely Han's own thesis that data-driven calculation, lacking the Other, "always reproduces the Same." Deleuze thus appears across the two books in three registers — diagnostic (the control society), resistant (pure immanence, the politics of silence), and now affirmative-adoptive (eros as the condition of thought). (Guattari, co-author of the cited work, has no vault note; he is credited here as its co-author.) `Directly stated`.
