# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Donna Haraway

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation and historical influence.** In *How We Became Posthuman*, [[N. Katherine Hayles]] names [[Donna Haraway]] at two load-bearing moments and adopts two of her terms outright. First, Hayles takes her sense of [[Informatics]] explicitly "[f]ollowing Donna Haraway." Second, she derives her whole account of [[The Cyborg]] — the figure that is at once "technological object and discursive formation," at once "entities and metaphors" — from Haraway's *[[A Cyborg Manifesto]]* (which Hayles cites under its earlier title "A Manifesto for Cyborgs"). Haraway supplies two of the book's three governing terms; Hayles re-embeds them in her own historical narrative of cybernetics, embodiment, and signification.

This is now a **two-sided** account. With Haraway's *[[A Cyborg Manifesto]]* ingested, the terms Hayles borrows can be traced back to their primary source: the "informatics of domination" Hayles adapts is Haraway's own coinage ("the scary new networks I have called the informatics of domination"); the cyborg that Hayles glosses as "simultaneously entities and metaphors" is Haraway's "creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction"; and Hayles's "three boundary violations" are Haraway's own "three crucial boundary breakdowns." The relationship remains **historical influence**, but is now documented from both ends.

## Explanation

Hayles's debt to Haraway is not a passing citation but a transfer of vocabulary that underwrites the book's architecture. Two terms migrate from Haraway into Hayles:

1. **Informatics.** Hayles coins her use of the word by direct attribution: "Following Donna Haraway, I take informatics to mean the technologies of information as well as the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes that initiate, accompany, and complicate their development." Where Haraway's "informatics of domination" names a regime in which the world is reconstrued as a problem of coding and command-control-communication, Hayles adapts the term to designate the [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] network binding bodies, texts, and technologies. The crucial inheritance is that informatics, in both, is a *relational field* that already contains "the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes" — never bare information-as-pattern. This is exactly the feature that lets Hayles use the term against the disembodiment fantasy she calls [[The Body as Data|the body as data]]. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

2. **The cyborg as simultaneously entity and metaphor.** Hayles's second of three stories — the construction of [[The Cyborg]] — rests on Haraway's claim that "cyborgs are simultaneously entities and metaphors, living beings and narrative constructions." From this doubleness Hayles builds her insistence that "[m]anifesting itself as both technological object and discursive formation," the cyborg "partakes of the power of the imagination as well as of the actuality of technology," and so "[c]yborgs actually exist." She also takes from "A Manifesto for Cyborgs" both the three boundary violations the cyborg performs (human/machine, human/animal, animate/inanimate) and its "pleasurably tight coupling" between parts not supposed to touch. The cyborg's standing as the privileged site where the figural and the material meet — the hinge of Hayles's [[Material vs Immaterial]] argument — is therefore borrowed wholesale from Haraway. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

Underlying both borrowings is a shared methodological commitment: a refusal to let "information" or "the cyborg" float free of material instantiation, and a feminist critique of mastery and "an objectivist account of science." Hayles treats Haraway as an ally in resisting the disembodiment of information even as she rebuilds these terms inside her own seriated history of cybernetics. The influence runs one way — Haraway is a source Hayles cites, not a contemporary she debates — which is why the relationship is best characterized as historical influence carried by direct citation. (Label: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

## ## Haraway's Own Text (A Cyborg Manifesto)

Read from the primary text, the three terms Hayles borrows are all Haraway's own. Each can be anchored verbatim, which converts this from a one-sided record of Hayles's debt into a two-sided account.

**The informatics of domination.** Hayles takes [[Informatics]] "[f]ollowing Donna Haraway"; the phrase she echoes is coined in Haraway's chapter "The Informatics of Domination," where Haraway charts the move "from an organic, industrial society to a polymorphous, information system" and names its endpoint: "Simultaneously material and ideological, the dichotomies may be expressed in the following chart of transitions from the comfortable old hierarchical dominations to the scary new networks I have called the informatics of domination." For Haraway the term names a regime in which "communications sciences and modern biologies are constructed by a common move — the translation of the world into a problem of coding," so that any body or object "can be reasonably thought of in terms of disassembly and reassembly." This is the source behind Hayles's relational sense of informatics; where Haraway diagnoses a network of domination, Hayles redeploys the word for the [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] field binding bodies, texts, and technologies. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

**The cyborg as creature of social reality and of fiction.** Hayles glosses [[The Cyborg]] as "simultaneously entities and metaphors, living beings and narrative constructions." The primary text behind this is Haraway's own opening definition: "A cyborg is a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction." Haraway insists the doubleness is constitutive — "The cyborg is a condensed image of both imagination and material reality, the two joined centers structuring any possibility of historical transformation" — which is exactly the fusion of [[Material vs Immaterial]] that Hayles draws out as the cyborg's "power of the imagination as well as ... the actuality of technology." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

**The three boundary breakdowns.** Hayles's "three boundary violations" are Haraway's own enumeration. After introducing the cyborg, Haraway writes: "now I want to signal three crucial boundary breakdowns that make the following political-fictional (politicalscientific) analysis possible." She then states each in turn — "the boundary between human and animal is thoroughly breached"; "The second leaky distinction is between animal–human (organism) and machine"; "The third distinction is a subset of the second: the boundary between physical and nonphysical is very imprecise for us." The "pleasurably tight coupling" that Hayles also attributes to Haraway appears here too: at the breached human/animal boundary, "cyborgs signal disturbingly and pleasurably tight coupling." Haraway's three breakdowns are the primary scaffolding behind the human/machine, human/animal, and animate/inanimate violations Hayles relays. (Label: `Directly stated`.)
