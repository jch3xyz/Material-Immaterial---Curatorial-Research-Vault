# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Donna Haraway

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation and historical influence.** In *How We Became Posthuman*, [[N. Katherine Hayles]] names [[Donna Haraway]] at two load-bearing moments and adopts two of her terms outright. First, Hayles takes her sense of [[Informatics]] explicitly "[f]ollowing Donna Haraway." Haraway supplies two of the book's three governing terms; Hayles re-embeds them in her own historical narrative of cybernetics, embodiment, and signification.

## Explanation

Hayles's debt to Haraway is not a passing citation but a transfer of vocabulary that underwrites the book's architecture. Two terms migrate from Haraway into Hayles:

1. **Informatics.** Hayles coins her use of the word by direct attribution: "Following Donna Haraway, I take informatics to mean the technologies of information as well as the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes that initiate, accompany, and complicate their development." Where Haraway's "informatics of domination" names a regime in which the world is reconstrued as a problem of coding and command-control-communication, Hayles adapts the term to designate the [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] network binding bodies, texts, and technologies. The crucial inheritance is that informatics, in both, is a *relational field* that already contains "the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes" — never bare information-as-pattern. This is exactly the feature that lets Hayles use the term against the disembodiment fantasy she calls [[The Body as Data|the body as data]]. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

2. **The cyborg as simultaneously entity and metaphor.** Hayles's second of three stories — the construction of [[The Cyborg]] — rests on Haraway's claim that "cyborgs are simultaneously entities and metaphors, living beings and narrative constructions." From this doubleness Hayles builds her insistence that "[m]anifesting itself as both technological object and discursive formation," the cyborg "partakes of the power of the imagination as well as of the actuality of technology," and so "[c]yborgs actually exist." She also takes from "A Manifesto for Cyborgs" both the three boundary violations the cyborg performs (human/machine, human/animal, animate/inanimate) and its "pleasurably tight coupling" between parts not supposed to touch. The cyborg's standing as the privileged site where the figural and the material meet — the hinge of Hayles's [[Material vs Immaterial]] argument — is therefore borrowed wholesale from Haraway. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

Underlying both borrowings is a shared methodological commitment: a refusal to let "information" or "the cyborg" float free of material instantiation, and a feminist critique of mastery and "an objectivist account of science." Hayles treats Haraway as an ally in resisting the disembodiment of information even as she rebuilds these terms inside her own seriated history of cybernetics. The influence runs one way — Haraway is a source Hayles cites, not a contemporary she debates — which is why the relationship is best characterized as historical influence carried by direct citation. (Label: `Interpretive synthesis`.)
