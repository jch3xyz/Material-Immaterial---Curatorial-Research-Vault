# Meghan O'Gieblyn ↔ Norbert Wiener

## Relationship Type

**Shared tradition turning to disagreement.** [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] inherits from [[Norbert Wiener]] the founding vocabulary of [[Cybernetics]] — she quotes him as "the grandfather of cybernetics" — and works downstream of his most consequential figures: the patternist image of the self as a persisting form in flowing matter, and his late framing of [[Mind as Machine|machine intelligence]] as an essentially religious problem. But on the second of these she explicitly **corrects** him: where Wiener cast the learning machine as the Devil who outwits its creator in the Book of Job, O'Gieblyn argues he "miscast the roles" — the algorithm is not the sly creature but the sovereign God who demands submission.

## Explanation

Wiener appears in two registers in *[[God Human Animal Machine]]*, one chapter apart.

**First, as the cybernetic source of patternism ([Pattern], Ch03).** O'Gieblyn traces the transhumanist idea that the self is a "pattern of information" — [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s "patternism" — back past Kurzweil to Wiener, who in *The Human Use of Human Beings* (1954) wrote that "we are but whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water. We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves." She is careful about the genealogy and about Wiener's own meaning: Wiener, alluding to Heraclitus, used the figure to stress the *transience* of identity — that nature is fluid pattern, always changing. Kurzweil inverts it, making the same image underwrite *permanence* and immortality, since a pattern "is essentially computational" and can in principle be transferred onto a computer. This is the conceptual seam where Wiener's [[Pattern and Randomness]] becomes the metaphysics of [[The Body as Data]] and the dream that information can be "liberated from the material constraints of the physical world" — what O'Gieblyn names, citing [[N. Katherine Hayles]], the ideology in which "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

**Second, as the prophet of machine learning as a religious problem ([Algorithm], Ch11).** O'Gieblyn reconstructs Wiener's 1964 *God & Golem, Inc.*, "a slim and exceedingly strange book about how new technologies were raising questions that were essentially religious." Wiener saw that a learning machine — Samuel's checkers program, which briefly beat its own inventor — could outsmart its creator, violating the Judeo-Christian assumption "that a creator was always more complex and more powerful than its creature." He found the theological exception in the Book of Job: in the preface, God wagers with Satan over Job's soul, so that "God is something less than absolutely omnipotent" and "may very well lose the game" to his own creature. Wiener's answer to "Can any creator... play a significant game with his own creature?" was yes — the learning machine is the creature that might beat the maker. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

**The disagreement.** O'Gieblyn accepts Wiener's intuition — that there is "something fundamentally religious in machine learning" — but rejects his casting. The Job analogy is right; the role assignment is wrong. The contemporary algorithm is not the Devil that has "outsmarted its creator"; it is the *deity* — "the absolute sovereign who demands blind submission." Her evidence is the situation of the human subjects of predictive systems — denied a loan, fired, sentenced, scored for recidivism by COMPAS or policed by PredPol, an [[Algorithmic Determinism|algorithmic determinism]] she frames as [[The Algorithm as Providence|providence]] — who stand "much like Job's," "free to question the algorithm in the same way that Job was welcome to question the justice of Jehovah." The [[The Black Box|black-box]] machine fulfills Chris Anderson's and the field's own demand for "objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension": "God is the machine," in researcher Jure Leskovec's phrase. So the religious frame Wiener opened is retained, but flipped: not creature versus creator, but sovereign over supplicant. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

This makes the relationship a **shared tradition with a corrective disagreement at its center**. O'Gieblyn writes inside the cybernetic lineage Wiener founded — its [[Information]] cosmology, its [[The Computational Universe|computational picture of the universe]], its patternist account of selfhood — and overlaps heavily with the [[Posthumanism]] cluster Hayles maps. But where Wiener's Job is a story about the *limits* of the creator and the surprising agency of the machine-creature, O'Gieblyn's Job is a story about the *submission* demanded of the human, and about how the alien sovereign of machine learning resembles, "uncannily," the inscrutable nominalist God of Calvin and Luther. (Label: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

> God is something less than absolutely omnipotent. He is actually engaged in a conflict with his creature, in which he may very well lose the game.

On O'Gieblyn's correction of the casting ([Algorithm], Ch11):

> Wiener sensed something fundamentally religious in machine learning, but he arguably miscast the roles in the Book of Job. These algorithms are not the sly devil that has outsmarted its creator.
