# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Philip K. Dick

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel — read at length, explicitly not a case of influence.** [[Philip K. Dick]] is the novelist [[N. Katherine Hayles]] reads most closely in the second-wave-cybernetics arc of *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*: she gives his mid-1960s novels an entire dedicated chapter, paired structurally with her chapter on [[Autopoiesis|autopoietic theory]]. The pairing is deliberate and the disclaimer is explicit — "Dick's relation to the work of Maturana and Varela is almost certainly not a case of direct influence." What links them is not transmission but a *shared problem*: both Dick and the creators of autopoiesis were "responding to the problem of incorporating the observer into the system" and so "experimenting with more or less radical espistemologies." Dick is therefore a literary register of the same epistemological crisis Hayles tracks in the science — read alongside [[Humberto Maturana]] and [[Francisco Varela]], not as their borrower.

## Explanation

Hayles uses Dick to do what she says narrative does better than [[Systems Theory|analytically driven systems theory]]: stage the cultural, psychological, and political stakes of [[Cybernetics]] in embodied, located form. Three claims organize the relationship.

**1. The shared problem of the observer.** Hayles places Dick and the autopoietic theorists on the same epistemological terrain. Once "system boundaries are defined by information flows and feedback loops rather than epidermal surfaces, the subject becomes a system to be assembled and disassembled," and the question of where the observer stands relative to the system becomes acute. [[Humberto Maturana|Maturana]] "turned first-wave cybernetics inside out," treating the environment as a black box and focusing on the reflexive processes by which a system produces itself; Dick, "working along apparently independent lines of thought," dramatized the same boundary disputes as a struggle to claim the privileged "outside" autopoietic position while forcing an opponent into the "inside" allopoietic one. The convergence runs deep enough that Hayles names them both "system builder[s]" who take seriously the idea (from Spencer-Brown) "that the observer creates a system by drawing a distinction between inside and outside." This is the [[The Observer|observer-in-the-system]] problem common to second-wave [[Reflexivity|reflexivity]]. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

**2. The schizoid android.** Hayles's master figure for the Dick chapter — [[The Schizoid Android|the schizoid android]] — is where the parallel becomes substantive. It is her own coinage, "a multiple pun" naming the fusion of "a person who acts like a machine with a literal interpretation of that person as a machine," gendered female through the autobiographical "dark-haired girl." It performs boundary work: "not so much a fixed symbol... as a signifier that enacts as well as connotes the schizoid, splitting into the two opposed and mutually exclusive subject positions of the human and the not-human." Through it Dick stages, in fiction, the dissolution of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal subject]] into "a system to be assembled and disassembled" that Hayles tracks across the cybernetic tradition. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

**3. The decisive divergence — Dick "leaps over" embodiment.** This is where the parallel breaks, and where Dick matters most for Hayles's overarching thesis. On the definition of the living, "Dick would agree with Maturana and Varela, who argued that artificially created systems can certainly qualify as living." But "unlike Maturana and especially Varela... he leaps over the importance of embodiment." His fiction "shows almost no interest in how intelligent machines are constructed"; bodies (other than women's, as "sexual markers") "rarely appear," and "the emphasis falls almost entirely on perception and thinking." The consequence is Hayles's signature point: "Without embodiment to stabilize his epistemological skepticism, the cracks he opens in the perceiver's knowledge widen into rifts in the fabric of the world." Where autopoiesis anchors its instabilities in biological closure — in [[The Materiality of Informatics|the materiality]] of living process — Dick has no such ballast, so his ontological skepticism runs to the tomb world and the inside-out collapse of self and cosmos. For Hayles this makes Dick a literary symptom of exactly the [[Embodiment vs Information|information/materiality]] split her book contests: he confirms, by negative example, that without [[Embodiment]] to ground it, the skepticism that autopoiesis can contain becomes world-dissolving. (Label: `Directly stated`; the tie to her overarching argument is `Interpretive synthesis`.)

A fourth, lighter thread: in chapter 10 Hayles uses Dick as the contrast case that marks how far autopoiesis travels from first-wave cybernetics. "The paradigmatic cyborg for autopoiesis is the state, not the kind of mechanical human imagined by Bernard Wolfe or [[Philip K. Dick|Philip K. Dick]]" — confirming that Dick's android belongs to the older man-machine amalgam, while autopoiesis redefines life expansively enough to ask whether social systems are alive. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

> Here Dick would agree with Maturana and Varela, who argued that artificially created systems can certainly qualify as living. Unlike Maturana and especially Varela, however, he leaps over the importance of embodiment.

On the consequence of un-embodiment (chapter 9):

> Without embodiment to stabilize his epistemological skepticism, the cracks he opens in the perceiver's knowledge widen into rifts in the fabric of the world.

On both as system builders sharing the inside/outside distinction (chapter 9):

> Like Maturana and Varela, Dickis a system builder. He takes seriously an idea they also propose (following Spencer-Brown): that the observer creates a system by drawing a distinction between inside and outside.

## Confidence

**High.** The relationship is grounded in a chapter-length, explicitly comparative reading: Hayles names Maturana and Varela repeatedly within the Dick chapter, states the parallel-not-influence thesis outright, and articulates the embodiment divergence in her own words. The three substantive claims and the chapter-10 contrast are all directly stated. Only the framing of Dick as a *negative confirmation* of Hayles's larger [[Embodiment vs Information]] thesis is labeled interpretive synthesis; the comparison to autopoiesis and the charge that Dick "leaps over" embodiment are explicit in the text.
