# Meghan O'Gieblyn ↔ N. Katherine Hayles

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation — twice — over a conceptual parallel that O'Gieblyn extends theologically.** This is the most load-bearing O'Gieblyn cross-author note in the vault, and it operates on two layers at once. The *citation* layer is unambiguous: in *[[God Human Animal Machine]]* O'Gieblyn names [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] by name twice, quoting *[[How We Became Posthuman]]* on (1) "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form" (Ch03) and (2) the Macy Conferences as the moment information "came to take precedence over materiality," becoming "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates" (Ch04). The *interpretive* layer is the more interesting one: O'Gieblyn supplies the **theological double** that Hayles brackets out. Where Hayles diagnoses the disembodiment of [[Information]] as a contingent technical history, O'Gieblyn reads the same disembodiment as "the materialist's substitute for the soul" — transhumanism as secular eschatology, [[The Computational Universe|the computational metaphor]] returning the Christian dream of the resurrected, incorruptible body. The relationship is therefore `conceptual_parallel` resting on two `direct_citation`s and one `shared_tradition` (both authors read the [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] decoupling of pattern from matter as the founding move of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]).

## Explanation

The two authors share a single diagnosis and split on what it means. Both hold that [[Posthumanism|transhumanist]] transcendence depends on a prior, contestable belief — that information can be liberated from its material substrate. O'Gieblyn borrows Hayles's *historicization* of that belief (the Macy Conferences, [[Claude Shannon|Shannon]]'s meaning-stripped information theory) wholesale, and then adds the layer Hayles deliberately holds at arm's length: theology. The relationship runs across three registers.

1. **The shared diagnosis — disembodiment as the founding move (Ch03 direct citation).** O'Gieblyn states the transhumanist premise in Hayles's own terms: "transcendence depends on the notion that information can be liberated from the material constraints of the physical world." She then cites Hayles by name and role ("the critic N. Katherine Hayles") for the formula that names what the premise produces: an ideology in which "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form." This is [[The Platonic Backhand and Forehand|Hayles's Platonic-backhand critique]] imported intact — the move by which a reified, weightless [[Information]] is treated as more real than the bodies that carry it. The figures O'Gieblyn marshals around this citation — [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s "patternism," [[Hans Moravec]]'s mind-uploading, [[Norbert Wiener]]'s "we are but whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water" — are precisely the genealogy of disembodiment that Hayles traces in *How We Became Posthuman*. ([[Hans Moravec|Moravec]] is in fact the *origin* of Hayles's book; see [[N. Katherine Hayles and Hans Moravec]].)

2. **The borrowed history — the Macy decoupling of information from matter (Ch04 direct citation).** O'Gieblyn names the book — "In her book *How We Became Posthuman*" — and reproduces Hayles's central historical claim: that it was at the Macy Conferences on [[Cybernetics]] (1946–1953) "that information came to take precedence over materiality." She follows Hayles step for step: the conferences were "radically interdisciplinary," speakers generalized their work to maximize portability, and as a result information "became decontextualized" and began to seem, in the verbatim Hayles phrase O'Gieblyn quotes, "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates." This is the exact mechanism documented in [[The Materiality of Informatics]] and [[The Body as Data]]: [[Claude Shannon|Shannon's information theory]] "stripped information of meaning," and at Macy "it became decoupled from matter." O'Gieblyn's gloss — that information thereby "became, in other words, the materialist's substitute for the soul" — is her own sentence, but it is built directly on top of Hayles's history.

3. **The theological double Hayles brackets (interpretive synthesis).** Here the parallel becomes O'Gieblyn's own argument. Hayles is a cultural-materialist critic; she contests the disembodiment fantasy on the grounds that [[Embodiment]] cannot be subtracted ("for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium") and that the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]] survives, lethally, inside the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]. O'Gieblyn accepts all of this but turns it ninety degrees: the disembodiment of information is not only the liberal subject in disguise, it is *the resurrected soul in disguise*. Transhumanism, she argues, "is ultimately fatalistic," its "lofty Platonic rhetoric about the spirit transcending the body" reiterating "the fundamental problem of our disenchanted age: the inability to account for the mind." The whole of Ch03–Ch04 reads [[Mind as Machine|the computational theory of mind]] as Christian eschatology by other means: Kurzweil as the information age's "academic prophet," the Singularity as the Resurrection, mind-uploading as the glorified body, Origen's *eidos* — "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter" — as the ancient ancestor of Kurzweil's "pattern of information." This theological reading is *outside* Hayles's text; it is the interpretive layer O'Gieblyn adds, and it is what makes this a `conceptual_parallel` and not merely a citation. The shared object is the same — the decoupling of [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] from matter, the dream of substrate-independence — but Hayles names its politics where O'Gieblyn names its religion.

The net relationship: O'Gieblyn is a *downstream reader* of Hayles who takes her history of disembodiment as established fact and uses it to underwrite a thesis Hayles herself does not advance — that the materialist's information-soul is the Christian soul smuggled back in. Where they fully converge is the refusal of substrate-independence as natural or inevitable; where they diverge is register, secular-critical (Hayles) versus theological-genealogical (O'Gieblyn).

## Source Passages

Two verbatim direct citations carry the relationship. From Ch03, O'Gieblyn names Hayles as "the critic" and quotes the formula "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form," after first stating the premise in Hayles's terms ("information can be liberated from the material constraints of the physical world"). From Ch04, she names *How We Became Posthuman* and reproduces the Macy thesis — information coming "to take precedence over materiality" and becoming "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates" — then glosses it as "the materialist's substitute for the soul." The interpretive layer is documented by O'Gieblyn's surrounding framing of the same disembodiment as Christian eschatology (the Resurrection, the glorified body, Origen's *eidos*), which has no counterpart in Hayles's text and is flagged below as `Interpretive synthesis`.
