# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Emmanuel Levinas

## Relationship Type

Direct citation and adoption-with-correction. Emmanuel Levinas's *Time and the Other* is the load-bearing philosophical source for Han's negative theory of eros in *The Agony of Eros*: Han quotes Levinas repeatedly and builds his account of the [[The Other|Other]] — the atopic, unpossessable alterity that eros reaches toward — directly on Levinas's texts. But Han also faults Levinas for stopping short: Levinas "does not glimpse the erotic abysses that find expression as excess and madness." So the relationship is historical influence carried forward with one decisive supplement (the [[Negativity|negativity]] of transgression, drawn from Bataille and Hegel, that Levinas's ethics of eros leaves out).

## Explanation

In chapter 3 of *The Agony of Eros* ("Being Able Not to Be Able"), [[Byung-Chul Han]] grounds his central claim — that [[Eros]] is "a relationship to the Other situated beyond achievement, performance, and ability" — on [[Emmanuel Levinas]]. The controlling idea is Levinas's: the [[The Other|Other]] "bears alterity as an essence," and it is precisely *because* the Other cannot be grasped, possessed, or known that eros can be a relationship to it at all. "Possessing, knowing, and grasping are synonyms of power," Han quotes; a relationship that could exercise such power over the Other would annihilate its otherness. Eros names the one relation "impossible to translate into powers" — its success, paradoxically, "finds expression as a kind of failure," the failure of grasping. This is the Levinasian core of Han's whole book: the [[Negative Potency|not-to]] of *Nicht-Können-Können* ("being able not to be able") is his rendering of Levinas's atopic alterity into the vocabulary of the [[Achievement Society|achievement society]].

Han takes three further figures directly from *Time and the Other*. First, the **temporality of the future**: erotic desire is tied not to the "absence of nothingness" but to "absence in a horizon of the future" — and "the future is the time of the Other." The atopic Other cannot be assimilated into the language of the Same (see [[Hell of the Same]]); it arrives only as the surprising, unattainable event on the horizon of a future that the total, museal present is busy abolishing. Second, the **caress and pleasure** as figures of erotic desire: the caress is "a game with something slipping away," reaching for "what is vanishing into the future without end," nourished by "what doesn't yet exist." Third, the **countenance** (*visage* as Han deploys it) that "at once gives and conceals the Other" — Han sets this "countenance," which keeps a secret, diametrically against the "face" of pornographic total visibility that "holds no secrets" and "hands itself over to total visibility and consumption" (see [[Pornography (Han)]]).

Han's supplement, and his one criticism, is precise. "Although Levinas does not glimpse the erotic abysses that find expression as excess and madness, his ethics of eros points unambiguously to the negativity of the Other." Han credits Levinas with the *ethics* of eros — the sacrosanct, atopic otherness that resists reification — while adding the register Levinas omits: the negativity of transgression, wounding, and excess that Han finds instead in Georges Bataille and in Hegel ("Love is not a possibility, is not due to our initiative, is without reason; it invades and wounds us," Han quotes, again from Levinas — so even the wound is partly Levinas's; what Levinas will not follow is the *abyss*). Han then extends Levinas politically in a direction Levinas did not: "Levinas's ethics of eros can be reformulated as resistance to the economic reification of the Other. Otherness is not consumable difference." Eros as "an asymmetrical relationship to the Other" "interrupts the exchange rate"; otherness "admits no bookkeeping."

**On Martin Buber (plain-text mention, forward-stub target).** Alongside Levinas, Han cites Martin Buber once, on "primal distance" (*Urdistanz*): Buber "claims that such distance serves as the very 'principle of being-human' and constitutes the transcendental condition for any alterity existing at all." Primal distantiation is what "frees — that is, distances — the Other into his or her otherness" and prevents the Other from being reified into an "it," so that the Other remains a "Thou" one can address rather than a sex object one can only call out to. This is a single, discrete citation (one endnote, to Buber's "Distance and Relation"), not the sustained load-bearing engagement Levinas receives. Buber is therefore treated here as a plain-text reference and a **forward-stub** author ([[Martin Buber]]) — his I–Thou / primal-distance apparatus is durable and vault-worthy, and a dedicated `Byung-Chul Han and Martin Buber` reference (and a [[Primal Distance]] concept) can be minted if a Buber source enters `raw/` or a later Han pass draws on him again. It does not warrant a separate reference note on the strength of this one passage alone.

## Source Passages

### Alterity impossible to translate into powers

> The other bears alterity as an essence. And this is why [we] have sought this alterity in the absolutely original relationship of eros, a relationship that is impossible to translate into powers.

(Han quoting Levinas, *Time and the Other*, 87–88.) Label: `Directly stated`

### Eros credited to Levinas, faulted for missing the abyss

> Although Levinas does not glimpse the erotic abysses that find expression as excess and madness, his ethics of eros points unambiguously to the negativity of the Other: sacrosanct, atopic otherness, which, in today's increasingly narcissistic society, is vanishing.

Label: `Directly stated`

### Desire as absence in a horizon of the future

> Erotic desire is tied to a particular absence of the Other—not the absence of nothingness, but rather "absence in a horizon of the future." The future is the time of the Other.

(The quoted phrase is Levinas, *Time and the Other*, 90.) Label: `Directly stated`

### The caress as a game with something slipping away

> Levinas interprets the caress and pleasure as figures of erotic desire. The negativity of absence is essential to both. The caress is a "game with something slipping away." It reaches for what is vanishing into the future without end.

(The quoted phrase is Levinas, *Time and the Other*, 89.) Label: `Directly stated`

### Countenance vs. face

> Yet eros awakens only in view of the "countenance" that "at once gives and conceals the Other." The "countenance" stands diametrically opposed to the "face" that holds no secrets, which exhibits itself in pornographic nakedness and hands itself over to total visibility and consumption.

(The quoted phrases are Levinas, *Time and the Other*, 78–79.) Label: `Directly stated`

### Buber and primal distance (the secondary citation)

> When the Other is perceived as a sexual object, "primal distance" (Urdistanz) erodes; Martin Buber claims that such distance serves as the very "principle of being-human" and constitutes the transcendental condition for any alterity existing at all.

Label: `Directly stated`
