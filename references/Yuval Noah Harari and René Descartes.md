# Yuval Noah Harari ↔ René Descartes

## Relationship Type

Disagreement — historical confrontation and partial reversal. Harari invokes Descartes as the named originator of the animal-as-automaton doctrine, then turns the doctrine's logic back on humans: if animals are mere biochemical algorithms, so are we.

## Explanation

Descartes held that only humans feel and crave; all other animals are mindless automata — vending machines of flesh that react without any inner experience. Harari names this position explicitly and concedes that seventeenth-century science could sustain it. He then dismantles the human exception from two directions at once. First, evolutionary biology has found no soul in humans any more than in pigs: the very concept of an indivisible, immutable essence cannot have emerged through natural selection. Second, the life sciences now treat emotions themselves as biochemical algorithms, which means the Cartesian asymmetry collapses: if animals are algorithms, humans are too, and the special status Descartes reserved for the *res cogitans* dissolves. What survives is not Descartes' dualism but a flat algorithmic ontology in which both humans and animals run on the same computational substrate — a conclusion Descartes would have found intolerable.

The disagreement is structural as well as empirical. Descartes' animal-machine thesis was designed to protect human uniqueness by drawing a hard boundary at language and reason. Harari's thesis erases that boundary by making [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] the universal description of life, relocating "human uniqueness" entirely in the domain of large-scale intersubjective cooperation and fiction-making, not in any inner essence. See also [[Meghan O'Gieblyn and René Descartes]] for a parallel confrontation between Cartesian dualism and contemporary information ontologies.

## Source Passages

### Passage 1 — Descartes named; the animal-machine doctrine stated

`Directly stated`

> This theory was upheld by the father of modern philosophy, René Descartes. In the seventeenth century Descartes maintained that only humans feel and crave, whereas all other animals are mindless automata, akin to a robot or a vending machine. When a man kicks a dog, the dog experiences nothing. The dog flinches and howls automatically, just like a humming vending machine that makes a cup of coffee without feeling or wanting anything.

— *Homo Deus*, ch. 3 "The Human Spark" (raw file: [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/04 The Human Spark]])

### Passage 2 — Descartes' doctrine accepted in its day; residual defenders noted

`Directly stated`

> This theory was widely accepted in Descartes' day. Seventeenth-century doctors and scholars dissected live dogs and observed the working of their internal organs, without either anaesthetics or scruples. They didn't see anything wrong with that, just as we don't see anything wrong in opening the lid of a vending machine and observing its gears and conveyors. In the early twenty-first century there are still plenty of people who argue that animals have no consciousness, or at most, that they have a very different and inferior type of consciousness.

— *Homo Deus*, ch. 3 "The Human Spark" (raw file: [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/04 The Human Spark]])

### Passage 3 — Evolution destroys the soul; the exception Descartes needed for humans is undone

`Directly stated`

> Scientists have subjected Homo sapiens to tens of thousands of bizarre experiments, and looked into every nook in our hearts and every cranny in our brains. But they have so far discovered no magical spark. There is zero scientific evidence that in contrast to pigs, Sapiens have souls.

— *Homo Deus*, ch. 3 "The Human Spark" (raw file: [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/04 The Human Spark]])

### Passage 4 — Evolution cannot produce an indivisible soul; the metaphysics of *res cogitans* fails

`Directly stated`

> The theory of evolution rejects the idea that my true self is some indivisible, immutable and potentially eternal essence. According to the theory of evolution, all biological entities – from elephants and oak trees to cells and DNA molecules – are composed of smaller and simpler parts that ceaselessly combine and separate.

— *Homo Deus*, ch. 3 "The Human Spark" (raw file: [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/04 The Human Spark]])

### Passage 5 — The Cartesian asymmetry collapses: humans too are algorithms

`Strongly implied` (Descartes is not re-named here, but the passage directly inverts his asymmetry)

> Over the last few decades biologists have reached the firm conclusion that the man pressing the buttons and drinking the tea is also an algorithm. A much more complicated algorithm than the vending machine, no doubt, but still an algorithm.

— *Homo Deus*, ch. 2 "The Anthropocene" (raw file: [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/03 The Anthropocene]])

### Passage 6 — Emotions as biochemical algorithms; the animal/human divide erased

`Strongly implied`

> In recent decades life scientists have demonstrated that emotions are not some mysterious spiritual phenomenon that is useful just for writing poetry and composing symphonies. Rather, emotions are biochemical algorithms that are vital for the survival and reproduction of all mammals.

— *Homo Deus*, ch. 2 "The Anthropocene" (raw file: [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/03 The Anthropocene]])

### Passage 7 — Human uniqueness relocated; the soul is not what distinguishes us

`Directly stated`

> No other animal can stand up to us, not because they lack a soul or a mind, but because they lack the necessary imagination.

— *Homo Deus*, ch. 3 "The Human Spark" (raw file: [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/04 The Human Spark]])

## Confidence

**Medium.** Harari names Descartes explicitly and attributes the animal-machine doctrine to him directly (Passages 1–2). The inversion — extending the algorithm description to humans and dissolving the soul — is Harari's own argument developed across multiple pages, always in implicit dialogue with the Cartesian framing he introduced. No passage in either chapter quotes Descartes' own words or cites a specific Cartesian text; the engagement is with the doctrine as received idea rather than with the primary source. The synthesis in the Explanation section is therefore `Interpretive synthesis` built on a `Directly stated` attribution of position.
