# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela

## Relationship Type

**Critical engagement with a shared tradition** — and, within that tradition, a tracked **disagreement** between the two collaborators. [[N. Katherine Hayles]] reads [[Humberto Maturana]] and [[Francisco Varela]]'s theory of [[Autopoiesis]] as the conceptual center of gravity of the second wave of [[Cybernetics]] (1960–1985), and as the moment when the [[Information|reification of information]] takes "a decisive turn." She is neither a disciple nor a debunker: she affirms autopoiesis's recovery of [[Embodiment|embodiment]] and [[The Observer|the observer]] while diagnosing its closure as a limitation, and she narrates the split by which Maturana clung to circular closure while Varela moved toward enaction, embodiment, and Buddhist not-self.

## Explanation

Hayles treats Maturana and Varela as a single intellectual event that then fractures. Out of the [[Reflexivity|reflexive]] insight of the frog-vision work — "the observer, like the frog, does not so much discern preexisting systems as create them through the very act of observation" — Maturana built the maxim that anchors the whole second wave: "Everything said is said by an observer." For Hayles the load-bearing payoff is that autopoiesis re-anchors information in the body. Information, coding, messages, [[Feedback Loops|feedback]], and teleology are demoted to "inferences drawn by an observer," so that autopoiesis "swerves from the trajectory traced in chapters 3 and 4 with regard to information, insisting that information without a body does not exist other than as an inference drawn by an observer." This is exactly the reattachment of the [[The Body as Data|body to data]] that Hayles wants, and it is why autopoiesis sits at the heart of [[Embodiment vs Information]] and [[Material vs Immaterial]].

Hayles's relationship to Maturana is therefore **sympathetic but critical**. She credits him with re-grounding subjectivity's autonomy in organizational closure rather than self-possession — "Closure and recursivity, then, play the foundational role in autopoietic theory that self-possession played in classic liberal theory" — and with pointing "toward the posthuman even as it reinscribes the autonomy and individuality of the liberal subject" by demoting consciousness to an epiphenomenon (see [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]], [[Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman]]). But she argues that the very closure giving the theory its "epistemological muscle" is also "its Achilles' heel": it cannot account for "living systems' explosive potential for transformation," it flattens language into mere "languaging," and it "vaporizes contingency by continuously circulating it within the space of its interlocking assumptions." Maturana's system, in her image, "closes on itself and leaves historical contingency on the outside."

The note's second axis is the **Maturana/Varela divergence**, which Hayles tracks closely. Maturana "continued to occupy essentially the same position and to use the same language as in *Autopoiesis and Cognition*," subordinating reproduction, evolution, and genetic code to autopoiesis and pushing closure to its solipsistic edge ("we do not see what we do not see, and what we do not see does not exist"). Varela, "speaking in a voice now not conjoined with his teacher and mentor," moved away: he restricted autopoiesis to "the domain of cells and animals" (refusing Maturana's extension to social systems and [[Systems Theory|social systems]] generally), conceded a "non-naive" role for information, and developed *enaction*, in which "perception is constituted through perceptually guided actions" rather than mere triggering. In *The Embodied Mind* he turned to a Buddhist-inspired account in which the "self" is "a story consciousness tells itself"; where Wiener and Maturana met the loss of the liberal subject with "anxiety and nostalgia," Varela "celebrates the moment when the self drops away and awareness expands into a realization of its true nature." Hayles reads this turn — toward [[Embodiment|embodied action]], openness, and the not-self — as the bridge from second-wave autopoiesis to the third wave of [[Artificial Life]] and to her own project of restoring the [[The Materiality of Informatics|materiality of informatics]].

On closure as both strength and limit:

> The very closure that gives autopoietic theory its epistemological muscle also limits the theory, so that it has a difficult time accounting for dynamic interactions that are not circular in their effects.
