# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Alan Turing

## Relationship Type

**Critical appropriation** — a blend of **historical influence** and **disagreement-by-proxy**. Hayles does not dispute Turing; she reads his 1950 imitation game as the founding scene of her whole book and turns it against the tradition it inaugurated. Turing supplies the **primal scene** ("the inaugural moment of the computer age") at which, in Hayles's reading, the [[Embodiment|erasure of embodiment]] is *performed*; her quarrel is not with Turing but with Turing's biographer, **Andrew Hodges**, whose dismissal of the gender component she reads as a symptomatic misreading. The relationship is therefore one in which Turing's text is more radical than its custodian allows, and Hayles recovers that radicalism on Turing's behalf.

## Explanation

Hayles opens *How We Became Posthuman* by restaging Turing's "famous 'imitation game'" as the scene in which "intelligence becomes a property of the formal manipulation of symbols rather than enaction in the human lifeworld." The Turing test is, for her, the moment the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]] begins its mutation into the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]: by routing identity through "the words that appear on your terminal," the test sets a body present "in the flesh" against a body "produced through the verbal and semiotic markers constituting it in an electronic environment" — a split that "necessarily makes the subject into [[The Cyborg|a cyborg]]." This is the genealogical root of her concept of the [[Flickering Signifier]] and of [[The Body as Data]]: the screen splices the reader into "a cybernetic circuit," "a distributed cognitive system" (her [[Distributed Cognition]]), so that "no matter what identifications you assign," "you have already become posthuman."

The note's distinctive move is Hayles's **recovery of the gender component** that "often [is] forgotten" — Turing's first example, distinguishing a man from a woman. Andrew Hodges, Turing's biographer, calls the gender case "in fact a red herring" and "one of the few passages of the paper that was not expressed with perfect lucidity," arguing that "[g]ender depended on facts which were not reducible to sequences of symbols." Hayles rejects this on textual grounds: "nowhere does Turing suggest that gender is meant as a counterexample; instead, he makes the two cases rhetorically parallel, indicating through symmetry, if nothing else, that the gender and the human/machine examples are meant to prove the same thing." Because the two cases are parallel, they prove a *single* thesis: that the coincidence of enacted and represented body "is no longer a natural inevitability but a contingent production." Identity — gendered or human — is not a natural given but a produced effect, "mediated by a technology that has become so entwined with the production of identity that it can no longer meaningfully be separated from the human subject."

Hodges's "red herring," on Hayles's reading, is thus not a neutral interpretive judgment but "an attempt to safeguard the boundaries of the subject," a "misreading" that does "violence upon the text" to drag it "back to safer ground where embodiment secures the univocality of gender." Hayles agrees with Hodges that embodiment must be "put back into the picture" but inverts what it secures: not "the distinction between male and female or between humans who can think and machines which cannot," but the recognition that "thought is a much broader cognitive function depending for its specificities on the embodied form enacting it." The disagreement with Hodges is therefore the vehicle by which Hayles converts Turing's test from a proof that "machines can think" into a demonstration of the [[Embodiment vs Information|embodiment/information]] problem at the book's core.

## Source Passages

The four load-bearing moments all sit in the Prologue: (1) the restaging of the imitation game as the scene where "the erasure of embodiment is performed"; (2) the recovery of the forgotten gender example and the framing of its symmetry — the pre-selected planner passage; (3) Hodges's "red herring" verdict, quoted so Hayles can refute it; (4) the conclusion that the test proves identity to be "a contingent production" rather than "a natural inevitability." The Prologue also models the test as a [[The Turing Test as a Magic Trick|magic trick]] and folds the reader's "will, desire, and perception" into a [[Feedback Loops|reverse feedback loop]] between "what can think" and "who can think."
