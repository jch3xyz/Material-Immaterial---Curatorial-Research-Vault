# Roy Ascott ↔ Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Relationship Type

**Possible resonance — interpretive synthesis, not a citation.** [[Roy Ascott]] does not cite [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]], and could not: his telematic essays run from the early 1980s through the late 1990s, while O'Gieblyn's *God, Human, Animal, Machine* appeared in 2021 and postdates him by decades. There is no influence in either direction. What the vault reads here is a **specimen-and-diagnosis pairing**: Ascott is, almost verbatim, the affirmative voice that O'Gieblyn later studies critically. He performs the very gesture she names and distrusts — the migration of theology, mysticism, and mythology into engineering and the network, the attempt to recover through science and technology the transcendence that science took away. He is, in her terms, a [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment cosmologist]] in his own words; she is the skeptic who reads such cosmologies as disenchantment turned inside out. Every claim below is the vault's cross-reading, labelled `Interpretive synthesis` or `Speculative connection`; none is a connection either author asserted.

## Explanation

**Ascott's "biotechnological theology" is O'Gieblyn's re-enchantment, stated from the inside.** Ascott's keystone formulation could stand as a definition of the impulse O'Gieblyn anatomizes: "Computer networking, in short, responds to our deep psychological desire for transcendence—to reach the immaterial, the spiritual—the wish to be out of body, out of mind, to exceed the limitations of time and space, a kind of biotechnological theology" (ch.18, *Gesamtdatenwerk*). Where O'Gieblyn defines [[Re-enchantment]] as the attempt to "restore through science the transcendent—and essentially religious—hopes that science itself obliterated," Ascott openly *names his own project theology* and frames the network as the organ of transcendence. He is not the disinterested specimen O'Gieblyn's other cases (Kurzweil, the transhumanists) sometimes are — he is the most self-aware instance, calling the move by its religious name without embarrassment. *(Interpretive synthesis — the bridge is the vault's; Ascott's phrase is his own.)*

**The same migration of myth and religion into technology — celebrated, not diagnosed.** O'Gieblyn's running thesis is that the eternal religious questions have become engineering problems, and that science has taken up "the mantle of reenchantment" (her gloss on [[Max Weber]]). Ascott says exactly this and welcomes it: "How quickly this science moves into metaphysics and brings us back to theology, mysticism, and mythology!" (ch.18). More strongly, he assigns the network the *function once held by religion itself*: "Virtual space and data space constitute the domain, previously provided by myth and religion, where imagination, desire, and will can reengage the forces of space, time, and matter in the battle for a new reality" (ch.20). This is O'Gieblyn's diagnosis — that [[Technology as Metaphor|technology has inherited the structures of theology]] — voiced as a manifesto. The cleanest map: what O'Gieblyn observes from outside as a *category error or a hollow consolation*, Ascott proclaims from inside as a *program*. *(Interpretive synthesis.)*

**The paranormal re-channelled through technology — Ascott's most literal demonstration of the thesis.** O'Gieblyn's account of [[Re-enchantment]] notes that "the most regressive superstitions of our era are distinctly modern and encoded with the assumptions of disenchantment" (her Charles Taylor caveat). Ascott supplies a startlingly exact illustration, listing the one-to-one substitution of nineteenth-century spiritualism by twentieth-century media: "table rapping and disembodied voices followed by radio; clairvoyance by TV; telepathy by telematics; remote viewing by remote sensing; out-of-body experiences by virtual reality (VR)" (ch.28, *Back to Nature II*). His *Technoetic Aesthetics* lexicon makes the equation a doctrine — "Technostic: Having esoteric, occult, spiritual knowledge revealed through technology" and "Paranormal experience, suppressed in industrial society, is reintegrating into everyday life" (ch.32). This is precisely the phenomenon O'Gieblyn studies: the séance does not return, it is *re-encoded* as VR. Ascott affirms the re-encoding as cultural progress; O'Gieblyn reads it as disenchantment wearing a sacred mask. *(Interpretive synthesis.)*

**Where the resonance becomes sharpest — and where the verdicts split.** Ascott holds that the long materialism of the industrial age has reversed itself through technology: "after the relentless materialism of the industrial age, it is now technology and science themselves that are foregrounding issues of consciousness, mind, and spirit" (ch.29, *The Mind of the Museum*), and that we must "recognise its sentience and to understand how intelligence, indeed, consciousness, pervades its every living part" (ch.31). This panpsychist re-animation of the network is exactly one of O'Gieblyn's named re-enchantment forms (she treats the "wood-wide web," tree consciousness, and "it feels like something to be the internet" under the same heading). The decisive divergence is O'Gieblyn's verdict that these cosmologies *fail on their own terms*: "even the most mystical end up simply reiterating the fundamental problem of our disenchanted age: the inability to account for the mind." Ascott has no such doubt — for him consciousness is not the unsolved residue but the *medium itself*, already pervading the system. His confidence is the precise thing her [[Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment]] argument is built to puncture: the re-enchantment is hollow, she says, because it has nowhere to lodge the very interiority it promises to restore. Ascott lodges it everywhere, by fiat. *(Interpretive synthesis / Speculative connection on the panpsychism mapping.)*

**The shared substrate: the immaterial, uploadable, distributed self.** Both authors converge on the dream of [[Mind as Machine|mind freed from the body]] into information — Ascott's "out of body, out of mind," his telepresent self "dematerialised, and reconfigured at one and the same time," the self that is "permeable." This is the [[Transhumanism]] / [[Posthumanism]] horizon O'Gieblyn reads as the engine of re-enchantment, denominated in [[Information]]. Ascott is its artist-evangelist; O'Gieblyn its critic. The pairing is therefore neither agreement nor flat opposition but **the same object seen from the two ends of forty years** — Ascott building the cathedral, O'Gieblyn writing its theology of suspicion. This is why the relationship is `possible_resonance`: a curatorial specimen-and-diagnosis pairing, not a dialogue. *(Interpretive synthesis.)*

## Confidence

**Medium.** This is an interpretive bridge between authors who never cite one another and write forty years apart; nothing here is asserted by either. But it is the *strongest* of Ascott's vault-internal re-enchantment pairings, because the textual fit is unusually exact: Ascott's own phrase "a kind of biotechnological theology," his assignment of the network to "the domain, previously provided by myth and religion," and his *Technoetic Aesthetics* term "Technostic" (occult knowledge revealed through technology) are near-perfect specimens of the category O'Gieblyn defines as [[Re-enchantment]] — "restore through science the transcendent—and essentially religious—hopes that science itself obliterated." Confidence is held at medium rather than high only because the relationship is one of *valence-inversion* (Ascott affirms what O'Gieblyn diagnoses) and because O'Gieblyn never names Ascott as a case; the diagnostic mapping is the vault's. The single point of genuine analytic tension — whether re-enchantment can account for the mind it promises to restore — is real and sharp (Ascott says consciousness pervades the network; O'Gieblyn says re-enchantment cosmologies fail precisely there), which is why the note is a curatorial specimen-and-diagnosis pairing, useful as a counterpoint, not a claim of influence. Compare [[Roy Ascott and Byung-Chul Han]] (low confidence, opposed verdicts on connectivity/transparency) and [[Roy Ascott and Marshall McLuhan]] (high confidence, direct citation).
