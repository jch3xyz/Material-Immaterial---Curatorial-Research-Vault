# Shoshana Zuboff ↔ John Searle

## Relationship Type

Direct citation — historical influence / borrowed theoretical apparatus. In Chapter 6, Zuboff explicitly names "the philosopher of language John Searle" and quotes him twice to import the **speech-act theory of the declaration** as the conceptual scaffolding for her account of [[Conquest by Declaration]] and the six founding declarations of surveillance capitalism. This is a documented authorial debt, not a vault-constructed parallel: Zuboff cites Searle by name and quotes his words verbatim, with footnotes (the chapter's notes [3] and [4]).

## Explanation

Zuboff builds her central claim about how surveillance capitalism was *inaugurated* on a borrowed philosophical instrument: John Searle's theory of the **declaration**, the class of speech acts that, in his analysis, brings institutional facts into existence merely by asserting them. Where most utterances either describe the world ("you have brown eyes") or seek to change it ("Shut the door"), a declaration does both at once — it "asserts a new reality by describing the world as if a desired change were already true." Zuboff distills Searle's mechanism in his own words: "We make something the case by representing it as being the case." She extends his further point that declarations need not be spoken at all — they may be enacted, "create a reality by representing that reality as created" — and that they "rise or fall on the strength of others' acceptance of the new facts," up to his sweeping conclusion that "all of human civilization is created by… declarations." `Directly stated` (Zuboff naming and quoting Searle).

This Searlean apparatus does load-bearing work in Zuboff's argument. It lets her recast Google's founding moves not as ordinary commercial claims but as **invasive declarations** that fabricated facts "out of thin air" and dared the world to let them stand — the same "conquest pattern" she traces from Columbus's edict to the Spanish *Requirimiento*. The six declarations of surveillance capitalism (we claim human experience as free raw material; we assert the right to translate it into behavioral data; to own that data; to know what it discloses; to decide how to use that knowledge; to secure the conditions that preserve these rights) are, on Searle's logic, performative: they make something the case by representing it as being the case, and they hold only "on the strength of others' acceptance of the new facts." Zuboff's term [[Conquest by Declaration]] is thus a direct application of Searle's speech-act category to political economy — the declaration as an instrument of [[Digital Dispossession]] rather than of benign institution-building. `Strongly implied` (Zuboff applies Searle's concept to her case; the application is hers).

The same chapter then pivots from this Searlean opening to the book's larger thesis about the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning in society]] — that the six declarations "laid the foundation" for an unprecedented concentration of knowledge and power. So Searle supplies the micro-mechanism (how a fact is conjured by assertion) that underwrites Zuboff's macro-claim (how an entire order of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] was conjured by a handful of unchallenged corporate assertions). `Interpretive synthesis` (linking the declaration mechanism to the division-of-learning thesis across the chapter's sections).

## Source Passages

### Passage 1 — Searle's definition of the declaration, quoted by Zuboff

> According to the philosopher of language John Searle, a declaration is a particular way of speaking and acting that establishes facts out of thin air, creating a new reality where there was nothing. Here is how it works: sometimes we speak to simply describe the world—“you have brown eyes”—or to change it—“Shut the door.” A declaration combines both, asserting a new reality by describing the world as if a desired change were already true: “All humans are created equal.” “They are yours to command.” As Searle writes, “We make something the case by representing it as being the case.”

Label: `Directly stated` — Zuboff names Searle and quotes him directly (chapter note [3]).

---

### Passage 2 — The declaration as enacted, and as the ground of institutional reality

> Not every declaration is a spoken statement. Sometimes we just describe, refer to, talk about, think about, or even act in relation to a situation in ways that “create a reality by representing that reality as created.” … In this way declarations rise or fall on the strength of others’ acceptance of the new facts. As Searle concludes, “All of institutional reality, and therefore… all of human civilization is created by… declarations.”

Label: `Directly stated` — Zuboff again quotes Searle (chapter note [4]); the second quote is the sweeping claim Zuboff leverages for her argument.

---

### Passage 3 — Zuboff's application: conquest by declaration and the six declarations

> Conquest by declaration should sound familiar because the facts of surveillance capitalism have been carried into the world on the strength of six critical declarations pulled from thin air when Google first asserted them. … Surveillance capitalism succeeded by way of aggressive declaration, and its success stands as a powerful illustration of the invasive character of declarative words and deeds, which aim to conquer by imposing a new reality.

Label: `Directly stated` (Zuboff's claim) — shows Searle's category transposed onto surveillance capitalism; the application to Google is Zuboff's own.
