# Roy Ascott ↔ Lucy Lippard

## Relationship Type

Conceptual parallel (interpretive synthesis), with a documented historical connection running the *other* direction. Ascott himself does not cite or discuss Lippard anywhere in *Telematic Embrace*; the conceptual rhyme between them — both naming the same dissolution of the art OBJECT — is the vault's synthesis, not a claim either author makes. The one concrete, directly-attested tie is that **Lippard quotes Ascott**, not the reverse: per the editor's introduction (Shanken), Lippard reproduces Ascott's 1964 essay "The Construction of Change" on the dedication page of *[[Six Years]]*, placing him within [[Conceptual Art]].

## Explanation

[[Lucy Lippard]] and [[Roy Ascott]] name the same event — the dematerialization of the art object — from opposite ends of a thirty-year arc. Lippard's *Six Years* (1973) tracks the [[Dematerialization]] of the OBJECT itself: conceptual art's shift from durable artifact to document, instruction, and information, accompanied by conceptual art's "death of the author." Ascott extends the same logic from the object to the INSTITUTION and the network: writing in the 1980s–2000s, he draws on the title of Lyotard and Chaput's exhibition *Les Immatériaux* to describe the "virtual 'immateriality' of the networks," and anticipates "the dematerialisation of academies, galleries, and museums." Where Lippard's dematerialization stops at the failure to escape the market and leaves the gallery standing, Ascott's pushes the same dissolution through the gallery wall — the [[Exhibition as Medium]] is not merely emptied of objects but absorbed into "pervasive and wide-reaching networks" — and then loops back to a *re*-materialisation of culture as artificial life. This is `Interpretive synthesis`: the convergence on a single word, *dematerialisation/immateriality*, is real and traceable in the texts, but the connecting through-line — object → author → institution → network — is the vault's reading, not Ascott's argument. Note also that Ascott's telematic "death of the 'individual,' 'original' author" (after Barthes) independently echoes the death-of-the-author motif that runs through Lippard's conceptual art, again with no cross-citation between them. See `[[Material vs Immaterial]]`, the eight-author tension whose object→system spine both writers occupy.

## Source Passages

Ascott invoking *Les Immatériaux* and the "immateriality of the networks" (ch.16); anticipating the dematerialisation of art institutions (ch.17); and folding dematerialisation forward into re-materialisation as artificial life (ch.32). The shared vocabulary is genuine; the extension from object to network is the vault's reading.
