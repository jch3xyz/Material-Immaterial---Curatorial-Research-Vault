# Shoshana Zuboff and Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Relationship Type

Conceptual parallel — **interpretive synthesis**. Zuboff does not cite O'Gieblyn (and O'Gieblyn does not cite Zuboff); this is a vault-internal cross-reading. The bridge is a single shared figure: the secular drive to **guaranteed outcomes**, read by Zuboff as a political-economic logic and by O'Gieblyn as a displaced theology of foreknowledge. A lighter, more circumscribed pairing than the Zuboff↔Han, Zuboff↔Harari, or Zuboff↔Kurzweil notes.

## Explanation

Zuboff's surveillance capitalism culminates in [[A Utopia of Certainty]]: the transformation of the market into "a project of total certainty," "a new collective order based on total certainty" enforced through [[Prediction Products]] and [[Instrumentarian Power]]. O'Gieblyn, in *God, Human, Animal, Machine*, traces the same appetite for certainty back to its religious source — [[The Algorithm as Providence]] and [[Algorithmic Determinism]] name the way the data-saturated worldview reinstalls a Calvinist God of foreknowledge under a secular, computational guise. Read together, Zuboff supplies the *economic mechanism* (extraction → prediction → behavioral modification) and O'Gieblyn supplies the *theological genealogy* (omniscience and predestination reborn as the algorithm): the same wish for a foreclosed, guaranteed future, described once as an emerging order of capital and once as an old doctrine of providence wearing new clothes (Interpretive synthesis).

A second, narrower convergence: both authors note that the predictive apparatus does not merely *describe* the world it forecasts but *helps make* it. Zuboff, quoting Frank Pasquale, says of Google's and Facebook's ranking algorithms that "they help create the world they claim to merely 'show' us" — a thread that runs into the prediction imperative's escalation from forecasting behavior to manufacturing it. This performative, world-making quality is exactly what unsettles O'Gieblyn's providence frame, where the determinism that claims only to *reveal* the future quietly *authors* it (Speculative connection).

## Source Passages

Zuboff names the endgame as total certainty in two registers — as the seventh clause of her formal definition of surveillance capitalism, and as the structural successor to totalitarianism:

> A movement that aims to impose a new collective order based on total certainty

> Totalitarianism was a transformation of the state into a project of total possession. Instrumentarianism and its materialization in Big Other signal the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty, an undertaking that is unimaginable outside the digital milieu and the logic of surveillance capitalism.

And the world-making (rather than merely world-showing) character of the predictive apparatus, quoting Frank Pasquale:

> They help create the world they claim to merely 'show' us.

Against these sit O'Gieblyn's [[The Algorithm as Providence]] and [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the reading of computational foreknowledge as a secularized inheritance of the theology of providence and predestination. (No Zuboff passage cites O'Gieblyn; the link is the shared structure of the certainty-wish, not a textual reference.)

## Confidence

Medium. The Zuboff side is **Directly stated** — "total certainty," "a new collective order based on total certainty," and the prediction-to-production escalation are verbatim and central to her argument. The cross-reading with O'Gieblyn's providence/determinism concepts is **Interpretive synthesis**: no citation runs either way, and Zuboff frames certainty as instrumentarian power and market logic, not as a theology she is consciously secularizing. The pairing is deliberately kept modest — the strongest "displaced theology of the guaranteed future" arguments in the vault belong to the Harari (Dataism) and Kurzweil (the Singularity) bridges; this note registers a real but lighter resonance.
