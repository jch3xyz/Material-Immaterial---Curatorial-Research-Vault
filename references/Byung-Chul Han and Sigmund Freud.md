# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Sigmund Freud

## Relationship Type

Direct citation and disagreement (with elements of historical influence). In the title chapter of *The Burnout Society* Han engages Freud's psychic apparatus directly and at length — only to argue that it has become historically obsolete. Freud is the named model of the *disciplinary, negative psyche*; Han's thesis is that the late-modern [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] has a fundamentally different psyche, so that the characteristic maladies of the present ([[Depression is caused by the imperative to achieve not the failure to become oneself|depression]], [[Burnout|burnout]], ADHD) elude psychoanalysis altogether.

Freud appears in the vault only as a forward-stub author (no book by him in `raw/`); this note is the substantive account of how Han uses and supersedes him.

## Explanation

Han reads Freud's psyche as the psyche of *negativity*. The Freudian apparatus is a "repressive apparatus with commandments and prohibitions," structured — like disciplinary society itself — by "walls, thresholds, borders, and guards." It is "dominated by negation [Verneinung], repression, and fear of transgression," and the ego is a "seat of anxiety" [Angststätte]. Psychoanalysis is therefore historically bounded: it "is only possible in repressive societies that found their organization on the negativity of prohibitions and commandments." This is the disciplinary, negative psyche — the psyche of the obedience-subject (which Han aligns with the Kantian moral subject of duty, conscience, and the superego).

Han's core claim is a periodizing one: "The late-modern achievement-subject possesses an entirely different psyche than the obedience-subject for whom Freud conceived psychoanalysis." The modal verb of achievement society is not the Freudian *Should* but *Can*; the subject is "poor in negation," "a subject of affirmation." Han pushes this to its limit — if the unconscious is bound to the negativity of repression [Verdrängung], "then the late-modern achievement-subject would no longer have an unconscious. It would be a post-Freudian ego," the Freudian unconscious being "a product of the disciplinary society… that we have long since left behind." Because depression, burnout, and ADHD "indicate an excess of [[Positivity|positivity]]" rather than [[Negativity|negation]] — "not… not-being-allowed-to-do-anything [Nicht-Dürfen], but… being-able-to-do-everything [Alles-Können]" — "psychoanalysis offers no way of approaching these phenomena." Depression "defies the conflict model — that is, depression eludes psychoanalysis." (This is the heart of his polemic against Ehrenberg: see [[Depression is caused by the imperative to achieve not the failure to become oneself]].)

The engagement is not pure rejection but historical transformation. Han borrows Freud's own categories to mark the shift. (1) **Superego → ego ideal.** "In the transition from disciplinary society to achievement society, the superego positivizes itself into the ego ideal." The repressive superego, with its "dictatorial 'Thou shalt'," issues *negative compulsion*; the ego ideal *seduces* and exercises a *positive compulsion*. The obedience-subject "subjects itself [sich unterwift] to the superego," whereas the achievement-subject "projects itself [entwirft sich] onto the ego ideal" — the move from subject to [[The Project (Han)|project]], where the gap between the real ego and an unattainable ego ideal "brings forth auto-aggression" and feeds [[Auto-Exploitation|self-exploitation]]. (2) **Melancholy vs depression.** Freud (in "Mourning and Melancholia") understands melancholy as a destructive, internalized relation to the lost/ambivalent Other — a phenomenon of negativity, "preceded by the experience of loss," still standing in a (negative) relation to an absent object. Han distinguishes contemporary depression from this Freudian melancholy: depression "is objectless and therefore undirected," "cut off from all relation and attachment," involving "no dimension of alterity" — the achievement-subject's "overexcited, overdriven, excessive self-reference." Freud's other named cases — hysteria (the typical malady of psychoanalysis's founding moment, presupposing repression and conversion) and the "Schreber Case" (paranoid delusion traced to repressed homosexuality) — are likewise read as residues of a vanished disciplinary negativity.

In short: Freud is the indispensable theorist of the *negative* psyche, and precisely for that reason — on Han's account — the wrong theorist for the *positive*, self-exploiting psyche of the present.
