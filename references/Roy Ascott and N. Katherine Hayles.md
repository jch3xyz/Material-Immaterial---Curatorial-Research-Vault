# Roy Ascott ↔ N. Katherine Hayles

## Relationship Type

**Conceptual parallel — no citation in either direction — best described as *the same translation, celebrated by one and contested by the other*, and explicitly an interpretive synthesis the vault constructs rather than a citation either author made.** [[Roy Ascott|Ascott]] (*[[Telematic Embrace]]*, essays largely 1983–2003) does not cite [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]; the central essays here ("Art and Telematics," 1988; "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?," 1990) **predate** *[[How We Became Posthuman]]* (1999), so there is no `direct_citation` and no `historical_influence` to point to. What binds them is that they perform the *same operation on the same object* from opposite valuations: both take the [[Embodiment|embodied]], skin-bounded human and watch it be translated by information technology into a distributed, networked mind that exceeds the body. Ascott supplies the **artist's celebration** of this translation — the [[Network Consciousness|networked consciousness]] that puts you "out of body," the [[Distributed Cognition|thinking system]] in which the lines between human, computer, and environment are "purely artificial and fictitious." Hayles supplies the **critic's genealogy and warning** that the very same translation, taken to its limit, becomes the fantasy of [[Virtuality|disembodiment]] she spends a book contesting. The relationship is `conceptual_parallel` with a sharp asymmetry of *stance*: Ascott is the optimistic inversion of Hayles's critique. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Explanation

The two converge on a premise and split on a verdict. **Premise (shared):** the mind is not contained by the skin or fixed to a single body; information technology distributes it across a network of human, machine, and environment, and the boundary between flesh and signal is artificial. **Verdict (divergent):** Ascott reads the [[The Telematic Embrace|telematic]] dissolution of the body's boundaries — "out of body, linking your mind into a kind of timeless sea," intelligence "amplified," authorship "dispersed," memory "time-free and global" — as **liberation and transcendence**, the long-desired escape from the isolated Cartesian individual into a planetary [[Network Consciousness]]. Hayles reads the *same* dissolution — pattern lifted off matter, the self distributed beyond the skin, [[Information Lost Its Body|information imagined as having lost its body]] — as the contingent and resistible production of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] as **disembodiment**, and writes [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment|against it]]. Ascott is thus Hayles's antagonist in valuation and, at three precise points, her structural twin.

1. **The distributed thinking system — the shared Batesonian premise.** Ascott's deepest theoretical commitment is borrowed from Gregory Bateson: "human plus computer system plus environment constitutes a thinking system," and therefore "the lines between human, computer, and environment are purely artificial and fictitious… they are not boundaries of the thinking system." This is, almost exactly, the cybernetic premise Hayles tracks under [[Distributed Cognition]] — that cognition is not housed in a bounded individual but spread across the human, the machine, and the surrounding field, so that "the" mind cannot be cleanly differentiated from its informational environment. Ascott reaches this position from Bateson's *Steps to an Ecology of Mind*; Hayles reaches it from the cybernetic tradition (Bateson, the Macy Conferences, Maturana and Varela) and makes it the ground of her own argument. They share the substrate-claim and even one of its sources. The difference is that Ascott takes the dissolution of the human/machine boundary as **good news** — the basis of [[Systems Consciousness|a planetary mind]] — where Hayles takes the *same* dissolution as the threshold at which embodiment can be made to drop out if it is not defended. (`Directly stated` on Ascott; `Interpretive synthesis` on the bridge to Hayles.)

2. **"Out of body" — Ascott as the celebratory inversion of "information lost its body."** Ascott's signature phrase for the telematic condition is "out of body": "networking puts you, in a sense, out of body, linking your mind into a kind of timeless sea"; "Telematics' means… effectively out-of-body, out-of-mind," and "Through telematic networks, intelligence is amplified, authorship is dispersed, and memory becomes time-free and global in its dimensions." This is the precise scenario Hayles narrates as the founding fantasy of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] — that consciousness is an informational pattern that can float free of its material substrate, that the self can be lifted off the body and distributed through a network. For Hayles this is the dangerous dream she names [[Information Lost Its Body|"information lost its body"]] and argues was [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable|contingent, not inevitable]]; for Ascott it is the artistic and spiritual achievement of the medium — the "profound human desire for transcendence: to be out of body, out of mind, beyond language." Ascott's "timeless sea" and "time-free… memory" are exactly the substrate-independent, materially-unanchored mind that Hayles's [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]], recoded as disembodied information, was supposed to become. Same picture; Ascott thrills to what Hayles warns against. This is the [[Embodiment vs Information]] / [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis stated on Ascott's own terms. (`Directly stated` on Ascott; `Interpretive synthesis` on the opposition.)

3. **Reembodiment and "mind requires matter" — where Ascott unexpectedly meets Hayles halfway.** Ascott's later technoetic vocabulary complicates the clean opposition and brings him, on two points, *toward* Hayles rather than against her. First, his definition of telepresence is not pure disembodiment but a doubling: "We are both here and there, out-of-body and reembodied, dematerialised, and reconfigured at one and the same time." The networked self is not subtracted from the body; it is *re*embodied elsewhere — which is close to Hayles's insistence that there is no disembodiment, only [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment|different embodiment]]. Second, and more strikingly, his definition of supervenience concedes the materiality Hayles fights for: "Mind is supervenient on the activity of its interfaces, whether organic or telematic, and although mind is ubiquitous and always present, it requires matter to become manifest." That mind "requires matter to become manifest" is, almost verbatim, Hayles's core thesis — that information is never substrate-free, that pattern always needs a material instantiation. Even Ascott's most celebratory account of [[Virtuality|virtual presence]] is of "others who are in their corporeal materiality at a distance, physically inaccessible or otherwise remote" — the remote body is *still corporeal*, never erased, only displaced. So the late Ascott does not finally claim escape *from* matter; he claims distribution *across* matter and interface. To that extent he is not Hayles's antagonist but an artist independently arriving at her materiality-of-information claim through the practice of telematic art. (`Directly stated` on Ascott; `Interpretive synthesis` on the convergence with Hayles.)

The net relationship: **Ascott celebrates, as the transcendent promise of telematic art, the distributed and disembodied networked mind that Hayles genealogizes and contests as the posthuman fantasy of disembodiment — yet his own later "reembodied" and "mind requires matter" formulations land him, almost despite his rhetoric, on Hayles's side of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] / [[Embodiment vs Information]] axis.** Both hold cognition to be distributed beyond the bounded individual ([[Distributed Cognition]], the Batesonian thinking system); both watch the network translate the embodied human into circulating information. Where Ascott reaches for the "timeless sea" and the dispersal of authorship and memory as liberation ([[Network Consciousness]], [[The Telematic Embrace]]), Hayles insists ([[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]], [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]]) that information never loses its body without violence and that the outcome was never inevitable. Read together they bracket the digital-network recoding of the human: Ascott as its enthusiast-practitioner, Hayles as its critic — with Ascott's supervenience clause as the unexpected place their accounts touch. (See the author notes [[Roy Ascott]] / [[N. Katherine Hayles]] for the fuller positions.)

## Source Passages

The Ascott side of the parallel is carried by verbatim passages from *Telematic Embrace*. The shared distributed-cognition premise is the "Art and Education in the Telematic Culture" line that "human plus computer system plus environment constitutes a thinking system" and that the boundaries between them are "purely artificial and fictitious." The "out of body" celebration is the "Art and Telematics" line ("networking puts you, in a sense, out of body, linking your mind into a kind of timeless sea") and the "Beyond Time-Based Art" line on intelligence amplified, authorship dispersed, and memory "time-free and global." The reembodiment and materiality concessions are the "Technoetic Aesthetics" glossary entries for *telepresence* ("both here and there, out-of-body and reembodied") and *supervenience* ("mind… requires matter to become manifest"), with the corporeal-at-a-distance qualification from "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" The Hayles half of this note is documented by cross-reference to her already-ingested notes ([[N. Katherine Hayles]], [[Embodiment]], [[Distributed Cognition]], [[Virtuality]], [[Information Lost Its Body]], [[Embodiment vs Information]], the disembodiment arguments) rather than by new quotation of her text; wherever an Ascott passage is paired with a Hayles commitment, the cross-author claim is labelled `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Confidence

**Medium.** The Ascott passages are verbatim and load-bearing, and each maps onto a securely documented Hayles position, so the *content* of the parallel — both treat the mind as distributed beyond the bounded body, both watch the human be translated into networked information — is well supported (`Directly stated` on the Ascott side taken alone). Confidence is held to medium, not high, for three reasons. First, there is **no citation in either direction**: the central Ascott essays predate Hayles's book, so the linkage is a juxtaposition the vault constructs, and the relationship itself is `Interpretive synthesis`, not a claim either author asserts. Second, the **valuations diverge sharply** — Ascott celebrates the out-of-body networked mind that Hayles writes against — so the parallel is genuine at the level of the shared object but must not be read as agreement. Third, the convergence in register 3 (Ascott's "reembodied" / "requires matter") is real but **partial**: Ascott still frames telematics as transcendence and never mounts Hayles's critique of disembodiment, so the meeting-halfway is a structural resonance the vault flags, not a position Ascott consciously shares with her. The "celebration vs critique, with an unexpected materialist concession" framing is the vault's interpretive scaffolding.
