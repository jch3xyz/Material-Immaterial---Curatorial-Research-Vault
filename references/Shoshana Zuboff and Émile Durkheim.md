# Shoshana Zuboff ↔ Émile Durkheim

## Relationship Type

Direct citation and historical influence. This is a **direct citation by Zuboff**, not a vault-constructed resonance: in Chapter 6, "Hijacked: The Division of Learning in Society," Zuboff names "the young Emile Durkheim," quotes *The Division of Labor in Society* (1893) verbatim, and explicitly builds her own keystone concept — [[The Division of Learning in Society]] — as the twenty-first-century successor to Durkheim's nineteenth-century division of labor. Durkheim supplies both the *analytic template* (an ordering principle migrating out of the economy to become the "axial principle of social order") and the *warning* (its "abnormal," sometimes-translated "pathological" form), which Zuboff transposes onto the surveillance-capitalist hijacking of learning. `Directly stated`

## Explanation

Three moves structure the borrowing. (1) **The migratory template.** Durkheim's discovery was that the division of labor was "no longer quarantined in the industrial workplace" but had "burst through those factory walls to becoming the critical organizing principle of industrial society." Zuboff lifts this exact figure: "the division of learning follows the same migratory path from the economic to the social domain once traveled by the division of labor," so that learning, like labor, "passes far beyond purely economic interests" to establish "the basis for our social order and its moral content." She makes the generational analogy explicit — "The division of learning is to us, members of the second modernity, what the division of labor was to our grandparents and great-grandparents, pioneers of the first modernity." (2) **The conditions-of-existence ground.** Durkheim explains the division of labor as society's response to the new "conditions of existence" of modern individuals when "the old sources of meaning that had bonded communities across space and time melted away" — the same Durkheimian frame Zuboff uses (under [[Conditions of Existence]] and [[Second Modernity]]) to explain why people turn to the digital. (3) **The pathology warning.** Durkheim "recognized that things can take a dark turn," yielding "an 'abnormal' (sometimes translated as 'pathological') division of labor" rooted in "extreme asymmetries of power that make 'conflict itself impossible' by 'refusing to admit the right of combat.'" Zuboff transposes this precisely: surveillance capitalism's "unprecedented asymmetries of knowledge and power" produce a "pathological" division of learning "that operate[s] precisely as Durkheim had feared," in which the very secrecy and illegibility of the apparatus removes the means of combat. Her recurring three-question rubric — "Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?" — is the operational test for whether the division of learning is healthy or pathological, the axis of her [[Knowledge vs Power (Zuboff)]] opposition. `Directly stated` (Durkheim citation and the labor→learning transposition); the contemporary application to surveillance capital is Zuboff's own argument, also `Directly stated`.
