# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Warren McCulloch

## Relationship Type

**Historical influence / critical reconstruction.** [[N. Katherine Hayles]] does not engage McCulloch as a contemporary interlocutor; she reads him as a historical figure — the chairman of the [[Cybernetics|Macy Conferences]] and originator of the McCulloch-Pitts neuron — whose work she reconstructs and critiques to expose how [[The Body as Data|the body was reconceived as a flow of binary code]]. Her stance is double: she admires McCulloch's refusal to abandon [[Embodiment]] even as she diagnoses the rhetorical move ([[The Platonic Backhand and Forehand|the Platonic backhand]]) by which his model made embodied reality look derivative of logical form.

## Explanation

McCulloch is, for Hayles, the Macy figure through whom [[Cybernetics]] acquired what he called "a setting in the flesh." The McCulloch-Pitts neuron supplied the biological warrant for treating the human as an information-processing machine: a neuron fires or does not, and neural nets so wired can "signify logical propositions." Hayles reads this as the second of cybernetics' three fronts — the construction of human neural structure as a flow of information — and names its cost directly: transforming the body into pulsing binary code "was an essential step in seeing human being as an informational pattern." This is the mechanism behind the larger thesis of [[The Body as Data]] and the loss of the body chronicled in [[Information Lost Its Body]]. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

Her central critical charge is that McCulloch deploys [[The Platonic Backhand and Forehand|the Platonic backhand]]: in his 1947 correspondence with Hans-Lukas Teuber he presents the theorem of his neural model "as though it preexisted specific mechanisms and then was later imperfectly instantiated in them," so that an abstraction derived *from* embodied tissue is reframed as a coercive law that the flesh merely *instantiates*. Hayles insists the theorem holds only for the simplified model, not for "embodied actuality," and aligns the move with Andrea Nye's feminist critique of how lawlike logic masks its own situated, contingent origins. This is the same reification — [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable|contingent, not inevitable]] — that she tracks across the chapter. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

Yet Hayles distinguishes McCulloch from the purer reifiers. "Unlike others who make this move," she writes, "McCulloch never relinquished his commitment to embodiment"; he insisted that "a signal is a proposition embodied in a physical process" and that even his "oversimplified" postulated neurons "are still physical." His search for an "empirical epistemology" keeps logical form and flesh in unresolved tension — the very tension that animates [[Embodiment vs Information]]. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

Finally, Hayles relays Gregory Bateson's image of McCulloch as a **Moses-like figure** who "could lead others to the brink of this new epistemology but was unable to enter into it himself." McCulloch fought to exorcise [[Reflexivity|reflexivity]] (associated by the Macy group with subjectivity and the [[The Observer|observer]]); Bateson, his self-described disciple, carried the cybernetic paradigm across into the reflexive second wave that McCulloch could glimpse but not occupy. (Label: `Directly stated`.)
