# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Georges Bataille

## Relationship Type

Shared tradition / historical influence carried forward. Georges Bataille is one of the load-bearing sources of the negative theory of eros that [[Byung-Chul Han]] mounts in *The Agony of Eros* — Bataille recurs across the whole book (Foreword, chs. 3, 4, 5, 6). Han does not argue *against* Bataille; he inherits and redeploys two of Bataille's figures — "holy eroticism" and "unproductive expenditure" (the shift from a restricted to a general economy) — as diagnostic instruments for the present. This note scopes the relationship to chapters 5–6; the transgression / eroticism-as-excess strand in chapters 3–4 (where Bataille supplies, alongside Hegel, the [[Negativity|negativity]] of transgression that Han finds missing in Levinas — see [[Byung-Chul Han and Emmanuel Levinas]]) is a separate chunk and will merge into this note later.

## Explanation

Two moments in the second half of *The Agony of Eros* run on Bataille.

**(1) "Holy eroticism" and deritualization (ch. 5, *Porn*).** Han enlists Bataille to name what pornography destroys. Where his contemporary target is [[Pornography (Han)|porn]] as "bare life on display," "the antagonist of eros," Bataille's older category of *holy eroticism* preserved exactly what porn dissolves: a ritual frame. "Bataille's conception of 'holy eroticism' still included ritualized communication: festivals and ceremonial games providing particularized sites, places at a remove." The erotic, for Han via Bataille, is inseparable from consecration, secrecy, and set-apartness — the negativity of the "place at a remove." Han's argument that "profanation is unfolding as deritualization and desacralization" and that "pornography completes the deritualization of love" therefore uses Bataille as the measure of a loss: the [[Positivity|positive]], hypervisible, [[Transparency|transparent]] order strips the ritual thresholds that made eros possible. (Han sets this alongside, and against, Agamben's *praise* of profanation and Baudrillard's account of seduction — see [[Byung-Chul Han and Giorgio Agamben]] and [[Byung-Chul Han and Jean Baudrillard]].) *(Directly stated.)*

**(2) Unproductive expenditure vs. the socioeconomic reading of Emma Bovary (ch. 6, *Fantasy*).** Here Bataille is the lever Han uses to pry Emma Bovary loose from a sociological explanation. Against [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz|Eva Illouz]]'s reading in *Why Love Hurts* — which "traces Emma's dissipation to early consumer culture in nineteenth-century France" and makes her ruinous spending an effect of consumerist-romantic desire — Han insists her behaviour "cannot be explained by the socioeconomic structure of nineteenth-century France." Instead "it finds expression as excess and expenditure, which represents a shift from what Bataille would call 'restrictive economy to … general economy.'" Bataille's *unproductive expenditure* — opposed to "forms of consumption that serve 'the means of production'" — reframes Emma as the *principle of loss*: her wasteful spending negates the bourgeois economy of "balanced accounts" embodied by the merchant Lheureux (a former money-changer whose name means "the happy one"). And because, as Han glosses Bataille, "absolute loss is death," Emma's death is "a due consequence of the logic of expenditure and loss." The Bataillean frame thus does the same work as Han's central claim that eros "interrupts the exchange rate" and that "otherness admits no bookkeeping": it names an economy of loss that the accountancy of production/consumption cannot register. *(Directly stated for the quotations; the tie to eros as the interruption of exchange is `Interpretive synthesis`.)*

Across both moments the shared tradition is the same: Bataille supplies Han a vocabulary of *sacred negativity* — ritual set-apartness, transgressive excess, expenditure toward death — that opposes the leveling [[Positivity|positivity]] of a de-ritualized, calculable, hypervisible order. Han updates the referent (porn, digital hypervisibility, the [[Hell of the Same]]) but keeps Bataille's structure of thought.

### The Bataillean reading set against Illouz's socioeconomic one (ch. 6)

> Counter to Illouz's assumption, however, Emma's behavior cannot be explained by the socioeconomic structure of nineteenth-century France. […] Lheureux, who is said previously to have exercised the profession of money changer, stands for the bourgeois economy that Emma's unproductive, wasteful spending negates. Emma's behavior contradicts the "economic principle of balanced accounts": the logic of production and consumption. As the "principle of loss," it renounces petty bourgeois happiness—which is precisely what the name Lheureux means. Absolute loss is death. Thus, Emma's death represents a due consequence of the logic of expenditure and loss.

Label: `Directly stated`
