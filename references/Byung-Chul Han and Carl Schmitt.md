# Byung-Chul Han and Carl Schmitt

## Relationship Type

Direct citation and instrumental conceptual borrowing within a shared tradition of political theory concerned with the necessary opacity of the political. In [[The Transparency Society]], Han twice mobilizes Schmitt — by name, with quotation, and with footnoted citations to *The Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy* and *Roman Catholicism and Political Form* — to argue that [[Transparency]] dismantles the [[Arcana of Power]] and so cripples politics as such. Han accepts Schmitt's premise that "to every great politics belongs the 'arcanum'" but uses it diagnostically rather than apologetically: the point is not to defend the secret praxis of sovereign power but to show that a regime of total visibility liquidates the constitutive negativity of political decision and slides into post-political administration. The relationship is thus direct citation in service of Han's broader critique of [[Positivity]], not a Schmittian programme.

## Explanation

Han's most explicit deployment of Schmitt occurs in Chapter 1 ("The Society of Positivity"), where, having argued that the [[Positivity|Society of Positivity]] expels [[Negativity]] from theory and from political life, he names politics as "strategic action" that "inhabits a realm of secrecy" and turns to Schmitt for the canonical formulation: the "postulate of openness" has as its "specific opponent" the idea that "arcana belong to every kind of politics." Han quotes Schmitt's *Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy* on the necessity of "political-technical secrets" for sovereignty, then quotes *Roman Catholicism and Political Form* on the eighteenth-century "aristocratic concept of secrecy," concluding with Schmitt's demand for more "courage to secrecy" (`Directly stated`). The argument is that without arcana there is no politics, only theatocracy and staging "before an audience of Papagenos."

In Chapter 9 ("The Society of Control"), Han redeploys the same conceptual nucleus without naming Schmitt. The "praxis of arcana" is identified as a technique that power employs, and "[[Transparency]] dismantles the arcane sphere of power." The Schmittian lexicon (*arcana*, *Gleichschaltung*) and the underlying claim — that total control destroys "free spheres of action" — bind this chapter back to the explicit Chapter 1 citation. The same passage cites a third author (an unnamed source on rulers, trust, and "freedom to act without constant auditing") to extend the point, and the chapter as a whole culminates in the claim that "[[Freedom turns out to be a form of control|freedom turns out to be a form of control]]."

Han's appropriation of Schmitt is partial and instrumental. He uses Schmitt's category of the *arcanum* to characterize what transparency liquidates, and he uses *Gleichschaltung* (the Nazi-era German term for forced coordination, which Schmitt himself was implicated in deploying) to name transparency's totalitarian structure. He does not endorse Schmittian decisionism, sovereign exception, or political theology as positive doctrine; Schmitt functions instead as a witness against the post-political pretensions of transparency. Where Schmitt defended secrecy in the name of sovereign decision, Han uses Schmitt's category negatively, to mark what the contemporary [[Transparency|Society of Transparency]] cannot accommodate — secrecy, distance, shame, and the gap that makes [[Thinking vs. Calculation|thinking]] and political decision possible. The political ambivalence of relying on Schmitt is unacknowledged in the text; Han neither rehearses Schmitt's authoritarianism nor distances himself from it.

## Source Passages

From Chapter 1, the direct citation of Schmitt on the *arcanum*:

> Politics is strategic action. For this reason alone, it inhabits a realm of secrecy. Total transparency cripples it. The "postulate of openness," Carl Schmitt wrote, "finds its specific opponent in the idea that arcana belong to every kind of politics"; "political-technical secrets . . . are in fact just as necessary for absolutism as business and economic secrets are for an economic life that depends on private property and competition." Only politics amounting to theatocracy can do without secrets. In such a case, however, political action gives way to mere staging. An "audience of Papagenos," in Schmitt's phrasing, makes the arcanum vanish:

> The eighteenth century staked much on self-confidence and the aristocratic concept of secrecy. In a society that no longer has such courage, there can be no more "arcana," no more hierarchy, no more secret diplomacy; in fact, no more politics. To every great politics belongs the "arcanum." Everything takes place on stage (before an audience of Papagenos).

> It follows that the end of secrecy would be the end of politics. Accordingly, Schmitt demands of politics more "courage to secrecy."

From Chapter 9, the reuse of the same conceptual nucleus without naming Schmitt:

> Transparency and power do not get along well. Power likes to cloak itself in secrecy. The praxis of arcana is one technique that power employs. Transparency dismantles the arcane sphere of power. Yet mutual transparency can only be achieved through permanent surveillance, which always takes on increasingly excessive forms. Such is the logic of surveillance society. Total control destroys the freedom of action and ultimately leads to Gleichschaltung.
