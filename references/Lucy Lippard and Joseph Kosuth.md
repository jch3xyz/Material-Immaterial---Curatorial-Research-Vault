# Lucy Lippard ↔ Joseph Kosuth

## Relationship Type

Chronicler and her documented subject, shading into disagreement. [[Joseph Kosuth]] is one of the artists [[Lucy Lippard]] tracks year-by-year in *[[Six Years]]*, and she reprints his statements at length. But his program — "art as idea as idea," an analytic [[Conceptual Art|conceptual art]] modeled on philosophy and addressed to an audience of artists — is the tight, category-defining pole against which Lippard positions her own looser, anti-category, politically inflected reading. The relationship is mostly disagreement-by-juxtaposition: she preserves his most doctrinaire claims and then declines to adopt his rigor.

## Explanation

Kosuth pushes [[Dematerialization|dematerialization]] to its analytic limit: the physical work is a mere "model," the real work is the idea ("All I make are models. The actual works of art are ideas"), and art is an "inquiry into the nature of art" whose only proper audience is other artists, so that "art becomes as 'serious' as science or philosophy." Lippard quotes all of this, but where Kosuth wants a self-referential, linguistic, tautological art purified of "visual experience," Lippard keeps "dematerialization" deliberately loose and resists "providing a new category in which disparate artists would be lumped." Crucially, the two converge on one diagnosis even as they diverge on everything else: Kosuth concedes that art's pull back toward "the traditional notion of art is the art market" because "Cash support demands 'goods,'" and Lippard's whole Postface verdict is that the [[Material vs Immaterial|escape from the object]] failed against exactly that market.

## Source Passages

Kosuth's "model" reduction (1967), his philosophy-of-art and audience-of-artists program with its market caveat (1970), and Lippard's own running unease about category and about whether [[Information|idea-and-language art]] can be told apart from criticism (1970). See `## Sources`.
