# Byung-Chul Han ↔ Slavoj Žižek

## Relationship Type

Disagreement / direct correction of a reading. In the *Fantasy* chapter of *The Agony of Eros*, [[Byung-Chul Han]] cites Slavoj Žižek's essay "Cyberspace, or The Unbearable Closure of Being" and rejects Žižek's Platonic interpretation of J. G. Ballard's short story "The Gioconda of the Twilight Noon." This is a single but substantive named engagement; Žižek is otherwise a forward-stub author in the vault (no book of his has been ingested).

## Explanation

The disagreement turns on the meaning of a single act at the climax of Ballard's story. The protagonist, Maitland, recovering from temporary blindness in a house by the sea, finds that inner dream images — a coastal landscape, a cave, a sorceress who is "the embodiment of his desire" — become more real and more desirable to him than the visible world. When a beam of light restores his sight and burns away those visions, he deliberately destroys his own eyes to get them back. *(Directly stated.)*

[[Slavoj Žižek]] reads this within an **idealist, Platonic dispositive** — the ascent out of the cave toward the sun. Han quotes Žižek's own framing: "how are we to pass from ever-changing 'false' material phenomenal reality to the true reality of Ideas?" and "from the cave in which we can perceive only shadows to the daylight in which we can catch a glimpse of the sun?" On this reading, Maitland stares into the sun, as Žižek puts it, "to view the scene in its entirety" — a movement *toward* total visibility, truth, and the light of the Idea. *(Directly stated.)*

Han rejects this. "Žižek mistakenly claims that Maitland stares into the sun hoping 'to view the scene in its entirety.' In truth, however, Maitland is following an **anti-Platonic dispositive**." For Han the gesture runs in exactly the opposite direction: not an ascent out of the cave into daylight but a deliberate *retreat into* it. "By destroying his eyes, he has made bold to withdraw from the world of truth and hypervisibility, to retreat into the cave—a twilight space of dreams and desire." *(Directly stated.)*

The correction is load-bearing for Han's larger argument in the chapter. Where Žižek makes Maitland's blinding a bid for the fullness of sight (an *increase* of visibility toward truth), Han makes it a withdrawal from visibility altogether — an embrace of the [[Negativity|negativity]] of not-seeing. This is continuous with the chapter's central thesis that [[Fantasy|fantasy]], desire, and the [[The Other|Other]] require *undefined*, atopic space, and that "hypervisibility" — the [[Positivity|positive]], high-information order of [[Transparency]] and [[Pornography (Han)|pornography]] — annihilates them: "Today's computerized high-definition imagery eliminates vagueness and ambiguity. Yet fantasy inhabits space that is undefined." Maitland's self-blinding is thus, for Han, the emblem of desire's retreat from truth and the visible into the twilight in which the Other and [[Eros|eros]] can still live. *(Interpretive synthesis.)*
