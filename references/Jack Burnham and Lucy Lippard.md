## Relationship Type

Conceptual parallel / historical convergence (NOT direct citation). Two near-simultaneous 1968 accounts — Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* and Lippard's developing argument later collected in [[Six Years]] (1973) — independently track the same trajectory: the art object losing its mass, durability, and substantiality. Burnham reads this as sculpture's object-to-system migration toward "pure energy itself" and a "systems consciousness"; Lippard reads it as the conceptual-art [[Dematerialization]] in which the idea, not the object, carries the work. The two converge on a single diagnosis from opposite ends of the medium (three-dimensional construction vs. post-studio conceptual practice).

## Explanation

In 1968 both writers were narrating a vanishing. Burnham, citing Herbert Read's characterization of postwar sculpture as a reduction to a "scribble in the air," traces a "steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's" and asks, after the fine-wire and transparent-plastic constructions, "Pure energy itself?" He frames the century's sculpture as a "dialectical tension" of "steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality," culminating in the displacement of "the cultural obsession with the art object" by "systems consciousness." This is the **same vanishing** Lippard names as [[Dematerialization]] — but where Lippard tracks the object dissolving into the document, the instruction, the proposition (see [[The Document (Conceptual Art)]], [[Conceptual Art]]), Burnham tracks it dissolving into the kinetic, luminous, and cybernetic system. Burnham even supplies a precursor to the conceptual readymade: Duchamp's Readymade is "all idea with no deliberate construction technique," and "the uniqueness of an artist can only be proven by the generative power of his ideas, not by material output" — a claim that anticipates the conceptualist primacy of idea over object that Lippard documents. The interpretive payoff is that Burnham is best read as a **precursor/parallel** to Lippard's dematerialization thesis, extending the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension into the sculptural and systems-theoretic register (and connecting to Burnham's own [[Systems Theory]] inheritance, his coinage of "systems consciousness" presaging "systems esthetics").
