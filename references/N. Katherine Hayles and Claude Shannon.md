# N. Katherine Hayles ↔ Claude Shannon

## Relationship Type

**Historical influence — and critical inheritance.** [[Claude Shannon]] is the figure whose 1948 mathematical theory of communication supplies the precise technical definition of [[Information]] that [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] builds *[[How We Became Posthuman]]* around — and against. He is not a contemporary interlocutor she debates but the historical origin point she returns to. Hayles works *from* Shannon's definition (she accepts it as exact engineering) and *against* its cultural elevation into an ontology. Crucially, her reading is double-edged in Shannon's favor: the reification of information she contests is not Shannon's doing. He repeatedly cautioned against over-extending the theory, and his victory over [[Donald MacKay]]'s meaning-based alternative was, in her account, contingent rather than inevitable.

## Explanation

The central object Hayles inherits from Shannon is a single definition: information as a probability function "with no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning." She compresses it into the formula that runs through the whole book —

> Itisa pattern, not a presence.

This is "a pattern, not a presence" — the disembodiment of information at its technical source. Everything Hayles contests downstream (the [[The Body as Data|body as data]], the downloading of consciousness, the posthuman assumption that we are essentially informational patterns) presupposes this definition. So her relationship to Shannon is foundational: he gives her the precise term whose history she then sets out to denaturalize.

Three features of the relationship are load-bearing:

1. **Hayles reads Shannon as the author of the disembodiment, but a careful one.** The decisive interpretive move in the book is that Shannon's divorce of information from meaning was a *local engineering convenience* — "an appropriate and sensible decision" in context — that later got "decontextualized" and hardened into an ideology. Hayles is explicit that this slide is not Shannon's error: "Shannon himself was meticulously careful about how he applied information theory," and "Shannon himself frequently cautioned that the theory was meant to apply only to certain technical situations, not to communication in general." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

2. **He himself cautioned against over-extending the theory.** At the eighth Macy Conference, responding to Alex Bavelas on group communication, Shannon drew the boundary in his own words — he did not see "too close a connection between the notion of information as we use it in communication engineering and what you are doing here." Hayles uses this to show that the reification of information is a *cultural appropriation* of the theory, not a claim Shannon ever made. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

3. **His victory over MacKay was contingent, not inevitable.** Shannon's formulation "was not the only proposal on the table." [[Donald MacKay]], "a British researcher, argued for an alternative definition that linked information with change in a receiver's mindset and thus with meaning" — information defined by what it *does* rather than what it *is*, which "necessarily implies context and embodiment." The Shannon definition became "the industry standard" not because it was forced but because it was quantitatively tractable while MacKay's required modeling changes in a receiver's mind. Hayles's genealogy of this contest is the historical engine of [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]]. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The net relationship: Shannon is the technical origin of the [[Material vs Immaterial|information/matter split]] the book contests, but Hayles is scrupulous to keep the engineer separate from the ideology built on his work. Her quarrel is with the reification — "a simplification necessitated by engineering considerations becomes an ideology" — not with Shannon, whose own restraint she repeatedly enlists as evidence against that ideology.

> Donald MacKay, a British researcher, argued for an alternative definition that linked information with change in a receiver’s mindset and thus with meaning. […] It is no mystery why Shannon’s definition rather than MacKay’s became the industry standard.

From "Contesting for the Body of Information," Shannon drawing the boundary himself:

> he cautioned that he did not see "too close a connection between the notion of information as we use it in communication engineering and what you are doing here."

## Confidence

**High.** The relationship rests on Hayles's repeated, named engagement with Shannon across two chapters. The four constitutive features of the definition ("no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning") and the formula "a pattern, not a presence" are quoted verbatim; Shannon's self-cautioning is documented both in Hayles's narration ("meticulously careful," "frequently cautioned") and in his own quoted words at the Macy Conference; the contingency of his victory over MacKay is stated directly. The only interpretive layer — reading Shannon's restraint as exculpatory, so that the book's target is the reification rather than the engineer — is itself something Hayles states, not something synthesized here.
