# Shoshana Zuboff ↔ Hannah Arendt

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation and historical influence.** Hannah Arendt is one of the load-bearing philosophical authorities of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*: [[Shoshana Zuboff]] names and quotes her across at least four chapters and builds two of the book's central moves on Arendt's work. (1) The constructive foundation of [[The Right to the Future Tense]] is taken directly from Arendt's account of **will** as "the organ for the future" and of **promises** as "islands of predictability" in an "ocean of uncertainty." (2) Arendt is the historian of totalitarian power whose method and warnings Zuboff repurposes to characterize the *unprecedented* new power she calls [[Instrumentarian Power]] — including Arendt's prophetic worry that behaviorism "could become true." (3) Arendt supplies the moral register of the book's closing call to resistance: "anger and indignation" and "the faculty of freedom itself, the sheer capacity to begin." This is documented citation, not vault synthesis. Arendt remains a **forward-stub** author (no book of hers is in `raw/`), so per policy her author page is not realized here; this reference note records the relationship from Zuboff's side, grounded in direct quotation.

## Explanation

**Arendt as the ground of the right to the future tense (Ch. 11).** Zuboff's foundational concept rests on an Arendtian theory of the will. To make a promise, Zuboff writes, "is to predict the future; to fulfill a promise through the exercise of will turns that prediction into fact" — and she immediately credits the source: "The philosopher Hannah Arendt devoted an entire volume to an examination of will as the 'organ for the future' in the same way that memory is our mental organ for the past." (The "entire volume" is Arendt's *The Life of the Mind*, vol. 2, *Willing*.) The crucial point Zuboff draws from Arendt is that the future is constitutively *uncertain* — "the future's main characteristic is its basic uncertainty, no matter how high a degree of probability prediction may attain" — and that freedom of will is what lets us undertake action "entirely contingent on our determination," acts we "could have 'left undone.'" Arendt's conclusion, quoted verbatim, becomes Zuboff's premise: "A will that is not free … is a contradiction in terms." From Arendt's image of promises as "islands of predictability" and "guideposts of reliability" in an "ocean of uncertainty," Zuboff derives her thesis that human beings, not prediction machines, are the rightful authors of the future — that uncertainty is the necessary medium of freedom (see the argument [[There is no freedom without uncertainty]] and the tension [[Inevitability vs Contingency]]). (`Directly stated`)

**Arendt as the analyst of an unprecedented power (Ch. 12).** When Zuboff insists that [[Surveillance Capitalism]] cannot be understood through familiar categories, she takes Arendt's study of totalitarianism as the model for confronting the genuinely new. She quotes Arendt's naming of "the great evil" — "Totalitarianism has discovered a means of dominating and terrorizing human beings from within" — and recounts that Arendt spent "the six years after World War II writing her extraordinary study of totalitarian power, published in 1951 as *The Origins of Totalitarianism*." Arendt's achievement of comprehension in the face of an unprecedented form of power is the methodological precedent for Zuboff's own attempt to name [[Instrumentarian Power]] as something the existing vocabulary (Big Brother, totalitarianism, the Panopticon) cannot capture. (`Directly stated`)

**Arendt's prophecy of behaviorism turned against instrumentarianism (Ch. 13).** The relationship sharpens into substantive argument when Zuboff enlists Arendt directly against the behaviorist core of instrumentarian power. "Arendt anticipated the destructive potential of behaviorism decades ago," Zuboff writes, and quotes from *The Human Condition* Arendt's warning about "the society of jobholders" that "demands of its members a sheer automatic functioning," culminating in the line Zuboff makes pivotal: "The trouble with modern theories of behaviorism is not that they are wrong but that they could become true … the best possible conceptualization of certain obvious trends in modern society." For Zuboff, [[Big Other]] and surveillance capitalism's "actuation" capabilities are precisely the instruments that make behaviorism "become true" — Skinner's vortex of stimulus-response-reinforcement finally operationalized. Arendt feared a future where "thought" devolves to a "brain" "transferable to 'electronic instruments'"; Zuboff argues that future has arrived as the engineering of behavior that replaces the engineering of souls. (`Directly stated` for the citation; `Strongly implied` for Zuboff's claim that instrumentarianism is the realization of Arendt's "could become true.")

**Arendt as the source of moral resistance (Ch. 18).** In her closing chapter Zuboff returns to Arendt for the ethical and political stance the book demands. Against the "message of inevitability that is power's velvet-gloved right hand" (see [[Inevitabilism]]), she quotes Arendt: "the natural human reaction to such conditions is one of anger and indignation because these conditions are against the dignity of man." And against fatalism she invokes Arendt's faith in new beginnings — "every beginning, seen from the perspective of the framework that it interrupts, is a miracle" — and the passage Zuboff offers as the book's note of hope: "What usually remains intact in the epochs of petrification and foreordained doom is the faculty of freedom itself, the sheer capacity to begin." Arendt thus brackets the whole argument: the *capacity to will an uncertain future* that grounds the right to the future tense at the start, and the *capacity to begin* that authorizes resistance at the end, are the same Arendtian faculty of freedom. (`Directly stated`)

The throughline is that Zuboff borrows from Arendt a single conviction in two registers — diagnostic and normative. Diagnostically, freedom requires uncertainty, and a power that manufactures certainty ("guaranteed outcomes," "certainty without terror") therefore abolishes freedom at its root. Normatively, the human faculty of beginning persists even under "foreordained doom," which is why instrumentarian power must be resisted rather than accepted as inevitable. Where Zuboff *departs* from Arendt is in the object of analysis: Arendt's power is the *state* converted into a project of total possession through terror; Zuboff's is the *market* converted into a project of total certainty without terror. She is explicit that instrumentarianism is "the precise antithesis of Orwell's Big Brother" and not a species of totalitarianism — so Arendt is invoked as the indispensable precursor in method and ethics, not as a template to be applied unchanged. (`Directly stated` for the totalitarian/instrumentarian distinction.)
