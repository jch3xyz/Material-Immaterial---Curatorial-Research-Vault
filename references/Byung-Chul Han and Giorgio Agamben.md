# Byung-Chul Han and Giorgio Agamben

## Relationship Type

Direct citation in the mode of **immanent critique and disagreement** — borrowing-and-inversion. Across *The Burnout Society* Han names Agamben repeatedly, quotes him verbatim, summarizes his positions fairly, and takes over two of his central conceptual instruments — the *homo sacer* / [[Bare Life|bare life]] couple and the Aristotelian *potenza di non* ([[Negative Potency|negative potency]], the "potential not to") — only to turn each one **against** Agamben's own use of it. Han's recurring charge is that Agamben holds to "the scheme of negativity" and so commits an *anachronism*: he describes a vanished society of sovereignty from a standpoint inside the society of achievement, and therefore cannot grasp "the violence of positivity." The relationship is therefore not shared tradition or homage but a sustained polemic conducted *through* Agamben's own vocabulary. *Giorgio Agamben has no book in the library*; his positions here are mediated entirely by Han's citation and paraphrase, so this note records Han's reading, not a two-sided exchange.

## Explanation

Han's critique of Agamben runs along three fronts, all driven by the same thesis: Agamben thinks the contemporary world in terms of *negativity* (exclusion, exception, the killable life produced by sovereign power), whereas Han argues that late-modern [[Achievement Society|achievement society]] runs on *[[Positivity|positivity]]* (inclusion, totalized normality, the un-killable life that must be preserved at any cost). What Agamben reads as the expansion of negativity, Han reads as its disappearance under a positivization that absorbs everything.

**(1) The Bartleby polemic (Ch. 7, "The Bartleby Case").** Against Agamben's "ontotheological interpretation" of Melville's [[Bartleby]] — in which the scrivener's "I would prefer not to" embodies "pure, absolute potentiality" (*potentia absoluta*), the "extreme figure of the Nothing from which all creation derives," an "Angel of Annunciation" heralding a second Creation or *de-creation* (*Ent-Schöpfung*) — Han offers a **pathological** reading. Bartleby's phrase "expresses neither the [[Negative Potency|negative potency]] of not-to nor the instinct for delay and deferral that is essential for 'spirituality.' Rather, it stands for a lack of drive and for apathy." The "Story of Wall-Street" is "not a tale of de-creation [*Ent-Schöpfung*], but rather a story of exhaustion [*Erschöpfung*]." Agamben's reading "already founders on the facts of the narrative" (Bartleby refuses every "errand," stares into the "dead wall," works the Dead Letter Office, dies in the "Tombs"), and it "fails to take note of the change of mental structure [*psychischer Strukturwandel*] in the present day" — Bartleby is still an obedience-subject of disciplinary society, not the depressive achievement-subject. The standalone argument note is [[Bartleby is a story of exhaustion not de-creation]]. (`Directly stated` — Ch. 7)

**(2) Homo sacer / bare life, universalized then inverted (Ch. 5, "Vita Activa"; Ch. 9, "Burnout Society").** Han accepts Agamben's account of [[Bare Life|bare life]] as a faithful summary — "[a]ccording to Giorgio Agamben, homo sacer stands for absolutely expendable life," exemplified by camp inmates, Guantanamo prisoners, the undocumented, and patients "rotting away in intensive care," a life produced by sovereignty in the [[State of Exception|state of exception]] — and then makes two decisive moves on it. First he **universalizes** it: "[i]f late-modern achievement society has reduced us all to bare life, then it is not just people at the margins or in a state of exception... but all of us, without exception, who are *homines sacri*." Then he **inverts the predicate**: this bare life "cannot be killed absolutely [*absolut untötbar (ist)*]. It is undead, so to speak. Here the word *sacer* does not mean 'accursed' but 'holy.'" Where Agamben's *homo sacer* is killable with impunity, Han's is the un-killable holy life that "must be preserved at any cost," "even barer than the life of homo sacer." Ch. 9 completes the reversal of agency: the injunction that makes us *homines sacri* "is not a sovereign injunction, but the injunction to achieve [*Bann der Leistung*]," so that "[t]he sovereign of achievement society, then, is the *homo sacer* of his own self" — the standalone argument [[The achievement-subject is the homo sacer of its own self]]. Han faults Agamben for "anachronism": Agamben "utterly fails to notice the topological change of power" from sovereignty to achievement; his perpetrator/victim, sovereign/homo-sacer scheme is "topologically" clean and negative, whereas in achievement society the two coincide in one self-exploiting subject. (`Directly stated` — Ch. 5, Ch. 9)

**(3) The vanishing state of exception (Ch. 6, "The Pedagogy of Seeing").** Here Han contradicts the keystone of Agamben's *State of Exception* directly. "Agamben overlooks this growing positivity. Counter to his diagnosis — that the state of exception is undergoing expansion and turning into the state of normality — general social positivization now is absorbing every state of exception." For Han the very prominence of the concept is evidence of its *disappearance*, not its triumph: "[b]ecause the world is being increasingly positivized, more attention is paid to concepts such as 'the state of exception' or 'immunitas.' However, such attention offers no proof for their actuality; rather, it shows that they are vanishing." Where Agamben sees the exception becoming the rule, Han sees normality being *totalized* so that no exception (and no rage, dread, or mourning — all "negative feelings") can arise. (`Directly stated` — Ch. 6)

**The shared instrument Han keeps while turning it.** Notably, Han does not simply reject Agamben; he **adopts** Agamben's *potenza di non* (negative potency, the Aristotelian "potential not to," *adynamia*) as a genuine and valuable category — it is the active core of his own *vita contemplativa* — and *then* reads it pathologically against him. Agamben supplies the vocabulary of the *not-to*; Han uses that vocabulary to convict Agamben of misreading Bartleby, insisting that Bartleby exhibits apathy and impotence ("the inability to act," "tied to something" it cannot do), not the sovereign power of the *not-to*. The conceptual home of this borrowing-and-inversion is [[Negative Potency]]; the dispute over its application to Bartleby lives here. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Source Passages

The Bartleby polemic — apathy, not negative potency or *potentia absoluta*; exhaustion, not de-creation:

> his signature phrase, "I would prefer not to," expresses neither the negative potency of not-to nor the instinct for delay and deferral that is essential for "spirituality." Rather, it stands for a lack of drive and for apathy, which seal Bartleby's doom.

> Agamben's ontotheological interpretation of "Bartleby," which pays no attention to pathological elements, already founders on the facts of the narrative. It also fails to take note of the change of mental structure [psychischer Strukturwandel] in the present day.

> This "Story of Wall-Street" is not a tale of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung], but rather a story of exhaustion [Erschöpfung].

Bare life — universalized, then inverted into the un-killable, holy, undead:

> If late-modern achievement society has reduced us all to bare life, then it is not just people at the margins or in a state of exception—that is, the excluded—but all of us, without exception, who are homines sacri.

> That said, this bare life has the particularity of not being absolutely expendable [tötbar]; rather, it cannot be killed absolutely [absolut untötbar (ist)]. It is undead, so to speak. Here the word sacer does not mean "accursed" but "holy." Now bare, sheer life itself is holy, and so it must be preserved at any cost.

The *Bann der Leistung* — the homo sacer of one's own self, and the charge of anachronism:

> The injunction that makes us all homines sacri is not a sovereign injunction, but the injunction to achieve [Bann der Leistung].

> The sovereign of achievement society, then, is the homo sacer of his own self.

> Agamben utterly fails to notice the topological change of power that lies at the basis of the society of sovereignty's transformation into achievement society. From a standpoint at the middle of the society of achievement, Agamben describes the society of sovereignty. Therein lies the anachronism of his thinking.

The vanishing state of exception — positivization absorbs the exception rather than expanding it:

> Agamben overlooks this growing positivity. Counter to his diagnosis—that the state of exception is undergoing expansion and turning into the state of normality— general social positivization now is absorbing every state of exception. That is, conditions of normality are being totalized.

## Confidence

**High** for the disagreement itself and for all three fronts: Agamben is named in every relevant chapter, quoted verbatim (the "philosophical constellation" / "pure, absolute potentiality" passage on Bartleby, plus Han's summaries of *Homo Sacer* and *State of Exception*), and explicitly contradicted ("Agamben overlooks," "Agamben utterly fails to notice," "founders on the facts of the narrative," "anachronism"). High, too, for the borrowing-and-inversion structure: Han demonstrably keeps the *homo sacer* / bare-life couple and the *potenza di non* while reversing their predicates and their application. **Medium** for any claim about Agamben's own position beyond what Han reports: because *Giorgio Agamben has no book in the library*, his views (the ontotheological Bartleby, *potentia absoluta*, the exception-becoming-rule thesis) are known here only through Han's citation and paraphrase. Whether Han's universalization of bare life ("all of us, without exception") is in fact *closer* to Agamben's own "the exception has become the rule" than his polemic admits is left open — it awaits a future [[Giorgio Agamben]] author-section to adjudicate. **Forward-stubs preserved per policy:** [[Giorgio Agamben]] (author, no book in `raw/`), [[Homo Sacer]] and [[State of Exception]] (Agamben-owned concepts), [[Bartleby]] and [[Herman Melville]] (the tale and its author, no source in `raw/`).
