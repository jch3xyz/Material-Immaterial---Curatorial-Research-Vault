# Byung-Chul Han and Richard Sennett

## Relationship Type

Direct citation that turns into a sharp disagreement within a shared diagnosis. In the title chapter of *The Burnout Society* ("Burnout Society"), Han quotes Richard Sennett twice — at length and in quotation marks — and credits him with correctly connecting contemporary psychic disturbance to narcissism and the absent relation to the Other. But Han then explicitly states that Sennett "draws the wrong conclusions," inverting Sennett's account of the failure of [[Narrativity|closure]]. Where Sennett holds that the narcissist *intentionally avoids* closure (because an objectified, finished form would exist independently of the self and so threaten it), Han holds the opposite: closure has become *socially impossible* under contemporary relations of production, and it is this impossibility — not any avoidance — that drives the subject into endless narcissistic self-repetition. The relationship is thus direct citation in the service of disagreement, not endorsement; Sennett supplies the symptom (narcissism, the crisis of gratification) and the conceptual vocabulary (Erfahrung/Erlebnis), while Han supplies a different cause (the economic foreclosure of the definitive *Werk*).

## Explanation

Han's engagement with Sennett structures the central diagnostic move of the title chapter. Having argued that "freedom from the Other switches into narcissistic self-relation," which "occasions many of the psychic disturbances afflicting today's achievement-subject," and that the absence of the Other "causes a crisis of gratification," Han turns to Sennett as a fellow witness: "Richard Sennett has also traced the gratification crisis to a narcissistic disturbance and the absent relation to the Other" (`Directly stated`). He then quotes Sennett on narcissism as "the very opposite of strong selflove," in which "erasing the line between self and other means that nothing new, nothing 'other,' ever enters the self," and on the narcissist who "is not hungry for experiences, he is hungry for Experience." It is through this Sennett passage that Han reaches the chapter's [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)|Erfahrung/Erlebnis]] distinction — *Experience* [Erfahrung] "involves encountering the Other" and "alters," whereas *Experiencing* [Erlebnis] "expands the ego into the Other" and "com-pares." So far the two thinkers converge (`Directly stated`).

The break comes over the cause of non-closure. Han quotes Sennett on the "lack of 'closure'": the narcissist avoids "the sense of having reached a goal" because "the experiences would then be objectified; they would have a shape, a form, and so exist independently of oneself," weakening a self that "is real only if it is continuous." Han then explicitly rejects the inference: "Sennett rightly connects contemporary psychic disturbances to narcissism, but he draws the wrong conclusions." Against Sennett's *intentional avoidance* — "Sennett maintains that the narcissistic individual intentionally avoids achieving goals" — Han asserts "precisely the opposite": "The socially conditioned impossibility of objectively valid, definitive forms of closure drives the subject into narcissistic self-repetition." It is "not a matter of intentionally 'avoiding' the achievement of goals." Rather, "the feeling of having achieved a goal never occurs": "It is not that the narcissistic subject does not want to achieve closure. Rather, it is incapable of getting there. It loses itself and scatters itself into the open" (`Directly stated`).

The economic ground of the disagreement is Han's own addition. The impossibility of closure, for Han, "depends, not least of all, on economic factors: openness and inconclusiveness favor growth." This connects the dispute back to the chapter's earlier claim that "[a] definitive work [Werk], as the result of completed labor [Arbeit], is no longer possible today" because "contemporary relations of production stand in the way of conclusion" — one "works into the open," and "[c]onclusive forms [Abschlußformen] with a beginning and an end prove wanting." The crisis of gratification, the failure to "achieve gestalt, stable self-image, or character," and ultimately [[Burnout|burnout]] itself, follow from this foreclosure of the finished work rather than from a self protecting itself by refusing to finish. Where Sennett locates the lack of closure in the *psychology* of the narcissist, Han relocates it in the *economy*: this is the same gesture by which, elsewhere in the chapter, he faults Alain Ehrenberg for analyzing depression "exclusively in terms of psychology" while failing "to consider economic context" (`Interpretive synthesis` — Han does not name the Ehrenberg parallel as such, but the structure of his correction of Sennett mirrors it).
