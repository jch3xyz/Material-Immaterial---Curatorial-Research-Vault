# Byung-Chul Han and Guy Debord

## Relationship Type

**Direct citation, with a broader conceptual inheritance that is interpretive synthesis.** The relationship is asymmetrical. [[Byung-Chul Han]] cites [[Guy Debord]] **directly and by name** at exactly one pivot: the closing sentence of the pornography chapter of *[[The Transparency Society]]* — "The society of pornography is a society of the spectacle" — which binds Han's [[Pornography (Han)|porno-society]] to Debord's [[Spectacle]]. Beyond that single citation, the deeper alignment (Han's [[Transparency]] and [[Exhibition Value]] as heirs of the [[Spectacle]]) is a cross-reading the vault performs, not a genealogy Debord could have drawn: Debord wrote *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]* in 1967, decades before Han. Debord grounds the pole; Han inherits and mutates it.

## Explanation

Debord's 1967 theses lay down the ontological floor on which Han's later diagnosis stands. The [[Spectacle]] is defined not as an image-glut but as a mediation: "The spectacle is not a collection of images; it is a social relation between people that is mediated by images" (thesis 4). It is a regime of pure surface — "an affirmation of appearances and an identification of all human social life with appearances" (thesis 10) — driven by a historical descent through the value-forms of the self: being degrades into having, and having, in the completed spectacle, into appearing (thesis 17). The commodity is the substance of this world, and the spectacle its "general expression" (thesis 42); it is "money one can only look at" (thesis 49), the point at which all use has been exchanged for "the totality of abstract representation."

Han's vocabulary descends directly from this floor. Where Debord names the shift to *appearing*, Han names its late-capitalist intensification: total [[Transparency]], compulsory self-[[Exhibition Value|exhibition]], the smooth pornographic image stripped of secret and hermeneutic depth. Han's [[Exhibition Value]] is the [[Reification|reified]] commodity-appearance that Debord's spectacle "materialized" as an "objective reality"; Han's porno-society is the spectacle's libidinal-aesthetic face. The single explicit citation makes the debt legible: when Han writes that pornographic, "deculturalized images … empty out into spectacle. The society of pornography is a society of the spectacle," he is reading his own diagnosis back into Debord's category. (Label: Directly stated for Han's citation; Interpretive synthesis for the broader inheritance — Debord does not cite Han and could not; the transparency/exhibition-value alignment is the vault's cross-reading.)

The registers differ, and the difference is the interest of the pairing. Debord's spectacle is **politico-economic**: a phase of the commodity economy, the "concrete inversion of life, an autonomous movement of the nonliving," and finally a machinery that "systematically destroys the 'faculty of encounter'" and imprisons the spectator "in a flattened universe bounded by the screen of the spectacle" (theses 217–218). Han's transparency is **libidinal-aesthetic and psychopolitical**: it works less by imposing a passive contemplation from outside than by soliciting voluntary self-exposure from within. Debord's spectator is *shown* an inaccessible world he can only look at; Han's subject *displays himself* into a transparency he has learned to want. Both diagnose the eclipse of lived experience by representation — but Debord locates the culprit in the separation the economy enforces, Han in the positivity the subject enacts.

## The society of transparency is a society of the spectacle

(Folded here as a labeled sub-section rather than as a separate argument note — it is the through-line of this reference, not a standalone thesis.)

Read against Debord, Han's [[The Transparency Society|Transparency Society]] is legible as the spectacle completed by other means. Debord's thesis 17 already contains the trajectory: the "general shift from having to appearing," in which "all 'having' must now derive its immediate prestige and its ultimate purpose from appearances." Han's transparency society is the terminus of that shift — a world in which appearing is no longer the prestige of having but the sole and compulsory mode of existence, and in which the affirmation of appearances (thesis 10) has become an affirmation of positivity that "systematically destroys the 'faculty of encounter'" (thesis 217) exactly as the [[Hell of the Same]] destroys the Other. Debord's flattened universe "bounded by the screen of the spectacle" (thesis 218) reappears in Han as the smooth, un-interrupting, [[Pornography (Han)|pornographic]] screen. The proposition holds most strictly where Han himself draws it — pornography = spectacle — and loosens into interpretive synthesis as it is extended across transparency and exhibition value. (Label: Directly stated at the pornography pivot; Interpretive synthesis for the generalization to transparency.)

## Source Passages

Debord grounds the pole (theses 4, 10, 17, 42, 49, and the encounter-destroying theses 217–218); Han's single direct citation of Debord is the closing line of the *Transparency Society* pornography chapter, already carried in [[Pornography (Han)]]:

> Pornographic, deculturalized images offer nothing to read. They function like advertisements—by direct, tactile, and infectious means. They are posthermeneutic. … They empty out into spectacle. The society of pornography is a society of the spectacle.
> — Han, *The Transparency Society*, ch. 4 (see [[Pornography (Han)]], Source 9)

## Confidence

**High.** The direct-citation half is unambiguous and verbatim: Han names the spectacle and binds his porno-society to it in *The Transparency Society* ch. 4 (quoted in full in [[Pornography (Han)]]). The Debord-side theses below are copied character-for-character from the Ken Knabb translation in `raw/`, with thesis numbers verified against the source.
