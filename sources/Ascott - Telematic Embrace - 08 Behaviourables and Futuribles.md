# Source Summary: Behaviourables and Futuribles

**Book:** [[Telematic Embrace]]
**Author:** [[Roy Ascott]]

## Orientation

"Behaviourables and Futuribles" (1967) is one of Ascott's earliest and most concentrated manifestos, produced as a poster-sized broadside and reprinted in *Control 5* (1970). It is a primary-text statement of his 1960s [[Behaviourist Art]] position — the participatory, feedback-driven pole of 1960s cybernetic art that runs alongside Jack Burnham's object-side [[Systems Esthetics]]. Where Burnham theorizes the migration of the art object into a system through sculpture's internal history, Ascott theorizes the artwork as a unit of *behaviour*: a cybernetic system completed by the participating spectator. The manifesto's compressed keynote — "Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure" — programmatically inverts the modernist art object, and the text coins two new categories of art-thing to do it.

The first is the *behaviourable* (the artwork read as behaviour, as a process in its history); the second is the *futurible* (the artwork as a speculative, anticipatory projection of possible futures — "the delineation of futuribles"). The art object is also a *trigger*: "a behaviourable in its history, a futurible in its structure, a trigger in its effect." Ascott then performs a striking adaptation of McLuhan, importing the hot/cool distinction and reframing it as the "thermodynamics of an artwork" ([[The Thermodynamics of an Artwork]]): a "hot" work is densely-packed, over-determined, and one-way (low feedback), while a "cool" work is loosely-stacked, ambiguous, and entropic, and so invites the observer to participate — Ascott's information-theoretic restatement of his participatory esthetic, and the keystone bridge to McLuhan's [[Media Hot and Cold]] and the vault's [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] tension.

The manifesto's rhetoric is aphoristic and exclamatory ("Object hustlers! Reduce your anxiety!"; "Message ends."). It advances the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] (CAM) as "essentially a futurible," calls for art schools "structured as homeostatic organisms," and — in its most prophetic move — gestures toward what will become Ascott's later technoetics: the claim that the post-industrial future will be carried "not [by] technology... so much as [by] psychotechnology" ([[Psychotechnology]]), taking us "beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering" into ESP, parapsychology, and "total telepathy." This 1967 essay is the documentable seed of that psi/technoetic arc, embedded in a cybernetic-art text. It closes by restating its thesis: "Art is now a form of behaviour."

## Key Concepts

- [[Behaviourist Art]] — art understood as behaviour: a feedback-driven process completed by the participating spectator; the canonical Ascott concept (a "behaviourable" is the artwork read as behaviour-in-its-history). The explicit counter-pole to Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]].
- [[Futuribles]] — the artwork (and CAM) as a speculative, anticipatory projection of possible/alternative futures; "the delineation of futuribles."
- [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — Ascott's projected institution fusing art, learning, and behaviour; "essentially a futurible — anticipatory and speculative."
- [[Psychotechnology]] — the post-industrial successor to technology, carrying culture "beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering" into the parapsychological; the 1967 seed of Ascott's later technoetics.
- [[New Media Art]] — Ascott's behaviourist/participatory lineage extends this Burnham-realized concept.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — Ascott's behaviour-as-art / participation-as-medium thesis extends this Burnham-realized concept.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the participatory, feedback-completed artwork; the observer who participates, "projecting his own sense of order."
- [[Feedback Loops]] — "feedback in the system artifact/observer"; "feedback of a high order is possible."
- [[Object and System]] — "system supersedes structure" / "software predominates over hardware" as the behaviourist twin of Burnham's object→system spine.
- [[Media Hot and Cold]] — Ascott's "thermodynamics of an artwork" is an art-specific adaptation of McLuhan's hot/cool media.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the pervasive frame: "the cybernetic era," "the cybernetic age is an age of silences."

## Key Arguments

- [[Process replaces product just as system supersedes structure]] — when art is a form of behaviour, software/process/system displaces hardware/product/structure (*Directly stated*).
- [[Behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object]] — process culture revalues rather than abolishes the object: "the behaviourist art object will come to be read like the palm of your hand" (*Directly stated*, rhetorically hedged — Ascott keeps the object, distinguishing him from Lippard's dematerialization).
- [[The post-industrial future will be carried by psychotechnology]] — not technology but psychotechnology will "carry us through," beyond behavioural engineering into the psychic/parapsychological (*Directly stated*; its content is visionary/speculative).

## Candidate Essay Uses

- Pairing Ascott's behaviour/participation pole against Burnham's object→system pole to map the two faces of 1960s cybernetic art — and to argue that "process replaces product" and "object to system" are the same dematerializing move read from different ends.
- Tracing the "thermodynamics of an artwork" as an information-theoretic translation of McLuhan's hot/cool, and using it to theorize participatory/interactive media as cool, low-determinacy, feedback-rich systems.
- Reading the 1967 "psychotechnology / total telepathy" passage as the documentable seed of Ascott's later technoetics — and as a bridge to O'Gieblyn's [[Re-enchantment]] thread on the parapsychological residue inside technological futures.
- Using "behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object" to complicate a naive narrative that all 1960s systems/behaviour art simply dematerializes the object (contrast Lippard).
