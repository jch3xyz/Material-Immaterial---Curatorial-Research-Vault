# Source Summary: Burnout Society

**Book:** [[The Burnout Society]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/09 Burnout Society]]

## Orientation

This is the title chapter of *The Burnout Society* (*Müdigkeitsgesellschaft*, 2010), and it is the primary source for much of the Han vocabulary the vault first grounded secondhand through *The Transparency Society* and *Psychopolitics*. The chapter mounts a sustained intrapsychic and politico-economic argument: the disciplinary society (Freud, Kant — built on the negativity of prohibition, repression, and the "Should") has given way to an achievement society built on the positivity of "Can," in which the obedience-subject becomes a self-projecting achievement-subject. Han opens by rewriting Kafka's "Prometheus" as an intrapsychic scene of self-exploitation — the eagle that eats the ever-regrowing liver is read as the subject's own alter ego — and from there develops the thesis that the achievement-subject is bound precisely where it deems itself free.

The rhetorical posture is polemical and corrective. Han argues largely against two interlocutors who, in his view, misdiagnose the present by clinging to the negativity scheme: Alain Ehrenberg (*The Weariness of the Self*), whose depression-as-failed-sovereignty thesis still holds to the conflict model and the unconscious, and Richard Sennett, who correctly links contemporary disturbance to narcissism but draws the wrong conclusion about closure. Against both, Han insists that depression, burnout, and ADHD are maladies of excess positivity, not repression — psychoanalysis cannot reach them because they involve no unconscious, no conflict, no transference. He traces the mechanism whereby the repressive superego positivizes into the seductive ego ideal, subjection becomes projection, and "the project turns out to be a projectile."

The chapter culminates in a re-reading of Giorgio Agamben's *homo sacer* / bare life. Han keeps the figure but rejects Agamben's negativity scheme — the topological separation of sovereign and victim, the state of exception. In achievement society the injunction is not sovereign but the injunction to achieve (*Bann der Leistung*), and the achievement-subject becomes "the homo sacer of his own self": perpetrator and victim, master and slave, *homo liber* and *homo sacer* collapsed into one. The closing pages diagnose the doping society — the absolutization of survival, bare life stripped of narrativity and teleology, the mania for health ("Health is the new goddess"), and the achievement-subject as one of the undead, "too alive to die, and too dead to live."

## Key Concepts

- [[Achievement Society]] — the late-modern society of self-exploiting achievement-subjects that supersedes disciplinary society; "a society of freedom" governed by Can; this chapter is its primary source.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — exploitation without domination; the exploiter is simultaneously the exploited, and the feeling of freedom that attends it makes it more efficient.
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — exploitation by another (external domination), which capitalism switches away from in order to accelerate.
- [[Can vs Should]] — the modal shift from the Freudian/Kantian "Should" of the obedience-subject to the "Can" of achievement society.
- [[Positivity]] — the defining structural feature of achievement society; the maladies of the era indicate "an excess of positivity."
- [[Negativity]] — the disappearing principle of the disciplinary order (prohibition, repression, conflict, the unconscious, character, the Other).
- [[The Project (Han)]] — the achievement-subject "liberates itself into a project," is no longer "subject" in the etymological sense; the project becomes a projectile.
- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — auto-compulsion that poses as freedom; "Freedom and violence now coincide."
- [[Narrativity]] — the reduction of life to bare vital functioning "strips it of all narrativity."
- [[Gleichschaltung]] — the consensual conformism behind the violence of positivity (de-ideologization, "the violence of consensus").
- [[Doping Society]] — the inner logic of achievement society dictating its evolution into a doping society; bare life maximized "by any and all means."
- [[Exhibition Value]] — the ego "exhibited as a commodity," with social-network "friends" as attention-granting consumers.
- [[Burnout]] — the title malady; the pathological consequence of voluntary self-exploitation that often precedes depression.

## Key Arguments

- [[The achievement-subject is the homo sacer of its own self]] — Han adopts Agamben's *homo sacer* but rejects his negativity scheme; the *homo liber* who is his own sovereign becomes the homo sacer of his own self.
- [[Depression is caused by the imperative to achieve not the failure to become oneself]] — against Ehrenberg, depression/burnout are maladies of excess positivity and self-exploitation, not the exhausted sovereignty of a self that fails to become itself.

## Important Definitions

- [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)]] — articulated here via Sennett: Experience [Erfahrung] involves encountering the Other and alters; Experiencing [Erlebnis] expands the ego into the world and "com-pares."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[Prometheus and the Eagle]] — Kafka's bound Prometheus as the achievement-subject; the eagle eating the ever-regrowing liver as the subject's own alter ego, a relation of self-exploitation.
- [[The Project as Projectile]] — "The project turns out to be a projectile that the achievement-subject is aiming at itself."
- [[The Like Button]] — social-network "friends" who heighten narcissism by granting attention, as consumers, to the ego exhibited as a commodity.

## Major Tensions

- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the master tension of the chapter: excess positivity (affirmation, Can, exhaustion, inclusion) vs negativity (prohibition, repression, conflict, exclusion).
- [[Discipline vs Achievement]] — the obedience-subject of disciplinary society vs the achievement-subject; Should vs Can.
- [[Subject vs Project]] — subjection (to the superego/Other) vs projection (onto the ego ideal).
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — auto-compulsion posing as freedom; freedom and violence coincide.
- [[I-Tiredness vs We-Tiredness]] — solitary, worldless "I-tiredness" vs the healing "tiredness that trusts in the world" (in nascent Kafka form here).

## Candidate Essay Uses

- The artwork-as-self-exploitation angle: reading the figure of the artist/curator who "works for pleasure" and self-projects into an unattainable ego ideal as the achievement-subject whose project becomes a projectile — a frame for fatigue, "hustle," and the dissolution of conclusive forms (the unfinishable *Werk*) in contemporary studio and curatorial practice.
- Surveillance/embodiment thread: pairing the "homo sacer of his own self" with *Psychopolitics* and *Transparency Society* to argue that self-exploitation and voluntary self-exposure are the same positivized structure of power — domination that no longer needs an external dominator.
- Material/immaterial thread: "Health is the new goddess" and the absolutization of survival as the reduction of the living body to bare vital function and exhibition/health value — life "as flat as a coin," stripped of narrativity.
- A critique-of-critique angle: Han's method of accepting a concept (Agamben's *homo sacer*, Ehrenberg's diagnosis, Sennett's narcissism) while inverting its scheme — a model for curatorial argument that re-reads inherited frameworks against their own negativity bias.
