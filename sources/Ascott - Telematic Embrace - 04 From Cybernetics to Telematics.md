## Orientation

This chapter is **Edward A. Shanken's long editorial introduction** to *Telematic Embrace*, the collected writings of Roy Ascott. As such it is not Ascott's prose but a curator-historian's orientation to his entire oeuvre: it periodizes Ascott's career, builds its genealogy and context, and both defends and critiques his utopianism. For the vault it is the **bridge document** of the Ascott ingest — the single densest cross-link node — because Shanken explicitly names the figures already in the vault (Burnham, McLuhan, Hayles, Lippard, Baudrillard) and maps which Ascott essay develops which durable concept, so the concept notes can be anchored in Ascott's own later chapters rather than over-built from this intro. (Confidence: Directly stated — from the chapter's role and content.)

Shanken organizes Ascott's career into two great phases. The first is the 1960s **cybernetic / behaviourist art** program: the artwork reconceived as a feedback system the spectator completes; the Change Paintings; the cybernetic art matrix as a social-pedagogical system; and the cybernetic art teaching at Ealing and Ipswich. The second is the 1980s–2000s **telematic art** program: computer-mediated networked art, distributed authorship, the "telematic embrace," and an emergent networked or planetary consciousness. The introduction also gestures toward the late technoetic / "moist" / shamanic phase. Shanken's running thesis is that the **conceptual idea** of Ascott's work — that networked telecommunications can produce a consciousness "greater than the sum of its parts" — is itself part of the artwork. (Confidence: Directly stated — Shanken's periodization and thesis are explicit in the chapter.)

Rhetorically, the chapter does three things at once: it **historicizes** (inserting telematic art into the parallel histories of kinetic, conceptual, mail, video, and satellite/computer-conferencing art, and tracing telecommunications-as-medium from Moholy-Nagy and Brecht forward), it **theorizes** (Wiener/Ashby cybernetics; second-order cybernetics and the observer→participator turn; Barthes and the dissolution of the author; the form/content/process debate), and it **adjudicates** (staging Ascott's liberatory claims against Baudrillard's media pessimism and the skeptical counter-image of the screen as prison or narcissistic mirror). It is Shanken, not Ascott, who weighs these — a point that matters for provenance: the cleanest *Ascott* sentences here are the few Shanken quotes from Ascott's own essays. (Confidence: Directly stated for the chapter's structure; Interpretive synthesis for the "three things at once" framing.)

## Key Concepts

- [[Telematic Art]] — Ascott's keystone form: art whose medium is computer-mediated telecommunications networks, liberated from the unique physical object and location; oriented by this intro, anchored across the book.
- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the title figure and concept: a planetary "embrace" of minds united through networked telecommunications, carrying Ascott's theory of love / passionate attraction.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the emergent, distributed field of mind that telematic exchange produces; Shanken distinguishes it from individual-phenomenology "cyberspace."
- [[Behaviourist Art]] — the 1960s program: art as behaviour rather than object — an interactive, feedback-driven context the spectator completes.
- [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — Ascott's proposed self-regulating educational/social feedback system, "the embryonic structure of a world brain."
- [[The Participator]] — Ascott's term for the active networked subject, aligned with second-order cybernetics' observer→participator turn.
- [[Distributed Authorship]] — the dissolving of the single author into many networked participators (demonstrated in "La Plissure du Texte").
- [[New Media Art]] — extended here with the Ascott/telematic pole (Burnham-realized note; oriented, not re-created).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — extended here with Ascott (behaviourist/telematic art as experiential technology par excellence).
- [[Cybernetics]] — the Wiener/Ashby/F. H. George science Ascott read in 1961 and rebuilt as an aesthetic; Shanken's account adds the art-specific application.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's object→system thesis; Shanken positions Ascott's participatory cybernetic art as its counterpole.

## Important Definitions

- [[Cybernetics]] — Shanken glosses Wiener's "control and communication in the animal and the machine" and frames it as the regulation of information in feedback loops; here applied to art.
- Telematics (Ascott's, via Shanken) — "computer-mediated communications networking between geographically dispersed individuals and institutions . . . and between the human mind and artificial systems of intelligence and perception"; the term *télématique* coined by Simon Nora and Alain Minc (1978). ## Major Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — Ascott's "field of objects" → "field of behaviour," the art-side counterpart to Burnham's object→system.
- [[Process vs Product]] — "Process replaces product in importance" (Shanken quoting Ascott).
