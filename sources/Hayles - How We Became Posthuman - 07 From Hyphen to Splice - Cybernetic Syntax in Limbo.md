> Sensitive-content note: this chapter analyzes Bernard Wolfe's *Limbo*, a deliberately transgressive 1952 dystopia. Hayles repeatedly flags the novel's misogyny and stages, as literary objects under critique, two depicted rape scenes, a depicted suicide, a mercy-killing, and theories of sexual difference she explicitly condemns. The summary below describes Hayles's *critical reading* of those textual entities; it does not endorse the novel's views.

## Orientation

Chapter 5 turns from the conceptual history of the first three chapters to a single close reading: Bernard Wolfe's *Limbo* (1952), which Hayles treats as "a novel of ideas" that "stages encounters between literary form and bodies represented within the text." The chapter's wager is that *Limbo* is doubly cybernetic — it both narrates a world reorganized by cybernetics and is *itself* reorganized as a textual body, so that "the textual corpus, no less than the represented world, bears the imprint of the cybernetic paradigm upon its body." Hayles reads Wolfe as the literary counterpart to [[Norbert Wiener]] (the novel's "patron saint"): a writer who grasps the revolutionary, boundary-dissolving force of [[Cybernetics]] and then tries, anxiously and unsuccessfully, to contain it in order to protect the autonomy of the (male) [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|Liberal Humanist Subject]].

The organizing figure is the move "from hyphen to splice." The hyphen joins polarities (human-machine, male-female, text-marginalia) "in a metonymic tension that can be seen as maintaining the identity of each"; the [[Cybernetic Splice]] — the cut-and-rejoin that turns a *cyb/ernetic org/anism* into a [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] — implies "a more reflexive and transformative union" in which modifying the circuit modifies consciousness. Wolfe clings to "the ideology of the hyphen"; the text's own [[Feedback Loops|feedback loops]] keep overrunning it. Hayles maps this onto the McCarthy-era body politic via Paul Virilio's shift from exo-colonization to [[Endo-Colonization]] — the implosion of war and control into the body's interior — and onto Wolfe's gendered anxieties, so that "the body politic and the politics of the body... are spliced together in an integrated circuit."

The chapter's most distinctive contribution is the [[Textual Cyborg]]: the typographic body of *Limbo* fragments into a "trunk" (Martine's narrative) and "prosthetic extensions" (marginal drawings, scrawled lines, puns), joined by the punning neologism [[Pros/e]] — "the truncated/spliced noun that speaks the name of the text's body (prose) as well as the name of the prostheses (pros)." From this Hayles draws her culminating claim about [[Emergent Control]]: in a cybernetic circuit "control... is not a localized function but is an emergent property," vocalization is nonlocalized, and "another voice" — the splice the author cannot fully articulate — speaks through the text. The reading thus extends the book's master argument that information's penetration of bodily boundaries dissolves the bounded liberal subject, here demonstrated at the level of literary form.

## Key Concepts

- [[Cybernetic Splice]] — the cut-rejoin-recircuit ("cyb/ernetic org/anism") that fuses human and machine into a transformative circuit, vs. the merely adjacent hyphen.
- [[Hyphen vs Splice]] — Wolfe's defensive "ideology of the hyphen" against the more radical implications of cybernetic fusion.
- [[Endo-Colonization]] — after Virilio, the post-WWII implosion of military/control operations inward onto a country's own population and body interior.
- [[The Cyborg]] — "simultaneously entities and metaphors, living beings and narrative constructions"; technological object and discursive formation at once.
- [[Textual Cyborg]] — the printed body of *Limbo* itself spliced into a circuit: trunk-narrative plus prosthetic marginalia.
- [[Pros/e]] — the splice-noun naming both *prose* and the *pros*(theses), destabilizing the natural body and the typographic text together.
- [[Emergent Control]] — in a cybernetic circuit, control is "not a localized function but... an emergent property"; subjectivity dispersed, vocalization nonlocalized.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the chapter's horizon: a "posthuman, posttypographic future" in which bodies of print are "punctuated with prostheses."
- [[Prosthesis as Writing]] — via David Wills, writing as a prosthesis articulating *with* (not issuing from) the body.

## Important Definitions

- [[Endo-Colonization]] — "a deep shift from exo-colonization to endocolonization throughout postmodern cultures" (Virilio, applied to McCarthyism).
- [[Pros/e]] — "the truncated/spliced noun that speaks the name of the text's body (prose) as well as the name of the prostheses (pros) attached to it."
- [[The Cyborg]] — entity and metaphor at once; "cyborgs actually exist," roughly 10 percent of the US population in the technical sense.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Hyphen and the Splice]] — typographic marks as rival figures for how human and machine are joined.
- [[Trunk and Prosthesis]] — the maimed torso and its detachable "pros" as the master image of body, text, and nation in *Limbo*.
- [[The Screen of Words]] — Martine's defensive verbal screen; "Limbo knows more than it can say."
- [[Immob]] — the ideology of voluntary amputation ("No Demobilization without Immobilization"); social mobility translated into physical immobility.
- [[The Baby Basket]] — the limbless torso's cradle, emblem of the regression to infantile dependence.
- [[White Box and Black Box]] — Wiener's discursive equation of the known laboratory model with the unknown human mechanism.

## Major Tensions

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the bounded liberal body vs. the subject dissolved into circuits and information flow.
- [[Control vs Emergence]] — the author's attempt to master the text vs. control as a nonlocalized, emergent property of the circuit.
- [[Liberal Subject vs Cyborg Subjectivity]] — the autonomous (male) liberal self vs. dispersed, spliced cyborg subjectivity.
