## Orientation

Chapter 6 ("Network," the second of the book's two Network chapters, pages 97–109) is the theological hinge of O'Gieblyn's robotics material. Where Chapter 5 set up the embodied-AI project at MIT — Rodney Brooks, Cynthia Breazeal, and the robots Cog and Kismet — this chapter reframes that engineering enterprise as the latest episode in an ancient religious drama: the human attempt to summon life from inert matter. It opens with Annie Dillard's parable of the man who teaches a stone to talk, which O'Gieblyn folds into a [[Disenchantment]] narrative of a world rendered mute. Following Dillard, she dates the "fall from grace" not to the Enlightenment but to the rise of monotheism and the doctrine of [[Imago Dei]] — the idea that human exceptionalism, and our "dominion" over creation, is what alienated us from a once-companionable nature.

The chapter then traces the persistence of religious structure inside ostensibly secular AI. Alan Turing's 1950 attempt to answer the "theological objection" inadvertently concedes it; the German theologian Anne Foerst, embedded at the Media Lab, harmonizes robotics with mystical Judaism (the Sefer Yetzirah, the practical Kabbalah, the golem of Rabbi Löw of Prague); and a possibly apocryphal MIT anecdote has Gerry Sussman, Joel Moses, and Marvin Minsky all turn out to be descendants of Löw who were given the golem-awakening incantation at their bar mitzvahs. George Dyson's epigram — "Computers arose from the mud, and code fell from the sky" — lets O'Gieblyn name the dualism animating [[Emergence]]: the hope that machines built from matter will be inhabited by abstract mathematical pattern, a kind of informational [[Imago Dei]] ([[The Computational Universe]]).

The analytic core is a critique of emergentism as, paradoxically, *not* mysticism but its opposite — a reductive [[Eliminativism]] that dissolves the very consciousness it claims to explain (Minsky's "society of agents," Brooks's consciousness as a "cheap trick," Jessica Riskin's [[Theological Mechanism (O'Gieblyn via Riskin)]]). O'Gieblyn argues that the charge of anthropomorphism against AI and plant-intelligence research misreads the direction of the figure: these projects do not so much elevate machines to the human as reduce the human to the machine ("phytomorphising the human"), a "risky ontological bargain" that shrinks humanity to "the exchange of information." The chapter closes on the failure of the embodied-AI dream (Cog never cohered, Kismet never spoke), Brooks's loss of faith in the computational metaphor ([[Mind as Machine]]), and Daniel Dennett's warning that the real danger is not malevolent superintelligence but our being seduced into prematurely granting machines human consciousness — capped by the political economy of likable robots displacing human labor.

## Key Concepts

- [[Disenchantment]] — the modern world rendered mute; O'Gieblyn (via Dillard) relocates the "fall from grace" to the rise of monotheism rather than the Enlightenment.
- [[Re-enchantment]] — the futile longing to coax nature back into dialogue, mirrored by the AI project of making "our own stones come to life."
- [[Imago Dei]] — human exceptionalism and "dominion"; Foerst's lived contradiction between humans as God's elected partners and as rebuildable machines.
- [[Emergence]] — the hope that consciousness or intelligence will arise spontaneously from interacting simple parts; O'Gieblyn accepts it for beehives and climates but calls consciousness-from-machines wishful thinking.
- [[Eliminativism]] — the view that there is no unified self; Minsky's mind as competing agents that "cannot think at all."
- [[The Computational Universe]] — "the very fabric of reality is informational"; O'Gieblyn supplies its Kabbalist prehistory (letters and numbers as a cosmic code preceding matter).
- [[Embodiment]] — the embodied-AI bet that sensorimotor interaction would bootstrap higher cognition; its failure (Cog, Kismet, iCub) is evidence the body cannot be skipped.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the decentering of the human ("humans…are after all mere machines"); O'Gieblyn reads transhumanism as posthumanism's religious double.
- [[The Computational Universe]] linked to [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — the technologies were built to exclude the conscious subject, so cannot be expected to develop one.

## Key Arguments

- [[The anthropomorphism charge misreads the flow of the metaphor]] — critics accuse AI/plant researchers of projecting humanity onto things; the researchers in fact aim to reconceive the human through inanimate systems.
- [[Intelligence does not require a unified knowing subject]] — Minsky's "society of agents" and Brooks's "cheap trick" locate intelligence in observable behavior, leaving no place for interior experience.
- [[Emergence is vitalism updated and uncomfortably like magic]] — emergentism superficially satisfies the golem's desire while bottoming out, paradoxically, in reductive materialism.
- [[The computer metaphor reintroduces the disembodied soul it was built to abolish]] — the "divine breath" of abstract pattern inhabiting material parts is a disembodiment fantasy underneath the machine talk.
- [[The true trauma of disenchantment is the loss of intrinsic meaning]] — the muted, immutable block of nature; "we doused the burning bush and cannot rekindle it."
- [[Without a religious framework human worth collapses into economic logic]] — likable, job-displacing robots and the "spoonful of sugar" political economy of phased automation.

## Important Definitions

- [[The Theological Objection (O'Gieblyn via Turing)]] — the religious argument that machines cannot think because thinking requires an immortal soul, which God grants only to humans; Turing's reply concedes more than it answers.
- [[Disenchantment (O'Gieblyn)]] — the Weberian frame, here dated to monotheism, in which a once-sacred and chatty world is rendered silent and mute.
- [[Theological Mechanism (O'Gieblyn via Riskin)]] — Riskin's account of how mechanistic explanations of mind end up dismissing the very intelligence they are meant to explain.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Golem]] — the clay android animated by God's name or by mathematical permutation; the ancestral template for AI and the dream of animating matter with informational "breath."
- [[Computers Arose from the Mud and Code Fell from the Sky]] — Dyson's dual creation myth (material body + abstract pattern) framing the emergence dream.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the historicized figure: clocks, mills, looms, the computer; Brooks's admission that the metaphor is not yet right.
- [[The Soul as an Empty Carapace]] — the eliminativist hollowing of interior life into observer-attributed behavior ("cheap trick," "tangled-up bureaucracy").

## Major Tensions

- [[Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment]] — the longing to re-animate a world we ourselves silenced versus Dillard's pessimism about return.
- [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality]] — humans as elected partners of God versus "bags of skin that can be rebuilt"; "phytomorphising the human."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the mud/sky split; "the informational as very separate from the physical."
