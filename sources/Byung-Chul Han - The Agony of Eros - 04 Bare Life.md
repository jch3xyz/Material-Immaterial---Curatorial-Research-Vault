# Source Summary: Bare Life

**Book:** [[The Agony of Eros]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/04 Bare Life]]

## Orientation

Chapter 4, "Bare Life," is the erotic re-grounding of a concept the vault already holds from *The Burnout Society*, where [[Bare Life|bare life]] named the achievement-subject reduced — through the loss of narrative — to survival, "health as the new goddess," the "undead" life that is "too dead to live, and too alive to die." Here Han routes the same reduction through **eros and death**. His hinge is Hegel's master–slave dialectic read as a "battle for life and death": the master rises above bare life because he possesses the "ability to die," while "the future slave" clings to bare life out of "fear of dying." From this Han draws the chapter's master thesis — that the same incapacity for death which makes the modern subject cling to bare life is what disqualifies it from erotic experience. "[[Eros]] as excess and transgression denies both work and bare life"; therefore "the slave, who holds fast to life and labors, proves incapable of erotic experience." Eros, on this account, is intrinsically bound to death: "assenting to life up to the point of death" (Bataille), "the medium for intensifying life to the point of death," the dissolution of the narcissistic ego, the *Liebestod*.

The chapter braids five threads around that hinge. (1) The **positivization and domestication of love** into a risk-free "consumer formula devoid of risk and daring, without excess or madness," argued explicitly *against* Eva Illouz's "feminization of love" thesis: love is not being feminized but positivized, so that "the desire for the Other is giving way to the comfort of the Same" (see [[Hell of the Same]], [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz]]). (2) **Hegel's master–slave dialectic re-read** so that the [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploiting]] [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] is "master and slave at once," an "enslaved master," which forecloses any Kojèvian "end of history" (the argument [[The achievement-subject is master and slave at once so there is no end of history]]). (3) A **defense of Hegel as the thinker of the Other and of love** — the Absolute as "the reconciled return out of another into self," the *Schluss* (conclusion), "tarrying with the negative" — read *against* Derrida, Deleuze, and Bataille, who take the "Absolute" to mean "violence and totality" (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Hegel]]). (4) **Capitalism as the absolutization of bare life**: aimless accumulation "specifically aimed against death, which counts as absolute loss," abolishing Aristotle's teleology of the good life — "This is how capitalism becomes obscene" (cross-links [[Capital as Religion]]). (5) The **survivor as the undead**, allegorized by the Flying Dutchman as a figure of burnout society, opposed by Senta's suicide-as-*Liebestod* and by Badiou's fidelity that "introduces an eternity into time" (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Alain Badiou]]).

Rhetorically the chapter is Han at his most quotation-driven and cross-textual: Ficino's Neoplatonic "eroticized eye," Plato's *Phaedrus*, Hegel across five works, Nietzsche's "last human beings," Aristotle's *Politics*, Bataille's *Erotism*, Wagner's *Flying Dutchman*, and — at the close — Badiou's *In Praise of Love*. It is the primary-source anchor for Han's thesis that the erosion of the capacity for death (the [[Negativity|negativity]] of the not-I) is the erosion of eros, and it is the chapter that most tightly welds the book's diagnosis of neoliberal survival to its metaphysics of love. **Note:** the closing quotation on fidelity and eternity is Badiou's (*In Praise of Love*), quoted by Han inside the chapter body (endnote 27) — it is attributed to Badiou, not drawn from the Foreword.

## Key Concepts

- [[Bare Life]] — life stripped of transcendence and reduced to survival, here re-grounded in the *incapacity for death*; the slave "clings to bare life," capitalism "absolutizes bare life," and "the defense of bare life is intensifying into the absolutization and fetishization of health." **PRIMARY EXTEND target** for this chapter (the *Agony of Eros* counterpart to the *Burnout Society* treatment).
- [[Eros]] — the erotic relation, defined by its intrinsic bond to death and negativity: eros "as excess and transgression denies both work and bare life"; "Eros is the medium for intensifying life to the point of death"; its power (*Macht*) is *Ohn-Macht*, a self-loss in and for the Other.
- [[The Other]] — the wholly other whose negativity the erotic subject must admit; "The priority of the Other distinguishes the power of Eros from the violence of Ares"; love as "the gift of the Other" preceded by the surrender of the self.
- [[Negative Potency]] — Hegel's "ability to die" / "freedom unto death" (*Freiheit zum Tod*), the faculty the master has and the slave lacks; its disappearance ("no one can conclude his or her life") is bound to the *Schluss* (conclusion) and to depression as *Unfähigkeit zum Ent-Schluss*.
- [[Hell of the Same]] — "the comfort of the Same," "the dull immanence of the wholly identical," and the Flying Dutchman's "inferno of the same"; the positivized love that seeks "only the confirmation of oneself in the Other."
- [[Positivity]] — the "positivization of all spheres of life" that domesticates love into a risk-free consumer formula and degrades it into "an object of consumption, a matter of hedonistic calculation."
- [[Negativity]] — the injury, transformation, and death that constitute love's negativity; "Negativity is essential to vitality"; "Whatever is merely positive is lifeless."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the entrepreneur of the self "is master and slave at once," exploiting himself "of his own volition"; the "enslaved master."
- [[Achievement Society]] — the formation whose subject "is equal to the Hegelian slave in all respects" except that he exploits himself; foreclosed from erotic experience.
- [[Capital as Religion]] — capitalism's "compulsive accumulation and growth... specifically aimed against death," abolishing the teleology of the good life (Aristotle: living vs. living well).
- [[Atopia]] — the placelessness of the Other that eros presumes (cross-link; the atopic Other that "admits no bookkeeping").
- [[The Politics of Eros]] — the political register of the priority of the Other developed in the following chapter; the *Liebestod*'s opposition to the capitalist economy anticipates it.

## Key Arguments

- [[The achievement-subject is master and slave at once so there is no end of history]] — the auto-exploiting subject is both the Hegelian slave clinging to bare life and the master who exploits him, an "enslaved master" whose persistent unfreedom means the history of freedom is not over.
- [[Eros and depression are opposites the achievement-subject cannot love]] — eros pulls the subject out of itself toward the Other, but the slave who "holds fast to life and labors" lacks the capacity for death and self-loss and so "proves incapable of erotic experience."

## Important Definitions

- Eroticism (Bataille) — "Eroticism, it may be said, is assenting to life up to the point of death"; erotic experience opens the way to a "continuity of being" that only death can provide.
- The Absolute as love (Hegel) — "Absolute means unrestricted"; the true essence of love "consists in giving up the consciousness of oneself, forgetting oneself in another self"; Spirit "looks the negative in the face and tarries with it."
- The *Schluss* / conclusion (Hegel via Han) — *Schluss* means both "inference" and "conclusion" (from *schliessen*, "to close"); a restricted, end-bypassing life yields a "short-circuit" (*Kurzschluss*), while the absolute conclusion is "long and slow," preceded by tarrying.
- The power of eros as *Ohn-Macht* (Han via Ficino) — "the power (*Macht*) of eros implies powerlessness and unconsciousness (*Ohn-Macht*)"; instead of self-affirmation the lover loses himself in the Other, "who then rights me again."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The boar's "eroticized teeth" (*erotikous odontas*) — the boar that killed Adonis protests it meant only to caress him; eros as deadly excess and madness (chapter-opening image).
- The eroticized eye (*erotikon omma*) — Ficino's blood-and-spirit transfusion of the gaze between Lysias and Phaedrus; eros as wounding communication.
- Health as the "great goddess" — Nietzsche's "last human beings" who "invented happiness" and blink; the deified survival-value of the doping society.
- The short-circuit (*Kurzschluss*) — the restricted life that bypasses the end/conclusion.
- The survivor as the undead — "too dead to live, and too alive to die."
- The Flying Dutchman — the ship "manned by the undead," "flying like an arrow without aim," damned "to journey eternally in the inferno of the same"; the chapter's central allegory of burnout / achievement society.
- Senta's spinning-song and her suicide / the *Liebestod* — the humming wheel of "blind production and performance," and its sublime negation.

## Major Tensions

- [[Eros vs Depression]] — the achievement-subject that cannot die cannot lose itself in the Other, cannot love; the eros/survival opposition here undergirds the eros/depression one.
- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — "Negativity is essential to vitality"; "Whatever is merely positive is lifeless"; the society of positivity is a society of bare life.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — obliquely: the "non-economy of eros and death" against the "economy of survival," the additive/accumulative capitalist economy against the *Schlussform* that "introduces an eternity into time."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- The chapter is the sharpest primary text for an essay on **survival vs. eros / the incapacity for death**: how a culture that "absolutizes bare life" and deifies health simultaneously forecloses love, since both love and vitality require the negativity of death that survival flees.
- A **materiality/immateriality** essay on the "non-economy of eros and death" against the additive "economy of survival": the *Liebestod* and Badiou's "eternity within time" as a form of *Schluss* (conclusion) that resists capital's endless, aimless accumulation — "This is how capitalism becomes obscene."
- A **reception-of-Hegel** essay: Han's defense of Hegel as "receptive to the Other like no other thinker" — the Absolute as love, "tarrying with the negative," the gift of the Other — read against the Derrida/Deleuze/Bataille "Absolute as violence and totality," and how this repositions dialectics as an ethics of alterity.
- An essay on the **Flying Dutchman as an allegory of burnout society**: the undead survivor "unable either to live or to die," yoked to Senta's *Liebestod* as the sublime negation of "blind production and performance."
- A **love-and-neoliberalism** essay contesting Illouz: love's crisis as positivization/domestication rather than "feminization" — "the desire for the Other is giving way to the comfort of the Same."
