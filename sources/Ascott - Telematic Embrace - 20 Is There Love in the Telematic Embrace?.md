# Source Summary: Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?

**Book:** [[Telematic Embrace]]
**Author:** [[Roy Ascott]]

## Orientation

This 1990 essay (originally published in *Art Journal* and developed for the College Art Association panel "Computers and Art: Issues of Content") is Ascott's single most cited statement and the keystone of his theory of [[Telematic Art]]. It confronts the standard humanist anxiety that art made with computers and telecommunications is cold, formalist, and content-empty — that the machine is "coming to dominate the human will." Ascott reframes that worry as a question of love: *"Is there love in the telematic embrace?"* His answer is yes, but only once we abandon the classical (Shannon–Weaver) model of communication as one-way transmission from artist-as-sender to viewer-as-passive-receiver, and accept that in telematic art meaning is not transmitted but *produced* through interaction between the participant and a system whose content is in perpetual flux. This is the core of [[In telematic art meaning is produced through interaction not transmitted]].

The essay's spine is the shift from observer to participator (the tension [[Observer vs Participator]]), which Ascott borrows directly from quantum physics — John Wheeler's "participatory universe," substituting "data" for "quanta" — and from second-order cybernetics, the move "from the analysis of observed systems to the (second-order) cybernetics of observing systems." He builds a chain of equivalences: the artwork is no longer a fixed object carrying content "out there" but a [[Gesamtdatenwerk]] (his neologism, an "integrated data work") whose meaning is "pure electronic différance" until reconstituted at the interface. The interface replaces the art object as the locus of the aesthetic; networking merely makes explicit the participatory creativity that was always implicit in aesthetic experience (Barthes). He grounds the lineage in art history — Duchamp's [[The Large Glass]] as a proto-interface, Pollock's horizontal all-over field as a proto-network — and in a horizontal/holistic versus vertical/Renaissance-perspectival opposition.

The back half develops the spiritual-planetary register that defines late Ascott: the global net as a "subtle body," "noosphere" (Teilhard), "mind at large" (Bateson), and "global brain" (Russell) — a [[Network Consciousness]] emerging from the symbiosis of the human mind and parallel distributed processing. He narrates four landmark telematic artworks (*La Plissure du Texte*, 1983; the *Les Immatériaux* / Minitel project, 1985; the Venice Biennale *Planetary Network* / *Laboratorio Ubiqua*, 1986; *Aspects of Gaia*, 1989) as instances of [[The Holomatic Principle]] — every node holding the whole network virtually present — and of dispersed authorship. The argument restates Burnham's object-to-system thesis ("the computer is not primarily a thing, an object, but a set of behaviours, a system, actually, a system of systems"), folds telematic content into the immaterial pole of [[Material vs Immaterial]], and closes by binding silicon — Navajo sand-painting pigment and processor chip alike — into a single spiritual-technological lineage, asserting that "the metaphor of love in the telematic embrace may not be entirely misplaced." Its rhetorical posture is unguardedly utopian; Ascott names but does not resolve the charge that his planetary vision is "uncritically euphoric."

## Key Concepts

- [[Telematic Art]] — the participatory, distributed, interface-based artwork of computer-mediated telecommunications networks, where meaning is produced, not transmitted; the keystone concept of the book.
- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the title figure: the convergence of computing and telecommunications drawing all electronic media into one networked field of operations, posed as a question of love and content rather than cold form.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the planetary consciousness / "subtle body" / noosphere / global brain emerging from the symbiosis of the human mind and networked parallel distributed processing.
- [[Gesamtdatenwerk]] — Ascott's neologism after Wagner's Gesamtkunstwerk: the integrated, hypermediated total data-work that engages the full sensorium by digital means.
- [[The Holomatic Principle]] — every access node of the network holds the whole network's data, so to be at any one interface is to be in the virtual presence of all interfaces.
- [[Global Vision]] — the new order of perception telematic networks constitute: a distributed sensorium and distributed intelligence networked across the planet, enabling holistic, integrative viewing.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A material/immaterial essay can use Ascott's "pure electronic différance" formulation as the strongest articulation of digital content's immateriality — content that has no fixed form until "reconstituted at the interface as image, text, or sound" — beside Lippard's dematerialization and Hayles's "information lost its body."
- A surveillance/systems essay can stage Ascott's euphoric celebration of the pervasive, planet-girdling network ("subtle body," "noosphere," distributed sensorium) directly against Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] reading of the same networked condition as domination — Ascott's 1990 utopia as the precursor Han later diagnoses.
- An embodiment essay can mobilize the tension inside Ascott himself: he claims the network "amplifies the individual's capacity" and keeps "her authenticity," yet his telos is transcendence — "to be out of body, out of mind, beyond language" — a contradiction productive against Hayles's "there is only different embodiment."
- A systems-esthetics genealogy can trace the [[Object and System]] spine from Burnham (1968) to Ascott (1990): the computer "is not primarily a thing, an object, but a set of behaviours, a system, actually, a system of systems."
- A participation/spectatorship essay can use the observer-to-participator shift, with Wheeler's "participatory universe" as the borrowed warrant, to historicize interactive and relational art's claim that the viewer completes the work.
- A re-enchantment essay can read Ascott's binding of Navajo sand painting and the supercomputer "through common silicon" as a paradigm case of technology occupying "the domain, previously provided by myth and religion."
