# Source Summary: Chapter 6 — Kineticism: The Unrequited Art

**Book:** [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]
**Author:** [[Jack Burnham]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/08 Chapter 6 - Kineticism - The Unrequited Art]]

## Orientation

Chapter 6 opens Part Two ("Sculpture as System") and carries the book's master thesis — the migration of sculpture from the inert object to the physical system — into its first concrete movement, [[Kinetic Art]]. Burnham's framing claim is that "the desire to make art kinetic is one of the prime artistic urges of the present century," yet the chapter's title names the movement [[The Unrequited Art]]: an art whose aspiration (real motion, immateriality, life-like temporal process) chronically outruns its means, the determinate, restrictive [[The Closed Kinematic Chain]] of the first age of machines. Burnham reads Kineticism as a heroic, productive failure — "Successful Kinetic Art until now has either defied or trivialized the principles of mechanical invention" — and is unembarrassed to call the present revival, by past standards, "with very few exceptions a failure" while still defending its open-ended promise.

The chapter proceeds as a long historical morphology. It first reconstructs the prehistory of Kineticism in classical machine theory (Reuleaux's "closed kinematic chain" and "pair closure"; Ampère's [[Kinematics vs Kinetics (Burnham)]] distinction) and in the nineteenth-century scientific recording of organic motion (Marey, Muybridge). It then surveys the "early failures" of the four pivotal pioneers — Duchamp, Gabo, Calder, and Moholy-Nagy — each of whom recognized the potential of motion, made a handful of experiments, and withdrew; here the chapter dwells on Gabo's dilemma that the motor "interfered" with the motion it produced, and on the avant-garde drive to humanize the machine by injecting imprecision and fallibility into it. Finally it tracks the c. 1955–1965 revival, [[The New Tendency and Field Kinetics]], in which painters (Fontana, Klein, Vasarely, Soto, Tinguely, Group Zero, NUL, the G.R.A.V., Group N and Group T) turn away from machine kinematics toward immateriality, the repetitive field/raster modeled on the physicist's energy field, the depersonalized "cool" laboratory-like object, and the active spectator who "lives" the work in real time.

The chapter is load-bearing for the vault's titular axis in several ways. It names the object-to-system move in a single phrase — the kinetic work "is not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art" — making this Burnham's primary-source proto-statement of systems esthetics. It threads "immateriality" as the explicit "rallying cry" of the whole New Tendency (Klein), a 1968 sculptural genealogy that precedes and feeds Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] (Tinguely even uses the literal word). It links the mobile's equilibrium-seeking and the machine-as-organism to the simulation of biological life, anticipating the cybernetic chapters. And it culminates in Ellul's "la technique" and Mumford's "technics" — [[Technics]] as a self-augmenting, inescapable force whose only refuge for art is madness/irrationality — and in the emergent artist–engineer symbiosis (Billy Klüver, E.A.T.), the systems-esthetics foreground.

## Key Concepts

- [[Kinetic Art]] — nonrepresentational art made with the parameters of real time and motion; the first major movement of "sculpture as system," and the celebration of man's determinate control of motion
- [[The Unrequited Art]] — the chapter's governing figure: an art whose aspiration to life-like motion and immateriality outruns the means of the first age of machines; a heroic failure that survives because it remains unrealized
- [[The Closed Kinematic Chain]] — Reuleaux's definition of the machine as "a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies"; the determinate root of Kineticism and its deepest limitation
- [[The New Tendency and Field Kinetics]] — the c. 1955–1965 European revival; a quest for "the physically sensed but insubstantial" expressed through the repetitive field structure (raster) modeled on the physicist's energy field
- [[Technics]] — Ellul's "technique" / Mumford's "technics," characterized by "self-augmentation": "Beyond a certain plateau, technology breeds technology"; only madness escapes it
- [[Scientific Idealism]] — the Constructivist pursuit of the unseen through regular geometry and Neo-Platonism; the New Tendency as a temporary theoretical advance beyond it toward field dynamics
- [[Systems Theory]] — reused; Burnham's "systeme d'art," the kinetic work as a series of interacting components needing repair and adjustment, is his proto-systems-esthetics statement
- [[Dematerialization]] — reused (Lippard); Burnham's "immateriality" as the New Tendency standard predates and feeds Lippard's dematerialization; Tinguely literally names "Dematerialization"
- [[Homeostasis]] — reused (Hayles); Calder's mobile as a system that seeks equilibrium after disturbance through diminishing thrusts; "any system... seeks equilibrium"

## Key Arguments

- [[The kinetic construction is not an object but a system]] — the keystone: a kinetic work is a serviceable system of interacting components, unlike the inert traditional object; purchasers return broken machines expecting a "lifetime guarantee"
- [[The true goal of the kinetic revival was immateriality not machine motion]] — the New Tendency (mostly painters) sought infinite spatial extension, immateriality, and the temporal dimension, not the clattering machine
- [[The machine must be humanized with imprecision and fallibility to make art live]] — the determinate machine forbids the irrational order art needs; to live, it must be injected with imprecision, fallibility, even self-destruction
- [[Art's correspondence with science is visual analogy not borrowed method]] — innovators "pull them from the air"; the field/Einstein analogy "stops short of physical reality and rests upon visual correspondences"
- [[Technics is self-augmenting and only madness escapes the machine]] — technology breeds technology; technics reduces every art form to technique; only madness is inaccessible to the machine
- [[An art movement dies by realizing its goals not by self-destruction]] — the closing inversion: the self-destroying machine is "only a Luddite solution"; Kineticism survives precisely because it is unrequited
- [[As art accepts technology it depends increasingly on the engineer]] — a "new and growing symbiosis"; the engineer may become "more than an equal partner"

## Important Definitions

- [[Kinematics vs Kinetics (Burnham)]] — kinematics is "the geometry of touching surfaces," motion unattached to forces; kinetics concerns motions resulting from forces directly connected to physical systems
- [[Technique (Ellul via Burnham)]] — Ellul's "technique" / Mumford's "technics" defined by its property of "self-augmentation"

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Bicycle Wheel as a Turtle on Its Back]] — Duchamp's utilitarian wheel rendered functionally immobile, "motion that goes nowhere and a machine that does not 'work'"

## Major Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — the inert objet d'art versus the interacting, serviceable systeme d'art; the vault's keystone axis, named in the chapter
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — reused; solid bronze/steel/stone versus Klein's gaseous, liquid, luminous "immaterial sensitivity" and the raster's "yearning for immateriality"
- [[Determinism vs Randomness]] — strictly programmed kinetics versus random/pseudo-random/statistical field motion, driven by the engineering cost of determinacy
- [[Art vs Technics]] — the irrational refuge of art versus the "compelling logic and almost authoritarian ethos" of technics; Schöffer (no incompatibility) versus Tinguely (regret) versus Ellul (oblivion)
