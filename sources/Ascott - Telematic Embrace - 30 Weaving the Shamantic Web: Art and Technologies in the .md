## Orientation

This 1998 essay (revised from "The Shamantic Web: Art et techologie de la conscience," trans. Myriam Bloedé, in *Pour une écologie des media*, Paris: ASTARTI, 1998) is the keystone of Ascott's late, consciousness-centered phase, and the chapter where the book's subtitle — *Art, Technology, and Consciousness* — is finally cashed out. Where the 1980s chapters built the telematic-art vocabulary (network consciousness, the telematic embrace, the Gesamtdatenwerk), here Ascott places *consciousness itself* at the literal center of art and names the resulting aesthetic [[Technoetics]] — the "technoetic aesthetic," from techno- plus the Greek *nous* (mind). The opening move is programmatically anti-reductionist: science "is trying hard to explain consciousness, with distinctly limited success," whereas "for the artist, consciousness is more to be explored than to be explained, more to be transformed than understood, more to be reframed than reported." This is the chapter's master thesis ([[For the artist consciousness is to be transformed not explained]]).

The essay's structuring device is a sustained parallelism between *two technologies of consciousness*: the digital/telematic (virtual reality, hypermedia, the Net) and the ancient "plant technology" of shamanism — specifically the Amazonian brew *ayahuasca*, the "vine of the soul," and the Santo Daime ritual. Ascott reports first-person fieldwork at both poles: the Human Interface Technology Laboratory in Seattle and the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill VR Lab, and the Kuikuru *pagés* (shamans) of the Xingu River Region of the Mato Grosso in Brazil ([[Digital and shamanic plant technology are parallel technologies of consciousness]]). From this parallel he derives his late coinages: [[Double Consciousness]] (simultaneous access to "two distinctly different fields of experience"), [[The Double Gaze]] ("seeing at once both inward realities and the outward surfaces of the world"), psi-perception as the shamanic analogue of technologically aided [[Cyberception]], and the title figure, [[The Shamantic Web]] — a fusion of *shamanic* and *semantic*, "the navigation of consciousness and the construction of meaning."

The chapter also carries Ascott's strongest single statement of the material/immaterial collapse and his most refined participation polemic. Through the scanning tunnelling microscope — which both images single atoms and lets us "manipulate them, one by one, atom by atom" — and the dry/moist artificial-life convergence (the seed of his later [[Moistmedia]]), he argues that "the prosthesis of vision can be at one and the same time instrumental in constructing what is envisioned" and that "the immaterial and material lose their categorical distinction" ([[The prosthesis of vision constructs what it envisions so material and immaterial lose their distinction]]). Finally, against the gallery model, he argues that networked interactive art is "incarcerated within the very status it despises, that of pure object," still presupposing a "second observer" and a "phantom audience"; invoking the *pagé*'s non-performative ritual ("a spiritual enactment, not a public performance"), he prescribes "an art without audience in its inactive mode" — an audience-less [[Telematic art must remove the second observer and the phantom audience|enactment of mind]]. The posture throughout is frankly spiritual and re-enchanting, the artist re-cast as a re-contextualized shaman who "cares for consciousness."

## Key Concepts

- [[Technoetics]] — the keystone late coinage: "technology plus mind" as the new ground of art; consciousness as both the context and content of art, to be explored and transformed rather than explained. Holds "the technoetic aesthetic," "the technoetic condition," and "the technoetic principle."
- [[Cyberception]] — perception extended and transformed by technology, "as much active and constructive as it is receptive and reflective"; here paired with its shamanic analogue, psi-perception (the ayahuasca-enhanced double gaze). This chapter is a strong supporting source; the titular anchor is ch. 23.
- [[Double Consciousness]] — "the state of being that gives access, at one and the same time, to two distinctly different fields of experience"; modeled on the shamanic trance and mirrored in the VR user's simultaneous inhabitation of cyberspace and the material world.
- [[The Double Gaze]] — the perceptual correlate of double consciousness: "seeing at once both inward realities and the outward surfaces of the world."
- [[The Shamantic Web]] — the title portmanteau (shamanic + semantic); the bio-telematic art practice that interweaves consciousness-navigation and meaning-construction.
- [[Moistmedia]] — proto-form here: the artificial-life sciences embrace "both dry (pixel) and moist (molecular) artificial organisms" plus nanotechnology, "eroding the boundaries between states of mind, between conception and construction." (Titular anchor is the later moistmedia essay; this is a supporting source.)

- [[New Media Art]] — extended: bio-telematic art (VR, hypermedia, augmented reality, multimedia installations) as the technoetic medium.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — extended: art as the audience-less "enactment of mind," an executed inner experience.

## Key Arguments

- [[For the artist consciousness is to be transformed not explained]] — the master thesis; the artist explores, transforms, and reframes consciousness where reductionist science fails to explain it.
- [[Digital and shamanic plant technology are parallel technologies of consciousness]] — the structuring analogy: VR labs and the ayahuasca ritual both produce a double consciousness and a double gaze.
- [[The prosthesis of vision constructs what it envisions so material and immaterial lose their distinction]] — the STM both images and manipulates single atoms; seeing and building fuse; the categorical distinction collapses.
- [[Telematic art must remove the second observer and the phantom audience]] — the gallery-installed interactive work is re-objectified by a "second observer"; the cure is an audience-less enactment of mind on the model of the *pagé*'s non-public ritual.
- [[Digital technique reduces art to craft replacing meaning with special effects]] — the opening polemic; a new Arts and Crafts "dumbing down" in which technique displaces the creation of meaning and value.
