# Source Summary: Chapter 17 — Comics: Mad Vestibule to TV

**Book:** [[Understanding Media]]
**Author:** [[Marshall McLuhan]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/21 Chapter 17 - Comics - Mad Vestibule to TV]]

## Orientation

Chapter 17 of [[Understanding Media|*Understanding Media*]] is a Part II case-study that uses the comic strip and the comic book as the analytic "vestibule" to the television image — the title's pun ("Mad Vestibule to TV") naming both *MAD* magazine and the antechamber through which a print-literate culture can be led to understand the new electric mosaic. McLuhan places it deliberately after the chapter on the print, fixing attention "upon the persistent print-like, and even crude woodcut, characteristics of our twentieth-century comics," and then arguing that the same low-definition qualities reappear in "the mosaic mesh of the TV image." The chapter is almost entirely a concrete reapplication of the Part I apparatus — [[Media Hot and Cold]], [[The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium]], [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]], and [[The Global Village]] — rather than a coining of new terms.

The governing claim is that comics, "being low in definition, are a highly participational form of expression," and that this is exactly what makes them "a useful approach to understanding the TV image." The TV image is itself a crude low-definition mosaic the viewer must complete: from the three million dots a second, "the viewer is able to accept, in an iconic grasp, only a few dozen seventy or so, from which to shape an image. The image thus made is as crude as that of the comics." McLuhan sets up a single line of descent in definition and participation — "The print is clue to the comic cartoon, just as the cartoon is clue to understanding the TV image" — making this chapter a worked demonstration of [[The Mosaic]] and the [[Hot vs Cool Media]] distinction (comics = cool, TV = cooling).

The chapter then stages a vivid case of the reversal of the overheated medium and the content principle: the arrival of cool TV cooled the whole American sensory environment and turned the previously *hot* media into comic-strip content. *MAD* "offers, merely, a ludicrous and cool replay of the forms of the hot media of photo, radio, and film," and "TV, in fact, turned the previous hot media of photo, film, and radio into a comic-strip world by simply featuring them as overheated packages." Al Capp's *Li'l Abner* — "the biggest casualty of the TV impact" — dramatizes the same reversal at the level of cultural perception: TV's "mandate of participation in depth" made Capp's hot satirical distortion stop working and pushed all of American enterprise, "from Life to General Motors, and from the classroom to the Executive Suite," toward "ever more audience involvement and participation." From this McLuhan extracts a periodizing corollary — "The pictorial consumer age Is dead. The iconic age is upon us" — and a clutch of memorable sub-claims (the "Johnny can't read" paradox; popular art as the clown / "integral man"; electric appliances as "new forms of work, decentralized and made available to everybody") that this summary captures and links to the Part I concepts rather than minting as new notes.

## Key Concepts

- [[Media Hot and Cold]] — comics are explicitly classed as low in definition and therefore highly participational, requiring completion or fill-in by the reader; TV is the cooling medium. The chapter is a direct application of the hot/cool distinction.
- [[The Mosaic]] — both the newspaper page and the TV image are mosaic forms the viewer must assemble; the comic is "perfectly adapted to the mosaic form of the newspaper," and TV is "the mosaic mesh of the TV image."
- [[The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium]] — the print- and woodcut-form persists as the content/configuration of the comic and re-emerges in TV: "MAD is the old print and woodcut image that recurs in various media today."
- [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] — cool TV turns the older hot media (photo, film, radio) into "overheated packages," a comic-strip world; *MAD* is the emblem of that cool replay.
- [[The Global Village]] — TV's "mandate of participation in depth" "involved everybody in everybody more deeply than before," forcing all American enterprise to refocus toward audience involvement.
- [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]] — the TV mosaic is "iconic," "tactile," "sculptural" and "shaggy" rather than the "sharp and glossy" pictorial/visual order of photo and print.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — TV "cooled off the American audience, giving it new preferences and new orientation of sight and sound and touch and taste."
- [[The Medium Is the Message]] — the elders read only the mayhem-content of comics and "noticed nothing about the form," so "they could discern nothing of the contents, either"; the form, not the content, is the message.
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the do-it-yourself participational character of electric technology means "all electric appliances, far from being labor-saving devices, are new forms of work, decentralized and made available to everybody."
- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — "The comic strip and the ad, then, both belong to the world of games, to the world of models and extensions of situations elsewhere."
- [[The Artist as Integral Awareness]] — popular art as the clown / "integral man" who reminds a specialist society "of all the life and faculty that we have omitted from our daily routines"; the highbrow (Joyce, Picasso) values it as "an authentic imaginative reaction to official action."

## Key Arguments

- Comics are a "useful approach" to the TV image because both are low-definition, high-participation mosaics — grounding [[Media Hot and Cold]], [[The Mosaic]], and [[The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium]].
- TV cooled the American sensorium, turning the older hot media into comic-strip content and forcing every enterprise toward participation in depth — grounding [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]], [[The Global Village]], and the [[Hot vs Cool Media]] tension.
- In the depth-involvement age "Johnny can't read" not from illiteracy but because consumer-style reading is "too superficial and consumerlike an activity"; the real loss is the ability to "visualize distant goals" — a print/electric formulation supporting [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]] and the [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] cluster.
- Popular art (the clown / integral man) supplies "an authentic imaginative reaction to official action" that genteel art evades — supporting [[The Artist as Integral Awareness]] and [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]].

## Important Definitions

- [[Hot and Cool Media (McLuhan)]] — reinforced by demonstration: comics as exemplary cool/low-definition/high-participation media, and TV as the cooling medium. This chapter is a corroborating source, not a fresh definition.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- "Mad Vestibule to TV" — the title figure: comics (and *MAD*) as the antechamber through which print culture enters the TV room.
- The clown / "integral man" — popular art as the clown who restores the wholeness omitted by a specialist society (realized via [[The Artist as Integral Awareness]]).
- Swift's mirror of satire — "Satire ... is a glass in which we see every countenance but our own."
- The "asphalt jungle" — the industrial-mechanical urban environment whose violence the comics translate.
- Comic books "as difficult to decipher as the Book of Kells" / "as exotic as eighth-century illuminations" — the low-definition mosaic comic likened to medieval iconic/illuminated forms.
- *MAD* magazine ("Build up your Ego with MAD") — period emblem of the cool replay of hot media; "a kind of newspaper mosaic of the ad as entertainment, and entertainment as a form of madness." (Captured here, not minted as a vault symbol.)

## Major Tensions

- [[Hot vs Cool Media]] — the master opposition: hot media (photo, film, radio; high-definition, glossy, pictorial, "Square") vs the cool TV/comic mosaic (low-definition, iconic, tactile, participational).
- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the "asphalt jungle" of the "industrial and mechanical environment" vs the participational do-it-yourself electric technology in which appliances are "new forms of work."
- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] — the "specialist situation" of the industrial/visual order vs the "integral man" and "depth involvement" of the iconic age; the pictorial-consumer age vs the iconic depth-age.
- [[Numbness vs Awareness]] — "our trained incapacity to relate one situation to another" let Capp's sardonic realism be mistaken for humor, and the elders "noticed nothing about the form."
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the detached spectatorship of the hot/consumer order vs TV's "participation in depth" that "involved everybody in everybody more deeply than before."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A media-archaeology of "low-definition" form for a material/immaterial essay: the print → comic → TV mosaic as a single line of descent in definition and participation, read alongside Hayles on the materiality of informatics and Lippard on the conceptual-art document.
- The "iconic age / end of the consumer phase" periodization as a hinge between McLuhan and Han: McLuhan's cool TV "involving everybody in everybody" and ending the "pictorial consumer age" set beside Han's exhibition value and the participation/transparency society (interpretive resonance only — they do not cite each other).
- "Johnny can't read" as a vivid case for an essay on attention, depth-involvement, and the loss of linear/distant goals under electric and digital media — a McLuhan precursor to Han's narration-vs-addition diagnosis.
- The clown / "integral man" against the specialist order: popular art as authentic reaction to "official action," useful for a curatorial argument about embodiment, wholeness, and the fragmentation of the specialist/industrial subject.
- "All electric appliances ... are new forms of work, decentralized and made available to everybody" as an early statement of the participational labour of electric technology — a bridge to immaterial labour and the do-it-yourself user of the networked age.
