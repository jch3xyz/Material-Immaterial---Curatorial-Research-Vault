# Source Summary: Conclusion — What Does It Mean to be Posthuman?

## Orientation

The Conclusion gathers the book's three braided stories — "how information lost its body, how the cyborg was constructed in the postwar years as technological artifact and cultural icon, and how the human became the posthuman" — and asks what the posthuman finally means by interrogating the two affects it provokes. Hayles opens where the book opened: the prospect of becoming posthuman "both evokes terror and excites pleasure." The terror she traces to the apocalyptic line of thinkers (Hans Moravec, and behind him the late, pessimistic Warren McCulloch) who read "post" literally, imagining humans displaced as the dominant life-form by intelligent machines. The pleasure she locates in the chance "of getting out of some of the old boxes" — of moving from the presence/absence dialectic into the pattern/randomness dialectic, in which randomness is "the creative ground from which pattern can emerge" rather than mere lack. The chapter's rhetorical posture is therefore corrective and recuperative: it concedes the dystopian readings but refuses to let them exhaust the term.

The central move — and the chapter's most quoted sentence — is a surgical separation: "What is lethal is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self." Moravec's dream of downloading "you" into a computer is not an abandonment of the autonomous liberal subject but its expansion into immortality; the danger is the smuggled-in humanism, not the posthuman. Against this, Hayles grounds the posthuman in embodiment (drawing on Jared Diamond and evolutionary biology to insist the body is "the net result of thousands of years of sedimented evolutionary history") and in distributed cognition. Edwin Hutchins's ethnography of naval navigation and his re-reading of John Searle's Chinese Room supply the key example: "it is not Searle but the entire room that knows Chinese." Cognition and even decision-making are distributed across human and nonhuman actors, so "conscious agency has never been 'in control'." This lets her stage a benign substitution list — emergence for teleology, reflexive epistemology for objectivism, distributed cognition for autonomous will, embodiment for the body-as-support-system, partnership for mastery.

The chapter closes on a programmatic, political note. Joseph Weizenbaum's insistence that judgment is "a uniquely human function" is shown to depend on exactly the liberal-humanist equation of selfhood with conscious agency that the posthuman dissolves; the feminist critics of science (Donna Haraway, Sandra Harding, Evelyn Fox Keller, Carolyn Merchant) supply the genealogy linking the desire for mastery to objectivist science and the domination of nature. Echoing Bruno Latour's "we have never been modern," Hayles offers her signature inversion — "we have always been posthuman" — and frames the term as a contested construction, not a destiny: "The best possible time to contest for what the posthuman means is now." ## Key Concepts

- [[Posthumanism]] — the posthuman as the end of "a certain conception of the human," not the end of humanity; a term to be contested rather than feared.
- [[Embodiment]] — "human being is first of all embodied being"; the body as "the net result of thousands of years of sedimented evolutionary history."
- [[Cybernetics]] — the discipline "born in a froth of noise" whose seriated history grounds the claim that "we have always been posthuman."
- [[Feedback Loops]] — recursive loops in intelligent-agent (email-sorting) programs that "readjust the weights," modeling distributed delegation of attention.
- [[Systems Theory]] — Bateson's claim that surviving organisms are those "whose internal structures are good metaphors for the complexities without"; the world as "one system."
- Distributed cognition — cognition residing "not in humans alone but in the complex interactions within an environment that includes both human and nonhuman actors" (Hutchins).
- Pattern/randomness — the dialectic in which "randomness rather than pattern is invested with plenitude," displacing presence/absence.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the posthuman "grounded in embodied actuality rather than disembodied information."
- [[The Body as Data]] — the body reframed as "a congealed metaphor," readable through information but irreducibly evolutionary and material.

## Key Arguments

- The lethal element is the graft, not the posthuman — "What is lethal is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self."
- Conscious agency was never in control — "the very illusion of control bespeaks a fundamental ignorance about the nature of the emergent processes."
- The benign substitution list — "emergence replaces teleology; reflexive epistemology replaces objectivism; distributed cognition replaces autonomous will; embodiment replaces a body seen as a support system for the mind."
- We have always been posthuman — by analogy with Latour's "we have never been modern," cybernetics' seriated history shows the human was never the bounded liberal subject.
- The meaning of the posthuman must be contested now — "before the trains of thought it embodies have been laid down so firmly that it would take dynamite to change them."

## Important Definitions

- The posthuman (Hayles, negative definition) — "it does not really mean the end of humanity. It signals instead the end ofa certain conception of the human."
- Distributed cognition (via Hutchins) — "it is not Searle but the entire room that knows Chinese"; thinking as bringing structures "into coordination so they can shape and be shaped by one another."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The body as "a congealed metaphor" — a physical structure whose constraints were "formed by an evolutionary history that intelligent machines do not share."
- The Chinese Room (Searle, re-read by Hutchins) — turned from an argument against machine thinking into an emblem of distributed cognition.
- Noise / "a froth of noise" — randomness as "the creative ground from which pattern can emerge"; "the noise crashes within as well as without."
- The splice — human capability "seen precisely to depend on the splice rather than being imperiled by it."

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — "extending embodied awareness in highly specific, local, and material ways" versus the "bodiless exultation of cyberspace."
- Terror vs pleasure — the posthuman "both evokes terror and excites pleasure"; apocalyptic displacement against the exhilaration of new conceptual configurations.
- Autonomous will vs distributed cognition — Weizenbaum's "ethical imperative that humans keep control" against Hutchins's distributed decision-making.
