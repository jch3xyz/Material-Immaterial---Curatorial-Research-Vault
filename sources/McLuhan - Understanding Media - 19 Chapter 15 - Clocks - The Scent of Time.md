# Source Summary: Chapter 15 — Clocks: The Scent of Time

**Book:** [[Understanding Media]]
**Author:** [[Marshall McLuhan]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/19 Chapter 15 - Clocks - The Scent of Time]]

## Orientation

This is a Part II case study in which McLuhan runs a single medium — the mechanical clock — through the apparatus assembled in Part I. The clock is treated as a machine of *uniform fragmentation*: "a machine that produces uniform seconds, minutes, and hours on an assembly-line pattern," processing time so that it is "separated from the rhythms of human experience." On this account the clock belongs with the phonetic alphabet, printing, and the assembly line as a means of "uniform fragmentation of processes," and it is therefore an instance of [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — "the machine is an extension or outering of a process." The chapter reinforces, rather than mints, McLuhan's theory; its work is to show that abstract, visualized, segmented time is a *technological* effect, not a natural given, and to read the clock against the larger movement from the visual-mechanical age into the electric one.

Its sharpest, most quotable claim corrects Lewis Mumford. Where Mumford gave the clock priority in mechanizing society, McLuhan insists the clock "takes no account of the phonetic alphabet as the technology that had made possible the visual and uniform fragmentation of time": [[Abstract time was created by literacy reinforced by the clock not by the clock alone]]. The clock can dominate only where "the prior acceptance of the visual stress that is inseparable from phonetic literacy" already exists — which is why monastic synchronization "was unthinkable, save in highly literate communities." The same logic ties the clock to [[Repeatability is the core of the mechanical principle that has dominated the West]] and to the analytic-sequential pattern McLuhan finds running through print, the wheel, and the assembly line.

The chapter is also the source text behind the vault's two olfactory time-metaphors. McLuhan's titular figure — the *scent of time*, drawn from the Chinese and Japanese incense clocks that "measured time by graduations of incense" — names an integral, involving, audile-tactile temporality opposed to the deodorized visual culture of the literate West. This is [[The Scent of Time (McLuhan)]], and it stands as the probable upstream source of Byung-Chul Han's later [[Fragrance of Time]] — a cross-author bridge recorded in [[Marshall McLuhan and Byung-Chul Han]]. Finally, the chapter closes by folding the clock into the reversal thesis: under electric, organic, simultaneous conditions, mechanical clock-time "begins to feel unacceptable, if only because it is uniform," and "Plurality-of-times succeeds uniformity-of-time" — an instance of [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] and of [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]].

## Key Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the clock as a case: "the machine is an extension or outering of a process"; the clock outers/processes time into uniform units, separating it "from the rhythms of human experience."
- [[Media as Translators]] — escapement (the c.1300 crown-rod-and-balance-wheel mechanism) as a literal translation of "the continuous force of the wheel into the visual principle of uniform but segmented succession," anticipating the infinitesimal calculus. (Escapement itself is not yet a note; rendered as plain text below.)
- [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] — under electric speed mechanical clock-time becomes "starkly incongruous"; "the wheel itself, is obsolescent and is retracting into animal form."
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — electric technology "extends, not our eyes, but our central nervous systems as a planetary vesture," making uniform mechanical time intolerable.
- [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]] — the closing eye/ear thesis; McLuhan recasts Eliade's sacred/profane as auditory/visual: "The visual desacralizes the universe."
- [[Sense Ratios]] — scent-time vs. clock-time embody different proportions among the senses; smell as "the most iconic" sense, engaging "the entire human sensorium."
- [[The Phonetic Alphabet]] — the literacy that "made possible the visual and uniform fragmentation of time" and that the clock presupposes.
- [[Typographic Man]] — the print-and-clock culture of "homogeneity and uniformity that ink-browed Gutenberg rules as his domain," with its "habits of detachment and specialist attention."
- [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] — the clock "dragged man out of the world of seasonal rhythms," yet electric conditions make the return to Nature and tribe "fatally simple."
- [[The Global Village]] — "On a planet reduced to village size by new media, cities themselves appear quaint and odd."
- [[Autoamputation]] / [[Narcosis]] — restated: "when the spell of the gimmick or an extension of our bodies is new, there comes narcosis or numbing to the newly amplified area."
- [[Money as a Medium]] — McLuhan explicitly cross-references the "Money" chapter: clocks, like money, "create new work and wealth by accelerating the pace of human association."

## Major Tensions

- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — uniform, sequential clock-time vs. organic, simultaneous electric time; "the mechanical begins to yield to organic unity under conditions of electric speeds."
- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] — time "hacked into uniform successive bits" vs. "the plastic image by which all of the senses coexist in a unified pattern."
- [[Numbness vs Awareness]] — the narcosis of the newly amplified area vs. the "full awareness" now possible of "the mechanical as an interlude between two great organic periods of culture."
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — odor-rich oral cultures "so profoundly unified as to resist every kind of change" vs. literate "habits of detachment and specialist attention."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **The deodorized clock vs. the scent of time** — pair [[The Scent of Time (McLuhan)]] with Han's [[Fragrance of Time]] to trace a single critique of abstract, uniform, accelerated time from 1964 to the present; the material/immaterial axis here is sensory (the inhaled, lingering, whole-body scent-clock against the seen, segmented, detached dial).
- **Time as a fabricated medium** — use the Mumford correction ([[Abstract time was created by literacy reinforced by the clock not by the clock alone]]) to argue that "natural" duration is an artifact of literacy + the clock, a precise instance of [[Media as Extensions of Man]].
- **Escapement as translation** — the c.1300 escapement as a clean, concrete figure for McLuhan's medium-as-translation thesis ([[Media as Translators]]): continuous force "translated" into uniform, segmented, visual succession, "a direct anticipation of the infinitesimal calculus."
