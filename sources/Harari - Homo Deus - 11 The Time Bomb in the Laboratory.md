## Orientation

This is the pivotal chapter of *Homo Deus*, in which Harari sets a "time bomb" under the liberal order using the findings of the twenty-first-century life sciences. The argument's posture is that of a fact-check rather than a value-attack: Harari concedes that science cannot adjudicate whether liberals are *right* to value liberty over equality or the individual over the collective, but insists that liberalism, "like every other religion," also rests on *factual* claims — and those claims do not survive scrutiny. The two factual pillars at issue are (1) that humans have free will and (2) that each human has a single, indivisible, authentic self. Both are the empirical premises [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] needs, and both are dismantled here.

The chapter proceeds in three movements. First, the demolition of free will: once scientists "opened up the Sapiens black box," they found "neither soul, nor free will, nor 'self' – but only genes, hormones and neurons." Brain events are deterministic, random, or a probabilistic mix — none of which is free — and evolution is "the last nail in freedom's coffin," since natural selection cannot operate on freely chosen desires. The decisive distinction is between *acting on* desires (which humans, parrots and dogs all do) and *choosing* them (which nothing does). This grounds [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]].

Second, the technological corollary: if organisms have no free agent inside, their desires can be predicted, installed and deleted by drugs, genetic engineering or brain stimulation — the seed of the book's signature thesis [[Organisms Are Algorithms]]. Harari illustrates with the remote-controlled "robo-rat" that "feels Nirvana," the Hadassah depression implant whose patient relapsed when "the computer's battery had run out of power," US military brain-chip and transcranial-stimulation programs, and journalist Sally Adee's sniper-simulator experience in which "everything in my head finally shut up." The political payload follows: once such manipulation is routine, "the supposedly free will of customers will become just another product we can buy." Third, the dissolution of the single self into [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self]] — argued via Sperry and Gazzaniga's split-brain studies (the left-brain "interpreter" that confabulates plausible stories, figured as [[The Private CIA and State Department]]) and Kahneman's cold-water and colonoscopy experiments (the duration-blind "peak-end rule"). Humans, Harari concludes, "aren't individuals. They are 'dividuals'," and the self itself is "an imaginary story, just like nations, gods and money." The chapter closes on its thesis sentence: doubts about free will and the self are ancient and harmless until "translated into everyday technology," at which point the real threat is technological, not philosophical.

## Key Concepts

- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the book's signature thesis in seed form: a living being is a biochemical/electrochemical system obeying physical law, with no soul or free agent inside, so desires can be predicted and engineered.
- [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]] — free will as an empty term on a par with "soul"; we act on desires we do not author.
- [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self]] — the moment-to-moment consciousness versus the story-telling, memory-keeping, "duration-blind" self we identify with when we say "I."
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the single, free, indivisible individual whose factual basis this chapter dismantles ("dividuals").
- [[Eliminativism]] — opening the "Sapiens black box" reveals "neither soul, nor free will, nor 'self' – but only genes, hormones and neurons."
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the free individual as "a fictional tale concocted by an assembly of biochemical algorithms."
- [[The Body as Data]] — the brain as readable, writable electric patterns (scanners predicting choice; transcranial stimulation).
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — the divided brain and "cacophony of conflicting voices": cognition with no central unified controller.
- [[The Black Box]] — the eighteenth-century human as "a mysterious black box" later opened to reveal only mechanism.
- [[Narrativity]] — resonance (not merger): Harari's narrating self privileges story over the mere stream of experience, rhyming with Han's faculty of narration.
- [[Smart Power]] / [[Dataism]] — resonance: brain-pattern prediction and "free will... just another product we can buy" as the *Homo Deus* analogue of power that steers desire from inside.
- [[Society of Control]] — Harari's "dividuals" is Deleuze's coinage (shared term, neuropsychological register).
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — bracketed here: the experiencing self is the "flash of experience," the narrating self its story.

## Important Definitions

- [[The Narrating Self and the Experiencing Self (Harari)]] — the experiencing self as "moment-to-moment consciousness" that "remembers nothing"; the narrating self as the entity that retrieves memories, makes big decisions, is "duration-blind," and applies the "peak-end rule."
- Free will (Harari) — the two senses Harari separates: (a) acting on one's desires (which humans, chimpanzees, dogs and parrots all do) versus (b) choosing one's desires (which no organism does). Liberalism needs (b); only (a) survives. *(Carried inside the free-will concept note; rendered as plain text.)*
- Peak-end rule / duration-neglect — the narrating self "remembers only the peak moment and the end moment, and evaluates the whole experience according to their average." *(Held within the experiencing/narrating-self notes; plain text.)*
