## Orientation

A short, dense catalogue essay (1992, written for the exhibition "Digital Photography" and first published in French) in which Ascott applies his telematic theory to a single medium. The argument is that photography has crossed a threshold "from a stable medium" into what he names "post-photographic practice": the digital photograph is no longer a fixed material image the viewer *absorbs* but a permeable, layered datum within *hypermedia* that the viewer *immerses* in, *navigates*, and — crucially — *transforms*. The essay re-stages Ascott's career-long thesis (the artwork as an open behavioural system completed by the participating spectator) for the digital image: authorship is distributed "across time" to a viewer who can "distort, collage, dismember, fractionise, and in every way transform the image at will."

Rhetorically the essay opens by dismissing the old documentary-truth anxiety as obsolete. The worry about "faking," digital retouching, and "truth to appearance" presupposes a real that photography fails or counterfeits; Ascott rejects the premise ("reality is built by us"), aligning with Paul Watzlawick's radical constructivism and Richard Rorty's anti-foundationalism. He then historicizes the new condition against Walter Benjamin, naming digital co-production "the second order of reproduction, a stage beyond Walter Benjamin's 'work of art in the age of mechanical reproduction.'" Where Benjamin's age reproduced the image, the digital age lets the viewer co-produce and transform the image itself. The interesting question shifts from *what* photographs mean to *how* they mean, and "the lust for verisimilitude gives way to the love of modeling."

This chapter is a high-value EXTENSION node for the vault's Ascott and Burnham systems/art clusters from the participatory, immaterial-image pole. ## Key Concepts

- [[Post-Photographic Practice]] — Ascott's name for the new condition: photography as a stable medium giving way to a practice that "celebrates instability, uncertainty, incompleteness, and transformation." This chapter is its fullest exposition.
- [[Hypermedia]] — the layered, nonsequential "virtual space" in which the digital photograph floats; "a site of interactivity and connectivity in which the viewer can play an active part in the transformation or affirmation of the images."
- [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]] — Ascott's epistemological frame here (via Watzlawick): "reality is built by us"; photography "has the potential to show how realities are constructed."
- [[The Participator]] — the viewer redefined as co-producer and "manipulator of that image," in whose hands "the destiny of the image may lie"; the digital image distributes authorship across time.
- [[New Media Art]] — EXTENDED from the Ascott pole: digital photography / hypermedia as new-media art; the catalogue artists treat the computer "always as communications devices," not "simply as painting tools."
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — EXTENDED: the digital photograph as an open, behavioural, viewer-completed experience; immersion and navigation supplant absorption.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — EXTENDED: co-production replaces the old one-way economy of the image; "the processes of transformation ... are now in the hands of the viewer as much as the artist."
- [[Information]] — linked: the "media flow ... unending and ubiquitous," data space, and Ascott's "sea of interdata."
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — linked: hypermedia navigation, authorship distributed "across time," and the coming "artificial observer ... the eye of the neural net" joining "the observing system."
- [[The Global Village]] — interpretive link: Ted Nelson's "globally connected knowledge network" and the ubiquitous media flow extend McLuhan's networked global village toward Ascott's network consciousness.
- [[Re-enchantment]] — interpretive link: "the radiance of a radical constructivism," reality "built by us ... to satisfy our conceptual longings," and the closing humanist register read against the technoculture-of-dehumanisation critique.

## Key Arguments

- [[Digital co-production is a second order of reproduction beyond Benjamin]] — periodization: Benjamin's age of mechanical reproduction reproduced the image; the digital age lets the viewer co-produce and transform the image itself. "This is the stage of co-production."
- [[Computerized photographic practice is profoundly human not dehumanising]] — the catalogue artists deploy high technology to make human themes (family, ecology, political oppression) more vivid, confounding critics of the electronic age.
- [[The dichotomy of artist and viewer resolves into a single participating user]] — linked: the openness of the digitized image plus its public distribution (CD-I, scanners) repositions the viewer from absorber to manipulator/co-author.

## Important Definitions

- [[Post-Photographic Practice]] — practice in which the photograph ceases to be a stable, finished material object and becomes an unstable, layered, viewer-transformable datum within hypermedia.
- [[Hypermedia]] — per Ascott's note 2: technology that "provides for a nonsequential inputting and navigation of multimedia data (photo, graphics, text, video, audio)."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Window vs the Doorway]] — the analogue photograph as a window onto actualities; the digital as a doorway into potentialities; the spatial figure for absorption→immersion.
- The aquatic / fluid metaphor — "the metaphor for this practice is emphatically aquatic"; images and meanings *float*, *flood*, and *flow* in a "sea of interdata" (anticipates Ascott's later "moist" / [[Moistmedia]]).
- The permeable, woven data field — the digital image as "a lightly woven structure," "a permeable data field," Esther Parada's "electronic loom."
- The ink-jet printer as metaphor and instrument — its "very precision" is what licenses "a semantic uncertainty" and "fuzzy shifts in meaning."
- The moved Giza pyramid (*National Geographic*, Feb 1982) — the emblematic "digitally moved" image; the origin-anecdote of the truth-anxiety Ascott dismisses.

## Major Tensions

- [[Absorption vs Immersion]] — classical absorption of a bounded image vs immersion-and-navigation in a hypermedia field.
- [[Verisimilitude vs Modeling]] — truth-to-appearance vs the construction of realities: "the lust for verisimilitude gives way to the love of modeling."
