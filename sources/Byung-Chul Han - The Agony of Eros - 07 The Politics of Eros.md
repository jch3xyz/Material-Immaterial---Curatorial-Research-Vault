# Source Summary: The Politics of Eros

**Book:** [[The Agony of Eros]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/07 The Politics of Eros]]

## Orientation

The penultimate and shortest chapter of *The Agony of Eros* extends Han's theory of love from the intimate register into the **political** one. Its thesis: eros is the ground of the *We* — of communal action, of political revolt, of the courage for radical change — so the contemporary erosion of eros (its degradation into sexuality and pornography under neoliberalism) is at the same time the erosion of the political. "Neoliberalism is depoliticizing society in general—and not least of all, by replacing eros with sexuality and pornography." Where eros withers, so does the capacity for a shared political project; "Communal action—a we—now proves impossible."

Han runs the argument through three frames. The **Platonic** frame supplies the tripartite soul — pleasure-based desire (*epithumia*), spiritedness or courage (*thumos*), and reason (*logos*) — and locates the political charge in the middle term: "Thumos, then, would be where eros and politics touch." Today desire dominates, *thumos* decays into mere annoyance "which lacks the negativity of rupture," and, stripped of eros, *logos* "is deteriorating into data-driven calculation, which is incapable of reckoning the event, the incalculable." The **Badiouian** frame recasts love as a "Two scene," an *event* marked by "the negativity of upheaval" and demanding "fidelity" — an eventfulness that "connects love with politics and art." The **surrealist** frame treats the reinvention of love as "an artistic, existential, and political gesture" and exalts eros as "the energetic source of renewal, which also feeds political action."

Rhetorically the chapter is argumentative rather than image-driven, and its posture is affirmative and utopian: eros is figured as a revolutionary energy "faithful to what is yet to come." Han is the authorial voice throughout, but he builds on quoted sources — **attribute the "Two scene," "secret resonance," "musical instruments," and *habiter* passages to [[Alain Badiou]]** (from *In Praise of Love* and *Ethics*, cited by Han), the "leading him further than the stars … is eroticism" line to **André Breton** (quoted by Han via Alyce Mahon), and the surrounding synthesis into a single "politics of eros" to Han. (Unlike the book's Foreword, chapter 7 is not itself by Badiou.)

## Key Concepts

- [[The Politics of Eros]] — the master concept of the chapter: eros is "a source of energy for political revolt and engagement," the ground of the *We*; its neoliberal collapse into *epithumia* is the collapse of the political.
- [[Eros]] — Plato's eros as a universal, soul-guiding, world-birthing power that "stands above both desire and thumos" and "makes thumos bring forth beautiful deeds"; this chapter supplies its Platonic and political-revolutionary face.
- [[The Other]] — the "wholly Other" whose negativity eros introduces and sexuality forecloses; the sexual object "is not the Other" and "never calls me into question."
- [[The Event]] — Badiou's event as "the negativity of rupture, which allows something wholly Other to begin"; fidelity to the event is "a universal quality of eros" that binds love, politics, and art.
- [[Pornography (Han)]] — the "One scene" of auto-affection that "erases otherness altogether" and "intensifies narcissification."
- [[Hell of the Same]] — sexuality and pornography reproduce "the order of the Habitual and the Same"; love "interrupts the Same in favor of the Other."
- [[Achievement Society]] — "a burnout society of isolated, self-alienated achievement-subjects" in which *thumos* and the We wither away.
- [[Negativity]] — eros as bearer of "the negativity of rupture," "the negativity of upheaval," "the negativity of the otherness"; the antithesis of neoliberal positivity.
- [[Dataism]] — the data-driven calculation into which eros-less *logos* degenerates, "incapable of reckoning the event, the incalculable."
- [[Bare Life]] — the reduction of love to consumable sexuality is continuous with the book's bare-life diagnosis of the achievement-subject.

## Key Arguments

- The erosion of eros is the erosion of the political — neoliberalism depoliticizes by replacing eros with sexuality and pornography, hollowing out *thumos* and making "a we" impossible. *(Directly stated; realized inside [[The Politics of Eros]].)*
- Thumos is where eros and politics touch — thumotic rage "radically breaks with convention and inaugurates a new state of affairs," and its decay into annoyance "allows circumstances to persist." *(Directly stated; realized inside [[The Politics of Eros]].)*
- Sexuality occupies the "One scene" and reproduces the Same, while love as a "Two scene" event admits the wholly Other and denarcissifies. *(Directly stated; realized across [[Eros vs Sexuality]] and [[Pornography (Han)]].)*
- Fidelity to the event binds love, politics, and art (following Badiou). *(Directly stated, Badiou-sourced; realized inside [[The Politics of Eros]] and [[The Event]].)*
- Without eros, *logos* degenerates into calculation blind to the event and the incalculable. *(Directly stated; cross-linked to [[Dataism]].)*

## Important Definitions

- **Thumos** — the spirited/courageous middle part of the Platonic soul, between *epithumia* (desire) and *logos* (reason); the seat of rage and rupture, and the point "where eros and politics touch." *(Han via Plato/Pfaller; captured inside [[The Politics of Eros]] and [[Eros vs Sexuality]] — no standalone note.)*
- **The event (Badiou, via Han)** — "a moment of 'truth'" that "introduces a new and entirely different way of being into the habit of habiter," whose essence is "the negativity of rupture, which allows something wholly Other to begin," and which demands "fidelity." *(Folded into [[The Event]].)*

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- **"Two musical instruments … mysteriously converge when unified by a great musician"** — Badiou's figure (quoted by Han) for the "secret resonance" between a life under a political idea and the intensity of love.
- **"A 'rupture,' a 'hole' in the order of the Habitual and the Same"** — the figure for what love as an event does to habituation and narcissism.
- **"Leading him further than the stars … is eroticism"** — Breton's figure for the transcending power of eros (no dedicated symbol note; the chapter is argumentative rather than image-driven).

## Major Tensions

- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — thumotic rage (the negativity of rupture) versus annoyance (which "lacks the negativity of rupture"); the wholly Other versus the self-identical sexual object.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **Eros as political energy against depoliticization** — pairs Han's claim that neoliberalism depoliticizes "by replacing eros with sexuality and pornography" with the [[Achievement Society]] / burnout diagnosis; an essay on why the exhausted, self-optimizing subject cannot form a *We*.
- **The event versus the calculable** — "without eros, logos is deteriorating into data-driven calculation, which is incapable of reckoning the event, the incalculable" bridges directly to [[Dataism]] and [[Big Data]]; an essay on how quantification forecloses the incalculable / the future.
- **The One scene and the Two scene** — Han's use of Badiou to distinguish narcissistic auto-affection (pornography, the Same) from the denarcissifying encounter with the [[The Other]]; ties the erotic critique to [[Hell of the Same]].
- **Revolutionary eros / the reinvention of love** — the surrealist frame (Breton) as a model for art that is simultaneously "artistic, existential, and political," "faithful to what is yet to come"; an essay on eros as the ground shared by love, politics, and art.
