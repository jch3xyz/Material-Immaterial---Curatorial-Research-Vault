# Source Summary: 15 Art and Telematics: towards a Network Consciousness

## Orientation

This 1984 essay (originally published in *Art + Telecommunication*, ed. Heidi Grundmann; Shanken edition pp. 186–201) is the foundational text of Ascott's *telematic* turn — the chapter in which he first systematically theorizes the convergence of computers and telecommunications as at once a new artistic medium, a new aesthetic, and a transformation of human consciousness. It opens autobiographically ("In Mill Valley, California in the spring of 1978, I got high on networking") and narrates Ascott's own early projects: his 1980 National Endowment for the Arts / Infomedia Notepad networking project; the 1980 Saturn Encounter computer conference shadowing NASA's Voyager II; the 1982 planetary I Ching throw in Bob Adrian's "World in 24 Hours" at Ars Electronica; and the 1983 distributed-authorship work "La Plissure du Texte" at the Musée d'Art moderne de la Ville de Paris. The rhetorical posture is hopeful, even visionary, and self-aware about it — Ascott repeatedly hedges his own optimism ("without being foolishly optimistic," "perhaps naively") while insisting on the medium's affirmative, convivial, "addictive" phenomenology against the cold connotations of "information technology." (Confidence: Directly stated — these projects, dates, and the conversion image are all in the raw text.)

The argument proceeds in three moves. First, it names a **new medium**: borrowing "telematics" from Nora and Minc's *L'Informatisation de la société*, Ascott designates the marriage of computers and communications networks a "paradigm change in our culture" and "a quantum leap in human consciousness," reserving "networking" for the activity and "telematics" for the medium as a whole. Second, it proposes a **new aesthetic**: telematic networking dissolves the one-way sender/receiver, artist/viewer dichotomy of post-Renaissance perspectival art into a single participating "user" who, in accessing the system, transforms it — so that the artwork is no longer a discrete object but "a web of relationships between ideas and images in constant flux." He grounds this art-historically in the tilt of the picture plane from vertical to horizontal (Duchamp; Steinberg's "flatbed picture plane"; Jackson Pollock's "alloverness" and "all-at-onceness" as "the very epitome of telematic networking"). Third, it points toward a **new consciousness** — out-of-body, timeless, oceanic-but-precise — figured through the master metaphor of weaving/web (Capra, Bohm, Barthes's "Text means tissue," tantra, Indra's net) and the cosmic register of the planetary brain (Teilhard's noosphere, Peter Russell's "global brain"). (Confidence: Directly stated.)

Within the vault this chapter is the telematic CORE: it anchors `Telematic Art`, `Network Consciousness`, `Distributed Authorship`, and `Telematic Discourse`, extends the Burnham-realized `New Media Art`, `Art as Experiential Technology`, and `Reciprocal Communication`, and cross-links forcefully to McLuhan (twice quoted — electric implosion, "no detachment of frame"), to the Hayles/Haraway problematic of the networked, out-of-body, distributed body/mind, and to the O'Gieblyn/Harari register of planetary consciousness and technological re-enchantment. It also carries an internal counterpoint: read through Foucault's *L'Ordre du discours*, Ascott deploys the order-of-discourse to argue that telematics *escapes* control, where Han later deploys Foucault for control — and Ascott himself notes that timesharing systems sit "in the hands of the multinational corporations and the military," the same networks Haraway reads as "the informatics of domination." (Confidence: Interpretive synthesis for the vault-internal bridges; Directly stated for the McLuhan/Foucault citations and the corporate/military observation.)

## Key Concepts

- [[Telematic Art]] — the convergence of computers and telecommunications as a unified artistic medium; "networking" is the activity, "telematics" the medium as a whole.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the planetary, shared consciousness Ascott sees emerging from global telematic networking; the title's destination, folding noosphere and global brain.
- [[Distributed Authorship]] — the dissolution of the solitary author; meaning generated and negotiated across scattered participants, "to which no single authorship is attributable" (realized in "La Plissure du Texte").
- [[Telematic Discourse]] — a new order of discourse, neither speech nor writing, "out-of-body, asynchronous, dispersed, interactive, and semantically layered," read through Foucault.

- [[The Participator]] — the spectator dissolved into a user who, in accessing the system, transforms it.

## Key Arguments

- [[Telematic networking is a quantum leap in human consciousness]] — the convergence of computers and telecommunications is a paradigm change, with a chorus (Nora/Minc, Bell, Teilhard, Russell, Henderson, Bateson) seeing a planetary consciousness emerge.
- [[The dichotomy of artist and viewer resolves into a single participating user]] — perspectival art is a one-way, isolating discourse; the horizontal/flatbed turn and now the network resolve sender/receiver into reciprocity.
- [[In telematic art meaning is produced through interaction not transmitted]] — meanings are "negotiated, distributed, transformed, and layered in multiple exchanges," not asserted and consumed in one-way linearity.
- [[Telematic networks constitute a planetary network consciousness]] — the network as "one wholeness... in which every participant reflects every other participant in the whole."
- [[The decentralized structure of telematic networks is subversive]] — read through Foucault, telematic discourse evades the institutional control of discourse and can form an inclusive, planetary "fellowship of discourse."
- [[Process replaces product just as system supersedes structure]] — the telematic artwork is never complete; "the creative use of networks makes them organisms."

## Important Definitions

- [[Telematics (Ascott)]] — after Nora and Minc, "the 'springing to life born of the marriage between computers and communications networks, which will culminate in the arrival of universal satellites, transmitting images, data and sounds'"; Ascott uses "networking" for the activity and "telematics" for the medium as a whole.

## Major Tensions

- [[Horizontal vs Vertical]] — the upright, isolating Renaissance/perspectival view vs the inclusive, holistic "flatbed" orientation (Duchamp → Steinberg → Pollock).
- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — the linear "components" model (source → receiver, persuasion) vs the convergence/cybernetic model of mutual exchange.
- [[Object vs System]] — the discrete artwork vs the never-complete networked organism (the Ascott/Burnham polarity).
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the text as "a concrete entity with its own space" yet "more ethereal and metaphysical than paint, though no less palpable." (extends the EIGHT-author tension)
- [[Liberation vs Control (Telematics)]] — telematic discourse as evading control vs the "controlling institutions," the "telematic imperative," and corporate/military timesharing.
- [[Determinism vs Randomness]] — the precise, deterministic message vs the search for and play with "uncertainty and ambiguity."
