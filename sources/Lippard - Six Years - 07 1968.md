# Source Summary: Six Years — 1968

**Book:** [[Six Years]]
**Author:** [[Lucy Lippard]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/07 1968]]

## Orientation

This is the pivotal year-chronicle of [[Lucy Lippard]]'s *Six Years*, and the year that gives the book its title. The section is a documentary anthology — books, exhibition records, dealer notices, symposium transcripts, and (above all) artist statements quoted verbatim — through which Lippard lets 1968 assemble the vocabulary of conceptual art almost in a single stroke. Her own argumentative voice is sparse; the chronicle is built to let the period speak. 1968 is anchored by the February publication of her essay with John Chandler, "The Dematerialization of Art" (*Art International*), which both names the book and states its thesis: that an "ultra-conceptual art that emphasizes the thinking process almost exclusively" is "provoking a profound dematerialization of art." This is the art-historical instance of the vault's titular axis, [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the object demoted to end-product, the studio "becoming a study."

What makes the 1968 file unusual as a source is that the book documents the critique of its own thesis. Six weeks after the essay, the Art-Language group (Coventry — Terry Atkinson, Michael Baldwin, with Ian Burn and Mel Ramsden in the orbit) sent Lippard and Chandler a letter contesting the very metaphor of "dematerialization" on matter-state grounds: the works in question "may not be an art-object as we know it in its traditional matter-state, but they are nevertheless matter in one of its forms, either solid-state, gas-state, liquid-state." A map is "just as much a solid-state object … as is any Rubens." Lippard prints the rebuttal in full alongside the thesis, so this section is the primary place where [[Material vs Immaterial]] is staged as a live dispute rather than a settled hierarchy — the immaterial pole asserted and immediately denied its autonomy on physical grounds.

Around this dispute, 1968 instantiates several strands the later years will elaborate. Systems aesthetics arrives through [[Hans Haacke]]'s January show and his statement that the work "merges with the environment in a relationship that is better understood as a 'system' of interdependent processes" — "A system is not imagined, it is real" — and through Jack Burnham's "Systems Aesthetics" essay (*Artforum*, September), linking the year to [[Systems Theory]]. The exhibition and catalogue become the medium itself in [[Seth Siegelaub]]'s projects — the Bradford and Windham shows, *The Xerox Book*, and the November [[Douglas Huebler]] catalogue that "exists solely as a catalogue." The document/record displaces the object: [[Jan Dibbets]] declares "The work of art is the photo," Huebler frames language and documents as equivalents of the picture frame, and the postal receipt, certificate, and map recur as both medium and authentication — a conceptual-art "information" aesthetic that links forward to [[Information]] and [[The Materiality of Informatics]]. Finally, the year carries Lippard's incipient political turn: [[Daniel Buren]]'s "art is objectively reactionary," and [[Carl Andre]] refusing the "property state" for his baled-hay piece because it is "not making a piece of sculpture for sale."

## Key Figures

- [[Lucy Lippard]] (with John Chandler) — author of "The Dematerialization of Art" (*Art International*, February 1968), the book's source essay and namesake.
- [[Hans Haacke]] — Howard Wise Gallery (January 13–February 3); first water/condensation boxes, weather and biological-growth pieces, *Wind in Water*, *Live Random Airborne System* (Coney Island, November 30). The year's emblem of systems aesthetics.
- [[Sol LeWitt]] — *46 3-Part Variations on 3 Different Kinds of Cubes* (Dwan Gallery, February), later published as a book; serial/systemic conceptual art.
- [[Seth Siegelaub]] — organizer of the Bradford and Windham shows, Weiner's *Statements*, *The Xerox Book*, and the November Huebler catalogue; pioneer of the catalogue/exhibition-as-medium.
- [[Carl Andre]], [[Robert Barry]], [[Lawrence Weiner]] — the Siegelaub triad (Bradford, February; Windham, April–May). Andre's *Joint* (183 units of baled hay); Barry's nylon-cord and wire pieces; Weiner's *Statements*.
- [[Daniel Buren]] — striped works, *Proposition Didactique* (Salon de Mai, April 27), "Is Teaching Art Necessary?"; the section's art-and-politics voice.
- [[Douglas Huebler]] — the *November 1968* catalogue-exhibition (Siegelaub); *42nd Parallel* (a certified-postal-receipt system piece).
- [[Jan Dibbets]] — perspective corrections and ephemeral-material demonstrations; "The work of art is the photo."
- [[Robert Smithson]] — "A Museum of Language in the Vicinity of Art" and "A Sedimentation of the Mind: Earth Projects" (*Artforum*, September).
- [[Robert Morris]] — "Anti-Form" (*Artforum*, April); *Nine at Leo Castelli* (warehouse, organized December).
- [[On Kawara]] — begins the "I met" / "I went" notebooks (July); date paintings; documentation of existence.
- [[Donald Burgy]] — *Rock #5* (exhaustive scientific documentation of a single rock).
- Mel Ramsden, Ian Burn, and the Art-Language group (Atkinson, Baldwin) — *Secret Painting*, *Six-Negatives*, and the dematerialization-critique letter.
- N. E. Thing Co. (Iain and Elaine/Ingrid Baxter) — *A Portfolio of Piles*, the ACT/ART certificates.
- [[Walter de Maria]] — *50 m² Level Dirt*; "Art Yard"; the desert-lines projects.
- Others recorded: Hanne Darboven, Michael Snow, Adrian Piper, Keith Arnatt, Ian Wilson, Bernar Venet, Dennis Oppenheim, Richard Serra, Rafael Ferrer, Joseph Kosuth, Dan Graham, and the Rosario group of Argentina (Graciela Carnevale, Eduardo Favario, Lia Maisonnave, Renzi).
