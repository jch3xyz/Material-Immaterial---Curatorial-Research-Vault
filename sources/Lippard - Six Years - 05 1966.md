# Source Summary: 1966

**Book:** [[Six Years: The Dematerialization of the Art Object from 1966 to 1972]]
**Author:** [[Lucy Lippard]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/05 1966]]

## Orientation

This is the opening year-chronicle of *Six Years*, and it sets the documentary form the whole book will keep: not an argument in Lippard's voice but an assembled bibliography and chronology — books, manifestos, exhibitions, and verbatim artist statements from 1966. Lippard's own first-person framing is almost entirely absent here. The section is the **seed-bed** of the book's thesis: it gathers the earliest emblems of what the title calls the *dematerialization of the art object*, instantiating that idea through cases rather than defining it. The durable vocabulary the book will later theorize — the object receding into idea, score, document, and process — is shown happening, year zero, across a scatter of scattered events.

The recurring threads are legible already. Art as *idea* or *information* that "need not be visual" (Baldessari). The *score* or *instruction* as the work, inherited from Fluxus (Brecht's *Time-Table Event*, Ono's "Events"). *Language* and logic as medium, "devoid of any formalistic/aesthetic content" (Bernar Venet). The first *earthworks* as models and proposals rather than objects (Long, Morris, Smithson, Weiner, De Maria). The *document* taking the object's plinth — Bochner's xerox notebooks shown as the exhibition. And the *photograph* demoted from fine art to neutral "technical data" (Ruscha's antiphotography books). Across these the artwork's vehicle migrates from the crafted object to the printed publication and the exhibition-as-anthology.

Two pressure points keep the year from reading as a one-way march into the immaterial. First, an embrace of the commodity even within the supposedly resistant gesture: Ruscha's book is a "mass-produced object" with a "very commercial, professional feel," so dematerialization arrives already entangled with the market form it is often read as refusing. Second, an explicit internal dissent: Beuys warns that equating invisibility with the conceptual is "a very materialistic way of thinking" — merely reversing visible into invisible secures nothing. Latham's *STILL & CHEW* furnishes the year's most literal emblem of the whole movement: Clement Greenberg's *Art and Culture* chewed by the guests, dissolved in acid into "a form of sugar," fermented, and bottled — the art-object ingested, transformed, and returned as a document (and a fired teaching post).

Because this is a chronicle, its yield is **entities and emblematic quotes**, not theses. The summary below forward-stubs the artists by canonical full name rather than minting a note per artist, and links the year to the vault's existing immaterial/information axis. The standout cross-book resonance to flag, not assert, is between Lippard's dematerialization of the art object and Hayles's disembodiment of information: two era-defining moves toward treating the immaterial — idea, information, pattern — as more essential than its material substrate.

## Key Concepts

- [[Information]] — the conceptual-art "information aesthetic": Baldessari's information that is "interesting in its own right and need not be visual," Ruscha's photographs as data "for technical or information purposes." Cognate with, but distinct from, Han's positivized language and Hayles's pattern.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the whole year stages the object's recession into idea, score, document, and process; yet Beuys's caution marks an *internal* dissent: invisibility does not equal conceptuality.
- Object vs document — the original drawing versus the xerox copy "not necessarily meant to be viewed as art" (Bochner).
- Resistance vs commodity — the dematerialized work read as refusal of the saleable object, yet Ruscha embraces the "mass-produced object" with a "very commercial, professional feel."
- Neutrality vs concession to art — Nauman tilting a film "as a concession to art" versus Ruscha's demand for "absolutely neutral material."

### Note on the Lippard ↔ Hayles resonance

The connection between Lippard's *dematerialization of the art object* (1966–72) and Hayles's *disembodiment of information* in [[How We Became Posthuman]] — both diagnosing an era-defining elevation of the immaterial (idea, information, pattern) over its material substrate (object, body) — is an **Interpretive synthesis / Speculative connection**, not a claim either author makes about the other. It is recorded here against the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis for the essay pass, flagged for human review.
