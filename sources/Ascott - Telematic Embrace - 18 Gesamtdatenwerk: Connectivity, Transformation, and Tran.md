## Orientation

This 1989 essay (originally published in German in *Kunstforum*, Cologne, 103) is the originating standalone exposition of Ascott's keystone coinage, [[Gesamtdatenwerk]] — the "integrated data work." It is one of his most fully realized telematic-art manifestos, written at the peak of his networked-art period. Its three-part subtitle — Connectivity, Transformation, and Transcendence — names the arc of the argument: from the technical fact of networked interconnection, through the network's power to *transform* information, identity, and reality, to a frankly spiritual claim that computer networking answers a "deep psychological desire for transcendence... a kind of biotechnological theology." The editorial front matter records that descriptions of Ascott's own telematic art projects have been omitted here (they are detailed in chapters 11, 16, and 19), so the chapter is the *theory* stripped of its project illustrations. (Confidence: Directly stated — from the raw front matter and body.)

Its rhetorical posture is visionary and polemical. Ascott opens by proposing that "Networking provides the metaphor for late twentieth-century culture" and that networking, "mediated, accelerated, and intensified by the computer," is uniquely suited to "the overarching project of our time: to make the invisible visible." The middle of the essay develops a sustained analogy he calls "a physics of information": replace "quanta" with "data" and the transformative, indeterminate, observer-dependent ontology of quantum physics is carried over to computer science — grounding a constructivist epistemology in which "reality is the production of observation." From this he derives an explicit rejection of "the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

The chapter also contains Ascott's most quotable redefinitions: the computer is "not primarily a thing, but a set of behaviours" that must be made *invisible*, and the interface should be designed not as a "membrane" walling off a discrete machine-object but as "a doorway into data space, a synaptic interval in a human-computer symbiosis." The keystone is the Gesamtdatenwerk itself: Ascott's deliberate echo of Wagner's *Gesamtkunstwerk* (total artwork), but relocated from the opera house to "the planet as a whole, its data space, its electronic noosphere." This planetary, permanently open, reciprocal work abolishes the distinction between artist and viewer, producer and consumer. For the vault, the chapter is the primary extension point for the art/systems-esthetics cluster Burnham realized ([[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], [[Reciprocal Communication]]) and the Ascott-side anchor for the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension via networking as the route "to reach the immaterial, the spiritual." (Confidence: Directly stated for the quotations; Interpretive synthesis for the vault placement.)

## Key Concepts

- [[Gesamtdatenwerk]] — the "integrated data work"; the planet-scale, permanently open, reciprocal networked artwork echoing Wagner's *Gesamtkunstwerk* but sited in global data space; this chapter is its originating standalone exposition.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — networking as the substrate of a collective, planetary "mind at large"; to network is to amplify individual thought "through the dynamic interaction with others," where "others" includes artificial intelligences, sensing systems, and memory stores.
- [[Data Space]] — the "phase space, the virtual space that data occupy"; data are ephemeral, asynchronous, discontinuous, "everywhere and nowhere," "time-free and time-resistant," making networking "a non-linear creative medium."
- [[The Computer as Behaviour Not Thing]] — Ascott's redefinition of the computer away from object/apparatus/machine toward "a set of behaviours," "pure system, universal transformative matrix," "the agent of the data field."
- [[The Interface as Doorway]] — the interface designed as a doorway into data space and an environment (body-, gaze-, heat-, environment-responsive), part of a "holographic unity," rather than a membrane separating machine from self.
- [[New Media Art]] — extended by this chapter at its network/telematic, planetary, participatory, immaterial pole (the Burnham section is the object/cybernetic precursor; Ascott is the networked successor).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the immersive, environmental, body-/gaze-responsive experiential pole, squarely Ascott's; this chapter is a prime source.
- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the broader telematic-art project of which the Gesamtdatenwerk is the work-form.

## Key Arguments

- [[The project of twentieth-century art is to make the invisible visible]] — networking, amplifying thought, memory, and the senses, is uniquely suited to bring within reach what lies "outside of our perceptual range, the far side of the mind."
- [[Networking answers a desire for transcendence a biotechnological theology]] — networking provides human/artificial-intelligence symbiosis and ultimately satisfies the wish "to reach the immaterial, the spiritual," to be "out of body, out of mind."
- [[Reality is constructed by participatory observation a repudiation of postmodern pessimism]] — from "reality is the production of observation" Ascott derives a plural, "virtual," participation-dependent epistemology that constructively repudiates postmodern relativism's pessimism.
- [[The computer is not a thing but a set of behaviours and must be made invisible]] — if art's project is to render the invisible visible, the "very visible computer" must itself become transparent; using it as "the apparatus of the accountant" condemns one to "art of the 'bottom line'."
- [[The dichotomy of artist and viewer resolves into a single participating user]] — because reciprocity and interaction are of its essence, the networked work "cannot differentiate between 'artist' and 'viewer,' producer and consumer."
- [[Telematic networks constitute a planetary network consciousness]] — "superconnectivity" produces a "multilayered culture, planetary culture, a holistic world art," explicitly *not* homogenization.

## Important Definitions

- [[Gesamtdatenwerk (Ascott)]] — "integrated data work," modeled on and contrasted with Wagner's *Gesamtkunstwerk*; sited on the planet/data space/electronic noosphere; indeterminate in duration; abolishing the artist/viewer split.
- [[A Physics of Information (Ascott)]] — "replace the term 'quanta' with 'data' and a physics of information is invoked"; data as the object whose transformation is computer science's subject, ephemeral, asynchronous, discontinuous.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — networking as the route "to reach the immaterial, the spiritual"; data as "everywhere and nowhere," "time-free and time-resistant." (Ascott's telematic extension of the vault's multi-author tension.)
- [[Object vs System]] — the computer as "thing, as object, as apparatus, as machine" versus as "pure system, universal transformative matrix," "a set of behaviours." (Ascott's behaviourist-telematic version of Burnham's object→system.)
- Finality vs open-endedness — "art of the 'bottom line'" (finalities, completion) versus the network medium's "open-endedness, nonlinearity, and fecund incompleteness." (No note yet; rendered plain text.)
