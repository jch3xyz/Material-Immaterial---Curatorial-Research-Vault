## Orientation

The "Algorithm" chapter is a braided memoir-essay whose driving subject is determinism, prediction, and the question of free will. It is the clearest single demonstration of O'Gieblyn's signature method — [[Technology as Metaphor]] — that each era understands the self and fate through its dominant machine: here the predictive algorithm is mapped explicitly onto the Calvinist God of predestination, Laplace's demon, and the inscrutable bureaucratic state. The chapter does *not* develop the book's transhumanist, mind-uploading, or panpsychist material (those live in other chapters); the computational metaphor is turned instead toward fate, surveillance, and theodicy. Its controlling claim is that to be perfectly predicted is to be governed, and that living under a system whose criteria are a [[The Black Box|black box]] reproduces the same self-fulfilling agony that Calvinist predestination and Weber's Protestant salvation-anxiety once produced.

Three movements are braided together. A memoir frame opens with a friend's recovering-addict story — the store that budgets "shrinkage," so her theft was "already accounted for," raising the problem of whether one is an individual or merely "a member of a data set" ([[The Body as Data]], [[Pattern and Randomness]]). This folds into O'Gieblyn's own ex-evangelical crisis of faith, provoked by predestination, the doctrine under which "to concede that one's mind is controlled by God is to become a machine" ([[Mind as Machine]]). A media-criticism movement then surveys contemporary algorithmic systems: black-box opacity and the GDPR "right to an explanation," Kafka's *The Trial* as the parable of bureaucratic information power (Solove's Kafkaesque-vs-Orwellian harms), predictive policing and [[Algorithmic Determinism]] (PredPol, COMPAS, Jackie Wang's *Carceral Capitalism*), behavior-modification experiments (Facebook's "I voted," Cambridge Analytica), Zuboff's "behavioral futures," and Harari's "Dataism" as the successor to [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanism]].

The chapter's interpretive climax is Hannah Arendt's reading of the Archimedean point (via Heisenberg and Kafka): the paradox that the harder we try to eliminate ourselves from our instruments — to make them "neutral" and "objective" — the more we meet "nothing but himself and man-made things." Algorithmic bias (Tay, COMPAS's racial disparities, the gender-aware 2016 election net) is exactly this — machines made in our image returning us to ourselves "in a different disguise" ([[Technology is a mirror that returns us to ourselves in a different disguise]]). A parallel theodicy movement runs Dostoevsky's Ivan Karamazov "returning his ticket" against a God whose justice requires the suffering of one tortured child: O'Gieblyn's "way out" of Calvinism's totalizing logic, an insistence on "the validity of human morality" against an inscrutable, superintelligent system you "cannot defeat… through rational argument, any more than you can beat a superintelligent algorithm in a game of Go." The closing Lanier/Anderson exchange reframes the stakes as the abdication of meaning-making — "meaning is an implicitly human category that cannot be reduced to quantification."

## Key Concepts

- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — O'Gieblyn's central method: the predictive algorithm is "the twentieth-century incarnation of Jehovah," the modern reincarnation of the predestining God.
- [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — the predictive system occupying the structural place of divine foreknowledge and decree.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — Polonski's term for predictions built on past behavior that trap people "within the mirror of their digital reflection" and so modify the future they forecast.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the cybernetic mechanism beneath algorithmic determinism: metadata that "becomes part of a feedback loop that then actively modifies real behavior."
- [[The Black Box]] — opacity in three registers — engineering (opaque ML), theological ("the divine will was a black box"), and psychological ("the heart is also a black box").
- [[The Body as Data]] — the friend "was not an individual but a member of a data set"; the person "described purely in terms of pattern and probabilities, without any concern for interiority."
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — predictions rely on measurable external states because the "internal states of the human" cannot be determined.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the subject Harari's "Dataism" ends: the assumption "that an individual knows what is best for herself."
- [[Disenchantment]] — the de-anthropomorphized, unreachable God whose face is "as blank as the baleen grin of its organ pipes."
- [[Society of Control]] — predictive, behavior-modifying systems whose power lies in the impossibility of knowing what is monitored.

## Key Arguments

- [[The algorithmic God is the modern God of Calvin and Luther]] — to be perfectly predicted is to be governed; the experience reproduces Calvinist/Protestant determinist agony.
- [[Prediction enacts the very fate it foresees]] — predictions are not neutral forecasts but behavior-modifying feedback loops; "the person is a suspect because the algorithm identified the area."
- [[Surveillance reproduces Protestant salvation anxiety]] — the impossibility of knowing what is monitored makes people self-censor and over-comply, exactly as salvation-doubt produced compulsive industriousness.
- [[Technology is a mirror that returns us to ourselves in a different disguise]] — the more we try to eliminate ourselves from our instruments, the more they return us our own bias; "the machine knew us better than we knew ourselves."

## Important Definitions

- [[The Black Box]] — "The divine will was a black box," transposed from engineering onto the inscrutable divine will and the human heart.
- [[Disenchantment (O'Gieblyn)]] — the loss of the anthropomorphized, addressable God, figured in the blank-faced organ and the silent dorm-room prayer.
- "Shrinkage" — the budgeted, predicted loss-to-theft a corporation builds into its annual expenses, the figure that makes the friend's theft "already accounted for" (entity, not a wiki note).
- "Algorithmic determinism" (Polonski) — see [[Algorithmic Determinism]]: prediction built on past behavior that traps people inside their digital reflection.
- "Dataism" (Harari) — the ruling ideology, succeeding humanism, that denies individual feeling as a legitimate source of truth and defers to the algorithm.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[Mind as Machine]] — "To concede that one's mind is controlled by God is to become a machine"; the theological route to the computational metaphor.
- [[Job and the Algorithm]] — *The Trial* as "a kind of 'midrash' on the book of Job"; the state as "the twentieth-century incarnation of Jehovah."
- The black box — fusing determinism, surveillance, and the unknowable self across engineering, theological, and psychological registers.
- The mirror / digital reflection — people "trapped within the mirror of their digital reflection"; Arendt/Heisenberg's man who meets "nothing but himself."
- The Archimedean point / the snail's shell — the vantage from which human activity becomes "overt behavior" and technology "a large-scale biological process."
- The Chinese finger trap — predestination "becoming further entrenched and inescapable the more I tried to fight it."
- Ivan's returned "entrance ticket" — moral rebellion against an unjust cosmic system; chess/Go against an unbeatable God.
- Microsoft's "Predestination" GPS software — the closing emblem: predictive software literally named after the doctrine.

## Major Tensions

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the chapter's central unresolved problem: prediction's accuracy "suggests… that human behavior is deterministic," yet "precise only at a very large scale."
- [[Transparency vs Opacity]] — the "right to an explanation" against black-box systems whose obstacles are "legal and economic"; even "the existence of a lack of explanation is concealed."
- [[Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment]] — the blank, de-anthropomorphized God against the longing for a deity one could address "as I would to a pen pal."
