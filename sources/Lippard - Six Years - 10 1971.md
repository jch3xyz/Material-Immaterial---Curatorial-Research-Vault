# Source Summary: 1971

**Book:** [[Six Years]]
**Author:** [[Lucy Lippard]]

## Orientation

1971 is the hinge year of *Six Years*. Lippard's annotated chronicle — pages of book listings, exhibition entries, dated postcards, and verbatim artist statements — registers conceptual art at the moment it became most politically charged and most theoretically self-aware. The year's signal event is the **Haacke/Guggenheim censorship affair**: on April 1, director Thomas Messer cancels Hans Haacke's projected show because its documentary "real-time social systems" — the Shapolsky real-estate holdings, the *Visitors' Profile* poll — "dealt with 'specific social situations' not considered art." The curator Edward Fry is fired "peremptorily for his actions in favor of the artist," and the Art Workers' Coalition stages a "mass demonstration inside the museum on May 1, protesting art censorship." This is the book's clearest case of [[Systems Theory|systems aesthetics]] colliding with institutional power: Haacke's work claims that "verifiability" and unmediated [[Information]] are its medium, and the museum's refusal to show it proves the point. *Interpretive synthesis.*

A second through-line is the **document/photograph as unstable residue**. As the object dematerializes, the photograph and the printed text become the work's carrier — but an unreliable one. In a dialogue with Lippard, [[Robert Morris]] insists that "the signifier-signified relation they set up is not at all clear or transparent" and that photographs "give too much information and not enough at the same time." [[Dennis Oppenheim]] and others reframe art as moving along an "information-to weight ratio of media, from the stone age hieroglyphs to the present high-information, lightweight media" — the residue, not the luxury item, becomes the product. The page itself, the catalogue, the mailed card, become the medium. *Directly stated.*

A third through-line is **dematerialization named and contested**. Lippard recovers a prehistory — "In 1963, Dibbets's Amsterdam mailbox was labeled 'Jan Dibbets Dematerialisateur'" — even as [[Jan Dibbets]] himself resists the term ("that dematerialization you are speaking about is going to be for me ... more and more visual"). [[Robert Barry]]'s art is described as "so dematerialized that it has no fundamental need" of the gallery or the gallery system, while [[Jeff Wall]], in *Cine Text*, gives the year's most dialectical formulation: "Dematerialization is the truth of materialism." Dematerialization is the art-historical instance of the vault's broader [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension — here it is neither a fact nor a slogan but an argument the artists themselves are having. *Directly stated.*

Around these poles the year crowds with secondary developments: serial and permutational systems (Darboven's *One Century in One Year*, On Kawara's *One Million Years*), the catalogue and mail-piece as medium (the Buenos Aires [[CAYC]] folios, Barry's index-card "shows"), and an emergent feminist line — [[Mierle Laderman Ukeles]]'s *Maintenance Art* manifesto ("after the revolution who's going to pick up the garbage on Monday morning?") and [[Adrian Piper]]'s *Catalysis* pieces, which dissolve the discrete art object into the artist's own bodily presence as "a catalytic agent."
