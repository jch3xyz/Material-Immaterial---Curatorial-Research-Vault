# Source Summary: Chapter 8 — Robot and Cyborg Art

**Book:** [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]
**Author:** [[Jack Burnham]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]

## Orientation

Chapter 8 is the climactic terminus of *Beyond Modern Sculpture* — the place where the book's two-part spine (Part One: sculpture as object → Part Two: sculpture as system) consummates in the coinage and theorization of [[Cyborg Art]], "the cybernetic organism as an art form." It opens with a Norbert Wiener epigraph — the dancing figures atop a music box, "blind, deaf, and dumb," receiving a one-way message that "goes from the machinery of the music box to the figures, and stops there" — and turns this into the chapter's master indictment: all free-standing sculpture, from the paleolithic to present-day [[Kinetic Art]], shares "an inherent defect" in that it does not respond to man intelligently. Such works are "dead souls made alive through 'art' and prearranged mechanized motion," one-way stimulation rather than communication. The next, "perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture" is an art capable of sustained two-way exchange — reciprocal feedback between the work and its viewer — grounded in [[Cybernetics]].

The chapter unfolds in three movements within this raw file. The first, "The Cybernetic Changeover," is a primer on cybernetics as "the science of organization": Wiener, Rosenblueth and Bigelow's "purpose tremor" and negative feedback, McCulloch and Pitts on nerve nets, Claude Shannon's switching theory, Cannon's [[Homeostasis]], and Bertalanffy's classification of life by abstract organization into [[Systems Theory|systems]]. Here the unit of art migrates from the coupled machine to "an assembly of systems"; the [[Object and System|object gives way to the system]]. The second, "Mock Robots as Sculpture," surveys sculptors who *simulate* machineness without function — Paolozzi, Trova, Segal, Niki de Saint-Phalle, Oldenburg, Gallo, Rivers, Ipousteguy, Marisol, Minujín, Kienholz — the realm of the [[The Mock Robot|mock robot]] and [[Subsculpture|subsculpture]], where "anthropomorphic sculpture does not imitate man, but imitates robots trying to become human." The third, "The Cybernetic Organism as an Art Form," theorizes the cyborg proper and introduces the first true cybernetic organisms — Grey Walter's tortoises (*Machina speculatrix*), Berkeley's "Squee," Shannon's maze "mice" — and the magical-versus-scientific imitation of life.

The rhetorical posture is that of the systems-minded historian writing at the leading edge of his own moment (1968): patient and taxonomic in its catalogue of artists and machines, but quietly programmatic and even prophetic. Burnham reads the disappearance of the art object not as a loss but as a [[Sculpture as Biological Signal|biological signal]] — sculpture preparing the human race, via the [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically|Faustian]] drive to wrest life from God, for its own self-inflicted evolution toward a "post-human species." For the vault this chapter is the single richest primary-text grounding of [[The Cyborg]] (Burnham uses the exact term seventeen years before Haraway), [[Cybernetics]], [[Systems Theory]], [[Feedback Loops]], [[Homeostasis]], and the [[Object and System|object-to-system transition]] that names the vault's titular material/immaterial axis.

## Key Arguments

- [[All prior sculpture is a one-way message that does not respond to man]] — the Wiener music-box indictment: all sculpture including Kinetic Art is "blind, deaf, and dumb," one-way stimulation rather than communication
- [[Sculpture seeks its own obliteration by becoming the life it imitates]] — sculpture, irreducibly anthropomorphic, ends as art that literally *is* alive, abolishing itself as object
- [[A machine may be more responsive to human needs than another person]] — the IBM "1500" teaching machine's patience produces real emotional attachment in children
- [[For Object Art presence is everything for Systems Art information is the key]] — the unit of art shifts from physical presence to information
- [[There are no qualitative differences between living and nonliving matter]] — bio-electronics collapses life and non-life into "an ascending scale of complexity," so artificial organisms may possess consciousness
- [[A biological society that builds superintelligent automata will be dominated by them]] — MacGowan and Ordway's reported claim about intelligent automata and their biological makers
- [[Only the machine not anthropomorphism can bring inert matter to life]] — the machine, not the figure, is the legitimate heir to sculpture's form-creating tradition
- [[Future automata will abandon the human form because every replacement-machine first preserves then sheds the appearance of what it replaces]] — Alloway on functional robots that need not be man-like

## Important Definitions

- [[Cyborg (Burnham)]] — "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism"; a man-machine system in which the human partner functions unaware of the machine connection
- [[Cybernetics (Burnham)]] — "preeminently the science of organization" / "the analysis of linked and interacting systems"
- [[System (Burnham)]] — "an assembly of isolable properties studied in terms of their transformations, either alone (closed) or in relation to other systems (open)"
- [[Homeostasis (Cannon via Burnham)]] — "the ability of the body to keep its quantities of liquids, solids, and gases in a state of equilibrium"
- [[Negative Feedback (Burnham)]] — "a fractional return of the energy output of a system in the form of information guiding the system's future activities"
- [[Robot (Capek via Burnham)]] — the robot as collective demon of the early machine age, becoming a real, fallible, semipersonal being
- [[Objecthood (Fried via Burnham)]] — Burnham's reception of Fried's term for the formalist self-awareness of the object (inverted in this chapter)

## Major Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — the book's master tension, here consummated: the fixed, durable, place-bound object versus the mobile, perishable, information-defined system
- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — the defect of all prior sculpture versus the reciprocal goal of cyborg art
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — reused vault axis; systems as energy and information rather than matter; "for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor"
- [[Magical vs Scientific Imitation of Life]] — Grey Walter's distinction: the former copies external appearances, the latter performance and behavior

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A primary-text genealogy of the cyborg: use Burnham's 1968 "Cyborg Art" and his definition of the cyborg as "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism" to predate Haraway (1985) and Hayles by nearly two decades — situate the cyborg's origins in art theory, not only feminist or literary theory.
- The object-to-system turn as the spine of the material/immaterial axis: read "for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor" against Lippard's dematerialization and Hayles's "information lost its body" as three moments of the same de-idealizing impulse, with Burnham supplying the cybernetic mechanism.
- One-way versus two-way as a curatorial criterion: deploy the Wiener music-box indictment ("dead souls made alive through 'art'") to interrogate interactive and responsive art — what counts as genuine reciprocity versus mere "one-way stimulation."
- The mock robot and the embalmed body: trace Burnham's "anthropomorphic sculpture does not imitate man, but imitates robots trying to become human" through Paolozzi, Segal, and Kienholz as a meditation on the body rendered as shell, manikin, and subsculpture.
- Systems consciousness as survival mechanism: pair Burnham's claim that artificial and living matter "differ only in complexity" with his teleological reading of sculpture as a "biological signal" to stage the posthuman/transhuman question through a 1968 art-historical lens.
