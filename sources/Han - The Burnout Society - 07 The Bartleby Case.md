# Source Summary: The Bartleby Case

**Book:** [[The Burnout Society]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/07 The Bartleby Case]]

## Orientation

This short chapter (printed pp. 25–29) is the literary-critical hinge of *The Burnout Society*. Han submits Melville's "Bartleby, the Scrivener" to a *pathological* reading — explicitly against the dominant reception of "metaphysical and theological interpretations" — and uses it to police the boundary of his own periodization. His principal interlocutor is [[Giorgio Agamben]], whose "ontotheological interpretation" elevates Bartleby's "I would prefer not to" into the highest [[Negative Potency|potentiality]]: the empty writing tablet, the "Angel of Annunciation," the herald of a second Creation, or *de-creation* (*Ent-Schöpfung*). Han argues this reading "already founders on the facts of the narrative" and "fails to take note of the change of mental structure [*psychischer Strukturwandel*] in the present day."

The decisive move is *periodizing*. Bartleby belongs to a [[Disciplinary Society]], not the [[Achievement Society]] Han is diagnosing. The story is "A Story of Wall-Street," saturated with the disciplinary architecture of walls, partitions, screens, the "dead wall," and the thick-walled prison called the "Tombs." Its office workers "have all degraded to the state of [[Animal Laborans|animal laborans]]," suffering the *neurotic* disorders (hyperactivity, psychosomatic complaints) of an obedience-subject world. Bartleby's own symptoms are those of neurasthenia — "a lack of drive and for apathy" — NOT the depression, self-reproach, fear of failure, and "wearing-out of the ego" that constitute the late-modern pathology of the achievement-subject. He "does not face the imperative to be himself," "does not fail in the project of being an 'I,'" and his monotonous copying "leaves no free space in which private initiative would prove necessary." He is sick from neither [[Positivity|excess positivity]] nor possibility.

Han wields [[Negativity|negativity]] as the evidentiary lever against Agamben. Bartleby's "dim eyes," his refusal of every "errand," his prior employment at the Dead Letter Office, and the "massive presence of death" convict the story of an irreducible negativity that "contradicts Agamben's ontotheological interpretation." The tale is therefore "not a tale of de-creation [*Ent-Schöpfung*], but rather a story of exhaustion [*Erschöpfung*]" — the controlling German pun in which Han folds his whole quarrel: where Agamben hears a theology of creative potentiality, Han hears the diagnosis of a depleted, used-up life. "On errands of life, these letters speed to death": all efforts to live lead to death. Bartleby is not the achievement-subject; he is the last man of the disciplinary epoch the achievement society has superseded.

## Key Concepts

- [[Disciplinary Society]] — the social form Bartleby still inhabits, signified by walls, partitions, screens, the "dead wall," and the "Tombs"; produces obedience-subjects rather than achievement-subjects. This chapter is Han's clearest worked example of disciplinary architecture.
- [[Achievement Society]] — invoked here negatively: Bartleby is NOT an achievement-subject. The chapter delimits the concept by contrast (no imperative to be oneself, no depression, no self-reproach, no "project of being an 'I'").
- [[Negativity]] — the chapter's reading principle: the story's pervasive negativity (death, dead walls, being-unto-death, refusal) is the proof it is exhaustion, not de-creation.
- [[Positivity]] — named here as absent: "What makes Bartleby sick is not excessive positivity or possibility." The limiting case for the book's excess-positivity thesis.
- [[Negative Potency]] — Agamben's category, here *denied* to Bartleby: "I would prefer not to" is not the "negative potency of not-to" but apathy and lack of drive.
- [[Animal Laborans]] — the figure of the degraded working world; Melville's office workers "have all degraded to the state of animal laborans."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — established by contrast: Bartleby's copying admits no auto-exploitation, since it "leaves no free space in which private initiative would prove necessary, or even possible."

## Key Arguments

- [[Bartleby is a story of exhaustion not de-creation]] — the clinching thesis, carried by the *Ent-Schöpfung* / *Erschöpfung* pun; the tale's pervasive negativity contradicts Agamben's reading of Bartleby as the herald of pure potentiality.
- [[The achievement society supersedes the disciplinary society]] — the periodizing frame the chapter applies: Bartleby is an obedience-subject of the prior disciplinary epoch, not the depressive achievement-subject of late modernity.
- [[The achievement-subject is the homo sacer of its own self]] — the broader Han–Agamben polemic this chapter feeds: Agamben's negativity-scheme misses the topological change of power; Bartleby is the limiting case where the achievement-subject's pathology is conspicuously *absent*.

## Important Definitions

- [[Negative Potency]] — the "power not-to," Agamben's *potentia* of the *not-to*; this chapter is where Han demarcates his own use of it against Agamben by denying it to Bartleby.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The "dead wall" / "Wall-Street" — disciplinary architecture as mortification; "the wall is always associated with death." (Rendered as plain text; motif of this chapter, captured in the argument and tension notes.)
- The empty writing tablet / "white sheet" — Agamben's rejected image of Bartleby as "pure being without any predicate."
- *Ent-Schöpfung* / *Erschöpfung* (de-creation / exhaustion) — the controlling paronomasia, carried by [[Bartleby is a story of exhaustion not de-creation]].

## Major Tensions

- [[Discipline vs Achievement]] — the chapter's structuring opposition; Bartleby sits on the disciplinary side (obedience-subject) against the achievement-subject.
- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — Han weaponizes negativity (death, being-unto-death, refusal) against Agamben's positivized "pure potentiality," and notes Bartleby is not sick from "excessive positivity."
- [[Positive Potency vs Negative Potency]] — the contested status of "I would prefer not to": active capacity-not-to (Agamben) vs. passive symptom of apathy (Han).

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A case study in how *periodization* does conceptual work: Han uses a 19th-century literary text to negatively define the contemporary achievement-subject, showing that diagnosis depends on getting the historical "mental structure" right.
- The Han–Agamben dispute over Bartleby as a model for the wider quarrel between a politics of *negativity* (exception, the killable life) and a regime of *positivity* (inclusion, the un-killable life preserved at all costs) — useful for surveillance/biopolitics arguments.
- The *Ent-Schöpfung* / *Erschöpfung* pun as a worked example of "reversible compression" in close reading: the entire interpretive verdict survives in one near-homophone.
- Reading "I would prefer not to" against the contemplative "say no" / negative potency that Han endorses elsewhere — when refusal is mere apathy and when it is a genuine capacity.
- The disciplinary "wall" and "Tombs" as architecture of mortification, set beside contemporary surveillance architectures — what changes when the wall becomes transparent.
