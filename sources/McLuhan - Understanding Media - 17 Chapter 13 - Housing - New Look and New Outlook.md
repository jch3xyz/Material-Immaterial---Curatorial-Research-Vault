# Source Summary: Chapter 13 — Housing: New Look and New Outlook

**Book:** [[Understanding Media]]
**Author:** [[Marshall McLuhan]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/17 Chapter 13 - Housing - New Look and New Outlook]]

## Orientation

This is a Part II case-study chapter and the explicit collective counterpart to the preceding Clothing chapter (Ch. 12). McLuhan reads housing as the *group* application of his master frame [[Media as Extensions of Man]]: where clothing extends the private skin, housing is "a collective means of achieving the same end for the family or the group" — "a collective skin or garment" that extends "our bodily heat-control mechanisms." Cities extend this further still, becoming "an even further extension of bodily organs," a thesis McLuhan illustrates with Joyce's *Ulysses* (city forms mapped onto bodily organs) and Baudelaire's *Les Fleurs du Mal* (the metropolis as a body in dialogue with itself). Housing thereby "perform[s] the basic functions of all other media": it stores and makes energy socially accessible, and in doing so reshapes "the patterns of human association and community."

The chapter's analytic engine is McLuhan's recurring acoustic-versus-visual, sense-ratio apparatus, here applied to architectural form. The historical shift from round to square dwellings (and from circular-dome to Gothic architecture) is diagnosed not as a stylistic accident but as a change in [[Sense Ratios]]: round/tactile dwellings (tent, wigwam, igloo, cave) "follow dynamic lines of force, like a triangle" and are not "enclosed" visual spaces, whereas the square "is the enclosure of a visual space" abstracted "from manifest tensions" and emerges only "when men have learned to practice specialization of their senses, and fragmentation of their work skills." Tribal man "freely extended the form of his body to include the universe" and treated house and city as iconic embodiments of cosmic order; literate man, once he has adopted "the visual dynamic of the phonetic alphabet," prefers "separateness and compartmented spaces." This binds the chapter to [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]], [[Typographic Man]], and [[The Phonetic Alphabet (McLuhan)]]. The igloo/primus-stove case supplies the title's mechanism: "the intensification of a single factor" (here artificial heat) introduces "a new pattern" into "an ancient way of life," setting up "a new equilibrium among all of the senses and faculties" — a "new look" and "new outlook."

The chapter's most distinctive move is the restatement of the book's keystone applied to electric light: "in this domain, the medium is the message," because "when the light is on there is a world of sense that disappears when the light is off." Light is "information without 'content,'" "a self-contained communication system in which the medium is the message" — the purest demonstration in the whole book of [[The Medium Is the Message]], for which electric light is McLuhan's canonical example. The closing pages then push toward the most cosmic statement of [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]]: electric light has already made "space without walls, and day without night," so that "painting with light is a kind of housing without walls"; extended to "global thermostatic controls" it points to "the obsolescence of housing," and extended to "collective consciousness" it might render "language walls obsolescent" via "massive extrasensory perception" — an event McLuhan forecasts "may easily occur in the 1960s." Several concept, argument, and metaphor notes already realize this chapter ([[Sense Ratios]], [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]], [[Consciousness Without Walls]], [[Listening with the Entire Epidermis]], and the two arguments below); this summary anchors and orients them.

## Key Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — housing as the *collective* extension of the skin and heat-control mechanism ("a collective skin or garment"); cities as "an even further extension of bodily organs." The chapter is an applied instance of this master frame, not a new concept.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — the chapter's analytic engine: "a new extension sets up a new equilibrium among all of the senses and faculties," and the round-to-square shift is caused by "alteration in the ratio or proportion of the sense lives in the members of a society."
- [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]] — round/tactile dwellings ("dynamic lines of force") versus the square as "the enclosure of a visual space ... abstracted from manifest tensions."
- [[Typographic Man]] — literate man "restrict[s] and enclose[s] space and ... separate[s] functions" once he has adopted "the visual dynamic of the phonetic alphabet."
- [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]] — the closing speculation: an "electric extension of the process of collective consciousness" effecting "an immediate simulation of consciousness" by "massive extrasensory perception."
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — electric light and global thermostatic controls as the planetary externalization of the body's functions, dissolving the enclosures of house and language.
- [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] — "Human sensitivity seems once more to be attuned to the universal currents that made of tribal man a cosmic skin-diver"; the electric age as a return to tribal, all-sensorium participation.

## Key Arguments

- [[The shift from round to square architecture is a change in sense ratios not a stylistic accident]] — the square encloses an abstracted visual space and becomes thinkable only after the phonetic alphabet specializes the senses and fragments work; this is the cause anthropologists noted but could not explain.
- [[Electric light is the clearest proof that the medium is the message because light is information without content]] — light has no content to credit, yet "when the light is on there is a world of sense that disappears when the light is off," so the effect *is* the medium itself.
