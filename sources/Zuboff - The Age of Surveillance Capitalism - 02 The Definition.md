## Orientation

This is the book's masthead — a single mock-lexicon entry that formally defines the title term. It is set out exactly as a dictionary headword (`Sur-veil-lance Cap-i-tal-ism, n.`) followed by eight numbered senses. The chapter contains no exposition, no examples, and no argumentation; it is four lines of definition. Yet it is the load-bearing definitional spine of the whole book: each of the eight senses is a one-clause thesis that the rest of the volume unpacks across its nineteen substantive chapters. Zuboff names her object the way a dictionary names a settled word, asserting [[Surveillance Capitalism]] as a real, established kind in the world rather than a hypothesis to be argued.

The eight senses forward-declare almost every major concept the corpus develops: the claiming of human experience as free raw material via the triad of extraction, prediction, and sales ([[The Extraction Imperative]]); the subordination of ordinary goods-and-services production to a "global architecture of behavioral modification" ([[The Means of Behavioral Modification]]); the diagnosis of surveillance capitalism as a parasitic "rogue mutation of capitalism" marked by unprecedented concentrations of wealth, knowledge, and power; the founding of a "surveillance economy"; the signature historical analogy that this is to twenty-first-century human nature what industrial capitalism was to the nineteenth- and twentieth-century natural world; the origin of a new [[Instrumentarian Power]]; the project of [[A Utopia of Certainty]] ("a new collective order based on total certainty"); and the political reframing of the whole as a [[The Coup from Above]] — an "overthrow of the people's sovereignty."

Because this is the definitional kernel, this source is the canonical verbatim anchor for the keystone concept [[Surveillance Capitalism]] and for the definition note [[Surveillance Capitalism (Zuboff)]]. The deeper exposition of each named concept flows from its primary-exposition chapter elsewhere in the book; this source declares the canonical names and supplies the verbatim definitional quotes that ground them. It is the hub that wires Zuboff into the vault's existing surveillance/data cluster — [[Digital Panopticon]], [[Big Data]], [[Dataism]], [[Smart Power]], [[Psychopolitics]], [[The Body as Data]] — naming the for-profit economic engine beneath Han's and Harari's diagnoses.

## Key Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the "new instrumentarian power that asserts dominance over society"; a new species of power, distinct from totalitarianism, developed across later chapters. Named here.

## Key Arguments

- [[Surveillance capitalism is a logic not a technology]] — by calling it "parasitic" and a "rogue mutation," Zuboff frames it as a contingent economic logic, a contestable deviation that hosts on and distorts ordinary capitalism, not the technology itself nor capitalism's inevitable essence.
- [[Surveillance capitalism threatens human nature as industrial capitalism threatened nature]] — the signature historical analogy: this century's reckoning over human nature parallels the last century's environmental reckoning over the natural world.
- [[Surveillance capitalism is a coup from above an overthrow of popular sovereignty]] — reframes an economic phenomenon as a political-constitutional one: the harm is the loss of self-rule, not merely market concentration.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- The dictionary-entry frame as a rhetorical strategy: naming a phenomenon into existence (compare Burnham's "systems esthetics," Han's "psychopolitics," Harari's "Dataism" as coinages that stake a category).
- The "free raw material" move as the economic counterpart to [[The Body as Data]] and Harari's [[Dataism]] — human experience rendered as an ownerless input, the material/immaterial expropriation at the root of the data economy.
- Surveillance capitalism as the for-profit institutional machinery beneath Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[Smart Power]]: where Han diagnoses voluntary self-exposure and steering-from-within, Zuboff names who profits and how (extraction → prediction → behavioral modification).
- "A rogue mutation of capitalism" as a contingency thesis: arguing that the surveillance economy is contestable and reversible rather than the inevitable telos of digital technology.
