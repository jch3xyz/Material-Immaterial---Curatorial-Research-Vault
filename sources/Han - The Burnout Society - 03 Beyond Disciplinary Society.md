# Source Summary: Beyond Disciplinary Society

**Book:** [[The Burnout Society]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/03 Beyond Disciplinary Society]]

## Orientation

This short, dense chapter is the foundational diagnostic move of *The Burnout Society* and the **primary source** behind a large cluster of Han's vocabulary that the vault had so far grounded mostly in *Psychopolitics* (which imported the apparatus secondhand). Here Han announces the historical-epochal claim that organizes his whole project: Foucault's **disciplinary society** of "hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories" has been superseded by an **achievement society** [Leistungsgesellschaft] of "fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories." Its inhabitants are no longer "obedience-subjects" but "achievement-subjects" who are "entrepreneurs of themselves." Foucault's analysis of power, Han argues, "cannot account for the psychic and topological changes" of this transformation, and Deleuze's "control society" is rejected as still containing "too much negativity."

The chapter advances four interlocking moves. First, the **modal shift from Should to Can**: disciplinary society is governed by the negativity of *May Not* and *Should* (prohibition, compulsion), while achievement society is governed by the positivity of *Unlimited Can* ("Yes, we can") — because "the positivity of Can is much more efficient than the negativity of Should," the social unconscious switches without revoking the Should (continuity in the drive to maximize production). Second, an extended **polemic against Alain Ehrenberg**, whom Han cites verbatim twice: Han accepts Ehrenberg's placement of depression in the disciplinary→achievement transition but rejects his "economy of the self" diagnosis (depression as the failure to become oneself), insisting instead that "the pressure to achieve" — together with impoverished attachment [Bindungsarmut] and the "systemic violence" of achievement society — is what causes "exhaustive depression." Third, a **corrective re-reading of Nietzsche**: the new human type is not the "sovereign man" but "the last man, who does nothing but work," and "lacks all sovereignty."

Fourth and most consequential, the theory of **auto-exploitation**: the achievement-subject, "lord and master of itself," subject to no external "instance of domination," gives itself over to "compulsive freedom — that is, to the free constraint of maximizing achievement." Auto-exploitation is "more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it"; the exploiter is the exploited, perpetrator and victim collapse, and the depressive is "predator and prey at once," "an animal laborans that exploits itself." Depression is figured as the sickness of "excessive positivity" — "a humanity waging war on itself," a self "wounded by internalized war" — which "eludes all immunological schemes." This chapter overwhelmingly *extends and corroborates* existing canonical notes rather than minting new vocabulary; it is where that vocabulary originates.

## Key Concepts

- [[Achievement Society]] — the 21st-century paradigm [Leistungsgesellschaft] superseding disciplinary society; populated by self-exploiting "achievement-subjects" who are "entrepreneurs of themselves," governed by Unlimited Can, producing "depressives and losers" rather than "madmen and criminals." This chapter is its locus classicus.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the achievement-subject exploits itself voluntarily and without external constraint, "more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it"; "the exploiter is simultaneously the exploited."
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — the foil term, defined here by contrast: classical exploitation by an external other, less efficient than self-exploitation because no feeling of freedom attends it.
- [[Can vs Should]] — the modal-verb shift: the negativity of *May Not* / *Should* yields to the positivity of *Unlimited Can*; "the social unconscious switches from Should to Can" because Can is more efficient at maximizing production.
- [[Positivity]] — depression and burnout are pathologies of *too much positivity*; "the sickness of a society that suffers from excessive positivity."
- [[Negativity]] — the negativity of prohibition (*May Not*, *Should*) that defines disciplinary society and is "abolished" by achievement society's "increasing deregulation."
- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — the disappearance of domination does not free the subject but makes "freedom and constraint coincide"; "compulsive freedom" / "the free constraint of maximizing achievement."
- [[Immunological Age]] — the self "still represents an immunological category," but depression "eludes all immunological schemes"; the new violence is internalized, not viral.
- [[Neuronal Violence]] — the auto-aggressive, system-immanent violence of the achievement-subject "fighting with itself," "wounded by internalized war"; "a humanity waging war on itself."
- [[Animal Laborans]] — the figure (Arendt's term) Han deploys for the self-exploiting depressive: "an animal laborans that exploits itself … predator and prey at once."
- [[Burnout]] — relocated from the "exhausted self" to "the exhausted, burnt-out soul"; the book's title-pathology.
- [[Society of Control]] — named and rejected here: Deleuze's "control society" does not "do justice to this change" because "it still contains too much negativity."

## Key Arguments

- [[The achievement society supersedes the disciplinary society]] — the foundational periodizing claim: a shift of institutional topology (prisons/factories → fitness studios/office towers) and subject-type (obedience-subject → achievement-subject), against which both Foucault's power analysis and Deleuze's control society fall short.
- [[Depression is caused by the imperative to achieve not the failure to become oneself]] — the chapter's central polemic against Ehrenberg: not the "economy of the self" but "the pressure to achieve," impoverished attachment, and systemic violence cause "exhaustive depression."
- [[Freedom itself is producing coercion]] — "the disappearance of domination does not entail freedom"; auto-exploitation under "compulsive freedom" is more efficient than external exploitation precisely because freedom is felt.

## Important Definitions

- [[Achievement Society]] — defined ostensively and by contrast (the institutional catalogue; "entrepreneurs of themselves"; governed by Unlimited Can).
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — self-exploitation that is voluntary and unconstrained, in which "the exploiter is simultaneously the exploited," "more efficient than allo-exploitation."

- [[Neuronal Violence]] as internalized war — the achievement-subject "fighting with itself," "a humanity waging war on itself."

## Major Tensions

- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the master tension in primary-source form: the negativity of prohibition/*Should*/*May Not* vs the positivity of *Can*; disciplinary "no" vs achievement "yes."
- [[Discipline vs Achievement]] — the periodizing opposition with its *continuity* caveat ("the Can does not revoke the Should"). Note: Han here rejects "control society" as still too negative and names **achievement** (not control) as the successor regime.
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — the adjacent Deleuze-anchored opposition this chapter relativizes by declaring control society insufficiently distinct from discipline ("too much negativity").
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — "freedom and constraint coincide"; "paradoxical freedom that abruptly switches over into violence."
