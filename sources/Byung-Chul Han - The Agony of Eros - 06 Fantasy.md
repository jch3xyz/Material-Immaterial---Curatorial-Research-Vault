# Source Summary: Fantasy

**Book:** [[The Agony of Eros]]

## Orientation

Chapter 6 ("Fantasy") is the chapter of *The Agony of Eros* in which Han pits [[Fantasy]] — imagination as the erotic faculty that dwells in the vague, the undefined, and the withdrawn — against *information*, the positivity of high-definition, hypervisible immediacy. Its wager is that the informational-pornographic image regime of the transparency society is killing fantasy and, with it, [[The Other]]: "Information and fantasy are opposing forces." The chapter is the book's most sustained engagement with the sociologist Eva Illouz, here through her *Why Love Hurts: A Sociological Explanation* (2012) — a different book from the *Cold Intimacies* that Han critiques in *Psychopolitics*, and so a second, distinct disagreement with the same interlocutor (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz]]).

Rhetorically the chapter is corrective and polemical: Han quotes Illouz at length and then turns each of her theses on its head. Against her claim that the modern imagination is "information-thick" and therefore less able to idealize, he answers that idealization was never the point — what constructs the Other is the *negativity of withdrawal*, not the quantity of available information ("there is no such thing as an 'information-thick' imagination incapable of 'idealizing' the Other"). Against her thesis that expanding choice "rationalizes" desire, he answers that unchecked freedom of choice is *ending* desire, because desire is always desire for the Other and lives on privation and absence. Against her "sociology of disappointment," he answers that raised *expectations*, not heightened *fantasy*, drive contemporary disappointment — and that Illouz fatally fails to distinguish the two.

The second half turns to close literary readings that positively theorize fantasy as the negativity of visual privation: Flaubert's coach-ride in *Madame Bovary* (the most erotic scene shows nothing); Emma Bovary reread, via Bataille, as a figure of unproductive expenditure and loss rather than a socioeconomic symptom; and J. G. Ballard's story "The Gioconda of the Twilight Noon," in which a man blinds himself to keep his inner dream images — a story Han reads *against* Slavoj Žižek as an *anti-Platonic* retreat into the cave of dream and desire rather than a Platonic ascent to the sun. Barthes (quoting Kafka on "shutting my eyes") supplies the figure of the closed eye as negativity, which [[Acceleration|acceleration society]] can no longer afford. The chapter closes on the thesis that hypervisibility is the *telos* of the [[Transparency|society of transparency]]: as thresholds, borders, and zones of mystery are smoothed away, the atopic Other — and the fantasy that constructs it — vanishes. "This is the agony of eros." A political-economic coda adds that today's real walls, drawn by capital, no longer *generate* the Other; money levels all difference, so these borders abolish fantasies of the Other instead of opening onto an elsewhere.

## Key Concepts

- [[Fantasy]] — the chapter's title concept: imagination as the faculty that "inhabits space that is undefined," opposed point-for-point to information, positivity, and hypervisibility; the negativity in which the Other is constructed.
- [[Eros]] — desire as always desire for the Other, nourished by "the negativity of privation and absence"; the detour and absence that informational/pornographic immediacy destroys. The chapter names the stakes: "This is the agony of eros."
- [[The Other]] — the atopic Other, brought forth only by "the negativity of withdrawal," not by more or less information; where "borders and thresholds vanish, fantasies of the Other disappear too."
- [[Atopia]] — the placelessness of the Other; thresholds and transitions are "zones of mystery and riddle—here, the atopic Other begins."
- [[Information]] — here figured as positivity: "Information, as such, is positivity that leads to a dismantling of the Other's negativity"; the opposing force to fantasy.
- [[Negativity]] — the negativity of withdrawal, privation, absence, and the closed eye — the constitutive condition of fantasy and the Other.
- [[Positivity]] — the positivity of information, choice, and hypervisibility that dismantles the Other's negativity.
- [[Transparency]] — hypervisibility is "the telos of the society of transparency"; space "becomes transparent when it is smoothed out and leveled."
- [[Pornography (Han)]] — the limit case of maximal visual information: "pornography—which maximizes visual information, as it were—destroys erotic fantasy."
- [[Hell of the Same]] — the closing coda: today's capital-drawn walls "extend through the inferno of the same, which obeys only economic laws."

## Key Arguments

- Idealization of the Other is a function of the negativity of withdrawal, not of information-scarcity — so there is no "information-thick" imagination incapable of idealizing the Other (contra Illouz).
- Information stifles fantasy and hypervisibility destroys erotic fantasy — new communications media do the opposite of giving flight to imagination.
- Unchecked freedom of choice is bringing about the end of desire, not "rationalizing" it — the Other, as object of desire, escapes the positivity of choice (contra Illouz).
- Contemporary disappointment stems from raised expectations, not heightened fantasy — Illouz conflates fantasy and expectation.
- Emma Bovary's dissipation is Bataillean expenditure and loss, not a socioeconomic symptom of nineteenth-century consumer culture (contra Illouz).
- Ballard's Maitland is an anti-Platonic figure — he destroys his eyes to withdraw *into* the cave of dream and desire, not to ascend toward the sun of the Ideas (contra Žižek).
- In acceleration/achievement society we can no longer close our eyes — the contemplative "closure" of perception is foreclosed by hypervisibility and hypervigilance.
- Today's walls no longer generate the Other — capital levels all difference, so borders now abolish rather than open fantasies of the Other.

## Important Definitions

- **Fantasy (Han)** — imagination as the faculty that "inhabits space that is undefined"; the opposing force to information, defined by the negativity of the vague and withdrawn.
- **Desire (Han)** — desire is "always desire for the Other," nourished by "the negativity of privation and absence," and categorically distinct from wanting and needing.
- **Hypervisibility (Han)** — the maximization of visual information; "the telos of the society of transparency," whose corollary is "the dismantling of thresholds and borders." ## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The white butterflies / scraps of paper — the close of Flaubert's coach scene, where Emma scatters paper that flutters "like white butterflies"; eros achieved through visual privation, the erotic as what is withheld from sight.
- The cave and the sun (inverted Plato) — the twilight cave of dreams and desire (fantasy) against the daylight of "truth and hypervisibility"; Han inverts the Platonic valuation.
- "Shutting my eyes" / the closed eye (Kafka via Barthes) — writing and fantasy as the negativity of the closed eye: "the inner music of things sounds only when you close your eyes."
- "An eager, unrepentant Oedipus" (Ballard) — Maitland's self-blinding scream, "half in pain and half in triumph," reframing the Oedipal blinding as the ecstatic choice of inner vision over outer sight.
- The wall / fence / border — the threshold that once opened onto the Other, now a capital-drawn partition of the same that "no longer constitute[s] thresholds or transitions leading somewhere else."

## Major Tensions

- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the master axis of the chapter: fantasy, withdrawal, and the closed eye (negativity) against information, choice, and hypervisibility (positivity).
- [[Transparency vs Opacity]] — hypervisibility as the telos of transparency versus the opacity, threshold, and concealment that fantasy requires.
