# Source Summary: Chapter 5 — Sculpture and Automata

**Book:** [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]

## Orientation

This is the opening chapter of Part Two ("Sculpture as System") and the structural hinge of the entire book: Burnham crosses from sculpture-as-object (Part One) to sculpture-as-system by tracing a ten-thousand-year genealogy of *automata* — "all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men." Automata are introduced as one branch of [[Subsculpture]], the substratum of figures (fetishes, idols, dolls, waxworks, manikins, puppets) "aesthetically ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts." Burnham's master claim is that the progressive mechanization of these images discloses [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] — "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge" by which "Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically." The chapter therefore reads the whole history of the moving image not as marginal craft but as the prehistory of [[Systems Esthetics]].

The genealogy runs in long arcs: Egyptian servant-figures and speaking oracle-statues as proto-automata; Greek ritual proto-automata and Hero of Alexandria's mechanical theatre of Dionysus; medieval clockwork "jacks" and the Strasbourg cathedral clock; the eighteenth-century golden age (Vaucanson's flute-player and his digesting Duck, Descartes's bodily mechanism, Newton's clockwork cosmos, and — decisively — Pascal's and Leibniz's *non-anthropomorphic* calculators); the nineteenth-century debasement of the android and the modern rise of [[The Robot]] (Čapek's *R.U.R.*, "robot" = "forced labor"); the post-1945 cybernetic pseudo-organisms (Grey Walter's tortoises, Ashby's homeostat, Shannon's mice); and the manikin/robot theme entering modern art after 1910 (Archipenko, Duchamp-Villon, Léger, Picabia, Duchamp's *Large Glass*, de Chirico, Schlemmer's Bauhaus stage, Bellmer's dolls).

Two through-lines carry the chapter's weight for the vault. First, the bifurcation of the automaton with Pascal and Leibniz into two branches — the amusing humanoid facsimile versus the logic/programming machine — which Burnham explicitly defers to his final chapter ([[Cyborg Art]]); this is the genealogical root of computation and [[Cybernetics]] inside the sculpture story. Second, the automaton as a sociological symptom: as the public reconciles itself to "an automaton existence" it "acquiesces to government by technological expediency," breeding alienation, "the cheerful robot," and Bettelheim's "Joey, the Mechanical Boy." The chapter substantively grounds [[Reciprocal Communication]] (the machine that may be "more effective than another person") and anticipates both Haraway's cyborg and Hayles's posthuman, mechanized body by years.

## Key Concepts

- [[Subsculpture]] — Burnham's coinage for "types of images aesthetically ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts"; the chapter's organizing category, into which automata fall and whose migration into fine art powers the object-to-system transition.
- [[Automata]] — "all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men," whose history "has always run close to that of technology"; the bridge-term between the static statue and the cybernetic system.
- [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] — the master thesis applied to subsculpture: the maker of anthropomorphic images ultimately seeks to "bring to life" the figure by nonbiological means.
- [[The Robot]] — the modern, non-anthropomorphic mechanical man, named by Čapek (Czech "forced labor"), abandoning skin/hair/clothing for "the modern, ultra-functional machine."
- [[The Mock Robot]] — the popular electromechanical folklore robot ("Televox," "Electro, the Man of Steel"), the conscious-seeming machine that was never really conscious.
- [[The Clockwork Universe]] — the Cartesian-Newtonian cosmos as "a great clock, operating cycle after cycle," wound by God; the metaphysics behind the eighteenth-century golden age of automata.
- [[Cybernetics]] — UPDATE (Burnham author-section): the post-1945 reframing of the automaton as electronic "pseudo-organisms" that model the nervous system rather than copying outward form; Leibniz named "the spiritual father of modern cybernetics."
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the responsive man-machine relationship: a machine "may be more effective than another person."
- [[Object and System]] — the book's master axis; this chapter is its pivot, from the inert place-bound statue to the self-animated, life-simulating assembly.

## Key Arguments

- [[The history of automata is the aesthetic core of a Faustian drive to re-create life nonbiologically]] — the genealogy of the moving image is a distinctly Western drive to make life without biology.
- [[Future automata will abandon the human form because every replacement-machine first preserves then sheds the appearance of what it replaces]] — the historical law (horseless carriages, sailed steamboats, stagecoach-trains) predicting non-anthropomorphic industrial robots.
- [[A machine may be more responsive to human needs than another person]] — the affective/economic case (teaching machines) that machines can displace people even in relational roles.
- [[A biological society that builds superintelligent automata will be dominated by them]] — the sociological-political stakes: reconciliation to robot life as acquiescence to technological government.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[Pygmalion and Galatea]] — Ovid's ivory figure brought to life: "the mystical equation of all art," though "not an altogether happy" exchange (flesh decays).
- [[The Music-Box Automata]] — the precision-programmed cylinder figures personifying the regulated framework of classical musical form.
- [[Vaucansons Duck]] — the digesting, defecating mechanical duck, "a step in the direction of modern physiological analysis": the high-water mark of the life-simulating automaton.
- [[The Mechanized Stage as Organism]] — Schlemmer's immersive stage as "a kind of metaphysical microcosm," with man as "the perfect engineer" at the central switchboard.
- [[The Hooded Machine]] — Duchamp's "Nine Malic Molds," hollow uniformed male types reduced to servile mechanical function.
- [[Joey the Mechanical Boy]] — Bettelheim's nine-year-old who "finds security in actually becoming a robot": internalized mechanization.
- [[The Strasbourg Cathedral Clock]] — "a mechanical, miniature cosmos," the Renaissance universe compressed into one machine.
- [[The Robot]] — the clanking stainless-steel folklore icon of the automatic age ("pathetic titans, more helpless and powerful than any human being").

## Major Tensions

- [[Apollonian vs Faustian]] — static sculpture's quietude and limit versus automata's "Faustian drive to transcend natural limitations."
- [[Object vs System]] — the inert contemplative object versus the self-animated assembly that "engages the mind on immediate terms."
- [[Anthropomorphic vs Non-Anthropomorphic Logic Machine]] — the two branches of automata: copying outward human form versus modeling thought and behavior.
- [[Magical vs Scientific Imitation of Life]] — the priest-magician's animated oracle versus the engineer's mechanized model.
- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — the one-directional statue/automaton versus the responsive, reciprocal machine.
