# Source Summary: Porn

**Book:** [[The Agony of Eros]]

## Orientation

This short chapter is *The Agony of Eros*'s dedicated statement of pornography as the *antagonist of eros* — the fifth movement of the book, following melancholia (ch. 2), the power not-to (ch. 3), and bare life (ch. 4). Its opening thesis binds it directly back to that fourth chapter: "Porn is a matter of bare life on display." Han's claim is counter-intuitive and deliberately so. Porn does not threaten sex with an excess of sex; it destroys sex more surely than morality ever could, because "it contains no sex at all." Following Baudrillard's *Fatal Strategies*, Han reads pornography as the "anticipation of dead sex in living sexuality" — sexuality fading not into repression or sublimation but into "the more sexual than sex." The threat is not the puritan's "pure reason" that shuns sex as dirty, but exhibition itself; and the process is universalizing, so that "even real sex is turning into porn."

The philosophical core of the chapter is a sustained polemic against Giorgio Agamben. Han equates "the pornographication of the world" with "the profanation of the world" and turns Agamben's own concept of profanation — celebrated in *Profanations* ("In Praise of Profanation") and *Nudities* — against him. For Agamben, profanation returns what consecration set apart to common use, liberating things into "means without ends"; the exhibited nudity of fashion models and porn stars opens the face, "loaded until it bursts with exhibition-value," to "a new form of erotic communication." Han rejects all of this. Agamben's thesis of secularization, he argues, blinds him to a phenomenon that "even stands opposed" to religious practice: musealization and exhibition do not liberate objects but *destroy their cult value and replace it with exhibition value*; tourism produces "non-sites," whereas pilgrimage is bound to place (Heidegger's dwelling and the "divine"). The exhibited, expressionless, secret-less face is not the site of a new eroticism but the very type of the obscene: "the erotic is never free of secrecy."

The chapter closes by generalizing this logic into a social diagnosis of *deritualization and desacralization*. Drawing on Bataille's "holy eroticism" (ritualized communication, festivals, ceremonial games) and Baudrillard's opposition of seduction to love, Han argues that pornography "completes the deritualization of love," and that capitalism aggravates the whole process "by making everything a commodity and putting it on display." Agamben's profanation, meant to redeem, actually *promotes* pornographication because it "suspects ritual spaces of constituting compulsive forms of sequestration." The rhetorical posture is polemical and diagnostic: a philosopher's argument (Agamben) is not merely disputed but shown to abet the very desacralization it imagines itself resisting. Several of the chapter's most vivid formulations — the cat and the ball of yarn, "loaded until it bursts with exhibition-value," "a new form of erotic communication," "one's own absolute mediality" — are Agamben's own words, quoted precisely so that Han can invert them.

## Key Arguments

- Porn destroys sexuality more effectively than morality does, because it is not an excess of sex but its absence — "dead sex in living sexuality." (Captured in [[Pornography (Han)]]; no standalone argument note this pass.)
- Exhibition does not profane things into a "new use" (contra Agamben) — it destroys their cult value and replaces it with exhibition value; the museum and tourism are the counter-examples. (Captured in [[Pornography (Han)]] / [[Exhibition Value]].)
- Pornography completes the deritualization of love; ritual distance and scenic illusion, not their liberation, are the condition of eros. (Captured in [[Pornography (Han)]].)

## Important Definitions

- [[Pornography (Han)]] — not a genre of explicit imagery but the mode in which sexuality "fades … into the more sexual than sex: porn"; obscene because "it contains no sex at all"; "the anticipation of dead sex in living sexuality" (Baudrillard).

The pornographic face — a face "loaded with exhibition-value to the point of bursting," yet "without mystery or expression," which "says nothing" and is therefore "obscene and pornographic" — is Han's key figure in this chapter; it is captured inside `Pornography (Han)` rather than given a standalone definition note. Agamben's own definition of "profanation" ("using things that have been set aside for the gods through consecration") is reported and rejected, not endorsed, and is held as plain text.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- Porn as *bare life on display*: a bridge between the surveillance/exhibition cluster (`Exhibition Value`, `Transparency`, the digital panopticon) and the erotic — exhibition value pushed to the limit where the exposed body becomes obscene precisely by having nothing left to hide. Useful for a material/immaterial argument about what visibility destroys.
- The Han-contra-Agamben dispute over profanation as a case study in how "liberation" arguments can abet the thing they mean to resist — profanation meant to free things into common use instead completing their desacralization. A model for critiquing emancipatory rhetoric around transparency and openness.
- Cult value vs. exhibition value read through the museum and the tourist "non-site": what setting-apart, distance, and place do that exhibition cannot — a Benjamin/Heidegger axis for essays on aura, the gallery, and the placelessness of networked images.
- The secret as the condition of the erotic ("the erotic is never free of secrecy") set against a regime of total exposure — the negative counter-term to a culture of the smooth, transparent, and displayed.
- Deritualization as a diagnosis extendable beyond sex: the disappearance of ritual spaces, scenic illusion, and ceremonial distance as a general feature of a commodified, exhibited world — a hinge to Debord's spectacle and to Baudrillard's seduction.
