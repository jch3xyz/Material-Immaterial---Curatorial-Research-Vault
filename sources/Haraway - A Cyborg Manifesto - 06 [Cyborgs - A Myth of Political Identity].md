# Source Summary: Cyborgs: A Myth of Political Identity

## Orientation

This is the concluding section of *A Cyborg Manifesto*. Having defined the cyborg in §01, mapped the historic shift to the informatics of domination in §03, and located women in the homework economy and the integrated circuit in §04–§05, Haraway now turns to myth and identity: she sets out to build "a myth about identity and boundaries that might inform late-twentieth-century political imaginations." She reads two overlapping groups of texts for their value as a "potentially helpful cyborg myth" — the construction of "women of color" as a cyborg identity (anchored in Audre Lorde's Sister Outsider and the figure of Malinche), and the "monstrous selves" of feminist science fiction (Russ, Delany, Tiptree, Varley, Butler, McIntyre). Both are mined for the same lesson: a politics that survives without recourse to original innocence, organic wholeness, or natural identity.

The rhetorical posture is mythopoetic and polemical rather than expository. Haraway is openly *making* a myth — choosing figures, retelling origin stories backwards, weaponizing irony — while insisting this is "not just literary deconstruction, but liminal transformation." The throughline is a refusal of the founding myth of original wholeness: Eden, the Fall, the "fall into writing," the apocalyptic "return to a deathly oneness." Against this she sets [[Cyborg Writing]] — "the power to survive, not on the basis of original innocence, but on the basis of seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other" — and a cyborg politics that "insist[s] on noise and advocate[s] pollution." She recapitulates the persistent Western dualisms (self/other, mind/body, culture/nature, maker/made) and argues that high-tech culture dissolves them: "There is no fundamental, ontological separation in our formal knowledge of machine and organism." The machine, she concludes, "is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment."

The section is the PRIMARY source for the cyborg as a *political myth* and the canonical anchor for the manifesto's signature closing slogan, "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess." It supplies the political-myth gloss on [[The Cyborg]] (the full definition lives in §01), realizes the manifesto's keystone tension [[Cyborg vs Goddess]], and closes on the salamander figure of [[Regeneration vs Rebirth]]. Throughout, the [[Material vs Immaterial]] entanglement is consummated: bodies "textualized... as code problems on the grid of C3I," and organisms become "biotic systems, communications devices like others."

## Key Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — Haraway's central political myth, here developed as an identity that "is not innocent," "was not born in a garden," and "takes irony for granted." The originating source behind Hayles's later reading; this section supplies the political-myth gloss (the verbatim definition is in §01).
- [[Cyborg Writing]] — the seizing of the tools of signification that marked colonized and othered groups as "other"; a politics of language *against* "perfect communication" and "the one code." Primarily anchored here.
- [[Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity]] — "women of color" / Sister Outsider / Malinche as "a potent subjectivity synthesized from fusions of 'outsider' identities," and the real labor force of the integrated circuit.
- [[The Refusal of Innocence]] — the rejection of origin myths, the Fall, the Garden, and the "dream of a common language"; refusing the politics of victimization and "the innocence of the merely violated."
- [[Affinity Not Identity]] — coalition grounded in chosen affinity and full implication in the world rather than essentialist natural identity, purity, or "mothering" (primary in §02; recapitulated and consummated here).
- [[The Breached Boundaries]] — the catalogue of persistent Western dualisms and the claim that high-tech culture dissolves them, with "no fundamental, ontological separation... of machine and organism."
- [[Embodiment]] — reworked here as distributed and prosthetic: "Why should our bodies end at the skin?"; "The machine is us... an aspect of our embodiment."
- [[The Informatics of Domination]] — invoked as the "world system's informatics of domination" that the cyborg myth challenges "in order to act potently" (charted in §03; supporting source here).
- [[Posthumanism]] — the cyborg's dissolution of the human/animal/machine boundary and refusal of unitary identity as a foundational posthumanist gesture.

## Important Definitions

- [[Cyborg (Haraway)]] — the cybernetic organism / hybrid of machine and organism; this section supplies the political-myth attributes (irony, no garden, no unitary identity) that gloss the §01 definition.
- [[The Informatics of Domination (Haraway)]] — the shift from organic, hierarchical dualisms to coded command-control-communication networks (C3I); invoked here as the system the cyborg myth contests.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Salamander (Regeneration)]] — the regrown limb, "monstrous, duplicated, potent," as the image of cyborg reconstitution after injury, opposed to flawless reproductive rebirth.
- [[I Would Rather Be a Cyborg Than a Goddess]] — the manifesto's signature closing slogan; the cyborg (irony, blasphemy, technology, partiality) chosen over the goddess (organicism, holism, return to the Mother). Sole source.
- [[Blasphemy]] — the manifesto's governing rhetorical mode, consummated here in the "powerful infidel heteroglossia" and the "feminist speaking in tongues to strike fear into the circuits of the supersavers of the new right."
- [[C3I]] — command-control-communication-intelligence; the militarized grid on which bodies are "textualized... as code problems," and which feminist cyborg stories aim to "recode... to subvert command and control."

## Major Tensions

- [[Cyborg vs Goddess]] — the manifesto's defining opposition: the ironic, partial, technological cyborg vs the organic, holistic, reproductive goddess; "both are bound in the spiral dance," but Haraway chooses the cyborg.
- [[Regeneration vs Rebirth]] — reconstitution after injury (monstrous, partial, potent) vs the reproductive matrix's demand for flawless rebirth and return to wholeness.
- [[Domination vs Possibility]] — science and technology as simultaneously "a matrix of complex dominations" and "possible means of great human satisfaction"; the cyborg as both offspring of militarism/capitalism and resource for resistance.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — organisms becoming "biotic systems, communications devices like others," and bodies "textualized... as code problems on the grid of C3I"; the catalogue and dissolution of the organism/machine dualism is this book's contribution to the vault-wide tension.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- The cyborg as a curatorial figure for *material/immaterial* entanglement: bodies "textualized as code problems on the grid of C3I" read alongside Hayles's [[Embodiment vs Information]] and Lippard's dematerialization — the body becoming "a communications device like others."
- Surveillance/control angle: Haraway's [[Domination vs Possibility]] double vision (technology as "a matrix of complex dominations" *and* of "human satisfaction") staged against Han's [[Freedom vs Control]] and Kurzweil's [[Promise vs Peril]] — three different verdicts on the same instrumented body. Note Haraway refuses *both* technophilia and "a demonology of technology."
- Embodiment and the boundary of the skin: "Why should our bodies end at the skin?" as an epigraph for work on prosthesis, distributed cognition, and the machine "as an aspect of our embodiment."
- Identity politics and affinity: [[Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity]], [[Cyborg Writing]], and [[The Refusal of Innocence]] for an essay on coalition without purity — survival by "seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other."
- The closing slogan "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess" as the anchor for a piece on irony, blasphemy, and the refusal of organic holism in contemporary art.
