# Source Summary: Author's Notes (1973 / 1996)

**Book:** [[Six Years]]

## Orientation

This brief front-matter section pairs two notes in Lippard's own voice, written twenty-three years apart, and together they state both the book's *method* and its *stakes*. It is short, but it is the place where the book explains how it is built — and the place where it admits, in retrospect, what became of the art it documents.

The **1973 note** describes the *form* of the book and, in doing so, makes the title's thesis operational rather than merely declared. *Six Years* "is basically a bibliography and list of events, arranged chronologically" (`Directly stated`): it has no continuous expository narrative but is assembled from book lists, periodicals, exhibitions, catalogues, symposia, articles, interviews, and "works by individual artists." Lippard then specifies a three-register typographic code — bold for "All factual information (bibliographical, chronological, and general)," roman for "all anthological" material (excerpts, statements, art works, symposia), and italics for "all commentary by the editor." The chronicle is thus engineered so that the documentary record, the anthologized voices, and the curator's own argument remain typographically distinct. This makes the book a worked example of [[Exhibition as Medium]] and [[The Document (Conceptual Art)|The Document]]: the catalogue/bibliography/list-of-events is treated as the adequate vehicle for an art whose objects had dematerialized into documents, statements, and events. The chronicle-as-medium *is* the argument — the formal correlate of [[Dematerialization of the Art Object]].

The **1996 retrospective note** (added for the University of California Press reprint, at editor Charlene Woodcock's urging) supplies the durable critical payload. Lippard records the book's afterlife: it "went out of print for over a decade and became, ironically, a very expensive collector's item, paralleling the fate of the art it espoused" (`Directly stated`). That single sentence is the recuperation kernel — the dematerialized, anti-commodity work did not escape the market but was reabsorbed by it, and the cheap, reproducible *book about* the dematerialization underwent the identical conversion into a scarce, expensive object. It is the art-historical instance of the vault's titular [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis: the immaterial re-materializes as commodity. See [[Dematerialized art did not escape the market]].

She closes by registering the genre's continuing fertility. Her later, politically engaged work — "involving feminism or photography or public art or art by contemporary Native Americans" — keeps "recalling past events in Conceptual art," which "was a far richer vein than any of us could have realized at the time" (`Directly stated`). This marks Lippard's own turn toward art and politics and frames [[Conceptual Art]] as an under-recognized, still-productive seam rather than a closed historical episode.

## Key Concepts

- [[Dematerialization of the Art Object]] — the book's title concept, here both *enacted* by the chronicle form (a record of documents and events, not objects) and *named by its afterlife* (the dematerialized work re-materializing as expensive collectible)
- [[Conceptual Art]] — the genre the book chronicles; described retrospectively as denser, more diverse, and more fertile than its participants recognized

## Key Arguments

- [[Dematerialized art did not escape the market]] — anti-commodity conceptual art (and the book documenting it) was recuperated as a scarce, expensive commodity; the recuperation is doubled and self-illustrating ("paralleling the fate of the art it espoused")
- [[The catalogue chronicle is the medium of conceptual art]] — the form of the book is the argument: a chronological bibliography and list of events, governed by the three-register typographic code, enacts the title's thesis rather than merely asserting it

## Important Definitions

- The book's **typographic code** (editorial method, explicitly defined): bold = factual/bibliographical/chronological; roman = anthological (excerpts, statements, art works, symposia); italics = editor's commentary. This is a definition of the apparatus by which the book stages documentation as the medium.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- **"A far richer vein"** — conceptual art figured as a deep geological seam still being worked; an image of latent, extractable richness
- **The expensive collector's item** — the out-of-print book turned scarce commodity becomes the emblem of recuperation: the dematerialized object's ironic re-materialization as a costly, fetishized thing

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the art-historical instance: the object dissolved into documents, ideas, and events (immaterial pole), yet the book and the art re-materialize as expensive objects (the material/commodity pole reasserting itself). The strongest cross-book hook.
- Anti-commodity intention vs market recuperation — dematerialization was meant as resistance to the salable object; the irony Lippard names is that the resistance, and its documentation, were both absorbed
- Documentary "fact" vs editorial "commentary" — the typographic code formalizes (without dissolving) the question of where neutral chronicle ends and Lippard's curatorial argument begins
