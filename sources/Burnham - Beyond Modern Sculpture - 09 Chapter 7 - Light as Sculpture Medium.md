# Source Summary: Chapter 7 — Light as Sculpture Medium

**Book:** [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]
**Author:** [[Jack Burnham]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/09 Chapter 7 - Light as Sculpture Medium]]

## Orientation

Chapter 7 is the third chapter of Part Two ("Sculpture as System") and tracks the entrance of *emitted light* — light directly discharged from the art object or system — into twentieth-century sculpture. Where Chapter 3 had concerned *reflected* light playing over surfaces, this chapter turns to light as a medium in its own right, and Burnham opens by stating the systems thesis that organizes the whole second half of the book: light is "the most flexible visual art form yet devised," and its trend is to dissolve the discrete art object into a responsive, life-sensitive environmental system. Emitted light, he writes, "best demonstrates one of the primary qualities of systems: the tendency to fuse art object and environment into a perceptual whole." This is the object-to-system migration of [[Object and System]], now realized as participatory, machine-mediated space — the strongest grounding so far in the Burnham ingest for the dematerializing trajectory the book is charting.

The chapter is built as a long historical survey: color organ (Castel, 1734) → the "color music" experimenters and stage-lighting pioneers → Wilfred's Lumia → the Bauhaus (Hirschfeld-Mack, Moholy-Nagy's Light-Space Modulator, Kepes) → postwar Europe (Fontana, Yves Klein, Piene's Light Ballet, Schöffer's luminodynamics, the GRAV labyrinths) → recent American light and neon art (Flavin, Chryssa, Raysse) → the future (laser/hologram, plasma, surveillance and computer-display systems). Beneath the catalogue of figures run several durable through-lines: [[Luminosity]] as the perceptual and emotional basis of light art; the recurring drive toward "total immateriality"; the claim that a medium matures not through new invention but through a change in public sensibility (Ceram's "mutation" from mechanical to technical thinking); the thesis that light art matures only where it has a remunerative commercial outlet; the figure of the art object as a fragile "system of variable life expectancy"; and "ephemeralization" as the cumulative movement of which light is the latest step.

The chapter closes on its widest horizon — a "cultural trend toward intangibility" in which "pure energy and information seem to be the essences of art" — and reaches it through two recollected sources internal to the vault: Otto Piene's "light... is the incarnation of visible energy," and Marshall McLuhan's lecture remark that a glowing light bulb "radiated pure information." This makes Chapter 7 the book's clearest bridge to the vault's material/immaterial and information axes, and the convergence point for the [[Jack Burnham and Marshall McLuhan]] reference. The chapter is also, in part, first-person practitioner testimony: Burnham appears here as a working neon/electroluminescent-tape artist, not only as critic.

## Key Concepts

- [[Light as a Sculpture Medium]] — realized here as a genre: emitted light "directly discharged from the art object or system," whose trend is to eliminate the specific object and convert the surrounding space into a responsive light-modulating system.
- [[Luminosity]] — the perceptual property defining emitted light (an area of light exceeding a brilliance threshold, perceived as internal energy) and acting as a primeval emotional stimulant tied to life-giving heat and light.
- [[Object and System]] — light "best demonstrates one of the primary qualities of systems: the tendency to fuse art object and environment into a perceptual whole"; the chapter's instantiation of the book's master object→system thesis.
- [[Dematerialization]] — Burnham's "total immateriality" / "ephemeralization" / "intangibility" language is the 1968 *precursor* root of Lippard's later coinage; "this desire for immateriality has always been an aspect of the Light artist's program."
- [[Information]] — the culminating claim that art is reducing to "pure energy and information," anchored by the recollected McLuhan light-bulb-as-pure-information remark.
- [[Systems Theory]] — light demonstrates "one of the primary qualities of systems"; the responsive environment "sensitive to responses from organisms which invade its presence."
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — the participatory, environment-fusing program that the GRAV labyrinths, viewer-controlled rheostats, and sensing photo-cells make concrete.
- [[Kinetic Art]] — Burnham draws the explicit parallel: light art, like kinetic sculpture, was a "stillborn affair" whose pioneers became heroes only once the medium began to make progress.
- [[New Media Art]] — light art as electromechanical/programmed system art (rheostats, photo-cells, laser projectors, plasma display panels) substantively grounds this committed research-focus.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — participatory responsive environments: spectators "provoked into acts of participation," viewers controlling current through neon, the entering spectator carrying "the imprint of the laser light on his body."

## Key Arguments

- [[Light not form determines our perception of sculpture]] — the Chapter 3 thesis whose logical end-point this chapter realizes: when modulation of light no longer needs a sculptural object at all, the environment itself becomes the work.

## Important Definitions

- [[Plasma (Burnham)]] — "the fourth state of matter: being neither solid, liquid, or gas, but the state that certain gases reach when they are 'ionized'"; neon, fluorescent, and arc welding are "forms of low-temperature plasma," and plasma is named as the ultimate future field of the light artist.
- **Emitted light** — "light directly discharged from the art object or system," defined by the quality of luminosity, and contrasted with the reflected and radiant light painters have long studied.
- **Lumia (Thomas Wilfred)** — Wilfred's name for an independent "Art of Light," to develop "free of all the usual comparisons with music"; an art that has "destroyed the experience of temporal duration."
- **Continuous discontinuity (Nicolas Schöffer)** — indeterminate light programs that go on indefinitely, "never quite the same but always appearing the same."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Light Bulb as Pure Information]] — McLuhan's recollected gesture toward a glowing bulb radiating "pure information," the chapter's emblem of light as information without content.
- [[The Electric Light]] — the McLuhan symbol with which Burnham's closing convergence is in direct dialogue.
- **Nearly frozen honey running out of a jar** — Wilfred's image for Lumia's ideal, durationless speed.
- **The cityscape at night as pure pulsating light sculpture** — Moholy-Nagy's vision, with car headlights "frozen into paths of luminous tracery."
- **Plasma as "gaseous jellyfish glowing luminously in the night"** — Gaddis's image of luminous space-organisms, the U.F.O. as the "Unidentified" the artist seeks to grasp.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the chapter's central axis: light dematerializes the object toward "total immateriality," yet light remains "wedded to glass tubing and cumbersome electrical equipment," fragile and low in durability — immaterial aspiration shackled to gross material apparatus.
- [[Form vs Light]] — the Chapter 3 opposition carried forward: where light is the medium, form "is what we make it appear to be."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A genealogy of dematerialization that *predates* Lippard: Burnham's 1968 "total immateriality" and "ephemeralization" as the immaterial program already latent in light art, against which Six Years can be read as a downstream coinage.
- The material/immaterial paradox staged literally: an art that aspires to pure energy yet stays "wedded to glass tubing," fragile and mortal — light as the limit case of the immaterial object that cannot shed its apparatus.
- Burnham–McLuhan as a vault-internal hinge: "light... radiated pure information" / "pure energy and information seem to be the essences of art" — a bridge from sculpture-as-system into the information axis of *Understanding Media*.
- The participatory, responsive light environment (GRAV labyrinths, viewer-controlled current, sensing photo-cells, the spectator carrying the laser's imprint) as a 1960s precedent for New Media and experiential-technology art.
- A medium matures by sensibility, not invention: Ceram's "mutation from mechanical to technical thinking" and Burnham's "what have changed are public sensibilities" as a model for how new art technologies become legible.
- The political-economic claim that art technology follows its commercial outlet (theatre, advertising, cinema) — useful for any essay on the entanglement of avant-garde light art with commerce.
