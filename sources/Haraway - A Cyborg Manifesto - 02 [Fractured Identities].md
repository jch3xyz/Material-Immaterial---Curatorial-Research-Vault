# Source Summary: Fractured Identities

## Orientation

"Fractured Identities" is the second section of *A Cyborg Manifesto* (cited from *Manifestly Haraway*, University of Minnesota Press, 2016). Where the opening section establishes the cyborg as an ironic ontology and political myth, this section turns to the political problem the cyborg is meant to answer: the collapse of any "essential" ground on which a feminist or socialist "us" might rest. Haraway opens by confessing that it has "become difficult to name one's feminism by a single adjective—or even to insist in every circumstance on the noun," and she diagnoses a "crisis in political identity" whose "sources... are legion." Identities, she argues, "seem contradictory, partial, and strategic"; once their social and historical constitution is recognized, "gender, race, and class cannot provide the basis for belief in 'essential' unity." Her rhetorical posture is internal and self-implicating — she writes from a named "historical location in white, professional middle-class, female, radical, North American, mid-adult bodies," and her own taxonomy is, she admits, "a reinscription of history."

The constructive heart of the section is the substitution of coalition for essence: "another response through coalition—affinity, not identity." Haraway builds this out through Chela Sandoval's model of "oppositional consciousness" and the self-consciously constructed identity of "women of color," and through Katie King's critique of feminist taxonomies that "police deviation from official women's experience." Against this affinity-politics she sets a paired critique of the two dominant totalizing feminisms of her moment: Marxist/socialist-feminism, which essentializes through the ontology of labor and inherits a "preeminently Western self," and Catharine MacKinnon's radical feminism, whose ontology of sexual appropriation constructs woman as "a nonsubject, a nonbeing." Both, she charges, refuse "the status of a partial explanation" and so leave "no structural room for race."

The section also grounds the manifesto's refusal of innocence and organic wholeness: "with the loss of innocence in our origin, there is no expulsion from the Garden either," and "no construction is whole." It closes by naming the danger of its own anti-essentialism — "lapsing into boundless difference" — and the discipline that answers it: "Some differences are playful; some are poles of world historical systems of domination. 'Epistemology' is about knowing the difference." ## Key Concepts

- [[Fractured Identities]] — identity (gender, race, class, even "woman") is contradictory, partial, strategic, and historically constituted, so no category grounds an essential unity; "woman disintegrates into women."
- [[Affinity Not Identity]] — Haraway's proposed basis for political coalition: unity built through "conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship," not natural identification.
- [[Oppositional Consciousness]] — Chela Sandoval's model (reported by Haraway): a political identity "born of the skills for reading webs of power by those refused stable membership in the social categories of race, sex, or class."
- [[Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity]] — a contested, self-consciously constructed coalitional identity defined "by conscious appropriation of negation," "out of otherness, difference, and specificity."
- [[The Refusal of Innocence]] — the rejection of an innocent origin, organic wholeness, and victimhood-as-the-only-ground-for-insight; "no construction is whole."
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the "preeminently Western self" of Marxian humanism whose dissolution ("the end of man") is the same event as woman's disintegration into women.
- [[Posthumanism]] — "the systematic breakdown of all the signs of Man in 'Western' traditions"; the proto-posthuman dispersal of the universal humanist subject.
- [[The Informatics of Domination]] — forward reference: the section's "unity-through-domination," "webs of power," and "world historical systems of domination" anticipate the coding/command grid named in the next section.

## Key Arguments

- [[There is no natural basis for the unity of women]] — there is nothing about being "female" that naturally binds women; consciousness is "an achievement... not a natural fact," so unity must be built through affinity, and the totalizing feminisms that posited a single natural ground (labor, or sexual appropriation) erased difference, above all race.

## Important Definitions

- "women of color" (Sandoval via Haraway) — not a criterial or natural category but a coalitional identity defined by negation and conscious affinity (captured within [[Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity]]).
- "oppositional consciousness" (Sandoval via Haraway) — a model of political identity formed from the skills of those refused stable category-membership, organized around "contradictory locations and heterochronic calendars" rather than relativism/pluralism (captured within [[Oppositional Consciousness]]).

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- Expulsion from the Garden / loss of innocence in our origin — the refused Edenic origin-myth; with no innocent origin there is also "no expulsion from the Garden." Carried by [[The Refusal of Innocence]].
- Denaturing protein ("it denatures them as heat denatures a fragile protein") — the figure for how recognizing the "noninnocence" of "woman" irreversibly alters all previous categories.
- Weaving "something other than a shroud for the day after the apocalypse" — the figure for constructive, non-apocalyptic feminist work amid "the fraying of identities."
- The schematic "caricature" of feminisms ("labor, by analogy reproduction, by extension sex, by addition race") — race appended last as a structural figure for its theoretical marginalization.

## Major Tensions

- [[Domination vs Possibility]] — the refusal of a "natural matrix of unity" keeps the cyborg world open to emergent kinship rather than closing it as a final grid of control; denaturalizing "woman" is a move toward possibility.
- [[Cyborg vs Goddess]] — the section's refusal of "any more natural matrix of unity" and of organic wholeness is the conceptual ground of the manifesto's later preference for the cyborg over the goddess.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- Anchor for an essay on anti-essentialist coalition: "affinity, not identity" set against identity politics, read through Sandoval's oppositional consciousness and the constructed identity of "women of color" — the constructive counterpart to the manifesto's later cyborg politics.
- A case study in the critique of totalizing theory: Haraway's paired reading of Marxist/socialist-feminism (the ontology of labor) and MacKinnon's radical feminism (the ontology of sexual appropriation) as theories that demand the status of a totality rather than a partial explanation, and that thereby leave "no structural room for race."
- The "refusal of innocence" passage ("no expulsion from the Garden"; victimhood as "the only ground for insight... has done enough damage") as a primary text for the manifesto's rejection of organic wholeness and origin myths — pairs with [[The Refusal of Innocence]] and the [[Cyborg vs Goddess]] tension.
- "The end of man" / "the symbolic system of the family of man... breaks up" as a feminist route into the vault's [[Posthumanism]] and [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] threads — Haraway's dissolution of the universal "Man" alongside Hayles, O'Gieblyn, and Harari.
- The closing distinction — "Some differences are playful; some are poles of world historical systems of domination" — as a hinge for an essay on how a politics of difference avoids collapsing into relativism.
