# Source Summary: Chapter 2 — The Biotic Sources of Modern Sculpture

## Orientation

Chapter 2 is the biotic-sources chapter of Part I ("Sculpture as Object"). Its governing question, stated outright, is: "Why, if vitalism played such a reactionary role in the biological sciences, did it provide some of the most advanced justification for avant-garde abstract sculpture?" Burnham answers with a long intellectual history that runs through five interlocking sections: organicism as an evolving creed (Mumford's "organic ideology," the gravitation of technology back toward organic models); the centuries-long mechanist–vitalist dispute in biology (Descartes, La Mettrie, Wöhler, Claude Bernard, Haeckel, and the steady erosion of the organic/inorganic boundary into "a single continuum"); Bergson's poetics of vitalism (the *élan vital* as a beautiful, poetic, scientifically defeated idea that nonetheless became the reigning aesthetic of modern sculpture); the aesthetics of neo-vitalism in Herbert Read and Henri Focillon (the [[Organic vs Geometric|organic–geometric]] fusion, Read's confession that "I am an unregenerate Bergsonian"); and close case studies of the vitalist sculptors — Gaudier-Brzeska, Brancusi, Arp, Hepworth, Moore — plus the postwar American welders.

The chapter's deepest claim, and the one that binds it to the book's master thesis, is that [[Vitalism is the transitional step from the sculptural object to the system|vitalism is a transitional step in the migration from inanimate object to system]]. Burnham distinguishes two senses of "organic": the appearance or metaphor of life in inert matter (the sculptor's [[Vitalism|vitalism]] — life is "plastically," not literally, present) and the functional organization of interacting systems (the engineer's and biologist's [[Organicism|organicism]]). His historical argument is that the meaning of "organic" has migrated from biotic appearance toward biotic functioning via the machine; vitalism reinvested sculpture with conviction after the decline of realism, but it drew on a finite stock of biotic forms and so was destined for [[Form Exhaustion|form exhaustion]]. The chapter thus quietly anticipates the move to actual life-simulation (kinetic, cybernetic) developed in Part II, and is the primary-text home of the vault's [[Object and System|object-to-system]] spine on its biological side.

Rhetorically the chapter is a work of synthesis and adjudication: Burnham is at once a historian of science (tracking the mechanists' cumulative victories from Wöhler's 1828 urea synthesis through DNA and the first synthetic-life experiments), a critic of art-historical method (testing Worringer/Hauser's [[Organic vs Geometric|organic–geometric]] polarity and Focillon's morphogenetic optimism), and a forecaster (his "anti-style or no-style aesthetic" prophecy). His verdict on the tradition he reconstructs is double-edged: vitalism was humane and indispensable as a source of artistic conviction, but as "a synoptic view of the real meaning of science and technology and their carry-over into the arts, it is now both outmoded and inadequate." This is also the chapter the vault's [[Emergence]] note implicitly points back to: O'Gieblyn's charge that emergence is "structurally identical to the discredited doctrine of vitalism" finds its primary-text history here.

## Key Concepts

- [[Vitalism]] — the chapter keystone; the poetic, metaphysical doctrine that life is instigated by a non-material principle (Aristotle's "entelechy," Bergson's *élan vital*); the reigning aesthetic of modern sculpture for some fifty years.

- [[Form Exhaustion]] — vitalist sculpture's dependence on a "decidedly finite" stock of biotic forms; the idiom died when its form-possibilities were used up.

## Key Arguments

- [[Vitalism is the transitional step from the sculptural object to the system]] — the meaning of "organic" migrates from biotic appearance to biotic function-via-machine; vitalism is the idealist halfway point before sculpture can actually simulate living systems.
- [[The mechanist-vitalist dispute was settled by sensibilities not facts]] — the controversy was "not of the right facts, but of the right sensibilities"; vitalism survived longer as aesthetic than as science because of a "time lag."
- [[There are no qualitative differences between living and nonliving matter]] — the cumulative mechanist victories (Wöhler, Bernard, Miller/Oparin/Abelson, DNA) erode the organic/inorganic boundary "into a single continuum."
- [[Biology machine theory and sculpture converge on understanding living matter through its creation]] — explicitly "one of the central ideas of this book": the three disciplines' meanings of organicism converge on creating, not merely imitating, life.
- [[Formalism and vitalism are exhausted because scientific reality has become invisible]] — vitalism (like formalism) depends on a finite, now-depleted vocabulary of biotic models, and on a visible scientific reality that has receded.
- [[Emergence is vitalism updated and uncomfortably like magic]] — cross-link to O'Gieblyn; this chapter is the primary history of the vitalism that emergence is charged with reviving.

## Important Definitions

- [[Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)]] — Bergson's "vital impetus" / Shaw's "life force"; the essence that makes life possible in inert matter, underwriting biomorphic sculpture.

## Major Tensions

- [[Mechanism vs Vitalism]] — the chapter's master opposition: a physical/electrochemical basis of life against a non-material entelechy.
- [[Vitalism vs Organicism]] — the poetic celebration of life against the functional organization of systems; "in some essential ways they remain in polar opposition."
- [[Organic vs Geometric]] — Worringer/Hauser's art-historical poles (amorphic/representational vs. linear/abstract), fused in abstract vitalist sculpture and, "for the first time," literally by technology.
- [[Object vs System]] — the book-spine opposition; this chapter places vitalism between the poles.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Moore's "truth to material" and the palpable mass of the sculptural object against the migration toward dematerialized, lifelike system.
- [[Metaphor vs Literalism]] — life "not literally, but plastically, present" against the technician's coming literal animation.
