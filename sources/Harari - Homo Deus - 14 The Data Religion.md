# Source Summary: The Data Religion

**Book:** [[Homo Deus]]
**Author:** [[Yuval Noah Harari]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/14 The Data Religion]]

> Numbering note: the raw file's front-matter labels this Harari's own `chapter: 11`, but it is the vault's `14 The Data Religion` and the closing chapter of *Homo Deus*. It functions as the book's synthetic conclusion — it gathers, names, and stress-tests the techno-religion toward which the whole argument has been building.

## Orientation

This is the culminating chapter of *Homo Deus*, where Harari names and anatomizes [[Dataism]] — the emerging "scientific dogma" (and, he argues, nascent religion) holding that "the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing." He stages Dataism as the confluence of two scientific tidal waves: the life sciences' post-Darwin reduction of organisms to biochemical algorithms ([[Organisms Are Algorithms]]) and computer science's post-Turing engineering of electronic algorithms. Their fusion — "exactly the same mathematical laws apply to both" — collapses the barrier between animals and machines and predicts that electronic algorithms will eventually decipher and outperform biochemical ones. For the vault this chapter is pivotal: Harari is the figure who turned "Dataism" into a worldview and a "Data Religion," so it supplies the believer's/genealogical account that Byung-Chul Han's note critiques ([[Yuval Noah Harari and Byung-Chul Han]]).

The chapter then runs the book's endgame. It rereads everything as data-processing: organisms, societies, and above all the economy and politics, where capitalism vs. communism and democracy vs. dictatorship become competing data-processing architectures ([[Distributed vs Centralised Processing]]) rather than ethical creeds — capitalism and democracy "won" the twentieth century not because they were "good" but because distributed processing handled accelerating technological change better ([[Society as Algorithm]]). It then diagnoses the hollowing-out of politics: as technology outpaces it, grand political vision dies, government becomes "mere administration," and power leaks into a system so complex that neither despots nor a cabal of billionaires can steer it — so that "having power means knowing what to ignore," a political mirror of Han's [[Hyperinformation]]. The "History in a Nutshell" section rereads the whole species as a single data-processing system whose history is the story of improving its efficiency, culminating in [[The Internet-of-All-Things]], after which "Homo sapiens will vanish."

The final movements turn the screw on the human. Harari identifies a genuinely new value — [[Freedom of Information]], the first since 1789, given not to humans but to information itself — and gives it a missionary creed (maximise data flow, connect everything, the greatest sin is to block the flow) and a martyr, [[Aaron Swartz, Dataism's First Martyr]]. He inverts the humanist "Know Thyself": where humanism commanded "Listen to your feelings," Dataism commands "Listen to the algorithms — they know how you feel," because [[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves]] now surpass our evolutionarily honed feelings, while the master algorithms themselves become an unknowable [[The Black Box]] likened to the Christian God ([[The Algorithm as Providence]]). Then, in "A Ripple in the Data Flow," Harari supplies the heretic's objections himself: we cannot explain how data flows produce consciousness, and life may not reduce to decision-making ([[Intelligence vs Consciousness]]). But even a false Dataism could conquer the world, shifting the worldview from homo-centric to data-centric ([[Homo-Centric vs Data-Centric]], [[Posthumanism]]) and doing to Homo sapiens what Homo sapiens did to the animals — reducing us to "a ripple within the cosmic data flow." The book closes on three questions about whether organisms are really algorithms, whether intelligence or consciousness is more valuable, and what happens when non-conscious algorithms know us better than we know ourselves.

## Key Concepts

- [[Dataism]] — the chapter's central subject: the worldview that the universe is data flows and value is contribution to data processing; "began as a neutral scientific theory" but is "now mutating into a religion," whose supreme value is information flow. Harari is its progenitor-side voice; Han is its critic.
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the "current scientific dogma" that giraffes, tomatoes and humans are "just different methods for processing data," and that emotions and intelligence are "just algorithms."
- [[The Internet-of-All-Things]] — Dataism's eschaton: the cosmic, all-knowing, all-powerful data-processing system "like God," whose completion means Homo sapiens vanishes.
- [[Freedom of Information]] — the first genuinely new value since 1789; given to information rather than to humans, and capable of overriding the human right to own and restrict data.
- [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]] — non-conscious algorithms may outperform conscious intelligence at every data-processing task, raising the question of what would be lost in replacing one with the other.
- [[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves]] — Google and Facebook algorithms that "know exactly how you feel" plus "a million other things about you that you hardly suspect," displacing introspection as the route to self-knowledge.
- [[Society as Algorithm]] — economy and politics reread as data-processing systems; capitalism/democracy as distributed processing, communism/dictatorship as centralised.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — Harari's "the universe consists of data flows" is an independent statement of the cosmos-as-information-processor thesis shared with Hayles and O'Gieblyn.
- [[Big Data]] — the algorithmic apparatus Dataists trust over "human knowledge and wisdom"; convergent with Han's account.
- [[The Black Box]] / [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — the master algorithms "nobody really understands," which "evolve independently," explicitly likened to God whose plan we cannot grasp.
- [[Patternism]] / [[Algorithmic Determinism]] / [[The Body as Data]] — fed by "organisms are algorithms": identity, choice, and the body recast as recodable data-patterns and algorithm outputs.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the homo-centric→data-centric shift and the "retire Homo sapiens" move that sideline the human as humanism sidelined God.
- [[Information]] — "information flow" as supreme value; the chapter's running currency.
- [[Hyperinformation]] — Han's parallel: Harari's "censorship by flooding" and "knowing what to ignore" reframe Han's hyperinformation politically.

## Key Arguments

> No chapter-specific argument notes were created in this ingest; the arguments below are stated in prose and grounded in the Source Citations. They are intentionally not wiki-linked, to keep every link resolving to a real note.

- Dataism is the convergence of two scientific waves (biology-as-algorithms post-Darwin; electronic algorithms post-Turing) and collapses the barrier between animals and machines.
- Capitalism beat communism (and democracy beat dictatorship) because distributed data-processing outperforms centralised processing under accelerating technological change — not because it was more ethical.
- As technology outpaces politics, grand political vision dies and power dissolves into a system no one controls; "having power means knowing what to ignore."
- A genuinely new value — freedom of information — has emerged, the first since 1789, given to information rather than to humans.
- Humanism commanded "Listen to your feelings"; Dataism commands "Listen to the algorithms — they know how you feel."
- Even a factually false Dataism could conquer the world and dethrone Homo sapiens as Homo sapiens dethroned the animals.
- The heretic's counter (supplied by Harari himself): life may not reduce to data flows, because we cannot explain how data produces consciousness, and life may not reduce to decision-making.

## Major Tensions

- [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] — the chapter's pivotal opposition and one of its three closing questions: non-conscious algorithms may outperform conscious intelligence at every task.
- [[Homo-Centric vs Data-Centric]] — the "religious revolution": humanism worships human experience; Dataism declares experiences "valueless if they are not shared" and migrates authority to algorithms.
- [[Distributed vs Centralised Processing]] — the master schema rereading capitalism/communism and democracy/dictatorship as architecture, not morality.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the human as embodied, savannah-evolved algorithm vs. the human as a recodable/retire-able pattern in the data flow.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — privileging free-flowing information over the embodied experience that "occurs inside us."
- [[Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman]] — the sidelining of the human subject by the data-centric worldview.
