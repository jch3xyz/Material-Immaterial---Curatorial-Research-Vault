## Orientation

"Art and Education in the Telematic Culture" (originally *Leonardo* 21, supplemental issue, 1988; source pages 212–219) is one of Ascott's foundational primary statements of [[Telematic Art]] and of the broader telematic culture it presupposes. It opens historically — Simon Nora's coinage of "telematics," the 1978 Nora–Minc report to the president of France, the resulting Programme Télématique, and the mass uptake of Minitel as the first ubiquitous public videotex system — and then mounts its central thesis: that **interactivity is the essence of all telematic systems**, and therefore art is not an object but a process and a system in which meaning is *co-created* by participants rather than transmitted from artist to viewer. Ascott imports the communication-theory critique of the linear "components" model (Rogers and Kincaid's convergence model) and the biology-of-cognition critique of the "tube" metaphor (Maturana and Varela's structural determinism) and applies both directly to art, arriving at what he calls a [[Distributed Authorship]] — "all those involved in negotiating for meaning in a given context." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

The essay's polemical spine is a historical contrast between the Renaissance perspectival paradigm and the emergent telematic / network paradigm. Renaissance space — "subject to the authority of the vanishing point," placing artist, observer, and scientist *outside* the world looking in — is read as "the perfect metaphor of the ordering of parts" in pyramidal societies where information flows one way, from apex to base. The telematic network overturns this: it renders "obsolete the distinction in absolute terms between the artist and viewer as producer and consumer," leaving "simply… participant in a system creating meaning seen as art." Ascott then enlists a "science of wholeness" — quantum "undivided wholeness," Bateson's ecology of mind ("human plus computer system plus environment constitutes a thinking system"), Bertalanffy's general system theory, and Peter Russell's global brain — to argue that telematic convergence makes the thinking system *planetary*, approaching [[Network Consciousness]] / global consciousness, with the whole-earth image beamed back from space figured as the planet at Lacan's mirror stage. (Confidence: Directly stated.)

The second half turns to institutions and pedagogy, framed by Ascott's own named pair of "isolation" (the crude separation of atelier, museum, library, concert hall, academy, each a one-way system identifying art with objects) and "convergence" (their fusion into pervasive networks). He casts the computer as a [[The Computer as Matrix|matrix]] — a universal machine, the "lingua franca" of digitisation — attacks the life drawing class as the central anomaly of the art academy ("the body immobilised, without mind… the ideal of all repressive cultures"), and calls for hand-to-mind over hand-to-eye coordination and "technological extensions of the senses" because "the project of the art of our century has been essentially to make the invisible visible." He warns against a homogenisation of culture (telematics must instead generate difference), against using networks "merely as tools of production" ("Socrates rather than… Cato"), and anticipates "the dematerialisation of academies, galleries, and museums," closing on "the symbiosis of computers and human beings and the integration of natural and artificial intelligence." This chapter is therefore a primary anchor for telematic art's pedagogical and institutional program, and a strong EXTENSION source for the systems-art and dematerialization clusters that [[Jack Burnham]] and [[Lucy Lippard]] grounded in the vault. (Confidence: Directly stated; the Burnham/Lippard bridges are interpretive synthesis.)

## Key Concepts

- [[Telematic Art]] — the keystone term: art of computer-mediated telecommunications networks; here grounded in its 1988 statement that "art is all process, all system" and that telematic networks make the artist/viewer distinction obsolete.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — Ascott's claim that telematic convergence makes the thinking system planetary, approaching "global consciousness"; "network as the metaphor for the emerging culture."
- [[Distributed Authorship]] — meaning is created out of interaction between people, not transmitted; the "author" may be the system of interaction itself, dispersed across all participants.
- [[The Computer as Matrix]] — the computer not as a stand-alone image generator but as "the matrix through which the abundance of data in all its modes can pass," the universal machine and "lingua franca" of digitisation.
- [[Network as Artwork]] — the whole telematic network treated as the art system within which "richly layered fields of meaning" are generated, regardless of media employed.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — Bateson's "human plus computer system plus environment constitutes a thinking system," with the lines between human, computer, and environment "purely artificial and fictitious."

- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — Ascott's rejection of "one-way linearity" for mutual, convergent exchange is the telematic continuation of Burnham's two-way communication (EXTEND).
- [[Dematerialization]] — Ascott extends dematerialization from the art *object* (Lippard/Burnham) to the art *institution* and network: "the dematerialisation of academies, galleries, and museums… enjoyed also as electronic 'traces'" (EXTEND).

## Key Arguments

- [[In telematic art meaning is produced through interaction not transmitted]] — "Art does not reside in the object alone… Art is all process, all system"; meaning "is created out of interaction between people, rather than being 'something' that is sent from one to another." (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- [[Telematic networks constitute a planetary network consciousness]] — "With the convergence of computers and telecommunications, the 'thinking system' becomes planetary," approaching "the next stage of planetary awareness—global consciousness." (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- [[The project of twentieth-century art is to make the invisible visible]] — art "has progressively sought to be in touch with unseen forces and fields, systems, relationships… and to make them visible," requiring "technological extensions of the senses." (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- The Renaissance perspectival paradigm encodes a one-way social pyramid that the telematic network overturns — the vanishing point as "the perfect metaphor of the ordering of parts" in societies where "information flows one way, from the apex of the social pyramid to the base"; the telematic network renders the producer/consumer (artist/viewer) distinction obsolete. (Confidence: Directly stated. No dedicated note realized this pass — folded into [[Distributed Authorship]] and [[Object vs System]].)
- Telematic culture must generate difference, not homogenise it — the massive memory of computers can preserve "all the differences of individual experience, local culture, and regional attributes"; "the aim of a telematic culture cannot be to homogenise… but to generate difference in the multiplicity of viewpoints." (Confidence: Directly stated. Captured by the [[Creativity vs Production]] / homogenisation-vs-difference framing; no standalone note.)
- Used merely as tools of production, telematic networks promote sterility and alienation — "Socrates rather than… Cato"; the universal machine "can contain as much creative thought… as we put into it," but a productivist deployment "will certainly and very effectively promote sterility and alienation." (Confidence: Directly stated. Carried by the [[Creativity vs Production]] tension; cross-links Han's productivist critique.)

## Important Definitions

- [[Telematics (Ascott)]] — Nora's coinage as Ascott deploys it: "the new electronic technology derived from the convergence of computers and telecommunications systems." (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- [[Interactivity (Ascott)]] — "the essence of the videotex system, as it is of all telematic systems, giving us the ability to interact in electronic space… beyond the normal constraints of time and space." (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- The "thinking system" (Bateson via Ascott) — "human plus computer system plus environment"; the lines among them "are not boundaries of the thinking system" but "the lines across the pathways along which information passes." ## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Planet at the Mirror Stage]] — "It is as if the planet is at the *stade du miroir*," gaining self-awareness from the whole-earth image "beaming… back to us" via astronauts and remote sensors, after Lacan's infant.
- [[The Artwork as Matrix and Catalyst]] — the computer/network as matrix: "much more than a stand-alone generator of images," it integrates and works between diverse media and catalyses the participatory generation of meaning.
- **Network as the metaphor for the emerging culture** — Ascott's master image, explicitly named: "Our assertion of network as the metaphor for the emerging culture appears to find support in fields beyond art." (Plain text — captured by [[Network Consciousness]] / [[Network as Artwork]].)

- **Renaissance space / the vanishing point** — perspectival space as "the perfect metaphor of the ordering of parts" in a one-way pyramid society. (Plain text.)
- **"Garbage in, garbage out" / the "horseless carriage" stage** — the universal machine reflects what is put into it; current (1988) interfaces are at the "'horseless carriage' stage." (Plain text figures.)
- **Minitel** — the emblem of the telematisation of culture; the seven-figure number on a Paris Métro poster as the sign "that another Minitel service is being advertised." (Plain text — one-off symbol, no note.)
- **The whole earth image from space** — the planet's reflected self-image; the trigger of planetary self-awareness. (Plain text.)

## Major Tensions

- [[Isolation vs Convergence]] — Ascott's own named pair: the problem (institutional isolation — "a rather clear separation between atelier, museum, library, concert hall, and academy") and the remedy (convergence into "pervasive and wide-reaching networks").
- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — the "tube" / "components" model and the Renaissance one-way despatch vs telematic mutual convergence; "the concept of interactivity… in contrast to the one-way linearity of older models."
- [[Creativity vs Production]] — telematic systems as convivial, creative media vs "tools of production" that "promote sterility and alienation" (Socrates vs Cato); also holds the homogenisation-vs-difference axis.
- [[Object vs System]] — "Art does not reside in the object alone… Art is all process, all system" carries Burnham's object→system spine into the telematic register (EXTEND).
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the eight-author tension; Ascott extends it into the networked, dematerialised register (object → institution → network → electronic "traces") (EXTEND).

- A keystone for an essay on the **dematerialization of the institution**: pairing Ascott's "dematerialisation of academies, galleries, and museums… enjoyed also as electronic 'traces'" with Lippard's dematerialization of the object and Burnham's object→system spine, to trace how the immaterial turn migrated from the artwork to the network and the museum.
