# Source Summary: Chapter 10 — Make Them Dance

## Orientation

"Make Them Dance" is the climactic chapter of Part II ("The Advance of Surveillance Capitalism"). Its thesis is that the [[The Prediction Imperative]] completes itself in a new phase Zuboff names [[Economies of Action]]: the move from merely *knowing* and *predicting* behavior to *actuating* it — intervening in the real world to "make them dance." Predictions are only extrapolations until they are made certain, and certainty requires intervention; so the apparatus adds "actuation" to its powers, completing what she calls [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — her deliberate echo and inversion of Marx's "means of production." She names three concrete techniques, gathered in the vault as [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]]: tuning (the nudge, subliminal cues, choice architecture), herding (controlling a person's immediate context to foreclose alternatives — the uncontract that shuts down a car), and conditioning (B. F. Skinner's operant reinforcement applied at scale). The most predictive surplus of all, she argues, is behavior that has already been modified — the route to [[Guaranteed Outcomes]] and [[A Utopia of Certainty]].

The chapter then runs two extended "experiments." First, **Facebook**: the 2010 sixty-one-million-person voter-mobilization study and the 2014 "emotional contagion" study (N=689,003), which proved Facebook can tune real-world behavior and emotion at scale through subliminal cues operating outside users' awareness, plus a leaked 2017 Australian document showing Facebook pinpointing the moments when young people feel "stressed," "defeated," "worthless." This becomes the chapter's normative core: because behavior modification works only beneath awareness, "human consciousness itself is a threat to surveillance revenues." Second, **Pokémon Go** (John Hanke / Niantic, incubated at Google): an augmented-reality game that herds players through the physical world to "sponsored locations" — a real-world incarnation of [[Behavioral Futures Markets]], "a game about a game," extending the logic of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] from the screen into reality and showcasing [[The Reality Business]].

The final section, "What Were the Means of Behavioral Modification?", is a historical pivot: in the 1960s–70s the United States, through Senator Sam Ervin's Subcommittee on Constitutional Rights and revelations about the CIA's MKUltra and Skinner's *Beyond Freedom & Dignity*, mobilized to reject behavior modification as state power, producing the Common Rule, institutional review boards, and the Belmont Report. Zuboff's devastating point: those same means did not die but "resurfaced... as a creature of the market," now aimed not at "them" (prisoners, enemies, captives) but at "us" — everyone — free of the constitutional constraints that once contained the state. The chapter recapitulates [[The Division of Learning in Society]] (Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?) and the figure of users "exiled from our own behavior," reduced to "human natural resources." Its rhetorical posture is that of an alarmed social scientist closing the indictment of Part II: this is not "more of the same" capitalism but a new engine that supplants self-determination ([[Autonomy vs Heteronomy]]) with externally engineered determination, asserting [[The Right to the Future Tense]] over us.

## Key Concepts

- [[Economies of Action]] — the third and culminating economy of the prediction imperative (after scale and scope); surveillance capitalists no longer merely know and predict behavior but *act on* it via "actuation" to drive it toward certainty.
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the global digital apparatus that modifies behavior automatically, at scale, for profit; Marx's "means of production" updated; this chapter is its fullest exposition of the *actuation* completion.
- [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]] — the three techniques of economies of action: subliminal nudging, context control, and Skinnerian reinforcement at scale.
- [[The Prediction Imperative]] — the competitive necessity to produce ever-more-certain predictions, which now compels actuation: surveillance capitalists "must use all means available to supplant autonomous action with heteronomous action."
- [[Guaranteed Outcomes]] — the operational goal: closing the gap between prediction and observation so that behavior already modified becomes maximally predictable.
- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — the horizon toward which economies of action drive; "the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty."
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the raw material; here surplus drives actuation, and "the most predictive source of all is behavior that has already been modified."
- [[Prediction Products]] — in the real-world game these "take the form of protocols that impose forms of telestimulation" to herd people to spend.
- [[Behavioral Futures Markets]] — Pokémon Go's "sponsored locations" reveal their physical-world form, herding players to paying merchants on a "cost per visit" basis.
- [[The Reality Business]] — the extension of extraction and actuation from the online world into physical reality; Pokémon Go is its showcase.
- [[Telestimulation]] — remote, undetectable stimulation of behavior across a population; both Facebook and Pokémon Go are "a living laboratory for telestimulation at scale."
- [[The Division of Learning in Society]] — recapitulated as the chapter's closing frame: Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the master logic whose "means of production" this chapter completes with the addition of actuation.

## Key Arguments

- Surveillance capitalism completes itself by adding actuation — it no longer only knows and predicts behavior, it acts on it to manufacture [[Guaranteed Outcomes]]. *(Directly stated)*
- Individual awareness is the enemy of behavior modification; therefore "human consciousness itself is a threat to surveillance revenues." *(Directly stated)* — grounds [[Awareness vs Evasion]].
- Economies of action shift the locus of control over the future tense from "I will" to "You will," exiling us from our own behavior — replacing autonomy with heteronomy. *(Directly stated)* — grounds [[Autonomy vs Heteronomy]] and [[The Right to the Future Tense]].
- Pokémon Go is "a game about a game" — the visible game herds players through the real world to constitute a hidden second board: a real-world [[Behavioral Futures Markets]]. *(Directly stated)*
- The means of behavioral modification, once rejected by US democracy as illegitimate state power in the 1970s, have resurfaced as a creature of the market — now aimed at "us," not "them," and free of constitutional constraints. *(Directly stated as history; Interpretive synthesis where Zuboff draws the resurfacing-as-market-power parallel)*
- Tuning exploits natural human empathy — "a risky strength" — turning a moral capacity into a vector of manipulation. *(Directly stated)*

## Important Definitions

- Tuning — "subliminal cues designed to subtly shape the flow of behavior at the precise time and place for maximally efficient influence"; includes the nudge (Thaler & Sunstein). *(captured in [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]])*
- Herding — "a second approach that relies on controlling key elements in a person's immediate context... foreclosing action alternatives and thus moving behavior along a path of heightened probability that approximates certainty." *(captured in [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]])*
- Conditioning — Skinner's operant "application of reinforcements to shape specific behaviors," applied at scale via always-on networked devices. *(captured in [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]])*
- Autonomy / heteronomy — "The word autonomy derives from the Greek and literally means 'regulation by the self.' It stands in contrast to heteronomy, which means 'regulation by others.'" *(grounds [[Autonomy vs Heteronomy]])*
