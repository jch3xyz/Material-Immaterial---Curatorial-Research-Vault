# Source Summary: Chapter 16 — The Print: How to Dig It

## Orientation

This is a Part II case-study chapter on a single medium — **the print**, in the precise sense McLuhan intends here: not typography (that is the next chapter, "The Printed Word"), but the *pictorial* print — the woodcut, the engraving, the map, the blueprint, the comic strip. He opens by defining it as "the art of making pictorial statements in a precise and repeatable form," a craft "we have long taken for granted in the West," and notes "that without prints and blueprints, without maps and geometry, the world of modern sciences and technologies would hardly exist." The chapter's headline thesis is that the supreme and most easily overlooked quality of the print is **repeatability**: "it is a pictorial statement that can be repeated precisely and indefinitely... Repeatability is the core of the mechanical principle that has dominated our world, especially since the Gutenberg technology." This restates the Part I apparatus in concrete pictorial form: the print is the visual [[Media as Extensions of Man|extension of man]] that fragments an integral action into uniform, repeatable units, and its *message* is repeatability itself, independent of any particular picture's content.

The chapter is theoretically load-bearing for three reasons beyond simple reinforcement. First, it is one of the clearest places where McLuhan applies the [[Media Hot and Cold|hot/cold distinction]] to a *visual* print medium, and does so counter-intuitively: the *old woodcut* and the modern comic strip/comic book are COOL — they offer "very little data about any particular moment in time, or aspect in space," compelling "a high degree of participation by the viewer in order to complete what is only hinted at" — and the TV image's "mosaic mesh of dots" is read as their structural descendant, while typography (high-definition) is HOT and, against popular intuition, "involves the reader much less than did manuscript." Second, the chapter contains McLuhan's most developed statement of the **visualization of space**: medieval and "native" man inhabited a world where "each thing made its own space" — discontinuous, heterogeneous, non-objective — whereas intensified visual information "transformed the print into a three-dimensional world of perspective and fixed point of view," producing "uniform, continuous and 'rational' space," which relativity (1905) then announced to be "an illusion or fiction." Third, it offers a compact statement of the storage/retrieval function of media — maps, blueprints, the alphabet as a visual store of [[Information]] — in the aphorism "to store is to expedite."

Everything maps cleanly onto the Part I concepts; this chapter mints no new node. Its distinctive thesis (the print as a precisely/indefinitely repeatable pictorial statement) is already anchored in the cross-chapter argument [[Repeatability is the core of the mechanical principle that has dominated the West]], where it supplies the *pictorial-print* facet beside Chapter 18's *typography → nationalism* facet. The "each thing made its own space" instance anchors inside [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]]. The chapter also supplies a rich vein of cross-author hooks: maps-as-media and the "store is to expedite" line connect to Hayles's [[Informatics]] and the [[Information]] cluster; the cartographic and "native" anecdotes (Prince Modupe's father, the Melanesian carver) dramatize abstract visual space against lived tactile space.

## Key Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the print and the alphabet are the *visual* extension of man; the alphabet "left the visual component as supreme in the word," and electricity now creates "non-visual means of storing and retrieving information," shifting science's "entire base and character."
- [[The Medium Is the Message]] — the print's effect lies not in any picture's content but in the imposed pattern: "The message of the print and of typography is primarily that of repeatability."
- [[Media Hot and Cold]] — the chapter's most distinctive application of the axis to a visual medium: the cool low-definition woodcut/comic/TV ("very low degree of data... high degree of participation") vs the hot typography that "involves the reader much less than did manuscript."

## Important Definitions

- [[Hot and Cool Media (McLuhan)]] — corroborated by the cool *visual* print: a medium with little data demands high participation; one with much data permits little. The woodcut, comic, and TV are cool; typography is hot.
- [[Extensions of Man (McLuhan)]] — the print/alphabet as the visual extension; electricity as the non-visual extension that shifts the base of culture and science.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

## Major Tensions

- [[Hot vs Cool Media]] — the cool woodcut/comic/TV (low data, high participation) vs the hot typography/print, with the counter-intuitive corollary that typography involves the reader *less* than manuscript.
- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] — the print's "segmenting and fragmenting [of] an integral action" and visual "blueprinting and precision" as "an explosive force that fragments the world of power and knowledge" vs the integral spoken word and "involvement of the whole man."
- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the print's repeatable, fragmenting mechanical principle ("since the Gutenberg technology") vs electricity's non-visual storage/retrieval and the relativity-age dissolution of uniform space.
- [[Numbness vs Awareness]] — the abstracted, detached visual sense numb to lived involvement: the map's measured miles vs the father's "cargo-laden, heat-weighted treks."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the print as the precise, repeatable *materialization* of the pictorial statement; the relativity-age dissolution of "rational" space (cross-book tension).

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **Repeatability as the deep grammar of the mechanical age.** Use the pictorial-print case (woodcut → movable type → "segmenting and fragmenting an integral action") to ground an argument that mechanization is fundamentally about *precise indefinite reproduction*, not about any single machine — bridging to the assembly line, to Lippard's repeatable conceptual [[The Document (Conceptual Art)|document]], and to digital copy-culture.
- **The cool image vs the hot text, counter-intuitively read.** Deploy McLuhan's claim that the low-definition woodcut/comic/TV demand *more* participation than high-definition typography to complicate naive "richer = more immersive" assumptions about media; pair with the contemporary low-resolution image and the meme.
- **Media as storage and retrieval — "to store is to expedite."** Run the map/blueprint/alphabet line into Hayles's [[Informatics]] and the [[Information]] cluster: the visual store of information as the precondition of Western science, and the electric shift to "non-visual means of storing and retrieving information."
- **Abstract visual space vs lived tactile space.** Use Modupe's father ("Maps are liars... The truth of a place is in the joy and the hurt that come from it") and the Melanesian carver to stage the material/immaterial and numbness/awareness tensions — the felt, embodied place effaced by the uniform measured grid.
- **The dissolution of "rational" space.** Trace the Bosch → Carroll → Einstein arc as McLuhan's claim that perspectival uniform space was a historical episode, reopened by relativity into discontinuous space-time — a hinge between [[Mechanical vs Electric]] and modern physics/art.
