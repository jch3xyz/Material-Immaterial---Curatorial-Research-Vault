## Orientation

This is Lippard's own framing text for *Six Years*, and after the long retrospective essay "Escape Attempts" it is the second-richest source in the book for durable concepts. It does three things. First, it names and immediately qualifies the title concept — the **dematerialization** of the art object — conceding that the term is "an inaccurate term" (a photograph or sheet of paper is "as much an object... as a ton of lead") yet keeping it "for lack of a better term," and redefining it operationally as "a deemphasis on material aspects (uniqueness, permanence, decorative" — the sentence cut off mid-word by an image/facsimile break in the source. Second, it declares the book's anti-narrative, anti-individualist *method*: "the form of the book intentionally reflects chaos rather than imposing order," foregrounding "timing, variety, fragmentation, and interrelationships above all."

Third — and at length — it reprints excerpts from a **December 1969 interview by Ursula Meyer**, deliberately left "not... revised according to what | think now," so that Lippard's 1969 positions "stand as things looked then" ("The Postface offers some contradictions"). These excerpts stage the book's sharpest arguments: that the art journals had not yet "entertained the idea that ideas can be art"; that non-object art is "a drastic solution to the problem of artists being bought and sold," yet the establishment "is probably going to be able to absorb conceptual art as another 'movement'" because it "depends so greatly on objects which can be bought and sold"; that dematerialized art lets artists "get... the power structure out of New York"; that the artist might work "as an interruptive device, a jolt, in present societal systems"; and — after a 1968 trip to Argentina — that "today everything, even art, exists in a political situation."

The Preface is therefore where the book's framing concepts and its central unresolved engine (resistance to the commodity vs. recuperation by the market) live; the year-files that follow are largely documentary. Its single most important sentence — the operational definition of dematerialization — is **truncated** by a facsimile break, and the entire chapter carries **heavy OCR damage** (every first-person "I" appears as "|", and several words are split across stray line breaks: "nadulterated" for "unadulterated," "nhardt's" for "Reinhardt's," "omena" for "phenomena"). All quotations below are copied character-for-character from the raw file, including those artifacts; downstream public-facing notes should mark `[sic]`/`[I]` or adopt a cleaned-transcription policy.

This Preface is the art-historical face of the vault's titular axis [[Material vs Immaterial]]: Lippard's concession that the "immaterial" artwork is never literally immaterial mirrors, from the art world, Hayles's insistence that information "must always be instantiated in a medium."

## Key Arguments

- Non-object art is a deliberate resistance to the commodity form — "a drastic solution to the problem of artists being bought and sold." Object art "lends itself more easily to capitalist marketing devices"; buyers of non-objects become "patrons rather than collectors," which makes the work "inapplicable to museums, because museums are basically acquisitive." (`Directly stated`)
- That resistance is likely to fail: the market recuperates conceptual art "as another 'movement'" because "the art establishment depends so greatly on objects which can be bought and sold." This is the book's title-page promise, here stated plainly. (`Directly stated`)
- Ideas can be art — conceptual art returns art to "content" and escapes "the Greenbergian standard of 'advance'." (`Directly stated`)
- "Everything, even art, exists in a political situation" — not that art must look political, but that the conditions of making, siting, and circulating it are political; biographically triggered by Argentina, fall 1968. (`Directly stated`)
- Method: the book's form "intentionally reflects chaos rather than imposing order"; "Fragmentation is more like direct communication than the traditionally unified approach." (`Directly stated`)

## Important Definitions

- **Dematerialization** (Lippard's operational redefinition): "a process of dematerialization, or a deemphasis on material aspects (uniqueness, permanence, decorative" — **the defining sentence is truncated mid-word by an image/facsimile break** (the lost word is almost certainly "attractiveness"; the next captured paragraph resumes "attractive ever, did come about partly as a reaction against the industrialized geometry... of much minimal art"). Lippard explicitly concedes it is "an inaccurate term." (`Directly stated`, with truncation flagged)
- **The scope-limit / phenomenological framing**: "This is not a book about a// dematerialized art and the point | want to make is phenomenological rather than historical." (`Directly stated`)
- **Ephemeralization** (Buckminster Fuller, quoted by Lippard): "the design science strategy of doing even more with even less per unit of energy, space, and time." (`Directly stated`)

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The artist as "interruptive device, a jolt, in present societal systems" — art figured as an electrical/mechanical interruption (Lippard cites John Latham and the APG group, London).
- "The devouring speed syndrome" — the accelerated condition art is "made in," which must be "not only... taken into consideration, but... utilized" (cross-link candidate to [[Acceleration]] — Han; speculative).
- The object as recurring antagonist — the hangable/garden-able saleable thing; "It's still an object" / "he's finally got past the object" as value judgments Lippard wants to dissolve.
- "Imposition of a foreign pattern or substance" — the art framework overlaid onto life; "anyone taking a photograph is geometricizing life."

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — dematerialization vs. the persistent objecthood of "even a piece of paper or a photograph"; the strongest cross-book link.
- Resistance vs. Recuperation (commodity) — non-object art as "a drastic solution" to being "bought and sold" vs. the prediction that the establishment will "absorb conceptual art as another 'movement'." The book's unresolved engine.
- Centralization vs. Decentralization — New York as indispensable "center because of the stimulus here" vs. dematerialized art as the means to "get the power structure out of New York."
- Order vs. Chaos (form) — the book's deliberate refusal to "impos[e] order."
