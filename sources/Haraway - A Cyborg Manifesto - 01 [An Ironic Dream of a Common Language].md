# Source Summary: An Ironic Dream of a Common Language for Women in the Integrated Circuit

## Orientation

This is the opening movement of Haraway's manifesto — the passage where the cyborg is introduced, defined, and installed as the governing political myth of the essay. Haraway frames the whole project methodologically as "an ironic political myth faithful to feminism, socialism, and materialism," written in the mode of *blasphemy* and held together by irony — "contradictions that do not resolve into larger wholes, even dialectically." From this ironic stance she delivers the canonical definition of [[The Cyborg]]: "a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction," and a string of programmatic theses — "The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics"; the cyborg as "a condensed image of both imagination and material reality"; the cyborg as a creature "completely without innocence," with "no origin story in the Western sense," that "would not recognize the Garden of Eden."

The section's analytic spine is the naming of three "crucial boundary breakdowns" that make the cyborg analysis possible — human/animal, organism (animal-human)/machine, and physical/nonphysical. It is through the third breakdown that Haraway develops her material/immaterial argument: late-twentieth-century machines are "quintessentially microelectronic devices," "everywhere and they are invisible," "made of sunshine" because "they are nothing but signals, electromagnetic waves." Against these fluid signal-machines she sets people, "both material and opaque." The coded logic of war — C3I, command-control-communication-intelligence — and the "grid of control" anchor the concrete politics here in the antinuclear/feminist formations Haraway reads as cyborg/affinity politics (the Greenham Women's Peace Camp, the Livermore Action Group, "Fission Impossible," Santa Rita Jail).

The section closes on the manifesto's signature *double vision*: a cyborg world is at once "about the final imposition of a grid of control on the planet" and "about lived social and bodily realities in which people are not afraid of their joint kinship with animals and machines." "The political struggle is to see from both perspectives at once." It ends with the founding definition of *affinity* — "related not by blood but by choice." As the originating primary text for the vault's cyborg cluster, this section grounds [[The Cyborg]], [[The Informatics of Domination]], and the Haraway side of the material/immaterial tension in Haraway's own words, where earlier they were anchored only in Hayles's second-hand citation.

## Key Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — the cybernetic organism; an ironic, blasphemous, postgender political myth fusing machine and organism, fact and fiction, declared "our ontology."
- [[The Breached Boundaries]] — the three "crucial boundary breakdowns" (human/animal, organism/machine, physical/nonphysical) that make the cyborg analysis possible.
- [[The Informatics of Domination]] — the coded command-control-communication network (C3I, the "grid of control") that supersedes organic, hierarchical dualisms; the source from which Hayles took the bare term "informatics."
- [[The Refusal of Innocence]] — the cyborg's rejection of origin myths, original unity, holism, and the dream of returning to a pre-fallen nature; "completely without innocence."
- [[Affinity Not Identity]] — political relation by choice rather than blood or nature; introduced here in its founding definition.
- [[Women in the Integrated Circuit]] — the figure named in the section's title; women's labor and lives within the microelectronic/military-industrial network ("making chips in Asia").
- [[Informatics]] — Hayles's later, adapted term; this section is the originating source Haraway provides for it.

## Key Arguments

- [[The cyborg is our ontology and it gives us our politics]] — because "we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism," the cyborg is not optional metaphor but our actual condition, and a politics follows from it.
- [[Take pleasure in the confusion of boundaries and responsibility for their construction]] — the manifesto's ethical thesis, positioned against the progressive tradition (Marcuse, Merchant) that "recalled us to an imagined organic body."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- The cyborg as a *material/immaterial* figure: read "machines made of sunshine… nothing but signals" against the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` thread (Hayles's disembodied information, Lippard's dematerialization, Kurzweil's pattern-over-substance) — Haraway's 1985 articulation precedes them all and insists machines are immaterial *and* "a matter of immense human pain in Detroit and Singapore."
- Surveillance and the "grid of control": pair C3I and "the final imposition of a grid of control on the planet" with Han's `Digital Panopticon` and the Dataism cluster — Haraway names the control function decades earlier and refuses to let critique collapse into either technophobia or innocent holism.
- Method as content: irony, blasphemy, and "double vision" as a curatorial/research stance — holding "incompatible things together because both or all are necessary and true" — usable as an essay's own organizing principle for material/immaterial research.
- The demotion of Foucauldian biopolitics: "Michel Foucault's biopolitics is a flaccid premonition of cyborg politics" as an early parallel to Han's later claim that Foucault "failed to make the turn to psychopolitics" — a convergence on the obsolescence of biopolitics from two very different traditions.
- Embodiment and labor in the integrated circuit: "the unnatural cyborg women making chips in Asia" as the gendered, racialized material substrate of the "immaterial" informatics economy.
