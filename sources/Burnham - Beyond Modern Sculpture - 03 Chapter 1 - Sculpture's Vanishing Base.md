# Source Summary: Chapter 1 — Sculpture's Vanishing Base

## Orientation

Chapter 1 opens Part One ("Sculpture as Object") by reading the entire transformation of modern sculpture through a single, seemingly minor element: the sculpture base — plinth, pedestal, base. Burnham's wager is that the steady decline, withering, and final disappearance of the base is "symptomatic" of a far larger transformation: the migration of sculpture away from the inert, gravity-bound, place-rooted, idealized object toward something mobile, environmental, and finally toward "a physical system including invisible forces." The base, he argues, is the sculptor's convention for rooting art to reality while keeping it apart; its function is at once biological (it reaffirms that mammals have a top and bottom, head and feet, a ventral and dorsal side, and gain mobility through contact with the earth) and psychic (it confers an aura of distance, dignity, presence, transcendence). As modern abstraction proceeds, the base "withers away" in what Burnham calls the "secularization" of sculpture: the transcendent "art object" is supplanted by objects accepted "for what they are, not for what they represent."

The chapter advances by a long evolutionary catalogue of examples rather than by argument alone: Degas's tableau bases (the tub itself as base), Rosso's fusion of base and figure, Rodin's craggy unified plinth for *The Burghers of Calais*, Boccioni's Futurist base-as-launching-pad, Duchamp's Readymade (a "Goedel's Theorem of aesthetics"), Brancusi's animist bases that *are* sculpture, the Constructivists' (Gabo, Pevsner) engineering-derived dematerialization of support, the postwar "withering" (Calder, Lippold, Bertoia), floor-bound and Minimal/"Object" sculpture (Judd, Morris) that is "solid, palpable, and real," ceiling-bound sculpture (Grosvenor), and finally "air-borne" sculpture — tensegrity (Snelson under Fuller), Takis's telemagnetic works, and Alberto Collie's electromagnetically levitated disk held in equilibrium by a feedback signal, where "the base has vanished altogether!"

The chapter's punchline is the book's master thesis in miniature: a sculpture freed from its base "becomes a different animal," whose reason for being "is no longer that it embodies formal qualities, but that it exists as a physical system including invisible forces. The duality between matter and energy enters a new phase." This is the OBJECT → SYSTEM transition — the vault's titular material/immaterial axis — stated at the level of the base, and it bridges forward to Lippard's dematerialization and to the later cybernetic, kinetic, and light chapters. The rhetorical posture is that of the systems-minded historian: patient, taxonomic, attentive to engineering detail, and quietly programmatic — every "minor detail of the craft" is read as a sign of a paradigm shift driven by science and technology.
