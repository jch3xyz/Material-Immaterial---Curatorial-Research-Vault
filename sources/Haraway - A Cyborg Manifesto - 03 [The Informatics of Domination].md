# Source Summary: The Informatics of Domination

**Book:** [[A Cyborg Manifesto]]
**Author:** [[Donna Haraway]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Haraway - A Cyborg Manifesto/03 [The Informatics of Domination]]]

## Orientation

This is the pivotal theoretical engine of *A Cyborg Manifesto*. Having introduced the cyborg as an ironic, blasphemous figure in the preceding sections, Haraway here names and diagrams the historical condition the cyborg inhabits: [[The Informatics of Domination]]. She frames the section as "an epistemological and political position" indebted to "socialist and feminist principles of design," and argues that worldwide social relations are undergoing a transformation "analogous in its novelty and scope to that created by industrial capitalism" — "a movement from an organic, industrial society to a polymorphous, information system—from all work to all play, a deadly game." She renders this as a two-column chart of paired transitions "from the comfortable old hierarchical dominations to the scary new networks" (Organics of Domination → Informatics of Domination): representation → simulation, organism → biotic component, depth/integrity → surface/boundary, reproduction → replication, white capitalist patriarchy → informatics of domination.

The section's central thesis is that communications sciences and modern biologies are constructed by a common move — [[The World as a Problem of Coding]] — a search for a common language in which "all resistance to instrumental control disappears and all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange." In communications, this is condensed in the military emblem [[C3I]] (command-control-communication-intelligence); in biology, the organism becomes a "biotic component," an "information-processing device," and "[b]iology here is a kind of cryptography." The material substrate of the whole regime is microelectronics — "the technical basis of simulacra—that is, of copies without originals." Through Rachel Grossman, Haraway introduces the figure of [[Women in the Integrated Circuit]] to name women's situation in this high-tech world system.

Two lines are load-bearing for the vault. First, the boundary-collapse thesis: "the difference between machine and organism is thoroughly blurred; mind, body, and tool are on very intimate terms," and "[t]he boundary-maintaining images of base and superstructure, public and private, or material and ideal never seemed more feeble" — a direct entanglement of [[Material vs Immaterial]]. Second, the explicit displacement of Foucault: "The cyborg is not subject to Foucault's biopolitics; the cyborg simulates politics, a much more potent field of operations." The section also stages the refusal of any return to origin or nature ("It's not just that 'god' is dead; so is the 'goddess'"), and closes by insisting this is not technological determinism but "the social relations of science and technology," and that these rearrangements "can make socialist-feminism more relevant to effective progressive politics."

## Key Concepts

- [[The Informatics of Domination]] — Haraway's coinage: the new system of world order that replaces the "comfortable old hierarchical dominations" (white capitalist patriarchy, organic dualisms) with "scary new networks" of coded command-control-communication; the shift from an organic/industrial society to a "polymorphous, information system." The primary source from which Hayles later borrowed "informatics."
- [[The World as a Problem of Coding]] — the "common move" by which both communications sciences and modern biologies operate: a search for a common language that dissolves all resistance to instrumental control and submits all heterogeneity to "disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange."
- [[Women in the Integrated Circuit]] — Haraway's adopted name (from Rachel Grossman, 1980) for the situation of women restructured through the social relations of science and technology; in the chart, "family/market/factory → women in the integrated circuit." (Primary anchor is the dedicated chapter; this section coins/names it.)
- [[The Breached Boundaries]] — the section restates and extends the manifesto's boundary breakdowns: the dichotomies "mind and body, animal and human, organism and machine, public and private, nature and culture, men and women, primitive and civilized are all in question ideologically," "cannibalized" or "techno-digested."
- [[Informatics]] — the broader relational field (Hayles); this section is the documentary source for Haraway's narrower, political-historical "informatics of domination" that Hayles borrowed.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the figure whose *world* this section supplies; here the cyborg is "a kind of disassembled and reassembled, postmodern collective and personal self," exempt from biopolitics and operating by simulation.
- [[The Refusal of Innocence]] — the refusal of return to nature, origin, or the goddess ("It's not just that 'god' is dead; so is the 'goddess'") as the rejected naturalistic ground.

## Key Arguments

- [[The world is being translated into a problem of coding]] — both communications sciences and modern biologies share one structural operation that produces "unhindered instrumental power (called effective communication)," condensed in C3I and in the organism-as-biotic-component.
- A periodizing claim (folded into [[The Informatics of Domination]]): the shift to the informatics of domination is a new "system of world order" "analogous in its novelty and scope to that created by industrial capitalism," diagrammed as the two-column chart and underwritten by the "mundane, largely economic reality" of microelectronics.
- A pointed displacement of Foucault (carried by [[Donna Haraway and Michel Foucault]]): where biopolitics governs the integrity of natural bodies, the informatics of domination operates on "boundary conditions and interfaces, on rates of flow across boundaries—and not on the integrity of natural objects," so "the cyborg simulates politics."
- A guarding qualification (folded into [[The Informatics of Domination]]): this is "a historical system depending on structured relations among people," not technology as autonomous cause, and one that can renew socialist-feminism.

## Important Definitions

- [[The Informatics of Domination (Haraway)]] — the historic shift from organic, hierarchical dualisms (the "comfortable old hierarchical dominations" of white capitalist patriarchy) to coded, networked command-control-communication systems; "a polymorphous, information system"; the world reconstrued as a problem of coding.
- [[Cyborg (Haraway)]] — the manifesto's central definition (anchored in the opening chapter); this section supplies the cyborg's world and its biopolitics/coding context rather than restating the defining sentence.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[C3I]] — command-control-communication-intelligence; the U.S. military's "symbol for its operations theory," taken as the condensed emblem of the entire coded-control regime.
- [[I Would Rather Be a Cyborg Than a Goddess]] — the refused goddess; this section provides the philosophical refusal of the goddess as a possible return ("so is the 'goddess'"). (Slogan anchored in a sibling chapter; secondary support here.)

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the section's strongest cross-vault contribution: the regime is "[s]imultaneously material and ideological"; base/superstructure and material/ideal "never seemed more feeble"; microelectronics as the material substrate of simulacra.
- [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] — Haraway joins Han in arguing that something supersedes Foucault's biopolitics; here it is the cyborg that "simulates politics," power over boundary conditions and rates of flow rather than the integrity of natural bodies.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A genealogy of the term "informatics" running Haraway → Hayles: this section is the documentary origin of the "informatics of domination" that Hayles borrowed and generalized; useful for an essay on how a militarized command-control metaphor became a general theory of the body as data.
- The Foucault-supersession cluster: pairing Haraway's "the cyborg simulates politics" with Han's psychopolitics and Deleuze's society of control to map what successive theorists claim displaces biopolitics — control by boundary conditions, rates of flow, and simulation rather than the integrity of natural bodies.
- The material/immaterial collapse as a curatorial frame: "the difference between machine and organism is thoroughly blurred" and microelectronics as "the technical basis of simulacra—that is, of copies without originals" — a strong anchor for work on copies without originals, dematerialization, and the body recoded as information.
- "Biology as cryptography": the living body recast as cipher and research as intelligence activity, read alongside the AIDS-as-boundary-confusion passage, for an essay on surveillance, the immune self/non-self distinction, and the inscription of pathology onto the body.
