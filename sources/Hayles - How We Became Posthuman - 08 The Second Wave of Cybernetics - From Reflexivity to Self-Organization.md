## Orientation

This is Chapter 6 of *How We Became Posthuman*, and it is the hinge of Hayles's "second wave" of [[Cybernetics]] (1960–1985), the wave defined by the move from reflexivity to self-organization. Where the Macy-era first wave wrestled unsuccessfully with how to include the observer in the system, this chapter follows Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela as they make the observer constitutive: "the observer, like the frog, does not so much discern preexisting systems as create them through the very act of observation." Hayles opens with the famous frog's-eye experiment ("What the Frog's Eye Tells the Frog's Brain") to show that perception constructs rather than copies the world, then traces how Maturana built an entire epistemology — autopoiesis — on that frog-sized hole in realism. Heinz von Foerster serves as the transitional figure carrying reflexivity over from the first wave, and G. Spencer-Brown's calculus of distinction supplies the founding "operation of distinction" from which the whole apparatus unfolds.

Rhetorically, the chapter is doing double work: it is both an exposition and a critique. Hayles is sympathetic to autopoiesis because it relocates the three stories she has been tracking — the reification of [[Information]], the construction of the cyborg, and the [[Posthumanism|transformation of the human into the posthuman]]. Most importantly for the vault's through-line of [[Embodiment vs Information]], autopoietic theory "draws attention to the fact that 'information,' so defined, is an abstraction that has no basis in the physically embodied processes constituting all living entities." Against the first wave's bodiless information, Maturana insists information without a body does not exist except as an inference an observer draws — a decisive swerve back toward [[Embodiment]] and the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis.

But Hayles also diagnoses autopoiesis's "Achilles' heel": its emphasis on organizational closure makes it bad at accounting for transformation, language, and evolution. The same closure that gives the theory epistemological muscle traps it in circularity. The chapter culminates in the divergence between Maturana — who clung to closure, claimed sole paternity of the theory, and "vaporizes contingency" by circling it — and Varela, who moved toward [[Embodiment]], enaction, and self-organization, ultimately toward a Buddhist deconstruction of the self in *The Embodied Mind*. A note of analytic sensitivity runs through one passage: Hayles invokes Simon Baron-Cohen's *Mindblindness* to argue that autopoietic closure "ironically describes autistic individuals more accurately than it does normally responsive people" — a rhetorical analogy used to expose the theory's neglect of the active, transformative force of language, not a clinical claim about autism.

## Key Concepts

- [[Autopoiesis]] — the circular organization that produces and maintains the very components that produce it; coextensive with life itself.
- [[The Constitutive Observer]] — the observer who creates rather than discerns systems; "Everything said is said by an observer."
- [[Organizational Closure]] — the system closes on itself, by itself; the foundational ground that replaces self-possession for the autonomous subject.
- [[Structural Coupling]] — an organism's ongoing interaction with its environment (and with other systems) required for it to keep living, distinct from observer-inferred causality.
- [[Allopoiesis vs Autopoiesis]] — autopoietic unities aim only to produce their own organization; allopoietic ones serve a goal other than their own production (the car turns the drive shaft for me).
- [[Enaction]] — Varela's later concept: cognition and perception constituted through "perceptually guided actions," open-ended and embodied rather than closed and circular.
- [[Reflexivity]] — the observer doubling back on its own representations; carried from the Macy Conferences and made operational here.
- [[Self-Organization]] — the fast, flexible, emergent dynamics Varela and Zeleny add to jog autopoiesis out of "relentless repetitive circularity."
- [[Cybernetics]] — the second wave (1960–1985) as the period of reflexivity and self-organization.
- [[Embodiment]] — the concreteness and specificity of biological processes that autopoiesis foregrounds and that Varela's enaction radicalizes.
- [[Information]] — recast as an observer's inference with no basis in embodied process.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — denied intrinsic reality: "there are no messages circulating in feedback loops, nor are there even any genetic codes."
- [[Systems Theory]] — the broader frame within which autopoiesis is read as a theory of living organization, including its over-extension to social systems.

## Key Arguments

- [[Information without a body does not exist]] — autopoiesis insists "information," disembodied, is an abstraction with no basis in physically embodied living processes. *(Directly stated)*
- [[Everything said is said by an observer]] — Maturana's founding maxim; time, causality, coding, teleology, and information are observer inferences, not intrinsic to autopoietic processes. *(Directly stated)*
- [[Organizational closure replaces self-possession as the ground of the subject]] — the liberal subject's autonomy is re-founded on closure and recursivity rather than C. B. Macpherson's possessive individualism. *(Directly stated)*
- [[Autopoietic closure cannot account for transformation]] — the closure that gives the theory its strength is its Achilles' heel, failing to handle language, evolution, and dynamic non-circular change. *(Directly stated)*
- [[Maturana and Varela diverge on closure]] — Maturana repeats the original formulation and claims sole authorship; Varela moves toward embodiment, enaction, and self-organization. *(Directly stated)*

## Important Definitions

- [[Autopoiesis (Maturana and Varela)]] — "The living organization is a circular organization which secures the production or maintenance of the components that specify it in such a manner that the product of their functioning is the very same organization that produces them."
- [[Structure vs Organization (Maturana)]] — organization is the web of relations a unity must have to be its kind (conserved until death); structure is the particular instantiation at a moment (changes over a lifetime).
- [[Allopoietic (Maturana)]] — a unity whose goal is something other than producing its own organization.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Frog's Eye]] — the perceptual system that constructs rather than copies reality; the experiment that "blew a frog-sized hole in realist epistemology."
- [[Men in Bowler Hats]] — von Foerster/Pask's infinite regress of an observer observing himself observing another.
- [[The Tar Baby of Self-Reflexive Language]] — the trap of trying to articulate the new in a language whose meanings settled along old realist lines.
- [[The Tree of Knowledge as Circular Engine]] — the book whose circular form "vaporizes contingency by continuously circulating it within the space of its interlocking assumptions."

## Major Tensions

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — autopoiesis re-embodies information against the first wave's disembodiment.
- [[Closure vs Transformation]] — circular self-production versus the open-ended, evolving, language-shaped organism.
- [[Relativism vs Absolutism (Autopoiesis)]] — does the observer's distinction create the autopoietic unity, or does the unity constitute itself independent of any observer? Maturana steers between this Scylla and Charybdis.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the operational (physical processes) versus the symbolic/systems-theoretic explanation Varela wants to keep "in full view."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A pivot essay on [[Embodiment vs Information]]: use autopoiesis as the moment cybernetics turns against bodiless information ("information without a body does not exist other than as an inference drawn by an observer"), bridging the first-wave Macy material and the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis.
- An essay on the constitutive observer and curatorial framing: "Everything said is said by an observer" and "We do not see what we do not see, and what we do not see does not exist" as a theory of how observation/curation brings worlds into being — and its slide toward solipsism.
- An essay on closure versus transformation: pairing Maturana's circular conservatism with Varela's enaction to stage [[Closure vs Transformation]] as a methodological choice for systems thinking.
- A genealogy of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman subject]]: how organizational closure displaces possessive individualism and demotes consciousness to an epiphenomenon ("the liberal subject ... is literally losing its mind as the seat of identity").
- A careful methodological essay on analogy and sensitivity: Hayles's use of Baron-Cohen's "mindblindness" as a critique of a theory (not of persons), modeling how to deploy a clinical figure rhetorically without pathologizing.
