## Orientation

Chapter 4 of *How We Became Posthuman* reads [[Norbert Wiener]], "the father of cybernetics," as a threshold figure caught between two incompatible commitments: a liberal humanist faith in the "coherent, rational self" with its "right to autonomy and freedom," and a [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] paradigm whose analogical machinery dissolves exactly the boundaries that liberal selfhood depends on. Hayles opens with Gregory Bateson's koan — is a blind man's cane part of the man? — to stage the chapter's central claim: that from the cybernetic viewpoint the human subject is "constituted by flows of information" rather than bounded by epidermal surfaces, so that "body boundaries are up for grabs." Wiener wants the cybernetic machine to *extend* the autonomous self "into the realm of the machine," yet keeps glimpsing himself in "the mirror of the cyborg" and making "a horrified withdrawal." The chapter is structured as a diagnosis of that ambivalence — its assumptions, its worldview, and the points at which it becomes unbearable.

Hayles traces the ambivalence to a deep substrate: Wiener's probabilistic cosmology. Following Willard Gibbs (whom Wiener ranks above Einstein, Heisenberg, and Planck as author of "the first great revolution of twentieth century physics"), Wiener takes the world to be irreducibly probabilistic — one world realized "from among a galaxy of possible worlds" — and then, in Hayles's pivotal formulation, he "took Gibbs's idea and substituted *word* for *world*," so that one message emerges from a cacophony of possible messages just as one world emerges from a field of possibilities. Because reality is probabilistic, [[Feedback Loops|control via negative feedback]] is necessary, decentralized, and flexible; because communication is relational, *analogy* becomes Wiener's master trope — "a universal exchange system that allows data to move across boundaries," the "lingua franca" that splices animal, human, and machine into one category. Holding [[Norbert Wiener|Wiener]]'s biography (his physical clumsiness, his abandoned biology PhD, his absence from the lab), Hayles argues that this analogical reflex is precisely what lets him "erase from view the very real differences in embodied materiality" — the chapter's core critique and a load-bearing instance of [[Embodiment vs Information]].

The cosmological stakes are entropic. Wiener inherits a "dematerialized materialism" (Mark Seltzer) in which entropy, generalized by Boltzmann into a probability function and linked to information through Maxwell's Demon, becomes the opposite of information — so that life is "an island of negentropy amid a sea of disorder," and "we are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves." On this stage Wiener scripts a moral drama: good, flexible cybernetic machines stand "shoulder to shoulder" with humans against entropy and noise, while rigid, engulfing machines that reduce humans to "cogs and levers and rods" are figured as evil. The chapter then turns to Wiener's "Argument for Celibacy" — his refusal, in *Cybernetics*, to fuse the physical and social sciences, dramatized through an erotically charged fantasy of an "intelligent savage" in the woods — to show that the withdrawal is at bottom an anxious defense of the bounded liberal subject against penetration. Hayles closes historically: the alliance between liberal humanism and self-regulation, "forged in the eighteenth century" (per Otto Mayr) and reinforced by Beniger's "control revolution," was "already stretched thin" by the 1960s "and by the 1980s, it was largely broken." Wiener could not contain cybernetics within liberal assumptions; the cyborg propagates "by all manner of promiscuous couplings" beyond his control.

## Key Concepts

- [[Cybernetics]] — control, communication, and information theorized as one integrated system that reconceives boundaries as constructed.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the subjectivity that "would soon displace the liberal humanist self" once body boundaries become negotiable.
- [[Embodiment]] — the "plenitude" of the flesh that Wiener's abstraction systematically elides; the chapter's normative counterweight.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the body reconstrued "as an informational system," a "permeable membrane through which hormonal information flows."
- [[Feedback Loops|Negative Feedback]] — the mechanism by which goal-seeking systems control randomness in a probabilistic universe; Wiener's redefinition of "teleology."
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-regulating, self-owning self whose values "deeply inform Wiener's thinking."
- [[Self-Regulation]] — the eighteenth-century notion (Invisible Hand, enlightened self-interest) that links liberal selfhood to automatic machinery, per Otto Mayr.
- [[Analogy as Universal Exchange System]] — Wiener's master trope; constitutes both terms through their relation rather than mapping pre-existing essences.
- [[Probabilistic Universe]] — the Gibbsian cosmos "like a baggy pair of pants," where initial conditions are never exactly knowable.
- [[Entropy]] — generalized into a probability function (Boltzmann) and tied to randomness, dissipation, and death.
- [[Negentropy]] — information as negative entropy (Brillouin); life as a local enclave running "upstream against the entropic tide."
- [[Pattern vs Presence|Pattern]] — what perception, mathematics, and information all preserve "apart from content" across interfaces.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the boundary-violating fusion (Haraway) whose "mirror" Wiener builds and then flees.
- [[Black Box]] — behaviorist engineering that produces the human-machine equivalence by bracketing internal structure.

- [[Behaviorist black-box analysis manufactures the human-machine equivalence]] — the equation "works because it is seen from a position formulated precisely so that it will work." *Directly stated.*

## Important Definitions

- [[Negative Feedback (Wiener)]] — "teleological behavior means simply 'behavior controlled by negative feedback'"; purpose as goal-directed action.
- [[Negentropy (Brillouin)]] — information considered as negative entropy; the more information, the less entropy.
- [[Augustinian vs Manichean Opponent]] — an opponent who plays honorably by fixed rules (nature, noise) versus one who manipulates (the chess player, chess-playing machines).
