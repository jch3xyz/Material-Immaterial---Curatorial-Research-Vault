## Orientation

1970 is the year dematerialized conceptual art went institutional. The annotated chronicle for this year is dominated not by studio objects but by exhibitions, magazines, and books that take the museum and the printed page themselves as the medium. Within a single span Lippard logs the Museum of Modern Art's *Information* (organized by Kynaston McShine), the Jewish Museum's *Software* (organized by [[Jack Burnham]]), the New York Cultural Center's *Conceptual Art and Conceptual Aspects* (organized by Donald Karshan), and [[Seth Siegelaub]]'s *July/August Exhibition* — a special issue of *Studio International* in which the magazine itself becomes the exhibition space, each of six critics handed an eight-page section to fill with artists rather than prose. The object has receded; what circulates instead is the document, the bulletin, the catalogue card, the telegram, the affidavit. This is the art-historical instance of [[Material vs Immaterial]] — what Lippard's book calls dematerialization.

The chronicle records, with unusual candor, the internal dissent over its own organizing term. The very year the curatorial apparatus absorbed "conceptual art," several of its practitioners refused the word "dematerialization." Douglas Huebler writes that "There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality"; Ian Burn and Mel Ramsden, working through a semiotic vocabulary, insist that "one can't loose material conditions by simplistic 'dematerialization'" and relocate the disappearance of the object to "fundamental procedural changes in the semiotic or conceptual sign-structure of the continuum itself." Language as an art form is pressed from several directions at once — Ian Wilson's "oral communication as artform," [[Joseph Kosuth]]'s "art as idea as idea," the Art-Language group's analytic philosophy of the art-proposition — while [[Sol LeWitt]]'s wall-drawing instructions (a draftsman executes lines the artist never touches) and Burnham's *Software* extend a [[Systems Theory]] aesthetic in which the work is a set of rules, a program, or an information flow.

Running underneath is the art-vs-market thread. Kosuth diagnoses the gravitational pull of tradition as economic: "Cash support demands 'goods.'" Jan Dibbets reframes the territory-sculpture so that its mode of existence is documentary — "only through its documentation can the viewer reconstruct its form in his mind." And Lippard closes the year with a recursive performance of the whole problem: her contribution to David Lamelas's *Publication* (which is also his exhibition) is a statement she has signed over, verbatim and in full, to Douglas Huebler in exchange for a piece of his — a single text that simultaneously enacts authorship, exchange, curation, and the collapse of the distinction between art and writing about art.

This is a chronicle entry, not an essay: its rhetorical posture is documentary accumulation. Lippard quotes artists, curators, and critics at length and lets the contradictions stand. The note below pulls the emblematic statements forward; most figures and exhibitions are forward-stubs to be realized when their own sources enter the vault.

## Key Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the year's master tension; dematerialization is its art-historical instance, contested from within by Huebler, Burn, and Ramsden
- [[Information]] — both the MoMA exhibition title and the operative idea: art as transmissible information rather than object
- [[Systems Theory]] — LeWitt's instruction-and-draftsman wall drawings and Burnham's *Software* treat the artwork as a program or rule-set
