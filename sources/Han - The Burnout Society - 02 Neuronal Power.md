# Source Summary: Neuronal Power

**Book:** [[The Burnout Society]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/02 Neuronal Power]]

## Orientation

"Neuronal Power" is the opening chapter of *The Burnout Society* and lays the diagnostic foundation for the whole book. Its method is periodization: "Every age has its signature afflictions," and the incipient twenty-first century is determined "neither by bacteria nor by viruses, but by neurons." Han distinguishes two pathological paradigms. The first is the **immunological / viral age** of the last century, structured by negativity — the defence reaction against the foreign Other (friend/foe, inside/outside, self/other, the Cold War as "an altogether military dispositive"). The second is the emergent **neuronal age**, structured by excess positivity, whose illnesses (depression, ADHD, BPD, burnout) "are not infections, but infarctions" and arise not from the intrusion of the foreign but from "too much of the Same."

Rhetorically the chapter is a sustained polemic against two named theorists who, in Han's reading, wrongly persist in the immunological paradigm. He quotes Roberto Esposito's programme of *immunitas* and calls it "a false assumption," arguing that "Esposito's immunological analysis does not address contemporary problems, but only objects from the past." He then mounts an immanent critique of Jean Baudrillard: Han credits Baudrillard with intuiting the violence of the Same ("He who lives by the Same shall die by the Same"; the "obesity of all current systems") but charges that Baudrillard fatally describes that violence "in immunological terms," so his theory is "riddled with leaps of argument and vague definitions."

Han's positive thesis is that the new violence is **neuronal violence** — "a terror of immanence," "systemic" rather than system-foreign, which "does not deprive, it saturates; it does not exclude, it exhausts." This chapter does not yet develop the achievement-society / auto-exploitation apparatus the book is famous for; that material belongs to later chapters. Here Han supplies only the immunological-to-neuronal periodization, closing on the formula that orients everything to come: "Burnout syndrome occurs when the ego overheats, which follows from too much of the Same."

## Key Concepts

- [[Immunological Age]] — the superseded twentieth-century paradigm of negativity: friend/foe, inside/outside, the Cold War dispositive, the dialectic of negativity, inoculation as negation of negation. This chapter is its fullest primary-source exposition.
- [[Neuronal Violence]] — Han's name for the new, system-immanent "violence of positivity," a "terror of immanence" that saturates and exhausts rather than depriving or excluding; "no longer 'viral.'"
- [[Positivity]] — the master register of the new age: "an excess of positivity," "the massification of the positive"; harm that comes "not just from the Other or the foreign, but also from the Same."
- [[Negativity]] — structurally tied to immunity and the Other; "the dialectic of negativity is the fundamental trait of immunity." Its disappearance defines the present: "we live in a time that is poor in negativity."
- [[Hell of the Same]] — the pathological register of the Same once Otherness dissolves: postmodern difference "represents the Same," and burnout "follows from too much of the Same."

## Key Arguments

- [[The pathologies of the present are infarctions not infections]] — depression, ADHD, BPD, and burnout derive from excess positivity, not immunological negativity; they "are not infections, but infarctions."

## Important Definitions

- Infarction vs infection — the load-bearing contrast: an infection is the intrusion of the foreign (immunological/negative), an infarction an internal overload (neuronal/positive). Captured in [[The pathologies of the present are infarctions not infections]].
- Immunological vs nonimmunological rejection — immunological rejection "reacts to the negativity of the Other," "independent of the quantum"; nonimmunological rejection "concerns the too-much-of-the-Same, surplus positivity," where "negativity plays no role." See [[Neuronal Violence]].
- The violence of positivity — defined by what it does not do: it "does not deprive, it saturates; it does not exclude, it exhausts." See [[Neuronal Violence]].

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- Signature afflictions of the age (bacterial → viral → neuronal) — the organizing conceit that each epoch's emblematic disease diagnoses its social form.
- Infection vs infarction — bodily-pathological figure for the negativity/positivity distinction.
- Obesity / fat — Baudrillard's "obesity of all current systems"; "Fat does not provoke an immune reaction." A metaphor of excess positivity that summons no defence.
- The ego overheating — burnout as thermal overload: "Burnout syndrome occurs when the ego overheats."
- The genealogy of hostility (wolf → rat → insect → virus) — Baudrillard's succession of enemy-forms, recounted to show it stays immunological.
- Medusa — "the immunological Other in its extreme form," "radical alterity that one cannot behold without perishing," set against the perception-eluding terror of immanence.
- The tourist / the exotic — the deactivation of the foreign into "a formula of consumption."
- The Cold War / the military dispositive — emblem of the attack/defence logic of negativity.
- The wall / fence / border — the "particular topology" of the immunological world: "borders, transitions, thresholds, fences, ditches, and walls."
- Inoculation / the vaccine — "A small amount of self-inflicted harm [Gewalt] protects one from a much larger danger"; the homeopathic emblem of the dialectic of negativity.
