# Source Summary: Women in the Integrated Circuit

**Book:** [[A Cyborg Manifesto]]
**Author:** [[Donna Haraway]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Haraway - A Cyborg Manifesto/05 [Women in the Integrated Circuit]]]

## Orientation

This is the fifth and most concretely socio-economic section of *A Cyborg Manifesto*. Haraway sets out to "summarize the picture of women's historical locations in advanced industrial societies, as these positions have been restructured partly through the social relations of science and technology." Building on the prior sections that introduce the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]] and the [[The Homework Economy|homework economy]], she discards the old public/private ideology — factory/home, market/home, personal/political — as "now a totally misleading ideology" and replaces it with "a network ideological image, suggesting the profusion of spaces and identities and the permeability of boundaries in the personal body and in the body politic." Networking, she warns, is "both a feminist practice and a multinational corporate strategy — weaving is for oppositional cyborgs."

The structural core of the chapter is a granular catalogue of women's dispersed "place" across seven idealized loci — Home, Market, Paid Workplace, State, School, Clinic-Hospital, and Church — each "logically and practically implied in every other locus, perhaps analogous to a holographic photograph." This is [[Women in the Integrated Circuit]] in its most fully worked-out form: a taxonomy of how computerized, militarized, multinational capitalism reorganizes women's labor, consumption, citizenship, education, bodies, and faith. The catalogue is saturated with the dissolution of bounded material life into immaterial, surveilled, abstracted flows — "surveillance systems through electronic funds transfer," "citizenship by telematics," "intensified market abstraction (commodification) of experience," "intensified machine–body relations" — which is this chapter's strongest contribution to the cross-book [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension. The political upshot is that "there is no 'place' for women in these networks, only geometries of difference and contradiction crucial to women's cyborg identities"; the task is "to survive in the diaspora" and to read "these webs of power and social life" toward "new couplings, new coalitions," explicitly refusing any reading "from a standpoint of 'identification,' of a unitary self."

The closing pages develop the manifesto's signature [[Domination vs Possibility|double vision]]: an "ambivalence toward the disrupted unities mediated by high-tech culture" that refuses to sort consciousness into "clear-sighted critique" versus "manipulated false consciousness," and a critique of orthodox Marxism, which "see[s] domination best" but reads complicity only as "false consciousness." The chapter ends on its anti-totalizing epistemology — "We do not need a totality in order to work well"; the dream of "a common language ... of perfectly faithful naming of experience, is a totalizing and imperialist one" — and on its most-quoted line: "Perhaps, ironically, we can learn from our fusions with animals and machines how not to be Man, the embodiment of Western logos."

Note: this chapter does NOT contain the cyborg's definition, the three breached boundaries, "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess," or the "Foucault's biopolitics is a flaccid premonition" line — those live in other sections. This section is the primary anchor for [[Women in the Integrated Circuit]] and a strong supporting source for [[Domination vs Possibility]], [[Material vs Immaterial]], and the affinity-not-identity cluster.

## Key Concepts

- [[Women in the Integrated Circuit]] — Haraway's title figure for women's dispersed, contradictory positions within computerized, militarized, multinational capitalism; mapped here across seven mutually-implied social loci with "no 'place' for women ... only geometries of difference and contradiction."
- [[The Informatics of Domination]] — used (not defined) here as the frame for the catalogue, and given its most damning summary characterization: "a massive intensification of insecurity and cultural impoverishment."
- [[The Homework Economy]] — operationalized inside the Home and Paid Workplace loci: the "feminization" of work — casualized, deskilled, home-based — regardless of the worker's sex ("most labor 'marginal' or 'feminized.'").
- [[Fractured Identities]] — the chapter's "the issue is dispersion," "no ... unitary self," and "new couplings, new coalitions" extend the manifesto's anti-essentialist politics of affinity rather than identity.
- [[Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity]] — the catalogue tracks distinct positions across "white women and people of color" (occupational mobility, scientific literacy, civil-service jobs), the differentiated subjects of the cyborg coalition.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the dispersed positions across the seven loci are "women's cyborg identities"; "weaving is for oppositional cyborgs."

## Key Arguments

- The public/private dichotomy is now "a totally misleading ideology" and must be replaced by "a network ideological image" of permeable boundaries — grounded in the seven-locus catalogue in which each domain bleeds into every other.
- There is no unitary standpoint from which to read women's situation; because "the issue is dispersion," identity-based reading fails and the political task is to "survive in the diaspora" and forge "new couplings, new coalitions" — see [[Fractured Identities]].
- High-tech culture demands [[Domination vs Possibility|double vision]], not the sorting of consciousness into true critique versus "manipulated false consciousness"; what is nostalgically mourned is "often virulent forms of oppression, nostalgically naturalized."
- Feminist politics needs no totality: "We do not need a totality in order to work well," and the dream of "a common language" or a dialectical resolution of contradiction "is a totalizing and imperialist one."

## Important Definitions

- [[The Informatics of Domination (Haraway)]] — characterized in this chapter as "a massive intensification of insecurity and cultural impoverishment, with common failure of subsistence networks for the most vulnerable" (primary definition anchored in section 03; cited here).

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The holographic photograph — each locus "logically and practically implied in every other locus" (the whole encoded in every part); the formal counterpart to the refusal of a "unitary self."
- Weaving / the web / networking — "weaving is for oppositional cyborgs"; both "a feminist practice and a multinational corporate strategy."
- The diaspora — politics as survival in dispersion rather than return to a homeland or unitary self.
- "Man, the embodiment of Western logos" — the unified rational masculine Subject the cyborg learns NOT to be, via "fusions with animals and machines."
- "Automated fetish gods" — the Church locus's image of the union of "electronic capital and automated fetish gods" solemnized by electronic "super-saver" preachers.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the integrated circuit dissolves the bounded organic body and home into immaterial, abstracted, surveilled, telematic flows: telematic citizenship, electronic funds transfer, "intensified market abstraction (commodification) of experience," "intensified machine–body relations."
- [[Domination vs Possibility]] — the integrated circuit is "a massive intensification of insecurity and cultural impoverishment" AND the site of "emerging pleasures, experiences, and powers"; the chapter refuses to resolve the two.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A surveillance-studies genealogy: Haraway's 1985 "citizenship by telematics" and "surveillance systems through electronic funds transfer" as a gendered, raced precursor to Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and Harari's [[Dataism]] — control read through *distribution* across women's lives rather than as a generic apparatus.
- A [[Material vs Immaterial]] essay anchor: the catalogue as a concrete inventory of how the bounded material body/home is "dispersed and interfaced" into abstracted networked flows, set against Hayles's disembodiment-of-information and Lippard's dematerialization.
- On method: [[Domination vs Possibility|double vision]] as a curatorial / critical stance — holding "emerging pleasures, experiences, and powers" together with "a massive intensification of insecurity," refusing both technophobia and uncritical embrace.
- The "informatics" lineage ([[N. Katherine Hayles and Donna Haraway]], [[Informatics]]): this chapter supplies the verbatim "informatics of domination," the source from which Hayles borrows "informatics."
- An anti-totalizing epistemology piece: "We do not need a totality in order to work well" and the critique of "a common language" as resources for partiality-as-strength against system-completeness.
