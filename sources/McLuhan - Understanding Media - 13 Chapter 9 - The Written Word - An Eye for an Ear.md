# Source Summary: Chapter 9 — The Written Word: An Eye for an Ear

**Book:** [[Understanding Media]]
**Author:** [[Marshall McLuhan]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/13 Chapter 9 - The Written Word - An Eye for an Ear]]

## Orientation

Chapter 9 of [[Understanding Media|*Understanding Media*]] is a Part II case-study of a single medium, the written word, and specifically of [[The Phonetic Alphabet]]. It is one of the most theoretically load-bearing chapters in the book because McLuhan treats the phonetic alphabet as the founding instance of the visual, fragmenting, detribalizing technology whose intensified industrial form will become [[Typographic Man]]. The chapter opens with Prince Modupe's West African encounter with print as "trapped words" and immediately contrasts that native eagerness with "the current anxieties of civilized man concerning the written word" — anxieties McLuhan attributes to the rise of "a new electric technology that threatens this ancient technology of literacy built on the phonetic alphabet."

The chapter's distinctive thesis is narrow and durable: among all writing systems, only the *phonetic* alphabet — in which "semantically meaningless letters are used to correspond to semantically [meaningful] sounds" — produces a "sharp division in experience, giving to its user an eye for an ear." This single technology, McLuhan argues, detribalizes its user, manufactures the separate "civilized" individual equal before a written code of law, and installs visual uniformity, continuity, and the lineal/sequential habit (mistaken for logic and causality) as the operating system of Western culture. The Cadmus myth — letters sown as dragon's teeth springing up armed men — capsulates this as the transfer of power from priestly to military classes, accomplished by the easy alphabet plus cheap, transportable papyrus.

The chapter reinforces the Part I apparatus. The alphabet is [[Media as Extensions of Man|an extension of man]] (specifically of the visual sense); its [[The Medium Is the Message|message]] is not its word-content but the imposed pattern of "visual uniformity and continuity"; it represses the other senses (a [[Sense Ratios|sense-ratio]] effect that does not occur in nonphonetic, ideogrammic cultures); and the [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System|electric age]] is described as threatening this ancient literacy by favoring "the inclusive and participational spoken word over the specialist written word" — a retribalization toward acoustic space and [[The Global Village|the global village]]. McLuhan is careful to bracket value judgments: "we are not here concerned with the question of values, but with the configurations of societies."

## Key Concepts

- [[The Phonetic Alphabet]] — the chapter's anchor: a unique technology that, by separating meaningless visual signs from meaningful sounds, detribalizes its user and fabricates the "civilized" visual individual ("an eye for an ear").
- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the alphabet as an intensification and extension of the visual function; electric media as an extension of the central nervous system.
- [[The Medium Is the Message]] — the alphabet's effect has "nothing to do with the content of the alphabetized words"; the message is the pattern of visual uniformity it imposes.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — phonetic literacy is a visual processing of culture that represses sound, touch, and taste; tribal cultures conversely repress the visual.
- [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]] — the master opposition: the resonant, all-inclusive, simultaneous auditory/tribal world vs the continuous, uniform, sequential visual/civilized world.
- [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] — a single generation of alphabetic literacy releases the individual from the tribal web; electric technology reverses the process.
- [[Typographic Man]] — the alphabet's lineal habit "became more intense with the uniformity and repeatability of the Gutenberg development."
- [[The Global Village]] — electric retribalization toward the inclusive spoken word, as "we move out of the Gutenberg era."
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — electric technology favors the participational spoken word over the specialist written word.

## Key Arguments

- [[Only the phonetic alphabet not writing in general created the detribalized civilized individual]] — the causal agent is the phonetic separation of sight from sound, not literacy as such; ideogrammic China retained tribal cohesion where alphabetic Africa and Gaul did not.
- [[The Western bias toward sequence as logic is a hidden effect of alphabetic technology]] — Hume showed sequence is "merely additive, not causative"; neither Hume nor Kant located the cause of the sequence-bias in the alphabet.
- [[Detachment the power to act without reacting is the chief gift of typography and an embarrassment in the electric age]] — phonetic culture lets man repress feeling during action; oral cultures "act and react at the same time."

## Important Definitions

- [[The Phonetic Alphabet (McLuhan)]] — "there is only one phonetic alphabet in which semantically meaningless letters are used to correspond to semantically [meaningful] sounds"; the technology of "civilized man," the separate individual equal before a written code of law.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[essays/Old Essays/An Eye for an Ear]] — the title figure: the phonetic alphabet trades the auditory for the visual sense.
- [[The Dragon's Teeth (Cadmus)]] — Cadmus sows the dragon's teeth and armed men spring up; letters as agents of "aggressive order and precision" (via Canetti, teeth as an "agent of power").

## Major Tensions

- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] — the cool, neutral, sequential, uniform visual/civilized eye vs the hyper-esthetic, all-inclusive, simultaneous auditory/tribal ear.
- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the ancient technology of phonetic literacy threatened by the new electric technology.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — "to act without reacting, without involvement" vs oral cultures that "act and react at the same time."
- [[Numbness vs Awareness]] — the "cool and neutral eye" and the unexamined, unfelt habits literacy installs.
- [[Hot vs Cool Media]] — implicit: the participational oral world vs the specialist alphabet (interpretive — the chapter never uses the hot/cool vocabulary).

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A media-archaeology of the document and the printed word for a material/immaterial essay: the phonetic alphabet as the founding "visual materialization" of speech, read alongside Hayles on inscription and Lippard on the conceptual-art document.
- An argument about detachment and embodiment: McLuhan's "act without reacting" as the numb, neutral, specialist Western stance — a counterpoint to participational, all-inclusive sensory engagement in installation and performance practice.
- A genealogy of lineal "logic" and sequence as a media effect (Hume/Kant via McLuhan), useful for critiquing the assembly-line, uniform-pricing, and database orders that organize contemporary systems and surveillance.
- The print-vs-electric / detribalization theme as a hinge between McLuhan and Han's later oppositions of narration and addition, and the erosion of narrativity under electric/digital media.
