## Orientation

This chapter is Harari's account of how *Homo sapiens* became, for all practical purposes, a god in relation to other animals — and what that domination reveals about humanity's coming projects. It opens with the blunt claim that "with regard to other animals, humans have long since become gods," gods who "have not been particularly just or merciful." Empirically it documents [[The Anthropocene]]: within 70,000 years one ape species became "the single most important agent of change in the global ecology," collapsing the planet's many independent ecosystems into a single human-managed unit, driving mass extinctions, and replacing wild animals with domesticated ones — to the point that "more than 90 per cent of the large animals of the world ... are either humans or domesticated animals." The instruction Harari draws from the comparison with asteroids and ice ages is that "Instead of fearing asteroids, we should fear ourselves."

The chapter's spine is a three-stage history of human–animal relations keyed to three revolutions and organized by a single question — who gets silenced. Hunter-gatherers were animists who "believed that there was no essential gap separating humans from other animals." The Agricultural Revolution "silenced animals and plants," inserting gods as mediators in what Harari calls "the agricultural deal." The Scientific Revolution then "silenced the gods too," leaving "a one-man show" in which humankind stands "alone on an empty stage ... acquiring enormous powers without any obligations." This periodization is the seedbed for the book's larger argument that the modern trade is power for meaning, and it sets up [[Humanism (Harari)]] — the worship of Man that replaces the worship of gods, and that supplies the moral cover for [[Industrial Farming]].

Philosophically, this chapter lays a keystone thesis of the whole book: [[Organisms Are Algorithms]]. Emotions and sensations are "biochemical algorithms" honed by natural selection for survival and reproduction; the human, the baboon, and the beverage vending machine differ only in complexity. This is where Harari first defines an [[Algorithm (Harari)|algorithm]] ("arguably the single most important concept in our world") and uses industrial farming — the gestation crate, the systematic breaking of the mammalian mother–infant bond — as the moral test case for humanist ethics. The chapter closes by turning that test on humanity itself: if a future superintelligence should not be allowed to exploit us despite its superior intelligence and power, what licenses our exploitation of pigs — some "magical spark," or nothing at all? This closing question is the seed of the book's later [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness|decoupling of intelligence from consciousness]].

## Key Concepts

- [[The Anthropocene]] — the proposed epoch (Harari's "last 70,000 years") in which a single species becomes the dominant force shaping global ecology, collapsing many ecosystems into one human-managed unit.
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the keystone thesis that living beings, humans included, are biochemical algorithms; sensations and emotions are themselves algorithms shaped by natural selection.
- [[Industrial Farming]] — the meat and dairy industries' confinement and exploitation of billions of animals, treated as the moral proving-ground of human exceptionalism.
- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — the modern "religion" that replaces gods with Man; *Homo sapiens* becomes the sole source of meaning and authority, and the justification for industrial farming "in the name of Man."
- [[The Body as Data]] — the somatic correlate Harari prefigures: "the baboon's entire body is the calculator," the living body rendered as computation rather than felt flesh.

## Key Arguments

- Humanity has become a planetary force on a par with the great geological agents, so we should fear ourselves, not asteroids — "within a century, our impact may surpass that of the asteroid that killed off the dinosaurs 65 million years ago."
- Humankind is poised to break evolution's two fixed rules at once — "to replace natural selection with intelligent design, and to extend life from the organic realm into the inorganic."
- Sensations and emotions are biochemical algorithms — "What we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms" — so the [[Organisms Are Algorithms|organism is an algorithm]] under quality control by natural selection.
- Each revolution silenced another class of beings: the Agricultural Revolution silenced animals and plants, the Scientific Revolution silenced the gods, leaving "a one-man show."
- [[Humanism (Harari)|Humanism]] sanctifies Man as theism once sanctified God — "humanism has justified modern industrial farming in the name of Man" — and science lets it exploit animals more cruelly than any pre-modern society could.
- Attributing emotions to pigs does not anthropomorphise but "mammalises" them, since emotions are mammalian equipment, not human property.
- Our treatment of animals previews how a future superintelligence might treat us — the [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality|"magical spark"]] symmetry between AI-over-humans and humans-over-pigs.

## Important Definitions

- [[Algorithm (Harari)]] — "a methodical set of steps that can be used to make calculations, resolve problems and reach decisions"; not a particular calculation but the method followed.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Gestation Crate]] — the two-metre-by-sixty-centimetre concrete-and-bars pen for pregnant sows; the objective/subjective need-gap made concrete and the emblem of industrial-farming cruelty.

## Major Tensions

- [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality]] — does humanity possess a unique "magical spark," or is it just another, more complex algorithm? The chapter's closing AI-vs-pig symmetry destabilises the ground of human supremacy.
- [[Objective vs Subjective vs Intersubjective]] — the sow "no longer needs" to socialise or bond from an objective standpoint, yet "from a subjective perspective ... still feels very strong urges" and "suffers greatly."
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — if 99 per cent of decisions are made by "the highly refined algorithms we call sensations, emotions and desires," the space for free agency narrows toward biochemical computation.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — life's four-billion-year confinement to "the organic realm" versus the coming extension of life "into the inorganic."
