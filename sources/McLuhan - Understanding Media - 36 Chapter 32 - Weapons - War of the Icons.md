# Source Summary: Chapter 32 — Weapons: War of the Icons

**Book:** [[Understanding Media]]
**Author:** [[Marshall McLuhan]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/36 Chapter 32 - Weapons - War of the Icons]]

## Orientation

This is a Part II case-study chapter whose ostensible subject is *weapons*, but McLuhan uses the topic to restate and integrate nearly the whole Part I apparatus and to push one of the book's broadest generalizations: in the electric age, all technology can be regarded as a weapon, and information itself has become the decisive weapon — "the war of the icons." The chapter opens on media events rather than battles (Valentina Tereshkova's untrained orbit "as a kind of defacing of the images of the male astronauts," the first sputnik as "a witty taunting of the capitalist world by means of a new kind of technological image or icon," Madison Avenue's pivot to the "corporate image") and reads them all as engagements in a single image-war. The cold war, on this account, is misnamed when imagined as a deferred hot war: "It is really an electric battle of information and of images that goes far deeper and is more obsessional than the old hot wars of industrial hardware." Where hot wars "knocked off the enemy, one by one," electric persuasion "works, instead, by dunking entire populations in new imagery" — a direct application of the [[Mechanical vs Electric]] and [[Hot vs Cool Media]] machinery and of [[The Global Village]].

The chapter's middle is McLuhan's most sustained statement that weapons are extensions of the body and that marksmanship is a literate, visual skill: "If the arrow is an extension of the hand and the arm, the rifle is an extension of the eye and teeth." He ties the trajectory of gunfire to the rise of lineal perspective — perspective "channeled perception in paths that led to the creation of gunfire" — and argues that accurate shooting is "not the gift of the native or the woodsman, but of the literate colonist," citing the literate Bostonians who outshot the British regulars, the Kentucky rifle, and the Marine Corps correlation between education and marksmanship. This is the [[Typographic Man]] / [[Sense Ratios]] thesis applied to ballistics: the visual, fragmenting bias of literacy produces the isolated-target-in-uniform-space habit that also "deters and obstructs literate man in modern physics." The reversal is already under way — in the Second World War the sighted marksman gave way to "automatic weapons fired blindly" in "perimeters of fire," a "tactual embrace" usable by night, and "Visuality has lost its primacy" (a worked [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] case).

The close restates the city as a collective extension of skin — "a collective shield or plate armor, an extension of the castle of our very skins" — that electric speed renders obsolete as the globe becomes a village and man returns "psychically and socially to the nomad state" of "information-gathering and data-processing." It lands on the keystone generalization and its paradox: because the new electric technology extends not the body but the central nervous system, "all technology can plausibly be regarded as weapons," and war is "a process of achieving equilibrium among unequal technologies"; yet the same electric inclusiveness that arms the war of the icons also "makes more vivid the fact of the unity of the human family," so that we are "poised between two ages —one of detribalization and one of retribalization," and weaponry — no longer usable except "to end all life at once, like the turning off of a light" — becomes "a self-liquidating fact." Almost everything here reinforces Part I; the one durably distinctive thesis is [[The War of the Icons]] itself.

## Key Concepts

- [[The War of the Icons]] — the chapter's signature concept: the cold war as "an electric battle of information and of images" in which rivals are defeated by "the eroding of the collective countenance of one's rivals" (sputnik, the lady cosmonaut, the corporate image) rather than by killing soldiers one by one. The corollary, folded into this note, is that in the electric age all technology — including language — can be regarded as a weapon, because all technology processes information.
- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the chapter's running inventory of extensions: the arrow extends "the hand and the arm," the rifle "the eye and teeth," clothing the individual skin, the city the collective skin, and language a "means of processing experience"; weapons "proper" merely accelerate "the processing of matter."
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the load-bearing premise of the "all technology is a weapon" generalization: electric technology is "not an extension of our bodies but of our central nervous systems," which lets us see all technology as information-processing and so as potential weaponry, and which makes electric weaponry self-liquidating.
- [[Typographic Man]] — marksmanship as a literate visual skill: accurate, isolated-target gunfire is "not the gift of the native or the woodsman, but of the literate colonist"; the same visual bias obstructs literate man in nonvisual modern physics.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — the visual/lineal bias of literacy reorganizes perception toward "a separate, isolated target in space," the trajectory of gunfire, and lineal navigation; in WWII the visual marksman yields to the "tactual embrace" of blind perimeter fire and "Visuality has lost its primacy."
- [[The Global Village]] — instant electric technology dissolves the city ("the globe itself can never again be more than a village") and returns man to a nomad state of "information-gathering and data-processing"; "Politics has gone international."
- [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] — weapons index the tribal/individualist axis: Roman militarism "extended civilization or individualism, literacy and lineality to many oral and backward tribes," while the electric age retribalizes ("the backward countries enjoy some specific advantages"); we are "poised between two ages."
- [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] — the marksman-to-perimeter-fire shift: the sighted, single-shot, visual mode (the bolt-action Springfield) overheats and flips into blind, all-at-once automatic fire usable by night.
- [[Hybrid Energy]] — the "seamless web" of interwoven technologies: gun-boring techniques "made possible the steam engine" (piston shaft and gun "presented the same problems in boring hard steel"), and perspective underwrote both gunfire and the compass.

## Key Arguments

- [[The War of the Icons]] (as argument-bearing concept) — the cold war is an electric war of information and images, not a hot war of industrial hardware; the decisive weapon is the icon, and the casualty is the rival's "collective countenance," not its soldiers. `Directly stated`
- All technology can be regarded as a weapon — because the electric age extends the central nervous system and all technology stores and speeds information, every technology (including language) is a means of processing experience and so capable of aggression; war becomes "a process of achieving equilibrium among unequal technologies." Captured inside [[The War of the Icons]] as its corollary. `Directly stated`
- Marksmanship is a literate visual skill, not native woodcraft — the rifle extends the eye; perspective channels perception into lineal trajectories; literate populations therefore shoot better, and the same visual habit cripples literate man in modern physics. Reinforces [[Typographic Man]] and [[Sense Ratios]]. `Directly stated`
- Electric weaponry is self-liquidating — mechanical weapons could be aimed and used; electric technique "cannot be used aggressively except to end all life at once," so its inclusiveness converts the information-weapon into "a daily reminder" of human fraternity and renders balance-of-power techniques obsolete. `Directly stated` (the closing Dewart attribution is OCR-garbled — see Citations).

## Important Definitions

McLuhan coins no crisp term-defining formula in this chapter on the order of the Part I "the medium is the message." The closest moves are descriptive characterizations — "the war of the icons" as "the eroding of the collective countenance of one's rivals," the city as "a collective shield or plate armor," weaponry finally as "a self-liquidating fact" — all of which are applications of definitions already minted in Part I rather than new defined terms. No new definition note is warranted here; these are captured inside [[The War of the Icons]] and [[Media as Extensions of Man]].

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- "the eroding of the collective countenance of one's rivals" — the central figure: the image as munition, the enemy's collective *face* as the target.
- "The pen daily becomes mightier than the sword" — the proverb literalized for the information war; "Ink and photo are supplanting soldiery and tanks."
- "the castle of our very skins" — the city/ship as plate armor and collective extended skin, paralleling clothing as the extension of the individual skin (echoing Ch. 12, "Clothing — Our Extended Skin").
- "dunking entire populations in new imagery" — electric persuasion as total immersion, against picking off the enemy one by one.
- "like the turning off of a light" — electric weaponry as an all-or-nothing switch (total annihilation), figuring its self-liquidating character.
- "dissolve like a fading shot in a movie" — the obsolescence of the city-form under electric speed, rendered as a cinematic dissolve.
- Sputnik / the "little fellow-traveler" — the technological *icon* as taunt, the recurring symbol of the image-war.
- The atom bomb — the limit-symbol of self-liquidating weaponry, whose mere existence "appears as a state of universal aggression."

## Major Tensions

- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the organizing opposition: hot/mechanical war kills "one by one" and persuades "one at a time"; cold/electric war immerses "entire populations in new imagery" all at once. The transition "is so very traumatic and severe for us all."
- [[Hot vs Cool Media]] — the "hot" wars of industrial hardware versus the cool, all-at-once electric battle of information and images.
- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] — sighted marksmanship, lineal perspective, and "points of view" (fragmentary, visual) versus the "tactual embrace" of perimeter fire and the nonvisual relations of modern physics; "Visuality has lost its primacy."
- [[Numbness vs Awareness]] — literate man is numbed to his own "artificial visual bias," taking it "as a thing natural and innate," and so is crippled in the electric information structure — habits that "could be controlled if we recognized whence they had been acquired."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- McLuhan as the precursor of the contemporary information/image war: "the war of the icons" and "dunking entire populations in new imagery" read forward into Han's image-saturated, exhibition-driven society (see [[Marshall McLuhan and Byung-Chul Han]] and [[Psychopolitics]]) — the icon as the munition of an [[The Transparency Society|exhibition]]-and-affect regime of power.
- "All technology, including language, as a means of processing experience, a means of storing and speeding information" as the [[Dataism]] / information-processing frame avant la lettre — pair with [[Information]] and the Harari/Hayles/Kurzweil cluster (see [[Marshall McLuhan and Yuval Noah Harari]], [[Marshall McLuhan and N. Katherine Hayles]], [[Marshall McLuhan and Ray Kurzweil]]) on the collapse of the distinction between weapon, medium, and information.
- The self-liquidating weapon and "the unity of the human family" as a material/immaterial inversion: the maximally aggressive (information as weapon) becoming the redemptive (the inescapable visibility of the whole) — a case for reading total mediation as both domination and "the concretization of human fraternity."
- Marksmanship-as-literacy as a concrete embodiment case: the rifle "as an extension of the eye," the literate target-in-uniform-space habit, and its obsolescence under "perimeter fire" — useful for any argument tying perception, the body, and the visual bias of print to a specific technology.
