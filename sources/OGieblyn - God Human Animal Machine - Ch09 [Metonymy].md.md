# Source Summary: Ch09 [Metonymy]

## Orientation

Chapter 9, "Metonymy," is O'Gieblyn's most sustained engagement with [[Panpsychism]] as the leading contemporary candidate for the re-enchantment of a disenchanted, materialist cosmos. It opens with the book's recurring simulation / false-awakening motif and advances the thesis that the Enlightenment may itself have been "a false awakening": scientific materialism keeps regressing into religious and animistic myth. O'Gieblyn weighs two readings of this persistence — Weber's psychological wishful thinking versus a genuine "instability at the very core of scientific materialism" — and argues for the latter on the ground that Cartesian materialism was a philosophical thought-experiment that no one ever empirically proved.

The argument then proceeds historically and critically. She traces [[The Mind-Body Problem]] from Descartes through two seventeenth-century women critics — Anne Conway and Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia — who exposed dualism's interaction problem and pushed it toward either pure mechanism or a monist alternative. Conway's single ensouled substance ("Spirit and body are one") becomes one of the first modern articulations of panpsychism, feeding Leibniz's monads. From there she surveys the modern revival (Russell, Eddington, then Strawson, Chalmers, Nagel, and especially Philip Goff), framing panpsychism as the simultaneous solution to [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] and the "problem of intrinsic natures." She pairs that philosophical strand with the neuroscientific one — [[Integrated Information Theory]] of Tononi and Koch, with its consciousness-measure phi — which arrives at panpsychism "not through philosophy but via information theory."

The chapter's distinctive move is reflexive and epistemological. Drawing on Bacon and Galileo, O'Gieblyn notes that the early-modern overthrow of enchantment rested on the charge of anthropomorphism — seeing agency in nature as projecting "the nature of man rather than... the nature of the universe" — and then turns that charge on panpsychism itself: is extending consciousness down the chain of being an escape from human centrality, or "merely another attempt to see all things in our image?" This is where the title concept does its work: if mind is the ultimate substrate, the boundary between subjective mind and objective world dissolves, and the mind/cosmos relation becomes one of metonymy rather than paradox. A memoir interlude (a poet friend devoted to astrology, reiki, and prophetic "correspondences") dramatizes re-enchantment as lived practice against Charles Taylor's point that [[Re-enchantment]] is never merely return. The chapter closes by turning IIT against the present: a sufficiently integrated internet would become conscious, Koch concedes "it feels like something to be the internet," and Goff warns individual brains could become "mere cogs" — Teilhard's noosphere reborn as a technological hive mind in which "the soul is little more than a data set."

## Key Concepts

- [[Panpsychism]] — the idea that consciousness is fundamental, even ubiquitous, in the natural world; mind/soul as the intrinsic nature of all matter.
- [[Integrated Information Theory]] — Tononi and Koch's theory that consciousness is integrated information, measured by phi; nonzero phi entails some consciousness, "even bacteria."
- [[Re-enchantment]] — the persistent counter-movement to Weberian disenchantment; here panpsychism as its leading modern vehicle, yet "never merely return."
- [[Disenchantment]] — Weber's thesis that modernity drains the world of spirit; the materialist worldview O'Gieblyn argues is unproven and unstable.
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — the unexplained gap between physical brain states and subjective experience; paired with the "problem of intrinsic natures."
- [[The Mind-Body Problem]] — how immaterial mind interacts with material body; Cartesian dualism collapsing toward mechanism or monism (Conway, Elisabeth of Bohemia).
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the cosmos-as-information worldview, here inverted: IIT makes mind (not bit) the substrate, and the internet a candidate conscious system.
- [[Emergence]] — the physicalist puzzle of how consciousness arose from blind matter, which panpsychism dissolves by placing it "there from the beginning."
- [[The Body as Data]] — anticipated in the closing reduction of the predictable, type-able self to information ("the soul is little more than a data set").
- [[Transhumanism]] — touched via Teilhard, whose Omega Point and noosphere "inspired aspects of transhumanism."
- [[Posthumanism]] — the secular sibling of O'Gieblyn's transhumanist eschatology; the noosphere as technologically realized collective mind.

## Key Arguments

- [[Scientific materialism is an unproven philosophical project not an empirical fact]] — Cartesian materialism was "a thought experiment dreamed up in an armchair by the fire," never empirically proven.
- [[Panpsychism dissolves the hard problem by making consciousness fundamental]] — identifying matter's unknown intrinsic nature with consciousness solves two gaps in one stroke.
- IIT grades consciousness by phi — nonzero phi entails some experience, so ravens, bees, atoms, quarks, and even bacteria have "a tiny glow of experience."
- [[The anthropomorphism charge misreads the flow of the metaphor]] — the early-modern critique of nature's "ends" as human projection can be turned against panpsychism, which may "see all things in our image."
- [[Re-enchantment]] is never merely return — modern mysticism is "encoded with the assumptions of disenchantment" and shaped by information glut.
- A hyper-integrated internet would absorb individual minds into a collective consciousness — applying Koch's integration-hierarchy answer to the combination problem, brains become "mere cogs."

## Important Definitions

- [[Phi (O'Gieblyn)]] — Tononi's measure of integration: "the amount of information generated by a complex of elements, above and beyond the information generated by its parts."
- [[The Omega Point and the Noosphere (O'Gieblyn)]] — Teilhard's prophecy that sufficiently connected humanity fuses into a single universal mind, the noosphere.
- The combination problem — "the challenge of explaining how conscious microsystems give way to larger systems of unified consciousness."
