# Source Summary: The ARS Electronica Center Datapool

**Book:** [[Telematic Embrace]]
**Author:** [[Roy Ascott]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/25 The ARS Electronica Center Datapool]]

## Orientation

This chapter is not an essay but a building program: Ascott's 1993 conceptual brief for the proposed **Ars Electronica Center (AEC)** in Linz, Austria, written while he served as consultant on the project and first published in German in the *Ars Electronica Center Projektstudie*, vol. 3 (Linz, 1993). It is simultaneously a curatorial manifesto, an institutional design document, and a floor-by-floor architectural-programmatic plan with extensive name-checked artists, works, and technical infrastructure (ISDN, EARN, satellite, networking). Its governing claim is that "older cultural institutions cannot adapt to the needs of the new electronic arts," so what is required is not a museum at all but "a dynamic organism rather than a fixed and static institution" — "a museum of the twenty-first century" Ascott images as a fluid **datapool**.

For the vault this chapter is the keystone primary-text anchor for Ascott's canonical schema of the electronic arts. Its opening states outright that "Connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence are the key elements of the electronic arts in the 1990s," and then defines each of the five in turn — the per-term definitions that turn Ascott's watchword list (named elsewhere "the five-fold path towards telenoia") into a working schema. The chapter is therefore the densest source for [[The Five-Fold Path]] and [[The Five Defining Features]], and the home of the master metaphor-concept [[The Datapool]]. It is also the strongest single statement of the **art-as-process-and-system, not object** thesis from the Ascott (institutional/curatorial) pole, EXTENDING the Burnham-realized cluster — [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], [[Systems Esthetics]], [[Object and System]], [[Material vs Immaterial]] — and the source for [[The Post-Biological Era]], the new-biological-metaphors thesis, and [[The Mind of the Museum]] (the institution as anticipatory cause rather than cultural effect).

Rhetorically it works by reframing opposition as energy. Ascott enumerates the contradictions of his own proposal — public vs advanced, local vs global, technological vs spiritual, digital vs human, immaterial output vs precise engineering, the technology of power vs democratic effects — and resolves them all in a single move: "these oppositions are in fact complementary, and the tensions are energising." The long back-matter (floor-by-floor lists of artists, works, ISDN/EARN/satellite specs) is reference and context only; no concepts are minted from it. [[Gesamtdatenwerk]] appears there as an Ars Electronica program category, corroborating its dedicated ch.18 anchor rather than re-anchoring it.

## Key Arguments

- [[The museum of the future must be a fluid organism not a static institution]] — older cultural institutions cannot host the open-endedness, immersion, and emergence of the electronic arts, so a "fluid and flowing system" / "dynamic organism" is required, not a museum.
- [[Art has moved from representing the world to creating new worlds]] — "the end of the era of mimetic art": art's role shifts from representing the world to "creating worlds, of inventing alternative realities," with the observer as active participant; "no longer to imitate life but to emulate it."

## Important Definitions

- [[The Datapool]] — "a 'datapool' into which and within which data in all their modes flow, endlessly transformed through human interaction, and from which they emerge, art-in-flux."
- Connectivity / Immersion / Interaction / Transformation / Emergence — the five glossed in turn ("connectivity of people, places, ideas, media, and systems" / "immersion of the viewer in the data space of the artist" / "interaction with elements of the systems that are encountered" / "transformation of media and of the consciousness of the viewer" / "emergence of new data, images, forms, structures, and systems"); these definitions live in [[The Five-Fold Path]].

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Datapool]] — the data sea, its tributaries, and the swimming register (splashing, paddling, plunging, diving) for "the variable degrees of physical, intellectual, and emotional engagement."
- The primeval rock pool — the evolutionary analogy: post-biological culture emerges from data pools as proto-organisms emerged from rock pools.
- The osmotic membrane — the building's permeable entrance "separating the Center from the street," the building-as-organism image.
- The electronic central nervous system — the building's cabling as a nervous system; cross-links [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] and [[Leasing the Central Nervous System]].
- Seedbed / test-bed / fertile ground — the institution as "a seedbed for the future, a test-bed of present inquiry and experiment, and a fertile ground of historical reference."
- The televator — the elevator with floor/ceiling screens giving "the impression of the museum, Linz, Austria, Europe, and the planet Earth slipping away," a telepresence / cosmic-scale image.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — explicit: "art not as finite object but as process and system," and the listed opposition "its output is fluid and immaterial, but its engineering and construction are precise"; EXTENDS the eight-author tension from the Ascott pole.
- [[Object vs System]] — the finite-object vs process-system pole, from the institutional/curatorial side.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the spectatorship shift: "no longer to observe, stand back, look from a distance, and judge, but to plunge into the datapool."
- [[Isolation vs Convergence]] — the listed local-vs-global opposition ("rooted in the community, but its networks reach out to the world"), resolved as complementarity.
