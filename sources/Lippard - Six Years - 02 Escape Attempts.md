# Source Summary: Escape Attempts

**Book:** [[Six Years]]
**Author:** [[Lucy Lippard]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]

## Orientation

"Escape Attempts" is the retrospective framing essay Lippard wrote (c. 1995, nearly thirty years after the events) for the reissue of *Six Years*. It is the book's richest source of durable ideas because it is wholly in her own voice: a "biased history" that theorizes the movement she helped name. She opens by warning that this is "an occasionally critical memoir," not an authoritative overview — "My own version is inevitably tempered by my feminist and left politics" — and quotes her younger self at length because, she says, "I knew more about it then than I do now." The essay is the primary anchor in this vault for both [[Dematerialization]] and [[Conceptual Art]], and for the recuperation argument that closes the book.

The essay does four things at once. First, it **defines** [[Conceptual Art]]: work "in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized,'" distinguished from Sol LeWitt's conceptual art "with a small c" (conventional material forms generated by a paramount idea) versus "Conceptual art 'with a capital C.'" Second, it narrates the **politics of the object**: the late-1960s drive toward the "de-mythologization and de-commodification of art," the wish for an art "that could not be bought and sold," and the attack on originality and "the artist's touch" as an attack on "the genius theory, the hitherto most cherished aspect of patriarchal, ruling-class art."

Third, it surveys the **formal repertoire** of dematerialized work — information and systems aesthetics (Hans Haacke, LeWitt, Mel Bochner), the document standing in for the object, the exhibition-and-catalogue as a medium (Seth Siegelaub, the *Studio International* "magazine exhibition," the 1970 MoMA *Information* show), ephemerality and the "(apparently) 'empty' room," and decentralized, internationalist distribution by mail, book, telex, and telephone. Fourth — and this is the book's signature move — it delivers the **self-critical verdict**: dematerialization was an *escape attempt* that failed to escape the market. By 1973 "the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums," "art and artists in a capitalist society remain luxuries," and "The escape was temporary."

Woven through all four is Lippard's turn to politics: Conceptual art's entanglement with the Civil Rights, anti-war, and women's movements; the Art Workers Coalition and the Guerrilla Art Action Group; and the claim that the cheap, "unintimidating" Conceptual mediums opened "a crack in the art world's walls" through which women artists entered.

## Key Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — the book's titular concept: the shift from the art object as material commodity toward work in which the idea is paramount and the material vehicle is ephemeral, cheap, invisible, or absent. The art-historical instance of the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis; closely shadowed by [[The Materiality of Informatics]], which insists the immaterial always leaves a material residue (here: xeroxes, index cards, photographs).
- [[Conceptual Art]] — Lippard's "ultra-conceptual" category of idea-paramount art; her usage versus LeWitt's "small c" / "capital C" distinction.
- [[Systems Theory]] — Haacke's "think in terms of systems" and LeWitt's permutational logic appropriate systems thinking into art; "information and systems replaced traditional formal concerns of composition, color, technique, and physical presence."
- [[Information]] — the information aesthetic of lists, diagrams, measurements, and neutral description; concretized in the 1970 MoMA *Information* exhibition, "born of an art-oriented interest in systems and information theory."
- [[Documentation]] — the secondary record (photo, xerox, text, affidavit, project description) as the characteristic material residue of dematerialized work.
- [[Exhibition as Medium]] — Siegelaub's reinvention of the dealer as "distributor extraordinaire"; the show and the printed catalogue become the artwork's primary site.
- [[Virtuality]] — light cross-link: the ephemeral, invisible, or merely-spoken artwork as an art-world figure of the immaterial.
- [[New Media Art]] — the video / performance / photography / narrative / text mediums Lippard credits with lowering the barrier for women artists; precursor terrain for this research focus.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — Adrian Piper's participatory blank-notebook contribution to *Information* (viewers "requested to write, draw, or otherwise indicate any response") as an early experiential, participatory gesture.

## Key Arguments

- [[Dematerialization failed to escape the market]] — the 1969 hope that non-object art could not be bought and sold is refuted by the 1973 outcome; "art and artists in a capitalist society remain luxuries."
- [[In conceptual art the form not the content carried the politics]] — the art "seems supremely apolitical," but the politics lives in the de-commodification of the object: "The frame was there to be broken out of."
- [[Conceptual art substitutes information and systems for physical presence]] — the dematerialized object leaves information and systems to do the work formerly done by composition, color, technique, and physical presence.
- [[Conceptual mediums opened a crack for women artists]] — the cheap, ephemeral mediums "encouraged women to participate, to move through this crack in the art world's walls."
- [[Conceptual art achieved distribution but not accessibility]] — "Communication (but not community) and distribution (but not accessibility) were inherent in Conceptual art."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Escape Attempt]] — the master metaphor of escape / recapture / parole / the "white cell": art as a prisoner breaking out of the frame-and-pedestal commodity system, dematerialization as a temporary jailbreak, the market as recapture.
- The "(apparently) 'empty' room" — the literal image of dematerialization (Haacke's fan, Barry's invisible magnetic field, Weiner's air-rifle pit, a "secret," a black blip).
- The index card / card catalogue — Lippard's randomly arranged catalogues as artwork and as discardable, distributable form.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the object versus the idea; physical presence versus information/systems; the salable thing versus the ephemeral or invisible work. This essay is the clearest art-historical realization of the vault's titular axis.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- The "dematerialization of the art object" (Lippard) as the visual-art twin of the "disembodiment of information" (Hayles): two cultural fantasies that the essential content can be abstracted from its material substrate, both ultimately denied — Lippard empirically (the object returns as commodity), Hayles theoretically (information must always be instantiated).
- A genealogy of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] from Conceptual art's cheap, participatory, decentralized mediums.
- The recuperation argument as a case study in how markets absorb anti-commodity art — readable against Han's [[Exhibition Value]].
