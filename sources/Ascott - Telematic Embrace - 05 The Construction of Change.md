# Source Summary: 05 The Construction of Change

**Book:** [[Telematic Embrace]]
**Author:** [[Roy Ascott]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/05 The Construction of Change]]

## Orientation

"The Construction of Change" (originally "Art And Didactics," *Cambridge Opinion*, January 1964) is Ascott's earliest essay in the Shanken volume and the chronological origin of his cybernetic and behaviourist art theory. It does two things at once. First, it sets out a theory of the artwork as a behavioural *system* that the spectator completes: Ascott makes "structures in which the relationships of parts are not fixed and may be changed by the intervention of the spectator," so that "the participant becomes responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning." Second, it argues — explicitly and programmatically — that the artist must turn to **cybernetics**, "control and communication in animal and machine," as the integrative science that can ground a discipline for art. The essay's title names Ascott's core 1960s practice: artworks built so that change is their substance, "analogues of ideas—structures that are subject to change and human intervention in the way that ideas themselves are."

The essay's rhetorical posture is that of a working artist-teacher writing a manifesto-cum-report. It moves from a first-person account of Ascott's own [[The Change Painting|Change Paintings]] and kinetic constructions, through a defence of the didactic and social role of art, to the central recommendation that art adopt cybernetics as its scientific reference, and finally to a description of the **[[The Groundcourse|Groundcourse]]** he initiated and directed at the Ealing School of Art in London — a two-year pedagogy that "disorients" the student through behavioural exercises (acquiring a contrived converse personality, forming six-member "sexagonal organisms," designing "calibrators" and "mind-maps" for one's own behaviour). Here art education itself becomes a cybernetic feedback system in which the "creative and pedagogic" activities "interact, each feeding back to the other."

Set against the just-ingested Burnham, this essay supplies the *other* pole of 1960s cybernetic art. Where Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) theorizes the [[Object and System|object→system]] shift in the autonomous art object, Ascott (1964, four years earlier) locates the system in the *behavioural* loop between artist, artwork, and participating spectator: the work "occupies a pivotal point between two sets of behaviour, the artist's and the spectator's. It is essentially a matrix, the substance between." This is the participatory/behavioural cybernetic art that the Burnham-realized stubs [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], [[Cyborg Art]], and [[Reciprocal Communication]] were committed to extend when Ascott was ingested (see [[Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham]]). It also reaches toward McLuhan's same-year *Understanding Media* — the machine that "extends his perception into furthest space" — and toward the network/posthuman cluster, with man "becoming more and more predominantly a controller and less an effecter." (Confidence: Interpretive synthesis for the Burnham/McLuhan positioning; Directly stated for Ascott's own claims, quoted below.)

## Key Concepts

- [[Behaviourist Art]] — art understood as a behavioural problem in which the spectator becomes a "decision maker," responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning; the participatory/behavioural pole of 1960s cybernetic art.
- [[The Groundcourse]] — Ascott's two-year Ealing foundation course; "a microcosm of a total process of art education" run as a cybernetic feedback system (disorientation, converse personalities, sexagonal organisms, calibrators, mind-maps).
- [[Cybernetics]] — invoked as "control and communication in animal and machine" and recommended as the integrative science FOR THE ARTIST; this chapter is the art-historical seed of that argument (extends the existing cross-author note with an Ascott section).
- [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — the work of art as "a matrix, the substance between," the pivot between the artist's and the spectator's behaviour, existing "neither for itself nor by itself."
- [[Object and System]] — Ascott's "first seeing the system or process before evaluating the 'thing'" is a 1964 precursor/parallel to Burnham's process-over-object thesis.
- [[New Media Art]] · [[Art as Experiential Technology]] · [[Reciprocal Communication]] · [[Cyborg Art]] — the Burnham-realized cluster that Ascott's behavioural/participatory practice extends.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — pedagogy and practice "feeding back to the other"; creativity figured as "loops of creative activity" to be unravelled.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] / [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — man as "controller" not "effecter"; the self-regulating machine that "extends his perception" (proto-extensions-of-man, McLuhan, same year).

## Key Arguments

- [[The spectator completes the artwork in a feedback loop]] — meaning is extended through the participant's behaviour within limits the artist sets, not fixed in advance by the artist.
- [[The artist must turn to cybernetics as the integrative science for art]] — cybernetics is "a coordinator of science, as art is the coordinator of experience," and is the proper scientific reference for art.
- [[Art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour]] — the chapter's behavioural turn: "behaviour relates to idea"; we should "first see the system or process before evaluating the 'thing.'"
- [[Behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object]] — the Change Paintings and kinetic constructions remain made objects even as behaviour becomes the medium.
- [[The kinetic construction is not an object but a system]] — the alterable, intervention-driven construction as a behavioural system rather than an autonomous object.

## Major Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — Ascott's "first seeing the system or process before evaluating the 'thing'" is the participatory/behavioural form of the object→system shift.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the process-over-object ontology and the artwork-as-"substance between" add the participatory pole to this multi-author tension.
- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — the spectator-completed work as a two-way behavioural loop rather than passive reception.
- [[Determinism vs Randomness]] — the artist "sets limits within which he may behave" yet the spectator becomes a decision maker who extends the meaning: authorship shared but bounded.
- [[Art vs Technics]] — art "does not eschew science" yet must not become "a scientific work"; a productive tension, not a contradiction.
