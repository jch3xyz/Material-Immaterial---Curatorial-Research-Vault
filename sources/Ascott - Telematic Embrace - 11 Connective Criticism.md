# Source Summary: 11 Connective Criticism

**Book:** [[Telematic Embrace]]

## Orientation

"Connective Criticism" is a very short (two-page) programmatic statement that Ascott prepared for a conference he organized at the Center for Critical Inquiry, San Francisco Art Institute, in 1977; it was previously unpublished before its inclusion in the Shanken edition (source pages 174–175). It is a manifesto-in-miniature for a new mode of art criticism Ascott names **connective criticism** — an interdisciplinary "metacritique" that would interconnect the methodologies of science, psychology, linguistics ("the structures of language"), social and economic theory, metaphysics, and philosophy into "a unified field of inquiry." Its diagnostic premise is that criticism is "moribund" and that "if there is a crisis in criticism, there is certainly a crisis in the understanding of art"; its remedy is to bring "all the resources of the human mind" into a single connective field that holds the contradictions of separate approaches while identifying "correspondences, similarities, and new relationships." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

The chapter's importance for the Ascott arc is that it transposes his 1960s cybernetic / [[Behaviourist Art]] vocabulary directly onto criticism. Art is declared "a system" that "finds its form in the interaction of many parts," and the critic is positioned as a *formative* element of that system — structurally equivalent to the participating spectator who completes the work: "The role of criticism in this system, like the role of the viewer or participant, is formative." The keystone one-liner, "Critical inquiry is a part of the making of art," collapses the old boundary between making and judging. Because "Art is an open system," criticism must be open too: "A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms." (Confidence: Directly stated.) The piece thereby restates, in the register of criticism, the same form→function move that drives Burnham's contemporaneous systems esthetics — though Ascott does not cite Burnham, so the convergence is an interpretive bridge, not a citation (see [[Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham]]). (Confidence: Interpretive synthesis.)

Given its brevity, the chapter's principal yield for the vault is the titular concept [[Connective Criticism]] (realized in this Ascott ingest) plus light extension of the existing systems-art cluster ([[Systems Esthetics]], [[Systems Theory]], [[Reciprocal Communication]], the participatory account in [[The Participator]]); it does not by itself warrant minting near-duplicate notes for "speculative criticism," "form vs function," or "art as an open system," all of which overlap with notes already grounded in Burnham and in Ascott's longer cybernetic chapters. The one borrowed authority, Northrop Frye (the "reforge the broken links" exhortation), is a single quotation and is left as plain text rather than a reference note. ## Key Concepts

- [[Connective Criticism]] — Ascott's coinage and the chapter title: the intersection of specialized critical inquiries into a "unified field" / "metacritique" that contains the contradictions of separate approaches while identifying new relationships.
- [[Behaviourist Art]] — the 1960s cybernetic/participatory program whose vocabulary ("triggering new behaviors," the viewer as completing agent) Ascott here applies to the critical act.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's art-specific object→system thesis; the closest existing kin to Ascott's "Art is a system… finds its form in the interaction of many parts" and the form→function shift.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the broader cross-author field whose open-system / "interaction of many parts" vocabulary Ascott borrows for criticism.
- [[The Participator]] — Ascott's spectator-as-completing-agent; the critic is assigned the same structural, formative role in the art system.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the participatory-feedback idea (Burnham-anchored, Ascott-extended) of which "the critic as completing agent" is the criticism-side application.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — weak, interpretive resonance: the connective/unified-field idea distributes knowing across disciplines and informed participants.

## Key Arguments

- Criticism is a formative part of the art system, not external commentary on it — because art is an open system completed by its participants, and criticism occupies the participant's structural role: "Critical inquiry is a part of the making of art." (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- Because art is an open system, criticism must be open-ended and speculative; "A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms." (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- A unified, interdisciplinary connective criticism is needed because both criticism and the understanding of art are in crisis, and valuable specialized inquiries remain siloed "within their narrow terms of reference." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

## Important Definitions

- **Connective criticism** — defined in the text as the field constituted by the intersection of specialized critical inquiries, one that "would not only contain the ambiguities, polarities, and contradictions of these separate approaches, but would identify correspondences, similarities, and new relationships." (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- **Metacritique** — used as the goal-object of connective criticism: a higher-order critical framework drawing on the "conceptual frameworks and methodologies" of multiple disciplines. (Confidence: Strongly implied — clarified by use, not formally defined.)
- **Definitive critique** — defined negatively as a self-contradiction, given art's open-endedness. (Confidence: Directly stated.)

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- **"Springboard"** — speculative criticism "acts as a springboard to further dimensions of creative work," a launching device that propels new art rather than a verdict on finished art. (Confidence: Directly stated.)
- **"Reforge the broken links"** — the Frye-borrowed image of broken links "between creation and knowledge, art and science, myth and concept" to be reforged; notably resonant with Ascott's later network/connectivity vocabulary, though here it is borrowed. (Confidence: Directly stated as a quotation of Frye.)
- **"Unfold and blossom"** — a quasi-organic figure for how "meanings, values, and realities" mature in a fully creative culture. (Confidence: Directly stated.)

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A keystone for an essay on the **systems turn in art criticism**: pairing Ascott's "Critical inquiry is a part of the making of art" with Burnham's 1968 systems esthetics to show two contemporaneous, mutually unaware routes (object→system; behaviourist participation→criticism) by which art was reconceived as an open system in the late 1960s–70s.
- Support for an argument that **participation dissolves the maker/critic/viewer hierarchy**: the critic as just one more formative feedback element in an open work, alongside Ascott's spectator and Lippard's dematerialized, distributed art document.
- A historical anchor for **interdisciplinary / "connective" methodology** itself — using Ascott's 1977 call for a "unified field of inquiry" to frame the material/immaterial vault's own cross-author method, with care to label the resonance interpretive.
- A short case in **the rhetoric of crisis-and-remedy** in art writing: how the diagnosis ("a crisis in the understanding of art") licenses an institutional proposal (a Center for Critical Inquiry) and a programmatic coinage.
