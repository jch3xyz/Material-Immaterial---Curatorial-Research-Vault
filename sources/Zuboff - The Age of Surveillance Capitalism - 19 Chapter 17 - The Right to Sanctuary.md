# Source Summary: Chapter 17 — The Right to Sanctuary

## Orientation

Chapter 17 is the penultimate chapter of the book and the second of Part III's two "rights" chapters, paired with the earlier "Right to the Future Tense." Across three sections it claims a second elemental human right — [[The Right to Sanctuary]] — diagnoses why the centuries-old legal solution to lawless power ("justice as sanctuary," the rule of law) no longer holds against surveillance capitalism, and confronts the life that remains if "taming fails." Zuboff opens autobiographically, with the loss of her own home to a lightning strike and its rebuilding behind walls "nearly a foot deep and packed with insulation," then enlists Gaston Bachelard's phenomenology of the home (*The Poetics of Space*, "topoanalysis") to argue that the home is "our school of intimacy, where we first learn to be human," the original space in which the self's elemental categories (house/universe, refuge/world, inside/outside, depth/immensity) are formed. [[Big Other]] — the ubiquitous sensate computational apparatus — is presented as the demolition of these walls: with Big Other "the universe takes up residence in our walls," and "there can be no secret hiding places because there can be no secrets."

Section II ("Justice at the New Frontier of Power") asks "What will taming require?" Zuboff surveys the legal terrain — the collapse of Anita Allen's distinction between "physical/spatial" and "informational" privacy under Big Other; the inadequacy of Fourth Amendment doctrine to the internet of things; and the EU's new GDPR (enforceable May 2018) as the chief hope, while warning it is no automatic remedy. The recurring claim is that the individual alone cannot bear the burden, dramatized by Paul-Olivier Dehaye's fifteen-month odyssey to extract his own data from Facebook — whose "Hive" answer reveals the proprietary "shadow text" (the "second text" of behavioral surplus queued for manufacture into prediction products) held wholly apart from the "first text" "which users experience." Zuboff calls (after Laura Nader, "the life of the law is the plaintiff") for collective action and synthetic declarations "institutionalized in new centers of democratic power," analogizing to early-twentieth-century labor organizing.

Section III ("Every Unicorn Has a Hunter") names the stakes if taming fails: a condition of "no exit, where the only walls are made of glass" — [[Glass Life]]. After cataloguing the "exquisite unicorns" of the liberal order that Big Other hunts, Zuboff surveys [[The Art and Science of Hiding]] — a vanguard of activists, artists, and inventors devising anti-surveillance wearables, masks, makeup, and software (Adam Harvey, Leo Selvaggio, Benjamin Grosser, Trevor Paglen, Ai Weiwei, the Backslash Tool Kit). But hiding, she warns, "risks becomes an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage" — "this wall must come down." The chapter closes on the danger of numbness: that we "come to feel at home in glass life," forfeiting the "life-sustaining inwardness, born in sanctuary, that finally distinguishes us from the machines." For the vault this is the chapter where the surveillance cluster meets the art/new-media cluster, and the strongest single bridge to Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[The Glassy Human]].

## Key Concepts

- [[The Right to Sanctuary]] — the second of Zuboff's claimed elemental human rights, the right to an inviolable refuge or "proper realm of inaccessibility" in which the self forms; grounded in the ancient sanctuary privilege (*asylon* = "unplunderable") and Bachelard's phenomenology of home. The chapter's title concept and primary anchor.
- [[Glass Life]] — the dystopian no-exit condition that follows the destruction of sanctuary: "the only walls are made of glass," total inescapable visibility; the shell reduced to "just another connected node."
- [[The Art and Science of Hiding]] — the vanguard of activists, artists, and inventors who devise anti-surveillance wearables, masks, makeup, and software to evade computer vision and facial recognition; "canaries in the coal mine" whose work risks becoming mere adaptation.
- [[Big Other]] — the ubiquitous sensate, networked computational apparatus, here developed through the home: the walls "must come down," "the universe takes up residence in our walls," it "outruns society and law."
- [[The Division of Learning in Society]] — the asymmetry of knowledge and power; here the "pathological division of learning" embodied by the shadow text and Dehaye's failed access, which "only law can compel."
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] / [[Prediction Products]] / [[Behavioral Futures Markets]] — the master economic concepts restated: behavioral surplus "queued up for manufacture into predictions products" in the shadow text, for trade in behavioral futures markets.
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] / [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the power that destroys sanctuary; "the institutionalization of its means of behavior modification" and the hive's "instrumentarian disciplines."
- [[The Hive]] — the model of "coercive confluence and preemptive harmonies" advocated by the architects (Pentland, Nadella, Schmidt) as our role model.
- [[The Uncontract]] / [[Radical Indifference]] — restated in the §III catalogue of unicorns: "the bypass of trust in favor of the uncontract's radical indifference."
- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the companion elemental right, repeatedly paired with the right to sanctuary as "essential for effective human life."
- [[Surveillance]] — the committed cross-author surveillance concept; this chapter contributes the home/sanctuary, glass-life, and anti-surveillance-art material.

## Key Arguments

- [[Big Other outruns society and law in the destruction of sanctuary]] — sanctuary has been "an antidote to power since the beginning of the human story," but the centuries-old solution "justice as sanctuary" no longer holds.
- [[The individual alone cannot bear the burden only collective action can tame surveillance capitalism]] — the asymmetries of knowledge and power are too vast for individuals (Dehaye); as workers once had to organize, "today's 'users' will have to mobilize."
- [[The shadow text is about us but not for us]] — surveillance capitalism keeps a proprietary "second text" of surplus and predictions, separate from the "first text" users experience; "only law can compel" its disclosure.
- [[Hiding is a counter-declaration that risks becoming adaptation the wall must come down]] — the art of hiding is poignant but defensive; "tunnels under this wall are not enough. This wall must come down."
- [[Surveillance capitalism threatens human nature as industrial capitalism threatened nature]] — "Industrial capitalism commandeered nature… Will we add to this burden with surveillance capitalism's invasion and conquest of human nature?"
- [[Only a synthetic declaration not defensive measures can transform surveillance capitalism]] — counter-declarations leave the offending facts intact; only synthetic declarations "institutionalized in new centers of democratic power" can change the game.

## Important Definitions

- *Sanctuary / asylon* — "The Greek word asylon means 'unplunderable' and founds the notion of a sanctuary as an inviolable space." (Contributes to [[The Right to Sanctuary]].)
- *Topoanalysis (Bachelard via Zuboff)* — "the study of how our deepest relationships to inner self and outer world are formed in our experience of space, specifically the space we call 'home.'" (Folded into [[The Right to Sanctuary]].)
- *Privacy as a boundary control process (Pedersen)* — privacy as "a 'boundary control process'" with six categories: solitude, isolation, anonymity, reserve, intimacy with friends, intimacy with family. (Contributes to [[The Right to Sanctuary]].)
- *The shadow text / the second text* — "this exclusive 'second text,' in which behavioral surplus is queued up for manufacture into predictions products," "completely separate from the 'first text,' 'which users experience.'" (Anchors the argument [[The shadow text is about us but not for us]].)
