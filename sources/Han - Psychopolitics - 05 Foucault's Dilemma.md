# Source Summary: 5. Foucault's Dilemma

**Book:** [[Psychopolitics]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/05 Foucault's Dilemma]]

## Orientation

This is the hinge chapter of *Psychopolitics*: Han turns the whole book on a diagnosis of where [[Michel Foucault]] stopped short. Having recognized in the late 1970s that disciplinary society "did not reflect the times in every respect," Foucault turned his attention to neoliberal forms of government — but, Han argues, he kept the wrong tools. He "stuck to the concepts of population and biopolitics," categories that (for Han) belong strictly to *disciplinary* society and are "unsuited to describing the neoliberal regime." Foucault therefore "failed to do what the circumstances actually called for: to make the turn to psychopolitics." Han even rereads Agamben's elegiac claim that Foucault's early death cut short the development of [[Biopolitics]]: the death did not deprive Foucault of *completing* biopolitics but of *abandoning* it for neoliberal psychopolitics. The rhetorical posture is immanent critique — Han accepts Foucault's diagnostic apparatus in order to argue that Foucault did not follow it far enough.

The chapter then makes the book's central periodizing move. [[Biopolitics]] "fundamentally concerns the biological and the physical"; it "constitutes a politics of the body in the fullest sense." Neoliberalism, "a further development — indeed, a mutated form — of capitalism," has by contrast "discovered the psyche as a productive force." This psychic turn is tied to a shift in the mode of production: what gets produced are "not material objects, but immaterial ones — for instance, information and programs." The body is "released from the immediate process of production" and becomes "the object of optimization," so that "physical discipline has given way to mental optimization," cosmetic surgery and fitness studios replace disciplinary orthopaedics, and Foucault's "docile body" "has no place in this production process." Han enlists [[Bernard Stiegler]] (biopower is "no longer the force behind our age," replaced by "psychotechnological psychopower") but corrects him for overemphasizing television and missing "the panoptic structure of digital networks" — and so missing neoliberal psychopolitics itself.

The chapter closes on Foucault's late "technologies of the self." Han argues that the neoliberal regime "utterly claims the technology of the self for its own purposes": perpetual [[Self-Optimization]], far from being an ethics opposed to power, "represents nothing so much as a highly efficient mode of domination and exploitation." As an "entrepreneur of himself," the neoliberal achievement-subject "engages in auto-exploitation willingly — and even passionately," and "the self-as-a-work-of-art amounts to a beautiful but deceptive illusion." Power here "takes on a subtle form"; it "does not lay hold of individuals directly" but "ensures that individuals act on themselves so that power relations are interiorized — and then interpreted as freedom." The terminal formulation — "Self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, fall into one" — is the engine of the entire book, and the precise blind spot Han names: "Such engineering of freedom and exploitation, which occurs in order to effect self-exploitation, is what escaped Foucault."

## Key Concepts

- [[Psychopolitics]] — the neoliberal turn in which power targets the psyche rather than the body; "the turn to psychopolitics" is exactly what Han says Foucault failed to make.
- [[Biopolitics]] — Foucault's concept, which Han historicizes and bounds: a category of disciplinary society and capitalism's disciplinary form, concerning "the biological, the somatic, the corporal."
- [[Self-Optimization]] — "the exemplary neoliberal technology of the self," recast as a mode of domination; physical discipline gives way to mental optimization.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the achievement-subject who exploits himself "willingly — and even passionately" as an "entrepreneur of himself."
- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — power interiorized and "then interpreted as freedom"; freedom and exploitation "fall into one."
- [[Achievement Society]] — "the neoliberal achievement-subject," the self-CEO whose self-as-work-of-art exhausts his own resources.
- [[Immaterial Labour]] — neoliberalism as mutated capitalism in which immaterial, non-physical production (information, programs) supplants material objects and releases the body from production.
- [[Smart Power]] — power that "takes on a subtle form" and "does not lay hold of individuals directly"; the seed of the book's smart-power thesis.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — invoked through Stiegler's blind spot: "the panoptic structure of digital networks," the medium of neoliberal psychopolitics.
- [[Information]] — among the "immaterial" things that contemporary capitalism produces in place of material objects.

## Key Arguments

- [[Foucault failed to make the turn to psychopolitics]] — Foucault recognized the inadequacy of disciplinary society but retained population and biopolitics, categories that cannot capture the neoliberal regime.
- Foucault's early death deprived him of *abandoning* biopolitics for psychopolitics, not of *completing* it — Han inverts Agamben's elegiac speculation about the unfinished study of biopolitics.
- Today's *homines sacri* are shut *into* the system, not *out* of it — capture by inclusion rather than exclusion, a reversal of Agamben's exclusion-model of bare life.
- Neoliberalism targets the psyche because production has become immaterial — when information and programs determine capitalism, the body ceases to be the central force of production.
- The docile body is replaced by the optimized body — discipline yields to aesthetic and health optimization; cosmetic surgery and fitness studios replace disciplinary orthopaedics.
- Stiegler's media critique misses digital psychopolitics — the literacy-versus-television opposition is a "superannuated scheme of cultural critique" that ignores the internet, social media, and the panoptic structure of digital networks.
- Neoliberalism captures the technology of the self — self-optimization is not an ethics opposed to power but "a highly efficient mode of domination and exploitation."
- Neoliberal power interiorizes domination as freedom — it makes individuals act on themselves so that "freedom and exploitation, fall into one," the fusion that "escaped Foucault."

## Important Definitions

- [[Psychopolitics (Han)]] — the neoliberal regime of power that, having "discovered the psyche as a productive force," optimizes psychic and mental processes rather than disciplining the body.
- [[Biopolitics]] — bounded by Han to the disciplinary form of capitalism that "socializes the body in its productive capacity"; it "fundamentally concerns the biological and the physical."
- "Technologies of the self" (Foucault, quoted) — "intentional and voluntary actions by which men not only set themselves rules of conduct, but also seek to transform themselves... and to make their life into an oeuvre."
- "Psychotechnological psychopower" (Stiegler, via Han) — what replaces biopower; the "telecratic" "programming industry" that reduces subjects to "impulsive... creatures of consumption" and induces "mass regression."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The "docile body" (Foucault, redeployed) — the disciplined, trainable body that "has no place in this production process."
- Cosmetic surgery and the fitness studio — the optimized, marketable body that replaces disciplinary orthopaedics.
- The "entrepreneur of himself" / the self-as-a-work-of-art — the neoliberal subject as self-CEO; "a beautiful but deceptive illusion."
- Shut into the system — bare life figured spatially as capture-by-inclusion ([[Homo Sacer]] inverted).
- Capitalism as a "mutated form" — neoliberalism figured biologically as a mutation of capitalism.

## Major Tensions

- [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] — the chapter's master opposition: power over the body/population versus power over the psyche.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — material objects versus information and programs; the body as force of production versus immaterial production.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the signature Han dialectic; freedom and exploitation "fall into one."
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — physical discipline and orthopaedics versus mental optimization and subtle, self-administered power.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A periodizing anchor for the material/immaterial axis of the vault: Han's claim that neoliberalism produces "immaterial objects — for instance, information and programs" and so "discovered the psyche as a productive force" is a precise economic counterpart to the dematerialization and disembodiment theses already in the vault (Lippard, Hayles). An essay could test whether Han's immaterial-production diagnosis is the political economy that those art- and media-theoretic accounts leave implicit.
- A frame for thinking about the optimized, self-curating body: cosmetic surgery, fitness studios, and "sexiness and fitness" as "new economic resources" map onto the self-exhibiting subject of surveillance culture. Useful for a curatorial essay on the body as an object of display and optimization rather than discipline.
- A methodological case study in immanent critique: Han's strategy of accepting Foucault's apparatus only to argue it stopped at biopolitics models how one inherits and turns a theoretical genealogy — relevant to positioning one's own practice against a precursor.
- A counterpoint for any account that treats "technologies of the self" or self-care as resistance: Han's claim that the neoliberal regime "utterly claims the technology of the self" supplies the adversarial reading.
