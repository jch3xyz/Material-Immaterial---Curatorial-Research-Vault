## Orientation

This is Chapter 3 of *How We Became Posthuman*, and it is the historical pivot of the book's whole argument: the chapter that asks "When and where did information get constructed as a disembodied medium?" and answers by reconstructing the contingent negotiations of the Macy Conferences on Cybernetics (1943–1954). Hayles treats the conferences as "a crossroads for the traffic in cybernetic models and artifacts" and charts the reification of [[Information]] along three fronts: information as a theoretical entity (Shannon and Wiener), human neural structure recast as information flow (the McCulloch-Pitts neuron), and physical artifacts that made the flows "real" (Shannon's electronic rat, Ashby's homeostat, Grey Walter's tortoise, Wiener's Moth and Bedbug). Her governing claim is one of contingency, not inevitability: reification "was triumphant not because it had no opposition but because scientifically and culturally situated debates made it seem a better choice than the alternatives." The chapter's pre-selected thesis sentence states it flatly — "Conceptualizing information as a disembodied entity was not an arbitrary decision, but neither was it inevitable." This is the chapter that grounds the vault's central axis of [[Embodiment vs Information]] and [[Material vs Immaterial]] in a specific archive.

Rhetorically the chapter stages two rival "constellations" — homeostasis versus reflexivity — and reads every technical dispute as a contest between them. The cleanest fault line is the disagreement between the Shannon–Wiener definition of information (a context-free probability function, "what it is") and Donald MacKay's rejected alternative (information as metacommunicative effect on the receiver's mind, "what it does"). Following Nicolas Tzannes, Hayles crystallizes the difference: Shannon and Wiener "define information in terms of what it is," whereas "MacKay defines it in terms of what it does." Homeostasis (information as a transportable thing) won the first wave because it was "more manageable quantitatively"; reflexivity lost because "specifying and delimiting context quickly ballooned into an unmanageable project." The chapter follows reflexivity's losing battle through the man-in-the-middle debates (Stroud vs Fremont-Smith), Kubie's "last stand" against the experimentalists, and finally its survival and transformation in Gregory and Catherine Bateson's 1968 conference, anticipating Maturana's autopoiesis in [[Cybernetics|the second wave]].

The McCulloch material supplies the chapter's most-cited move, the **Platonic backhand**: McCulloch's neural net, proved formally equivalent to a Turing machine, is rhetorically inflated from "an effect of the model to a cause of the model's efficacy," so that "embodied reality [becomes] a blurred and messy instantiation of the clean abstractions of logical forms." Hayles holds this against feminist critiques of logic and objectivity — Andrea Nye on the political situatedness of "lawlike" logic, and Dorothy Smith on how "men of a certain class are prone to decontextualization and reification because they are in a position to command the labors of others." The chapter closes on its most memorable figure of embodiment: Janet Freed (misremembered by Stewart Brand as "Janet Freud"), the conference assistant who transcribed the recordings and is largely erased from the record she produced — "the outside observer who watches a system that she constructs through the marks she makes on paper." Freed becomes Hayles's living proof that "information is never disembodied, that messages don't flow by themselves." ## Important Definitions

- [[Selective vs Structural Information (MacKay)]] — selective information (measured in "metrons") concerns the selection of message elements; structural information (measured in "logons") is "a message about how to interpret a message—that is, it is a metacommunication."
- [[Signal vs Sign (McCulloch)]] — a signal "always implies its occasion"; a sign is "an enduring affair which has lost its essential temporal reference" — McCulloch's distinction to handle hallucination and phantom-limb causalgia via reverberating loops.
