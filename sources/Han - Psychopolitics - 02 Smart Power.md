# Source Summary: 2. Smart Power

**Book:** [[Psychopolitics]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/02 Smart Power]]

## Orientation

This short chapter is Han's compressed phenomenology of power under neoliberalism, and it supplies the book's titular concept in nuce. It opens with a counter-intuitive thesis about power as such: the highest form of power is not violence or coercion but the kind that works invisibly and "quietly," without drawing attention to itself — power that need not say "no." Violence, for Han, betrays weakness rather than strength, because the very formation of an opposing will already attests to the power-holder's failure. From this premise Han maps two regimes. *Disciplinary power* is "negative" — inhibitive, prohibitive, "commanded by negativity," and therefore inefficient, since it expends energy forcing people "into the straightjacket of commandments and prohibitions." The *neoliberal regime*, by contrast, "beams forth in positivity": its technology of power is "subtle, supple and smart," escapes all visibility, and leaves the subjugated subject unaware of its own subjugation, so that "the subject thinks itself free."

The chapter's core construct is **smart, friendly, permissive power**. Rather than repressing, this power "activate[s], motivate[s] and optimize[s]"; it pleases and fulfils rather than forbidding and depriving; it makes people *dependent* rather than *compliant*; it says "yes" more often than "no"; it "operates seductively, not repressively"; it "meets the subject halfway." Crucially, smart power "cosies up to the psyche rather than disciplining it" — the proto-statement of [[Psychopolitics]] as power that targets and inhabits the psyche. Han ties this to the digital surface of contemporary life: smart power "is constantly calling on us to confide, share and participate," it "reads and appraises our conscious and unconscious thoughts," and it "places its stock in voluntary self-organization and self-optimization." Its emblem is the **Like button**: "Neoliberalism is the capitalism of 'Like.'"

Rhetorically the chapter is a series of sharp antitheses — negativity against positivity, prohibition against permission, repression against seduction, compliance against dependence, visibility against invisibility — that converge on Han's signature reversal of the [[Exploitation of Freedom]]: today's power "does not negate or repress freedom so much as exploit it." The reversal is sealed in a lexical hinge, free choice (*Wahl*) collapsing into a mere free selection (*Auswahl*) from items on offer, and in the closing borrowed "warning label" for the capitalism of Like, "Protect me from what I want." This is the chapter that turns the diagnostic apparatus of *The Transparency Society* into a theory of power proper, and that hands [[Society of Control]] its affective texture before the next chapter ("The Mole and the Snake") supplies the Deleuzian diagram.

## Key Concepts

- [[Smart Power]] — the dominant neoliberal technology of power: permissive, seductive, pleasing, psyche-targeting, and most efficient when invisible; it "guides their will to its own benefit" and "meets the subject halfway."
- [[Psychopolitics]] — the book's master concept appears here in its first concrete instantiation: power that "cosies up to the psyche" instead of disciplining the body.
- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — Han's reversal of the crisis of freedom: power no longer represses freedom but exploits it, so the subject's own freedom becomes the medium of domination.
- [[Positivity]] — the neoliberal regime "beams forth in positivity"; smart power is the technology proper to that radiance.
- [[Negativity]] — the modality of disciplinary power, which is "commanded by negativity," "inhibitive, not permissive."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — smart power "places its stock in voluntary self-organization and self-optimization," so subjects exploit themselves.
- [[Achievement Society]] — the self-optimizing, self-subordinating subject smart power produces.
- [[Society of Control]] — smart/positive power is the affective texture of post-disciplinary control; the Deleuzian diagram is named in the next chapter.
- [[Smoothness]] — smart power's "subtle, supple" and "friendly" character is the political register of Han's aesthetics of the smooth.
- [[Arcana of Power]] — Han's "invisible power" here is the *inverse* of arcane secrecy: power hidden by ubiquity and positivity rather than by concealment. Noted as a contrast, not a merge.

## Key Arguments

- [[Freedom itself is producing coercion]] — neoliberal power does not stand opposed to freedom but exploits it; "presenting itself as freedom," it makes the feeling of freedom the very form of subjugation.
- The highest form of power is invisible, not coercive — power that relies on violence "does not represent power of the highest order"; "the greater power is, the more quietly it works."
- Permissive power is more efficient than disciplinary power — disciplinary power is inefficient because it forces compliance; smart power is efficient because subjects "subordinate themselves to power relations on their own."
- Smart power colonizes the psyche through seduction and self-disclosure — it "does not impose silence" but constantly solicits confession, sharing, and participation.
- Neoliberalism is the capitalism of "Like" — subjugation now occurs "by consuming and communicating," emblematized by the Like button, and is qualitatively distinct from disciplinary nineteenth-century capitalism.

## Important Definitions

- [[Smart Power (Han)]] — "Smart power with a liberal, friendly appearance - power that stimulates and seduces - is more compelling than power that imposes, threatens and decrees."
- [[Psychopolitics (Han)]] — the chapter supplies psychopolitics' founding gesture: power that "cosies up to the psyche rather than disciplining it through coercion or prohibitions."
- Free choice (*Wahl*) vs. free selection (*Auswahl*) — freedom redefined downward from authoring options to picking among supplied ones: "Free choice {Wahl) is eliminated to make way for a free selection (Auswahl) from among the items on offer."

- [[Friendly Big Brother]] — the chapter's "smart and friendly" power anticipates the friendly-faced surveillor of Ch. 8; affirmation ("yes") as the form of subjugation.

## Major Tensions

- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the master opposition: disciplinary power "commanded by negativity" vs. the neoliberal regime that "beams forth in positivity."
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — freedom is not the opposite of power but its medium; power "presents itself as freedom."
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — the historical periodization from disciplinary, prohibition-based capitalism to the smart "capitalism of Like."
- [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] — the pivot from a power that disciplines the body to one that "cosies up to the psyche."
- [[Visibility vs Understanding]] — the visibility-of-power problem: the more powerful, the more it "manages not to be seen at all."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A founding text for an essay on how "friendliness," permissiveness, and the affirmative gesture (the Like, the smooth interface, the seamless onboarding flow) operate as techniques of control in contemporary platforms and institutions — power that recruits rather than coerces.
- A lens on curatorial and exhibitionary practice: when participation, "engagement," and the solicitation to "confide, share and participate" become the medium through which audiences and artists subordinate themselves — the gallery or platform as an apparatus that says "yes."
- A bridge piece pairing Han's *Wahl* / *Auswahl* distinction with critiques of choice architecture and recommendation systems: the hollowing of freedom into selection among pre-given options.
- A counterpoint to surveillance-as-attack narratives: smart power does not break the will but guides it, which reframes resistance as the harder problem of refusing what one is made to want ("Protect me from what I want").
