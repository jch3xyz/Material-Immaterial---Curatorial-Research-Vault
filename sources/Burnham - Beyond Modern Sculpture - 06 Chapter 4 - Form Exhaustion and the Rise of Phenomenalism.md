# Source Summary: Chapter 4 — Form Exhaustion and the Rise of Phenomenalism

**Book:** [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]
**Author:** [[Jack Burnham]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/06 Chapter 4 - Form Exhaustion and the Rise of Phenomenalism]]

## Orientation

This is the climactic chapter of Part I ("Sculpture as Object") and the hinge of the whole book. Burnham opens with a "Resume" that restates his master thesis: sculpture has moved from "material idealization of human form in Classical Greece" toward "seeming immateriality," becoming "a continuum of steadily changing ideas about the world" rather than a sequence of styles. He then diagnoses the simultaneous exhaustion of the two governing programs of twentieth-century sculpture — classical/optical [[Formalism (Burnham)]] and [[Vitalism]] — and announces their succession by a new posture he calls [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]]: an art whose subject is the *experience* of perception rather than the form of the object. This is the chapter that names [[Form Exhaustion]] as a positive force pushing "sculpture" out of its own domain.

The middle sections clear the ground polemically. Under "Academicism in Modern Sculpture," Burnham attacks the retroactive erasure of prewar academic realism by "official taste change," and then the postwar mass-produced biomorphic and reductivist academy — a "correspondence school" run by art magazines — whose *reductio ad absurdum* is reached in Sam Goodman's [[The No-Sculptures (Goodman)]]. Under "Pure Form Becomes Pure Experience," he argues that vitalism and classical [[Scientific Idealism]] collapse for the same reason: scientific conceptualization has moved into "a world of invisible values" (wave mechanics, electronic circuitry, statistical formulae) that a "strictly visual-geometrical" art can no longer mirror — the contrast dramatized by [[The Transistor Radio vs the Watch]].

The intellectual core imports phenomenology — Husserl ([[Phenomenological Reduction (Husserl via Burnham)]], absolute consciousness, the "crisis in European science") and especially Merleau-Ponty (perception as an existential bodily act, [[Carnal Intersubjectivity (Merleau-Ponty via Burnham)]], "I can only think it" of the cube) — to theorize the new [[Object Art]] of Judd, Morris, Flavin, Tony Smith, and LeWitt. Object/Minimal sculpture "admits to being itself — nothing else," abandons transcendent imagery, and shifts the burden of "vitalizing" the work onto the viewer's own perceptual self-awareness: it replaces "transcendent idealism" with "transcendent phenomenalism." Burnham closes with a candid caveat that phenomenalism "cannot imbue an object with attributes which are not there." For the vault this chapter is doubly load-bearing: it gives the earliest primary-text articulation of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension as a dialectic internal to the art object (pre-dating Lippard's [[Dematerialization]]), and it stages the [[Idealism vs Phenomenalism]] turn that Part II will reframe as the move toward [[Object vs System]].

## Key Concepts

- [[Form Exhaustion]] — the titular thesis: both optical/classical formalism and vitalism have run out their possibilities, and "invention" has pushed sculpture beyond its own domain.
- [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] — Burnham's art-critical coinage: an art whose subject is the experience of perception; "transcendent phenomenalism" succeeding "transcendent idealism."
- [[Object Art]] — the new a-sculptural "Object" / "Minimal" work that "admits to being itself — nothing else" and presents meaning "as a series of perceptual experiences."
- [[Formalism (Burnham)]] — diagnosed here as exhausted: "the prehistory of scientific technique itself," dying as the scientific world becomes invisible and statistical.
- [[Vitalism]] — the strategy of "essential" spirits / "inner life" in abstract sculpture, "a partial surrogate for traditional religious values"; here recorded at its death.
- [[Scientific Idealism]] — the classical visual-geometrical picture of reality whose decline parallels the decline of formalism in art.
- [[Reification]] — sculpture's history reconceived as "a series of technological reifications," rendering style criticism obsolete.
- [[Anthropomorphism in Sculpture]] — the human-figure ideal sculpture has "forsaken" to become "a continuum of steadily changing ideas about the world."

## Key Arguments

- [[Formalism and vitalism are exhausted because scientific reality has become invisible]] — both were "visual-geometrical" programs and cannot mirror "a world ruled by invisible energy and statistical formulae."
- [[Object art shifts the obligation to vitalize sculpture onto the viewers perception]] — the de-idealized form makes the viewer "reconstruct the sculpture for himself" and analyze "his private methods of seeing."

## Important Definitions

- [[Phenomenological Reduction (Husserl via Burnham)]] — Husserl's method of distilling "pure" conscious experience increment by increment, the philosophical engine behind Object art's turn to perception.
- [[Carnal Intersubjectivity (Merleau-Ponty via Burnham)]] — the relational perceptual field that "arises from the first glance" between viewer and object(s).
- [[Object (Burnham)]] — the object-status of sculpture, here shown to be "deceptive."
- [[Reification (Burnham)]] — Verdinglichung, the "technological reifications" that supersede style.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

## Candidate Essay Uses

- Anchor a "material → immaterial" genealogy: Burnham's 1968 "gravitation toward seeming immateriality" as the sculptural precursor to Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] and Hayles's information-loses-its-body thesis — a pre-Conceptual articulation of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension.
- Trace the object-to-experience pivot (and its sequel, object-to-system): use [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] and [[Object Art]] to argue that Minimalism relocated the work's "life" from the object into the viewer's perception, grounding an essay on art as a vehicle for perceptual experience.
- Read [[The Transistor Radio vs the Watch]] beside McLuhan's mechanical/electric axis to argue that the "invisible values" of the electronic order dissolved the visual-geometrical basis of both science and art.
- Use Goodman's [[The No-Sculptures (Goodman)]] and the "correspondence-school" academy as a case study in how an avant-garde vocabulary reaches its *reductio ad absurdum* and how "instantaneous communication" turns any school into a signal for revolution.
