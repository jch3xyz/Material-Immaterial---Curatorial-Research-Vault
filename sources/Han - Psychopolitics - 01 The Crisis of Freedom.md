# Source Summary: Chapter 1 — The Crisis of Freedom

**Book:** [[Psychopolitics]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/01 The Crisis of Freedom]]

## Orientation

This is the opening chapter of [[Psychopolitics]] and Han's foundational statement of the book's thesis: that freedom itself has entered into crisis because, under neoliberalism, freedom has become the very vector of coercion. The chapter unfolds across three named sections — "The Exploitation of Freedom," "The Dictatorship of Capital," and "The Dictatorship of Transparency" — each tightening the same argument from a different angle. Its rhetorical posture is diagnostic and polemical: Han writes in aphoristic, declarative bursts, mobilizes Marx both as ally and as target, and closes each section with a theological or liturgical figure (Capital as God, the smartphone as rosary) that converts the economic argument into a claim about devotion and submission.

In "The Exploitation of Freedom," Han reframes the subject as the *project* — a self that believes it has shed external constraint but has instead internalized constraint as "compulsive achievement and optimization." The freedom of *Can* is shown to be more coercive than the disciplinary *Should* precisely because *Can* has no limit. The achievement-subject who "willingly exploits itself without a master" is "an absolute slave." Han inverts Hegel's master–slave dialectic — the slave's labour does not free him, and the slave now forces the master to work too ("the totalization of labour") — and recovers an etymological, relational concept of freedom ("'Freedom' and 'friendship' have the same root in Indo-European languages") that neoliberal isolation destroys. Neoliberalism is named as "a highly efficient, indeed an intelligent, system for exploiting freedom," because "Only when freedom is exploited are returns maximized." Marx is then turned against individual freedom: free competition is capital's "real conduct… as capital," so "individual freedom amounts to servitude" and individuals "degrade into the genital organs of Capital."

In "The Dictatorship of Capital," Han argues against Marx and Negri that the contradiction between forces and relations of production admits "no dialectical *Aufhebung*"; capitalism "can always escape into the future" and has mutated into "neoliberalism and financial capitalism" with their "immaterial mode of production." Neoliberalism, not revolution, abolishes the working class — by turning workers into entrepreneurs, "master and slave in one," practising [[Auto-Exploitation]] voluntarily. With no proletariat and no class antagonism, the system is stable; class struggle becomes "an inner struggle against oneself," and the failed subject turns aggression inward, producing "not… revolution so much as depression." The section ends in the theological register: via Benjamin's "Capitalism as Religion," Capital is a new transcendence, a new God before whom we are debtors and guilty — a "cult that creates guilt, not atonement." In "The Dictatorship of Transparency," Han recapitulates and extends the digital-panopticon argument of [[The Transparency Society]]: the early internet's promise of boundless liberty flipped into "total control and surveillance"; [[Transparency]] is exposed as "a neoliberal dispositive" that exteriorizes persons and produces conformity; neoliberalism makes citizens into consumers, yielding a "spectator democracy." The chapter culminates in the announcement of "the age of digital psychopolitics" — the move "from passive surveillance to active steering" via [[Big Data]] — and a closing riff figuring the smartphone as devotional object, *Like* as "the digital Amen," and Facebook as "the church."

This chapter is the seam between [[The Transparency Society]] (already ingested) and [[Psychopolitics]]: it carries forward [[Auto-Exploitation]], [[Achievement Society]], [[Digital Panopticon]], [[Transparency]], [[Exploitation of Freedom]], and the [[Freedom vs Control]] tension, while introducing the book's signature new concepts — [[Psychopolitics]] itself, the [[The Project (Han)|project]] subject, the [[Can vs Should]] opposition, [[Capital as Religion]], and [[Big Data]] / [[Dataism]].

## Key Concepts

- [[Psychopolitics]] — the book's titular concept, named here at the chapter's end: a regime that no longer merely surveils bodies but steers the psyche and pre-empts free will, "passing from passive surveillance to active steering"
- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — neoliberalism as an intelligent system that exploits freedom rather than repressing it, because coercing people against their will "yields scant returns"
- [[The Project (Han)]] — the contemporary self that deems itself a free, self-refashioning "project" yet thereby installs "a more efficient kind of subjectivation and subjugation"
- [[Can vs Should]] — the formal core: the disciplinary *Should* "has a limit," whereas the neoliberal *Can* "has none," so its compulsion is unlimited
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the achievement-subject as "master and slave in one," exploiting itself voluntarily without a master
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — its foil: exploitation against one's will, which "yields scant returns" but still permits solidarity and revolt
- [[Achievement Society]] — the achievement-subject who "deems itself free" but "in reality… is a slave… an absolute slave"
- [[Immaterial Labour]] — the post-industrial, financialized "immaterial mode of production" under which "everyone already owns the means of production him- or herself," dissolving the proletariat
- [[Dictatorship of Capital]] — Han's replacement for the "dictatorship of the proletariat": classless auto-exploitation means "the Dictatorship of Capital rules over everyone"
- [[Capital as Religion]] — Capital as "a new kind of transcendence," a "new God, making us guilty and debt-ridden," via Benjamin a "cult that creates guilt, not atonement"
- [[Transparency]] — re-described as "a neoliberal dispositive" that turns "everything inside out by force," exteriorizing persons and producing conformity
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — the networked panopticon whose inmates "actively communicate" and "willingly expose themselves," so "Digital Big Brother outsources operations to inmates"
- [[Society of Control]] — "Digital control society makes intensive use of freedom"
- [[Big Data]] — the psychopolitical instrument that makes the future "calculable and controllable" and "has announced the end of the person who possesses free will"
- [[Dataism]] — the data-positivism this chapter seeds: persons "positivized into things, which can be quantified, measured and steered"
- [[Positivity]] — the positivization of "the negativity of freely made decisions into the positivity of factual states"
- [[Negativity]] — what is dismantled: "the negativity of freely made decisions"; "the negativity of otherness or foreignness"
- [[Smoothness]] — "Communication goes faster when it is smoothed out — that is, when thresholds, walls and gaps are removed"
- [[Information]] — "Information represents a positive value; inasmuch as it lacks interiority, it can circulate independently, free from any and all context"
- [[Self-Optimization]] — internal self-constraint "taking the form of compulsive achievement and optimization"

## Key Arguments

- [[Freedom itself is producing coercion]] — freedom, defined as the absence of constraint, has "switched over" into compulsion; depression and burnout are its pathological symptoms (`Directly stated`).
- The freedom of *Can* is more coercive than the disciplinary *Should* — *Should* "has a limit," *Can* "has none," so its compulsion is "unlimited" (`Directly stated`). See [[Can vs Should]].
- The auto-exploiting subject is "an absolute slave" — it works without a master yet absolutizes bare life and labour; Han inverts Hegel so the slave now "force[s] the master to work too" (`Directly stated`).
- Neoliberalism exploits freedom because coercion is inefficient — "Only when freedom is exploited are returns maximized" (`Directly stated`).
- Individual freedom is a ruse of Capital — in free competition "it is, rather, capital which is set free"; individuals "degrade into the genital organs of Capital" (`Directly stated`, via Marx).
- Capitalism escapes into the future instead of resolving into communism — the contradiction admits "no dialectical *Aufhebung*" (`Directly stated`; against Marx).
- [[Auto-Exploitation|Neoliberalism abolishes the proletariat so no revolution is possible]] — under immaterial production there is "no proletariat… at all," class antagonism dissolves, and "no political We is even possible"; Negri's "Multitude" is "yet another illusion" (`Directly stated`; against Marx and Negri).
- Auto-exploitation produces depression, not revolution — "auto-aggressivity means that the exploited are not inclined to revolution so much as depression" (`Directly stated`).
- [[Capital as Religion|Capital is a new transcendence that rules through debt and guilt]] — perpetual debt (*Schuld* = guilt) lets us avoid the burden of being free; capitalism is "a cult that creates guilt, not atonement" (`Directly stated`, via Benjamin).
- [[The digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure]] — its inmates "collaborate," data is "offered out of an inner need," and "Digital Big Brother outsources operations to inmates" (`Directly stated`).
- [[Transparency is a neoliberal dispositive that dismantles negativity]] — transparency "turn[s] everything inside out by force," exteriorizing persons to accelerate information (`Directly stated`).
- [[Transparency abolishes the Other and produces compulsive conformity]] — "total networking… already has a levelling effect"; "primary, intrinsic surveillance proves much more problematic than the secondary, extrinsic surveillance" (`Directly stated`).
- [[Compulsive transparency stabilizes the existing system|Neoliberalism turns citizens into consumers]] — the consumer-citizen "react[s] only passively," yielding a "spectator democracy" (`Directly stated`).
- [[Prediction enacts the very fate it foresees|Big Data ends the free-willed person]] — prediction makes the future "calculable and controllable," "positiviz[ing]" persons into steerable things (`Directly stated`).

## Important Definitions

- Subject (etymological) — "Such is the destiny of the subject; literally, the 'one who has been cast down'."
- [[The Project (Han)|The project]] — the self "as a project deeming itself free of external and alien limitations," that "is now subjugating itself to internal limitations and self-constraints, which are taking the form of compulsive achievement and optimization."
- Freedom (relational) — "Fundamentally, freedom signifies a relationship. A real feeling of freedom occurs only in a fruitful relationship - when being with others brings happiness."
- Proletarian (etymological) — "Literally, 'proletarian' means someone whose sole possessions are his or her children: self-production is restricted to biological reproduction."
- [[Transparency (Han)|Transparency (as dispositive)]] — "It means turning everything inside out by force and transforming it into information."
- [[Psychopolitics (Han)|Digital psychopolitics]] — "passing from passive surveillance to active steering," using Big Data as "a highly efficient psychopolitical instrument" that yields "knowledge for the sake of domination and control (Herrschaftswissen)."
- Devotion — "Devotion and related words mean 'submission', or 'obedience'."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The project (*Projekt*) — the self figured as a refashionable design that carries the affect of freedom while installing self-constraint. See [[The Project (Han)]].
- The genital organs / copulation of Capital — "individuals degrade into the genital organs of Capital"; Capital "copulates with the Other of itself by way of individual freedom."
- Master and slave in one — the neoliberal subject as both Hegelian poles fused, so "class struggle has transformed into an inner struggle against oneself." See [[Subject vs Project]].
- Capital as God — "Could it be that Capital is a new God, making us guilty and debt-ridden again?" See [[Capital as Religion]].
- [[Friendly Big Brother|Digital Big Brother]] — the Orwellian surveillant reconfigured as one that "outsources operations to inmates."
- The smartphone as rosary / mobile confessional — "the smartphone works like a rosary"; it is also "a mobile confessional."
- [[The Like Button]] — "Like is the digital Amen. When we click Like, we are bowing down to the order of domination."
- [[The Panopticon]] — social media as "digital panoptica keeping watch over the social realm."
- Bare life — "Health represents the ideal of bare life"; the neoliberal slave "worries only about bare life."

## Major Tensions

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the chapter's master tension: "freedom itself, which is supposed to be the opposite of constraint, is producing coercion."
- [[Can vs Should]] — limitless neoliberal possibility vs. limited disciplinary obligation.
- [[Subject vs Project]] — the cast-down subject vs. the self-fashioning project that re-subjugates itself.
- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — freely made decisions (negativity) converted into factual states (positivity).
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — industrial/material vs. post-industrial/immaterial production that dissolves the proletariat.
- [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] — the surveillance of bodies vs. the steering of the psyche that this chapter announces.
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — the disciplinary panopticon of duress vs. the digital panopticon of voluntary self-exposure.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- An essay on surveillance and consent in participatory/relational art could use Han's claim that "data is not surrendered under duress so much as offered out of an inner need" to read audience-data capture in interactive installations as voluntary self-illumination rather than coercion — pairing the [[Digital Panopticon]] with Hayles on [[The Body as Data]] and the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension.
- An essay on labour, optimization, and the artist-as-entrepreneur could braid the [[The Project (Han)|project]] subject and [[Can vs Should]] with [[Achievement Society]] and [[Auto-Exploitation]] to critique the residency/portfolio economy in which the artist "willingly exploits itself without a master."
- An essay on the immaterial and the dematerialized could set Han's "immaterial mode of production" ([[Immaterial Labour]]) against Lippard's dematerialization (via [[Material vs Immaterial]]), distinguishing Han's productivist sense of the immaterial from the conceptual-art sense.
- An essay on the theology of the digital could deploy [[Capital as Religion]], the smartphone-as-rosary, and "Like is the digital Amen" alongside O'Gieblyn on re-enchantment and the algorithm-as-providence to read platform devotion as a cult that "creates guilt, not atonement."
