## Orientation

This chapter (Ch. 7) reads Philip K. Dick's mid-sixties novels — *We Can Build You*, *The Simulacra*, *Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?*, *The Three Stigmata of Palmer Eldritch*, *Dr. Bloodmoney*, and *Ubik* — as literature that enacts "the progressively deeper penetration of cybernetic technologies into the fabric of the world." Hayles uses Dick to extend the book's argument that once system boundaries are defined by information flows and feedback loops rather than epidermal surfaces, the subject ceases to be an organic whole and becomes "a system to be assembled and disassembled." Dick's recurring figure for this anxiety is the android, which is "consistently" associated with "unstable boundaries between self and world." The chapter's rhetorical posture is sympathetic and diagnostic: Hayles treats Dick not as a thinker to be corrected but as a fiction-maker whose obsessions stage cybernetic problems more vividly than theory can.

The chapter's organizing claim is that Dick performs **boundary work** — the labor of constituting an "inside" and an "outside" — and that he runs in parallel to the second-wave cybernetics of [[Autopoiesis]]. Hayles is explicit that this is "almost certainly not a case of direct influence": Dick and the creators of autopoiesis (Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela) were independently "responding to the problem of incorporating the observer into the system." Where Maturana and Varela stabilize their system by locating boundary-formation in reality and by assuming a rational observer, Dick assumes no such restraint. The crucial divergence is embodiment: unlike Varela especially, Dick "leaps over the importance of embodiment," and "without embodiment to stabilize his epistemological skepticism, the cracks he opens in the perceiver's knowledge widen into rifts in the fabric of the world." This is the chapter's load-bearing thesis and its tightest tie to the book's central argument about [[Embodiment vs Information]].

Around this spine, Hayles weaves three further strands. First, a **gendered reading**: the "schizoid android" is "typically gendered female," the bright, cold, unempathic "dark-haired girl" whom Dick traces back to his mother Dorothy and his dead twin sister Jane, and whose proximity destabilizes the male subject's boundaries to the point of psychosis. Second, a **political-economic reading** drawn through Carl Freedman, Scott Durham, and Fredric Jameson: the android stages **reification** and **incorporation**, "the projection of social relations onto the relations of objects," in landscapes where market relations have made the boundary between person and product permeable. Third, the late **Exegesis** material: after the 2-3-74 visions Dick "constructs an outside, authorized with the name of God and made invulnerable by continuing to infinity," contrasted against the more modest, embodied accommodation that ends *Do Androids Dream*. The chapter closes by preferring the modest ending — "tolerance and affection for the creatures, biological and mechanical, with whom they share the planet" — as a fitting figure for the ethical problems second-wave cybernetics raised but did not solve.

## Key Concepts

- [[The Schizoid Android]] — Dick's central figure, a "multiple pun" fusing a person who acts like a machine with the literal android, splitting "into the two opposed and mutually exclusive subject positions of the human and the not-human"
- [[Boundary Work]] — the constitution and defense of an "inside" versus an "outside"; "a boundary dispute in which one tries to claim the privileged 'outside' position"
- [[The Erasure of Embodiment]] — Dick "leaps over the importance of embodiment," which is precisely what lets epistemological skepticism unravel reality itself
- [[Autopoiesis]] — Maturana and Varela's self-producing system, to which Dick runs parallel without influence
- [[Reification]] — "the projection of social relations onto the relations of objects"; the android stages it pointing "painfully in both directions"
- [[Incorporation]] — market capitalism that "incorporates living beings by turning humans into objects at the same time that it engineers objects so that they behave like humans"
- [[The Tomb World]] — the entropic landscape "where entropy rules," where time stops and inside/outside merge
- [[Cybernetics]] — the scientific paradigm whose deeper penetration Dick's fiction enacts
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the information dynamics that redefine system boundaries away from "epidermal surfaces"
- [[Systems Theory]] — the frame within which Dick, "like Maturana and Varela, is a system builder"

- [[The Rational Observer vs the Psychotic Observer]] — Maturana and Varela "assume rational observers"; Dick assumes minds whose stability "cannot be assumed"
