# Source Summary: The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-Biological Era

## Orientation

This 1994 essay (originally delivered at ISEA94 in Helsinki, revised for *Artlink* in 1996) is Ascott's manifesto for a model of art education fit for what he names the post-biological era. Its rhetorical posture is prophetic and polemical: traditional art education, built on the material object — the canvas-on-its-easel, the atelier, the demographically convenient 18-to-23 age group — is in "terminal decline," because the nature of art itself has changed. Art is now "invisible," "transformative," distributed, interactive, and emergent. Ascott reaches not to art theory but to the natural sciences for his model, taking the Artificial Life program's principle of bottom-up, distributed, local determination of behaviour (quoted at length from Chris Langton) and transferring it wholesale onto networked art. The argument that follows is an analogy stretched into a program: if telematic art is itself bottom-up and emergent, then its pedagogy must be rebuilt from the bottom up to match.

The essay is the vault's primary-text anchor for several of Ascott's signature terms. It crystallizes the load-bearing reformulation that "art as the behaviour of forms has become art as a form of behaviour," lists [[The Five Defining Features]] of telematic art (connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, emergence), and names [[The Planetary Collegium]] as the non-hierarchical, non-linear, intrinsically interactive learning organism already taking root within the Internet — "a paradigm of the twenty-first-century interversity." It introduces four "new ways of being and doing" — [[Telenoia]], [[Cyber-estate]], [[Inter-Reality]], and [[Cyberception]] — each defined crisply (and each capped with Ascott's punning negation: "Not paranoia!", "Not real estate!", "Not phoney reality!", "Not natural deception!"). It closes with the apparition/appearance reversal and the post-biological condition of "bionic evolution."

For the vault this chapter is the manifesto pole of Ascott's corpus and the explicit material→immaterial spine: "the aesthetic of the material object" gives way first to "electronic flow" and then to "the metaphor of emergence." It directly extends the Burnham-realized committed stubs [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]], supplies the participatory/emergent counter-pole to Burnham's object→system systems-esthetics ([[Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham]]), and positions the planetary network as the inheritor of McLuhan's global village ([[Roy Ascott and Marshall McLuhan]]). It also carries a sharp market-capture warning — if artists do not seize digital education, "the politicians, merchants, and entrepreneurs will" — that shadows the chapter's network optimism with the fear of corporate enclosure.

## Key Arguments

- [[Traditional object-centred art education is in terminal decline]] — the object-centred atelier cannot survive because art has become invisible, distributed, interactive, and emergent.
- [[Telematic art is emergent and bottom-up like artificial life so education must be rebuilt from the bottom up]] — Langton's AL definition transferred onto networked art, making top-down pedagogy obsolete.
- [[Art as the behaviour of forms has become art as a form of behaviour]] — interaction is the very form of art; the locus of the work shifts from the object to the participant's behaviour.
- [[The networked Planetary Collegium must succeed the university]] — a non-hierarchical learning organism is already arising bottom-up within the Internet and cannot be planned top-down.
- [[The creative potential of telematic networks depends on artists resisting corporate and military colonisation]] — if poets, artists, and musicians do not seize the digitalisation of culture and education, the politicians, merchants, and entrepreneurs will.
- [[Western art's preoccupation with appearance is dying]] — apparition (coming-into-being) is replacing appearance (representation) as the defining quality of Western art.

- "Nomads of the Internet, navigators of the data seas" — the new extended learning public, no longer bound to a fixed academy.
- The book as "the embodiment of authority," "fixed and frozen," against fluid interactive media.
- The metaphor of emergence replacing the metaphor of electronic flow (which had replaced the material object) — the three-stage genealogy of art's defining metaphor.
- The "minority group, a terrorist group even" — Ascott's self-description of the digital-art vanguard.
- "A computer that can't feel can't think" — Gelernter's spiritual/emotional computer.
