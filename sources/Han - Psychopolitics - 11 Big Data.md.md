# Source Summary: 11. Big Data

**Book:** [[Psychopolitics]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/11 Big Data]]

## Orientation

Chapter 11 is the longest and most pivotal chapter of *Psychopolitics*: Han's full theory of [[Big Data]] as the instrument of digital [[Psychopolitics]]. He opens with Bentham's "Columbus and his egg" to ask whether Big Data is the panopticon's successor, and answers that it is far more. Where Bentham's panopticon was *perspectival* — it left blind spots in which prisoners "could indulge in secret wishes and thoughts without being observed" — digital surveillance is *aperspectival*: it eliminates blind spots and "can peer into the human soul itself." This marks the threshold at which power passes from watching behaviour to steering the psyche, the [[Digital Panopticon]]'s aperspectival update.

The chapter's rhetorical posture is diagnostic and polemical. Han diagnoses [[Dataism]] (David Brooks's coinage, riding Chris Anderson's "The End of Theory") as a "second Enlightenment" and a new faith that, by a "fatal dialectic," repeats the first Enlightenment's collapse into barbarism — the "barbarism of data," transparency as "false clarity," "another name for myth." Dataism is "digital Dadaism" and "nihilism," because data are *additive*, not narrative: "counting is not recounting," the wordplay around which the whole chapter turns ([[Counting vs Recounting]], the [[Psychopolitics]] sibling of [[Narration vs Addition]]). The argument then proceeds through a sequence of sub-arguments — Quantified Self, Life Logged in Full, micro-targeting, the digital unconscious, Big Business / the ban-opticon, Forgetting, Spirit, and the Event — each tightening the case that data-driven knowledge evacuates meaning, [[Narrativity]], and Hegelian Spirit while extending surveillance into the unconscious.

The chapter is the hinge where Han's transparency-society thesis becomes an explicit *psychopolitics*: a surveillance that reaches the unconscious, monetizes the person (Big Brother allied with Big Business; the "ban-opticon" sorting humans into "waste"), and forecloses freedom by predicting and pre-empting the will — "even faster than free will." It closes on the statistical genealogy of Big Data (Arbuthnot, Kant, Rousseau) and the Nietzschean revolt against statistical sameness (*Zunehmen im Gleichwerden*), to argue that Big Data, calculating only averages, is "wholly blind to the event" and therefore "blind to the future too."

## Key Concepts

- [[Big Data]] — the central instrument of digital psychopolitics: an aperspectival, never-forgetting surveillance-and-analytic apparatus that draws a psychogram of the unconscious, predicts and pre-empts behaviour, and is additive rather than narrative.
- [[Dataism]] — the "second Enlightenment" faith that everything measurable must be measured and that data is a "transparent and reliable lens"; Han unmasks it as ideology, "data totalitarianism," and nihilism.
- [[Psychopolitics]] — neoliberal power that intervenes in the psyche rather than the body; micro-targeting is "the practical microphysics of power… data-driven psychopolitics."
- [[Biopolitics]] — the prior, demographic-statistical technology (the census) that "is incapable of enabling subtle interventions in the psyche"; Big Data crosses the limit biopolitics cannot.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — updated here as *aperspectival*: it eliminates blind spots and, unlike the forgetful Big Brother, "never forgets anything at all."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the self-tracking "entrepreneur of the self" who "carries around its own labour camp," is "perpetrator and victim," and becomes "a panopticon of itself."
- [[Society of Control]] — the political form whose completion ("digital control society") Web 3.0, the internet of things, brings about.
- [[Narrativity]] — the pole Big Data cannot supply; "meaning… is based on narration," and "counting is not recounting."
- [[The Event]] — the singular, improbable rupture that "will shape history" but to which statistical Big Data has "no access."
- [[Gleichschaltung]] — the homogenizing tendency Big Data augments: Nietzsche's *Zunehmen im Gleichwerden*, "hypertrophied sameness."
- [[Hell of the Same]] — transparency and data's "compulsion to conform, which eliminates the Other, the Alien and the Deviant."

## Key Arguments

- [[Big Data is blind to the event and so blind to the future]] — because data-mining is statistics and calculates only averages, it "has no access to what is unique"; the unlikely singular shapes history, so the predictive instrument is blind to precisely what prediction would most need to see.

## Important Definitions

- [[Dataism (Han)]] — the new "faith"/"philosophy" that everything measurable must be measured and that data is a transparent lens; Han's gloss: "dataism itself is an ideology… leading to digital totalitarianism" and "Dataism is nihilism."
- [[Psychopolitics (Han)]] — the book's master concept made operational here: power that intervenes in psychic processes prospectively, "even faster than free will," via micro-targeting and the digital unconscious.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- Columbus and his egg — Bentham's figure for the panopticon as a deceptively simple, world-changing invention, transferred by Han to Big Data ("Will Big Data also prove to be Columbus's egg…").
- The 360-degree / wraparound view — Acxiom's "360-degree customer view"; the digital panopticon with no blind spots.
- Big Data as a movie camera / digital magnifying glass — the optical-unconscious analogy by which data-mining discloses the "digital unconscious."
- The ban-opticon — the exclusionary correlate of the panopticon, sorting the economically valueless human as "waste," "trash," bound "for transportation to a refuse tip."
- The undead — digital memory as "indifferent — as it were, undead — points of presence"; "digital temporality belongs to the undead."
- The digitus as phallus — the libidinal/pornographic figure of dataism: "Dataists mate with their data."

## Major Tensions

- [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] — power over the body/population (census, demography) versus power over the psyche (micro-targeting, prediction); the book's master tension, made explicit here.
- [[Counting vs Recounting]] — the additive operation that produces data versus the narrative operation that produces meaning, self, and memory: "Counting is not recounting."
- [[Narration vs Addition]] — the parent temporal-aesthetic opposition: narrative time versus the additive accumulation of data that "never comes to an end, to a conclusion."
- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the loss of the blind spot, the opaque, the negative: aperspectival surveillance as pure additive positivity.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A core text for an essay on the *material/immaterial* status of the person under surveillance capitalism: Han's claim that human beings are "treated and traded as packages of data" and "have become a commodity" lets the vault's material–immaterial axis be read as a politics of value, where the "ban-opticon" sorts dematerialized persons into "waste."
- A lens for curatorial practice on the archive-as-database: the [[Counting vs Recounting]] tension names exactly what is lost when an archive becomes a feed and interpretation is replaced by correlation — useful for an essay on what a *narrative* counter-practice to data positivism would look like.
- A bridge to the vault's posthuman cluster (Hayles, O'Gieblyn): Han's "a complete picture of our lives… of our very souls" and Big Data "peer[ing] into the human soul itself" resonate with the reduction of person to data-pattern, a possible interpretive cross-reference to embodiment and [[Narrativity]].
- A pivot for an essay on prediction and freedom: "even faster than free will… this would herald the end of freedom" stages the strongest form of the determinism worry, to be tested against [[The Event]] as the singular blind spot predictive psychopolitics cannot foreclose.
