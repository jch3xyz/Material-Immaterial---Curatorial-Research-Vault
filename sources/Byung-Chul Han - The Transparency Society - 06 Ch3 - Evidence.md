# Source Summary: The Society of Evidence

**Book:** [[The Transparency Society]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/06 Ch3 - Evidence]]

## Orientation

Chapter 3, "The Society of Evidence," argues that the society of transparency is structurally hostile to pleasure. Han stages this through a libidinal-economic objection to transparency: desire requires the negativity of the secret, the veil, and concealment, and so the demand for full disclosure annihilates the *Spiel-Räume* (room for play) in which eros, fantasy, and seduction operate. The chapter's central opposition is the pair Evidence vs Seduction — "Evidence admits deduction, not seduction" — which Han uses to redescribe the contemporary fusion of transparency and pornography: where appearance, mask, and equivocation collapse, what remains is the naked, hyperreal, pornographic image.

Han then widens the argument into a political and theological register. Transparency, he claims, is a condition of symmetry that liquidates the asymmetrical relations in which power, seduction, and strategic play unfold; following Foucault, he treats power not as an evil but as a generative open game whose productivity depends precisely on intransparency and incalculability. Against the Kantian categorical imperative's rigid self-transparency, Han enlists Nietzsche's defense of masks and ruse ("Ruse, better than force") and Augustine's account of figural garb in Scripture, where deliberate obscuration turns reading into hermeneutics as erotics. The chapter closes by binding desire and the holy together as zones that refuse evidence: the Lacanian [[das Ding]] and Agamben's [[Halo (Agamben)|halo]] both name a structural non-transparency without which neither love nor messianic peace is thinkable. "The coming Kingdom of Peace will not be called a society of transparency."

## Key Concepts

- Society of transparency — the social formation Han indicts; here defined by its hostility to pleasure and its symmetry.
- [[Libidinal Economy]] — the economy of desire that transparency is "foreign to"; depends on concealment and deferral.
- [[Porno-Society]] — Han's diagnosis that the transparency society is simultaneously pornographic because evidence is the death of eros.
- Postprivacy — the demand for "unrestricted mutual uncovering" Han treats as detrimental to desire.
- Symmetry — the condition transparency represents; eliminates asymmetric relations including power.
- Asymmetry — the precondition of power, seduction, and strategic play.
- Power as strategic game — Foucault-derived account of power as productive open play, not evil.
- [[Halo (Agamben)|Halo]] — Agamben's name for the minimal peripheral displacement that makes the holy "more brilliant" while keeping it non-transparent.
- [[das Ding]] — Lacan's name for the impenetrable, hidden object of desire that "defies representation."
- [[Anamorphosis]] — Lacan's figure for courtly love's "bent" object that appears only in distorted form.

## Key Arguments

- [[There is no erotics of transparency]] — pleasure depends on the negativity of the secret; transparency annihilates the *Spiel-Räume* of desire.
- Evidence admits deduction, not seduction — seduction works through ambiguous codes, mask, and indirection that evidence forecloses.
- Transparency society is porno-society — straightforwardness equals the end of eros, i.e. pornography; hyperreal media images suffocate fantasy.
- Transparency eliminates asymmetric power — as symmetry, transparency dismantles the asymmetries on which productive power and libidinal strategy depend.
- Hermeneutics becomes erotics through concealment — Augustine's figural garb makes the Word seductive; nakedness flattens it.
- The holy is structurally non-transparent — via Agamben's halo, the Kingdom of Peace is defined by mysterious indistinctness, not transparency.

## Important Definitions

- Evidence (Han) — that which "admits deduction, not seduction"; the regime of unambiguous, naked, fully visible signs.
- Seduction (Han) — art of "detour, digression, and indirection" using "equivocal signs," ambiguous codes, and masks.
- [[Halo (Agamben)]] — a peripheral "glow at the edges" produced by minimal displacement; renders the holy *clarior* without making it transparent.
- [[das Ding]] — the impenetrable object of desire whose hiddenness "prohibits its image to be fashioned."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- Figural garb — Augustine's image of Scripture clothed in obscurity to inflame the "pious inquirer's senses."
- [[The Mask]] — the mask as "protective skin" around the profound spirit; what is most profound "even hates image and parable."
- [[The Veil]] — opposition between the veiled Word (eros) and the "naked" information (flatness, porno).
- The Lady (frouwe) — the courtly-love object as "black hole" around which desire thickens.
- [[Halo (Agamben)]] — peripheral vibration that envelops things in "mysterious radiance."
- Midnight (Nietzsche) — origin of the desire that seeks "eternity," against the noonday of full illumination.

## Major Tensions

- Evidence vs Seduction — the chapter's organizing opposition.
- Transparency vs Eros — pleasure requires the negativity of concealment.
- Symmetry vs Asymmetry — political-libidinal stakes of equality understood as transparency.
- Categorical Imperative vs Ruse — Kantian self-transparency against Nietzschean cunning ("List besser als Gewalt").
- Transparency vs the Holy — the Kingdom of Peace is defined by indistinctness, not visibility.
- Information vs the Word — naked information flattens; figural garb eroticizes.

- An essay on the curatorial stakes of opacity: drawing on Han's evidence/seduction pair to argue that exhibition design that withholds — captions, lighting, sightlines configured as "figural garb" — generates desire that wall-text transparency cannot.
- A cross-reading of Han, Lacan, and Agamben on the negativity of concealment as the shared structural condition of eros, the holy, and the political; useful for a piece contrasting "transparent" public art with works that operate through halo, anamorphosis, or veiling.
