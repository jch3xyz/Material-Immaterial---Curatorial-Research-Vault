# Source Summary: Chapter 12 — Two Species of Power

**Book:** [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]]
**Author:** [[Shoshana Zuboff]]

## Orientation

This is the keystone "power" chapter of Part III, and it performs the book's central theoretical move: naming the new species of power that surveillance capitalism wields. Zuboff calls it instrumentarianism, "the instrumentation and instrumentalization of behavior for the purposes of modification, prediction, monetization, and control." The whole chapter is built as a definition-by-contrast: [[Instrumentarian Power]] is established by saying what it is *not*, namely totalitarianism. Section II is a substantial intellectual history of totalitarianism (Gentile, Mussolini, Stalin, Hitler; the comprehension-failure of contemporaries like Walter Duranty, *Look*, and *Time*; the postwar naming work of Friedrich, Arendt, Conquest, and Lefort) deployed precisely to model a method — totalitarian power "literally defied human comprehension," and the task now is the same labor of naming "a strange form of power unprecedented in the human experience" so that resistance becomes possible. The chief polemical target is the reflexive reach for Orwell's Big Brother and "digital totalitarianism" as today's interpretive lens; Zuboff argues this is "the syndrome of the horseless carriage," attaching new peril to old facts, and that the equation "impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist."

The "opposite horizon" of Section III is the crux. Totalitarianism worked through violence and the engineering of the soul "from the inside out"; instrumentarian power works through [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] and is "profoundly and infinitely indifferent to our meanings and motives." This is Zuboff's signature [[Radical Indifference]], developed here at length: instrumentarian power has no interest in our souls, only that our behavior is accessible to its operations. Sections IV–VI then supply instrumentarianism's intellectual genesis in [[Radical Behaviorism]] — Max Planck's physics → Max Meyer's 1921 *Psychology of the Other-One* → B. F. Skinner. The "Other-One" viewpoint recasts the human as "an organism among organisms," makes [[Freedom Is Ignorance]] the governing axiom, and demands a "technology of behavior."

Section VII stages [[Walden Two and 1984]] — Skinner's 1948 utopia and Orwell's 1949 dystopia — as the figureheads of the two species of power. The chapter closes on its haunting thesis: surveillance capitalism may finally realize Skinner's dream, but with a twist he never foresaw — "The knowledge that now displaces our freedom is proprietary. The knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us." The chapter thus anchors the master opposition [[Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism]] and extends the vault's surveillance/data/determinism cluster — resonant with Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[Society of Control]], Harari's [[Dataism]] ("organisms among organisms"), and O'Gieblyn's [[Algorithmic Determinism]].

## Key Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the new, unprecedented species of power that sustains surveillance capitalism; the instrumentation and instrumentalization of behavior for modification, prediction, monetization, and control. The chapter is its definitional anchor.
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — Zuboff's deliberate echo of Marx; under surveillance capitalism the "means of production" serves the "means of behavioral modification."
- [[Radical Indifference]] — the moral signature of instrumentarian power: no interest in the soul, meaning, or motive, only that behavior is accessible to its operations.
- [[The Other-One]] — Meyer's and Skinner's "viewpoint of observation" that studies the human as an external other, "an organism among organisms," declaring inner life scientifically void.
- [[Radical Behaviorism]] — Skinner's program restricted to observable operant behavior and aimed at a "technology of behavior."
- [[Freedom Is Ignorance]] — the behaviorist axiom inherited from Planck via Meyer: freedom and will are illusions filling a void of ignorance; knowledge and freedom are adversaries.
- [[The Division of Learning in Society]] — the social order of who knows, who decides, who decides who decides; surveillance capitalists are said to have "hijacked" it.
- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] / [[Guaranteed Outcomes]] — the market telos: predictions grow valuable as they approach certainty; machine processes replace human relationships so that certainty can replace trust.
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] · [[Behavioral Surplus]] · [[Rendition]] · [[Big Other]] — the book-spanning concepts (defined in earlier chapters) that Section I recapitulates as the assembly instrumentarian power animates.

## Key Arguments

- [[Instrumentarianism is a new species of power not a form of totalitarianism]] — conflating the two cripples resistance; the "horseless carriage" error.
- [[Naming an unprecedented power is the first act of resistance]] — totalitarianism also "defied human comprehension," so the first task is the work of naming.
- [[Surveillance capitalism may realize Skinners behavioral utopia but the knowledge is proprietary]] — the closing reversal: Skinner's dream realized, but the displacing knowledge is theirs and the lost freedom ours.

## Important Definitions

- [[Instrumentarian Power (Zuboff)]] — the instrumentation and instrumentalization of behavior; "instrumentation" = the puppet (the connected material architecture of sensate computation), "instrumentalization" = the puppet masters' social relations.
- [[Radical Indifference (Zuboff)]] — indifference to meaning and motive; concern only with accessible, measurable action.
- [[The Other-One (Meyer and Skinner via Zuboff)]] — the viewpoint that considers only objectively observable behavior of one person in relation to prior environmental history.
