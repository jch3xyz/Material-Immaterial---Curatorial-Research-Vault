# Source Summary: Foreword — "The Reinvention of Love"

**Book:** [[The Agony of Eros]]

**Foreword by:** Alain Badiou (the quotes below are Badiou's, not Han's)

## Orientation

This chapter is the **foreword to *The Agony of Eros*, written by Alain Badiou**, not by the book's author Byung-Chul Han. It does double duty: it is at once an appreciative chapter-by-chapter map of Han's slim book and a staging of Badiou's own philosophical position *against* it. Every verbatim quotation in this summary is therefore **Badiou's prose** — where Badiou reports Han's own words, that is flagged inline. The book's author remains Han; the durable yield of this chapter is (1) the cross-author edge recorded in [[Byung-Chul Han and Alain Badiou]] and (2) Badiou's signature thesis that love is [[Love as the Scene of the Two]].

Badiou frames the book as a "phenomenology of true love" that diagnoses love as "gravely ill," perhaps already dead, under contemporary individualism and "globalized capitalism." He restates Han's master thesis — that love is not a coexistence pact but "the radical experience... of the existence of the Other" — and that its minimum condition is the courage of self-negation. He then walks the reader through all eight chapters: von Trier's *Melancholia* and the irruption of the wholly [[The Other]]; the critique of Foucault and the appreciation of Levinas and Buber ([[Eros]] as a relation beyond achievement, performance, and "Can"); Hegel and Bataille on love and death; [[Pornography (Han)|pornography]] as the profanation of eros; the "inferno of the same" via Eva Illouz; the [[The Politics of Eros|politics of eros]] via Plato and Badiou's own "Two scene"; and, finally, love as the ground of thought.

The chapter is also polemical. Badiou endorses Han's project but contests one of its demands: he asks whether, "after the death of God," the total self-abolition Han requires is really the only route to alterity, proposing instead "a world beginning with the Two of love." He closes on Rimbaud's word — the "reinvention" of love — the title figure and the "pressing struggle" the book joins. Because the foreword previews every chapter, this file also serves as a book-level map; but the quotable material is Badiou's, and the primary Han text for the concepts named here arrives in chapters 2–8.

## Key Concepts

- [[Eros]] — love as the radical experience of the Other, "beyond achievement, performance, and Can"; the book's master thesis, framed here by Badiou.
- [[The Other]] — the wholly Other whose negativity/[[Atopia|atopia]] "eludes all ability" and is "constitutive of erotic experience"; its irruption is both catastrophe and redemption.
- [[Atopia]] — the placelessness of the Other, the operative term for what escapes all "ability."
- [[Love as the Scene of the Two]] — Badiou's own thesis, adopted by Han as a "basic political matrix": love as a dual perspective, a world of "we two."
- [[Negative Potency]] — "being able not to be able," which Badiou calls "the matrix of the work as a whole": only through this non-ability does the Other appear.
- [[Pornography (Han)]] — pornography as the profanation of eros; capitalism "profanes eros—into porn" by making everything a commodity on display.
- [[Hell of the Same]] — the "inferno of the same": the universe of normalization and capitalization where money levels all difference and "abolishes fantasies of the Other."
- [[The Politics of Eros]] — love as revolutionary yearning for "another kind of society," maintaining "fidelity to what is yet to come."

## Key Arguments

- Love is under threat — "gravely ill," perhaps dead — struck down by individualism, the market, and monetary interest.
- The minimum condition of true love is the courage of self-negation for the sake of discovering the Other.
- Only [[Negative Potency|"being able not to be able"]] lets the Other appear — the negativity of Otherness eludes all "Can" (against Foucault's valorization of ability/performance).
- [[Pornography (Han)|Pornography is the profanation of eros]]: capitalism, knowing no other use for sexuality, exhibits it and degrades the mystery of the Other into "a dull article for consumption."
- Capitalist exclusion derives from the identical, not from difference: money "makes everything the same" and abolishes fantasies of the Other ([[Hell of the Same]]).
- Eros grounds politics — love is a "Two scene," a basic political matrix and the site of revolutionary yearning ([[The Politics of Eros]]).
- [Badiou's counter-thesis] After the death of God, total self-abolition may no longer be the only route to the Other; a "world beginning with the Two of love" is another road (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Alain Badiou]]).

## Important Definitions

- Love (Badiou, framing Han) — not a pact of coexistence but "the radical experience... of the existence of the Other."
- [[Eros]] (Badiou quoting Han) — "a relationship to the Other situated beyond achievement, performance, and Can."
- [[Atopia]] of the Other (Badiou quoting Han) — the negativity of Otherness that "eludes all ability" and constitutes erotic experience.
- Eroticism (Bataille, via Han/Badiou) — "assenting to life up to the point of death."
- Amorous oblativity (Badiou) — "the vanishing of the self in the Other," a self-abolition with a long mystical history (Saint John of the Cross).

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The agony / death of Eros — love in its death-throes; the title figure ("gravely ill").
- The inferno of the same — the contemporary universe leveling all difference (a variant translation of [[Hell of the Same]]).
- The irruption of pure exteriority as catastrophe — the wholly Other breaking in as a disaster that is also "the good fortune of escape... and ultimately shows the way to redemption."
- The reinvention of love (Rimbaud) — the closing figure; love as a struggle to be reinvented.

## Major Tensions

- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — love's "absolute negativity" against "the crass positivity of repetitive, self-serving gratification."
- The Same vs the Other — the "inferno of the same" abolishing the atopic Other ([[Hell of the Same]] vs [[The Other]]).
- Absolute self-abolition (Han) vs the affirmative "Two of love" (Badiou) — the foreword's staged disagreement, carried in [[Byung-Chul Han and Alain Badiou]].
- Ability / "Can" vs powerlessness — Foucault's valorization of performance against the erotic powerlessness of [[Negative Potency|"being able not to be able"]].

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **Love as counter-force to the immaterial economy of the same.** Badiou's framing — love as "the radical experience... of the existence of the Other" set against "globalized capitalism" and money-as-leveler — connects Han's [[Hell of the Same]] to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] spine: what capital abolishes is not matter but difference, the atopic Other.
- **The erotics of powerlessness against the achievement subject.** [[Negative Potency|"Being able not to be able"]] as the "matrix of the work" offers an eros-side counterpart to *The Burnout Society*'s critique of the "Can" — a possible bridge essay on passivity, negativity, and the limits of performance.
- **Pornography, exhibition, and the profanation of the Other.** The claim that "capitalism... profanes eros—into porn" links [[Pornography (Han)]] to Debord's [[Spectacle]] and to [[Exhibition Value]] — an essay on display, transparency, and the disappearance of the erotic veil.
- **The scene of the Two: a politics of difference.** Badiou's [[Love as the Scene of the Two]] and his disagreement with Han over self-abolition could anchor an essay on whether the answer to the "inferno of the same" is the dissolution of the self or the founding of a shared "we two."
