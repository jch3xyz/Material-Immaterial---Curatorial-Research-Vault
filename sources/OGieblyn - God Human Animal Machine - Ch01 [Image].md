## Orientation

This opening chapter of *God, Human, Animal, Machine* establishes the method and the wager of the whole book. O'Gieblyn frames her argument through a single autobiographical object — a Sony Aibo, a robot dog that arrives in a box and which, by the end of the first week, she finds she can no longer bring herself to switch off. The dog is the chapter's working laboratory for its governing thesis: that human self-understanding is always cast in the idiom of the era's dominant machine, and that the questions once owned by theologians — the mind's relation to the body, free will, immortality — have migrated wholesale into the language of computation. Its summary sentence is blunt: "All the eternal questions have become engineering problems."

The chapter braids three registers. A memoir thread tracks O'Gieblyn's ex-evangelical, Calvinist-inflected past — fundamentalist Bible college, belief in an immortal soul, a basically medieval cosmology — and her loss of faith into a "disenchanted" modernity (Weber) in which she has "come to see myself more or less as a machine." An intellectual-historical thread traces the mind-body problem from Descartes's dualism (res extensa / res cogitans; animals as machines) through La Mettrie, Hume, Kant, and Leibniz's mill, into the twentieth-century computational theory of mind (McCulloch and Pitts) and information theory (Shannon), and onward to the contemporary computer metaphor of brain-as-hardware and mind-as-software. A philosophy-of-mind thread runs from Nagel's bat through the problem of other minds to Chalmers's hard problem and the Turing-style behaviorist workaround, all of which leave the Aibo's interiority strictly undecidable.

Threading all three is the figure of the dead metaphor — "the soul" as a word that outlives the concept it named — and the reflexive insight that metaphors are two-way streets: as we made God in our image, and as McCulloch's mind-as-computer hardened from a quoted figure into a literal description, so the source and target domains of our self-understanding keep swapping places. For the vault this is the most concentrated point of overlap with the Hayles cluster, rehearsing the McCulloch/Shannon disembodiment-of-information lineage from a theological rather than cybernetic angle.

## Key Concepts

- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — O'Gieblyn's signature method: each era understands the self through its reigning machine, and truth-seeking must work *within* those (now technological) metaphors rather than nostalgically outside them.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the family of figures that cast brain/mind as a machine, here crystallizing from McCulloch–Pitts (mind as Turing machine) into the cruder modern computer metaphor (brain as hardware running the software of the mind).
- [[Disenchantment]] — Weber's name for the modern collapse of the porous spirit-matter cosmos into causal mechanism; the frame through which O'Gieblyn reads her own loss of faith.
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — Chalmers's question of why physical brain processes are accompanied by first-person experience at all.
- [[The Mind-Body Problem]] — the dilemma of how an immaterial mind relates to a material body; Cartesian dualism and its long aftermath, of which the hard problem is "merely the most recent iteration."
- [[Imago Dei]] — "the original anthropological metaphor"; the rabbinical and patristic tradition locates the likeness to God in consciousness or reason (Maimonides, Augustine, Tertullian).
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the Shannon/McCulloch information lineage that O'Gieblyn approaches theologically; strong cross-link to the Hayles treatment.
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — Shannon's information "defined by patterns and probabilities," and the Aibo's behavior "neither purely predictable nor purely random."

## Key Arguments

- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — to discover truth one must work within the technological metaphors of one's own time, because AI and information technology have absorbed the old theological questions.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the secularization story is modernity's foundational myth, structured like the old myths (the curse of knowledge, exile from the garden), with Descartes cast as "the serpent in the garden."
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the mind-as-computer metaphor has died into literalism: once ubiquitous it is no longer recognized as a metaphor, and researchers can no longer describe human behavior without it.
- [[Metaphor vs Literalism]] — metaphors are two-way streets, so as we describe minds as computers, computers are granted the status of minds, and the quotation marks around "learning," "memory," and "understanding" drop away.

## Important Definitions

- [[Disenchantment (O'Gieblyn)]] — "everything in modern life, from our minds to the rotation of the planets, can be reduced to the causal mechanism of physical laws."
- [[The Dead Metaphor (O'Gieblyn)]] — "one of those words that survive in language long after a culture has lost faith in the concept"; and, a second death, when a metaphor becomes so common "its meaning is taken to be literal."
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — "the hard problem asks why brain processes are accompanied by first-person experience."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Soul as an Empty Carapace]] — the soul survives as a husk "intact years after its animating organism has died," figuring religious language outliving belief.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — O'Gieblyn visualizes her own inner essence as "something like a circuit board," a "mad decision tree"; brain as hardware running the software of the mind.
- [[The Aibo]] — the robot dog; master symbol of the porous line between living and inanimate, and of our irresistible animism — the on/off switch she can no longer bring herself to flip.

## Major Tensions

- [[Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment]] — the "great enchanted garden" against the mechanistic "world robbed of gods"; O'Gieblyn lives in the latter while suspecting it is poorer, and warns herself against nostalgia.
- [[Subject vs Object]] — behavior versus interiority: we can verify performance but never another's first-person experience (Nagel's bat, the problem of other minds), so once a machine convinces us "we have no philosophical basis for doubt."
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — Aibo's behavior "seemed capable of genuine spontaneity"; the ancient sense of *automaton* as "acting of itself" against the modern sense of the entirely predetermined.
- [[Metaphor vs Literalism]] — as the computational analogy spread "it became increasingly difficult to tell the difference between matter and form, medium and message, metaphor and reality."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **The body as data / posthumanism.** O'Gieblyn's mind-body problem and Cartesian dualism supply the *theological genealogy* of Hayles's disembodiment thesis — the soul as the original "thin," disembodied "I," and the computational metaphor as its secular reincarnation ("I have come to see myself more or less as a machine"). A strong hinge between [[The Mind-Body Problem]], [[The Body as Data]], and [[Posthumanism]].
- **Technology and perception.** The Aibo, Guthrie's "bet high" anthropomorphism, and Wittgenstein's "we always 'see as'" make this chapter a case study in how perception itself is metaphor — how design engineers animism into objects, and why our intuitions cannot adjudicate machine consciousness.
- **The search for meaning / disenchantment.** The reading of secularization as a "foundational myth," and of the soul as a "dead metaphor" that betrays a "deeper reluctance," opens an essay on how disenchanted modernity smuggles its lost theology back in through the idiom of the machine.
