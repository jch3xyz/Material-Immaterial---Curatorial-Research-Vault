# Source Summary: Concerning Nets and Spurs: Meaning, Mind, and Telematic Diffusion

## Orientation

This 1985 essay (originally published in *Artificial Intelligence in the Arts*, ed. Richard Kriesche, Graz: Steirischer Herbst, source pages 202–211) is the densest "theory bridge" in Ascott's early telematic writings, and it sits at the hinge between his 1960s cybernetic/behaviourist work and his fuller 1980s telematic period. Its double title encodes two moves. "Nets" — taken from the ninth-century Byzantine scholar Photius's summary of Dionysios of Aegeae's lost book *Concerning Nets* — recovers **weaving / the network** as an ancient metaphor of cosmic creation, and shows that this same metaphor now structures both quantum physics (Capra's "cosmic web," Bohm's "implicate order") and the planetary "mind at large" diffused by computerised telecommunications. "Spurs" — taken from Derrida's *Éperons: Les Styles de Nietzsche* — aligns telematics with the poststructuralist / deconstructive project (Barthes, Foucault, Derrida) as its "necessary (and perhaps inevitable) correlative."

Ascott defines [[Telematic Art|telematics]] not merely as the convergence of computers and telecommunications but as "a whole class of consciousness and culture" in which meaning is "not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined." He argues that telematic networks dissolve the sender/receiver model of information theory into "users," dissolve the single "Author God" into dispersed authorship, and dissolve the discrete object into process and field. The argument is built by laying ancient myth (Kore weaving the universe, the net as instrument of demolition in *Hamlet's Mill*), modern physics (Capra, Bohm, Heisenberg), and poststructuralist literary theory (Barthes's "tissue of quotations," Derrida's *différance* and *Glas*, an extended quotation of Nietzsche's *Twilight of the Idols*) into a single woven case for [[Network Consciousness]].

[[Marcel Duchamp]] is positioned as the seminal precursor: the *3 Stoppages—étalon* and *Réseaux des stoppages*, the liberation of the text in the *Large Glass*, and above all the spectator who "adds to the creative act." The essay closes on Ascott's own first major telematic artwork — *La Plissure du Texte* (1983), a planetary collaborative "fairy tale" woven across eleven cities for the ELECTRA exhibition — as the realisation of Barthes's intertext. The essay is the keystone link between Ascott's telematic theory and both the dematerialization tradition (he names the "Les Immatériaux" exhibition) and the participatory-system lineage the vault has grounded in Burnham's systems esthetics.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Screen of Transformations vs the Mirror of Reflections]] — Ascott's signature figure: telematic process replaces representation/reflection with branching, interactive transformation.
- The Net / Weaving as Cosmic Creation — Kore "weaves out the universe" in "the roaring loom of time"; the net both knits the new order and ensnares and "pull[s] down the structure."
- The Cosmic Web (Capra) — particles as "interconnections in an inseparable cosmic web"; the Avatamsaka Sutra's "perfect network of mutual relations"; *tantra* = "to weave."
- The Dance of Data / the Dance of the Pen — telematic process as "a planetary field for the dance of data," echoing Nietzsche's "a dance of concepts and the pen."
- The Spur / Éperon (the ram) — Derrida's "spurs" as "hooks into images and texts"; the "ram (éperon)" pushed into the cracks of the old order.
- The Two Glasses — Duchamp's *Large Glass* and Derrida's *Glas*, "curiously linked (not causally but associatively)," both bearing "the trace of massive rupture."
- The Net / Knotted Carpet (symbol) — Hamlet throws "the knotted carpet net" over the crowd and burns the hall; Clytemnestra's net over the king (the net as demolition of the old order).
- *La Plissure du Texte* (symbol) — Ascott's 1983 planetary artwork; a woven "textile, which could not be classified."
