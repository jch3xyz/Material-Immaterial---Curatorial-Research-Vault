# Source Summary: Chapter 6 — Media as Translators

## Orientation

This is Chapter 6 of Part I of *Understanding Media* — a short but pivotal theory chapter that fuses McLuhan's two master slogans (media as extensions of man; the content of a medium is always another medium) into a single operation: **translation**. Its governing thesis is that all technologies translate one kind of knowledge into another mode, and that "all media are active metaphors in their power to translate experience into new forms." Mechanization is defined as "a translation of nature, and of our own natures, into amplified and specialized forms," and the spoken word is cast as the first such technology, "the first technology by which man was able to let go of his environment in order to grasp it in a new way." Words are "a technology of explicitness," "complex systems of metaphors and symbols that translate experience into our uttered or outered senses." The chapter thus names the mechanism — metaphor as a carrying-across — by which every medium reprocesses experience.

The chapter's most consequential move for this vault is its escalation of the extension thesis to the **extension of consciousness itself**. "In this electric age we see ourselves being translated more and more into the form of information, moving toward the technological extension of consciousness." Where previous mechanical technologies "were partial and fragmentary," the electric is "total and inclusive": the outering of the central nervous system means that all prior bodily extensions — "including cities" — "will be translated into information systems," and that man now "wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide." McLuhan projects this to its limit: having extended the central nervous system into electromagnetic technology, "it is but a further stage to transfer our consciousness to the computer world as well," and the global translation of "our entire lives into the spiritual form of information" might make "of the entire globe, and of the human family, a single consciousness." This is the chapter's deepest cross-book bridge — a 1964 precursor of the dematerialization of body, mind, and world into information that runs through Hayles, Kurzweil, Harari, and O'Gieblyn.

Rhetorically the chapter is a dense weave of literary citation (Joyce, Shakespeare, Mallarmé, Bacon, Herbert) and offhand analysis (the "rain-check" idiom, the radio-weather joke). Two further durable threads sit alongside the consciousness argument: a **mechanics of metaphor** ("in all metaphors, there are complex ratios among four parts"; "money is also a metaphor"), and an **inter-sensory redefinition of touch and the "common sense"** as the translation of one sense into all the senses — directly the material of the Sense Ratios cluster. The chapter also restates the Narcissus numbness in a translation key (failure to translate experience is Freudian repression, a mechanism that numbs us to media as extensions of ourselves) and closes with a redemptive inversion: a programmed consciousness that "cannot be numbed nor distracted by the Narcissus illusions of the entertainment world."

## Key Concepts

- [[Media as Translators]] — the chapter's title concept: technologies translate "one kind of knowledge into another mode," and all media are active metaphors that carry experience across into a new form. The hinge between the extension thesis and the content-is-another-medium thesis.
- [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]] — the electric age translates us "into the form of information," extending and ultimately transferring consciousness to the computer world; the chapter's most distinctive durable idea.
- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — restated and escalated: previous technologies are "mere extensions of hands and feet and teeth," while electric media put the body inside the extended nervous system, so the organism "wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide."
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the electric outering of the CNS that translates all prior bodily extensions, and cities, into information systems.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — perception as inter-sensory translation: "'touch' is not skin but the interplay of the senses," and the "common sense" is the power of translating one sense into all the senses as "a unified image to the mind."
- [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions]] — man must serve electric technology "with the same servo-mechanistic fidelity with which he served his coracle, his canoe, his typography."
- [[The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium]] — operative here: the spoken word translates "immediate sense experience into vocal symbols," McLuhan's mechanism for content-as-another-medium.
- [[Narcosis]] — restated in a translation register: untranslated experience is repressed, a mechanism that "serves to numb us in the presence of those extensions of ourselves that are the media."
- [[Autoamputation]] — the numbing self-amputation that besets "mankind when he encounters himself extended in his own gimmickry."
- [[Money as a Medium]] — money as a stored, amplified metaphor: "money is also a metaphor."
- [[Immaterial Labour]] — anticipated: under electric technology "all forms of employment become 'paid learning,'" and "all forms of wealth result from the movement of information."

## Key Arguments

- [[All media are active metaphors that translate experience]] — technologies translate experience into new forms exactly as a metaphor does ("for just as a metaphor transforms and transmits experience, so do the media"), supported by the four-part-ratio analysis of the "rain-check" idiom and of money.
- [[Electric technology translates the body and consciousness into information systems]] — the electric extension of the central nervous system is total and inclusive (where the mechanical was partial and fragmentary), translating body, cities, and ultimately consciousness into information, toward "a single consciousness."
