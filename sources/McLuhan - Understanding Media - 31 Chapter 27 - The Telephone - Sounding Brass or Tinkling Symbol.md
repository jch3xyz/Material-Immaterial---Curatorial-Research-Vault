## Orientation

This is a Part II case-study chapter, and one of the more theory-dense of them, because the telephone is McLuhan's purest exemplar of the cool, high-participation, intensely personal electric form — the medium that "demands complete participation, unlike the written and printed page." It re-derives the Part I apparatus almost point for point. The telephone is the "extension of ear and voice that is of extra sensory perception" — a direct application of [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — and belongs to "the second great extension of the central nervous system-electric technology," wedded to the first (the spoken word), the thread carried by [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]]. It is a cool medium of "very poor auditory image" that the user must "strengthen and complete... by the use of all the other senses," and so it "demands participation of our senses and faculties" and "cannot be used as background" like the hot medium radio — the definition-and-participation logic of [[Media Hot and Cold]], re-stated through the can't-visualize-while-phoning experiment and the urge to doodle. And it is an agent of [[Explosion vs Implosion|implosion]], the decentralizing reversal that retribalizes Western man into [[The Global Village]] by playing "the movie of his 2,500-year explosion backward."

The chapter's signature, durably distinctive contributions are organizational and political rather than perceptual. First, the telephone dissolves delegated, hierarchical, "lineal, visual" authority and substitutes [[The Authority of Knowledge]] — "nonlineal, nonvisual, and inclusive" — because instant electric speed bypasses the chain of command and "involve[s] people in depth," introducing a "seamless web" of interlaced patterns in management and decision-making. Second, because in an electric structure there are "no margins," restraint of power can no longer take the Western form of the separation of powers but only [[A Pluralism of Centers]] — "dialogue only among centers and among equals" (illustrated by the Kremlin–White House hot line and Kennedy's preference for teletype). Third, where electric media dissolve the delegated job, [[The Restoration of the Role]] follows: "A person can now be reinvested with all kinds of nonvisual character" — the king/emperor as collective ego, and the modern movie star as a "jobless image" given "a mystic role."

The rhetorical posture is the familiar diagnostic one. McLuhan insists the telephone is organic in design — its receiver "directly modeled on the bone and diaphragm structure of the human ear," with "such natural congruity with the organic" — and that the print man's content-fixation (Bell Labs, the Shannon-Weaver Information Theory, Morgenstern's Game Theory) is "oblivious to the telephone as a form," a direct re-statement of [[The Medium Is the Message]]. He frames the device's invention as "an incident in the larger effort of the past century to render speech visible" (Melville Bell's *Visible Speech*; the Bells' work for the deaf; the Braille and Morse dot-codes), an [[Acoustic vs Visual Space|acoustic/visual]] irony in which a visual ambition produces an audile-tactile medium. The chapter therefore mostly reinforces Part I, while stating three of McLuhan's electric-age political claims more sharply than anywhere else in the case studies.

## Key Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the telephone is "the extension of ear and voice"; its receiver is literally modeled on the human ear; "natural congruity with the organic."
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the telephone as "the second great extension of the central nervous system-electric technology," wedded to the first (the mass media of the spoken word).
- [[Media Hot and Cold]] — the telephone as the model cool, low-definition, high-participation medium: poor auditory image, completed by all the senses, demanding total involvement, never usable as background.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — the senses re-balanced to complete the telephone's poor auditory image (the urge to doodle, the auditory/visual completion rule).
- [[The Authority of Knowledge]] — on the telephone "only the authority of knowledge will work" (nonlineal, nonvisual, inclusive), against delegated authority (lineal, visual, hierarchical); the "seamless web" of electric decision-making.
- [[A Pluralism of Centers]] — because the electric structure has "no margins," restraint of power requires "a pluralism of centers" — dialogue "only among centers and among equals" — not the center-to-margin separation of powers.
- [[The Restoration of the Role]] — electric media restore the collective, nonvisual "role" in place of the delegated, specialist job (king/emperor, movie star).
- [[The Global Village]] — implosion/retribalization, decentralization ("decentralize every operation"), the party-line "communal participation," and the "no margins" planetary field.
- [[The Medium Is the Message]] — the Bell Labs / Information Theory / Game Theory content-fixation "oblivious to the telephone as a form."
- [[Explosion vs Implosion]] — the 2,500-year mechanical explosion (fragmentation) run backward into electric implosion (wholeness, decentralization).
- [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]] — the effort "to render speech visible" (the visual ambition) versus the audile-tactile, participational medium it actually produced; the visualizing Westerner versus the audile-tactile Teuton and Slav.

## Key Arguments

- The telephone demands complete participation because it is a cool, low-definition medium the senses must complete; this is why it irritates literate man and cannot be background. Supports [[Media Hot and Cold]], [[Hot vs Cool Media]].
- Electric speed dissolves delegated hierarchical authority and substitutes the inclusive authority of knowledge; restraint of electric power therefore requires a pluralism of centers, not a separation of powers. Supports [[The Authority of Knowledge]], [[A Pluralism of Centers]].
- Electric media restore the collective, nonvisual role in place of the delegated, specialist job. Supports [[The Restoration of the Role]], [[Job vs Role]].
- The print man's content-fixation makes Information Theory and Game Theory blind to the telephone as a form; the form, not the content, makes the social change. Supports [[The Medium Is the Message]].
- The telephone is intensely personal and so heightens the violation of telephonic privacy (wiretapping as the most odious surveillance). Interpretive bridge to [[Digital Panopticon]].
- The telephone decentralizes every operation (ending localized prostitution and creating the call-girl, as the typewriter "fuses the functions of composition and publication"). Supports [[The Global Village]], [[Decentralization]].

## Important Definitions

- [[Hot and Cool Media (McLuhan)]] — the chapter gives McLuhan's clearest one-paragraph statement of the sense-completion mechanism: a high-definition auditory image (radio) is completed with sight, a high-definition visual image with sound, and a low-definition image (the telephone) with all the senses.
- [[Extensions of Man (McLuhan)]] — the telephone as an extension of ear and voice; the receiver modeled on the organ it extends.
- [[The Medium Is the Message (McLuhan)]] — the applied gloss: studying "only the content aspect of wire service" misses the form that "altered the whole of our lives."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The title pun "Sounding Brass or Tinkling Symbol?" — on 1 Corinthians 13 ("sounding brass, or a tinkling cymbal"), with *cymbal* → *symbol*; picks up the chapter's "phony" wordplay.
- The "seamless web" of interlaced patterns in management and decision-making — the chapter's governing organizational image, against the lineal "pyramidal structure."
- The receiver modeled on the human ear — the medium as organic mimic.
- Playing the movie of the 2,500-year explosion backward — implosion as a reverse-running film.
- The married jet-fighter pilots ("till death do you part") — total involvement in role.
- The call-girl (paired with the typewriter as "most unidentical twins") — emblem of the telephone's decentralizing power.
- The Unruh phone call (Camden, 1949) — the mass shooter who stops firing to answer the phone, McLuhan's "bizarre testimony to the cooling participational character of the telephone."
- "All Alone by the Telephone" comes full circle — loneliness migrating from caller to instrument.
