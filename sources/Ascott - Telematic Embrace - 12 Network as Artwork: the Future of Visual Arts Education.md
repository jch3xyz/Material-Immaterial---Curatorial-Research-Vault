# Source Summary: Network as Artwork: the Future of Visual Arts Education

## Orientation

This is one of the shortest primary texts in the collection — an **abstract** Ascott wrote for the *World Future Society Education Section Meeting* at the University of Houston, Clear Lake City, in October 1978, noted by the editor as "Previously unpublished." Despite running barely two pages (~330 words of body), it is a keystone seed-text in Ascott's arc: it is the first place in the volume where his title-phrase **[[Network as Artwork]]** is articulated as a paradigm, and it arrives, characteristically, through the institutional side door of *art-education futures* rather than as art theory proper. The piece is programmatic and predictive in posture — a list of futurist tendencies rather than an argued essay — but every prediction is a downstream consequence of one idea: that art, education, criticism, and the museum must all be reconceived as networks rather than objects.

Ascott's claim is that post-secondary visual-arts education must be re-imagined in a "post-institutional context" governed by **mobility and networking**. He predicts the dissolution of the discipline-based curriculum (departments of painting, sculpture, etc.) in favour of a global, exploratory "learning net" organized around polarities of information/structure/concept and probes into cultural/technological/noetic areas. The physical apparatus of art — colleges on a 9-to-5 schedule, monolithic permanent buildings, museums, and the gallery-marketplace "art centres" of New York and London — will "physically disintegrate," replaced by **exhibition systems** and **moving centres**. Crucially these tendencies are not merely administrative: increased connectivity "breeds new hybrids," produces "more holistic thought and images," deepens participation, and culminates in a "connective, network paradigm for art and art theory, calling for a connective criticism."

For the vault, this 1978 abstract is the institutional/pedagogical face of the same idea that becomes the telematic embrace in the 1980s. It sits squarely on the [[Material vs Immaterial]] / [[Object vs System]] spine — the museum dematerializing into an "exhibition system," the art centre dematerializing into "moving centres" — and it translates McLuhan's [[The Global Village|global village]] into a pedagogical and curatorial program. It is also the germ of Ascott's lifelong pedagogy that culminates in [[The Planetary Collegium]]. Following the analysis's recommendation, the post-institutional learning-net material is folded into the [[Network as Artwork]] concept rather than minted as a standalone note, and `Connectivity` is deferred to a denser later chapter; the word "noetic" here is flagged as a pre-coinage seed of Ascott's later technoetics, not minted from this passage alone.

## Key Arguments

- **The future of art is a connective, network paradigm — "Network as Artwork."** Increased connectivity (driven by mobility, travel, teleconferencing, networking) breeds new hybrid practitioners and more holistic thought, deepens participation, and leads to a connective/network paradigm for art, theory, and criticism. (Directly stated.) Captured in [[Network as Artwork]] / [[Connective Criticism]].
- **Art education must be reconceived post-institutionally, dissolving the discipline-based curriculum.** Mobility and networking become the binding constraints; the curriculum shifts from rigid departmental compartmentalization to a global, exploratory learning net, dissolving the fine-art/applied-design dichotomy — while "skill banks" for media-specific instruction survive. (Directly stated.) Folded into [[Network as Artwork]]; see [[The Planetary Collegium]].
- **The physical institutions of art will dematerialize into systems and moving centres.** Networked access via travel and teleconferencing makes fixed attendance redundant, so colleges, monolithic buildings, museums, and gallery art-centres give way to "exhibition systems" and "moving centres." (Directly stated.) Cross-links [[Dematerialization]], [[Exhibition as Medium]], [[Material vs Immaterial]], [[Object vs System]].
