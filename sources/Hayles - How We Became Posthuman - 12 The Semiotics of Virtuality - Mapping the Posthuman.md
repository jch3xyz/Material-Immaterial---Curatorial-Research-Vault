# Source Summary: The Semiotics of Virtuality — Mapping the Posthuman

## Orientation

This is Hayles's penultimate chapter (her chapter 10), where she steps back from the historical narrative of [[Cybernetics]] and turns the accumulated conceptual apparatus into a map. Opening with Ihab Hassan's prophecy that "five hundred years of humanism may be coming to an end," she sets out to chart the posthuman "as a literary phenomenon." The mapping instrument is a Greimasian semiotic square built from the book's two governing dialectics — presence/absence and pattern/randomness — which she now treats as complementary rather than antagonistic axes. The diagonal joining presence and pattern she labels *replication* (continuation), the diagonal joining absence and randomness *disruption*. Setting the square in motion produces four synthesizing terms at its edges: *materiality*, *mutation*, *hyperreality*, and *information*. The chapter's rhetorical posture is deliberately anti-totalizing: Hayles insists "the map is not the territory," declines to "weave the discussions into a seamless web," and asks her readers to treat the sections like "hypertext lexias" so the posthuman does not appear "more unified than it is."

The body of the chapter reads four "tutor texts" mapped onto the square: Greg Bear's *Blood Music* (mutation), Cole Perriman's *Terminal Games* (hyperreality), Richard Powers's *Galatea 2.2* (materiality), and Neal Stephenson's *Snow Crash* (information). Each pair is framed by complementary questions. The horizontal axis is dominated by anxieties over body boundaries — *Blood Music* asks what if humans were taken over by their component parts (Vergil Ulam's intelligent cells, Bernard, Suzy McKenzie), *Terminal Games* what if humans were made to function as components of another entity (the virtual trickster-clown Auggie subsuming his human "cells," Marianne Hedison resisting him). The vertical axis foregrounds modes of inscription: *Galatea 2.2* asks what if a computer behaved like a person (Rick training the neural net Helen, shadowed by his failed loves C. and A.), and *Snow Crash* what if people behaved like computers (Hiro Protagonist, L. Bob Rife, and the brainstem-hacking nam-shub virus). Throughout, Hayles works the theoretical pairing of incorporation and inscription introduced earlier in the book, showing how the four novels stage evolving and devolving exchanges between the two.

The chapter's payoff is conceptual. Reading *Galatea 2.2*, Hayles delivers one of the book's signature claims: disembodiment is not a state any presence in the world can achieve, so Helen's difficulty is not that she lacks a body but that her embodiment differs radically from a human's. From the four readings she draws two general theses about posthuman subjectivity — that it is a *postconscious subject* (consciousness can be hijacked, absorbed, or back-propagated, so it no longer guarantees the self) and that the posthuman is *seriated* (some elements of the [[liberal humanist subject]] are rewritten into it while others are changed, never a clean break). She closes by mapping incorporation/inscription back onto the square as evolving/devolving vectors, insisting that "evolving incorporation, for all its frailties," is "finally more robust than evolving inscription" — a verdict that quietly re-grounds the posthuman in [[Embodiment]].

## Key Arguments

- [[Disembodiment is a state no presence in the world can achieve]] — Helen's problem is differential embodiment, not disembodiment.
- [[The posthuman subject is a postconscious subject]] — consciousness can be hijacked, mutinied against, absorbed, or back-propagated, so it no longer secures selfhood.
- [[The posthuman is embraced when it preserves agency and resisted when it does not]] — *Blood Music* embraces, *Terminal Games* resists; the pattern of seriation persists.
- [[Evolving incorporation is finally more robust than evolving inscription]] — human language grows from embodied experience; Helen must extrapolate back to it.
- [[The map is not the territory]] — the square is a heuristic that reveals "the complexity of the contexts" rather than a unified answer.

## Important Definitions

- [[Replication (Hayles)]] — the presence–pattern diagonal: "it points to continuation."
- [[Disruption (Hayles)]] — the absence–randomness diagonal: "Randomness tears holes in pattern, allowing the white noise of the background to pour through."
- [[Hyperreality]] — following [[Jean Baudrillard]], "a collapse of the distance between signifier and signified," culminating in "the precession of simulacra."
