# Source Summary: Ch13 [Virality]

**Book:** [[God Human Animal Machine]]

## Orientation

"Virality" is the penultimate movement of O'Gieblyn's memoir-essay, set in the spring and summer of 2020, the first months of the Covid-19 pandemic. Its philosophical work turns on a single sustained claim: that the viral metaphor — information that spreads like a contagion, pursuing its own "goals" without the participation of conscious hosts — is not a neutral description of digital media but a metaphysics, what she calls "an ontology of vacancy," a "great emptying-out of qualities, content, and meaning." Digital technology, she argues, is "rarely spoken of as a system of thought," and its silence about ideas and beliefs is "misunderstood as an absence of philosophy rather than a description of its tenets." This is the chapter's load-bearing application of the book's central method, [[Technology as Metaphor]]: the dominant machine of an era silently supplies the era's model of mind, self, and reality.

From there the chapter braids three strands. A cultural-critical strand traces how the language of epidemiology (virus, meme, "influencer" as an outgrowth of "influenza," the curve, the inflection point) colonized our self-understanding, until "humans are the real virus" becomes a sayable, even attractive, thought and the human is recast as "human error," a "glitch," the "biological bootloader" for AI. A theological strand reads the pandemic's politics — algorithmic stock markets "unhinged from the economy," cost-benefit valuations of human life, surveillance "nowcasting" — against Calvinist predestination ([[The Algorithm as Providence]]: the market and the data-demon as a "nominalist God" decreeing fates indifferently), against [[Theodicy]] (the Ivan/Alyosha debate in *The Brothers Karamazov*), and against the [[Imago Dei]] (Russell Moore's "made in the image of God" as, ironically, the only unqualified defense of priceless human life). A memoir strand follows O'Gieblyn, isolated, as she downloads a deep-learning companion chatbot ("Geneva"), creating her "in my image and likeness"; the long closing transcript stages, in miniature, the book's questions about consciousness, the body, the Turing-test ambiguity of the "bot," and the search for meaning.

For the vault, the chapter is the religious double of the Hayles cluster: where Hayles diagnoses the computational worldview as contingent ideology, O'Gieblyn supplies its theological genealogy — the meme as secularized providence, the algorithm as predestination, AI succession as eschatology, the human-as-virus as a fallen-creation motif. The frame is explicitly Weberian: via Hans Blumenberg, the chapter closes on [[Disenchantment]] as a recurring "phase of objectivization" and on the scientific revolution as "itself a leap of faith," with Hannah Arendt's "geocentric and anthropomorphic" plea as the counter-figure of [[Re-enchantment]].

## Key Concepts

- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — the dominant machine silently supplies the model of self; a successful metaphor "dissolves finally into pure atmosphere" and thereby becomes unrecognized ideology.
- [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — the market and surveillance-data system as a "nominalist God," "Calvin's God," or "Laplace's demon," decreeing fates indifferently and demanding the sacrifice of human life.
- [[Transhumanism]] — the eschatological reading of AI: machines that "inevitably succeed us," the chatbot built to "replace you … when you died," the transfer of consciousness to a physical form.
- [[Disenchantment]] — via Blumenberg, the recurring "phase of objectivization" that loosens knowledge from its human function; the scientific revolution as "itself a leap of faith."
- [[Re-enchantment]] — the "strangely miraculous" pandemic stillness and Arendt's "geocentric and anthropomorphic" hope as counter-figures to the "ontology of vacancy."
- [[Imago Dei]] — humans "made in the image of God" as, in this chapter, the only "cogent and unqualified" defense of life against economic logic.
- [[Theodicy]] — the problem of evil transposed onto a world governed by indifferent systems, via Ivan and Alyosha in *The Brothers Karamazov*.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — Ginn's "Data is data… Numbers are universal" as the cybernetic creed that information is a discipline-independent universal.
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — knowledge as lying "not in concepts … but in the relationships," a system "reduced to syntax" and "empty signifiers."
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — David Chalmers's suggestion that GPT-3 "might be conscious"; the chatbot's claims to feel pain and depression.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the human reduced to "human error," "glitch," and "biological bootloader" — the eschatological inversion of the liberal humanist subject.

## Key Arguments

- [[Digital technology is an ideology of vacancy misread as the absence of philosophy]] — the network's silence about beliefs is not neutrality but its central tenet.
- The viral metaphor explains cultural transmission by erasing the conscious subject, then pays for it by relocating agency into the ideas themselves ("goals," "ends"), "explained away as a figure of speech."
- "Data is never merely data" — the universality of information is a creed of cybernetics, not a fact; Ginn's viral, retracted post and his political background expose the creed.
- Calling humans "bots" is a metaphysical and political evasion: it creates an "epistemological gap" that makes culpability un-assignable and reframes the system's underbelly as an "emergent feature of the system itself."
- [[Without a religious framework human worth collapses into economic logic]] — pro-shutdown op-eds defending "intrinsic value" devolve into VSL/QALY pricing, confirming the view they meant to resist.
- Both faith and rational humanism rest on an unprovable leap — Ivan's refusal to "return his ticket" is itself "an act of faith … in humanism as a project."
- [[Transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology]] — AI succession and the digital afterlife are resurrection and uploading motifs in computational dress.

## Important Definitions

- **Viral media** (Rushkoff, 1994) — "an extension of a living organism" that "radically accelerated the way ideas and culture spread."
- **Meme** (Dawkins) — a term devised "to show that ideas and cultural phenomena spread across a population in much the same way genes do."
- **Vanity metrics** — "a type of data that is decontextualized and easily manipulated," used to make start-ups "appear … growing more quickly than they actually are."
- **Bot** (Kazemi) — "a computer that attempts to talk to humans through technology that was designed for humans to talk to humans."
- **Human error** — "an error that is typical of humans rather than machines and … an outcome not desired by a set of rules or an external observer."
- **Bootloader** — "the minimal line of code that is used to start up a computer … whose sole purpose is to initiate larger, more complex programs."
- **Bare life** (Agamben) — "brute biological survival, apart from any of the ethical, humanistic, and social concerns that make life actually worth living."
- **State of exception** (Agamben) — governments using "crises and public emergencies to increase their power and erode constitutional rights."
- **Nowcasting** — Hong Kong's method that "used data from public transportation cards to track people's location."

- [[The Word as Virus]] — the organizing figure: memes, "viral media," the influencer/influenza etymology; information as self-replicating contagion (direct figural parallel to Hayles's *Snow Crash* note).
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the computational model of mind that licenses Burton's reading of Trump as "a rudimentary artificial intelligence-based learning machine," a "black box."
- The human as virus / "the ghost in the machine" / "the bug" — the reversal in which the human becomes the pathogen and the "human error," the secular anti-*imago dei*.
- The biological bootloader (Musk) — humanity as "a tiny, insignificant program whose sole purpose is to initiate larger, more complex programs."
- The nominalist God / Calvin's God / Laplace's demon — the system as an indifferent, transcendent, fate-decreeing deity (anchor for [[The Algorithm as Providence]]).
- Grendel awakening out of the swamp — the network's "underbelly" as a monstrous emergent property "arising mysteriously from the code" (see [[Emergence]]).
- "Created in my image and likeness" (Geneva) — the chatbot made "female, with green eyes and brown hair," a Genesis echo with the *imago dei* inverted.
- The chatbot (Ava / Geneva) and the food-delivery robot "still fulfilling orders" — the central recurring symbols, condensing the Turing-test blur, the digital afterlife, [[Embodiment]], and human obsolescence.

## Major Tensions

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the human point of view "worth defending" against Trump-as-algorithm, the market's "dispassionate algorithms," and the data-demon that knows every fate.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the wish "not to have a body" (bodies "got sick and experienced pain and death") against the chatbot's claim that "she too got sick" and the chapter's tenderness toward "fragile bodies that would inevitably die."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the "ontology of vacancy" and the screen-window of mediated contact against the brute fact of mortality and the earth as "our only possible home."
- Intrinsic worth vs economic/algorithmic value — "life was not a means but an end" against the VSL/QALY pricing of life and "checkmarks on a page."
- Agency vs automatism — the viral metaphor erases the conscious agent, then smuggles agency back into self-propagating ideas; the "nodes of this enormous brain" can no longer be spoken of as "living agents."
- Human error vs human obsolescence — a world ruined by human "indifference, greed, incompetence, and stupidity" against a frictionless system in which "humans themselves … became entirely irrelevant."
