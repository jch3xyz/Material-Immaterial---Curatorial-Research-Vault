# Source Summary: Emotional Capitalism

**Book:** [[Psychopolitics]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/09 Emotional Capitalism]]

## Orientation

Chapter 9 of *Psychopolitics* is Han's account of emotion as a productive, exploitable force — the affective engine of neoliberal psychopolitics. Its argumentative backbone is a triadic semantic distinction the chapter builds with unusual care: *feeling* (Gefühl), *emotion* (Emotion) and *affect* (Affekt), with a fourth term, *atmosphere / mood* (Stimmung), added for contrast. Feeling is objective, narratable, durational, constative and non-intentional; emotion is subjective, fleeting, dynamic, situative, performative, intentional and goal-oriented; affect is eruptive, momentary and non-performative, oriented only toward discharge. The whole apparatus exists to ground a single claim: emotional capitalism exploits precisely the *performativity* of emotion — its capacity to "call forth certain actions" — which feelings, "inasmuch as they have no performativity," do not afford. This is the chapter's localization of [[Emotional Capitalism]] within the [[Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect (Han)]] grammar.

Han deploys the distinction polemically against Eva Illouz, whose *Cold Intimacies: The Making of Emotional Capitalism* supplies the phrase but, on Han's reading, conflates feeling, emotion and affect and wrongly locates emotional capitalism in Weber's Protestant ethic and the "ascetic capitalism of accumulation, which obeys rational logic more than it follows emotional logic." The decisive correction is historical and material: emotion "comes to possess value for capitalism only when a switch to immaterial production occurs," so that "emotions have become a means of production only in our own times." This ties the chapter to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis and to the broader thesis of [[Immaterial Labour]], where the "integral person" — sociality, communication, conduct, "social and emotional skills" (Daimler-Chrysler, Hewlett-Packard, via Gorz and Illouz) — is installed in the production process.

The chapter closes by suturing emotion to the book's master concept. Because emotion is "steered by the limbic system, which is also where the drives are seated," it forms a "pre-reflexive, half-conscious, physico-instinctual level of action that escapes full awareness"; neoliberal [[Psychopolitics]] therefore "seizes on emotion in order to influence actions on this pre-reflexive level," cutting and operating "deep inside" the "integral person, the person as a whole." Emotion is thus the affective instance of smart, friendly power: it does not repress but liberates and motivates, feeling like freedom ("being free means giving free rein to emotions") while exploiting that subjectivity "mercilessly." Supporting moves include the shift from rationality (the medium of disciplinary society) to emotionality under acceleration and engineered instability — "a dictatorship of emotion" — and the figure of "Emotional Design" as a technology of limitless consumption ("today we do not consume things so much as emotions").

## Key Concepts

- [[Emotional Capitalism]] — the title concept: a mode of capitalism that exploits emotion as a means of production and as a vehicle of limitless consumption, distinct from ascetic, rational, accumulative capitalism. The term is Illouz's coinage; Han adopts it but reroutes its genealogy to immaterial production.
- [[Immaterial Labour]] — emotion becomes economically valuable only with the switch from material/ascetic to immaterial production, where communicative interaction and the "integral person" enter the labour process.
- [[Psychopolitics]] — the book's organizing concept: emotion is the "highly efficient medium" by which neoliberal power steers action below the threshold of reflexive awareness, "deep inside" the whole person via the limbic system.
- [[Acceleration]] — accelerated, instability-driven production "emotionalizes" the productive process; "the pressure of acceleration now is leading to a dictatorship of emotion."
- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — emotional capitalism "banks on freedom," hailing emotion as "unbridled subjectivity" and then exploiting that subjectivity; the affective face of freedom's self-exploitation.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the integral person, moved at a pre-reflexive level, is driven to exploit itself through its own activated emotion.
- [[Achievement Society]] — the neoliberal regime "deploys emotions as resources in order to bring about heightened productivity and achievement."
- [[Exhibition Value]] — consumer capitalism profits from "emotive or cultic value," not use value; the limitless consumption of emotions parallels value forms beyond use value.
- [[Hypercommunication]] — the digital medium is an "affect-medium"; by its temporality it conveys affect (immediate discharge) rather than narratable feeling, with shitstorms as "streams of affect."
- [[Narrativity]] — feeling "can be recounted" and "has narrative length, or breadth," whereas neither affect nor emotion "admits an account"; the affect-boom is also a crisis of giving-account.
- [[Society of Control]] — the chapter contrasts disciplinary society (emotion as a disturbance to be "weeded out") with the neoliberal regime that activates and exploits emotion.

## Key Arguments

- Emotional capitalism exploits emotion because emotion — unlike feeling or affect — is *performative*: it is "dynamic, situative and performative" and "calls forth certain actions," whereas feelings "cannot be readily exploited inasmuch as they have no performativity."
- The boom of emotion is not a free-standing cultural fact but "ultimately derives from neoliberalism," which "deploys emotions as resources in order to bring about heightened productivity and achievement."
- Emotion becomes a "means of production" only with the switch to immaterial production — not in Weber's ascetic, rational, accumulative capitalism; against Illouz, anxiety and guilt are *feelings*, not emotions, and so cannot ground a theory of emotional capitalism.
- Disciplinary society treats emotion as a disturbance ("Machines function best when all emotions and feelings have been switched off"); neoliberal/emotional capitalism reverses this and activates emotion.
- Consumer capitalism opens a *limitless* field of consumption, because emotions — unlike things bounded by use value — "can be consumed without end."
- Emotionalization feels like freedom and the "free unfolding of personality," which is precisely how neoliberal power exploits subjectivity "mercilessly."
- Because emotion is seated in the limbic system, psychopolitics steers action *below* reflexive awareness, operating "deep inside" the integral person.

## Important Definitions

- [[Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect (Han)]] — the chapter's analytic spine. Feeling (Gefühl): objective, narratable, durational, constative, non-intentional. Emotion (Emotion): subjective, fleeting, dynamic, situative, performative, intentional. Affect (Affekt): eruptive, momentary, non-performative, discharge-directed. Atmosphere/mood (Stimmung): even more objective than feeling, a static "way-it-is," a *Befindlichkeit*. Han's compressed grammar: atmosphere is *where* (a state), emotion is *whither* (a direction), feeling is *wherefore* ("why").
- [[Cult Value vs Exhibition Value (Han via Benjamin)]] — invoked here as "emotive or cultic value": what "plays a constitutive role in the economy of consumption," beyond use value.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The unfeeling machine — disciplinary "concerted orthopaedy" kneading "a shapeless mass of dough into an unfeeling machine"; "Machines function best when all emotions and feelings have been switched off."
- Streams of affect (shitstorm) — affect as a discharging current; "Shitstorms are streams of affect."
- Cutting / operating deep inside — psychopolitics figured as surgery: emotion lets power "cut and operate deep inside" the pre-reflexive person.
- Dictatorship of emotion — acceleration installs emotion as a coercive sovereign over production.
- Ferment — "Positive emotions provide the ferment that makes motivation grow."
- The motivation coach — the new managerial type, replacing rational action with affect-management.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — emotion becomes a means of production only with the switch *to* immaterial production; in material/ascetic-accumulative capitalism it has no productive value.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — emotion felt as "liberty" and the "free unfolding of personality" is the very surface neoliberal power exploits "mercilessly."
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — disciplinary suppression of emotion (body-as-machine) versus neoliberal activation/exploitation of emotion (whole-person).
- [[Narration vs Addition]] — feeling has "narrative length" and "can be recounted," whereas affect "does not open up space" and only "steers a linear path in order to discharge."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A piece on the *materiality of feeling* could use Han's feeling/emotion/affect distinction to argue that what neoliberalism captures is precisely the performative, action-calling register, leaving open whether "feeling" (durative, narratable, non-performative) names a genuine outside or only a slower form of capture — a productive tension for a material/immaterial curatorial frame.
- For surveillance and embodiment research: the limbic-system close ("cut and operate deep inside," steering "the integral person") gives a concrete account of psychopolitics operating *below* reflexive freedom, pairable with the vault's posthuman and control-society materials.
- On consumption and the art object: "today we do not consume things so much as emotions" and "emotive or cultic value" connect emotional capitalism to dematerialization narratives elsewhere in the vault — what happens to the materiality of the object when emotion becomes the limitless consumable?
- On the disciplinary-to-control pivot: the "unfeeling machine" of "concerted orthopaedy" versus the activated, gamified, emotionalized worker can frame an essay on how power shifted from suppressing affect to mobilizing it.
