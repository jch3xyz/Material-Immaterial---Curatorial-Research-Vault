## Orientation

This is Chapter 3 of Part One ("Sculpture as Object," pp. 110–166), Burnham's diagnosis of *formalism* as "the weary vocabulary" of modern sculpture. He defines the formal as "those material-optical-tactile features of sculpture which can be identified and logically examined as separate physical entities" — shape, proportion, scale, structure, texture, color, context — and argues that this prescientific reservoir of qualities "has both changed and decreased since the beginning of the century." The chapter's master claim is that formalism is exhausting itself ([[Form Exhaustion]]): to stay vital its vocabulary "must constantly change and grow," yet "this has happened only marginally," leaving each generation of sculptors charged with imitation and stranded in "a world of fifty-year-old manifestoes." The brute residue of the predicament is that, however dematerialized sculpture becomes, the sculptor "cannot rid themselves of the presence of objects. That is their metier."

The chapter's engine is the disjunction between the form-world of science and the form-world of sculpture. Burnham argues that the 1890–1925 revolution in physics — relativity, quantum theory, the dissolution of solid matter into "events," fields, and probability clouds — together with the rise of technics progressively de-legitimized the inert, unique, place-bound art object. He develops this across a sequence of dense sections: formalism as a prescientific, exhausting methodology; the [[The Scientific Demiurge]] (the shared psychic drive of scientist and sculptor to reveal invisible underlying order, plus the artist's love-hate envy of science's resources, anonymity, and teamwork); invention in sculpture as industrial-style hybridization (the welded box traced from David Smith's *Cubi* back to American neon pylon signage); the [[The Scientific Model]] as the sculptor's "most exotic reservoirs of forms," whose obsolescence in science accounts "in part for the steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's"; and mathematical influences (Vantongerloo, Pevsner, Gabo, Bill) culminating in [[Topology as the New Formalism]] and Spengler's "liberation of geometry from the visual."

Rhetorically the chapter is a sweeping art-historical synthesis underwritten by primary-source quotation (Gabo, Mondrian, Malevich, Vantongerloo, Bill) and by Spengler, Ellul, Russell, and Banham. Its final two sections pivot toward the book's larger argument: that "considerations of scale, proportion, space, and structure are largely irrelevant," that space "has become a dead issue," and — most consequentially — that "light—not form—determines our perception of sculpture," so that sculpture becomes a [[Light as a Sculpture Medium|modulator of light]] and "a prisoner of this earthly continuum" reaching for "effect without the hindrance or fallibility of objects." This is foundational for the vault's titular [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis and the [[Object vs System]] migration: Burnham (1968) names the sculptural prehistory of Lippard's [[Dematerialization]], periodized to 1910–1950s and rooted in physics and mathematics. ## Key Concepts

- [[Form Exhaustion]] — the master concept: sculpture's stock of classical formal qualities has decreased, and the formalist vocabulary cannot change and grow fast enough to stay vital, leaving sculpture in stasis.
- [[Formalism (Burnham)]] — the "material-optical-tactile features of sculpture which can be identified and logically examined as separate physical entities"; a prescientific, intuition-based "strategic necessity."
- [[The Scientific Demiurge]] — the shared drive of scientist and sculptor "to reveal and analyze underlying truths"; in science it yields progress, in sculpture "growing stasis."
- [[The Scientific Model]] — the scientist's model (substantive, formal/mathematical, computer-simulation) as the sculptor's reservoir of "pure form"; its obsolescence tracks sculpture's dematerialization.
- [[Topology as the New Formalism]] — the migration of sculptural form from metric/proportional geometry to the "innate and unchanging" topological properties of objects (Gabo's *Spheric Theme*, Bill's Möbius variations).
- [[Object Art]] — Minimal/Object sculpture as the "thingified," de-idealized endgame of the mathematical model — "tacit admission that the mathematical model can no longer solve the iconic problems of sculpture."
- [[Reification]] — sculpture becoming "thingified" by means perceptible only through "the rules for ordering finite or infinite sets of abstract points" (Judd's set-theory wall reliefs).
- [[Vitalism]] — the rationalization that sculpture (or the machine) harbors an inner "life force"; the optical/reflective turn is "a reaction against the vitalist myth that the psyche of sculpture is buried within the object itself."
- [[The Black Box]] — the modern machine's internalized, inaccessible function ("a black box to all but the most initiated"); its smooth covering as a symbol of "psychic inaccessibility."
- [[Technics]] — Ellul's "principal law of the age": "technique uses what is at hand, regardless of the implications," the amoral logic the present avant-garde has internalized ("Since it was possible, it was necessary").
- [[Dematerialization]] — Burnham's explicit periodization of a "steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's," rooted in physics and "the thought processes of the mathematician."
- [[Light as a Sculpture Medium]] — the late-chapter thesis that "light—not form—determines our perception of sculpture," with works "solely designed as modulators of light."
- [[Scientific Idealism]] — the conviction, running through the Constructivists, that science (and the model) discloses a truer, invisible order the sculptor can mine for "pure form."

## Key Arguments

- [[Formalism and vitalism are exhausted because scientific reality has become invisible]] — formalism's vocabulary cannot grow fast enough; sculpture cannot follow science into "nonvisual analysis" because it stays bound to phenomenological, object-present means.
- [[The obsolescence of the visual scientific model drove the dematerialization of sculpture]] — sculptors took up ball-and-stick/atomic/topological models as "pure form" just as science abandoned visual models for mathematics, so sculpture dematerialized with its referents.
- [[Constructivist sculpture dematerialized its support from the conceptual models of science]] — Gabo, Pevsner, and Bill drew the airiness, openness, and topological logic of their constructions from scientific and mathematical models while denying direct imitation.
- [[Light not form determines our perception of sculpture]] — across a century and all styles, the impetus has grown that we perceive forms via their light-emitting properties; the endpoint is sculpture as a modulator of light.
- [[A base-less sculpture ceases to be an object and becomes a physical system of forces]] — the move (via Smith's late *Cubi* and Caro) away from the base and toward antistructural assembly tilts the object toward a relational system.
- [[Object art shifts the obligation to vitalize sculpture onto the viewers perception]] — the optical/reflective turn locates what matters "on its surface for optical examination," refuting the vitalist interior psyche.

## Important Definitions

- [[Formalism (Burnham)]] — "those material-optical-tactile features of sculpture which can be identified and logically examined as separate physical entities ... shape, proportion, scale, structure, texture, color, context."
- [[The Scientific Model]] — "an analogy of a proposed system or set of relationships, though not necessarily as a physical object," divided into the *formal* (mathematical relationships) and the *substantive* (physical-system analogies and spatial-theory models).
- *Topology* — "the study of the innate properties of objects in space ... unaffected by changes in magnitude, position, or proportion and ... only altered by the tearing or joining of an object's parts." (no dedicated note; captured under [[Topology as the New Formalism]])

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A keystone for the vault's titular [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis: Burnham's "steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's" gives the art-historical *prehistory* of Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] (see [[Jack Burnham and Lucy Lippard]]) — useful for an essay tracing the long arc from bronze mass to immaterial light/information.
- The closing "desire to embody space without matter—like music ... to produce effect without the hindrance or fallibility of objects" is a precise, citable thesis for any piece on the immaterial aspiration of Western technology, and bridges to Hayles's information-losing-its-body register (see [[Jack Burnham and N. - The [[Object vs System]] migration: Smith's base-less late *Cubi*, the antistructural "formalism of the absurd," and the move from mass to light document the precise pivot where the object begins tilting toward the relational system — the hinge of the whole book.
- [[Technics]] and the loss of uniqueness ("the uniqueness of an artist can only be proven by the generative power of his ideas, not by material output") for an essay on mass reproduction, "multiples," and the migration of artistic value from object to idea — a 1968 anticipation of [[Conceptual Art]].
- The neon [[The Pylon Sign (Las Vegas)|pylon sign]] as the "arch-underground the American art form" behind the welded box: a vivid case study in invention-as-industrial-hybridization and the porous boundary between vernacular signage and high sculpture.
