# Source Summary: 1967

**Book:** [[Six Years]]
**Author:** [[Lucy Lippard]]

## Orientation

This is the 1967 entry of Lippard's documentary anthology — a dense, deliberately non-narrative chronicle that stitches together exhibition records, bibliographic citations, work descriptions, and verbatim artist statements for the year. Lippard's own argumentative voice is sparse; the year speaks through what was made, shown, and printed. But for the book's thesis 1967 is the pivotal naming year, the moment the field acquires its vocabulary and its preferred media.

This is where [[Sol LeWitt]]'s "Paragraphs on Conceptual Art" (*Artforum*, summer 1967) names "conceptual art" and figures the artwork as a program: in his formulation the idea becomes a machine that makes the art, and "the execution is a perfunctory affair." It is where [[Joseph Kosuth]] asserts that "The actual works of art are ideas" and exhibits *"Titled" (Art as Idea as Idea)* as a negative photostat. It is where [[Dan Graham]]'s *Schema* defines the work "only as information." And it is where Lippard, in her own editorial voice, glosses [[Robert Ryman]]'s stretcherless white paintings — freed from "a bulky, difficult-to transport object" — as "notable within the 'dematerialization' process," the chronicle's clearest in-text application of its titular concept. This is the art-historical instance of the vault's titular axis [[Material vs Immaterial]]: the art object thinned toward idea, model, language, and document.

Equally, 1967 is when the medium shifts off the wall. The artist's book ([[Edward Ruscha]]'s *Royal Road Test*), the xeroxed checklist, the photostat, and above all the periodical and the box become primary vehicles: *0–9* (edited by Vito Acconci and Bernadette Mayer), *Aspen* no. 5–6 (the boxed issue edited by Brian O'Doherty containing LeWitt's *Serial Project #1, 1966* and Graham's *Schema*), and the Paris broadsheet *Robho*. Notably, [[Seth Siegelaub]] — later the exhibition-as-catalogue impresario of conceptual art — is *not* named anywhere in the 1967 text; the catalogue-as-medium thread this year runs through *Aspen*, *0–9*, and xeroxed checklists, not through Siegelaub. The same year registers the parallel emergence of earthworks ([[Michael Heizer]], [[Dennis Oppenheim]], [[Robert Smithson]], [[Richard Long]]), of Arte Povera through [[Germano Celant]]'s Genoa shows (*Arte Povera/im Spazio*, Galleria La Bertesca, October 1967), and of the anti-painting demonstrations of Buren, Mosset, Parmentier, and Toroni in Paris, whose distributed tract declared "WE ARE NOT PAINTERS."

The raw extraction is heavily OCR-damaged: several of the year's defining sentences (LeWitt's "machine that makes the art," parts of the Kosuth and Ryman entries, Graham's *Schema* paragraph) survive only in garbled form in the source file. The Source Citations below copy the raw text character-for-character; where a word is corrupted in the original extraction it is preserved as-is and flagged, rather than silently corrected.

## Key Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the vault's titular axis; 1967 supplies its art-historical instance, "dematerialization of the art object," applied by Lippard in her own voice to Ryman.
- [[Conceptual Art]] — named and figured this year by LeWitt; the idea, not the execution, is the work *(forward-stub)*.
- [[Information]] — conceptual art's "information" aesthetic: Graham's *Schema* defines the work "only as information"; the document/periodical/box as the work's support.
- [[Systems Theory]] — serial and systems aesthetics as art: Bochner's "Serial Art Systems: Solipsism," LeWitt's serial projects, [[Hans Haacke]]'s real-time wind/water systems.

## Key Arguments

- Lippard's editorial gloss: Ryman's stretcherless, transport-light white paintings belong to the dematerialization process — a formal strategy (freeing painting from the bulky object) read as an instance of the book's titular thesis. (`Directly stated`, Lippard's own voice.)

## Important Definitions

- Conceptual art (LeWitt, 1967) — "In concept art the idea or concept is the most important aspect of the work."
- Art as idea (Kosuth, February 1967) — "All I make are models. The actual works of art are ideas."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The idea as machine that makes the art (LeWitt) — the most cited figure of the year; the artwork-as-program.
- NOIT / "chair of nonentity" ([[John Latham]]) — matter as event-in-time rather than solid "particle."
- The xerox / photostat — Kosuth's negative photostat, [[Christine Kozlov]]'s xeroxed canceled calendars, xeroxed checklists: the reproducible document as the artwork's bearer.
- The invisible / nonvisual work — [[Robert Barry]]'s near-invisible nylon sculptures and white-screen films; [[Mel Ramsden]]'s canvas turned face-to-wall, "UNTITLED (NONVISUAL ART)."

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — object-bound painting giving way to idea, model, and document; the art object thinned toward invisibility, language, and information.
- Idea vs execution — LeWitt: planning is the art, the execution "a perfunctory affair."
