# Source Summary: The Homework Economy "Outside the Home"

**Book:** [[A Cyborg Manifesto]]

## Orientation

This is the most materialist, political-economic section of the manifesto. Having mythologized the cyborg and mapped the abstract mutation from "the comfortable old hierarchical dominations" to the "scary new networks" of [[The Informatics of Domination]], Haraway here grounds those networks in concrete labor relations, bodies, households, and geographies. The chapter's organizing concept is [[The Homework Economy]] — a term she borrows from economist Richard Gordon and enlarges — which names the worldwide restructuring of work as "literally female and feminized." The "New Industrial Revolution" is "producing a new worldwide working class, as well as new sexualities and ethnicities"; capital becomes extremely mobile; an emerging international division of labor reorganizes "reproduction, sexuality, culture, consumption, and production" simultaneously. None of this, Haraway insists, is gender- or race-neutral.

The chapter's central operation is *feminization*. To be feminized is "to be made extremely vulnerable; able to be disassembled, reassembled, exploited as a reserve labor force; seen less as workers than as servers" — a structural position, not a sex, since work is "redefined as both literally female and feminized, whether performed by men or women." From this Haraway derives the feminization of poverty, the collapse of the family (male) wage, the loading of social-reproductive labor onto women under a collapsing welfare state, a periodizing scheme of three stages of capitalism and three forms of family (borrowed in part from Jameson), and the looming danger of "a strongly bimodal social structure." Throughout she is precise about causation: the homework economy "is made possible by (not caused by) the new technologies" — technology is enabling infrastructure that integrates and controls dispersed, decentralized labor, not a determining cause. This careful refusal of technological determinism keeps the chapter on the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis without letting the immaterial float free.

This is also where the manifesto's relation to surveillance is most concrete and where its content most directly meets the vault's [[The Body as Data]] cluster. The new communications technologies are "fundamental to the eradication of 'public life' for everyone," underwriting "a permanent high-tech military establishment" and militarizing imagination (video games, miniaturized televisions, the "extraterrestrial warfare" of privatized leisure). Women's bodies become "boundaries newly permeable to both 'visualization' and 'intervention,'" and sociobiology's "high-tech view of the body as a biotic component or cybernetic communications system" gives the body-as-information thesis an early, explicitly feminist-critical statement. The masses confined to the homework economy are "controlled by high-tech repressive apparatuses ranging from entertainment to surveillance and disappearance" — a diagnosis that runs parallel to Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and the dispersed regime of [[Society of Control]]. Yet the chapter closes by pivoting from diagnosis to program — shared vulnerability makes coalition "necessary, not just nice," and an "adequate socialist feminist politics" must intervene *inside* the production of science and technology — enacting the manifesto-wide [[Domination vs Possibility]] double vision on the terrain of labor.

## Key Arguments

The chapter advances four high-confidence claims. None has a dedicated argument note yet (they will be minted or folded at the book-level finalize), so they are stated here as prose and grounded in the Source Citations below; they are not wiki-linked to avoid dangling targets.

- **The homework economy is made possible by, but not caused by, the new technologies.** Haraway distinguishes enabling-condition from cause and locates power in the *use* of communications technology to integrate and control dispersed, decentralized labor — which is what made "the success of the attack on relatively privileged, mostly white, men's unionized jobs" possible.
- **The new communications technologies are fundamental to the eradication of "public life" for everyone.** Through "privatization" (per Petchesky), militarization, right-wing family ideology, and intensified definitions of corporate/state property as private synergistically interact, displacing politics into militarized fantasy and consumption and underwriting a permanent high-tech military establishment "at the cultural and economic expense of most people, but especially of women."
- **Shared structural vulnerability under the homework economy makes coalition necessary, not nice.** As feminization intensifies and many more men come to share women's (and especially black women's long-known) vulnerability, "cross-gender and race alliances on issues of basic life support" become a material necessity — the manifesto's affinity-not-identity politics rendered economic.
- **An adequate socialist-feminist politics must intervene in the production of science and technology.** Because the informatics of domination is produced *in* science and technology, an effective oppositional politics must reach women "in the privileged occupational categories," build accountability across "the scientific-technical hierarchies separating us," and ally with antimilitary science-conversion groups — a proto-statement of feminist science / situated knowledges.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

This chapter contributes vivid images that do not (yet) have dedicated metaphor/symbol notes; they are recorded here as prose, grounded in the citations, rather than as dangling wiki-links:

- **"Hunting with the camera" / "the deeply predatory nature of a photographic consciousness"** — visualization technology figured as predation; a strong image for surveillance-of-the-body.
- **The body as "a kind of private satisfaction- and utility-maximizing machine"** — sociobiology's reduction of the body to an instrumental, calculating machine.
- **"Disassembled, reassembled"** — the feminized worker as a modular, interchangeable component, resonant with the cyborg as assemblage.
- **The speculum** — "an icon of women's claiming their bodies in the 1970s," now an "inadequate" "handcraft tool" against "the practices of cyborg reproduction."
- **The video game** — symbol of privatized, militarized "private life": individual competition, "extraterrestrial warfare," and "a sci-fi escape" from a destroyed planet.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **Surveillance research focus.** "From entertainment to surveillance and disappearance" and "the deeply predatory nature of a photographic consciousness" give a 1985 feminist-materialist anchor for an essay linking Haraway's bimodal-society enforcement apparatus to Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and the [[Society of Control]] — surveillance as the control technology of a feminized labor regime, not merely a political abuse.
- **Material/immaterial and the body-as-data.** The "high-tech view of the body as a biotic component or cybernetic communications system" supplies the explicitly *critical* and *feminist* precursor to the cybernetics/posthuman body-as-information thesis (Hayles, O'Gieblyn, Harari, Kurzweil) for an essay on how the body is dematerialized into code — and who controls "the interpretation of bodily boundaries."
- **Immaterial labor across decades.** A controlled comparison of Haraway's feminization (gendered, racialized, made-possible-not-caused) with Han's de-gendered [[Immaterial Labour]] and auto-exploitation — what is lost analytically and politically when the gendered framing drops out.
- **Embodiment and obsolete handcraft feminism.** The speculum as "inadequate … handcraft tool" against "the practices of cyborg reproduction" and "Self-help is not enough" for an essay on the obsolescence of the bounded organic body and the turn to a cyborg body-politics.
- **Technological non-determinism in curatorial framing.** "Made possible by (not caused by)" as a methodological caution for exhibitions about technology and power: how to make a dispersed, decentralized domination legible without reifying the machines as the cause.
