# Source Summary: Toward Embodied Virtuality

## Orientation

This is the book's introductory chapter (the raw file's internal frontmatter labels it "chapter 1"; the vault filename numbers it 03). It opens autobiographically with the scene that started the project: reading Hans Moravec's fantasy of a robot surgeon "puree[ing] the human brain" to download consciousness intact into a computer, a "roboticist's dream that struck me as a nightmare." From that shock Hayles generalizes the book's diagnostic claim — that "a defining characteristic of the present cultural moment is the belief that information can circulate unchanged among different material substrates" — and announces the three interlaced stories the book will tell: how information lost its body, how the cyborg was constructed as postwar artifact and cultural icon, and how the human gave way to the posthuman. The rhetorical posture is at once historical and polemical: Hayles is not a neutral chronicler but an interventionist who wants to "keep disembodiment from being rewritten, once again, into prevailing concepts of subjectivity."

The chapter's conceptual core is a four-assumption definition of the posthuman (informational pattern privileged over material instantiation; consciousness as epiphenomenon; the body as "the original prosthesis"; human being configured to articulate seamlessly with intelligent machines) followed by a surprising continuity thesis. Against readings that treat the posthuman as a clean break from the liberal humanist subject, Hayles argues that "the erasure of embodiment is a feature common to both" — the liberal subject "possessed a body but was not usually represented as being a body," and the cybernetic posthuman, glossed through Gibson's "data made flesh," likewise treats embodiment as the mere instantiation of thought/information. So the posthuman, to the extent it keeps this emphasis on cognition over embodiment, "continues the liberal tradition rather than disrupts it." Hayles is careful to say she does not mourn the liberal subject's passing; her aim is to recover the erased flesh, the "version of the posthuman" that "celebrates finitude" and embeddedness in a material world.

The back half of the chapter lays out method and stakes. Hayles charts cybernetics in three waves (homeostasis, 1945–60; reflexivity, 1960–80; virtuality, 1980–present), tracking the Macy Conferences, second-order cybernetics, and autopoiesis. She offers three method-concepts borrowed from archaeological anthropology and her own coinage — seriation (overlapping replication and innovation rather than Kuhnian or Foucauldian rupture), the skeuomorph (a no-longer-functional design feature that alludes back to a functional ancestor, e.g. homeostasis lingering as a "threshold device" in the second wave), and the conceptual constellation (the bundle of mutually entailing ideas whose assembly and disassembly mark a period). She narrates the Shannon/MacKay contest over the definition of information — Shannon's dimensionless, meaningless "pattern, not a presence" winning industry-standard status over Donald MacKay's meaning-and-receiver-based alternative for reasons of "reliable quantification and theoretical generality." She names the twin ideological maneuvers that build the information/materiality hierarchy — the Platonic backhand (inferring a simplified abstraction from noisy multiplicity, then making it the originary Form) and the Platonic forehand (evolving a complex world out of simple abstractions via simulation). And she closes by defending narrative — "a more embodied form of discourse than is analytically driven systems theory" — as the resource for contesting the disembodying metanarrative of Moravec and Minsky, insisting the disembodiment of information "was not inevitable."

## Key Concepts

- [[Posthumanism]] — a point of view defined by four assumptions, in which "there are no essential differences or absolute demarcations between bodily existence and computer simulation, cybernetic mechanism and biological organism, robot teleology and human goals."
- [[Embodiment]] — the flesh Hayles wants to "put back into the picture," erased alike by the liberal subject and the cybernetic posthuman.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the postwar synthesis of information, control, and communication ("steersman") narrated here across three waves.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the figure that makes "the boundaries of the autonomous subject up for grabs," looping within the subject and between subject and environment.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the "analytically driven" mode of discourse against which narrative is positioned as more embodied.
- [[The Body as Data]] — "data made flesh" (Gibson, Neuromancer); embodiment construed as the instantiation of thought/information.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the materiality/information separation Hayles sets out "to contest," in which information is given the dominant position and materiality "runs a distant second."
- Virtuality — "the cultural perception that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns," strategically defined to bridge VR technologies and the pervasive late-century sense of pattern over presence.
- Reflexivity — "the movement whereby that which has been used to generate a system is made, through a changed perspective, to become part of the system it generates"; the second wave's key concept.
- Autopoiesis — Maturana and Varela's "self-making"; informationally closed systems that "turn the cybernetic paradigm inside out."
- Seriation — overlapping replication and innovation (from archaeological anthropology), Hayles's alternative to Kuhnian paradigms and Foucauldian epistemic breaks.
- Skeuomorph — "a design feature that is no longer functional in itself but that refers back to a feature that was functional at an earlier time"; a Janus-faced threshold device.
- Conceptual constellation — the cluster of mutually entailing ideas whose coherence "defines it as an operational unit" and whose assembly/disassembly marks a period.
- Homeostasis — the first wave's central concept (organisms maintaining steady states), later functioning as a skeuomorph.
- Distributed cognition — the posthuman's "collective heterogeneous quality" implying cognition "located in disparate parts that may be in only tenuous communication."

## Key Arguments

- The liberal subject and the posthuman both erase embodiment — "one could argue that the erasure of embodiment is a feature common to both the liberal humanist subject and the cybernetic posthuman."
- Information has been wrongly conceived as substrate-independent — "a defining characteristic of the present cultural moment is the belief that information can circulate unchanged among different material substrates."
- Information always requires a material instantiation — "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium"; treating it as separable is "a prior imaginary act."
- The Platonic backhand and forehand build the information/materiality hierarchy — both moves "share a common ideology — privileging the abstract as the Real and downplaying the importance of material instantiation."
- Shannon's pattern-definition won for quantifiability, not truth — Shannon's information beat MacKay's meaning-based definition because it served "reliable quantification and theoretical generality."
- Wiener intended to extend liberal humanism, not subvert it — "For Wiener, cybernetics was a means to extend liberal humanism, not subvert it."
- The disembodiment of information was contingent, not inevitable — narrative's situated specificities can "replace a teleology of disembodiment with historically contingent stories."

## Important Definitions

- The posthuman (four-assumption definition, Hayles) — privileges informational pattern over material instantiation; treats consciousness as epiphenomenon; treats the body as "the original prosthesis we all learn to manipulate"; configures human being to articulate "seamlessly" with intelligent machines.
- Virtuality (Hayles) — "the cultural perception that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns."
- Reflexivity (Hayles) — "the movement whereby that which has been used to generate a system is made, through a changed perspective, to become part of the system it generates."
- Skeuomorph (Hayles, via archaeological anthropology) — "a design feature that is no longer functional in itself but that refers back to a feature that was functional at an earlier time."
- Information (Shannon, as glossed by Hayles) — "a probability function with no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning. It is a pattern, not a presence."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The Platonic backhand and forehand — paired tennis-stroke metaphors for the two abstracting moves, "a new variation on an ancient game, in which disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form."
- "Data made flesh" — Gibson's Neuromancer phrase for the posthuman body; embodiment as instantiated information.
- The skeuomorph as Janus figure — "looks to past and future, simultaneously reinforcing and undermining both" (the Camry's vinyl "stitching"; the "Catholic Turing Test" confession booth).
- The seriation chart shaped "like a tiger's iris" — attributes waxing and tapering as artifacts and concepts overlap across time (wicks giving way to filaments).
- "Beam me up, Scotty" — the cultural icon of substrate-independent information, the body "dematerialized into an informational pattern and rematerialized, without change."
- "Rememory" (Toni Morrison's Beloved) — the book as "putting back together parts that have lost touch with one another."
