# Source Summary: Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision

**Book:** [[Telematic Embrace]]
**Author:** [[Roy Ascott]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/07 Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision]]

## Orientation

This is the foundational manifesto of Ascott's 1960s cybernetic art — a two-part essay first published in *Cybernetica* 9 (1966) and 10 (1967). Chronologically it is the proto-text of the entire vault art/systems cluster: it predates Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) by one to two years and stakes out the participatory, behavioural, feedback pole of 1960s cybernetic art, against which Burnham's object-to-system [[Systems Esthetics]] is the complementary pole. Ascott states the essay's purpose plainly: "(a) to bring into focus the emergence of a cybernetic vision in art, characterised by an evolving behavioural tendency in the artistic attitudes and forms of this century; and (b) to make provisional recommendations for a Cybernetic Art Matrix (CAM)." Where Burnham reads the history of sculpture as a drift from the object toward the responsive system, Ascott reads the whole of modern art as a drift from a "deterministic vision" toward a "behavioural tendency" in which the artwork is no longer a finished message but a feedback loop completed by the spectator.

Part one builds the theory of [[Behaviourist Art]]: art as an open-ended, retroactive (feedback) process in which "the spectator now engages in decision-making play," the artist plays a game that "is never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play," and "the artifact functions as both matrix and catalyst." Ascott proposes "behavioural" as a more inclusive term than "visual," and surveys the modern tradition under six headings — art as a behavioural analogue, trigger, environment, structure, ritual, and synthesis — citing cubism, futurism, Duchamp, kinetic art, Cage, McLuhan's electric media and "global village," and his own change-paintings. The argument is that modern art, far from anarchic, has "unity in its diversity," marking the transition "from the old deterministic culture to a future shaped by a cybernetic vision."

Part two defines [[The Cybernetic Vision]] not as the art *of* cybernetics or robot art but as *l'esprit cybernétique* — "the spirit of cybernetics, which may inform art and in turn be enriched by it" — a "stance" before it is a technique, grounded in "the idea of the perfectibility of systems" and "the perfectibility of man." It then elaborates the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] across four subsystems (CAM/LIGA: Learning, Information, Generative, Amenities), a self-organising networked institution servicing artists, the new leisured class, and students in a coming "cybernated society," and envisions a network of CAMs that "would constitute the creative element in a 'world brain.'" The rhetorical posture is diagnostic-then-programmatic: a theoretical reading of the recent past followed by a speculative proposal for distributed, satellite-linked creative infrastructure — the conceptual seed of telematic art two decades before Ascott named it. (A dark seam runs through CAM/A's "controlled environments—internal," which prescribe direct chemical and electrical modification of the central nervous system; this prefigures Ascott's later altered-states/technoetics work.)

## Key Concepts

- [[Behaviourist Art]] — Ascott's name for the participatory, feedback-driven art of the modern "behavioural tendency": art that initiates events rather than transmits messages, whose evolution "is governed by the intimate involvement of the spectator."
- [[The Cybernetic Vision]] — *l'esprit cybernétique*; cybernetics as a "spirit" or "stance" that may inform art, distinct from "the art of cybernetics" or robot art.
- [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — the speculative networked, self-organising institution (CAM) built from CAM/LIGA, scaling toward a "world brain"; the proto-telematic infrastructure proposal.
- [[The Cybernated Society]] — the coming automated, instant-communication, leisured order in which cybernation is "a difference not merely in degree but in kind"; producer-oriented and participatory.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the two-way, retroactive feedback loop that "makes an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork / observer"; Ascott (1966) is the precursor/complement to Burnham's later note.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — feedback as the "basic principle" of participatory art; the artifact/observer system "furnishes its own controlling energy."
- [[Cybernetics]] — before a method, "a field of knowledge that shapes our philosophy, influences our behaviour, and extends our thought"; "the science of the organisation of effects" (de Latil).
- [[Systems Theory]] — the self-organising system / organism, "the perfectibility of systems," homeostasis as the not-yet-reached condition of the artwork.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — the object-to-behaviour/system shift in art; Ascott is the earlier participatory pole to Burnham's sculptural object-to-system thesis.
- [[Object and System]] — "the vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour"; aesthetic value migrating from object to system.
- [[Systems Consciousness]] — the cybernetic vision as a consciousness or stance that unifies art, science, and human values.
- [[Emergence]] — the self-organising, growth-bearing artwork as "an organism... which derives its initial programme or code from the artist's creative activity."
- [[Information]] — "Participation produces information"; involvement removes uncertainty about a "set of possibilities."
- [[New Media Art]] — behaviourist/cybernetic art plus the CAM as the participatory genealogy of new media art (extends the committed stub).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the artwork as experiential, feedback technology completed by the spectator (extends the committed stub).
- [[The Global Village]] — McLuhan cited directly; the CAM-network "world brain" as the network-consciousness parallel to the global village.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — the CAM network of person-to-person TV links, holography/visual telex, and satellite-fed reciprocal information.
- [[Kinetic Art]] — Ascott's "behavioural structure" ("structures that behave"); Schöffer, Calder, Tinguely, the Nouvelle Tendance.
- [[Cyborg Art]] — CAM/A's "cybernetic machine extension of skills" cites the "cyborg concept of Manfred Clynes and Nathan S. Kline."

## Key Arguments

- [[Art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour]] — modern art is unified by a single behavioural tendency, from message to event; "unity in its diversity."
- [[The spectator completes the artwork in a feedback loop]] — feedback makes the artist/artwork/observer triad an integral whole; "the game is never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play."

(See also the chapter's further claims, captured at concept level: the cybernetic vision is a stance not a technique; aesthetic value is migrating from object to system and process; the computer is a tool for the mind, an "intelligence amplifier," not an extension of physical power; cybernation is a difference in kind not degree and will be participatory and producer-oriented; art education must be re-founded on system and process in the cybernetic spirit.)

## Important Definitions

- [[Behaviourist Art (Ascott)]] — "a retroactive process of human involvement, in which the artifact functions as both matrix and catalyst."
- [[Cybernetics (Ascott)]] — "before it is a method or an applied science, it is a field of knowledge that shapes our philosophy, influences our behaviour, and extends our thought"; "the science of the organisation of effects, and of the automatic control of effects" (de Latil).

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Artwork as Matrix and Catalyst]] — the work as the substance between two sets of behaviours and the catalyst of the spectator's total behaviour.
- [[The Change Painting]] — the interchangeable transparent-panel works and hinged reliefs; the material emblem of behaviourist participation (Appendix A).
- The behavioural Tarot pack — each artwork as endlessly reshuffleable coordinates that always produce meaning.
- The deterministic "deep-freeze vision" — Renaissance perspectival art as a frozen, time-banished, mechanistic universe viewed "through a window let into the wall."
- CAM as organism / genetic code — "CAM/LIGA serves as a sort of genetic code for the nascent organism CAM."
- The world brain — the international CAM network as the embryonic creative element of a planetary mind.

## Major Tensions

- [[Process vs Product]] — process and event over object and fact; the work as perpetual transition whose resolution comes from the observer.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the spectator as "passive receptor" of a finite message versus the decision-maker who completes the work.
- [[Object vs System]] — art's shift "from the field of objects to the field of behaviour"; aesthetic value from object to system.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the migration from the styled object to the immaterial system and process (Ascott as the participatory pole of this multi-author tension).
- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — "cybernation is merely mechanisation intensified" refuted; "a difference not merely in degree but in kind."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- The two poles of 1960s cybernetic art. Pairing Ascott's participatory/behaviourist feedback art (the spectator completes the work) with Burnham's object-to-system systems esthetics gives a precise frame for the vault's material/immaterial axis — and corrects the chronology: Ascott (1966–67) is the earlier, participatory pole, not a follower of Burnham (1968).
- A genealogy of the network and telematic art. The CAM "world brain," satellite-fed reciprocal information net, person-to-person TV links and "visual telex" read as a 1966 blueprint for telematic art and for network consciousness; pair with McLuhan's global village (cited directly here) and the later Ascott telematics chapters.
- Feedback as the content of art. "The modern means of communication, of feedback and viable interplay—these are the content of art" anchors an essay on reciprocity, the dissolution of the passive viewer, and the artwork as an information-theoretic system whose variety exists only insofar as the observer participates.
- The self-organising / growth artwork as proto-artificial-life. Ascott's "artifact... a self-organising system, an organism" that "derives its initial programme or code from the artist's creative activity" prefigures generative and artificial-life art; pair with Burnham's vitalism and Hayles's systems body.
- The dark utopian seam. CAM/A's prescription of direct chemical and electrical modification of the central nervous system ("direct to the central nervous system of any individual") sits in productive tension with the Han/Harari surveillance-and-control cluster and forward-points to Ascott's own technoetics.
- Cybernation as a difference in kind. Ascott's producer-oriented, participatory "cybernetic-politic" against "mass-production dystopia" reads usefully against Han's later achievement/leisure diagnoses and McLuhan's automation-as-learning-a-living.
