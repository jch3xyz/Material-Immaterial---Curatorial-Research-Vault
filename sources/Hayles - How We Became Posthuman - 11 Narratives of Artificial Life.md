## Orientation

This is Hayles's chapter on the **third wave** of cybernetics — Artificial Life (AL) — and it operates as the culminating case study for the book's master thesis that information "lost its body." Where the second wave (autopoiesis, reflexivity) is figured as a self-organizing **circle**, the third wave is figured as a **spiral**: it imparts "an upward tension to the recursive loops of self-organizing processes" so they "leap outward into the new." Francisco Varela bridges the second and third waves (as Heinz von Foerster bridged the first and second), and the relation between waves is one of *seriation* — "an overlapping pattern of replication and innovation" — rather than rupture. The chapter's analytic posture is **narratological**: Hayles reads AL not by adjudicating its science but by dissecting the stories told "about and through" its programs. She argues that AL's defining "strong claim" — that computer codes are not merely simulating life but *are* alive — is not produced by the programs themselves but constructed through "a multilayered system of metaphoric and material relays."

The chapter's spine is a three-level reading of Thomas Ray's **Tierra** program. The first level is the anthropomorphic naming of the code-creatures ("ancestor," "parasite," "hyperparasite," "mother cell," the "soup," the "reaper"), where explanation and interpretation are "inextricably entwined" and binary polarity-flips become "the high drama of a Darwinian struggle." The second level is the rhetorical positioning of AL as legitimate theoretical biology — Christopher Langton's separation of "logical form" from "material basis," his "life-as-we-know-it" versus "life-as-it-could-be," and the synthetic-chemistry analogy. The third level is the speculative narrative about humanity's relation to its "silicon cousins," where the **observer** — central to second-wave cybernetics but here retreated "to the periphery" — survives explicitly as "narrator and narratee" and where one respondent concedes the strong claim is "in the eye of the beholder." Hayles reads Langton's definition as both tautological and a "site of reinscription" of a form-over-matter dichotomy running "at least since Plato," and she names the resulting worldview the **computational universe**, in which "the essential function of the universe as a whole is processing information."

Crucially, Hayles splits the field rather than simply indicting it. Against the disembodied camp (Langton, Edward Fredkin's "cosmic computer," Hans Moravec and Marvin Minsky downloading consciousness onto "a little disk," Stuart Kauffman's "edge of chaos," and the cellular-automata genealogy back through von Neumann and the McCulloch–Pitts neuron) she sets an **embodied countercurrent**: Varela, Rodney Brooks's "subsumption architecture" and situated robots ("the world is its own best model"; consciousness as a "cheap trick" / "epiphenomenon"), Mark Tilden's nervous-net robots, the evolutionary psychologists Barkow, Cosmides and Tooby, and Antonio Damasio's *Descartes' Error* ("Human mind without human body is not human mind"). The chapter closes on the book's signature normative move: in the AL paradigm "the machine becomes the model for understanding the human" and "the human is transfigured into the posthuman," but posthumanity "is already here"; the open question is "what kind of posthumans we will be," and the answer Hayles defends is that "bodies can never be made of information alone, no matter which side of the computer screen they are on."

## Key Concepts

- Artificial Life (the third wave of cybernetics) — building "life" bottom-up across wetware/hardware/software fronts; figured as a spiral breaking out of autopoietic circularity into emergent novelty. *(Directly stated.)*
- The strong claim — AL's contested assertion that computer codes are not simulating life but are themselves alive, depending on separating logical form from material basis. *(Directly stated.)*
- The Observer — Hayles's recurring figure; central to the second wave, it "retreats to the periphery" in the third yet survives as "narrator and narratee" of AL stories ("it's in the eye of the beholder"). *(Directly stated.)*
- Emergence — properties or programs appearing spontaneously from simple local rules, "often developing in ways not anticipated by the person who created the simulation." *(Directly stated.)*
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the recursive looping beneath emergence, "in which the outputs of a system are repeatedly fed back in as input," magnifying small deviations. *(Directly stated.)*
- The computational universe — the worldview in which everything (humans, machines, the cosmos) is fundamentally information processing; "information is king." *(Directly stated.)*
- [[Cybernetics]] — first/second/third order; AL is named "the grandparent and parent of Artificial Life," and AL "realizes the cybernetic dream" of [[Norbert Wiener]]. *(Directly stated.)*
- [[Posthumanism|Posthumanism]] — the refashioning of the human "into the posthuman" as the machine becomes the model for the human; posthumanity "is already here." *(Directly stated.)*
- [[Embodiment]] — the chapter's normative counterweight ("murmurs from the body"); feelings as "the body murmuring to the mind"; materiality "does useful work." *(Directly stated.)*
- The genotype = phenotype collapse — in AL the "creatures" do not express codes, they *are* codes: "the organism is the code, and the code is the organism." *(Directly stated.)*
- The material base → functionality → representational code transformation — Hayles's schematic for how information technologies (and their narratives) translate a material object into visual codes, eliding material difference. *(Directly stated.)* Relates to [[The Body as Data]].
- Cellular automata and the edge of chaos — on/off finite-state building blocks of the computational universe; computation and life are most likely to emerge "at the boundary between ordered structures and chaotic areas." *(Directly stated.)*
- The liberal humanist subject — "dismantled" in the posthuman turn, leaving "many parties" contesting what counts as (post)human. *(Directly stated.)*

## Key Arguments

- The strong claim of Artificial Life is constructed through narrative, not produced by the programs — AL's "aliveness" is established through "a multilayered system of metaphoric and material relays," dissected across three narrative levels around Tierra. *(Directly stated; central thesis.)*
- Artificial Life reinscribes the Platonic privileging of form over matter — Langton's definition is tautological *and* a "site of reinscription" of assumptions running "at least since Plato": form separable from, and privileged over, matter. *(Hayles's interpretive synthesis about AL; she attributes the Plato lineage, not Langton.)*
- In Artificial Life the machine becomes the model for the human — contrasting AI (human as the measure; the Turing test) with AL (intelligence evolved within the machine; consciousness as epiphenomenon), Hayles concludes the human is "transfigured into the posthuman." *(Directly stated.)*
- The computational universe is a contingent ideology that privileges information — it "becomes dangerous when it goes from being a useful heuristic to an ideology," and its framework rigs the options (fight the machines or become them) so the human race's "days are numbered." *(Directly stated.)*
- Bodies can never be made of information alone — marshalling the embodied countercurrent (Varela, Brooks, Tilden, evolutionary psychology, Damasio) as "fracture lines," Hayles insists embodiment "matters" and posthumanity's open question is "what kind of posthumans we will be." *(Directly stated; the chapter's normative close.)*
- The three waves relate by seriation, not rupture — the third wave is "again one of seriation, an overlapping pattern of replication and innovation," with Varela bridging the second and third. *(Directly stated.)* Relates to [[Systems Theory]].

## Important Definitions

- Artificial Life (Langton) — "the study of man-made systems that exhibit behaviors characteristic of natural living systems," synthesizing "life-like behaviors within computers and other artificial media." *(Directly stated.)*
- The strong claim (Langton) — "the 'logical form' of an organism can be separated from its material basis of construction, and that 'aliveness' will be found to be a property of the former, not of the latter." *(Directly stated.)*
- The three research fronts of AL — wetware (artificial biological life), hardware (robots / embodied life-forms), software (programs instantiating emergent or evolutionary processes). *(Directly stated.)*
- First-order vs second-order emergence (Steels) — first-order = any property generated by component interactions; second-order = emergent properties that "bestow additional functionality on the system, particularly the ability to process information." *(Directly stated.)*
- Autonomy (Varela & Bourgine) — the living's "basic and fundamental capacity to be, to assert their existence and bring forth a world that is significant and pertinent without being pre-digested in advance." *(Directly stated.)*

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The spiral vs. the circle — the third wave as "a spring compressed and suddenly released," leaping "outward into the new" beyond autopoietic circularity. *(Directly stated.)*
- The "soup," the "reaper," and the "slicer" — Tierra's primeval-soup memory block, its death program, and its time-allocator, figured as agents of origin, mortality, and fate. *(Directly stated.)*
- The disembodied flying POV / the Genesis script — the Santa Fe video makes the viewer "a disembodied pov flying over the lifeworld of the 'creatures'," following "the script of Genesis." *(Directly stated.)*
- "Murders by dissection" vs "procreate by emergence" — analysis against synthesis; AL claims to escape the vitalist charge against reductionism. *(Directly stated.)*
- Consciousness as a "cheap trick" / "epiphenomenon" — Brooks's deflationary figures for consciousness as a late, non-essential add-on. *(Directly stated.)*
- "Silicon cousins" / the road not taken — humans' kinship with AL-forms as an alternate evolutionary pathway. *(Directly stated.)*
- The little disk holding a downloaded consciousness — Minsky's and Moravec's emblem of disembodied immortality. *(Directly stated.)* Symbol of [[Material vs Immaterial]].
- The death's-head / the reaper — mortality intruding on the pastoral idyll of the soup. *(Directly stated.)*

## Major Tensions

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the chapter's organizing opposition; the Macy split between "a disembodied view of information and one for a contextualized embodied view" reappears in AL. *(Directly stated.)*
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — form privileged over matter, information as "immaterial fluid" against the "resistance of materiality"; the Platonic form/matter dichotomy. *(Directly stated.)*
- Simulation vs. life (model vs. reality) — if AL is "only" simulation its results are "artifacts"; the strong claim dissolves the worry by declaring the programs alive. *(Directly stated.)*
- AI vs. AL — human as measure (cognition as logic; Turing test) against machine as model (cognition as nervous-system operation; bottom-up). *(Directly stated.)*
- Consciousness-as-essence (Moravec/Minsky) vs. consciousness-as-epiphenomenon (Brooks) — two opposed narratives of the posthuman. *(Directly stated.)*
- Order vs. chaos — too much order blocks information transfer, too much chaos blocks storage; computation and life live "at the edge of chaos." *(Directly stated.)*
