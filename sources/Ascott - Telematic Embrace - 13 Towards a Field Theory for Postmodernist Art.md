## Orientation

This is the hinge essay of *Telematic Embrace* — the bridge from Ascott's 1960s cybernetic/behaviourist art to the 1980s telematic/network art that gives the volume its name. Delivered on the panel "Towards Post-Modernist Form" at the College Art Association Annual Conference (New York, February 1978) and first published in *Leonardo* 13, no. 1 (February 1980), it proposes "a field theory that would replace the formalist modernist aesthetic." The new paradigm "takes as its focus not form but behaviour": the artwork is not a one-way message to be decoded but "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer)," a "field of psychic probability, highly entropic," in which "the final responsibility for meaning lies with the viewer." This is the clearest single statement of Ascott's career-spanning move from the artwork-as-object to the artwork-as-system, and from the spectator as passive receiver to the **participator** who completes the work.

Rhetorically the essay works by negation and by import. It first defines formalist modernism by what it "was not" — "connective, inclusive, transactional, associative, referential, interactive, changeable, discontinuous, multilayered, impure, ambiguous" — and then claims postmodernist art as the encompassing of exactly those qualities, a "connective paradigm, which in turn demands a connective criticism." Because the new model has no vocabulary inside art, Ascott reaches outward: to cybernetics (Wiener's information-as-transmission-of-alternatives), the new physics (Heisenberg's indeterminacy, the observer who is "part of what he is observing"), the new linguistics and Sufi thought (Eco's "gambling on the possibility of semiosis"), psi research (Vallée's physics of information), and Eastern spiritual models of consciousness (Capra's *The Tao of Physics*). The argument is built to interlock these previously unrelated fields rather than to close them.

For the vault this chapter is the strongest single primary-text anchor for EXTENDING the Burnham-realized systems/art cluster — [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], [[Reciprocal Communication]], [[Systems Esthetics]], [[Object and System]], [[Material vs Immaterial]] — from the Ascott (participatory/behavioural) pole, complementing Burnham's object→system spine from the other side of 1960s–70s systems-oriented art. It also announces a transition "from materialist culture" toward "the spiritual in art" — the 1980 seed of Ascott's later technoetics — and ends on a methodological commitment: networking as "the antithesis of reductionism," and inconclusiveness as "the essence of the postmodernist condition." The later coinages (telenoia, the telematic embrace, network consciousness, cyberception, technoetics) do NOT appear by name here; only their seeds.

## Key Arguments

- Art is a behavioural field in which the viewer generates meaning — the sender→receiver model is replaced by a two-system matrix; meaning is negotiated, not transmitted, so the participator is constitutive.
- Formalist modernism is Newtonian and therefore faux modernist — despite claiming to overthrow the Newtonian worldview, formalist modernism is "totally Newtonian" — linear, irreversible, progress-bound — and so not truly modern.
- The language of a field theory of art comes from cybernetics physics and consciousness research — the new paradigm needs "new terms, a new language, a new set of metaphors," found not "among 'art' terms at all" but in Wiener, Heisenberg, Eco, Vallée, and Capra.
- The art participator is the quantum observer the consciousness is part of the observed — the measurement-problem insight (the observer participates in the observed) and the cybernetic insight (information = transmission of alternatives) ground the participator ontologically.
- Networking is the antithesis of reductionism and the postmodernist condition is inconclusive — a connective network of ideas cannot be closed without betraying its form; inconclusiveness is constitutive, not a failure.
