## Orientation

The Postface (pp. 263–264) is Lippard's two-page retrospective verdict, written in her own curatorial voice in 1973, on the conceptual-art moment her anthology documents. It is the single most concentrated statement of the book's master thesis and its self-critique — the keystone essay against which the year-by-year chronicle should be read. Three moves structure it, and they do not resolve into a single judgment: a market verdict (failure), an aesthetic credit (success), and a political verdict (failure).

The first move is the **recuperation argument**, the book's central self-criticism. The hope animating the 1969 Preface — that the [[Material vs Immaterial|dematerialization]] of the art object (xerox sheets, documentary photographs, projects never realized, words spoken but unrecorded) would make conceptual art unsellable and so "forcibly free" artists "from the tyranny of a commodity status and market-orientation" — is declared "for the most part unfounded." Three years on, "the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums" in "the world's most prestigious galleries." Lippard draws the structural conclusion: the commodity form was never a property of the object's materiality, so dematerializing the object changed nothing economically. "[A]rt and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries."

The second move, marked by the concessive turn "On the other hand," separates the aesthetic ledger from the economic one. Even granting the market failure, Lippard credits "idea art" with a real gain: "[a]n informational, documentary idiom has provided a vehicle for art ideas that were encumbered and obscured by formal considerations," and idea art "sets up new critical criteria by which to view and vitalize itself" — the self-vitalizing loop she attributes to "the Art-Language group and its growing number of adherents." This is conceptual art as a parallel to (not a replacement for) the object, generating its own reflexive critical apparatus.

The third move is the **political and interdisciplinary failure**. Conceptual art "has not... as yet broken down the real barriers between the art context and those external disciplines—social, scientific, and academic—from which it draws sustenance." Interactions between politics, philosophy, mathematics, literature and art remain "at a very primitive level" (with named exceptions: Hans Haacke, Daniel Buren, Adrian Piper, the Rosario group, Douglas Huebler). The diagnosis is sociological: a "ghetto mentality" in the "narrow and incestuous art world," whose "resentful reliance on a very small group of dealers, curators, critics, editors, and collectors" leaves them "bound by invisible apron strings to the 'real world's' power structure." This passage is the documented seed of Lippard's own subsequent turn to art and politics, and it holds the book's deepest tension — between aesthetic autonomy and worldly engagement — explicitly unresolved.

## Key Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the vault's titular axis; the Postface is its art-historical instance. *Dematerialization* is Lippard's name for art's move from the durable material object to immaterial idea, document, situation, or unrealized project. Her verdict — that the immaterial did not free art from the market — is the art-world sibling of Hayles's claim that disembodiment is an ideology, not an escape.
- [[Information]] — conceptual art's "informational, documentary idiom" is an art-world cousin of the Han/Hayles information aesthetic; the document carries the idea where the formal object once did.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the Art-Language self-critical loop ("new critical criteria by which to view and vitalize itself") and the Haacke exception point to art's "systems aesthetics." Linked as a cousin lineage, not merged — art-world systems aesthetics (Haacke, LeWitt, Burnham) is distinct from Hayles's cybernetic systems theory.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — Hayles's thesis that the immaterial is materially produced is the theoretical counterpart to Lippard's empirical finding that dematerialized art remained a market commodity.
- [[Virtuality]] — the catalogue/magazine piece "shown... in infinite locations at one time" prefigures information interpenetrating material objects (light cross-link).
- [[Exhibition Value]] — Han's value-as-circulation illuminates how the dematerialized work is recuperated precisely through frictionless display and reproduction rather than the durable object (interpretive).

## Key Arguments

- Dematerialization failed to free art from the commodity/market — conceptual art was recuperated by the very market it hoped to escape; "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries."
- Idea art's aesthetic contribution was real even where its economic resistance failed — the informational/documentary idiom freed art ideas from formal encumbrance and generated new self-critical criteria.
- Conceptual art failed politically — it did not break the barrier between the art context and external disciplines; its "ghetto mentality" leaves it no better equipped to affect the world than less ephemeral art.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- "[T]he tyranny of a commodity status" — the market as a despotic regime the ephemeral object was hoped to escape.
- "[I]nvisible apron strings" — the art-world elite tied to the "real world's" power structure by unseen domestic bonds; dependency disguised as autonomy.
- "[G]hetto mentality" / the "narrow and incestuous art world" — the art world as an enclosed, self-reproducing enclave.
- The xerox sheet — the emblematic dematerialized, supposedly unsellable artifact that the market nonetheless absorbed.

## Major Tensions

- Resistance vs Recuperation — the immaterial, unsellable form was meant to escape the commodity and was absorbed by it.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the art-historical instance of the vault axis.
- Aesthetic autonomy vs political engagement — art begun from non-aesthetic goals "rarely produces anything more than illustration or polemic," yet conceptual art's failure is precisely its inability to engage the external; Lippard wants both and holds them unresolved.
