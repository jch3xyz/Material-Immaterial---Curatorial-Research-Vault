# Source Summary: Chapter 14 — Money: The Poor Man's Credit Card

## Orientation

This is a Part II case-study chapter, and its medium is **money**. McLuhan treats money exactly as he treats every other technology: as an extension of man, "a staple, a natural resource," and a storehouse and translator of communally achieved work and skill. He opens by setting aside the psychoanalytic "filthy lucre" tradition (Ferenczi's money as "odorless dehydrated filth that has been made to shine," elaborating Freud's "Character and Anal Erotism") precisely because it "does not correspond sufficiently to the nature and function of money in society." In its place he installs the book's master frame — money is a medium — and reads the *history* of money (commodity money → metallic coin → paper/"representative" money → the credit card) as the same arc the book draws for every technology: from the tactile, magical, participatory, inclusive forms of nonliterate culture, through the visual, fragmenting, specialist intensification driven by the phonetic alphabet and print, and back again toward inclusive, organic, "tribal" wholeness under electric automation.

The chapter reuses the Part I apparatus almost wholesale. Money is an extension of man whose study illuminates every other medium ("any study of one medium helps us to understand all the others. Money is no exception"); it is "a specialist technology like writing" that "separates work from the other social functions," "action at a distance, both in space and in time." Its commodity stage shows the staple as a medium of communication (whales' teeth, rats, leather, tobacco, cigarettes), retaining always "its commodity and community character" — the lesson Robinson Crusoe learns at the wreck, where the useless coins are meaningless apart from communal participation. Its print stage de-magicks money as writing de-magicked speech: "when printed money supplanted gold the compelling aura of it disappeared," and the abstract printed money of the industrial age becomes "an adjunct of that specialist alphabetic technology." McLuhan ties the whole modern apparatus of prices, markets, and statistics to print literacy: a fixed-price system "does not become possible until printing prepares the ground," and "the West is little aware of the way in which the world of prices and numbering is supported by the pervasive visual culture of literacy."

The chapter's signature payoff is money's **electric reversal**. As work is replaced "by the sheer movement of information, money as a store of work merges with the informational forms of credit and credit card"; "from coin to paper currency, and from currency to credit card there is a steady progression toward commercial exchange as the movement of information itself," approaching "once more the character of tribal money." The credit card is thus one of the book's clearest worked examples of the overheated specialist medium flipping back toward its inclusive, retribalized opposite — and "electric technology puts the very concept of money in jeopardy." Alongside this run two further durable threads: the "Midas touch" restatement of the general law that every extension bends all other functions to its own form, and a closing bridge (via Elias Canetti's *Crowds and Power*) between the money hoard and the crowd, both driven by "the urge to rapid and unlimited growth." The chapter mints no genuinely new concept; it is anchored by the chapter-distinctive concept note [[Money as a Medium]].
