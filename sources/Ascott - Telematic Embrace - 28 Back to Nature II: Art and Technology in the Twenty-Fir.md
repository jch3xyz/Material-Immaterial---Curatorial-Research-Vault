## Orientation

This 1995 essay (first published in *Convergence: The Journal of Research into New Media Technologies* 1, no. 1, Spring 1995; an earlier shorter German version, "Zurück zur (künstlichen) Natur," appeared in 1993) is the hinge chapter between Ascott's mature *telematic* phase and his late *moistmedia / technoetic* turn. Its thesis is deliberately counterintuitive and programmatic: the most artificial, immaterial technologies — telematic networks, and next nanotechnology and artificial life — will carry us "back to nature," but to a *new* nature, "second-order nature, emergent nature, Nature II." Ascott argues that the old metaphor of nature (the pure, the pastoral, the god-given) is exhausted, "past its 'sell by' date," and that the binary oppositions that sustained it (town/country, natural/artificial, material/immaterial) are dissolving under ubiquitous telematic networks and molecular biology. (Confidence: Directly stated — the publication history, the "sell by" image, and the central paradox are all in the raw text.)

In their place Ascott proposes the **connectivist paradigm** — "everything is connected, everything interacts with and affects everything else" — grounded in [[Second-Order Cybernetics (Ascott)|second-order cybernetics]] (the observer included in the system), in connectionism / neural nets / parallel distributed processing in science, in quantum non-locality (Bohm, Bell, Aspect), and in the holistic, anti-reductionist sciences of metaphor (Sheldrake's morphic resonance, Lovelock's Gaia). He renames telematic art practice "connectivism" so as to flee the romantic/modernist baggage of the word "art." The essay also forecasts the **rematerialization of art** — the convergence of the electronic/virtual trajectory with the biological/molecular trajectory, "A marriage of the immaterial and the material" — which is the explicit seed of the later "the future will be moist" thesis. Along the way it develops Ascott's signature couplings: *electronic space* ↔ *molecular time*; the *virtualisation* of presence and the *distributed self*; the parallel between artificial life (AL) and interactive art (IA) ("For 'life,' read 'art'!"); and the shift from "What is reality?" to "How do we interact with a proliferation of separate realities?" (Confidence: Directly stated.)

Within the vault this chapter is the **`Material vs Immaterial` turning point**: Ascott is the first author to argue not for one-directional dematerialization (Lippard/Burnham) or for immateriality (Han/Hayles) but for their reconciliation — a virtualisation that flips into a new materialisation. It is also the strongest single statement of Ascott's *participatory/connectivist* pole against Burnham's *object→system* `Systems Esthetics` (the interpretive Ascott/Burnham polarity — not a citation Ascott made), and it cross-links forcefully to McLuhan (the rear-view-mirror effect, the penalties of literacy, the changing ratio of the senses), to the Moravec/Hayles/Haraway posthuman-and-bionic-body problematic, to Gibson's *Neuromancer* cyberspace, and to the O'Gieblyn register of re-enchantment and transcendence (the séance/spiritualism list; art as mediation to the post-human as it once mediated to the gods). (Confidence: Directly stated for the McLuhan/Moravec/Gibson citations; Interpretive synthesis for the Burnham polarity and the Hayles/Haraway/O'Gieblyn bridges.)

## Key Concepts

- [[Connectivism]] — Ascott's name for both the worldview and the renamed telematic art practice: "everything is connected, everything interacts with and affects everything else"; in art it "calls for interactivity, reciprocity, and negotiation."
- [[Nature II]] — the title concept: a "new nature, second-order nature, emergent nature, Nature II," produced by the convergence of telematics and molecular technology; we become participants in, not antagonists of, nature.
- [[The Rematerialization of Art]] — the prediction that the virtualizing (electronic) and materializing (biological/nano) trajectories converge: "A marriage of the immaterial and the material"; the direct precursor to [[Moistmedia]].
- [[Electronic Space and Molecular Time]] — Ascott's space/time couplet: "Just as space becomes 'electronic,' so time is becoming 'molecular.'"
- [[The Distributed Self]] — telematics virtualises presence and distributes the self across the "connection matrix": "Here and not here."
- [[Network Consciousness]] — "the telematisation of consciousness on the planet is carrying us all forward to another level of evolutionary development."
- [[Telematic Art]] — the practice this chapter calls "connectivism"; "all art practice involving computer-mediated systems and electronic media of whatever complexion is by definition telematic."
- [[Moistmedia]] — the convergence of the wet biological and the dry electronic; named here in nascent form as the "marriage of the immaterial and the material." (extends Ascott's own later chapters)
- [[Cybernetics]] — "the study of control and communication in living and artificial systems"; the parallel between cybernetics' own evolution and our evolving relation to nature. (reused, cross-author)
- [[Artificial Life]] — the AL ↔ IA parallel via Langton: bottom-up, distributed, local determination of behavior "with no global controller." (reused, cross-author)
- [[Emergence]] — "emergent, emergent behaviours," "emergent nature," life as "the behavior that emerges from all of the local interactions." (reused)
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — connectionism, parallel distributed processing, neural networks; the distributed self and distributed authorship. (reused)
- [[Posthumanism]] — "the potential for consciousness to evolve beyond the human organism," into silicon "descendants" (Moravec). (reused)
- [[The Cyborg]] — "We are all becoming, to a greater or lesser extent, bionic"; the line between natural and artificial systems rendered meaningless. (reused)
- [[Embodiment]] — prostheses that "amplify and extend the body and its five senses but also augment cognition and memory." (reused)
