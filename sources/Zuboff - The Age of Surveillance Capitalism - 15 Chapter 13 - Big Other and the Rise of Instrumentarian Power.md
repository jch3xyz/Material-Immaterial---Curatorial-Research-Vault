# Source Summary: Chapter 13 — Big Other and the Rise of Instrumentarian Power

## Orientation

This is the theoretical keystone of the book's Part III. Having established the economic *logic* of surveillance capitalism in earlier chapters — the unilateral claim on human experience as free raw material, refined into behavioral data, prediction products, and behavioral futures markets — Zuboff here names the *form of power* that logic produces and the *apparatus* through which it operates. Two coinages carry the chapter. **Big Other** is "the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior" — the ubiquitous digital apparatus, deliberately staged against Orwell's Big Brother ("There is no brother here of any kind"). **Instrumentarian power** is "a new species of power" that, unlike totalitarianism, is morally agnostic, operates remotely and in stealth, and "replac[es] the engineering of souls with the engineering of behavior." Its way of knowing is [[Radical Indifference]] — "a form of observation without witness" — and its aim is not domination of the soul but [[Guaranteed Outcomes]], "a condition of certainty without terror."

The rhetorical posture is at once analytic and prophetic. Zuboff defines instrumentarianism almost entirely by *contrast* with totalitarianism (the chapter's Figure 4, "Two Species of Power"): state versus market, total possession versus total certainty, terror versus indifference, the engineering of souls versus the engineering of behavior. Her warning is that the *absence* of terror invites complacency — "Because it does not claim our bodies for some grotesque regime of pain and murder, we are prone to undervalue its effects and lower our guard." She enlists B. F. Skinner's radical behaviorism (the "technology of behavior" Skinner foresaw but lacked the instruments to build) and Hannah Arendt's analysis of totalitarianism and the laboring society ("The trouble with modern theories of behaviorism is not that they are wrong but that they could become true") as the chapter's twin philosophical anchors.

The abstractions are then grounded in three concrete fields: a **market project of total certainty** that fulfills Skinner's vision and erodes democracy from within; the empirical decline of **social trust** in the US as the void instrumentarian power rushes to fill, "substituting machines for social relations, which amounts to the substitution of certainty for society"; and the **China syndrome** — the social credit system read not as "digital totalitarianism" but as "the apotheosis of instrumentarian power" fused with an authoritarian state. The chapter closes at "a fork in the road": a synthetic third modernity that strengthens democracy versus surveillance capitalism's antidemocratic third modernity, "a future of certainty accomplished without violence" in which "The price we pay is not with our bodies but with our freedom." It is the densest cross-bridge in the corpus to Han ([[Digital Panopticon]], [[Friendly Big Brother]]), Harari ([[Dataism]], [[Society as Algorithm]]), and Kurzweil (ubiquitous, disappearing computing — "The internet will disappear into Big Other").

## Key Concepts

- [[Big Other]] — the sensate, networked, computational apparatus through which surveillance capital renders, monitors, computes, and modifies behavior; the means of behavioral modification; the deliberate anti–Big Brother.
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — a new species of power that instrumentalizes behavior toward guaranteed outcomes; replaces the engineering of souls with the engineering of behavior; morally agnostic, remote, stealthy.
- [[Radical Indifference]] — instrumentarianism's way of knowing: observation without witness that reduces experience to measurable behavior while remaining indifferent to its meaning; produces "equivalence without equality."
- [[Guaranteed Outcomes]] — the aim of instrumentarian power: "a condition of certainty without terror"; the substitution of certainty for society.
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — Zuboff's deliberate echo of Marx's "means of production"; the new locus of power is ownership of Big Other.
- [[The Division of Learning in Society]] — the new axis of social ordering displacing the division of labor; instrumentarian power usurps the right to answer "Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?"
- [[The Other-One]] — Big Other's viewpoint: humans reduced to "organisms that behave," to "the lowest common denominator of sameness."
- [[Surveillance]] — realized here as a research focus: state and market surveillance, surveillance-as-a-service, the internet of things as a surveillance channel, predictive policing, social credit.
- [[Quantified Self]] — the rendering of body, location, and conduct as data (Fitbit, pacemaker, smart utility meter, biometric ID) feeding the apparatus.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the reservoir poached for surplus; "the surplus that is ripped from your life."
- [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]] — the economies-of-action operations by which behavior is shaped toward preselected outcomes (explicit in the China account).
- [[Economies of Action]] — the actuation logic by which the apparatus produces preselected behaviors, "performing to plan."
- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the mental agency and self-possession that instrumentarian automaticity submerges; named here as what is lost.
- [[The Right to Sanctuary]] — the refuge of private life and public freedom that instrumentarian power displaces.
- [[The Will to Will]] — the self-determination steadily displaced by the apparatus.
- [[Inevitabilism]] — the ideology that equates surveillance capitalism with nature, "a thing that can only be joined or endured."
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the parent logic; the "puppet master" willing through Big Other.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's subject-side account of the same ubiquitous frictionless surveillance Zuboff theorizes from the apparatus/capital side.
- [[Big Data]] — the dream of total prediction; Zuboff's "market project of total certainty" in the surveillance register.
- [[Dataism]] — Harari/Han's data-worldview; "the substitution of certainty for society" is its political face.
- [[Society as Algorithm]] — Harari's concept; China's social credit = "the automation of society."
- [[The Body as Data]] — extended here by the rendering of body and conduct as data (Fitbit, pacemaker, smart meter, biometric ID).
- [[Smart Power]] — Han's power that pleases rather than represses; Big Other "can mimic intimacy through the tireless devotion of the One Voice."
- [[Society of Control]] — the Deleuzean control society to which instrumentarian power is a candidate successor.

## Key Arguments

- [[Instrumentarianism is a new species of power not a form of totalitarianism]] — defined by contrast: state/total possession/terror/engineering of souls versus market/total certainty/indifference/engineering of behavior; the danger is *under*reaction precisely because there is no terror.
- [[Instrumentarian power erodes democracy from within by substituting certainty for society]] — it grows by declaration, self-authorization, euphemism, and stealth, not by suspending institutions; it fills the void of collapsed social trust.
- [[Surveillance capitalism may realize Skinners behavioral utopia but the knowledge is proprietary]] — Big Other supplies the technology of behavior Skinner foresaw but could never build; the prize "is now a flourishing fact."
- [[We are not the product but the raw material of surveillance capitalism]] — "You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass."
- [[Inevitabilism is an ideological fraud that erases human agency]] — equating the apparatus with a river or glacier makes friction the only sin and submission the only rational response.
- [[Big Other outruns society and law in the destruction of sanctuary]] — the steady displacement of the will to will, self-determination, and the sanctuary of private life and public freedom.

## Important Definitions

- [[Big Other (Zuboff)]] — "the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior"; defines the apparatus by its dual function of knowing and doing.
- [[Instrumentarian Power (Zuboff)]] — power that instrumentalizes and controls human experience to predictably shape behavior toward others' profitable ends, operating "from the vantage point of the Other-One."
- [[Radical Indifference (Zuboff)]] — "a new way of knowing… a form of observation without witness" that reduces experience to measurable behavior and is indifferent to its meaning.
- [[The Other-One (Meyer and Skinner via Zuboff)]] — the behaviorist viewpoint from which the human appears as "organisms that behave."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Puppet Master Not the Puppet]] — surveillance capital is the puppet master; Big Other is the puppet — distinguishing the *capital* that wills from the *apparatus* that acts.
- [[The Abandoned Carcass]] — "You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass"; Big Other poaches behavior for surplus and discards the meaning, "not unlike the monstrous slaughter of elephants for ivory."
- [[The Veil]] — the "robotized veil of abstraction" across which social relations and economic exchange now occur.

## Major Tensions

- [[Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism]] — the chapter's explicit Figure-4 opposition: market/total certainty/indifference versus state/total possession/terror.
- [[Big Brother vs Big Other]] — terror, relationship, and intimacy versus indifference, no relationship, and objectification — the keystone cross-author tension with Han's Friendly Big Brother.
- [[Certainty vs Uncertainty]] — "the substitution of certainty for society"; certainty achieved at the price of freedom.
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — Skinner's behaviorism ("acts of ignorance that we foolishly value as free will") versus the autonomy that instrumentarianism treats as friction.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **The anti-Big Brother**: use Big Other to argue that the dominant twentieth-century image of surveillance (Orwell, terror, the engineering of souls) misnames the twenty-first-century apparatus, which is indifferent rather than punitive — and pair it with Han's Friendly Big Brother and the Digital Panopticon to show two thinkers rewriting Orwell from opposite sides (subject vs. apparatus).
- **Material/immaterial in the surveillance register**: "The price we pay is not with our bodies but with our freedom" reframes the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` spine — domination that leaves the body intact and instead severs "our insides from our outsides," targeting agency rather than flesh; contrast with biopolitics and Orwellian terror.
- **Certainty as the new political object**: read "the substitution of certainty for society" against Harari's Dataism / Society as Algorithm and Han's Big Data to build an essay on the dream of total prediction as the displacement of politics by computation.
- **Behaviorism realized**: trace the Skinner-to-Big-Other line — a technology of behavior that finally has its instruments — and its bearing on free will, autonomy as "friction," and the right to the future tense.
- **The carcass, not the product**: deploy "You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass" to dismantle the popular "if it's free, you are the product" cliché and clarify what surveillance capitalism actually extracts and discards.
- **China as apotheosis, not exception**: use the social-credit reading ("how similar it is to the path technology is taking elsewhere") to resist the "digital totalitarianism" framing and argue for a single instrumentarian logic with divergent state–market entanglements.
