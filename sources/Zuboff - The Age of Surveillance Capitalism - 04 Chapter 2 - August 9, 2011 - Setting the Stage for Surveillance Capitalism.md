## Orientation

This is the **stage-setting** chapter of Zuboff's book — the historical and economic scaffolding erected *before* the formal anatomy of surveillance capitalism (behavioral surplus, prediction products, instrumentarian power) that begins in Chapter 3. It does not yet define those terms; instead it establishes the **[[Conditions of Existence]]** — the Durkheimian causal category Zuboff treats as "a touchstone for us throughout this book" — that "summoned both the Apple 'miracle' and surveillance capitalism's subsequent gestation and growth." Its argumentative engine is **[[The Collision]]** between two long-running historical vectors: the centuries-long process of **[[Individualization]]** that produced the **[[Second Modernity]]** (the condition of psychological individuality, after Beck and Bauman), and the decades-long imposition of the **[[Neoliberalism|neoliberal paradigm]]**, whose aim is "to reverse, subdue, impede, and even destroy the individual urge toward psychological self-determination and moral agency."

Zuboff anchors the whole chapter on a single date — **[[August 9, 2011]]** — and three geographically scattered events that emblematize the stakes: Apple surpassing Exxon Mobil as the world's most valuable corporation (the digital promise, read through **[[The Apple Inversion]]**); the London riots (the grim legacy of "growth through exclusion," the detonation of "inequality's facts" against "inequality's feelings"); and Spanish citizens demanding **[[The Right to Be Forgotten]]** (democracy clawing the digital future back from Google). Around this triptych she develops the theory of the two modernities (rooted in Durkheim's account of the division of labor), the **[[Neoliberalism|neoliberal habitat]]** (Hayek's "extended order," Friedman, the Jensen–Meckling **[[The Shareholder Value Movement|shareholder value movement]]**, and the deliberate demolition of Polanyi's **[[The Double Movement]]**), Piketty's account of the instability of the second modernity (r > g, neofeudalism, "capitalism should not be eaten raw"), and a corrective reading of Schumpeter's **[[Economic Mutation|mutation]]** against Silicon Valley's "creative destruction" cult.

The chapter closes by naming **[[Surveillance Capitalism]]** as the "new breed of economic power" that "filled the void" left by the failed/incomplete Apple inversion, foreshadows the new harms it poses to "the sanctity of the individual" — chief among them **[[The Right to the Future Tense]]** and **[[The Right to Sanctuary]]** — and ends with a "Naming and Taming" coda that frames the entire book as an exercise in identifying the new form "on its own terms and in its own words." This is the **historical-economic ground floor** of the vault's surveillance cluster: it realizes the committed forward-stubs **[[Surveillance]]** and **[[Surveillance Capitalism]]**, grounding surveillance as "a foundational mechanism in the transformation of investment into profit," and is the chronological-economic substrate beneath Han's **[[Digital Panopticon]]** and Harari's **[[Dataism]]**.

## Key Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — first NAMED here in the narrative ("a unique logic of accumulation in which surveillance is a foundational mechanism in the transformation of investment into profit"); the master concept of the whole book, given its early economic-logic kernel.
- [[Surveillance]] — the committed long-standing vault forward-stub, realized here in its economic-historical grounding (surveillance as a foundational mechanism of accumulation); the hub note for Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] / Harari's [[Dataism]] surveillance cluster.
- [[Second Modernity]] — the historical condition of psychological individuality: people "born to a sense of psychological individuality, with its double-edged birthright of liberation and necessity"; distinct from first-modernity mass society and from neoliberal "individualism."
- [[Third Modernity]] — the hoped-for synthesis that "transcends the collision," institutionalized as "a new rational digital capitalism aligned with a society of individuals and supported by democratic institutions"; the book's normative horizon.
- [[Individualization]] — the centuries-long modernization process that made each life "an open-ended reality to be discovered rather than a certainty to be enacted"; explicitly distinguished from neoliberal "individualism" and from psychological "individuation."
- [[The Apple Inversion]] — the digital flip of mass-production logic, shifting "the focus of consumption from the mass to the individual"; the chapter's master example of a potential third-modernity capitalism and its "transition failure."
- [[The Collision]] — the central structural concept: "the destructive collision of two opposing historical forces" — modernizing self-determination versus the neoliberal paradigm aimed at reversing it.
- [[Conditions of Existence]] — Zuboff's Durkheimian causal category: capitalism is summoned by "the needs of people in a time and place," not by the drive to produce more; the chapter's methodological keystone.
- [[Neoliberalism]] — the "decades-long elaboration and implementation" of radical free-market doctrine (Hayek/Friedman) that "provided the habitat in which surveillance capitalism could flourish."
- [[The Double Movement]] — Polanyi's name for the countervailing institutions that "tether" the market to society; their deliberate "demolition" under neoliberalism is a central historical claim.
- [[The Shareholder Value Movement]] — Jensen and Meckling's 1976 reinterpretation of the manager as a "parasite" on ownership; "another crucial antecedent to the surveillance capitalist corporation."
- [[Economic Mutation]] — Zuboff's recovery of Schumpeter's real theory against the "creative destruction" cult: genuine mutation "creates," is rare and slow, and is bound to new consumer needs through democratic institutions.
- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — named here (fully developed in Ch.11): the elemental right of individual sovereignty to act free of others' manipulation of the future; embodied in the Spanish struggle.
- [[The Right to Sanctuary]] — named here (developed in Ch.17): the second elemental right of individual sovereignty.
- [[The Right to Be Forgotten]] — the concrete legal struggle (Spanish Data Protection Agency → Court of Justice of the EU, 2014) that instantiates the right to the future tense.
- [[Contracts of Adhesion]] — the "weightless" digital terms-of-service mechanism that strips consent; folds click-wrap, browse-wrap, and Radin's "private eminent domain."

## Key Arguments

- [[Capitalism is summoned by the conditions of existence not the drive to produce more]] — the Durkheimian thesis: new market forms are "most productive when they are shaped by an allegiance to the actual demands and mentalities of people."
- [[Our era is a collision between inequalitys facts and inequalitys feelings]] — inequality has reverted to a feudal pattern but "we, the people, have not"; the mismatch detonates into the rage of the London riots.
- [[Raw capitalism is antisocial and must be cooked by democratic institutions]] — the Polanyi/Piketty thesis: the self-regulating market "left to itself" contains "powerful forces of divergence."
- [[Neoliberalism provided the habitat for surveillance capitalism]] — neoliberal ideology + the shareholder-value firm + the demolished double movement created the policy vacuum and corporate form in which surveillance capitalism could root.
- [[Silicon Valleys creative destruction misreads Schumpeter]] — the "boys and their toys theory of history" misreads Schumpeter, whose mutation "creates," takes decades, and is bound to consumer needs.
- [[The pattern of corporate privacy violations is a feature not a bug]] — Gmail, Beacon, click-wrap looked like firms failing to grasp their own logic; the recurring pattern "signaled a feature, not a bug."
- [[Technological inevitability is a fiction the digital future is contestable]] — the Spanish right-to-be-forgotten victory shows even Google's capitalism "was made by humans to be unmade and remade by democratic processes, not commercial decree."
- [[Taming surveillance capitalism begins with careful naming]] — the HIV-vaccine analogy: "every successful vaccine begins with a close understanding of the enemy disease"; we must name the new form "on its own terms and in its own words."
