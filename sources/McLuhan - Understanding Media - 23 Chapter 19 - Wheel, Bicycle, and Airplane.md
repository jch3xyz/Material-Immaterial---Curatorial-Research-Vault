# Source Summary: Chapter 19 — Wheel, Bicycle, and Airplane

**Book:** [[Understanding Media]]
**Author:** [[Marshall McLuhan]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/23 Chapter 19 - Wheel, Bicycle, and Airplane]]

## Orientation

Chapter 19 of [[Understanding Media|*Understanding Media*]] is a Part II case-study chapter. It takes a single mechanical family — the wheel and its descendants the bicycle and the airplane — and reads it through the Part I master thesis [[Media as Extensions of Man]]. The wheel is "an extended foot": it accelerates the rotary motion of the feet just as the chair is the "ablative absolute of backside." Each extension, McLuhan argues, is the *acceleration of an existing process*, and each, pushed to a "peak of performance," reaches a "point of reversal" where the mechanical recreates the organic — a concrete application of [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]]. The movie camera (the "most advanced and complicated" use of the wheel) rolls the world up on a spool; the airplane "rolls up the highway into itself" until "the wheel is reabsorbed into the form of bird or fish." The bicycle "lifted the wheel onto the plane of aerodynamic balance, and not too indirectly created the airplane" — and McLuhan offers the fact that the Wright brothers were bicycle mechanics as confirming evidence.

The chapter's second engine is McLuhan's explosion/implosion and centralization/decentralization apparatus, sharpened here by an explicit dispute with [[Marshall McLuhan and Lewis Mumford|Lewis Mumford]]. The wheel and road are *centralizers*: by [[Acceleration|acceleration]] they create "larger and larger centers," specialism, war chariots, and the radiational "center-margin pattern" of empire ([[Center-Margin Structure]]). But acceleration past a threshold — the automobile and the plane — and "all electric forms whatsoever" reverse this, producing "decentralism in the midst of the older centralism," the origin of "the urban chaos of our time." Against Mumford, who had called ancient urbanization "implosion," McLuhan insists "it was really an explosion": specialism and explicitness are explosion (centralism), while true implosion belongs to the electric age and to prehistoric, tribal, oral cultures. This is the chapter's most durably distinctive intervention, captured in the argument [[Urbanization was explosion not implosion]].

The chapter therefore *reinforces* rather than founds the Part I apparatus. It closes on two figures of electric implosion: the airline executive's cairn of pebbles, by which "in one spot one could touch every part of the world," an emblem of the "mosaic or iconic principle of simultaneous touch and interplay" inherent in electric speed ([[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]], [[The Global Village]], [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]]); and the present age "playing the ancient explosion backward, as on a film," reassembling "the pieces of man's being" — the Humpty-Dumpty whom "electromagnetic automation" can finally put back together. Throughout, the organizing oppositions are [[Mechanical vs Electric]] and [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] (the specialist acrobat vs. the integral clown, the fragmented wall vs. the whole egg).

## Key Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the chapter's master frame: the wheel, bicycle, and airplane as extensions of the foot; tools as "a yielding to this bodily stress by means of extensions of the body"; the body engendering its own further extensions in "a ceaseless process."
- [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] — every extension, pushed to a peak, reverses into the organic: the movie "recreates organic process and movement by pushing the mechanical principle to the point of reversal"; the airplane reabsorbs the wheel into bird/fish form.
- [[Explosion vs Implosion]] — the load-bearing periodizing concept: specialist mechanical centralism is explosion, electric/tribal contraction is implosion; McLuhan's correction of Mumford turns on it.
- [[Decentralization]] — "all electric forms whatsoever have a decentralizing effect"; acceleration past a threshold (auto, plane) reverses the wheel-and-road centralism into decentralism.
- [[Acceleration]] — the chapter's mechanism: each technology is "the acceleration of an existing process," and acceleration of the wheel produces centers, specialism, and aggression (here distinct from Kurzweil's celebratory exponential thesis — McLuhan's is a media-evolution claim).
- [[Center-Margin Structure]] — the wheel and road carry power outward "by a radiational or center-margin pattern"; "Centralism depends on margins that are accessible by road and wheel."
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the implosive "mosaic or iconic principle of simultaneous touch and interplay," "even more characteristic of electric information movement of all kinds."
- [[The Global Village]] — the cairn-of-pebbles emblem of electric/aviation simultaneity: "in one spot one could touch every part of the world."
- [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]] — the "iconic," "mosaic," and "plastic and iconic space" vocabulary, set against the "visual principle of mobile lineality" of the bicycle's tandem wheels.

## Key Arguments

- [[Urbanization was explosion not implosion]] — McLuhan's explicit correction of Mumford: ancient urban concentration is the centralizing, center-to-margin fragmentation of pastoral life by wheel and road (explosion); implosion belongs to the electric and the prehistoric/tribal. The chapter's distinctive contribution.
- [[Electric speed reverses explosion into implosion]] — strongly reinforced here: the wheel "pushed beyond a certain intensity of movement, no longer centralizes," and electric forms decentralize, so the present age replays "the ancient explosion backward, as on a film."

## Important Definitions

- [[Extensions of Man (McLuhan)]] — the chapter supplies applied glosses on this Part I definition: the wheel is "an ablative absolute of feet," "an accelerator of feet, not of hand"; the airplane "a missile, a self-contained transportation system." No new canonical term is defined here.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The wheel reabsorbed into bird or fish (the airplane as the mechanical reversing into the organic).
- The ablative absolute (the wheel of feet, the chair of backside): "when such ablatives intrude, they alter the syntax of society."
- The bagpipe in a symphony — electric forms "cutting across the older mechanical patterns."
- Playing the explosion backward, as on a film — the electric age of implosion reassembling fragmented man.
- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness|The clown vs. the acrobat]] (Beckett's bicycle) — the bicycle as "the prime symbol of the Cartesian mind," the acrobat the specialist, the clown "the integral man."
- Humpty-Dumpty and the King's horses and men — the integral/whole egg fallen on the fragmented brick wall; the fragmented King's men cannot reassemble him, but "electromagnetic automation" can.
- The cairn of pebbles (the airline executive) — pebbles from every airline office in one cairn, an emblem of implosive electric/aviation simultaneity.

## Major Tensions

- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the master tension: mechanical wheel/road (centralism, specialism, explosion, war) vs. electric forms (decentralism, implosion, simultaneity, interplay).
- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] — the specialist acrobat using "a limited segment of his faculties," the fragmented wall and King's men, vs. the integral clown and the whole egg, and the electric age "using all of our faculties at once."
- [[Explosion vs Implosion]] — the wheel/road/word as centralizing explosive force vs. the electric/tribal as imploding, simultaneous force; explicitly disputed against Mumford.
- [[Center vs Margin]] — power radiating from the citadel core to road-accessible margins as the geometry of the wheel-and-road explosion.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A material/immaterial reading of the body's extension into machines: the wheel as "ablative absolute of feet," tools as "extensions of the body," and the airplane reabsorbing the wheel into "bird or fish" — McLuhan's organic/mechanical reversal beside Hayles on [[Embodiment]] and the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension. (Treat the resonance with Kurzweil's exponential thesis as interpretive only — McLuhan's "acceleration of an existing process" is a media-evolution claim, not exponential tech growth.)
- A genealogy of centralization vs. decentralization for systems/surveillance research: wheel-and-road centralism (the war chariot, the citadel, the "direct force" of empire) reversed by electric decentralism — a precursor framing for later accounts of distributed networks and control.
- The explosion/implosion correction of Mumford as a case study in how a media theorist contests a predecessor's vocabulary: useful for an essay on periodization and the rhetoric of the "electric age."
- Beckett's bicycle (the clown vs. the acrobat) and Humpty-Dumpty as figures of specialist fragmentation vs. integral wholeness — a hinge for writing on specialization, disciplinary boundaries, and the demand to "use all of our faculties at once."
