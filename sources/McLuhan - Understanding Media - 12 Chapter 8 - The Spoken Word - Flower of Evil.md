# Source Summary: Chapter 8 — The Spoken Word: Flower of Evil?

## Orientation

This is the first of the Part II case studies, the chapter where McLuhan turns his Part I apparatus — media as extensions of man, sense ratios, the visual stress of phonetic writing, hot versus cold — onto the spoken word itself. The chapter is built as a contrast: speech against writing. The spoken word "involves all of the senses dramatically" and generates intense audience participation; phonetic writing, by contrast, "separates and extends the visual power of words," detaches, specializes, and underwrites privacy and individualism. McLuhan opens with a disk-jockey transcript (Dave Mickie) as a living specimen of the all-sense, participatory oral mode — a voice that "soars, groans, swings, sings, solos, intones, and scampers, always reacting to his own actions" — and brackets it with the tactile, nonliterate-leaning sensorium of the Greeks clicking their "worry beads" and the literate native who plugs his ears so as not to "violate the privacy" of others' letters.

The chapter's distinctive material is its appropriation of Henri Bergson. McLuhan reads language, via Bergson's *Creative Evolution*, as "a human technology that has impaired and diminished the values of the collective unconscious": speech is "the extension of man in speech that enables the intellect to detach itself from the vastly wider reality." The famous analogy — "Language does for intelligence what the wheel does for the feet and the body" — makes speech a wheel-like prosthesis that grants the mind ease, speed, and "ever less involvement," at the cost of dividing the faculties and dimming the "bliss of union in the collective unconscious." This is McLuhan's extension-and-amputation logic carried to its most interior application: even consciousness is "an extension of man."

The closing pages project the thesis forward. Electric technology "extends our senses and nerves in a global embrace" and "points the way to an extension of the process of consciousness itself, on a world scale, and without any verbalization whatever." Computers promise instant translation and so "a Pentecostal condition of universal understanding and unity"; the next logical step is "not to translate, but to by-pass languages in favor of a general cosmic consciousness." The title's question — is the spoken word a "Flower of Evil" (a Baudelairean allusion) — frames language ambivalently as both man's richest art form and the very "Tower of Babel" of division. The chapter is therefore best read as reinforcing the Part I concepts while supplying one durable, much-quoted idea: speech as the first, dividing technical extension of consciousness, set against electricity's promised post-verbal reunification.
