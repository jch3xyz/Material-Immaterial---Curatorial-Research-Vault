# Source Summary: Ch11 [Algorithm]

**Book:** [[God Human Animal Machine]]
**Author:** [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch11 [Algorithm]]]

## Orientation

This is the chapter where O'Gieblyn turns her central method — [[Technology as Metaphor|technology as metaphor]], the conviction that "the divinity of each age reveals the needs and desires of its human creators" — onto the black-box algorithm, and where the book's theological argument is made most explicit. She braids three registers into a single claim: a tech-criticism essay on big data and deep learning, a Calvinist memoir of her evangelical theological education, and a Blumenbergian intellectual history of [[Nominalism (O'Gieblyn)|nominalism]] and [[Disenchantment|disenchantment]]. The essay opens with Chris Anderson's 2008 *Wired* polemic "The End of Theory," which argued that with petabytes of data we should abandon causal explanation: "[[Correlation Is Enough (O'Gieblyn via Anderson)|Correlation is enough]]." O'Gieblyn reads the subsequent rise of deep learning (AlphaGo, COMPAS, PredPol) as the literal fulfillment of that call — a world in which we obtain "objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension."

Because deep-learning models are [[The Black Box|black boxes]] whose internal nodes "correspond to [no] terms in human language," O'Gieblyn argues we are placed in the position of [[Job and the Algorithm|Job]]: denied the right to know *why*, forced "to accept the decisions of our algorithms blindly, like Job accepting his punishment." The memoir thread supplies the theological depth — her Reformed (New Calvinist) training, predestination and double predestination, and her crisis over the problem of evil in the Book of Job, where Job's suffering had a hidden cause (a wager between God and Satan) presided over by a God who "does whatever he pleases." This is the seed of voluntarism: the fear that God "exists in an eternal state of exception" where human morality "breaks down."

The intellectual-history thread, drawn from Hans Blumenberg's *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age*, supplies the genealogy. Late-medieval nominalism (William of Ockham) destroyed the idea of universals — the Platonic correspondence between mind and cosmic order — making the world "radically contingent" and reason "a construct specific to our species." This crisis, peaking in Descartes, *produced* the Enlightenment, disenchantment, humanism, and empiricism, and still "continues to haunt our debates about how to interpret quantum physics" and "our theories of mind." The chapter's organizing thesis closes the loop: [[The algorithmic God is the modern God of Calvin and Luther|the boundless alien deity that technology critics mistake for a Dark-Age regress is in fact the thoroughly modern God of Calvin and Luther]] — "It is not coincidental that the deity we have created resembles, uncannily, the one who got us into this problem in the first place."

## Key Concepts

- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — O'Gieblyn's signature method, stated explicitly here: each age invents the God (and model of mind and world) it needs, and that invention exposes its makers.
- [[The Black Box]] — deep-learning models whose internal logic is irreducible to human-language explanation; the technical core of the chapter's theology, later turned reflexively onto the human brain.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the modern stripping of meaning and teleology from the world, here given a nominalist genealogy via Blumenberg.
- [[Re-enchantment]] — the claim (Weinberger, Bridle) that big data returns us to a premodern epistemology of revelation without understanding, which O'Gieblyn partly accepts and decisively corrects.
- [[Nominalism (O'Gieblyn)]] — the late-medieval doctrine that universals are mere names; the engine of modern contingency and of the inscrutable, voluntarist God.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the predictive algorithm as secular fate; the predestination/determinism parallel made literal in sentencing and probation systems.
- [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — the opaque algorithm occupying the seat of an inscrutable sovereign whose verdicts arrive as revelation beyond appeal.

## Key Arguments

- [[The algorithmic God is the modern God of Calvin and Luther]] — the chapter thesis: the alien AI deity is not a Dark-Age regress but the radically other, voluntarist God of the Reformation, "a thoroughly modern entity."
- [[Prediction enacts the very fate it foresees]] — the citizen before COMPAS or a probation algorithm, like Job before Jehovah, cannot know or contest the reason for a verdict that nonetheless "predetermines" their fate.

## Important Definitions

- [[Correlation Is Enough (O'Gieblyn via Anderson)]] — Anderson's claim that with big data we abandon hypothesis and causal explanation, throwing the numbers into computing clusters to "let statistical algorithms find patterns where science cannot."
- [[Nominalism (O'Gieblyn)]] — for the nominalist, "justice" and "humanity" are just names given to perceived commonalities, not universal truths (Mill: "there is nothing general except names").
- [[Disenchantment (O'Gieblyn)]] — Blumenberg's nominalism-to-modernity thesis: the loss of universals that rendered the world "radically contingent" and made the medieval God "superfluous."

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[Job and the Algorithm]] — the master analogy: the human subject denied the reason for its suffering, demanding submission before an inscrutable power (Clancy, Loomis, Gates).
- [[Mind as Machine]] — realized here in its reflexive, inverse form: the human brain itself "black-boxed" (Berk, Dennett) so that trusting an algorithm comes to seem as reasonable as trusting our own minds.
- [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — "God is the machine. The black box is the truth"; algorithmic output as revelation, refused interpretation as a technological *sola fide* and *sola scriptura*.

## Major Tensions

- [[Visibility vs Understanding]] — Anderson's "Correlation is enough" against Lanier's "Science is about understanding"; the opening correlation-vs-comprehension axis.
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — "the shadowy line between prediction and determination"; predestination's secular double in the predictive algorithm.
- [[Disenchantment]] vs [[Re-enchantment]] — Weinberger and Bridle's "New Dark Age" return-to-revelation reading against O'Gieblyn's corrective that the algorithmic God is a *modern* nominalist deity.
