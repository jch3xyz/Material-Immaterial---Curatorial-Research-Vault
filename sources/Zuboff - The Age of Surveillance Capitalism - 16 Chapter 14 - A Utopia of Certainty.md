# Source Summary: Chapter 14 — A Utopia of Certainty

**Book:** [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]]

## Orientation

Chapter 14 is the culmination of Part III's argument about [[Instrumentarian Power]]: it shows that power scaling up from the individual to society itself as a totalizing project. Where the earlier chapters defined instrumentarianism as a new species of power that tunes, herds, and conditions behavior toward [[Guaranteed Outcomes]], this chapter reveals its societal endgame — [[A Utopia of Certainty]], in which the whole of "society," "social relations," and "key societal processes" is annexed as fresh terrain for rendition, calculation, modification, and prediction. The pursuit of totality begun in [[The Reality Business]] (extending extraction from the virtual to the physical world) now necessarily, Zuboff argues, includes society.

Rhetorically the chapter is an inventory and an unmasking. Zuboff musters the surveillance capitalists' own words — Microsoft's Satya Nadella, Google's Sundar Pichai, Eric Schmidt, and Larry Page, Facebook's Mark Zuckerberg — quoting their developer-conference triumphalism and "magical age" utopianism, and then reading those promises against the practices they conceal. She develops the keystone distinction between two species of power: instrumentarianism and totalitarianism both "yearn toward totality" but in profoundly different ways — totalitarianism seeks political totality through violence; instrumentarianism seeks market-dominance totality through its control over [[The Division of Learning in Society]], enabled and enforced by [[Big Other]]. Using Frank and Fritzie Manuel's scholarship on utopian thought, she frames the surveillance capitalists as sui generis utopianists practicing [[Applied Utopistics]] — they "seize the world in practice," reversing the normal sequence of theory and practice.

The chapter's most distinctive contribution is its derivation of the instrumentarian social vision from machine practices. Through Nadella's IoT factory and construction-site demonstrations, Zuboff reads off a model of collective action — [[The Hive]] / confluence — in which "the 'freedom' of each individual machine is surrendered to the knowledge that they share," waging "an all-out war on accidents, mistakes, and randomness in general," and she traces the lineage explicitly to Skinner's *Walden Two* and to the behavioral theorists Planck, Meyer, and Skinner. [[The Machine Hive Becomes the Human Hive]]: in the resulting human hive "individual freedom is forfeit to collective knowledge and action," nonharmonious elements "preemptively targeted with high doses of tuning, herding, and conditioning." This, she concludes, is "the signature of the third modernity offered up by surveillance capital as its answer to our quest for effective life together."

- [[Instrumentarianism is a new species of power not a form of totalitarianism]] — both yearn toward totality, but totalitarianism pursues political totality through violence while instrumentarianism pursues market totality through control of the division of learning, enforced by Big Other.

- [[Surveillance capitalists realize their utopias in practice without an explicit contestable theory]] — they reverse the normal sequence of theory and practice, foreclosing the critical-inquiry gap with "shock and awe."
