# Source Summary: The New Human Agenda

**Book:** [[Homo Deus]]
**Author:** [[Yuval Noah Harari]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/01 The New Human Agenda]]

## Orientation

This is the framing chapter of *Homo Deus*, and it states the book's whole thesis in miniature. Harari's argument runs in three beats. First, for all of recorded history humanity's three perennial enemies — famine, plague and war — were treated as fixed features of God's plan or of imperfect nature, but "in the last few decades we have managed to rein in famine, plague and war," demoting them "from incomprehensible and uncontrollable forces of nature into manageable challenges." His signature inversion supplies the proof: more people now die from obesity than starvation, from old age than infectious disease, and "more people commit suicide than are killed by soldiers, terrorists and criminals combined." Second, because "history does not tolerate a vacuum," new projects will rise to the top of [[The New Human Agenda]]; "given our past record and our current values," these are immortality (death recast as [[Death as a Technical Problem|a technical problem with a technical solution]]), happiness ([[Happiness as Biochemistry|redefined by the life sciences as a matter of bodily sensation, to be reached by re-engineering biochemistry]]), and divinity ([[Three Paths to the Upgrade|the upgrade of Homo sapiens into Homo deus]]). Third, these are not science-fiction eccentricities but "the long-standing humanist ideals to their logical conclusion."

The chapter's rhetorical posture is that of the historian-of-the-trajectory, not the prophet or the manifesto-writer. Harari is careful that this is "a historical prediction, not a political manifesto," and "less of a prophecy and more a way of discussing our present choices." Two methodological excursions formalize that stance. [[The Paradox of Knowledge]] argues that human development, unlike the weather, reacts to predictions about it, so that "knowledge that changes behaviour quickly loses its relevance" (the Marx illustration: capitalists read *Das Kapital* and falsified him). And the "Brief History of Lawns" makes the case for [[History as Liberation from the Past|history as liberation rather than prediction]] — "to free yourself of the past and imagine alternative destinies." Between them sits the diagnosis that we cannot "hit the brakes": no one understands the whole system any more, and [[The Growth Imperative|the modern economy needs constant and indefinite growth in order to survive]].

The closing move plants the book's "gun in Act I" ([[The Gun in Act I]]). For 300 years the world has been ruled by [[Humanism (Harari)|humanism — "the worship of humankind"]], and the attempt to fulfil its dream "simultaneously exposes humanism's inherent flaws"; "the same technologies that can upgrade humans into gods might also make humans irrelevant." The chapter is strongly resonant with material already in the vault — O'Gieblyn on [[Transhumanism]] and [[Mind Uploading]] (the shared cast of Kurzweil and de Grey, the immortality industry, divinity-as-engineering), Hayles on [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] and [[Posthumanism]] (upgrading away from the flesh), and Han on the capitalist growth imperative ([[Capital as Religion]]) and the smartphone as instrument of voluntary self-surrender.

## Key Concepts

- [[The New Human Agenda]] — having reined in famine, plague and war, humanity's next targets are immortality, happiness and divinity; the chapter's title thesis and the book's organizing frame
- [[Death as a Technical Problem]] — modern science reframes death not as metaphysical mystery but as an accumulation of solvable "technical glitches," so the war against death becomes the century's flagship project
- [[Happiness as Biochemistry]] — the life-sciences redefinition of happiness as "different balances of bodily sensations," so that lasting contentment can only be reached by rigging human biochemistry
- [[Three Paths to the Upgrade]] — the taxonomy of the divinity project: biological engineering, cyborg engineering, and the engineering of non-organic beings
- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — "the worship of humankind," the dominant modern religion that "sanctifies the life, happiness and power of Homo sapiens"
- [[The Sanctity of Human Life]] — the supreme value of contemporary culture; "the right to life" as humanity's most fundamental value, the driver that makes the war against death seem inevitable and that, in the geriatric ward, begins to undermine itself
- [[The Growth Imperative]] — the capitalist dogma that the economy "needs constant and indefinite growth in order to survive," the economic reason we cannot stop the agenda
- [[The Paradox of Knowledge]] — the reflexivity of historical knowledge: predictions about human development change the behaviour they predict, so more data makes history wilder and less predictable
- [[History as Liberation from the Past]] — Harari's defence of doing history: not prediction but the loosening of the past's grip, opening "alternative destinies"
- [[Homo Deus]] — the upgraded human-as-god; "turn Homo sapiens into Homo deus" — the umbrella project that subsumes immortality and happiness
- [[Upgrading Inequality]] — the warning that the elites will reach for "eternal youth and godlike powers" while billions still "deal with poverty, illness and violence"

## Key Arguments

- [[Death is a technical problem with a technical solution]] — modern medicine treats death as accumulated technical glitches, so "a couple of geeks in a lab" could in principle overcome it
- [[The new agenda is humanism taken to its logical conclusion]] — immortality, bliss and divinity simply maximize the three values humanism already sanctifies: life, happiness and power
- [[Fulfilling humanism will destroy it]] — the gun in Act I: the same technologies that upgrade humans into gods can make humans irrelevant, so realizing the humanist dream exposes its flaws

## Important Definitions

- [[A-mortality (Harari)]] — the condition of having "no expiry date" while remaining killable by accident or violence; distinct from the immortality of God, and source of unprecedented anxiety
- [[Divinity (Harari)]] — "not a vague metaphysical quality" and "not the same as omnipotence," but the Greek-/Hindu-god possession of specific super-abilities of creation and destruction

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Gun in Act I]] — Chekhov's Law as the book's structural conceit: "a gun that appears in Act I, to fire in Act III" — humanism's self-undoing — counterbalanced by the unfired Cold War gun (ability is not motivation)
- [[The Glass Ceiling of Happiness]] — the invisible cap on subjective well-being that prosperity cannot raise, held up by a psychological and a biological pillar
- [[The Lawn]] — the worked symbol of historical contingency: an aristocratic status display that became a bourgeois necessity and a global craze; the case study for history as liberation from the felt-natural

## Major Tensions

- [[Healing vs Upgrading]] — the boundary that will not hold: "no clear line separates healing from upgrading"; every enhancement enters disguised as a cure
- [[Growth vs Ecology]] — the growth imperative that drives the whole agenda against the ecological collapse that is its real limit; when forced to choose, "politicians, CEOs and voters almost always prefer growth"
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the third path's hope that "life will break out into the vastness of the inorganic realm," and the upgrade away from organic chemistry, touch the vault's central tension

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A material/immaterial reading of the "third path": the chapter's hope that "life will break out into the vastness of the inorganic realm" as the political-economic counterpart to Hayles's disembodiment of information and Lippard's dematerialization — three vocabularies for the same flight from the organic/material.
- Surveillance and embodiment: the chapter's mundane, opt-in cyborg (the Stockholm microchip, the $400 mind-reading helmet, "Every day millions of people decide to grant their smartphone a bit more control over their lives") read beside Han's voluntary self-exposure and Hayles's body-as-data — the upgrade arriving "in baby steps," not as apocalypse.
- Curatorial framing of "Healing vs Upgrading": the slippery slope from cure to enhancement (Viagra, plastic surgery, the three-parent embryo, the "genetic child catalogue") as a structure for exhibitions on the engineered body and on the disappearing line between therapy and design.
- The biochemical re-definition of happiness as a critique of the experience economy: "happiness is pleasure. Period." for "the capitalist juggernaut," set against Buddhist craving-reduction — an essay on engineered affect, addictive design, and "more addictive games for our smartphones."
- History-as-liberation as curatorial method: the lawn genealogy as a model for denaturalizing a felt-inevitable preference, applicable to any exhibition that historicizes "cultural cargo" mistaken for nature.
