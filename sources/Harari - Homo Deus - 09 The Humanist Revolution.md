# Source Summary: The Humanist Revolution

**Book:** [[Homo Deus]]
**Author:** [[Yuval Noah Harari]]

## Orientation

This is the keystone chapter of *Homo Deus*'s middle movement (*Homo Sapiens Gives Meaning to the World*). Having argued in the previous chapter that modernity is a "deal" — [[The Modern Covenant]] in which "humans agree to give up meaning in exchange for power" — Harari here explains how order survived the renunciation of a cosmic plan. The answer is the "cunning escape clause" in the modern deal: humans may find meaning *without* deriving it from a great cosmic plan. The antidote to "a meaningless and lawless existence" was [[Humanism (Harari)|humanism]], "a revolutionary new creed that conquered the world during the last few centuries," which "worships humanity" and "expects the experiences of humans to give meaning to the great cosmos." The central religious revolution of modernity, Harari insists, was "not losing faith in God; rather, it was gaining faith in humanity."

The chapter's first half traces the *relocation of meaning and authority* from an external God to the internal human voice, and shows the same logic operating across five domains — ethics ("if it feels good – do it"), politics ("the voter knows best"), aesthetics ("beauty is in the eye of the beholder"), economics ("the customer is always right") and education ("think for yourself") — each contrasted with its medieval, God-anchored predecessor (the priest, God's will, the muses, the guilds, scriptural memorization). It then sets out humanism's epistemology, the formula [[Knowledge = Experiences × Sensitivity]], contrasted with the medieval `Scriptures × Logic` and the scientific `Empirical Data × Mathematics`. This founds the modern view of life as a string of experiences — "the founding myth of numerous modern industries, from tourism to art" — and transforms even our perception of war, relocating its truth from the general on the hilltop to the eyes of the common soldier (Otto Dix, Tom Lea, *All Quiet on the Western Front*).

The second half narrates the humanist schism. Humanism "fragmented into several conflicting sects": [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)|liberal humanism]] (the orthodox branch; the individual's unique inner voice), socialist humanism ("the party knows best"), and [[Evolutionary Humanism]] (the Nazis its extreme; conflict as the engine of natural selection and the superman). Harari recounts the twentieth century as "the deadliest war of religion in history" between these three sects, ending with liberalism's 1989 victory — "The supermarket proved to be far stronger than the gulag." He closes by arguing that radical Islam and the theist religions offer no real alternative because they have "long since turned from a creative into a reactive force," out of touch with the technological realities of the day; and that liberalism's very triumph "may contain the seeds of its ruin," setting up Part III's decoupling and Dataism arguments. In the vault this chapter is the historian's statement of the religion whose subject is [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] (Hayles, O'Gieblyn) and whose disenchanted cosmos is the [[Disenchantment]] terrain (O'Gieblyn, Weber).

## Key Concepts

- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — the modern religion that "worships humanity" and relocates meaning and authority from an external God to inner human experience
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — the orthodox sect: each individual is "a unique individual possessing a distinctive inner voice"; the five domain-slogans are its creed
- [[Evolutionary Humanism]] — the sect (Nazism its extreme) that treats conflict between human experiences as "the raw material of natural selection" and seeks the superman
- [[Knowledge = Experiences × Sensitivity]] — humanism's formula for ethical knowledge, contrasted with the medieval `Scriptures × Logic` and scientific `Empirical Data × Mathematics`
