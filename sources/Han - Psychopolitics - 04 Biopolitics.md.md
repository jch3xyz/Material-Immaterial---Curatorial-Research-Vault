# Source Summary: 4. Biopolitics

**Book:** [[Psychopolitics]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/04 Biopolitics]]

## Orientation

This short, pivotal chapter is Han's genealogical setup for the book's master thesis: the passage from biopolitics to [[Psychopolitics]]. Following [[Michel Foucault]], Han narrates a periodization of power — from the sovereign power of the sword (a power to *deal death*, "the 'privilege to seize hold of life in order to suppress it'") to disciplinary power, a power *over life* that "invests life through and through" through the "administration of bodies" and the "calculated management of life." He grounds this transition materially in a change of production: the shift "from agrarian to industrial production" required that the body be disciplined and "calibrated to be a production-unit," fashioned out of "shapeless dough" into a "machine." Disciplinary power is normative power — it subjects the subject to norms, commandments and prohibitions and eliminates anomalies — and it works through the *negativity* of training and drills (Abrichtung), a negativity it shares with sovereign levying and conscription (Abschöpfung). Both pursue [[Allo-Exploitation|allo-exploitation]] and both "bring forth the obedience-subject."

Han then marks the decisive limit that the whole book hangs on. Disciplinary technology reaches into the mental sphere ("industry" means "hard work"; the "industrial school" was the "reform school"; [[Jeremy Bentham|Bentham]] claimed his panopticon "assures inmates' moral improvement"), but its "orthopaedic technology" is "too crude to penetrate into the deeper layers of the soul." Bentham's "Big Brother only observes inmates from the outside"; the panopticon "is bound to the optical medium" and "has no access to inner thoughts or needs." Han then defines biopolitics proper as "the governmental technology of disciplinary power" — devoted to "population" as "a productive and reproductive mass," regulating "reproductive cycles, birth and death rates, levels of general health, and life expectancy."

The chapter closes by opening the door to the rest of the book. Precisely because biopolitics works through population statistics, it "has no access to the psychic realm" and "can deliver no material for drawing up a psychogram of the population": "Demography is not the same thing as psych-ography." This is the hinge. [[Big Data]] and statistics "lie worlds apart" — Big Data can establish "not just an individual but a collective psychogram — perhaps even the psychogram of the unconscious itself," and so "may yet shine a light into the depths of the psyche and exploit the unconscious entirely." Biopolitics governs the body and the population; the neoliberal regime, "which exploits the psyche above all," requires a successor that biopolitics cannot supply. The rhetorical posture is genealogical and corrective: Han adopts Foucault's periodization in full, only to disqualify its terminal category for the present.

## Key Concepts

- [[Biopolitics]] — "the governmental technology of disciplinary power"; the careful administration of the population as a biological mass, defined here by its limit — it operates via demography and "has no access to the psychic realm."
- [[Psychopolitics]] — the book's master concept, named here only by contrast: the neoliberal regime "exploits the psyche above all," where biopolitics cannot reach.
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — exploitation by an *other* (external domination), the structure shared by sovereign and disciplinary power; it "brings forth the obedience-subject." The explicit antonym of [[Auto-Exploitation]].
- [[Big Data]] — introduced at the chapter's close as categorically different from statistics: it can draw an individual and collective psychogram, "perhaps even the psychogram of the unconscious itself."
- [[Negativity]] — disciplinary power works through the "negativity of training and drills," aligning it with the sovereign "negativity of levying and conscription"; the negativity pole that the neoliberal regime of [[Positivity]] supersedes.

## Key Arguments

- Modern power shifted from a power to *deal death* (sovereignty) to a power *over life* (discipline/biopolitics): "the 'old power of death' yields to the careful 'administration of bodies' and 'the calculated management of life'."
- The shift from sovereign to disciplinary power was driven by a change in the mode of production — from agrarian to industrial — that required fitting the body to machinic production.
- Disciplinary power is normative power that works through negativity: it subjects the subject to "norms, commandments and prohibitions" and drills out deviation.
- Disciplinary power cannot reach the psyche; its "orthopaedic technology" is "too crude" and remains external and optical (Bentham's "Big Brother only observes inmates from the outside").
- [[Biopolitics]] is unsuited to the neoliberal regime because it governs the body and the population through statistics, while neoliberalism exploits the psyche.
- Statistics and [[Big Data]] are categorically different: Big Data can produce a psychogram — even of the unconscious — and so reach where biopolitics cannot.

## Important Definitions

- [[Biopolitics]] (Han via Foucault) — "Biopolitics is the governmental technology of disciplinary power"; its objects are "reproductive cycles, birth and death rates, levels of general health, and life expectancy."
- Disciplinary power (Han via Foucault) — "not a power to deal death, but a power over life: its function is no longer to kill but to 'invest life through and through'"; "disciplinary power is normative power."
- [[Allo-Exploitation|Allo-exploitation]] — defined by opposition: exploitation by an other (external domination), the structure shared by sovereign and disciplinary power, producing the obedience-subject.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The Might of the Sword — sovereign power figured as the blade that deals death: "The power of sovereignty is the might of the sword."
- Shapeless Dough into a Machine — the disciplined body as raw dough kneaded into a machine: "A 'concerted orthopaedy' fashions 'shapeless dough' into a 'machine'."
- The Body as Production-Unit — discipline as the calibration of the body to machinic production: "The body is calibrated to be a production-unit."
- Light into the Depths of the Psyche — the psyche figured as a dark interior that Big Data can finally illuminate: "it may yet shine a light into the depths of the psyche."
- [[The Panopticon]] — Bentham's disciplinary architecture, here strictly characterized as optical and exterior, with no access to interiority.
- [[Friendly Big Brother]] — the chapter supplies the foil: "Bentham's Big Brother only observes inmates from the outside," the merely external surveillance that the book's later "friendly Big Brother" of Big Data supersedes.
- The Psychogram — the figure of the map of the psyche/unconscious that Big Data can produce and demography cannot.

## Major Tensions

- [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] — the body and population (administered by demography) versus the psyche and unconscious (reached only by Big Data): "this approach proves altogether unsuited to the neoliberal regime, which exploits the psyche above all."
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — this chapter anchors the *disciplinary* pole (normative, optical, external) of the periodization whose control pole is the neoliberal successor.
- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — discipline's "negativity of training and drills" maps onto Han's standing axis; this chapter establishes the negativity pole.
- Allo-exploitation vs auto-exploitation — sovereign/disciplinary external domination (the obedience-subject) versus the neoliberal self-exploitation of the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]]; the chapter supplies the *allo-* pole.
- Demography vs psych-ography — quantitative population-biology (statistics) versus a map of the psyche (Big Data): "Demography is not the same thing as psych-ography."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A genealogical scaffold for any argument about surveillance and embodiment: the chapter's three-stage periodization (sovereign death → disciplinary body → biopolitical population) and its hinge to the psyche gives a clean spine for tracing how "the material/immaterial" axis maps onto power's changing object — the body of discipline versus the psyche of Big Data.
- A frame for the body-as-data thread: Han's claim that biopolitics is "too crude" and demography "has no access to the psychic realm" can be tested against the practices that *do* claim to read interiority from data — useful for an essay on whether the "collective psychogram of the unconscious" is the supersession of biopolitics or its continuation by other means.
- A curatorial lens on the move from optical to ambient surveillance: Bentham's panopticon "bound to the optical medium" versus a regime that "shines a light into the depths of the psyche" reframes exhibition and display — when does putting a body or a self on view stop being optical exposure and become psychic extraction?
- A materialist counter-reading: the production-genealogy ("the shift from agrarian to industrial production," body as "production-unit") invites an essay pressing on whether the body really drops out under immaterial production, or is merely displaced as the privileged site of exploitation.
