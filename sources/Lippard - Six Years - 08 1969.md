# Source Summary: 1969 (the year conceptual art crystallized)

**Book:** [[Six Years]]
**Author:** [[Lucy Lippard]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/08 1969]]

## Orientation

1969 is the densest and most pivotal year in Lippard's annotated chronicle — the year in which, in her own later phrasing, "concept art" finally "crystallized." The entry opens with a flood of artists' books, then pivots to the two events that organize everything after: [[Seth Siegelaub]]'s January 5–31 show (Barry, Huebler, Kosuth, Weiner) in which "the exhibition consists of (the ideas communicated in) the catalog; the physical presence (of the work) is supplementary to the catalog," and the March *One Month* — "First exhibition to exist in catalogue form alone; distributed free worldwide." With these, the catalogue stops documenting art and *becomes* the art: Siegelaub's distinction between primary and secondary information makes the printed page a medium, the instance of [[Material vs Immaterial]] proper to the art object's dematerialization.

This is the year the founding statements arrive almost simultaneously. [[Sol LeWitt]]'s "Sentences on Conceptual Art" (35 numbered propositions) and Joseph Kosuth's "Art After Philosophy" both appear; [[Lawrence Weiner]]'s three-clause "Statement of Intent" ("1. The artist may construct the piece. 2. The piece may be fabricated. 3. The piece need not be built.") is set into the January catalogue and repeated thereafter as the canonical form of his work; Douglas Huebler announces "The world is full of objects, more or less interesting; I do not wish to add any more"; and Robert Barry pushes the object toward sheer invisibility — inert-gas releases, carrier waves, telepathy, "All the things I know but of which I am not at the moment thinking." Hans Haacke's working premise — "to think in terms of systems" — and the year's wave of exhibitions (Szeemann's *When Attitudes Become Form*, *Op Losse Schroeven*, *Earth Art*, Lippard's own *557,087*) make 1969 read as a manifesto-year.

Crucially, Lippard sets a counter-pole inside the same entry. [[Robert Smithson]]'s long interview with P.A. Norvell insists from the opposite direction: "There is no escape from matter. There is no escape from the physical nor is there any escape from the mind." Smithson treats the object as "a mental problem rather than a physical reality" yet refuses dematerialization as an exit — "I think that conceptual art which depends completely on written data is only half the story." His suspicion of the very word "system" ("System is a convenient word, like object. It is another abstract entity that doesn't exist") makes him the dialectical brake on the year's idealist momentum, and an anchor for [[Systems Theory]] read against the grain.

Lippard's own voice enters not only as the organizer of *Number 7* and *557,087* but as a theorist wary of the category she is helping to name. Her retrospective note on *557,087* — "I was particularly concerned not to be providing a new category in which disparate artists would be lumped" — is the anti-category aside that the whole book formalizes ("yet another attempt to emphasize fragmentation, variety, and disparity of intention"). The rhetorical posture of the 1969 entry is therefore double: chronicle of a crystallization and a running refusal to let it harden into a movement.

## Key Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — dematerialization's decisive year; the catalogue, the carrier wave, the inert gas, and the idea-in-the-mind all displace the physical object (the art-historical instance of this canonical tension).
- [[Information]] — Siegelaub's primary/secondary information distinction; Kosuth's media presentation; Baxter's "liquid information"; the document as the work's mode of existence.
- [[Systems Theory]] — Haacke's "think in terms of systems," Pulsa's programmed environments, Burnham's object-to-systems thesis, and Smithson's pointed rejection of "system" as another abstract entity.
- [[Virtuality]] — Barry's works "actual but not concrete," existing "in the minds of so many people"; the piece that exists "if you have any ideas about it."
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — Pulsa's electronic programming of light and sound, Barry's electromagnetic carrier waves, Haacke's systems, and the move toward art "developed, to deal with these phenomena."
- [[New Media Art]] — Gerry Schum's *Land Art* "TV gallery" exists "only in a series of transmissions"; NETCo.'s telex/telecopier; Kosuth's newspaper and billboard placements.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — Siegelaub's claim that "the original of the art, the fact of the art, is not the presentation of the art" stages the question of where art's materiality now resides.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the year's defining axis: LeWitt/Weiner/Huebler/Barry pulling toward idea and document, Smithson pulling back toward "ponderous, weighty" matter.
- The crystallization of "conceptual art" vs Lippard's explicit refusal of the category (the *557,087* aside).
- Idea/idealism (LeWitt: "Conceptual Artists are mystics rather than rationalists") vs Smithson's dualistic "collision course" of mind and matter.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- Dematerialization as the art-historical name for [[Material vs Immaterial]]: read Siegelaub's "primary information" beside Hayles's account of [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — both ask where the "fact" of a work lives once it leaves the object.
- The catalogue-as-medium and Schum's "TV gallery" as a 1969 prehistory of [[New Media Art]] and distribution-as-form.
- Smithson contra "systems" as an immanent critique of [[Systems Theory]] in art — the artist who refuses both the object and its supposed dissolution.
- LeWitt's "Sentences" vs Kosuth's "Art After Philosophy": two incompatible foundations (intuitive/serial vs analytic/tautological) for the same crystallizing movement.
