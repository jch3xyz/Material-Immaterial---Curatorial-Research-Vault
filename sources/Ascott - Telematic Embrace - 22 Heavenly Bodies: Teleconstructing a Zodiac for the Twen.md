## Orientation

This short chapter is not a discursive essay but a **telematic art project proposal / instruction-score**, written by Ascott in 1993 for the *Teleskulptur III* exhibition in Graz, Austria (edited by Richard Kriesche; Graz: Kulturdata, 1993). It is one of the rare *practice* documents in the collection — a working blueprint that shows what Ascott's theory of telematic art looks like when it is actually built, scheduled, and wired. The work, "Heavenly Bodies," proposes the collaborative, remote construction of "twelve new signs of the zodiac" by twelve groups of geographically dispersed participants, coordinated through a central node — "the Graz Interface Zone" — via videophone and fax over strictly timed transmission periods (*Directly stated*).

Each group contributes fragments — a video image of an "actual, mythical, or artificial" animal/human part, a three-second synthesized sound bite, and a "divinatory, prophetic, or anticipatory" text — which are spatially configured on a gallery wall grid, joined into a linear figure, and absorbed into "the evolving circle of zodiac signs." Six transmission points (Santa Monica, Toronto, Paris, Den Bosch and Utrecht in Holland, and Gwent, Wales) feed the single Graz interface. The document is operational throughout — divided into OBJECTIVE, STRUCTURE, PROCESS, and EXAMPLE — and coins no durable new theoretical term; its value for the vault is as a primary-source EXEMPLAR of concepts Ascott's theory-essays argue abstractly (*Interpretive synthesis*).

The score is most useful as a concrete, machinic embodiment of [[Telematic Art]] and [[Network Consciousness]]: the distributed, networked artwork assembled from many remote authors and completed only in aggregate; the spectator/participant as co-constructor; and a deliberate fusion of high telecommunications technology with archaic divinatory and cosmological systems (the zodiac, prophecy, the "music of the spheres"). That last move — re-enchanting a global telematic network with myth and cosmos — prefigures Ascott's later [[Technoetics]] vocabulary and bears lightly on [[Re-enchantment]]; the divinatory framing is *enacted, not theorized*, here, so the heavyweight re-enchantment claims should be sourced from Ascott's explicitly technoetic chapters, not this proposal (*Strongly implied*).

## Key Arguments

This is a project score, so it advances no propositional arguments in the ordinary sense; the implicit, performative claims worth recording:

- A work of art can be **teleconstructed** — built collaboratively across a distributed network in real time — rather than made by a single author in one place. The claim is enacted by the protocol, not asserted (*Strongly implied*).
- **Strict scheduling of input and output is constitutive of the telematic artwork; time is the medium.** The headnote elevates timing from logistics to essence (*Directly stated*).

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The celestial map / illuminated zodiac on the gallery wall — the grid of twelve videophones, each holding a fragment, read together as "an illuminated celestial map"; the networked distributed image figured as a collaboratively charted night sky.
- The harmony / music of the spheres — the synthesized aggregate of twelve sound bites figured as the Pythagorean *musica universalis*, an ancient cosmology recruited to name a real-time networked sound assembly.
- The zodiacal creature assembled from remote parts — a single mythic animal-figure composed from twelve fragments sent by twelve dispersed groups; an image of the collective body stitched from distributed contributions.
- The Zodiac (teleconstructed) — the central symbol: the twelve signs rebuilt for "the Twenty-First Century" out of networked fragments; ancient divinatory cosmology as scaffold for a telematic artwork.
- The Interface / Interface Zone (Graz) — the central node that mediates all twelve groups; the place where the network becomes a single shared image.
- The grid (the North Wall grid) — the coordinate matrix onto which remote images are mapped by grid reference, turning scattered transmissions into one configured whole.
