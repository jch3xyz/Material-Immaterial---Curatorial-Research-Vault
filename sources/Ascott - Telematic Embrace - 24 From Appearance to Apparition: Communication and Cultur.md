## Orientation

This is the title-essay for the volume's central conceit and one of the cleanest statements of Ascott's mid-career thesis. Delivered at FISEA: Fourth International Symposium on Electronic Arts (Minneapolis, 1993) and reprinted with permission, it is Ascott's own primary text (not Shanken's editorial introduction; the sole editorial intrusion is the bracketed 2000 note on Ed Harris's *Pollock* film). The essay argues that Western art's millennial preoccupation with **appearance** — surface, representation, "the look of things," the retinal gaze, ready-made reality — is dying, displaced by an art of **apparition**: emergence, becoming, transformation, "the coming into being of a new identity," the construction of reality. It opens with Schrödinger's Cat and Greg Egan's novel *Quarantine* to install quantum-measurement / observer-created reality as the deep model for an art in which the observer constructs meaning, and locates the point of wave-function collapse "right in the brain."

The rhetorical posture is polemical and political. Ascott reframes the artwork as no longer a finished object but a **context** — an open-ended, "inherently cybernetic" system "structurally coupled" to its user's behaviour — and aligns this with radical constructivism (he names Paul Watzlawick and Heinz von Foerster). He names connectivity, interaction, and emergence as the new "watchwords," predicts the systems interface moving "closer and closer to the body and into the brain" (the post-biological body as interface), and frames the shift "from object to process" as a question of the **democratisation of meaning** — "a shared participation in the creation and ownership of reality." Galleries, museums, academies, dealers, critics, and "cultural theory" are cast as the de facto controllers presiding over the demise of the art of appearance. The single pre-telematic ancestor he allows is Jackson Pollock, whose "intimations of connectivity" and removal of the picture from the gallery wall "onto the surface of the Earth" model a networked sensibility — "a matter of attitude before it is anything to do with machines."

For the vault this chapter is the keystone primary-text anchor for the [[Appearance vs Apparition]] tension (the book's title figure) and EXTENDS the Burnham-realized art/systems cluster — [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], [[Reciprocal Communication]], [[Object and System]], [[Material vs Immaterial]] — from the Ascott (participatory/cybernetic) pole. It cross-links powerfully to [[Jack Burnham]] (the two poles of 1960s cybernetic art: Burnham's object→system spine vs Ascott's object→process), to [[Marshall McLuhan]] (the network "extending our sensorium," "that larger field of consciousness we call the world"), to [[Donna Haraway]] and [[N. Katherine Hayles]] (the dissolving natural/artificial boundary, the post-biological body), and to [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] (observer-created reality, the measurement problem, Taoist flux / re-enchantment). The closing register — "Cyberspace cannot remain innocent. It is a matrix of human values. It carries a psychic charge" — seeds the technoetic / spiritual turn of Ascott's later work.

## Key Arguments

- [[Western art's preoccupation with appearance is dying]] — appearance-art served church/state ideologies and clockwork-determinist classical science; it "is dying, because it is no longer relevant to a culture that is progressively concerned with... apparition."
- [[Reality is constructed by participatory observation a repudiation of postmodern pessimism]] — human consciousness is the instrument for creating reality, so "to construct art is to construct reality"; the observer at the centre constructs meaning.
- [[Telematic art rejects representation in favour of simulation]] — "Representation in art was always essentially mendacious, illusory, and counterfeit. The mirror always lies"; the processes of apparition are authentic and appearance is a fraud.
- [[In telematic art meaning is produced through interaction not transmitted]] — the artist designs context, not content; the observer "is now at the centre of the creative process, not at the periphery looking in."
- [[The prosthesis of vision constructs what it envisions so material and immaterial lose their distinction]] — linked: the retinal gaze "can penetrate very little"; twentieth-century science addresses "the forces, fields, and relationships" invisible to retinal vision.
