# Source Summary: Prologue

**Book:** [[How We Became Posthuman]]
**Author:** [[N. Katherine Hayles]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/02 Prologue]]

## Orientation

The Prologue stages the book's founding scene: [[Alan Turing]]'s 1950 "imitation game." Hayles reads the [[The Turing Test|Turing test]] not as a thought experiment about machine intelligence but as a "primal scene" in which the [[Embodiment|erasure of embodiment]] is *performed* — the moment at which "intelligence" is redefined as the formal manipulation of symbols rather than enaction in the human lifeworld. She places this scene at "the inaugural moment of the computer age" and treats it as the agenda-setting gesture of [[Cybernetics]] and artificial intelligence: all that comes to matter is the generation and manipulation of informational patterns, abstracted from any material substrate. Behind this lies the definition of [[Information]] formalized by [[Claude Shannon]] and [[Norbert Wiener]] — information as "a kind of bodiless fluid that could flow between different substrates without loss of meaning or form" — which Hayles uses to introduce the book's governing problem, [[Embodiment vs Information]].

The Prologue's rhetorical posture is corrective and polemical. Hayles recovers two things she argues the standard reception has suppressed. First, the *gender* component of Turing's game: Turing's first example asks the interrogator to distinguish man from woman, and Hayles makes this rhetorically parallel to the human/machine case rather than, as biographer [[Andrew Hodges]] insists, a "red herring." Against Hodges — who reads the gender test as nonsignifying because "gender depended on facts which were not reducible to sequences of symbols" — Hayles argues that Hodges "is willing to practice violence upon the text" to protect the boundaries of the liberal subject from transformation. She agrees with Hodges on one point only: the importance of "putting embodiment back into the picture." Second, she extends the trajectory forward to [[Hans Moravec]], whose proposal to download human consciousness into a computer she names the "Moravec test," the "logical successor to the Turing test" and the endpoint of treating [[The Body as Data|human identity as informational pattern]].

The chapter's payoff is the claim that the test does its decisive work *before* the interrogator guesses. The "important intervention" comes when the test "puts you into a cybernetic circuit" — a [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] joining represented and enacted bodies through "mutating and flexible machine interfaces" — so that the participant is already a cyborg by the time any identification is assigned. This is the mechanism by which Hayles claims the [[The Liberal Subject|liberal humanist subject]] is transformed into the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]. Framed as a "magic trick," the Prologue holds in compressed form the minor figures and moves (Hodges, J.S. Mill, the magic-trick framing, the parallelism dispute) that the rest of the book will unfold at length.

## Key Concepts

- [[The Turing Test]] — the 1950 imitation game, read by Hayles as the "primal scene" performing the erasure of embodiment.
- [[Embodiment]] — material, enacted being; what the Turing test erases and what Hayles works to restore.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the book's central opposition, introduced here via Shannon/Wiener's substrate-independent information.
- [[The Body as Data]] — identity reconceived as informational pattern rather than embodied enaction (the Moravec proposition).
- [[Cybernetics]] — the field whose founding move the Prologue locates in the Turing/Shannon/Wiener abstraction of information from body.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the "reverse feedback loop" by which asking "what can think" changes "who can think"; the cybernetic circuit that constitutes the cyborg.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the condition into which the liberal Enlightenment subject is transformed once embodiment is recognized as constitutive of thought.
- [[The Liberal Subject]] — the Enlightenment "model of the human" (the J.S. Mill liberal) whose boundaries the Turing test renegotiates.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the figure produced when enacted and represented bodies are joined through the connecting technology.

## Key Arguments

- [[The Turing test performs the erasure of embodiment]] — at the inaugural moment of the computer age, intelligence is redefined as formal symbol-manipulation, not enaction in the lifeworld.
- [[Information is a bodiless fluid abstracted from its substrate]] — Shannon and Wiener's definition lets information "flow between different substrates without loss," enabling the Moravec dream of downloading consciousness.
- [[Embodiment makes thought a function of the form enacting it]] — Hayles' counter-thesis: embodiment secures not the univocality of gender or human identity but the dependence of thought on its embodied form.
- [[The Turing test makes the participant a cyborg before any guess is made]] — the cybernetic circuit splices will, desire, and perception into a distributed cognitive system, so "you have already become posthuman."

## Important Definitions

- [[The Moravec Test]] — Hayles' coinage for Moravec's scenario of downloading consciousness, "the logical successor to the Turing test," designed to show machines can become the repository of human consciousness.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Turing Test as a Magic Trick]] — the test relies on accepting early assumptions that determine later interpretation; the real intervention precedes the guess.
- [[Flickering Signifier|Flickering Signifiers]] — the symbols scrolling down the screen at which one gazes while "already" becoming posthuman (developed fully in the following chapters).
- [[The Cybernetic Circuit]] — the splice joining enacted and represented bodies through flexible machine interfaces.

## Major Tensions

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — enacted body present in the flesh versus the represented body produced through verbal and semiotic markers.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — substrate versus bodiless informational pattern; the material body versus its representation on screen.
- [[Saying vs Doing]] — Hodges' insight (via Turing's biography) that what can be *said* is not limited by puzzle-solving intelligence but by restrictions on what can be *done*.
