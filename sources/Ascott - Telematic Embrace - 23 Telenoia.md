# Source Summary: Telenoia

## Orientation

"Telenoia" (1993, originally published in *On Line–Kunst im Netz*) is the essay in which Ascott coins his keyword **telenoia** — "networked consciousness, interactive awareness, mind at large" — and pitches it explicitly as the convivial, networked OPPOSITE of the *paranoia* of the isolated, alienated single self bred by industrial "smokestack culture." The piece opens by confronting two questions repeatedly put to him about art in the telematic culture ("Can computer communications technology sustain art?" and "Will telematic systems destroy the humanness of human communication?"), then turns both back on the questioner: telematics can not only sustain but extend art into unprecedented forms, while the "human" communication it supposedly threatens was always already mediated, top-down, and controlled. Telematics, Ascott insists, "is both a technology and a medium" — and as a medium it is "at the heart of interactivity, of diffusion with reciprocity, of telepresence and informatic negotiation."

From this base the essay mounts a sweeping case that telematic networking is not merely a new technology but a fundamental evolutionary and spiritual rupture: "Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness; it is spiritual before it is in any way utilitarian." Ascott narrates his own networking projects as evidence — *La Plissure du Texte* (1983, the origin of "distributed authorship"), the *Organe et Fonction d'Alice* Minitel piece inside Lyotard's "Les Immatériaux" (1984), the *Planetary Network* at the 1986 Venice Biennale, *Gaia* at Ars Electronica (1989), and the *Telenoia* V2 event (1992) — and announces the founding of CAiiA, his "data garden," the seed of what would become the Planetary Collegium. The chapter is the primary anchor for the keyword [[Telenoia]] and for [[The Holomatic Principle]] ("to be at any one interface is to be in the virtual presence of all other interfaces"), and it consolidates Ascott's canonical five-fold schema — connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, emergence — as "the five-fold path towards telenoia."

The essay is exceptionally cross-link-rich for the vault. Ascott explicitly endorses [[Donna Haraway]]'s "Cyborg Manifesto" as more useful for the telematic condition than most male-dominated technoculture conferences, and [[Hans Moravec]]'s downloading of personality "from wetware to hardware" as offering "more to offer us than Freud." His rhetorical posture is visionary, polemical, and self-mythologising — but with a rare flash of self-criticism (he flags the "callous indifference to homelessness" born of the longing to escape the earthly body). Crucially, he REFUSES the dematerialisation/disembodiment reading: the erasure of the Cartesian body "leads, however, not to a disembodiment of the person, nor to an immateriality, but to the rematerialisation," a position that gives him a distinctive pole within the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension and a direct counterpoint to Lippard's dematerialization.

## Key Concepts

- [[Telenoia]] — networked consciousness / interactive awareness / "mind at large" (after Bateson); the convivial networked successor to industrial paranoia. This chapter is its naming and primary anchor.
- [[Telematic Art]] — telematics as "the fusion of computers and telecommunications systems," "both a technology and a medium"; the work that generates meaning only through participation from inside the network.
- [[The Holomatic Principle]] — each interface is an aspect of a telematic unity; to be at one interface is to be in the virtual presence of all others; data flow at one point is the data flow at all points.
- [[Distributed Authorship]] — the dissolution of the solitary author across scattered nodes; Ascott dates his coinage to 1983 (*La Plissure du Texte*).
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the "growing together of minds"; the planetary, distributed mind of networks.
- [[The Distributed Self]] — "l'homme éclaté" (Virilio); the unitary self becomes "the multiple, distributed presences of a set of many selves."
- [[The Post-Biological Body]] — the body-as-interface; the bionic, prosthetic corporeality of the cyborg culture; "the post-biological era."
- [[Emergence]] — the fifth element of the five-fold path; art "emergent from a multiplicity of interactions in data space."
- [[New Media Art]] — the post-biological, electronic, online arts as process and system rather than finite object; the museum/academy of the twenty-first century.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — immersion, interaction, and transformation of the viewer; electronic space "as a space for personal transformation."
- [[Cyborg Art]] / [[The Cyborg]] — "the creation of a cyborg culture"; "a new corporeality, which is almost totally artificial, bionic, prosthetic" (Ascott citing Haraway).
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — "diffusion with reciprocity," the two-way interactive interface inherited from Ascott's behaviourist/cybernetic period.
- [[Virtuality]] — the power of virtual reality "to transform the world," opening "the inevitable pathway to our co-evolutionary development of artificial life."
- [[Artificial Life]] — the post-biological extension of the environment; "the replacement of artifice with artificial life."
- [[Autopoiesis]] — Maturana and Varela's "structural coupling," the autonomous self-creating organism that constructs its world, replacing the mind-as-mirror model.

## Key Arguments

- [[Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness spiritual before utilitarian]] — the cultural rupture is driven by a longing that also produced the technologies; the technology is fundamentally about consciousness, not utility.
- [[Networking re-materialises the body it does not dematerialise it]] — the networked erasure of the material body is not disembodiment or immateriality but re-materialisation into a new bionic corporeality.
- [[In telematic art meaning is produced through interaction not transmitted]] — a telematic work generates meaning through intimate participation and cannot be viewed as a discrete object from outside.
- [[The artist is a system that generates meaning and its humanity is of little significance]] — the artist becomes a distributed (human + artificial) system; human value resides in the (human) viewer's intervention.
- [[The unified individual is dead the networked self is multiple]] — the disappearance of the single, hermetically isolated self; "the explosion of the one and the connectivity of the many."
- [[Telematic art rejects representation in favour of simulation]] — the end of the mimetic/symbolic; art shifts from representing the world to creating worlds and emerging from participation.
- [[Telematic networks constitute a planetary network consciousness]] — the global Net integrates connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence into a convivial life in the net.

## Major Tensions

- [[Telenoia vs Paranoia]] — THE keystone opposition: networked, distributed, convivial consciousness vs the isolated, alienated, secretive single self of industrial culture.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott's distinctive RE-MATERIALISATION pole within this eight-author tension: he explicitly refuses immateriality/disembodiment.
- [[Object vs System]] — "art not as finite object but as process and system"; the participatory/networked pole beside Burnham's object→system spine.
- [[Representation vs Simulation]] — the move from imitating life to emulating it, "as part of the co-evolution with nature."
