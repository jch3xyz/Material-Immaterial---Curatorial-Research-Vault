## Orientation

This chapter is the pivot of *How We Became Posthuman*: it is where Hayles stops diagnosing the erasure of the body and starts building the positive theoretical machinery she will use to resist it. Her target is what she calls "the postmodern orthodoxy that the body is primarily, if not entirely, a linguistic and discursive construction" — an orthodoxy she sees converging from two directions at once, the cybernetic stripping of information from its material substrate and the humanities' archaeological reading of the body as "a play of discourse systems." Against the ecstatic dematerialization rhetoric she quotes (Baudrillard, the Krokers, O. B. Hardison echoing Hans Moravec), she makes her signature move: the disappearance of the body is not a fact but the symptom of a new, materially produced subjectivity, "constituted by the crossing of the materiality of informatics with the immateriality of information." The dream of leaving the body behind is itself an embodied, material achievement.

To talk about embodiment without re-absorbing it into discourse, Hayles erects a four-term framework of two interacting polarities. The first is **the body vs. embodiment**: the body is the normalized, Platonic, culturally constructed abstraction, while embodiment is the contextual, instantiated, performative experience that "never coincides exactly with 'the body'" — echoing Grosz's dictum that "there is no body as such; there are only bodies." The second is **inscription vs. incorporation**: inscription is an abstract, transportable system of signs, while incorporation is a practice "encoded into bodily memory by repeated performances until it becomes habitual." The whole chapter is organized by a homology — "as the body is to embodiment, so inscription is to incorporation" — with the two polarities imagined as "two sine waves moving at different frequencies."

The first half is a critical survey. Foucault's Panopticon is the exemplary case of analytic power purchased through disembodiment: its abstraction "beyond any obstacle, resistance or friction" gives the analysis universality but "diverts attention away from how actual bodies … impose, incorporate, and resist." Hayles assembles a corrective tradition — Elaine Scarry on torture, Elizabeth Grosz's Möbius-strip "volatile bodies," Hubert Dreyfus on non-formalizable embodied learning, Pierre Bourdieu's *habitus*, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Paul Connerton on how societies remember through bodily ritual, and Mark Johnson on how "the body writes discourse" through metaphoric networks. The second half is a literary demonstration: a reading of William Burroughs's *The Ticket That Exploded*, situated in the media history of audiotape (Poulsen's Telegraphone, steel and film tape, the BBC, the late-1950s convergence of permanence and erasability). Burroughs "turns the tables" on the dematerializers — "Instead of discourse dematerializing the body, in Ticket the body materializes discourse" — making "the word" a literal viral parasite and the body a tape-recorder. Hayles closes by aligning Burroughs with Maturana and Philip K. Dick on reflexivity, and reads his mutating bodies as "a harbinger of the posthuman body."

## Key Arguments

- The disappearance of the body is not a fact but the symptom of a new, materially produced subjectivity — the dematerialization rhetoric is re-read as ideology. (Directly stated)
- Abstraction buys analytic power by erasing embodiment, and Foucault's Panopticon is the exemplary case; Foucault "reinscribes as well as challenges" the disembodiment he exposes. (Directly stated)
- Embodiment is neither essentialist nor algorithmic: embodied experience is dispersed along a spectrum (no privileged position), and embodied learning has an open "inner horizon" that cannot be fully formalized. (Directly stated)
- The body writes discourse, not only the reverse — bodily orientation in space generates metaphoric networks (Johnson); "To look at thought in this way is to turn Descartes upside down." (Directly stated)
- Incorporation is the crucial link in the feedback loop between technology and discourse. (Directly stated)
- In Burroughs's *The Ticket That Exploded*, the body materializes discourse rather than discourse dematerializing the body. (Directly stated)

## Important Definitions

- **Embodiment** (defined against "the body") — "embodiment is contextual, enmeshed within the specifics of place, time, physiology, and culture, which together compose enactment"; the body, by contrast, "is always normative relative to some set of criteria." (Directly stated)
- **Incorporating practice** (following Connerton/Merleau-Ponty) — "an action that is encoded into bodily memory by repeated performances until it becomes habitual." (Directly stated)
- **Inscription** — a system of signs "operating independently of any particular manifestation"; "a conceptual abstraction rather than as an instantiated materiality." (Directly stated)
- **The *habitus*** (Bourdieu, quoted) — "the 'durably installed generative principle of regulated improvisations.'" (Directly stated)
- **Medial ecology** (Kittler, named) — the niche-configuring of a medium "through the interlocking qualities of the audio technologies that preceded it." (Directly stated)

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- **Two sine waves in syncopation** — the body/embodiment and inscription/incorporation polarities "acting in complex syncopation with each other, like two sine waves moving at different frequencies."
- **The Möbius strip** (Grosz) — outside becomes inside becomes outside; Hayles adopts it for "volatile bodies" / mutating surfaces but critiques its inability to chart gradations.
- **Funes the Memorious** (Borges) — pure particularity without abstraction; embodiment articulated apart from the body would be "a froth of discrete utterances."
- **The body as tape-recorder** — the central literary-technical metaphor; "for certain texts after 1950, the body became a tape-recorder."
- **The word as virus / the "Other Half"** — discourse as bodily infestation; "crab parasites" and "green fish parasites" in the flesh.
- **"Word dust" / "picture dust"** — inscription literalized as falling particulate from magnetized walls.
- **The "reality studio" / "Grey Room"** — culture's central control room producing prerecorded sound and image tracks; the "film bank" that is empty.
- [[The Panopticon]] — the master symbol of disembodied, universalized, frictionless power.

## Major Tensions

- **The body vs. embodiment** — normalized Platonic abstraction vs. instantiated contextual particular; the chapter's first axis.
- **Inscription vs. incorporation** — transportable abstract sign vs. context-bound embodied practice.
- **Abstraction / analytic power vs. attention to bodies** — the productive double-bind in Foucault: power gained = bodies lost.
- **Presence vs. absence / repetition vs. mutation** — the paradoxical "technoconceptual actors" of magnetic tape.
- **Pattern vs. randomness** — randomness as the only non-prerecorded element; "pattern is always already penetrated by randomness."
- **Liberal humanist subject vs. reflexive/posthuman subject** — the observer who "cannot stand apart from the systems being observed."

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **Material vs. immaterial as a single circuit.** Use the "crossing of the materiality of informatics with the immateriality of information" to argue that immateriality is a material achievement — a direct counter to dematerialization rhetoric, and a bridge to Han's transparency/information and to post-internet "image objects."
- **The body as data, by negation.** PET imaging normalizing embodiment into "a body," and Cherryh's "psychset," give concrete emblems for [[The Body as Data]]; pair with Dreyfus/Bourdieu on knowledge that *resists* conversion to data.
- **Inscription/incorporation as a media-history method.** The audiotape case (inscription + erasability) models how a technology's material affordances seed new subjectivities — portable to McLuhan, Kittler, and to curatorial questions about recording media.
- **Reflexivity and the dissolved observer.** The Burroughs–Maturana–Dick convergence ("the observer cannot stand apart from the systems being observed") is a hinge between second- and third-wave cybernetics and the end of liberal humanism — usable in a [[Posthumanism]] / [[Systems Theory]] essay.
- **Critique of disembodied power.** The Foucault/Panopticon argument ("abstraction gains power by erasing embodiment") connects Hayles's [[The Panopticon]] reading to Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] from a materialist angle.
