# Source Summary: Chapter 23 — Ads: Keeping Upset with the Joneses

**Book:** [[Understanding Media]]
**Author:** [[Marshall McLuhan]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/27 Chapter 23 - Ads - Keeping Upset with the Joneses]]

## Orientation

This is a Part II case-study chapter on advertising, and one of the more conceptually generative of the Part II "media" chapters: it both reapplies the Part I apparatus and pushes one genuinely distinctive thesis. McLuhan's controlling observation is that advertising has abandoned the consumer "picture" of the product in favor of the producer's iconic "image of process" that folds seller, consumer, and society into "a single image." "The product matters less as the audience participation increases"; "The trend in ads, then, is away from the consumer picture of product to the producer image of process." The ad has become an icon — "not specialist fragments or aspects but unified and compressed images of complex kind" — and so it shares the compressed, participatory, in-depth structure of the TV image and of "mosaic news" (the post-TV upsurge of *Time* and *Newsweek*), which has "greatly weakened" the older fragmentary pictorial magazine.

From that diagnosis McLuhan draws the chapter's signature claim, the one durably distinctive idea worth its own note: advertising is "a crude attempt to extend the principles of automation to every aspect of society," using "handicraft methods" to grope "toward the ultimate electronic goal of a collective consciousness," aiming at "a programmed harmony among all human impulses and aspirations and endeavors" — at which terminus "advertising will have liquidated itself by its own success." Because the ad team out-researches the sociologist (with "billions to spend annually on research and testing of reactions"), "any acceptable ad is a vigorous dramatization of communal experience," and "the ads of our time are the richest and most faithful daily reflections that any society ever made of its entire range of activities." McLuhan thus inverts the moralizing critique of advertising: the scandal is not deceptive copy but the automation of the social unconscious toward pre-established harmony.

The rest of the chapter restates the Part I machinery. It is a textbook [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] case — the "cool" TV world has snagged the advertiser's old hot hard-sell, made the "earnest-talking salesmen" obsolete, and rendered any consciously attended ad "comical" (the Mort Sahl / *MAD* mockery). It names movie and radio flatly as "hot media" ([[Media Hot and Cold]]), deploys the [[The Mosaic|mosaic]] / icon-versus-"point of view" opposition (the "literate and private 'point of view'" yielding to "the complex and inclusive world of the group icon"), reuses [[Detribalization and Retribalization|retribalization]] (oral cultures translate radio "into tribal politics," "the retribalized Nazi"), and runs the subliminal/depth-onslaught thesis ([[Sense Ratios]]): ads are "subliminal pills for the subconscious," working by "noise" and "repetition." The rhetorical posture is diagnostic and structural, not censorious — McLuhan treats the literate moralist who attacks "false and misleading ad copy" as a "godsend to advertisers."

## Key Concepts

- [[Advertising as the Extension of Automation Toward a Collective Consciousness]] — the chapter's keystone: ads reframed not as persuasion of individuals but as a "crude attempt to extend the principles of automation to every aspect of society," reaching toward "a programmed harmony among all human impulses" and "the ultimate electronic goal of a collective consciousness."
- [[The Mosaic]] — the iconic ad is the advertising form of the TV mosaic: a compressed, "unified," non-fragmentary image that fuses producer, consumer, seller, and society at once; "mosaic news" (*Time*, *Newsweek*) is its journalistic parallel.
- [[Media Hot and Cold]] — movie and radio named flatly as "hot media" whose arrival "pepped up everybody," giving the Roaring Twenties and the platform for "sales promotion as a way of life"; the "new cool TV world" reverses it. The participation axis is explicit: "the product matters less as the audience participation increases."
- [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] — "Since the advent of TV, the exploitation of the unconscious by the advertiser has hit a snag"; TV "favors much more consciousness concerning the unconscious," making the hot hard-sell obsolete.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — the subliminal/depth thesis applied to advertising: ads are "subliminal pills for the subconscious," asserting "a small pellet or pattern in a noisy, redundant barrage of repetition." "The sensory tolerance of the audience has changed."
- [[Typographic Man]] — the "Gutenberg structural assumptions" and the "literate and private 'point of view'" that the TV mosaic replaces; "highly literate people cannot cope with the nonverbal art of the pictorial."
- [[Acoustic vs Visual Space]] — the visual/literate "point of view" yielding to the all-at-once "group icon"; the deep grammar beneath the picture-versus-icon shift.
- [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] — oral cultures translate "the message of radio into tribal politics, rather than into a new means of pushing Cadillacs"; "the retribalized Nazi" versus "the American consumer."
- [[Explosion vs Implosion]] — the 1920s "expanding and exploding, in separating and teasing and tearing" versus TV's "opposite process of integrating and interrelating."
- [[The Medium Is the Message]] — the form of the ad (icon/mosaic), not its "content" (copy), does the work; "the job of the ad copy is as incidental and latent, as the 'meaning' of a poem is to a poem."

## Key Arguments

- [[Advertising will liquidate itself by its own success]] — advertising strains toward a programmed, collective consciousness in which production, consumption, desire, and effort are pre-harmonized; total success would dissolve the need for it.
- [[Ads are the richest daily reflection a society ever made of itself]] — built on tested public stereotypes by teams who out-research the sociologist, "any acceptable ad is a vigorous dramatization of communal experience," making the ads of an era the truest daily record a society ever kept (the Egyptian hieroglyph "lags far behind").

## Important Definitions

No new canonical term is formally defined here; the chapter applies the Part I definitions ([[The Medium Is the Message]], [[Media Hot and Cold]], [[Media as Extensions of Man]]). Two passages function as compact applied glosses, captured inside the concepts above rather than as standalone definition notes:

- Advertising "as a crude attempt to extend the principles of automation to every aspect of society" — the gloss grounding [[Advertising as the Extension of Automation Toward a Collective Consciousness]].
- "Mosaic news" — "neither narrative, nor point of view, nor explanation, nor comment," but "a corporate image in depth of the community in action"; folded into [[The Mosaic]].

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- The Madison Avenue "frogmen-of-the-mind" — admen as covert divers into the unconscious, designing ads "for semiconscious exposure."
- "Subliminal pills for the subconscious" — ads as medication swallowed below awareness "to exercise an hypnotic spell."
- The expensive ad "built on bedrock like a skyscraper" — engineered on "the tested foundations of public stereotypes or 'sets' of established attitudes."
- "Silver Threads among the Gold" for a strip-tease — the grotesque incongruity of communal experience used "in grotesque ways" when "looked at consciously."
- The teetotaler and the brewer / the censor and the book — the moral protestor who only advertises and accelerates what is protested.
- The movie as a "nonstop ad" / the "Arabian Nights treatment" — when movies came, "the entire pattern of American life went on the screen as a nonstop ad."
- "Fur and fuzz, blur and buzz" — the skin-diver — the post-TV softening of the ad image for the tactile, depth-immersed TV viewer.
- The Container Corporation of America ad — the master emblem of the icon-of-process: it features "the container function, by means of great art," not the product.
- The Egyptian hieroglyph that "lags far behind" — the historical record-foil to the modern ad.

## Major Tensions

- [[Hot vs Cool Media]] — the hot, hard-sell, earnest-salesman advertising of the movie/radio age versus the cool TV world that makes it comical.
- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] — the fragmentary consumer "picture of product" versus the unified "icon" and "the complex togetherness of the corporate posture."
- [[Numbness vs Awareness]] — ads "intended as subliminal pills for the subconscious" versus the TV-induced "consciousness concerning the unconscious" that makes "any ad consciously attended to" comical.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the arm's-length consumer-picture versus the icon that "include[s] producer and consumer, seller and society in a single image" and "invites maximal participation in the social process."
- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the "individualist hard-sell salesman and the docile consumer" of the mechanical/hot age versus the "all-inclusive and never-ending process" of the corporate-electric image.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A genealogy of the attention/affect economy that puts McLuhan upstream of Han's [[Psychopolitics]] / [[Emotional Capitalism]] and Harari's [[Society as Algorithm]] / [[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves]]: advertising's "programmed harmony among all human impulses" as the handicraft forerunner of behavioral data — "keeping upset with the Joneses" as the engineered, productive anxiety that drives consumption.
- The icon-of-process (the Container Corporation ad) as a material/immaterial case for [[Material vs Immaterial]]: advertising deserting the "individual consumer-product" for the corporate "Image" of an "all-inclusive and never-ending process" — the product dematerializing into a managed social field. Pairs with Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] and Hayles's information-as-pattern.
- McLuhan's inversion of the critique of advertising — "any acceptable ad is a vigorous dramatization of communal experience," the ads of an era "the richest and most faithful daily reflections that any society ever made" — as a method for reading ads as archival evidence rather than ideology to be debunked; the literate protestor as the advertiser's "godsend."
- The TV-cooling-of-the-hard-sell ([[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]]) as a case in any argument about how a new medium retires the sensibility of the prior one — *Death of a Salesman* and "the advent of TV" not coinciding "by accident."
