## Orientation

This is the second chapter of the book's opening "Image" section, and it keeps the memoir frame of Chapter 1: O'Gieblyn has a Sony Aibo robot dog on loan, and her marriage becomes the domestic stage on which the chapter's larger philosophical questions play out. Her husband's shifting reactions to the dog — first "creepy," then suspicious of its camera and surveillance, then unconsciously affectionate (naming and petting it, comparing it to his old rescue dog Oscar), then paranoid about Sony harvesting their data — braid a personal narrative through an essayistic argument about consciousness, animal minds, and machine minds. [[The Aibo]] is the chapter's master symbol: a simulation of life that elicits real attachment while doubling as a data-extraction device.

The chapter's argumentative spine is O'Gieblyn's signature method, [[Technology as Metaphor]] — the recurring observation that "we say humans are like a god, like a clock, or like a computer," and that each era's dominant machine becomes the lens through which the self is understood. Here that method is turned on the [[Mind as Machine]] metaphor (the mind as software running on the brain's hardware), which she argues has not escaped but merely re-inscribed Cartesian dualism: the soul "keeps slipping back in." From there she traces a cluster of consequences — the moving goalposts of machine intelligence (chess, Deep Blue vs. Kasparov, Searle, Hofstadter); the long Western denial of animal minds (Descartes's beast-machine, Darwin's "godlike intellect," Goodall and the chimps); [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] and its eliminativist deniers ([[Eliminativism]] — Dennett's "Cartesian theater," Strawson's "the Great Denial"); the [[Disenchantment]] of the West against the un-disenchanted animism of Japan; and the simulation-versus-reality problem (Frazer's imitative magic, Searle's rainstorm).

The ex-evangelical memoir thread carries real argumentative weight. O'Gieblyn contrasts her former Christian "naive, unquestioning faith in the faculty of higher thought" (Augustine, reason connected to the Absolute) with her present-day distrust of mind in a materialist university town where everyone talks about bodies, diets, and drugs and is told in yoga to "let go of the thinking mind." The theological register is not decoration: the chapter argues that the computational metaphor is a secularized continuation of the soul, and that eliminativism is itself "much like Descartes's soul," placing consciousness outside time and space. It closes on Nagel's "view from nowhere" and the claim that science succeeded precisely by bracketing consciousness out — so the computer metaphor has not solved the mind-body problem but only "transferred them to a new substrate."

## Key Concepts

- [[Eliminativism]] — the position that consciousness/interior experience simply does not exist (Dennett's "Cartesian theater," Strawson's "Great Denial"); O'Gieblyn treats it as itself crypto-Cartesian.

## Key Arguments

- [[The computer metaphor reintroduces the disembodied soul it was built to abolish]] — "mind = software, brain = hardware" is structurally identical to Descartes's soul/body split; physicalists' own language ("generate," "give rise to") betrays a covert dualism.
- [[The computer metaphor has not solved the mind-body problem only transferred it to a new substrate]] — the metaphor entrenches rather than dies; the old problems (Papert) "come up in equivalent form in the new context."
- [[Science succeeded by bracketing consciousness then denied it exists]] — Descartes's mind/matter division enabled modern science (Nagel's view from nowhere), but the methodological exclusion of consciousness hardened into metaphysical denial.
- [[The anthropomorphism charge misreads the flow of the metaphor]] — the twin tendencies to attribute mind to machines/animals and to redraw human distinction as machines master formerly "human" tasks (chess to emotion/perception).
- [[Emergence is vitalism updated and uncomfortably like magic]] — building brain-simulations and hoping consciousness will emerge is a form of Frazer's imitative magic ("like produces like"); a simulation of a thing is not the thing.

## Important Definitions

- [[The Dead Metaphor (O'Gieblyn)]] — soul-talk and "intuition of distinctness" persisting at the level of grammar; Nietzsche: "we haven't gotten rid of God because we still believe in grammar."
- [[Disenchantment (O'Gieblyn)]] — the Weberian draining of soul/spirit from the world, against which Japanese animism stands as the un-disenchanted alternative.
- res cogitans / res extensa (Descartes) — the subjective mind set apart from the objective, mathematical realm of physical facts; the precondition of the "view from nowhere."
- kuyō — the Japanese purification rite given at funerals, extended to long-used broken objects (needles, hooks, the discontinued Aibo).

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[Mind as Machine]] — the chapter's central figure: mind as software on the brain's hardware; the brain as hard drive, the mind as supervening pattern/algorithm.
- [[Graven Images]] — "We are not gods, capable of creating things in our likeness. All we can make are graven images"; AI as a failed, idolatrous imitation of life.
- [[The Aibo]] — boundary-object between living and nonliving, mind and machine; companion and surveillance device at once.
- The "Cartesian theater" (Dennett) — the homunculus watching the brain's representations on a movie screen, generating the infinite-regress problem.
- Imitative magic / "like produces like" (Frazer) — the effigy and rain-magic; "the belief in metaphor as magic," applied to consciousness-engineering.

## Major Tensions

- [[Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment]] — the disenchanted West (Descartes, materialism) against an un-disenchanted Japan where objects have soul; the Aibo sits on the fault line.
- [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality]] — anthropomorphism and animist mind-matter continuity against the differentiation impulse that keeps moving the bar of human distinction.
- [[Metaphor vs Literalism]] — whether the computer-mind metaphor is a heuristic or a literal identity; simulation conflated with reality.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — higher cognition (chess, equations) versus the embodied perception/feeling machines fail at; mind as immaterial pattern versus flesh and blood.
- [[Subject vs Object]] — the view from nowhere purchased by excluding res cogitans against the irreducibility of first-person experience.
