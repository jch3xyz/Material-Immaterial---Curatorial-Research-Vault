# Source Summary: Chapter 3 — Being Able Not to Be Able

**Book:** [[The Agony of Eros]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]

## Orientation

"Being Able Not to Be Able" is the pivotal conceptual chapter of *The Agony of Eros* and the fullest primary-text statement in the vault of Han's *negative* theory of eros. Its title names the book's core positive proposal: eros as *Nicht-Können-Können* — "being able not to be able," the negative counterpart of the achievement society's boundless *You can*. The chapter's argument runs in a single arc. First, it re-states the diagnosis of the [[Achievement Society]] carried over from *The Burnout Society* and *Psychopolitics*: disciplinary *should* has been replaced by *can*, and the subject, now an "entrepreneur of the self," has become "perpetrator and victim in one," self-exploiting under a feeling of liberty. Then it turns that same "You can" into the very structure that annihilates alterity, because ability, mastery, grasping, and possessing all convert the Other into an object to be consumed. Eros, by contrast, is "a relationship to the Other situated beyond achievement, performance, and ability" — and its condition is *the atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability*.

Han grounds the positive account almost entirely in two authorities. Emmanuel Levinas's *Time and the Other* supplies the atopic Other "impossible to translate into powers," the caress as "a game with something slipping away," pleasure, and absence "in a horizon of the future"; Martin Buber's "Distance and Relation" supplies "primal distance" (*Urdistanz*) as "the very 'principle of being-human'" and the Thou/It distinction. Around this spine the chapter mounts three sharp polemics: against Foucault (the neoliberal *Homo oeconomicus* "only thinks himself free"); against Benjamin (capitalism "is not a religion" because it has debt but no atonement, so depression is "psychic insolvency"); and against the positivized, health-obedient sexuality Han reads out of *Fifty Shades of Grey*, where "every form of negativity is prohibited" and even torture must be "delicious."

For the vault this chapter is the anchor of the book's negative-eros cluster. It is the densest single source for the realized concepts [[Eros]], [[The Other]], [[Atopia]], and [[Primal Distance (Urdistanz)]], and it delivers the erotic application of [[Negative Potency]] — Agamben's power-not-to reformulated as the courage to *not* master, grasp, or possess. Its rhetorical posture is diagnostic-then-redemptive: the first half is a merciless anatomy of auto-exploitation and its unredeemable guilt; the second recovers eros, via Levinas, as the one relation that "interrupts the exchange rate" because "otherness admits no bookkeeping."

## Key Concepts

- [[Eros]] — the relation to the Other "beyond achievement, performance, and ability"; the chapter closes by defining it economically as "an asymmetrical relationship to the Other" that "interrupts the exchange rate." This chapter is its primary Han anchor.
- [[The Other]] — the wholly other whose alterity "eludes all ability"; if one "could possess, grasp, and know the other, it would not be other." Its disappearance is the chapter's central diagnosis: "When otherness is stripped from the Other, one cannot love—one can only consume."
- [[Atopia]] — "the atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability—is constitutive of erotic experience"; the placeless otherness "which cannot be assimilated into the language of the Same," bound to futurity and the event.
- [[Primal Distance (Urdistanz)]] — Buber's term for the distance that "serves as the very 'principle of being-human'" and "constitutes the transcendental condition for any alterity"; when the Other becomes a sexual object it erodes, and the Other is "no longer a 'Thou.'"
- [[Negative Potency]] — the faculty here given its erotic form as "being able not to be able (Nicht-Können-Können)," the negative counterpart of *You can*; "Only by way of being able not to be able does the Other appear."
- [[Achievement Society]] — "wholly dominated by the modal verb *can*," in contrast to disciplinary society; its "entrepreneur of the self" self-exploits and, when he fails, "personally bears the guilt."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — "The exploiter is the exploited"; self-exploitation "proves much more efficient than allo-exploitation because it is accompanied by a feeling of liberty."
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — the older, less efficient exploitation "by a commanding and exploiting Other," now displaced by auto-exploitation "without domination."
- [[Positivity]] — love "positivized into a formula for enjoyment"; "This world of positivity admits only things that can be consumed" — the frame of the *Fifty Shades* reading.
- [[Negativity]] — the animating power the achievement world has lost: "things are enlivened precisely by their opposite. Mere positivity lacks any such power to animate."
- [[Capital as Religion]] — engaged here in its *dissenting* valence: capitalism, "counter to widespread belief (e.g., Benjamin)—is not a religion," because it has "debt and default" but "no possibility for atonement."
- [[Hell of the Same]] — "the temporality of the Same," the total present that abolishes the absence and futurity of the Other.
- [[The Event]] — "the future lies open to the event, which is absolutely surprising"; futurity as the time of the atopic Other.
- [[Narrativity]] — memory as "a progressive, living, narrative process" opposed to timeless "data storage"; the erosion of the decisive moment into "the temporality of the click."
- [[Pathos of Distance]] — resonant background to Han's insistence that "the otherness of the Other commands distance" and that dignity, decency, and propriety are "matters of maintaining distance."
- [[Pornography (Han)]] — anticipated by the pornographic "face" of "total visibility and consumption," opposed to the eros-bearing "countenance."
- [[Spectacle]] — cross-linked via the pornographic face "that holds no secrets" and the society "as a search engine, a machine for consumption."

## Key Arguments

- [[The freedom of Can is more coercive than the disciplinary Should]] — "You can exercises even greater constraint than You should. Auto-compulsion proves more fatal than allo-compulsion, because there is no way to resist oneself." The chapter's sharpest instance of this standing Han argument.
- [[Freedom itself is producing coercion]] — the "seeming freedom" of the achievement-subject conceals a "compulsive structure"; the paradoxical imperative *Be free* "plunges the achievement-subject into depression and exhaustion."
- *Absolutizing ability annihilates the Other; only "being able not to be able" lets the Other appear* — because "possessing, knowing, and grasping are synonyms of power," any object one *can* master ceases to be Other, and "a successful relationship with the Other finds expression as a kind of failure." *(Rendered as plain text — no canonical argument note exists yet for this claim; carried conceptually by [[Negative Potency]], [[Eros]], and [[The Other]].)*
- *Capitalism is not a religion: it has debt without atonement, and this unredeemable debt is the ground of depression* — "Every religion operates with both debt (guilt) and relief (pardon). But capitalism only works with debt and default," so failure is "psychic insolvency." *(Plain text — no argument note yet; see the reference [[Byung-Chul Han and Walter Benjamin]] and the concept [[Capital as Religion]].)*

## Important Definitions

- **Being able not to be able (*Nicht-Können-Können*)** — the negative potency proper to eros: the capacity, against the compulsive *You can*, to *not* be able, so that the Other may appear as Other. *(Directly stated; no dedicated definition note exists — carried by [[Negative Potency]] and [[Eros]].)*
- **Primal distance / *Urdistanz*** — the transcendental distance that "prevents the Other from being reified into an object, an 'it.'" *(Carried by [[Primal Distance (Urdistanz)]].)*
- **Psychic insolvency** — depression figured as the "impossibility of paying off a debt" (from the Latin *solvere*), because capitalism offers no atonement. *(Directly stated; carried interpretively by [[Achievement Society]].)*

## Candidate Essay Uses

- **The positive proposal of the negative-eros book.** This chapter supplies the affirmative core the vault otherwise only names — *Nicht-Können-Können*, the courage to relinquish mastery — grounding an essay on [[Negative Potency]] as the through-line from Agamben's power-not-to (*The Burnout Society*) to the erotic power not-to master here.
- **Freedom as the perfected form of coercion.** Han's reading of Foucault's *Homo oeconomicus* ("only thinks himself free when in fact he is exploiting himself") is the sharpest Agony-of-Eros instance of the standing "Foucault failed to make the turn" critique — a spine for an essay bridging [[Auto-Exploitation]], [[Freedom itself is producing coercion]], and [[Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault]].
- **Capital's missing atonement.** The "capitalism is not a religion" refutation of Benjamin sits in productive contradiction with the vault's [[Capital as Religion]] (Han's own *Psychopolitics*, and Harari's techno-religion) — an essay on debt, guilt, and depression could hold both valences: capital *as* religion in its worship, *not* a religion in its refusal of pardon.
- **Distance and the disappearance of the Other.** Buber's *Urdistanz* against social-media "crowding" ("we are making the Other disappear") connects [[Primal Distance (Urdistanz)]], [[Pathos of Distance]], and [[The Other]] to the vault's transparency/proximity themes — the abolition of remoteness as the abolition of nearness itself.
- **Pornography, the spectacle, and the secretless face.** The "countenance that gives and conceals" versus the pornographic "face" of "total visibility and consumption" links [[The Veil]], [[Pornography (Han)]], and Debord's [[Spectacle]] — a genealogy of exhibition running from spectacle to transparency to the consumed Other.
- **Otherness admits no bookkeeping.** The closing economic formulation — eros "interrupts the exchange rate" — is a strong hinge for [[Material vs Immaterial]] and any essay on what capitalism's "consumable difference" destroys when it eliminates non-marketable alterity.
