# Source Summary: Ch04 [Pattern]

## Orientation

"Pattern" is the keystone of the book's historical argument that transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology. O'Gieblyn opens from a Weberian premise — that modernity rests on the belief history is cumulative progress rather than cyclical repetition — and asks why "the same problems—and even the same metaphors—keep appearing century after century in new form," and specifically how the computer metaphor, an analogy "expressly designed to avoid the notion of a metaphysical soul," has returned us "these ancient religious ideas about physical transcendence and the disembodied spirit." The chapter is where the book's title-thesis — technology as the metaphor through which each era understands the self — is stated most fully and self-reflexively.

Her answer braids three strands. An intellectual-historical genealogy draws on Hayles's account of the Macy Conferences to show how information became decoupled from materiality and "the materialist's substitute for the soul," and how functionalism's "multiply realizable" mind underwrites mind-uploading. A theological genealogy traces the resurrection-by-technology tradition through Roger Bacon's elixir, Nikolai Fedorov's Russian-cosmist "common task," and — decisively — Pierre Teilhard de Chardin, whose Omega Point and coinage of "Trans-Human" she names as the direct precursor of Kurzweil's Singularity, an ancestry transhumanists suppress to preserve their image as "modern rationalism." A memoir strand frames her own ex-evangelical obsession with Kurzweil as a "transference" expressing "sublimated longing for the religious promises I'd abandoned."

The chapter then turns the screw with a "darker irony": transhumanism, while claiming to elevate humanity, "is actually nudging us down to a lower ontological status" — fatalistic, machine-supremacist, and indifferent to whether the uploaded self retains any subjective experience ("nothing will be going on inside"). Via Jaron Lanier she argues its real function is "doctrinal" not "prophetic," sacralizing information over human beings. The closing movement — an exchange of letters with Ray Kurzweil himself — crystallizes the method: existential answers are "necessarily metaphoric," atheism and theism differ only in "the choice of metaphor," and the danger is "taking metaphors—be it the Resurrection or the computational theory of mind—too literally."

## Key Concepts

- [[Transhumanism]] — read here not as Enlightenment rationalism but as "an outgrowth of Christian eschatology."
- [[Posthumanism]] — Hayles's posthuman as the critical/secular double of O'Gieblyn's transhumanism; the same disembodiment fantasy seen from the other side.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the Weberian modern condition: history as cumulative progress, and "the inability to account for the mind."
- [[Re-enchantment]] — transhumanism as one of Abou Farman's "reenchantment cosmologies" that nonetheless reiterates disenchantment's failure.
- [[Mind Uploading]] — technological resurrection; Musk's Neuralink promise to "upload into a new unit … Literally."
- [[Patternism]] — selfhood reduced to an abstract, substrate-independent pattern that can in principle be re-instantiated.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the "humans are nothing more than computation" / information-is-king substrate that Lanier's sacralized data presupposes.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the "second self," a "data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes" lodged in third-party aggregators.
- [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]] — Shannon's weightless information becoming "the materialist's substitute for the soul."
- [[Imago Dei]] — being made in God's image read as a portent of "future godhood" (Lewis, Stewart Brand's "We are gods").
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — the worry, treated obliquely, that an uploaded self may lack any subjective experience.
- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — O'Gieblyn's signature method: each age answers existential questions through analogies native to its dominant machine.

## Key Arguments

- [[Transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology]] — the unbroken lineage Bacon → Fedorov → Teilhard → Huxley → Kurzweil, suppressed to seem rational.
- [[The computer metaphor reintroduces the disembodied soul it was built to abolish]] — decontextualized information plus functionalism reproduce the dualism materialism meant to banish.
- [[The computer metaphor has not solved the mind-body problem only transferred it to a new substrate]] — the "darker irony" that uploading lowers rather than exalts our ontological status and may preserve "nothing inside."
- [[Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image]] — Kurzweil's claim that atheism and theism differ only in "the choice of metaphor," and the danger of literalizing the figurative.

## Important Definitions

- [[Functionalism and Multiple Realizability (O'Gieblyn)]] — mental states "multiply realizable," instantiated by any medium, biological or mechanical.
- [[The Omega Point and the Noosphere (O'Gieblyn)]] — Teilhard's planetary network of minds converging into an "'etherised' universal consciousness," culminating in an intelligence explosion.
- [[The Singularity (O'Gieblyn)]] — Kurzweil's intelligence explosion, named as the "obvious" descendant of the Omega Point.
- [[Disenchantment (O'Gieblyn)]] — modernity's defining inability to account for the mind, which even reenchantment narratives reiterate.

- [[Information Lost Its Body]] — Shannon's information as "a mathematical quantity weightless as sunshine," re-theologized as a secular soul.
- [[The Soul as an Empty Carapace]] — the uploaded self that behaves like us while "nothing will be going on inside."

## Major Tensions

- [[Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment]] — the most mystical narrative reproducing disenchantment's core failure.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — a materialist program that nonetheless reinstalls the disembodied, immaterial spirit.
- [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality]] — promised elevation to a "higher realm" versus reduction to machines, "objects … lower than plants and animals."
- [[Metaphor vs Literalism]] — the danger of "misconstruing the figurative as plainspoken truth," whether Resurrection or the computational theory of mind.
