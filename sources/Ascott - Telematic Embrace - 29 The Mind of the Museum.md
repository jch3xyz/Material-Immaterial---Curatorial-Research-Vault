# Source Summary: 29 The Mind of the Museum

**Book:** [[Telematic Embrace]]
**Author:** [[Roy Ascott]]

## Orientation

This 1996 essay is the keynote address for "The Total Museum: An Interactive Multimedia Conference" at the Art Institute of Chicago, October 1996 — previously unpublished, and reproduced from Ascott's own typescript. It is one of his clearest mid-1990s [[Technoetics|technoetic]]-era primary texts and the single strongest primary-source anchor in the *Telematic Embrace* corpus for the late-Ascott cluster — [[Technoetics]], [[Cyberception]], [[Telenoia]], and [[Moistmedia]] — which earlier chapters had only gestured at. Writing fresh from designing the Ars Electronica Center in Linz and helping evolve the NTT InterCommunication Center in Tokyo, Ascott deliberately "set[s] the debate on the museum ahead by a few years" to sketch a "post-museum scenario" for the early twenty-first century. (Confidence: Directly stated — from the raw front-matter footnote and opening paragraphs.)

Its rhetorical posture is polemical, visionary, and self-consciously "outrageous." Ascott opens with the banner provocation "I have seen the future— and it's moist," staging his [[Moistmedia|moist]] thesis that "artificial life and artificial consciousness meet our own wet biology and our telematic society of mind." He then runs an origin-myth-and-fall: the museum began as a "site of collective memory," "celebration," "creative imagination," and above all "spiritual transformation," but "went wrong when it adopted the values of the old industrial culture" — "a society of paranoia" — and must be reborn for the networked society of [[Telenoia|telenoia]], "consciousness-at-large." The central thesis ([[The Mind of the Museum]]) is that the museum "must become intelligent, must get smart": modeled on the brain, embodying its own [[Hypercortex (Ascott)|hypercortex]], it must be allowed to "think for itself," even "to assist at the birth of artificial consciousness." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

From there the talk develops the museum's true function as the control of *meaning* not merely information ([[The museum controls meaning not merely information]], citing Foucault and Donald Horne on "the autocratic subtext of museum mediation"); the museum's blind spot to telematic / [[Net Art]] ("art in the Net which has been with us for over a decade"); the inseparable intertwining of science, technology, and spirituality at the roots of twentieth-century art (the keystone [[Re-enchantment]] thread); the [[The Distributed Self|distribution of the self]] via telepresence and "teleprescience"; the [[Net Art|Net gallery]] as an "implicate" (Bohm), "deseriated," observer-dependent field of [[Emergence|emergence]]; the [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)|radical-constructivist]] claim that "all is metaphor" and "we talk the world into being," with museums as "traps and cages of imprisoned metaphors" to be replaced by [[Gardens of Hypotheses|gardens of hypotheses]]; the Disneyland / "world as museum" critique; the three coordinates of culture (technoetics / artificial life / intelligent architecture); and the closing [[The Wormhole|wormhole]] figure — shamanic time-travel → hyperlink → Kip Thorne's quantum-foam wormholes → "the museum must become a wormhole." For the vault this is the realization point and primary anchor for the technoetic cluster and the strongest single Ascott extension of [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], [[Re-enchantment]], [[Material vs Immaterial]], [[Embodiment]], and [[The Body as Data]]. (Confidence: Directly stated for the quotations; Interpretive synthesis for the vault placement.)

## Key Concepts

- [[The Mind of the Museum]] — the title thesis: the museum must "become intelligent," "get smart," and "think for itself," modeled on the brain and embodying its own [[Hypercortex (Ascott)|hypercortex]].
- [[The Museum of the Third Kind]] — Ascott's three-stage typology; the third kind is where "the close encounter of minds with minds, cortex to hypercortex, virtual with the actual, leads to interactivity at the level of the spirit."
- [[Technoetics]] — the convergence of technology and consciousness (*techne* + *noetic*), "concerning mind and consciousness"; this chapter is a primary defining text.
- [[Cyberception]] — "the technologically amplified faculties of perception and cognition"; the new faculty the museum must learn the cultural consequences of.
- [[Telenoia]] — the networked opposite of paranoia, from *tele* "far off" + *nous* "mind"; the "society of connectivity, open-ended creativity, collaborative consciousness."
- [[Moistmedia]] — the "future will be moist" thesis: artificial life "engendered in silicon" taking on "a moister reality," integrating with "the wet reality we call nature."
- [[Net Art]] — telematic art "in the Net," the "implicate," "deseriated," observer-dependent gallery; "the product of the connectivity of minds before it is anything else."
- [[Gardens of Hypotheses]] — the radical-constructivist replacement for the museum: "plant gardens of hypotheses and grow forests of metaphors, as real, physical sites to inhabit"; "knowledge landscapes."
- [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]] — "all is metaphor"; "we talk the world into being, that our descriptions are its becoming."
- [[The Distributed Self]] — through telematic networks "our presence is distributed"; "the many co-existing selves" displacing "the Western model of a unitary personality."
- [[Network Consciousness]] — "the community of mind for which the Net … is the substrate"; "telematic society of mind"; "mind at large."
- [[Re-enchantment]] — extended here as the technoetic re-enchantment thesis: "technology and science themselves … foregrounding issues of consciousness, mind, and spirit."
- [[Emergence]] — extended at its Net / poetic-emergence pole: "Emergence is the key behaviour in the Net"; Langton's "bottom-up, distributed" behaviour as the model of Net art.
- [[New Media Art]] — extended at its telematic / Net-art pole (Burnham's object/cybernetic art is the precursor; Ascott is the networked-immaterial successor).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — extended: "interactivity at the level of the spirit"; cyberception as the new experiential faculty.
- [[Hypermedia]] — the Net's "images, texts, sounds, and gestures co-exist in this hypermedia"; the wormhole-linked field of the new gallery.

## Key Arguments

- [[The museum controls meaning not merely information]] — against those who say its function "is simply to control information," Ascott holds it "serves and has always served to control meaning … to shape consciousness" (Foucault, Horne).
- [[The museum of the future must be a fluid organism not a static institution]] — "We have to stop thinking for the museum and allow it to think for itself"; the museum modeled on the brain, even assisting "at the birth of artificial consciousness."
- The museum lost its sacred heritage when it adopted industrial values, becoming "a society of paranoia" — and must be reborn for the society of [[Telenoia|telenoia]]. (Folded into [[Telenoia vs Paranoia]] and [[The Mind of the Museum]].)
- All is metaphor; museums "imprison" metaphors that should "roam freely in [[Gardens of Hypotheses|gardens of hypotheses]]." (Folded into [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]] / [[Gardens of Hypotheses]].)
- In telematic culture the self is distributed and embodiment ceases to be "the primary measure and sign of reality" — yet the body is not junked; we need "new metaphors of embodiment." (Folded into [[The Distributed Self]]; cross-links [[Embodiment vs Information]].)
- Net art is the connectivity of minds; "Noetic process precedes social process." (Folded into [[Net Art]] / [[Network Consciousness]].)
- Science, technology, and spirituality are inseparably intertwined at the roots of twentieth-century art. (Folded into [[Re-enchantment]].)
- The museum must become a [[The Wormhole|wormhole]] — "a rift in space/time" linking minds and worlds.

## Important Metaphors / Symbols

- [[The Wormhole]] — the closing keystone figure: the shaman's "wormhole in consciousness," the Net's "data foam" wormholes, Thorne's spacetime "handles," the museum as "a rift in space/time, a hole in space"; tied to Galloway & Rabinowitz's "Hole in Space" (1980).
- [[Hypercortex (Ascott)]] — the museum as brain, its hyperlinks "the cognitive nexus … generated in interspace." (Carries the museum-as-brain figure beside the concept [[The Mind of the Museum]].)
- [[Gardens of Hypotheses]] — "plant gardens of hypotheses and grow forests of metaphors," vs. "the tenements of reason, squares of certainty, and office blocks of determinism." (Concept + figure.)
- [[The Window vs the Doorway]] — recurrent Ascott figure, restated here: "The commanding metaphor of art shifts from that of a window onto the world to that of a doorway into negotiable (data) space."
- The museum as whorehouse / "maison close of metaphor" — nineteenth-century cultural edifices "soaked in semiosis," each "the greatest little whore-house in the world"; Genet's *The Balcony* "could as well have been set in a museum as in a bordello." (No standalone note; rendered plain text.)
- The butterfly / lepidopterist — "Unlike the butterfly of the museum lepidopterist, we shall not be pinned down… We fly from metaphor to metaphor." (Plain text.)
- Disneyland / Mickey Mouse — the theme park as "the definitive twentieth-century museum," "in a state of franchise-readiness to replay myth and history"; "the museum itself now aspires to the condition of a micro-theme park." (Plain text; no Disneyland note in the vault.)
- "A chip in my brain rather than a chip on my shoulder" — the moist/cyborg desire for direct neural-Net interchange, played as a pun. (Folded into [[Moistmedia]] / [[Cyberception]].)

## Major Tensions

- [[Telenoia vs Paranoia]] — the industrial "society of paranoia" (the unified self, secrecy, exclusivity) vs. the networked "society of connectivity … collaborative consciousness."
- [[Appearance vs Apparition]] — art "deals with apparition rather than appearance, initiating and managing the emergence of realities rather than the expression of a given reality." (See also the concept [[Apparition]].)
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the chapter's pervasive spine: "the materiality of art, in all its glorious and discrete objecthood" / "repositories of old pots and pans" vs. "an immaterial world, a scenario in constant flux"; "As the museum dematerialises, we might then see new metaphors of what it is to be human materialize all around us." (Ascott's museum/Net-gallery extension of the multi-author tension.)
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — "we are leaving behind embodiment as the primary measure and sign of reality" vs. the explicit refusal to "junk the body" and the call for "new metaphors of embodiment." (Cross-links Hayles; a productive convergence/disagreement.)
- Formation vs autopoiesis — museum education as control ("the euphemism 'training'") vs. Maturana & Varela's self-creation. (Folded into [[Autopoiesis]]; rendered plain text as a standalone tension.)
- Authoring vs authorizing — "art in the past … concerned with authoring (the one-way channelling of meaning) … museums … that of authorizing (a one-way system of validating and valorising)" vs. participatory Net art. (Maps to [[Reciprocal Communication]]; "The user is the content, the interface creates the context.")
