## Orientation

This short 1990 essay (first published in *New Observations*, no. 76, the special issue *Navigating the Telematic Sea* edited by Bruce Breland) is Ascott's most concentrated meditation on **time** in telematic culture, and the chapter where he most explicitly maps the new physics onto his theory of networked art. Refusing the "classical dialectic" of comparison, he proposes to *locate* time by a non-analytical, creative method of **triangulation**, bringing together three "threads of ideas" he abbreviates ESP, PDP, and PU. **ESP** points to *media* — at once Extra-Sensory Perception (modes of knowing "outside the Cartesian-Newtonian frame of being") and the initials of Edwin S. Porter (the filmic discovery that time can be "compressed, expanded, or reversed" by editing), a lineage running through D. W. Griffith's parallel editing to "the mosaic screen of digital discontinuity ... which we are now beginning to exploit through telematic media." **PDP** is *parallel data processing* / computer science — the computer as the machine that "works with time" (J. David Bolter's "Turing's man"), processing nanosecond pulses into information at speeds "outside human experience" and, networked globally, generating "a kind of schizo-time" and the conditions for a "world culture" and a "global consciousness." **PU** is *parallel universes* / the many-worlds interpretation of quantum physics, the strand Ascott says "resonate[s] most sympathetically with our current sensibilities," where "it is the observer who creates the reality of the act by observation."

The payoff is the chapter's keystone argument: **telematic art moves beyond both object art and time-based art, and rejects representation in favour of simulation.** Representation, Ascott argues, demands the art object, a fixed creator/observer separation, coherence in space and time, and a passive observing subject — it "always masks the inherent instability, the chaos, of process in the world." Telematic art instead simulates "the unstable, chaotic, fleeting, and numinous quality of its subject," substituting "negotiation, participation, collaboration" for assertion, and rendering "what is invisible visible." Against the "deconstructive pessimism of late postmodern art" he holds out hope for "a radically constructive art" of "parallel time-worlds." The user — "neither autonomous creator nor passive viewer" — moving through these worlds is "like Schrödinger's Cat, neither precisely here nor there, actual or virtual," and "like Heraclitus ... can never put our foot into the same river twice."

For the vault, this chapter is the primary anchor for the new [[Representation vs Simulation]] tension and the [[Telematic Time]] / [[Triangulation (Ascott)]] / [[The Computer as an Extra-Sensory Device]] cluster, and it strongly EXTENDS [[Telematic Art]], [[Network Consciousness]], [[Distributed Authorship]], [[The Participator]], and the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension (the object→system, representation→simulation spine). It cross-links to McLuhan's [[The Mosaic]] (digital discontinuity), to O'Gieblyn's [[The Measurement Problem]] and [[Re-enchantment]] (the new physics, the observer effect, the "seamless universal web of which ... only mystics and madmen would speak"), to Hayles/Haraway on the dispersed networked mind/body ([[Distributed Cognition]], [[Embodiment]], [[The Body as Data]]), and to the Burnham systems cluster ([[Object Art]], [[Object and System]]) at the "beyond object art" pole. The later Ascott coinage [[Cyberception]] is seeded here ("the computer is an extra-sensory device") but is named only in later chapters; it is forward-linked, not asserted.

## Key Arguments

- [[Telematic art rejects representation in favour of simulation]] — the keystone: representation presupposes the object, a fixed creator/observer separation, coherence, assertion, and passivity, and "masks the inherent instability, the chaos, of process"; simulation reproduces that chaotic, numinous process and substitutes negotiation/participation/collaboration for assertion.
- [[Telematic art moves beyond both object art and time-based art]] — networking no longer presents a discrete art object in fixed space-time, nor a linear sequence of moving images, nor a pre-ordered coherent space-time entity; dispersed intelligence and asynchronic interaction dissolve the "fixed point in a perceiving system." (Cross-links Burnham's [[Object Art]] / [[Object and System]].)
- The new physics supplies the language for telematic art practice — the many-worlds observer, parallel universes, and the observer effect "mirror" the telematic creative process, so "the models and even the terminology of quantum physics can be put to work in the development of new descriptions of art practice"; "the one field maps inspiringly onto the other." (No standalone note; carried by the citations and by [[The Measurement Problem]] / [[Telematic Time]].)
- The computer is an extra-sensory device that renders the invisible visible — the new physics' events are "totally invisible to our everyday perception"; the computer can simulate these patterns and so "glimpse the unseeable, to grasp chaos and the secret order in the disordered." (Carried by [[The Computer as an Extra-Sensory Device]]; forward-links [[Cyberception]].)
- Networked dispersed intelligence transforms linear time into schizo-time — linear time "implies a fixed point in a perceiving system, a self-standing intelligence"; when intelligence is dispersed, the perceiving system global, and interactions asynchronic, time "becomes something other." (Carried by [[Telematic Time]].)
- Telematic art is a radically constructive answer to deconstructive postmodern pessimism — against "the deconstructive pessimism of late postmodern art" / "deconstructivist postmodernism" Ascott projects "a radically constructive art" of "parallel time-worlds." (Connects to [[Postmodernist Art (Ascott)]].)

## Important Definitions

- **Telematics (Ascott, glossed here)** — "beyond its technical designation of the convergence of computer and telecommunications systems—effectively out-of-body, out-of mind ... asynchronous connectivity, whereby individuals can communicate (negotiate for meaning) through electronic networks." (Folds into [[Telematic Art]].)
- [[Triangulation (Ascott)]] — "to use the non-analytical creative process to locate it, by employing a form of triangulation. The three coordinates, threads of ideas ... ESP, PDP, and PU."
- [[Simulation (Ascott)]] — telematic art "rejects representation in favour of simulation," seeking to "simulate in its digital space-time the unstable, chaotic, fleeting, and numinous quality of its subject."
- **Representation (Ascott's antagonist sense)** — "Representation demands work that is determinate, coherent, and consistent. It produces an art of assertion ... in which the observing subject must be essentially passive." (The old order's mode; carried by [[Representation vs Simulation]].)
