# Source Summary: 3. The Mole and the Snake

**Book:** [[Psychopolitics]]
**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/03 The Mole and the Snake]]

## Orientation

This very short chapter (two pages) is Han's most direct reading of Gilles Deleuze's "Postscript on the Societies of Control," and it supplies, in compressed and almost diagrammatic form, the architecture on which the whole book turns. Han takes Deleuze's two zoomorphic figures — the burrowing **mole** of disciplinary society and the space-making **snake** of neoliberal control society — and uses them to stage the passage from a power that confines bodies to a power that modulates souls. The mole is the animal of the milieus of confinement (family, schools, prisons, barracks, hospitals, factories); it "moves within a closed system," is "a labourer," and is "a subjugated subject" whose restricted movement caps its productivity. The snake, by contrast, "does not move in closed spaces" but "makes space by means of its own movement"; it is "an entrepreneur," a "project" rather than a subject. This is the first appearance in the vault where Han names and cites Deleuze directly — the very provenance the [[Society of Control]] note (built from *The Transparency Society*) had to defer.

The chapter's load-bearing claim is that this passage from mole to snake — "from subject to project" — is not liberation or an exit from capitalism but "a mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same." The disciplinary mole "cannot exceed a certain level of productivity"; the snake eliminates that ceiling "through new forms of movement." The openness, borderlessness and self-steering that look like freedom are, in Han's reading, a productivity strategy: "the capitalist system is switching from the mole-model to the snake-model in order to generate more productivity." Apparent rupture, underlying continuity.

Han then maps Deleuze's contrast onto his own biopolitics/psychopolitics periodization. The disciplinary regime "organizes itself as a 'body'" and "is a biopolitical regime"; the neoliberal regime "seems like a 'soul'," and "psychopolitics is its form of government," operating through "Motivation, projects, competition, optimization and initiative" — "an inexorable rivalry presented as healthy competition . . . wonderful motivation." The chapter closes on the snake's signature instrument: guilt and debt (the German pun *die Schuld / die Schulden*), "instruments of domination" the neoliberal regime employs. This rhetorical posture is diagnostic and periodizing — Han borrows Deleuze's bestiary as a ready-made periodization and adds the neoliberal, psychopolitical, and debt-as-domination twist that makes it his own.

## Key Concepts

- [[Psychopolitics]] — the form of government proper to the neoliberal "soul," governing through motivation, competition, optimization and initiative rather than bodily confinement; the book's title concept, defined here by contrast with biopolitics.
- [[Biopolitics]] — the disciplinary regime that "organizes itself as a 'body'," whose animal is the mole; the superseded foil against which psychopolitics is defined.
- [[The Project (Han)]] — the snake as "project" rather than subject: the self that "creates space through the course it steers," the neoliberal successor to the subjugated subject.
- [[Society of Control]] — the post-disciplinary neoliberal regime "to which disciplinary society has yielded"; this chapter realizes its deferred Deleuze provenance and supplies the mole/snake image.
- [[Capital as Religion]] — the home of the *Schuld/Schulden* (guilt/debt) theme: the snake "embodies the guilt and debts" the neoliberal regime "employs as instruments of domination."

## Major Tensions

- [[Subject vs Project]] — the mole as "subjugated subject" versus the snake as "project," where the apparently freer pole is the more efficiently subjugated.
- [[Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics]] — the body-organizing disciplinary regime versus the soul-governing neoliberal regime.
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — closed milieus of confinement versus open, borderless, self-steering control.

## Candidate Essay Uses

- A compact engine for periodizing surveillance: the move from enclosure (the panopticon, the cell, the institution) to modulation (the open, networked, self-tracking subject), useful for framing the material/immaterial shift in surveillance and embodiment research.
- The "intensification, not liberation" thesis as a critique of techno-optimist freedom rhetoric: openness, flexibility, and the borderless "project self" read as productivity strategies rather than emancipation.
- The snake-as-entrepreneur/project as a lens on curatorial and artistic self-presentation, where "the project" is literally the unit of creative labour and self-branding.
- Debt and guilt (*Schuld/Schulden*) as an affective technology of control — pairing this chapter with [[Capital as Religion]] for a reading of indebtedness as a soul-binding, rather than body-confining, instrument of domination.
- The body/soul figure (biopolitics → psychopolitics) as a bridge between Foucauldian disciplinary critique and Han's affective/psychological account of power.
