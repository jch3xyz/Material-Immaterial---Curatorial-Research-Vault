# Source Summary: TEN Wings

**Book:** [[Telematic Embrace]]
**Author:** [[Roy Ascott]]
**Raw file:** [[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/14 TEN Wings]]

## Orientation

This very short chapter (source pages 184–185, dated 1982) is not an essay but the surviving text of an actual artwork: Ascott's contribution to Robert Adrian's "The World in 24 Hours," a networked telecommunications event staged at the Ars Electronica exhibition in Linz, Austria, and run over the Sharp APL / ARTBOX electronic-mail timesharing service. The chapter consists of an editorial one-line gloss followed by the (heavily OCR-corrupted, ALL-CAPS) operating *instructions* for the work — a score or protocol rather than a discursive argument. As such it is the collection's most concrete artifact of [[Telematic Art]]: where Ascott's other essays *theorize* art whose medium is computer-mediated telecommunications, TEN Wings *enacts* it on a real 1982 network.

The work is a distributed I Ching divination engine run across a telephone network. ("The Ten Wings" — *Shih I* — is the traditional name for the ten classical commentaries appended to the *I Ching*.) Remote participants are directed to throw coins, transmit the numeric totals via ARTBOX to Ascott (network address `<ASCOT>`), and receive back — by the same process of divination — a generated "JUDGEMENT, A COMMENTARY AND AN IMAGE 'IN TEXT'," which is displayed at the Ars Electronica Center. All participants orient their attention toward a single shared "WORLD QUESTION," then mail in five images responding to the resulting text within twenty-four hours, to be collated and documented back to the whole network. The send→process→return→respond loop makes the piece a literal instance of [[Reciprocal Communication]]: interactive and bidirectional, not broadcast.

Within the book's arc the chapter matters as the earliest concrete instantiation of the telematic thesis and as a bridge between Ascott's 1960s cybernetic/[[Behaviourist Art]] roots and his later [[Technoetics]]. The artwork has essentially no material object — only the immaterial distributed process, its data transmissions, and ephemeral mailed cards — making it a clean telematic-side instance of [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Object vs System]]. The fusion of an ancient chance-oracle with a deterministic digital network prefigures the divination/altered-states strand Ascott would later name [[The Shamantic Web]]; the text closes on the machine handshake `MSG ACCEPTED: ASCOT 33`, foregrounding the network protocol itself as part of the work. (The shamantic/technoetic reading is interpretive: Ascott uses none of that vocabulary in this 1982 chapter.)

## Key Concepts

- [[Telematic Art]] — art whose medium is computer-mediated telecommunications networks; here demonstrated as an actual distributed, participatory networked event rather than theorized.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the convergence of geographically dispersed minds into a shared field via the network; literalized as many participants all holding one common "WORLD QUESTION" in attention.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the send/process/return/respond cycle: totals out, a divined judgement/commentary/image back, images then mailed in and re-documented to all. (Burnham-anchored; this chapter extends it to the telematic case.)
- [[Behaviourist Art]] — the artwork delivered as behavioural instructions (throw, transmit, receive, collate, mail) executed by participants who *complete* the work; continuity with Ascott's 1960s cybernetic-art thesis.
- [[New Media Art]] — the broad field of art made in and with electronic/computational media; TEN Wings is an exemplary early-1980s networked, electronic-mail artwork. (Burnham-realized; extended here.)
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the participant experiences and executes a technological procedure; the work is an experiential process, not an object. (Burnham-realized; extended here.)
- [[The Shamantic Web]] — Ascott's later coinage for the fusion of oracular/altered-state practice with electronic networks; *prefigured* here by routing the I Ching oracle through a computer system. (Interpretive antecedent.)
