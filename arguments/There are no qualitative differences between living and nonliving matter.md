# There are no qualitative differences between living and nonliving matter

## Claim

Burnham reports the consensus among scientists in bio-electronics that there are no qualitative physical differences between living and nonliving matter — both are simply points on an ascending scale of complexity in the organization of matter — and draws the consequence that artificially created organisms may therefore possess consciousness. *(Directly stated as a report)*

## Expanded Explanation

The argument is one Burnham relays rather than originates: he attributes it to "scientists working in the field of bio-electronics," for whom the living/nonliving boundary is not a difference of kind but only of organizational complexity. On this view life is not a separate substance or animating force superadded to matter, but a region of a single continuum — "an ascending scale of complexity in the organization of matter." The immediate inference Burnham extracts is that consciousness is not the proprietary endowment of biological organisms: "organisms artificially created may possess consciousness." *(Directly stated)*

Burnham qualifies the consequence in the same breath: an artificial organism's "level of subjectivity and intelligence depends upon their creator's ability to simulate or improvise upon biological principles." Consciousness, subjectivity, and intelligence are thus treated as achievable design targets — matters of how well the maker reorganizes matter — rather than as thresholds that nonliving matter is in principle barred from crossing. This is the physicalist counterpart, at the level of the whole organism, of the chapter's report (via the cybernetician researchers and via Latil/Condillac) that "intelligence is purely a matter of degree and that thought in a meaningful sense exists on a continuum of levels, even in some rather uncomplicated machines." *(Directly stated)*

The position is the explicit negation of the vitalist objection Burnham canvasses a few pages earlier — the "view tinged with the vitalist hope that a few life-centered properties remain out of the reach of the scientist to recreate." If there are no qualitative differences between living and nonliving matter, then nothing about life is in principle off-limits to synthesis, and the question of machine consciousness becomes (as James Culberson's physicalistic theory proposes) one of building the right physical state rather than of capturing an irreducible spiritual property. The premise also runs underneath the whole book: in the Introduction Burnham names "the rise of the conviction that physicochemical conditions rather than spiritual energies are responsible for the origination of biological life" as one of the factors dissolving sculpture's base. *(Directly stated / Strongly implied)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. Scientists in bio-electronics generally acknowledge that there are no qualitative physical differences between living and nonliving matter. *(Directly stated, as a report)*
2. Living and nonliving matter are, instead, two groupings on a single ascending scale of complexity in the organization of matter. *(Directly stated)*
3. Therefore the organization of matter that produces life — and the higher organization that produces consciousness — is, in principle, reproducible by other means. *(Strongly implied)*
4. Therefore organisms artificially created may possess consciousness. *(Directly stated)*
5. The level of subjectivity and intelligence such an organism attains is set by its creator's ability to simulate or improvise upon biological principles — i.e. consciousness is a matter of organizational degree, not of kind. *(Directly stated)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham reports the claim as a consensus *in a field* ("it is generally acknowledged by scientists working in the field of bio-electronics"); it is an attributed report, not his own demonstrated thesis, and the inference to artificial consciousness is hedged ("may possess"). *(Directly stated)*
- The vitalist counterposition is given standing in the same chapter: "there are large numbers of educated people-scientists included-who see no possibility, ever, for a machine to approach a true state of consciousness." *(Directly stated)*
- Culberson's physicalist theory is explicitly unproven in practice: "It remains to be proven by the construction of physical models whether Culberson's or anyone else's theory of consciousness has any basis in practice." The continuum/degree view of intelligence is likewise contested — "many artificial intelligence researchers have since raised serious doubts about the real thinking ability of the tortoises." *(Directly stated)*
- Burnham notes that the question is partly definitional rather than factual — "the problem becomes one of defining what attributes one will accept as being consistent with true mental processes" — so "no qualitative difference" can be read as a stipulation about where to draw the line as much as a discovery. *(Directly stated)*
- The leap from "no qualitative difference in matter" to "artificial organisms may possess consciousness" presupposes that consciousness supervenes wholly on organizational complexity — the very point the vitalist denies and that Burnham concedes is unsettled (he calls a conscious work of art "an incredible but not unlikely eventuality"). *(Strongly implied)*
