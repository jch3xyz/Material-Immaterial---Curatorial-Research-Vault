# Technics is self-augmenting and only madness escapes the machine

## Claim

Following Ellul and Mumford, Burnham argues that technology self-augments — it breeds more technology and erodes our capacity to resist it — while simultaneously replacing traditional symbolism and reducing every art form to technique; therefore an art of machines is internally contradictory, and the artist's only remaining refuge from technological proliferation is madness or irrationality (the Dada strain in modern art).

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham draws the argument from Jacques Ellul's *The Technological Society*, glossed through Lewis Mumford's term "technics." The core property is **self-augmentation**: "there is no such thing as introducing 'a little technology.'" Past a threshold, technology becomes self-propelling — more scientists, more apparatus, more method — and in the same motion our reluctance to accept its "fruits and methodology" grows "fainter and fainter each passing decade." This is the technological analogue of an autonomous system: it expands by its own logic, not by human choice.

The aesthetic consequence is severe. Ellul, by Burnham's report, doubts "whether any art as we know it can stem from the ultra-rationality of technics," because technical apparatus tends to "replace traditional symbolism in the human mind." If art lives by symbol and irrationality, and technics dissolves both, then a Kinetic Art built *of* machines courts its own annihilation — hence the "internal contradictions" Burnham sees the Kinetic revival maintaining. The argument is the dark twin of [[Art vs Technics]]: not merely that art and technics differ, but that technics actively consumes the irrational ground art stands on.

This leaves one exit. Burnham relays Ellul's conclusion that "the artist finds only one hiding place from technological proliferation": madness. "Only madness is inaccessible to the machine. Every other 'art' form can be reduced to technique." The Dada lineage — Duchamp, Picabia, Klee, de Chirico, who "mixed extremes of deranged fantasy with the materiality of technology" — becomes, on this reading, "a breath of sanity": deliberate irrationality is the last region [[Technics]] cannot colonize and reduce to method.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Technique/technics possesses the property of self-augmentation: beyond a certain plateau, technology breeds technology, and human resistance to it weakens with each decade (Ellul via Mumford).
2. Technical apparatus replaces traditional symbolism in the human mind and proceeds by an ultra-rationality alien to art.
3. Every art form can be *reduced to technique* — absorbed into the rational, augmenting machinery — so an art made of machines turns against itself (internal contradiction).
4. Only madness is inaccessible to the machine; irrationality is the single faculty technics cannot reduce to method.
5. Therefore the artist's only refuge from technological proliferation is madness/irrationality, of which Dada is the exemplary modern instance.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself records the dissenting view: artists like Schaffer "see no incompatibility between art and advanced technology," holding that "the artist has always used what technical facilities were at hand and that it is nonsense to interpret the new technology as anti-artistic."
- A second internal counter: art may be "a subtle balance between irrational and rational human characteristics," so technics need not abolish art but only shift the balance — though Burnham notes technics tends either to undermine irrationality or to force it "into a position where it must manifest itself in stronger and more antithetical terms."
- The argument is borrowed: it is Ellul's thesis as relayed and applied by Burnham to Kinetic sculpture, not Burnham's own first-principles claim, and rests on Ellul's contested premise that technics is autonomous and self-augmenting.
