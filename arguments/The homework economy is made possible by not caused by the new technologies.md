# The homework economy is made possible by not caused by the new technologies

## Claim

The [[The Homework Economy|homework economy]] is "made possible by (not caused by) the new technologies": communications technology is the enabling infrastructure through which capital integrates and controls dispersed labor, not the determining cause of the restructuring.

## Expanded Explanation

In "The Homework Economy 'Outside the Home'" (ch. 4 of *A Cyborg Manifesto*), [[Donna Haraway]] makes a deliberately precise causal claim about the relation between technology and the worldwide restructuring of labor she calls the homework economy: "The homework economy as a world capitalist organizational structure is made possible by (not caused by) the new technologies." `Directly stated`. The parenthetical "(not caused by)" is the load-bearing phrase. Haraway refuses **technological determinism** — the position that the new communications technologies themselves *produce* the feminization of work, the loss of unionized jobs, and the bimodal society. The cause is a "world capitalist organizational structure"; the technologies are its condition of possibility, its enabling medium, not its motor.

The agent in Haraway's account is power exercised *through* the technologies, not the technologies as such. The crucial sentence locates the decisive capacity in use: "The success of the attack on relatively privileged, mostly white, men's unionized jobs is tied to the power of the new communications technologies to integrate and control labor despite extensive dispersion and decentralization." `Directly stated`. What the technologies supply is a *capacity* — to coordinate, integrate, and discipline a labor force that has been scattered and decentralized — and it is the deployment of that capacity by multinational capital that "made possible" the dissolution of privileged unionized work. The technologies do not decide to attack those jobs; they make the attack feasible at scale, "despite extensive dispersion and decentralization," which a less-instrumented capital could not have managed.

This is a methodological commitment, not merely a turn of phrase. Reading technology as enabling-but-not-causal lets Haraway hold two things at once: that the new communications technologies are genuinely consequential (the homework economy is unthinkable without them), and that their consequences are political and economic choices rather than technical destiny. The same refusal of determinism animates the wider manifesto's insistence that the cyborg is a site of contested politics rather than a fated outcome — technology is the ground of struggle, not its verdict. `Interpretive synthesis` (the connection to the manifesto's broader anti-determinism is the vault's; the made-possible/not-caused distinction is Haraway's own).

## Reasoning Structure

1. The homework economy is "a world capitalist organizational structure" — i.e., its cause is a mode of capitalist organization, not a machine.
2. The new technologies are "made possible by (not caused by)" that structure: they are enabling infrastructure, the condition under which the restructuring can be carried out, not its determining cause.
3. The specific historical achievement — the "successful attack" on "relatively privileged, mostly white, men's unionized jobs" — is "tied to the power of the new communications technologies to integrate and control labor despite extensive dispersion and decentralization."
4. Therefore the technologies' role is to confer a *capacity* (integration and control of dispersed labor) that capital then uses; the politics lies in the use, not in the tools.
5. Conclusion: causal responsibility belongs to the capitalist organizational structure and its agents; the technologies enable but do not determine.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The defining statement: "The homework economy as a world capitalist organizational structure is made possible by (not caused by) the new technologies." (ch. 4)
- The mechanism of enablement: the technologies' "power ... to integrate and control labor despite extensive dispersion and decentralization," which made the attack on white men's unionized jobs successful. (ch. 4)
- The downstream effects felt by women — "loss of the family (male) wage" and the capital-intensification of "their own jobs, which are becoming capital-intensive—for example, office work and nursing" — presented as *consequences of* technological-economic arrangements, not as technologically inevitable. (ch. 4)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The made-possible/caused distinction can blur in practice: if a restructuring is impossible without a given technology, critics may argue the line between "enabling condition" and "cause" is rhetorical rather than analytic. Haraway asserts the distinction but does not give a developed criterion for it.
- The claim is a 1985 diagnosis of the early "New Industrial Revolution"; its specific objects (electronics assembly, the family wage, early telecommuting) are dated, even if the anti-determinist *form* of the argument has aged well.
- Haraway names "power" as the agent without fully specifying whose decisions, in which institutions, exercise it — the structure is named (multinational capital) but the causal chain from boardroom to deskilled worker is gestured at rather than traced.

## Tensions

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the technologies confer freedom-as-dispersion (decentralized, flexible, "homework") that is simultaneously a new capacity for control "despite extensive dispersion and decentralization"; the same infrastructure that scatters labor re-integrates and disciplines it.
