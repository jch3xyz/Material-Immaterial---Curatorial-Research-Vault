# The computer metaphor has not solved the mind-body problem only transferred it to a new substrate

## Claim

Computational theories of mind are widely taken to have proved that the brain is a computer and to have explained the functions of consciousness; but for Meghan O'Gieblyn the analogy demonstrates nothing of the kind — all it shows, in Seymour Papert's phrase, is that the problems that long stumped philosophers and theologians "come up in equivalent form in the new context." The [[Mind as Machine|computer metaphor]] has not *solved* the [[The Mind-Body Problem|mind-body problem]]; it has only *re-housed* it, transferring the existential impasse of consciousness to a new substrate without resolving it.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the closing thesis of Chapter 2. O'Gieblyn's target is a popular triumphalism: "Many people today believe that computational theories of mind have proved that the brain is a computer or have explained the functions of consciousness." Her counter is deflationary and precise. The analogy is real and even useful, but its success is misread. What it actually exhibits is the *survival* of the old questions under new vocabulary — not their dissolution. To translate the soul into software, cognition into algorithm, and the brain into hardware is not to answer how matter gives rise to mind; it is to restate the question in machine idiom.

The argument lands because O'Gieblyn has spent the chapter showing the metaphor failing in exactly this way. The computer figure "was invented to get around the metaphysical notion of a soul and the long, inelegant history of mind-body dualism," yet "the soul keeps slipping back in." The "popular notion that the mind is software running on the brain's hardware is itself a form of dualism," reiterating "the Cartesian assumption that the mind is something above and beyond the physical." Hilary Putnam celebrated the mind-as-software picture as having one's cake and eating it too — "an autonomous mental life" with "no mysteries, no ghostly agents, no élan vital." But an autonomous mental life *above* the physical substrate is precisely [[René Descartes|Descartes]]'s *res cogitans* in new clothing. The metaphor does not escape the dualist inheritance; it launders it.

So when Papert's line arrives, it functions as the chapter's verdict. The impasse O'Gieblyn names is not a transient gap in knowledge but a structural feature: science "put a bracket around consciousness because it was too difficult to study objectively, but this methodological avoidance eventually led to metaphysical denial." Whatever the era's apex machine — mill, clock, telegraph, computer — the metaphor inherits the same bracketed remainder, subjective experience, and carries it forward intact. "So long as this is the case, our metaphors, no matter how modern or inventive, will continue to reiterate this central impasse of science." The substrate changes; the problem is conserved.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The computer metaphor was introduced to dissolve mind-body dualism by recasting mind as information processing — software on neural hardware — apparently compatible with physicalism. (See [[Mind as Machine]].)
2. But the metaphor covertly re-inscribes the very split it claims to abolish: mind-as-software is "itself a form of dualism," reiterating the Cartesian premise that the mind is "above and beyond the physical." "The soul keeps slipping back in." (See [[The Mind-Body Problem]], [[René Descartes]].)
3. Therefore the metaphor does not *resolve* the dilemma of how an immaterial mind relates to a material body; it restates it in computational vocabulary.
4. Generalizing: because each age's mind-as-machine figure inherits the same bracketed remainder — first-person experience science cannot quantify — "our metaphors, no matter how modern or inventive, will continue to reiterate this central impasse."
5. Conclusion (via Papert): the analogy demonstrates only that the old problems "come up in equivalent form in the new context." "The metaphor has not solved our most pressing existential problems; it has merely transferred them to a new substrate."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The physicalist rejoinder.** Proponents insist the metaphor *is* compatible with physicalism — "the mind cannot exist without the brain, so it is ultimately connected to and instantiated by something physical." On this view the figure does dissolve dualism and Papert's worry is overstated. O'Gieblyn's reply is that the metaphor is "arguably appealing *because*" it nonetheless reiterates the Cartesian premise that the mind is "something above and beyond the physical" — but this turns on a reading of its appeal, not a demonstration that physicalist versions must fail.
- **It is a borrowed verdict.** The thesis rests on a single attributed remark from Papert and an interpretive gloss; O'Gieblyn endorses rather than independently proves that *no* computational account could resolve the problem. The chapter establishes that the dominant popular version re-inscribes dualism, not that every possible one must.
- **The eliminativist exit.** A determined denier (Dennett's "Great Denial") accepts the metaphor *and* dissolves the problem by denying that interior experience exists at all — "there is no 'hard problem' because that which the problem is trying to explain ... is not real." This is not a solution O'Gieblyn accepts, but it is a route by which the "impasse" could be declared void rather than merely transferred; she treats it instead as one more way modernity remains "captive ... to these seventeenth-century assumptions."
- **Risk of unfalsifiability.** If every new metaphor is read in advance as merely "transferring" the impasse, the thesis can absorb any technical advance as further confirmation, which weakens its force as a prediction about what computational science could in principle achieve.

## Related Arguments

- [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]] — Hayles's genealogy supplies the historical mechanism O'Gieblyn presupposes: information had to be stripped of meaning and matter before mind could be recast as substrate-independent pattern. The "new substrate" O'Gieblyn says the problem was transferred *to* is the one Hayles shows was constructed, not discovered.
- [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]] — the embodiment counter-tradition explains *why* the transfer fails to dissolve anything: if mind is inseparable from its material instantiation, no change of substrate can resolve a problem that arose from bracketing the body in the first place.
- [[The strong claim of artificial life is constructed through narrative]] — a parallel deflation: a computational program is taken to have *demonstrated* something about life or mind when it has only restaged the claim in a new medium.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the argument is a move within this tension: the computer metaphor promises to collapse the immaterial mind into material computation, but O'Gieblyn shows it instead preserves an immaterial remainder ("the soul keeps slipping back in") on a new material substrate.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the "new substrate" is the informational pattern that supersedes flesh; the claim that the impasse survives the transfer is the claim that recasting mind as information does not escape the body it brackets.
