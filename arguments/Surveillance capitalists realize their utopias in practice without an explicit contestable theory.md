# Surveillance capitalists realize their utopias in practice without an explicit contestable theory

## Claim

Surveillance capitalists reverse the normal sequence of theory and practice: where utopian thought has historically published a contestable theory that others later applied, they seize the world in practice at high velocity while keeping their theory thin and secret, so the critical inquiry that a published theory invites never gets its purchase.

## Expanded Explanation

In Zuboff's account, the normal modern utopian operates as a theorist first. The most ambitious utopianists — culminating in Marx — articulate a thickly worked-out theory that is, by virtue of being articulated, available to be inspected, debated, and refused. The gap between theory and its eventual implementation is precisely the space in which critique can do its work: one can read the theory, weigh its claims, and organize against them before they are made real. [[Surveillance Capitalism]] inverts this order. Its leaders — Nadella, Page, Zuckerberg — fit five of the six elements of the classic utopian profile, but they are *sui generis* utopianists because they do not need a Lenin, Stalin, or Mao to apply their ideas to real life. They seize the world in practice. Their theories are thin, at least in what they share with the public, while their power is monumental and largely unimpeded.

The mechanism that holds this gap closed is velocity plus opacity. Practices move ahead faster than understanding can form around them, in displays of [[Instrumentarian Power]]'s "shock and awe" that leave onlookers dazed, uncertain, and helpless. Because there is no clear articulation of the theory, the rest of us are left only with its practical effects — the engine that shuts off, the route that arrives with the destination, the purchase that flashes the moment endorphins peak, [[Big Other]]'s continuous tracking. We are forced to reverse-engineer the project from its symptoms, always after the fact and never in time to contest it.

This is why Zuboff insists the utopian rhetoric of a "magical age" is not harmless hype to be waved off as "techno utopianism." It plays a strategic role: it is the minesweeper that precedes the foot soldiers and the canny diplomat sent ahead to disarm the enemy and smooth the way for a quiet surrender. The promise of the magical age simultaneously distracts from and legitimates the totalistic ambitions of surveillance capitalism — the drive of [[Big Other]] toward totality that deletes every source of friction and authority. The rhetoric is the soft edge of the practice-first strategy, doing the work that an exposed, contestable theory would make impossible.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Normally theory precedes practice and is published, so it can be inspected, debated, and refused; the gap between theory and implementation is the habitat of critical inquiry.
2. Marx exemplifies the normal case: a thickly articulated theory that, lacking power of its own, required Lenin, Stalin, and Mao — "applied utopistics" — to implement.
3. Surveillance capitalists reverse this sequence: they seize the world in practice, their public theory is thin, their power monumental and unimpeded.
4. They keep the gap closed through high velocity and shock-and-awe, leaving onlookers too dazed to form the understanding that contestation requires.
5. The "magical age" rhetoric is therefore not naive hype but a strategic minesweeper that distracts from and legitimates totalistic ambitions while the thin theory stays out of reach.
6. Conclusion: because the contestable theory is withheld and the practice outruns comprehension, the space for democratic critique is foreclosed by design rather than by accident.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Zuboff herself anticipates the dismissive reading she rejects: the magical-age talk "is usually dismissed as mere 'techno utopianism'" and so as harmless — a reading that, if correct, would deflate the argument that the rhetoric is a deliberate strategic weapon. Her counter is that the discourse "is no mere hogwash" but serves a critical strategic role.
- The "thin theory" claim is qualified to what the capitalists "share with the public"; the argument turns on secrecy of theory, not its absence, which leaves open the interpretive question of how thin the internal theory actually is.
- The reversal is itself a theoretical reconstruction by Zuboff: the actors do not declare that they are reversing theory and practice, so the claim is an interpretation of their conduct rather than a stated program — though it rests on their own publicized practices and utopian rhetoric.
