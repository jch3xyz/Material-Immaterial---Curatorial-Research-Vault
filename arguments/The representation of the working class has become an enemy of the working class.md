# The representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class

## Claim

In taking over the proletariat's power to speak and act for it, the party, union, and workers' state externalized and alienated that power against the class itself — so that "the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class" (th.100).

## Expanded Explanation

This is the keystone historical thesis of Chapter 4 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*, the point at which [[Guy Debord]]'s theory of the [[Spectacle]] is grounded in the actual history of the workers' movement. Debord argues that when a class delegates its power to a body that stands in for it — a social-democratic party, a Bolshevik vanguard, a union bureaucracy, a workers' state — the same logic of [[Separation]] that governs the spectacle in general returns inside the revolutionary movement. The class's own living power is split off, autonomized, and returned as an alien order that now confronts it. The representative that was supposed to *carry* the proletariat's project becomes its opposite. Debord dates this inversion precisely: "The historical moment when Bolshevism triumphed for itself in Russia and social democracy fought victoriously for the old world marks the inauguration of the state of affairs that is at the heart of the modern spectacle's domination: the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class" (th.100). (Directly stated.)

The thesis has two poles, one for each wing of the twentieth-century workers' movement, and Debord condemns both. In Germany, social democracy fought "victoriously for the old world": the SPD leadership, "when the overwhelming majority of the party rallied to the im perialist war," and then, "following the German defeat, when the party crushed the Spartakist revolutionaries" (th.97). Debord cites Rosa Luxemburg's *Die Rote Fahne* of December 21, 1918 — "the troops protecting the old order are not fighting under the insignia of the ruling class, but under the banner of a 'social-democratic party'" — to name the moment the radical German proletariat "discovered the secret" that "the revolutionary representation of the prole tariat had at this stage become both the primary cause and the central result of the general falsification of society" (th.101). In Russia, Bolshevism "triumphed for itself": "By seizing a state monopoly as sole representative and defender of working-class power, the Bolshevik Party justified itself and became what it already was: the party of the owners of the proletariat, a party ownership that essentially eliminated earlier forms of property" (th.102). The proletarian power of the soviets could not maintain itself "against its own representation which had become externalized and alienated in the form of a working-class party that maintained total control over the state, the economy, the means of expression, and soon even over people's thoughts" (th.103). (Directly stated.)

The inversion is completed by Stalinism, which for Debord is not a betrayal of Bolshevik representation but its logical extension. The bureaucracy consolidates "its power as sole owner of a system of state capitalism" (th.104), and its members "have to play the role of the proletariat governing a socialist society; they have to be actors faithful to a script of ideological betrayal" (th.107) — a class whose ownership "has to be masked because it is based on false consciousness." The party form is thus not a neutral tool that Stalin corrupted; it is, as Debord frames the whole chapter, the *falsification of the class itself*. Representation here is not merely a picture standing in for a reality but a delegation of power that turns against its principal — which is why this argument extends [[Representation]] from the register of images into the register of political delegation, and why the answer Debord names in the same chapter is a refusal of representation as such: a genuine revolutionary organization "cannot claim to represent the working class" but must "embody a radical separation from the world of separation" (th.119). (Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The spectacle is a social order founded on [[Separation]]: the living powers of human beings are split off from them and returned as an autonomous order that confronts them ([[Spectacle]], th.2, th.25).
2. When a class delegates its power to a representative body (party, union, state), that same separation operates *inside* the movement: the class's power is externalized and alienated into the representative ("externalized and alienated in the form of a working-class party," th.103).
3. Social democracy realized this by fighting "for the old world" — rallying to the imperialist war and crushing the Spartakists (th.97, th.101); Bolshevism realized it by "seizing a state monopoly as sole representative" and becoming "the party of the owners of the proletariat" (th.102).
4. Stalinism completes the inversion: the bureaucracy becomes the "sole owner of a system of state capitalism" whose members merely "play the role of the proletariat" (th.104, th.107).
5. Therefore the representation of the class is not a servant of the class but its enemy: "the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class" (th.100) — the very "inauguration of the state of affairs that is at the heart of the modern spectacle's domination."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The historical dating of the inversion to the twin events of 1917–1919: Bolshevik triumph in Russia and social-democratic victory "for the old world" (th.100) — [[raw/Debord - The Society of the Spectacle/07 Ch4 - The Proletariat as Subject and Representation]].
- The German case: the SPD's rally to the imperialist war and the crushing of "the Spartakist revolutionaries" (th.97); Ebert's admission that he hated revolution "like sin"; his summary of the new alienation as "Socialism means working a lot."
- Rosa Luxemburg's *Die Rote Fahne*, December 21, 1918 — the old order fighting "under the banner of a 'social-democratic party'" (th.101), read by Debord as the radical proletariat discovering "the general falsification of society."
- The Russian case: the Bolshevik Party "seizing a state monopoly as sole representative" and becoming "the party of the owners of the proletariat" (th.102); the soviets defeated "against its own representation which had become externalized and alienated in the form of a working-class party" (th.103); Lenin's ultimatum at the Tenth Congress as Kronstadt "was being crushed by arms."
- The Stalinist completion: the bureaucracy as "sole owner of a system of state capitalism" (th.104); bureaucrats forced "to play the role of the proletariat governing a socialist society" (th.107).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The thesis presupposes that the proletariat is a genuine historical *subject* whose power can be alienated — the other half of the chapter's title ("The Proletariat as Subject and Representation"). A reader who denies that unified class-subject would read the "enemy" not as an inversion of a real power but as the reification of a fiction.
- Debord's condemnation is total: it treats *all* delegated representation of the class as tending toward this inversion, which leaves little room to distinguish a merely reformist party from a counter-revolutionary one except by outcome. His positive alternative — the workers' council / "radical separation from the world of separation" (th.119) — is asserted more than demonstrated in this chapter.
- The reading of Stalinism as the logical completion of Leninist representation, rather than its perversion, is a strong interpretive claim contested by the Trotskyist and Leninist traditions Debord is explicitly writing against ("the neo-Leninist illusion carried on by present-day Trotskyism," th.111).

## Tensions

- [[Unity vs Separation]] — the argument is the political instance of Debord's master opposition: the party that promises the *unity* of the class in fact institutionalizes its *separation* from its own power.
- [[Reality vs Appearance]] — delegated representation is the appearance that displaces the reality it claims to embody; the class becomes "an immense accumulation" of its own representations, standing over against it (the register [[Representation]] extends from images to political delegation).
