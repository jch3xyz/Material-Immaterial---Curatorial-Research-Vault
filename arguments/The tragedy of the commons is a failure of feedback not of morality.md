# The tragedy of the commons is a failure of feedback not of morality

## Claim

The tragedy of the commons happens not because its users are greedy or wicked but because the structure severs the feedback link between the condition of the shared resource and the decisions of the people using it — so the remedy is to rebuild that missing link, not to appeal to conscience.

## Expanded Explanation

Meadows takes over Garrett Hardin's 1968 parable of the open pasture and re-reads it as a piece of systems architecture rather than a morality play. Each herdsman "receives all the proceeds from the sale of the additional animal," while "the effects of overgrazing are shared by all," so the private benefit is undiluted and the cost is diffused across everyone. On that structure the herdsman's conclusion is not vice but arithmetic: "The rational herdsman concludes that the only sensible course for him to pursue is to add another animal to his herd. And another; and another." Meadows glosses the whole scene in three words — "[[Bounded Rationality|Bounded rationality]] in a nutshell!" The ruin is reached "by each and every rational herdsman," which is precisely why blaming the herdsmen misses the mechanism. `Directly stated`

What makes the trap a trap is a structural fact about information, not about character. A commons "needs users of the resource... which increase at a rate that is not influenced by the condition of the commons." The individual "has no reason, no incentive, no strong feedback, to let the possibility of overgrazing stop him from adding another cow." From this Meadows draws the load-bearing sentence of the whole section: "The tragedy of the commons arises from missing (or too long delayed) feedback from the resource to the growth of the users of that resource." Because "there is no feedback to the user, overharvesting will continue," until the erosion loop — "[a]nother reinforcing feedback loop running downhill" — destroys the resource. The pollution case is symmetric: a firm "reaps a large gain, while itself having to live with only a fraction of its own pollution," so "[t]here is no rational reason why a polluter should desist," and "the feedback influencing the rate of use of the common resource—whether it is a source or a sink—is weak." `Directly stated`

The decisive move — and the reason this is an argument about feedback rather than about morality — is Meadows's reading of the three remedies. She lists exhortation first: "Appeal to their morality. Persuade them to be temperate. Threaten transgressors with social disapproval or eternal hellfire." This is the *moral* solution, and it merely "tries to keep use of the commons low enough through moral pressure." The two solutions that actually work do so by re-wiring the diagram. "The second, privatization, makes a direct feedback link from the condition of the resource to those who use it, by making sure that gains and losses fall on the same decision maker." "The third solution, regulation, makes an indirect feedback link from the condition of the resource through regulators to users." Privatization and Hardin's "mutual coercion, mutually agreed upon" look like opposite politics, yet Meadows shows they are the same structural intervention performed by different means — restoring the [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] from resource-condition to user-decision. Her everyday catalogue of the fix — the electric meter moved to the front hall, traffic lights, bank statements, fishing permits, garbage-by-weight fees, carbon taxes — are all, at bottom, feedback links reconnected. `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. In a commons, the private benefit of using the resource falls wholly on the individual while the cost of depletion is shared across all users.
2. Given that asymmetry, adding another unit of use is locally rational for every user — this is [[Bounded Rationality|bounded rationality]], not immorality; the ruin is reached "by each and every rational herdsman."
3. Users therefore increase their use "at a rate that is not influenced by the condition of the commons": the feedback link from resource-condition to user-decision is missing, weak, or too long delayed.
4. Because the corrective signal never reaches the decision, use runs past carrying capacity and the erodable resource collapses — a [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop|reinforcing loop]] "running downhill."
5. Moral exhortation does not touch the missing link, so it cannot durably solve the trap; only privatization (a *direct* feedback link) and regulation (an *indirect* one) reconnect condition-of-resource to decisions-of-user.
6. Therefore the tragedy is a failure of feedback, and its cure is structural: restore the missing link, do not reform the character of the users.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Hardin's grazing-commons parable, quoted at length: the +1 private gain versus the shared fraction-of-−1 cost, and the herdsman who is "locked into a system that compels him to increase his herd without limit—in a world that is limited" ([[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/09 Chapter 5 - System Traps and Opportunities]]).
- The common-sink (pollution) variant, showing the same feedback-failure independent of the source/sink distinction: the polluter who "reaps a large gain" and has "no rational reason... to desist."
- The three-remedy taxonomy — exhortation vs. privatization vs. regulation — read explicitly as "no feedback link" (moral appeal) vs. "a direct feedback link" (privatization) vs. "an indirect feedback link" (regulation).
- Real-world commons in collapse offered as instances: the overcrowded national park, fossil-fuel use and greenhouse CO₂, and family size against socially borne costs (Hardin's originating case).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Regulation only works if "the regulators must have the expertise to monitor and interpret correctly the condition of the commons, they must have effective means of deterrence, and they must have the good of the whole community at" heart — the indirect feedback link can itself fail, so restoring feedback is necessary but not automatically sufficient.
- Privatization presupposes a resource that *can* be divided so that "gains and losses fall on the same decision maker"; many commons (the atmosphere, oceans, climate) resist clean partition, which is why Meadows does not treat privatization as universal.
- The argument brackets rather than denies moral responsibility: Meadows notes elsewhere that a structural diagnosis "does not provide an excuse for narrow-minded behavior" — locating the fault in feedback explains the behavior without exonerating it.
- Elinor Ostrom's later work on self-governed commons suggests communities can sometimes generate their own feedback and norms without either privatization or top-down regulation — a fourth path Meadows's three-way taxonomy does not foreground.
