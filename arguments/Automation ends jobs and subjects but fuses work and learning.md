# Automation ends jobs and subjects but fuses work and learning

## Claim

Because automation is information, it ends both the "job" (the specialist slot of the world of work) and the "subject" (the specialist division of the world of learning), and in dissolving these two specialisms it fuses production, consumption, and learning into one continuous process in which paid learning becomes the dominant form of employment and the source of new wealth.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan's argument turns on a single identity: [[Automation (McLuhan)|automation is information]]. Once that identity holds, the two organizing categories of the industrial-mechanical order collapse together. The "job" was a fragmented, specialist slot — a "specialist slot for 'workers'" — and the "subject" was its pedagogical mirror, the partitioning of knowledge into discrete disciplines descended from the medieval trivium and quadrivium. Both are products of the same mechanical logic of fragmentation that printing perfected. When the [[Mechanical vs Electric|electric]] principle of instantaneous, interrelated [[Information]] invades that order, the partitions cannot survive: "Any subject taken in depth at once relates to other subjects."

Crucially, McLuhan insists this ends jobs and subjects but does *not* end work or learning. Instead it raises learning to the level of the principal economic activity. The same process that withdraws the present work force from industry makes "learning itself ... become the principal kind of production and consumption." This is why he can call alarm about unemployment a "folly": the apparent loss of jobs is the reappearance of learning as the dominant employment — "Paid learning is already becoming both the dominant employment and the source of new wealth in our society." The empirical anchor is that "teachers are already the largest employee group in the U.S. economy, and may well become the only group." (See [[Automation as Learning a Living]] for the full concept; this note isolates the structural claim that anchors it.)

The fusion is total. McLuhan writes that "Energy and production now tend to fuse with information and learning" and that "electric automation unites production, consumption, and learning in an inextricable process." The old dichotomies — culture and technology, art and commerce, work and leisure — dissolve. The consumer "becomes producer in the automation circuit," and the worker's "very toil" becomes "a kind of enlightenment." What looks like the end of work is the conversion of work into a form of education.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Automation is information, not an extension of mechanical fragmentation (the medium of automation is the instant, interrelating speed of electricity).
2. The "job" (specialist work-slot) and the "subject" (specialist knowledge-division) are both products of mechanical fragmentation, descended from print and modeled on the obsolete trivium/quadrivium.
3. Therefore information-as-automation ends both jobs and subjects simultaneously — it ends specialism in work *and* in learning.
4. But ending jobs and subjects does not end work or learning; it makes the source of energy independent of the work operation, so process (making *and* learning) gains primacy.
5. The same withdrawal of the work force from industry makes learning the principal kind of production and consumption — production, consumption, and learning fuse.
6. Conclusion: "Paid learning" becomes the dominant employment and source of wealth; alarm about unemployment is therefore a "folly," a projection of obsolete mechanical categories onto the electric age.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- **Teachers as the largest U.S. employee group.** "For this reason, teachers are already the largest employee group in the U.S. economy, and may well become the only group." (`[[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/37 Chapter 33 - Automation - Learning a Living]]`) — McLuhan's single quantitative anchor that paid learning is already the dominant employment.
- **The medieval trivium and quadrivium as the obsolete subject-division model.** "In education the conventional division of the curriculum into subjects is already as outdated as the medieval trivium and quadrivium after the Renaissance." The historical analogy supplies the pedagogical half of the argument: just as the Renaissance made the medieval subject-divisions obsolete, electricity makes the print-era curriculum obsolete.
- **The light/information identity.** "Light is a nonspecial-ist kind of energy or power that is identical with information and knowledge" — the physical model for why electric energy (being nonspecialist) dissolves specialism in both work and learning.
- **The end of the assembly line / lineal sequence** — the industrial demonstration that fragmentation has ended ("The assembly line has gone the way of the stag line").

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **McLuhan offers no political economy of distribution.** He asserts that paid learning becomes "the source of new wealth" but does not explain who pays, how learning is remunerated, or how the "withdrawal of the present work force from industry" is socially survived in the interim. The "folly of alarm about unemployment" is asserted, not demonstrated against the lived experience of displacement.
- **The harassment of the retrained executive cuts against the optimism.** McLuhan himself notes the "harrowing" burden on "senior executives" forced to acquire "new basic knowledge and skill ... in middle age," which sits uneasily with the liberatory framing of fused work-and-learning.
- **Sharpest disagreement — Harari's [[The Useless Class]].** Where McLuhan reads the withdrawal of the work force as a *promotion* into learning (a "fate that calls men to the role of artist in society"), Yuval Noah Harari reads the same automation as producing a permanently unemployable, economically and politically superfluous "useless class." McLuhan's continuous-learning subject is, in Harari's account, exactly the figure that algorithms that know us better than we know ourselves render obsolete. The two arguments share the premise (automation ends the industrial job) and diverge on whether the displaced human is elevated into learning or discarded.
