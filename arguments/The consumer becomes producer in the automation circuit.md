# The consumer becomes producer in the automation circuit

## Claim

In the mechanical economy, production and consumption are separated stages connected by a one-way flow of goods. McLuhan argues that automation, being a feedback loop rather than a lineal sequence, collapses that separation: "the consumer becomes producer in the automation circuit." Because automation "deals with all the units and components of the industrial and marketing process exactly as radio or TV combine the individuals in the audience into new interprocess," consumption and marketing are folded into the same information circuit as production, and the consumer is no longer the passive endpoint of a line but an active participant in the process.

## Expanded Explanation

The argument follows directly from McLuhan's identification of automation with feedback and with information. Perfecting a machine "into automatic" involves "feedback" — "introducing an information loop or circuit, where before there had been merely a one way flow or mechanical sequence." Once production is a loop rather than a line, there is no stable downstream "consumer" position outside the circuit; "automation affects not just production, but every phase of consumption and marketing." The consumer is drawn into the interprocess on the same footing as every other component.

McLuhan's controlling analogy is the newspaper. The reader of the "mosaic telegraph press" does not passively receive a finished, lineal narrative; confronted with "a surrealistic mosaic of bits of 'human interest' in vivid interaction," the reader "makes his own news, or just is his own news." The mosaic form requires the reader to complete it — to participate in producing the meaning — exactly as the cool TV image demands "audience participation." McLuhan generalizes this from media to industry: "commodity industries under automation share the same structural character of the entertainment industries in the degree that both approximate the condition of instant information." Mass production under automation is therefore "not in terms of size, but of an instant inclusive embrace" in which "everybody becomes involved... at the same time."

The deeper warrant is the chapter's master identity, automation = information. Once "energy and production... tend to fuse with information and learning," and "marketing and consumption tend to become one with learning, enlightenment, and the intake of information," the categories that kept producer and consumer apart — making versus buying, supplying versus using — dissolve into a single informational process. This is the same dissolution that grounds [[Automation as Learning a Living]] (work fused with learning); here it is stated on the consumption side. The argument is a remarkably early articulation of what later theory calls the "prosumer," and it is the McLuhan-side precursor to the surveillance- and platform-capitalist condition in which the user's activity is itself the product — read approvingly by McLuhan, critically by his later interlocutors.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Automation is a feedback loop — "an information loop or circuit" — replacing the mechanical one-way flow. *(Directly stated)*
2. In a loop there is no fixed downstream position outside the circuit; automation therefore "affects not just production, but every phase of consumption and marketing." *(Directly stated)*
3. The mosaic press is the model: its reader "makes his own news, or just is his own news" — consuming the form requires producing its meaning. *(Directly stated)*
4. Generalizing, commodity industries "approximate the condition of instant information," so consumption and marketing fuse with "the intake of information." *(Directly stated)*
5. Therefore the producer/consumer boundary dissolves: "the consumer becomes producer in the automation circuit." *(Directly stated)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- McLuhan presents the dissolution as structural fact, not as a political development to be evaluated; he does not ask who captures the value the consumer-as-producer creates. This is the omission his later critics fill.
- **Han's critique.** The consumer-producer fusion McLuhan describes is, in Byung-Chul Han's reading, the structure of [[Auto-Exploitation]] and [[Immaterial Labour]]: when the user is also the producer, the "freedom" to participate becomes uncompensated, self-driven labor folded into capital. *(Interpretive synthesis)*
- **Harari / Dataism.** The reader who "is his own news" prefigures the data subject whose every act of consumption is itself the production of behavioral data — the productive self-exposure later theorized as the engine of the information economy. *(Speculative connection)*
- McLuhan's "instant inclusive embrace" elides the difference between participation and capture — a slippage the vault preserves rather than resolves.
