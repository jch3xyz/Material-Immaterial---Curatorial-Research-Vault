# The achievement society supersedes the disciplinary society

## Claim

Twenty-first-century society is no longer Foucault's [[Disciplinary Society|disciplinary society]] of confinement but an [[Achievement Society|achievement society]] whose self-exploiting achievement-subjects produce "depressives and losers" where discipline produced "madmen and criminals."

## Expanded Explanation

This is the foundational periodizing argument of Han's corpus, here in its primary source. Foucault's disciplinary world of "hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories" has, Han claims, "long been replaced by another regime, namely a society of fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories." The institutional walls that "separate the normal from the abnormal" now "seem archaic." What changes is not merely the architecture of power but the form of the subject: the disciplinary "obedience-subject" becomes the achievement society's "achievement-subject," an "entrepreneur of itself." `Directly stated`

The hinge of the periodization is a switch of modal verbs. Disciplinary society is governed by negativity — by the *May Not* of prohibition and the *Should* of compulsion. Achievement society discards negativity and runs on the positive *Can*: "Unlimited *Can* is the positive modal verb of achievement society." Han does not posit a clean rupture. "On one level, continuity holds in the paradigm shift": the drive to maximize production persists, and once disciplinary negativity "hits a limit," the regime switches from *Should* to *Can* because "the positivity of *Can* is much more efficient than the negativity of *Should*." The achievement-subject is "faster and more productive than the obedience-subject," but the *Can* "does not revoke the *Should*" — discipline is completed, not abolished. (This modal axis is the subject of [[Can vs Should]].) `Directly stated`

Crucially, Han names *achievement*, not *control*, as the regime, and he does so by explicitly rejecting two rival names. Foucault's analysis "cannot account for the psychic and topological changes" of the transformation, and "the commonly employed concept of 'control society'" — Deleuze's term — "does justice to this change" no better, because "it still contains too much negativity." The diagnostic payoff is pathological: where disciplinary negativity "produces madmen and criminals," achievement society's excess positivity "creates depressives and losers." The self that exploits itself voluntarily, "without external constraints," is at once "predator and prey," waging "internalized war" — the engine of [[Auto-Exploitation]] and the source of burnout. `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. Foucault's disciplinary institutions of confinement (prisons, barracks, factories, madhouses) have been displaced by sites of voluntary performance (fitness studios, office towers, banks, malls). `Directly stated`
2. With the sites, the subject changes: the externally coerced "obedience-subject" becomes the self-driving "achievement-subject," an entrepreneur of itself. `Directly stated`
3. The mechanism of the shift is a switch of modal verbs from the negative *May Not / Should* to the positive, unlimited *Can*, adopted because positivity is "much more efficient" at maximizing production. `Directly stated`
4. Neither Foucault's "power" nor Deleuze's "control society" names the present correctly — the latter "still contains too much negativity"; the regime is *achievement*, governed by positivity. `Directly stated`
5. Therefore the characteristic pathologies invert: disciplinary negativity yields "madmen and criminals," achievement-society positivity yields "depressives and losers." `Directly stated`

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The two catalogues of institutions — disciplinary ("hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories") versus achievement ("fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories") — as the topological proof of the supersession. ([[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/03 Beyond Disciplinary Society|03 Beyond Disciplinary Society]]) `Directly stated`
- Alain Ehrenberg's location of depression "in the transition from disciplinary society to achievement society" — though Han corrects Ehrenberg, who misreads the cause as the imperative "only to belong to oneself" rather than "the pressure to achieve." `Directly stated`
- The **Bartleby case (ch.7)** as a *worked counter-example*: Melville's scrivener belongs to the *disciplinary* world, not the achievement society, and so confirms the periodization by negative instance. "The society that Melville describes is still a disciplinary society." Bartleby is hemmed by "walls and partitions, the elements of disciplinary architecture"; the tale is "A Story of Wall-Street." "He still represents an obedience-subject. He does not develop symptoms of depression, which is a hallmark of late-modern achievement society." Han stresses that "monotonous copying ... leaves no free space in which private initiative would prove necessary," so "what makes Bartleby sick is not excessive positivity or possibility." Bartleby is exhausted by the disciplinary *Should*, not by the achievement society's *Can* — which is precisely why he cannot be the achievement-subject's prototype. ([[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/07 The Bartleby Case|07 The Bartleby Case]]) `Directly stated`

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Han's own continuity caveat.** He concedes that "no break exists between *Should* and *Can*; continuity prevails" and that the obedience-subject "remains disciplined" after "completing the disciplinary stage." The supersession is therefore an *intensification* of the same productivist logic, not a clean historical rupture — which raises the question of whether "achievement society" names a new regime or a deepening of the old one. `Directly stated`
- **The rejection of "control society" is a contested move.** Deleuze's own "control society" was itself meant to name the post-disciplinary present; Han's charge that it "still contains too much negativity" reads control through his positivity/negativity axis rather than through Deleuze's vocabulary of modulation. (In *Psychopolitics* Han later *adopts* "control society" via the mole/snake figure — see [[Society of Control]] and [[Discipline vs Control]] — so the relationship between "achievement society" and "control society" across Han's corpus is not fully stable.) `Interpretive synthesis`
- **Distinct from the methodological complaint.** This periodizing argument ("the regime has changed") is not the same as Han's later methodological charge that [[Foucault failed to make the turn to psychopolitics|Foucault failed to make the turn]] from biopolitics to psychopolitics; here the issue is that Foucault's *object* (the disciplinary regime) is past, not that his *method* stopped short. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **Periodization risks over-generalization.** The thesis universalizes a particular (Western, post-industrial, neoliberal) experience of labor; disciplinary confinement evidently persists for many populations. Han offers the catalogues as illustration rather than as empirical demonstration. `Interpretive synthesis`
