# The obsolescence of the visual scientific model drove the dematerialization of sculpture

## Claim

Sculptors adopted the ball-and-stick, atomic, topological, and mathematical model as a reservoir of pure form precisely when science was abandoning visual models for mathematics; because their works were attempts at visible representation of the invisible, as the referents dematerialized so did the sculpture — Burnham's explicit periodization of "the steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham's chapter on formalism traces a precise mechanism behind the loss of mass, presence, and material density in twentieth-century sculpture. The scientific model — the carbon-molecule diagram, the Rutherford-Bohr atom, the plaster casts and stringed figures filling German mathematics departments — gave the sculptor "a more unworldly and idealistic vocabulary of sculptural forms," a reservoir of pure form "not invested with functionalist overtones." What attracted the sculptor was that the model "seemed to represent the creation of form purely through mental activity with no reference to visible reality." This links the argument directly to the concept of [[The Scientific Model]] as Burnham develops it.

The irony Burnham insists upon is one of timing. The Constructivists and their successors took up these models as "exciting configurations" in the 1920's, just as physics was discarding them: Bertrand Russell's 1926 doubt about "the ultimate reality of the solar model," the rise of quantum theory and Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, and the shift Russell called the end of "sight-physics." Visual object-models gave way to field models and "pure mathematics." Sculptors were therefore clinging to a picture of matter — the open, disconnected, ball-and-stick atom — that the sciences had already abandoned as a false image.

The decisive move in the argument is that the sculptor's referent was always invisible: subvisual matter, sub-atomic events, post-Euclidean and topological space. The sculptural work was "visible representation of the invisible relationships of physical reality." As science accepted that this reality could no longer be pictured at all (electrons as "cloud formations," the "lack of individuality of the particle"), the sculpture chasing it grew thinner, more open, more cantilevered, less material — it dematerialized in pursuit of a referent that had itself ceased to be picturable. The model "has been simply one way for the sculptor to cling to a tenuous reality." This is the keystone provenance bridging Burnham's 1910–1950's periodization to Lippard's later [[Dematerialization]] of the art object.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Science offered the sculptor a reservoir of "pure form" — the substantive model (atomic, molecular, mathematical) — whose attraction was that it referred to no visible thing but seemed made "purely through mental activity."
2. The sculptor's referent was therefore by definition invisible: subvisual matter, post-Euclidean space, the morphology of mathematical operation. The work was an "attempt at visible representation of the invisible."
3. At the same moment (Russell 1926, quantum theory, the uncertainty principle), physics and mathematics abandoned the visual model itself as a false or misleading picture of matter, moving to field models and pure mathematics — the obsolescence of the visual model.
4. With its invisible referent now declared unpicturable, the sculpture that pursued it had nothing solid left to render, so its mass, presence, and material density steadily fell away.
5. Conclusion: the obsolescence of the visual scientific model drove "the steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's"; dematerialization has its basis "not only in physics and technology, but in the thought processes of the mathematician."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself hedges with "in part" — the dematerialization is over-determined; he names technology (the loss of object uniqueness in the "information-energy network," mass-produced "protosculpture") as a co-cause alongside the obsolete visual model. The argument is one strong strand, not a sole cause.
- The Constructivists, especially Gabo, "hotly refuted" the claim that their work imitated the scientific model at all: "The shapes we are creating are not abstract, they are absolute... released from an already existent thing in nature." Burnham concedes the influence was often unconscious and that Sorbonne measurements of Pevsner's constructions found no formulae — so the causal link runs through sensibility, not literal copying. (`Directly stated` that the artists denied it.)
- The periodization ends in the 1950's; Burnham treats the later conceptual erasure of the object as a separate, technologically driven development, so this argument grounds but does not by itself complete the bridge to Lippard's 1966–1972 dematerialization.
