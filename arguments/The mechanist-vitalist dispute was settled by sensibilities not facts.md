# The mechanist-vitalist dispute was settled by sensibilities not facts

## Claim

Burnham argues that the centuries-long mechanist-vitalist controversy was decided not by the marshalling of correct facts but by a clash of sensibilities, which is why vitalism — refuted in biology — nonetheless prevailed in modern sculpture, having been won for art by Henri Bergson's poetic articulation rather than by any empirical proof.

## Expanded Explanation

The chapter sets up a puzzle: if vitalism "played such a reactionary role in the biological sciences," why "did it provide some of the most advanced justification for avant-garde abstract sculpture?" Burnham's answer turns on the *kind* of victory the dispute admitted. Vitalism never won the laboratory — for Burnham, "The history of biology is the refutation of vitalism" (quoting von Bertalanffy), and biology's attacks "were not pat polemical speeches or writings, but the weight of new discoveries." Mechanism advanced fact by fact; vitalism retreated, redefining the "life force" across "an ever-receding sphere of influence." On the terrain of facts, the vitalist always lost.

But Burnham insists the dispute was never really *on* that terrain. It was "essentially one, not of the right facts, but of the right sensibilities toward all that makes life bearable as a state of consciousness." Because the stakes were affective and existential rather than evidentiary, the contest could be won by whoever made the more beautiful and bearable picture of life — and there [[Henri Bergson]] was unmatched. Burnham frames him first as a writer: his philosophy "must be studied as literature before considering [it] as philosophy," and his ideas "invariably found great sympathy among writers and artists" even when they "failed to impress scientists." Bergson's method is itself poetic: "As a poet, he conveys without the hindrance of empirical proofs; he explains by explaining the futility of explanation," deploying "the use of intellect to undermine intellect — through the aid of artistry."

The settlement, then, is asymmetric across domains. Mechanism won biology by refutation; vitalism won art by sensibility, and persisted there longer "owing to a time lag." Crucially, science never bothered to refute Bergson on his own ground: "Scientists as a rule have rarely attempted to refute interpretations of the kind mounted by Bergson; rather they have allowed them an unnoticed, gradual demise — suffocation by the weight of subsequent discovery." Vitalism in biology was not argued to death but buried under accumulating fact, while in sculpture, where facts were never the currency, the *élan vital* became "the rallying cry for one of the twentieth century's most powerful aesthetics."

## Reasoning Structure

1. The mechanist-vitalist dispute is "essentially one, not of the right facts, but of the right sensibilities toward all that makes life bearable as a state of consciousness" — its true stakes are affective and existential, not evidentiary.
2. In biology, the dispute was nonetheless *adjudicated* by fact: mechanism advanced by "the weight of new discoveries," forcing vitalism into an "ever-receding sphere of influence."
3. Scientists therefore never had to refute vitalism's interpretations directly; they let them die by "suffocation by the weight of subsequent discovery" rather than by counter-argument.
4. Where facts are not the currency — in art — the contest is decided by sensibility, and Bergson, "a poet" who "conveys without the hindrance of empirical proofs," won it through "the use of intellect to undermine intellect — through the aid of artistry."
5. Conclusion: vitalism could be simultaneously defeated in biology and victorious in sculpture, because the two domains weighed the dispute by different measures — fact versus sensibility — and Bergson supplied the sensibility art required.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself notes vitalism "did have its scientifically respectable side" (Driesch's blastula experiments, the biologists Oken, Blumenbach, Treviranus, Müller), complicating any clean fact/sensibility split — some of vitalism's appeal was empirical, drawn from problems "where material evidence was scant."
- The claim is Burnham's interpretive diagnosis of a historical reception, not a demonstrable causal mechanism; it credits a settlement to "sensibilities" that is hard to verify against the actual reasons individual scientists and artists held their positions.
- Burnham also concedes a counter-current: "in the last fifteen years the development of molecular biology has given us the beginning of a poetic view of living systems which may well in time far outshine the delicate imprecisions of Bergson" — i.e., the *sensibility* advantage he grants vitalism may itself be migrating to mechanism, which would undercut the asymmetry the argument depends on.
