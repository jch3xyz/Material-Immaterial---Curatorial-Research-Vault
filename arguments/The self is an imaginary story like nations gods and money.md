# The self is an imaginary story like nations gods and money

## Claim

The unified, indivisible self that liberalism treats as the source of all meaning and authority is not a discovered fact but a fabrication of the narrating self — "an imaginary story, just like nations, gods and money."

## Expanded Explanation

In the second half of *The Time Bomb in the Laboratory* (the section headed *Who Are I?*), Harari turns the critique of free will into a critique of the individual. Liberalism, he argues, rests on a second factual premise beyond free will: that "we have a single and indivisible self." The very word advertises the claim — "To be an individual means that I am in-dividual" — and the political machinery of liberalism depends on it: only if I have "one – and only one – true self" do my votes, purchases and choices carry the authority liberalism invests in them. Harari's contention is that the life sciences have falsified this premise as decisively as they falsified free will. Deep introspection does not deliver a single authentic voice; it dissolves "into a cacophony of conflicting voices, none of which is 'my true self'." His coinage compresses the point: "Humans aren't individuals. They are 'dividuals'." `Directly stated`

The positive content of the self, on this account, is supplied not by an inner essence but by a fiction-making mechanism. Drawing on Gazzaniga's split-brain experiments — where the speech-controlling left hemisphere, given no access to what the right hemisphere saw, "just invented something credible" — and on Kahneman's cold-water and colonoscopy studies, Harari distinguishes the [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self|narrating self from the experiencing self]]. The narrating self is "akin to Gazzaniga's left-brain interpreter," forever "spinning yarns about the past and making plans for the future," and "most people identify with their narrating self. When they say 'I', they mean the story in their head, not the stream of experiences they undergo." The "I" is therefore an *assembled* artefact: "Each of us has a sophisticated system that throws away most of our experiences, keeps only a few choice samples, mixes them up with bits from movies we saw, novels we read, speeches we heard, and from our own daydreams, and weaves out of all that jumble a seemingly coherent story about who I am." `Directly stated`

The load-bearing move is the analogy that closes the section: "We see, then, that the self too is an imaginary story, just like nations, gods and money." This binds the self-critique to Harari's central thesis of [[Intersubjective Reality]]. The same fiction-making capacity that lets Sapiens cooperate by believing in money, deities and nations is the capacity that fabricates the self — the self is the same *kind* of entity as those collective fictions, only turned inward and held by a single nervous system. Crucially the word "imaginary" is not, for Harari, equivalent to "worthless"; elsewhere he insists fiction "is vital." The claim is ontological and deflationary, not dismissive: the self is real the way money is real — sustained by an ongoing act of narration rather than anchored in any underlying essence. The argument is the inward terminus of the book's logic and underwrites the vault's update to [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]]: the autonomous, self-knowing chooser at the heart of liberal humanism is, on Harari's telling, a story the brain tells about itself. `Directly stated` (the analogy and the dividual coinage are verbatim; the framing as the inward extension of intersubjective reality is `Strongly implied` by the chapter's own sequence.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Liberalism requires not only free will but a "single and indivisible self" — only one authentic inner voice can ground the authority liberalism grants to the voter, customer and viewer ("To be an individual means that I am in-dividual").
2. The life sciences find no such unity: rigorous introspection dissolves into "a cacophony of conflicting voices, none of which is 'my true self'." Hence "Humans aren't individuals. They are 'dividuals'."
3. What produces the *feeling* of a unified self is the narrating self — Gazzaniga's left-brain "interpreter" — which fabricates coherent stories from partial clues and which "most people identify with" when they say "I."
4. This narrating function is the very same fiction-making capacity that builds money, gods and nations (Harari's intersubjective reality).
5. Therefore the self is "an imaginary story, just like nations, gods and money" — real as a sustained narration, not as a discovered essence — and the liberal individual is a myth, "as real as the eternal Christian soul, Santa Claus and the Easter Bunny."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The etymological argument: "To be an individual means that I am in-dividual" — and its inversion, "Humans aren't individuals. They are 'dividuals'." (Ch. 11)
- Sperry and Gazzaniga's split-brain studies: the left-hemisphere interpreter that, lacking the right hemisphere's data, "just invented something credible" — generalized to all humans as "my own private CIA" acting without the "State Department's" knowledge, the latter then cooking up a story. (Ch. 11)
- Kahneman's cold-water experiment and the Kahneman–Redelmeier colonoscopy study: the duration-blind "peak-end rule" demonstrates that the [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self|narrating self averages rather than aggregates]], exposing "at least two different selves within us." (Ch. 11)
- The assembly metaphor: the "sophisticated system" that discards most experience and "weaves out of all that jumble a seemingly coherent story about who I am." (Ch. 11)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The felt sense of unity.** People "feel free" and feel singular; Harari concedes the phenomenology ("This is true") but argues that feeling a self no more proves one exists than feeling free proves free will — "people erroneously jump to the conclusion" that the feeling of unity entails a unifier.
- **It changes nothing in practice (Harari's own caveat).** He grants that "the individual self is an illusion" is more than 2,000 years old (India, China, Greece) and that "such doubts don't really change history unless they have a practical impact." Even Dawkins and Pinker, after "deconstructing the self," perform "breathtaking intellectual somersaults" back to liberalism. The argument is thus diagnostic, not yet world-historical — what will force the issue is "concrete technologies," not philosophy.
- **Does "fiction" unmask or merely classify?** If money, nations and the self are all "imaginary stories" yet fiction "is vital," the deflationary "just a story" risks equivocation: it is unclear whether calling the self a fiction debunks it or simply assigns it Harari's third ontological category (see the tension below).
- **Reductive premise.** The argument inherits the eliminativist commitment of [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]] — that the self reduces to "genes, hormones and neurons"; readers who reject that reduction can grant the empirical split-brain and peak-end findings without conceding that no unified self exists.
