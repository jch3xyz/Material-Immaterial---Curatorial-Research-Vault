# The unified individual is dead the networked self is multiple

## Claim

The myth of the unified individual has been destroyed; the networked self is de-centred, distributed, and multiple — to connect is to be multiplied, not unified.

## Expanded Explanation

Roy Ascott stages a deliberate inversion of the founding gesture of Western subjectivity. Where Descartes grounded the self in a single, indivisible, self-present thinking substance — *cogito ergo sum* — Ascott offers a telematic counter-formula: "I connect, therefore I am multiple." The Cartesian self was unitary precisely because it was sealed: a private interior that reasoned alone. The networked self is constituted by its connections, and connection does not consolidate the one — it proliferates the many. Identity in the Net is "non-linear," and the act of joining the web of telematic relations splits rather than centres the person who joins.

This is also a post-Freudian claim. "Freud is dead," Ascott declares, alongside the myth of the unified individual. The Freudian psyche, however internally conflicted between agencies, still presupposed a single bounded organism to be cured back toward integration; depth psychology aimed at the reunification of a divided self. Ascott abandons that telos. The technoetic self is "made up of many selves: de-centred, distributed, and tele-schizophrenic" — and the schizophrenia here is not a pathology to be healed but a constitutive, even desirable, condition, what he elsewhere names a "constructively schizophrenic" cyberself. Multiplicity is the new normal of consciousness in the network.

The argument completes the trajectory of [[The Distributed Self]]: the unitary personality dissolves into multiple distributed presences, and that dissolution is to be embraced rather than mourned. Connection is the operative mechanism. The more one is networked — telepresent across locations, extended through [[Cyberception]], woven into [[Network Consciousness]] — the less one coincides with a single, located, sovereign ego. The self becomes a field of relations, an "infinity of phreno-fractals constantly creating alternative realities," rather than a point.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The Western individual was modelled on the Cartesian cogito: a single, self-present, indivisible thinking subject (and reinforced by the Freudian project of reintegrating a divided psyche). This is "the myth of the unified individual."
2. Telematic connection does not deliver the self back to itself; it distributes the self across nodes, presences, and relations.
3. Therefore the unified individual is not merely diminished but obsolete ("dead"): the self is de-centred, distributed, and "tele-schizophrenic."
4. The operative formula is no longer "I think, therefore I am" (one) but "I connect, therefore I am multiple" — connection multiplies the self.
5. This multiplicity is not a defect to be cured but the constructive, normal condition of identity in the Net ("non-linear identity"; the "constructively schizophrenic" cyberself).

- The companion "Cyberself" definition affirming the same multiplicity as constructive rather than pathological: "We are each made up of many selves: de-centred, distributed, and constructively schizophrenic." (Directly stated)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim is asserted aphoristically, in the compressed form of a glossary entry ("100 Terms and Definitions"), not argued out; it states a creed more than it demonstrates a thesis.
- The figure of the "schizophrenic" self is rhetorical and celebratory; it risks aestheticising a clinical condition and presumes that distribution is liberating rather than, say, fragmenting or exploitable — a tension with diagnoses that read the networked, exposed self as a site of control rather than freedom ([[Digital Panopticon]]).
- Whether "I connect, therefore I am multiple" describes an ontological fact or a programmatic aspiration is left open; the slogan-form blurs the descriptive and the prescriptive.
- The continuity of *who* is multiplied is unaddressed: if there is no unified individual, it is unclear what binds the many selves into a single biography at all.
