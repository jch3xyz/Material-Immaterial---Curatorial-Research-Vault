# Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness spiritual before utilitarian

## Claim

For Roy Ascott, telematic technology "is the technology of consciousness; it is spiritual before it is in any way utilitarian" — its true subject is not communication or use but mind, and it is bound to a cultural longing (to escape the earth and the body) that the technologies at once express and intensify. (*Directly stated*.)

## Expanded Explanation

This is the deepest stratum of Ascott's telematic program, stated near the close of "Telenoia" (ch. 23). Where his earlier essays argue that telematic networking is [[Telematic networking is a quantum leap in human consciousness|a quantum leap in human consciousness]] and that the network can constitute [[Telematic networks constitute a planetary network consciousness|a planetary consciousness]], here Ascott names the *order* of the claim: the consciousness register is not one effect of telematics among others but its essential nature. Telematic technology is "the technology of consciousness" — and, crucially, "spiritual before it is in any way utilitarian." Utility is downstream; the priority is mind, transformation, the sacred. This is the explicit seed of what Ascott would later name technoetics — the study of technology's role in the evolution of consciousness.

The argument's force comes from a reciprocal, almost dialectical move that immediately precedes the claim. Ascott diagnoses a "cultural transformation" he reads as "an enormous rupture from the past, indeed from history itself": a "quality of indifference born of longing… the longing to escape the earth, to escape the body, to reframe ourselves." This longing is both cause and consequence of the technology — "the same longing brought forth the very technologies that have provoked this rupture." So the relation is circular rather than instrumental: the desire to leave behind the [[The Body as Data|earthbound body]] produces telematics, and telematics in turn deepens and provokes that desire. A merely utilitarian account (telematics as a faster channel for information) cannot explain a technology that is born of, and feeds, a spiritual longing to "recontextualise ourselves on the cosmic plane." Hence: spiritual before utilitarian.

This is why Ascott insists the displacement of the body "leads… not to a disembodiment of the person, nor to an immateriality, but to the rematerialisation… the reinvention of the human being" — a body "made ready to fly, outward bound for the stars." The claim therefore re-enchants the very technologies that, in the disenchanted modern frame, would be read as mere tools or as instruments of domination. It belongs to the vault's [[Re-enchantment]] thread (against [[Disenchantment]]): telematics is presented as a vehicle for spiritual transformation rather than for control or efficiency. (*Interpretive synthesis* for the placement; the consciousness/spiritual priority itself is *directly stated*.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. A profound cultural longing — to escape the earth and the body, to "reframe" and "recontextualise" the human — marks the present as a rupture from history.
2. This longing is not merely served by technology but *produced* the technologies that provoke the rupture; reciprocally, those technologies deepen the longing. (The relation is circular, not instrumental.)
3. A technology born of and feeding a spiritual longing cannot be understood primarily as a tool for use.
4. Its real subject is consciousness — its transformation, expansion, and reinvention of the human being.
5. Therefore telematic technology "is the technology of consciousness; it is spiritual before it is in any way utilitarian."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The verbatim claim itself: "Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness; it is spiritual before it is in any way utilitarian" (ch. 23).
- The reciprocity premise: "The same longing brought forth the very technologies that have provoked this rupture" (ch. 23) — longing both produces and is produced by the technology.
- The diagnosis of the longing: "a quality of indifference born of longing. The longing to escape the earth, to escape the body, to reframe ourselves, to recontextualize our lives" (ch. 23).
- The rematerialisation thesis: the erasure of the Cartesian material body "leads, however, not to a disembodiment of the person, nor to an immateriality, but to the rematerialisation… the reinvention of the human being… made ready to fly, outward bound for the stars" (ch. 23) — the spiritual telos is reinvention, not mere immateriality.
- The three "cardinal factors" of the rupture — the growing-together of minds (telematic connectivity), the dispersal of the body (telepresence), and the extension of the environment (artificial life / space) — which Ascott frames as a transition of *mind*, not of utility (ch. 23).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Self-flagged utopianism.** The spiritual-before-utilitarian claim is openly visionary; as Shanken notes elsewhere, Ascott's theories are "subject to criticism for their deterministic and utopian attitude regarding technology and the future" (ch. 04). The same telematic convergence is read across the vault as homogenisation and surveillance — Han's [[Hell of the Same]] and the [[Digital Panopticon]] — i.e. as eminently utilitarian instruments of power, not vehicles of spirit.
- **The longing as pathology.** Ascott concedes the longing produces "profound neglect of the earthbound condition… apparently callous indifference to homelessness, to the disintegration of society." A critic could read the "spiritual" register as a sublimation of an escapist refusal of material, embodied, social responsibility rather than a genuine elevation of consciousness.
- **Spiritual or merely rhetorical?** "Consciousness" and "the spiritual" may be honorific figures projected onto an infrastructure of information exchange; the boundary between a real transformation of mind and a mystified [[Distributed Cognition|distributed cognition]] is exactly where the claim is least demonstrable (cf. ch. 04 on the global brain that "may never achieve the type of consciousness manifested in humans").
- **Provoked by, not chosen.** Because Ascott makes the longing both cause and effect, the account risks a soft technological determinism: it is unclear what agency, if any, could redirect a technology that is said to be born of and to feed an irresistible cultural longing.
