# The pathologies of the present are infarctions not infections

## Claim

The neurological illnesses that define the present age — depression, ADHD, borderline personality disorder, and burnout — are not infections produced by a foreign, negating Other but *infarctions* produced by an excess of positivity, so the twenty-first century is "determined neither by bacteria nor by viruses, but by neurons."

## Expanded Explanation

Han opens *The Burnout Society* with a periodization of pathology. Every age, he argues, has its signature afflictions: a bacterial age ended with antibiotics, and the immunological technologies of the past century have already carried us beyond a viral age. What remains, "from a pathological standpoint," is an age "determined neither by bacteria nor by viruses, but by neurons." The illnesses that "mark the landscape of pathology at the beginning of the twenty-first century" — depression, ADHD, BPD, and burnout — are categorically different from the diseases of the [[Immunological Age]]. They are, in his signature formulation, "not infections, but infarctions."

The distinction is metaphorical and diagnostic at once. An *infection* presupposes an outside: a foreign agent that intrudes, against which the self mounts an immunoreaction following "the dialectic of negativity." An *infarction* is system-immanent — a blockage, an overload, a too-much that chokes from within. The neuronal pathologies "do not follow from the negativity of what is immunologically foreign, but from an excess of positivity." Harm, Han insists, "does not come from negativity alone, but also from positivity—not just from the Other or the foreign, but also from the Same." This is why the present afflictions are invisible to the apparatus built to fight the alien: "they elude all technologies and techniques that seek to combat what is alien." Immunology has no purchase on a sickness that has no enemy.

Chapter 8 restates the thesis as the diagnosis of the [[Achievement Society]]'s exhaustion. The conditions of excessive tiredness "are not immunological reactions presupposing the negativity of the immunologically Other. Rather, they are caused by a too-much of positivity. The excessiveness of performance enhancement leads to psychic infarctions." The same epochal claim thus brackets the book: positivity does not deprive but saturates, does not exclude but exhausts, and its violence registers not as infection from without but as infarction from within.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Each historical age has a characteristic class of affliction (bacterial, then viral/immunological).
2. Immunological pathology operates by negativity: a foreign Other intrudes and provokes an immunoreaction (the dialectic of "negation of negation").
3. The present age is poor in negativity because Otherness is "disappearing," replaced by a non-immunogenic difference that "represents the Same."
4. Its pathologies (depression, ADHD, BPD, burnout) therefore cannot be infections — there is no negating Other to be infected by.
5. Instead they arise from an *excess of positivity*: overproduction, overachievement, overcommunication, the overheating of the ego.
6. Conclusion: these illnesses are infarctions, not infections; the twenty-first century is the age of the neuron, not the virus, and harm now comes from the Same.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The historical sequence of "signature afflictions" — the bacterial age ending with antibiotics, the viral age already left behind via immunological technology — grounding the periodization (`02 Neuronal Power`).
- The naming of the pathologies themselves: depression, ADHD, BPD, and burnout as the markers of present pathology (`02 Neuronal Power`).
- Baudrillard's aphorism "He who lives by the Same shall die by the Same," cited to corroborate that violence can issue from positivity and the Same (`02 Neuronal Power`; see [[Byung-Chul Han and Jean Baudrillard]]).
- Esposito's *immunitas* theory, marshalled as the counter-position Han rejects: Esposito's examples "do not address contemporary problems, but only objects from the past" (`02 Neuronal Power`).
- The doping / neuroenhancement of the [[Achievement Society]] — the human reduced to a "performance-machine" whose overdrive yields "psychic infarctions" (`08 The Society of Tiredness`).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Han stages and rejects an explicit rival: Roberto Esposito's immunological reading of the present (immigration, epidemics, computer viruses as a shared "horizon" of immunization). Han's reply is that "even the so-called immigrant is not an immunological Other," and that Esposito analyzes "only objects from the past." A reader who finds immunological framing still apt for contemporary politics would contest the periodization.
- Baudrillard is the other limit case: he names the "obesity of all current systems" and the violence of the Same, yet "pictures the totalitarianism of the Same from an immunological standpoint" — proof, for Han, that even acute diagnosticians of positivity keep reaching for the obsolete vocabulary of virus and antibody.
- The infection/infarction split is a *metaphor* doing diagnostic work; its force is conceptual rather than clinical, and Han does not adjudicate the biomedical etiology of the named disorders.
- `Interpretive synthesis`: that the chapter-2 framing and the chapter-8 restatement express one continuous thesis is a synthesis across the two passages; each individually states the infarction claim, and chapter 8 explicitly echoes the "too-much of positivity."
