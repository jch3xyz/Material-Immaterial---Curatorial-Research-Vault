# Prediction enacts the very fate it foresees

## Claim

Predictions built on historical data are not neutral forecasts but behavior-modifying feedback loops: they send agents into the world in ways that generate the very data that confirms them, laundering confirmation bias as empirical evidence and reincarnating, in code, the structure of predestination.

## Expanded Explanation

[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]'s "Algorithm" chapters are organized around a single question, which she calls her "confusion about the relationship between foresight and freedom": "To what extent does the act of prediction enact the very fate it foresees?" The intuitive reading of predictive analytics is that their accuracy reveals a fixed, deterministic world — that human behavior was always "the end of a long and rigid causal chain." O'Gieblyn's counter-thesis is that the accuracy is partly *manufactured* by the prediction itself. Because predictive models "rely on past behavior and decisions—not just of the individual but of others who share the same demographics," the forecast is not a reading of an independent future but an intervention that helps bring that future about. The "metadata—the shell of human experience—becomes part of a feedback loop that then actively modifies real behavior." Prediction is therefore performative, not descriptive: it is the social-scale variant of [[Feedback Loops|the cybernetic loop]], with one arm a profiled population and the other an institution acting on the profile.

The mechanism has three moves. First, the self is reduced to [[The Body as Data|external, measurable states]] — "your zip code," "the tone of your voice," "whom you're emailing and how often" — on the cybernetic premise, inherited "since the early days of [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]]," that "a person can be described purely in terms of pattern and probabilities, without any concern for interiority." Second, this data-shell is fed back as a constraint: the prediction routes real agents toward the conditions it forecast. Third — and this is what distinguishes prediction from ordinary guesswork — the loop's outputs arrive "bolstered by the authority of empirical evidence," so that the circularity is not merely hidden but relabeled as objectivity. PredPol's red boxes, Facebook's "I voted" sticker, and Amazon's "anticipatory shipping" are O'Gieblyn's three exhibits of prediction sliding from forecast into the production of its own confirming data, and finally into overt behavior modification. This is the process a Google researcher named [[Algorithmic Determinism|"algorithmic determinism"]]: people "become trapped within the mirror of their digital reflection."

The argument's deepest claim is genealogical. O'Gieblyn reads the predictive algorithm as [[The Algorithm as Providence|the modern reincarnation of predestination]]: the structure she traces in PredPol and the surveillance state is the same one that produced her own crisis of faith over Calvinist election. [[John Calvin|Calvin]]'s God "not only foresees but controls" the future; the predicted subject, like the elect or the reprobate, cannot know the verdict's grounds and cannot appeal it. She reads [[Max Weber|Weber]]'s *Protestant Ethic* directly onto the surveillance state: just as "those who cannot know whether or not they are chosen will do everything in their power to act as though they are," so "those who cannot know whether or not they pose a risk will do everything in their power to demonstrate their innocence." The post-Snowden self-censorship of search terms is her proof case — users "had simply absorbed the logic of the surveillance state into their behavior, such that it seemed like a choice." That [[Disenchantment|a disenchanted civilization]], having banished providence, rebuilt it in code is, for O'Gieblyn, no accident; she notes the "dark irony" that Microsoft named its first predictive software *Predestination*.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Predictive models are trained on historical behavioral data, the "shell of human experience," with interiority deliberately excluded (the cybernetic premise that a person is "pattern and probabilities").
2. Acting on a prediction routes real agents into the world — police into a red box, a voter toward the polls, a shipment toward a customer — in ways that generate fresh data biased toward the predicted outcome.
3. That self-generated data is read back as confirmation, so the prediction's circularity is laundered as "empirical evidence"; this is what "engender[s] confirmation bias in a way that intuition does not."
4. Hence prediction is a behavior-modifying feedback loop, not a neutral forecast: it can "enact the very fate it foresees."
5. The accuracy of such predictions therefore overstates the world's determinism, since the accuracy is partly an artifact of the loop — the structure O'Gieblyn identifies, via Calvin and Weber, as the predestination problem returning in secular, algorithmic form.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- **PredPol's red boxes.** Predictive-policing systems "gather their predictions from historical crime data," sending officers "to precisely the same poor neighborhoods they patrolled when they were guided by their intuition alone"; officers entering a box "expect to stumble on crimes," so "the person is a suspect because the algorithm identified the area as one where suspects might be located." (Ch12, citing Jackie Wang's *Carceral Capitalism*.) Grounds the claim that prediction manufactures its own confirming data.
- **Facebook's "I voted" experiment.** In 2010 and 2012 Facebook affixed "I voted" stickers to a percentage of user home pages, "tactics that were meant to use social pressure to nudge users to vote" — an "experiment" whose laboratory "had been real voters in an actual democratic election." The overt case where "prediction slides into behavior modification."
- **Amazon's "anticipatory shipping."** Amazon's 2018 patent presumes "it will eventually be able to predict what customers are going to buy before they actually do so" — prediction reaching toward the dissolution of "the line between prediction and control."
- **Post-Snowden self-censorship.** A *Berkeley Technology Law Review* study found searches for terms like "Al Qaeda" and "jihad" declined after the Snowden disclosures, not from waning interest but because users "had simply absorbed the logic of the surveillance state into their behavior." The Weberian salvation-anxiety mechanism in action.
- **Laplace and Calvin.** Pierre-Simon Laplace's intellect that could "predict any future event" vs. [[John Calvin|Calvin]]'s God who "not only foresees but controls" — the two poles between which the algorithm blurs.
- **The "Predestination" software.** Microsoft's naming of its first predictive GPS software *Predestination* — O'Gieblyn's emblem of the genealogy ("dark irony" or "total historical amnesia").

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **O'Gieblyn herself concedes the scale caveat.** When her friend reads the "shrinkage" anecdote as proof of a "fixed and unchangeable" world, O'Gieblyn replies that "the analytics were not really that precise... they were precise only at a very large scale." The performative-loop claim is strongest for institutionally-acted-upon predictions (policing, voting, shipping); a purely passive forecast that no agent acts on would not obviously enact its own outcome.
- **The determinist rejoinder.** A hard determinist could grant the feedback loop yet insist the underlying behavior was fixed anyway, so the prediction merely reveals rather than enacts. O'Gieblyn does not refute determinism; she relocates the question, insisting on "the validity of human morality" rather than disproving the causal chain — closer to Ivan Karamazov's returned "entrance ticket" than to a metaphysical proof.
- **Bias-as-the-real-problem framing.** O'Gieblyn notes that "many of the proposed solutions only confuse the moral parameters of the debate": demands for "less error-prone" or "unbiased" algorithms can license "mission creep" and "dragnet surveillance," since "making better... algorithms inevitably requires more data." The feedback-loop critique cuts against the accuracy framing rather than joining it.
- **Attribution of intent.** O'Gieblyn leaves open "whether the creators of these technologies understand these dynamics or whether they are simply repeating a historical pattern with the mindlessness of the algorithms themselves" — so the predestination genealogy is offered as structural resemblance, not as a claim about deliberate theological design.

## Tensions

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the argument's central stake. Prediction dissolves the line between "individual agency and the inexorable logic of the clickstream," between "desire and fear"; the self-censoring user acts as if freely while having "absorbed the logic of the surveillance state."
- [[Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman]] — Harari's "Dataism" ("Listen to the algorithms! They know how you feel") marks, on O'Gieblyn's reading, "the end of liberal humanism," which "depends on the assumption that an individual knows what is best for herself." Prediction that "know[s] the customer better than she knows herself" undermines the autonomous chooser from outside.
