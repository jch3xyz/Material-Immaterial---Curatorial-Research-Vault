# Transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology

## Claim

Transhumanism — with its promises of bodily resurrection, mind-uploading, immortality, and a Singularity that redeems all of history — is not merely *similar* to Christian resurrection theology but is genealogically descended from it, a secularized eschatology whose lineage runs from medieval alchemy through Fedorov, Teilhard, and Huxley to [[Ray Kurzweil]], even as its outspokenly atheist proponents suppress that ancestry to make the movement appear grounded in pure rationalism.

## Expanded Explanation

O'Gieblyn's signature argument turns on the difference between *analogy* and *descent*. Many critics had already noticed that transhumanism resembles religion; she insists the resemblance is "historical and genetic," that the parallels "bear the fingerprints of human thinkers" who carried these ideas forward, and so cannot be coincidental. [[Ray Kurzweil]], "the academic prophet" of the information age, believes "that the dead would rise, that the earth would be transformed, that humans would become immortal" — the literal content of biblical prophecy, recast as the output of [[Moore's Law]] and the [[The Computational Universe|computational universe]]. The Singularity is a point of "final redemption"; uploading is resurrection; the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] body is the glorified, "incorruptible" body of Pauline epistle.

The genealogy is the load-bearing part of the claim. The very word *transhuman* originates in Dante's coinage *transumanar* in the *Paradiso*, where it names the metamorphosis of mortal flesh into a glorified resurrection body; it slept in the language for centuries before being revived by the Jesuit Pierre Teilhard de Chardin, whose "Omega Point" is "an obvious precursor to Kurzweil's Singularity," and only then by Teilhard's secular-humanist friend Julian Huxley, who in 1957 stripped the religious narrative away and kept the name. Behind these stand the alchemist Roger Bacon, who sought an elixir mimicking the "four dowries" of the resurrected body, and the Russian Orthodox ascetic Nikolai Fedorov, whose program of scientifically resurrecting all the dead from ancestral "dust" prefigures DNA and cloning. The patternist metaphysics that makes uploading thinkable — identity as a [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] of [[Information|information]] persisting through changing matter — even recapitulates Origen of Alexandria's third-century *eidos*, "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter."

The final move is ideological diagnosis: transhumanists *deny* this descent. They are "outspoken atheists, eager to maintain the notion that their philosophy is rooted in modern rationalism." Histories of the movement trace it to Enlightenment humanists and "rarely mention Teilhard as an influence, even though the central ideas of transhumanism were there in his writings." The suppression is structural, not incidental: the movement's claim to be sober empiricism — "without resorting to wishful thinking or mysticism," in Bostrom's words — depends on concealing that it is, in O'Gieblyn's account, what science promised after [[Secularization|disenchantment]] hollowed the world of meaning: a way to "restore through science the transcendent — and essentially religious — hopes that science itself obliterated."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Kurzweil's transhumanist eschatology states the literal content of biblical prophecy — the dead rise, the body is made immortal, history culminates in redemption — only translated into the idiom of computation and [[Mind as Machine|mind-as-machine]]. (Resemblance.)
2. The resemblance is not coincidental but traceable through an unbroken chain of named thinkers: Dante's *transumanar* → Bacon's elixir → Fedorov's scientific resurrection → Origen's *eidos*-pattern → Teilhard's Omega Point → Huxley's 1957 coinage → Kurzweil's Singularity. (Descent, not analogy.)
3. Therefore transhumanism is genealogically *an outgrowth of* Christian eschatology, the same hope expressed "through analogies that were native to each era."
4. Transhumanists are predominantly atheists who insist the movement is rational humanism and erase the theological ancestry from their own histories.
5. This erasure is motivated: the claim to rationalism requires denying the religious genealogy, so the suppression is itself evidence of the descent it conceals.
6. Conclusion: transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology, and its self-presentation as pure rationalism is a disavowal of its own origin.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Genetic fallacy risk.** Even granting the historical chain, descent does not by itself refute the truth or feasibility of transhumanist claims; showing where an idea came from is not showing it false. O'Gieblyn's aim is diagnostic (to "demystify"), not strictly to disprove — but the rhetorical force of the chapter trades on the implication that exposed origins discredit.
- **Convergence vs. transmission.** The materials also support a weaker reading: that recurring existential problems independently generate similar metaphors ("why do the same problems — and even the same metaphors — keep appearing century after century in new form?"). The cybernetic decontextualization of [[Information|information]] at the Macy Conferences, as [[N. Katherine Hayles]] shows, made information "the materialist's substitute for the soul" by an internal logic, not by borrowing from theology — so some resemblance is structural reinvention rather than inheritance.
- **Self-aware proponents.** Kurzweil's own reply to O'Gieblyn concedes an "essential equivalence" between transhumanist and Christian metaphors and frames atheism vs. belief as "a matter of the choice of metaphor" — complicating the charge that transhumanists simply *suppress* the parallel rather than reinterpret it.
- **Scope of "transhumanism."** The claim is strongest for the Kurzweil/Singularity strand; it is weaker for transhumanist projects (life-extension medicine, cognitive enhancement) that make no eschatological promises.

## Related Arguments

- [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]] — Hayles' historical argument that information's separation from the body was a contingent construction supplies the mechanism by which "the materialist's substitute for the soul" became available; transhumanist transcendence depends on it.
- [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]] — the embodiment counter-tradition against which transhumanist uploading is the foil; uploading is the purest dream of "the mind as pure information, pure spirit."
- [[The liberal subject and the posthuman both erase embodiment]] — situates transhumanism within the broader posthuman erasure of the body that O'Gieblyn's "darker irony" passage echoes.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — transhumanist resurrection stakes everything on liberating mind from matter, "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form"; its theological ancestor (intact-body resurrection) lay on the *material* pole, making the lineage itself a migration across this tension.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the eschatological hope is realized only by recoding the self as [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] rather than [[Embodiment|embodied]] flesh; the argument exposes how a religious promise about *bodies* was converted into a claim about *information*.
