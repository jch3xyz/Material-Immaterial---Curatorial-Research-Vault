# Every extension of man is an autoamputation that numbs perception

## Claim

For McLuhan, every invention or technology is an [[Autoamputation|autoamputation]] — a self-protective severing by which the central nervous system isolates an over-stressed organ, sense, or function — and the amplification of that isolated function is bearable to the nervous system only through a numbness that makes the user blind to the very extension he is using. The wisdom of the Narcissus myth is exactly this: Narcissus was numbed not by self-love but by failing to recognize his reflection as an extension of himself.

## Expanded Explanation

Chapter 4 of *Understanding Media* is the load-bearing physiological argument of the book. McLuhan imports the stress physiology of the medical researchers Hans Selye and Adolphe Jonas, for whom "all extensions of ourselves, in sickness or in health, are attempts to maintain equilibrium" and any such extension is an "autoamputation," a strategy "resorted to by the body when the perceptual power cannot locate or avoid the cause of irritation." He is explicit that this transposition is his own move and not theirs: "it was no part of the intention of Jonas and Selye to provide an explanation of human invention and technology," yet "they have given us a theory of disease (discomfort) that goes far to explain why man is impelled to extend various parts of his body by a kind of autoamputation." `Directly stated`

The mechanism is generalized from disease to invention in a single sentence: "In the physical stress of superstimulation of various kinds, the central nervous system acts to protect itself by a strategy of amputation or isolation of the offending organ, sense, or function. Thus, the stimulus to new invention is the stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load." Invention is therefore not curiosity or improvement but defense — the nervous system offloading an over-loaded function to keep its equilibrium. McLuhan's worked example is the wheel: under "the acceleration of exchange by written and monetary media," the foot is amputated and re-extended as the rotating wheel, which then "brings about a new intensity of action by its amplification of a separate or isolated function (the feet in rotation)." `Directly stated`

This is where numbness enters as a structural necessity, not an accident. The amplified, isolated function cannot be tolerated as such: "Such amplification is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception. This is the sense of the Narcissus myth." The numbness is the price of the extension — the condition of being able to use it at all. McLuhan reads Narcissus against the popular interpretation: the youth did not fall in love with himself; "the wisdom of the Narcissus myth does not convey any idea that Narcissus fell in love with anything he regarded as himself," and "obviously he would have had very different feelings about the image had he known it was an extension or repetition of himself." He was the "servomechanism of his own extended or repeated image," and "self-amputation forbids self-recognition" — the severed extension returns as an image whose origin in the self has been numbed out of perception. The narcotic culture that misreads the myth as self-love is itself an instance of the numbness it fails to see. `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. Stress physiology (Selye, Jonas): every extension of the body is an "autoamputation," a self-protective gesture to maintain equilibrium when the body cannot locate or avoid an irritant.
2. McLuhan's transposition: under "the stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load," the central nervous system protects itself by amputating or isolating the offending organ, sense, or function — and *this* is the stimulus to new invention.
3. So every technology is an extension that is simultaneously a self-amputation (the wheel amputates the foot, etc.).
4. The isolated function is then amplified; but "such amplification is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception."
5. Therefore the use of an extension *requires* being numb to it — the numbness is structural, not optional.
6. The Narcissus myth states this: numbed by his self-amputated image, Narcissus could not recognize it as his own extension. "Self-amputation forbids self-recognition." He was numbed by non-recognition, not by self-love.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Selye/Jonas stress physiology of autoamputation as equilibrium-maintenance, explicitly named as a theory of disease repurposed to explain invention ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/07 Chapter 4 - The Gadget Lover - Narcissus as Narcosis]]).
- The central-nervous-system mechanism: amputation/isolation of the offending function under "the stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load."
- The wheel as worked case: the foot amputated under accelerating exchange, re-extended and amplified as rotation, bearable "only through numbness."
- The re-reading of the Narcissus myth: against the self-love interpretation, the numbness of non-recognition — "Self-amputation forbids self-recognition."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument rests on an analogy McLuhan himself flags as unauthorized by its sources — Selye and Jonas never intended their disease physiology as a theory of technology. The leap from organic self-amputation to cultural invention is asserted rather than demonstrated. `Interpretive synthesis`
- If numbness is the necessary condition of using any extension, it is unclear how McLuhan's own diagnosis — or the artist who "can correct the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures" (Ch. 7) — escapes the numbness it describes. The claim risks being self-undermining or, at best, requiring a special exemption for media study itself.
- "Acceleration of pace and increase of load" is a single causal story for all invention; it leaves little room for technologies adopted for surplus, play, or desire rather than relief of stress (though McLuhan partly anticipates this with "pleasure is a counter-irritant").
