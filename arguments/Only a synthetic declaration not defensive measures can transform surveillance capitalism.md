# Only a synthetic declaration not defensive measures can transform surveillance capitalism

## Claim

Defensive counter-declarations such as encryption, privacy tools, opt-out, and arguments for "data ownership" leave surveillance capitalism's offending facts intact and thereby paradoxically contribute to their legitimacy; only a [[The Counter-Declaration and the Synthetic Declaration|synthetic declaration]] that asserts an alternative framework can transform those facts and change the game.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff distinguishes two broad forms that the withdrawal of social agreement from [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance capitalism]] can take. The first, the *counter-declaration*, is a defensive measure: encryption, privacy tools, or claims to "data ownership." Such measures may help in discrete situations, but they accept the persistence of the very facts they resist and so, in acknowledging them, lend them legitimacy. The paradigmatic case is the opt-out: "if I 'opt out' of tracking, I opt out for me, but my action does not challenge or alter the offending practice." Because surveillance capitalism founds itself by [[Conquest by Declaration|declaration]], a merely defensive response operates inside the game its adversary has already declared.

The second form is the *synthetic declaration*. Zuboff frames the contrast in the idiom of chess: if the declaration is "check," the counter-declaration is "checkmate," but the synthetic declaration *changes the game*. It does not defend a position within the existing framework; it asserts an alternative framework that transforms the opposing facts. Counter-declarations make life more tolerable while we bide our time, but only a synthetic alternative vision can transform raw surveillance capitalism toward a digital future "that we can call home." This is why Zuboff insists that defensive privacy measures, however necessary, cannot by themselves be the answer.

Zuboff illustrates the distinction with the Berlin Wall. From 1961 through the early 1980s, East Berliners carved seventy-one escape tunnels beneath the city, letting several hundred people flee to the West. These tunnels were testament to the *necessity* of counter-declarations, but they "did not bring down the wall or the power that sustained it." What brought down the wall was the synthetic declaration that gathered force over decades and found full expression on the night of November 9, 1989, when a crowd certain of itself and officials unsure of themselves produced the answer "We will." The tunnels evaded the wall; the synthetic declaration dissolved the framework that made the wall possible. This is the structural lesson Zuboff applies to surveillance capitalism: escape is not transformation.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Withdrawal of agreement from surveillance capitalism takes two forms: the counter-declaration and the synthetic declaration. (Directly stated)
2. Counter-declarations (encryption, privacy tools, "data ownership," opt-out) are *defensive* and *individual* — they protect the actor without altering the offending practice. (Directly stated)
3. By accepting the persistence of the opposing facts, counter-declarations acknowledge those facts and thus paradoxically contribute to their legitimacy. (Directly stated)
4. The synthetic declaration instead asserts an alternative framework that transforms the opposing facts — it "changes the game" rather than defending a move within it. (Directly stated)
5. The Berlin Wall's seventy-one escape tunnels (counter-declarations) did not bring down the wall; the synthetic declaration ("We will") did. (Directly stated)
6. Therefore only a synthetic declaration, not defensive measures, can transform raw surveillance capitalism into a digital future we can call home. (Directly stated)

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The two-form typology of disagreement, with the chess figure ("check" / "checkmate" / changing the game). (Ch. 11, "The Right to the Future Tense")
- The opt-out example: opting out protects only the individual and "does not challenge or alter the offending practice." (Ch. 11)
- The Berlin Wall: the seventy-one tunnels carved from 1961 through the early 1980s as the emblem of the counter-declaration's necessity *and* insufficiency. (Ch. 11)
- The fall of the wall on November 9, 1989 — Harald Jäger opening the Bornholmer Street gates, twenty thousand surging across — as the synthetic declaration's full expression in the historian's "We will." (Ch. 11)
- Karl Polanyi's prophecy that unchecked market dynamics annihilate what they aim to buy and sell, invoked to frame why "our own synthetic declarations" are needed against surveillance capitalism. (Ch. 11, "Prophecy")

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Zuboff does not dismiss counter-declarations: she calls the tunnels "testament to the necessity of counter-declarations" and grants that defensive measures "may be effective in discrete situations" and "make life more tolerable." The argument is about sufficiency, not worth.
- The synthetic declaration's content is, within this chapter, gestured at rather than fully specified — its concrete programmatic form (new rights, new collective institutions) is developed across later chapters; the chapter establishes the *form* of the needed response more than its full substance. (Strongly implied)
- The Berlin Wall analogy is illustrative, not a demonstration: that a synthetic declaration toppled the wall does not by itself prove one can transform an economic logic embedded in markets and infrastructure. (Interpretive synthesis)
