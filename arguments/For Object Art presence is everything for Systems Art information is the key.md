# For Object Art presence is everything for Systems Art information is the key

## Claim

For Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art the key factor is "information" — the unit of art shifts from the irreplaceable located object to a replicable, maintained, blueprinted set of instructions.

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham draws the contrast at the level of what *is* the work. With [[Object Art]] the artwork is coextensive with a singular physical body, so its identity, value, and survival hang on the preservation of that one mass of matter in place. With Systems Art — Burnham's [[Object and System|systems]] orientation, the program of [[Systems Esthetics]] — the artwork is carried by information that can be stockpiled, replaced, transmitted, and reconstructed; the physical instantiation becomes contingent. This is the move the vault tracks as [[Dematerialization]] and as a case of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension: the locus of the work passes from material presence to immaterial pattern.

He grounds the shift in maintenance practice. A systems exhibit "is expected to wear out": breakable parts are stockpiled against a predicted rate of loss, exactly as in production-line automobile maintenance, and "the stockpiling of parts according to need — as the body changes cells every few days or weeks — is an essential tenet of the systems philosophy." The organic metaphor is precise: as a living body persists through cellular turnover, the systems work persists through the continual replacement of its components. What endures is not the matter but the organizing [[Information|information]] — and "an entire assembly can be reduced to blueprint form for future reconstruction," whose "organic parallel, of course, is the genetic encoding of hereditary traits."

The two poles dramatize the difference. Michelangelo's Pietà, place-oriented Object sculpture, had to be sealed "in a series of containers surrounded by an enormous amount of shock-absorbing material" for a double ocean voyage because the irreplaceable crystalline mass might carry a hidden fracture. Against this Burnham sets Robert Morris, who, asked to submit work to a 1966 Chicago show, "sent plans from which the carpenters at the museum constructed two gray L-shaped plywood forms" — the work travels as instructions, not as cargo, and "the step beyond this" is to exhibit the plans themselves and invite the public to "imagine" the sculpture. Presence shipped versus information mailed.

## Reasoning Structure

1. With Object Art the work is identical with one physical body, so its value and survival depend on preserving that irreplaceable mass in place (the Pietà, sealed against fracture for its ocean voyage). `Directly stated`
2. With Systems Art the work is an organized system whose parts are stockpiled and replaced against predicted wear — "as the body changes cells" — so what persists is the organizing information, not the matter. `Directly stated`
3. Because the system can be "reduced to blueprint form for future reconstruction," the work can be transmitted as instructions (Morris's mailed L-beam plans), even reduced to the exhibited plans themselves. `Directly stated`
4. Therefore the unit of art shifts from located physical presence to replicable, maintained, blueprinted information: "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." `Directly stated`

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself notes the limit on the dichotomy: even a systems work "is a physical presence," and "increasingly the very preservation of art objects depends upon the uses of safety and atmosphere control systems" — the object pole already leans on systems, so the contrast is a shift of emphasis, not an absolute break.
- The claim concerns where the *identity* of the work resides; it does not assert that Systems Art is immaterial in execution (the stockpiled parts and reconstructed assemblies remain material). The dematerialization is of the locus, not of all matter.
- The Morris example is partly programmatic: Burnham frames the fully blueprinted, "imagine"-the-sculpture step as the next move "of course," signaling a tendency he projects rather than a settled practice of 1966.
