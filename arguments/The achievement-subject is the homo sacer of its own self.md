# The achievement-subject is the homo sacer of its own self

## Claim

Under the self-imposed injunction to achieve [Bann der Leistung], the achievement-subject collapses sovereign and homo sacer into a single person — becoming "the homo sacer of his own self" through an auto-exploitation that Agamben's negativity scheme, with its distinct topological poles of sovereign and bare life, cannot grasp.

## Expanded Explanation

Han takes over Agamben's figure of [[Bare Life]] but re-routes it through the [[Achievement Society]]. Agamben's account, Han argues, remains bound to a "scheme of negativity": sovereign and homo sacer are "clearly distinguished — and topologically, too," occupying "the two extreme limits of the [same] order," and the injunction [Bann] that produces bare life is a *sovereign* injunction issued from a power of exclusion and the state of exception. This is a topology of poles — perpetrator at one end, victim at the other — held apart by negativity, prohibition, and exclusion. `Directly stated`

For Han this topology no longer describes contemporary power. In achievement society the achievement-subject "stands free from external instances of domination forcing it to work and exploiting it. It is subject to no one if not to itself." Freedom and compulsion coincide: the subject "gives itself over to freestanding compulsion in order to maximize performance," so "the exploiter is simultaneously the exploited." Because the same person is "simultaneously perpetrator and victim, master and slave," the two poles Agamben kept apart fold into one. The achievement-subject "that understands itself as its own master, as homo liber, turns out to be homo sacer," and so "the sovereign of achievement society is simultaneously his own homo sacer." `Directly stated`

The hinge is the redefinition of the injunction. The injunction "that makes us all homines sacri is not a sovereign injunction, but the injunction to achieve [Bann der Leistung]" — not a command issued by an external sovereign but a self-imposed imperative the subject mistakes for freedom. This makes [[Auto-Exploitation]] devastatingly efficient: "auto-exploitation is significantly more efficient and brings much greater returns than allo-exploitation, because the feeling of freedom attends it." What Han accuses Agamben of missing is precisely this register: "he cannot grasp the violence of positivity, which expresses itself as the exhaustion and inclusion that characterize the society of achievement." Where Agamben sees exclusion, Han sees *inclusion*; where Agamben sees the deadly exception, Han sees a "totalized state of normality." `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. Agamben's homo sacer holds to a scheme of negativity: sovereign and bare life are distinct, topologically opposed poles, and the injunction that produces bare life is a *sovereign* injunction grounded in exclusion and the state of exception.
2. In achievement society there is no external sovereign — the subject "is subject to no one if not to itself"; freedom and compulsion coincide, so the same person is at once exploiter and exploited, perpetrator and victim, master and slave.
3. The two poles therefore collapse: the achievement-subject who takes itself for homo liber turns out to be homo sacer — the sovereign is "his own homo sacer."
4. The injunction at work is not sovereign but the self-imposed injunction to achieve [Bann der Leistung]; auto-exploitation under it is more efficient than allo-exploitation "because the feeling of freedom attends it."
5. Conclusion: Agamben's negativity-based topology (exclusion, the exception, distinct poles) cannot grasp "the violence of positivity, which expresses itself as the exhaustion and inclusion" of achievement society — only Han's collapse of the poles can.

## Key Definition Folded In

**The injunction to achieve [Bann der Leistung]** — Han's renaming of the Agambenian *Bann* (the sovereign ban/injunction that produces bare life). For Agamben the *Bann* is sovereign: it excludes the homo sacer from the legal order and exposes him to absolute expendability. Han keeps the word but removes the sovereign: the *Bann der Leistung* is the self-imposed imperative to perform, under which the subject "considers itself free and fashions itself as homo liber — its own sovereign and self-made [Unternehmer seiner selbst]" while in fact making "itself into homo sacer." It is an injunction with no one on the issuing end except the subject itself; hence its violence is felt as freedom. `Directly stated`

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument turns on a contestable reading of Agamben. Han charges Agamben with "anachronism" for describing the society of sovereignty "from a standpoint at the middle of the society of achievement"; a defender of Agamben might reply that the camp, Guantanamo, and the stateless asylum-seeker (the very examples Han cites from Agamben in `05 Vita Activa`) remain live forms of *exclusionary* sovereign violence that Han's universal "we are all homines sacri without exception" risks flattening. `Interpretive synthesis`
- Han's own move generalizes homo sacer to *everyone* ("all of us, without exception, who are homines sacri"), which loosens the term from its juridical specificity — the bare life of achievement society "cannot be killed at all," is "undead," "too alive to die, and too dead to live," a metaphorical extension rather than Agamben's literal exposure to lawful killing. `Strongly implied`
- Whether the "feeling of freedom" is genuinely freedom or merely "deceptive" is asserted rather than demonstrated; the claim that auto-exploitation is *more* efficient than allo-exploitation is offered as systemic observation, not measured. `Interpretive synthesis`
