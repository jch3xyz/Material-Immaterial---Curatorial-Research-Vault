# A machine may be more responsive to human needs than another person

## Claim

In the domain of interaction and sensitivity to human needs a machine may be more effective than another person, and under commercial sponsorship machines are becoming the accepted surrogate for human attention and affection.

## Expanded Explanation

[[Jack Burnham]], in the "Sculpture and Automata" chapter of *[[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]*, advances a claim that is at once economic and affective. Economically, the machine wins on cost and programmability: in the debate over teaching machines, he predicts that "machines may win out because it is cheaper and more efficient to mass produce teaching machines with effective programs than to train sufficient quantities of good teachers." The machine is reproducible, programmable, and standardizable in a way the good teacher is not, so the logic of mass production favors it. (`Directly stated`)

The affective dimension is the more disquieting one. Burnham frames the machine not merely as a labor-saving instrument but as a competitor for the relational goods we ordinarily seek from other people — attention, responsiveness, affection. Backed by "enormous commercial sponsorship and methods of enticement," the machine becomes "the accepted surrogate for human attention and affection." For those who find human relations unbearable, it offers "an escape route." (`Directly stated`) This reframes the machine as an answer to a human need for non-threatening responsiveness rather than as a threat to human labor.

This argument directly grounds Burnham's larger program of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — art conceived as entering into reciprocal relationships with human beings — by establishing that a constructed apparatus can be responsive, sensitive, and relationally adequate. It also supplies the diagnostic backdrop for [[Joey the Mechanical Boy]]: the boy who becomes a machine to escape unbearable humanity is, for Burnham, the limit case of a culture in which [[The Robot]] is already an accepted surrogate for human attention. (`Strongly implied`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. As psychology and sociology document the alienation of work, literature registers humans gearing themselves to the rhythm and communication style of machines.
2. In interaction and sensitivity to human needs, a machine may therefore be more effective than another person.
3. The teaching-machine debate is the test case: machines are cheaper and more efficient to mass produce with effective programs than good teachers are to train, so in the long run machines may win out.
4. Generalizing beyond instruction: backed by commercial sponsorship and enticement, machines become the accepted surrogate for human attention and affection.
5. Conclusion: for the unprotected, the responsive machine is an available escape route from unbearable human relation.

- The contemporary debate over teaching machines, where the machine's advantage is cost and programmability rather than pedagogy alone — [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]
- Postwar cybernetic "pseudo-organisms" (Grey Walter's tortoises, Ross Ashby's homeostat, [[Claude Shannon]]'s maze-running mice) as adaptive, role-playing automata "tenuously linked with life itself" — establishing that machines can model organic, responsive behavior — [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]
- Bruno Bettelheim's case study "Joey, the Mechanical Boy" — the child who becomes a machine to escape humanity, read by Burnham as a symptom of machines having become the accepted surrogate for attention and affection — [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham hedges with "may be" and "may win out" — the claim is a projection about tendency, not a demonstrated fact; the confidence label per the analysis is medium accordingly.
- The teaching-machine evidence establishes an *economic* superiority (cost, programmability) more securely than an *affective* one; the leap from cheaper instruction to "surrogate for human attention and affection" is asserted rather than argued.
- The closing rhetorical question ("what better escape route is there for the unprotected?") frames the surrogate machine as pathology, via Joey's case — so Burnham's own framing treats the machine-as-surrogate as a danger, not simply an achievement, complicating any straightforward reading of the machine as "more responsive."
- The argument is a mid-1960s prediction conditioned on the cybernetic automata of its moment; it does not specify the threshold of "sensitivity" a machine must reach.
