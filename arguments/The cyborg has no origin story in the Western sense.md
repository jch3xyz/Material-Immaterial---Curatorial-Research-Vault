# The cyborg has no origin story in the Western sense

## Claim

The cyborg has no origin story in the Western sense — it "skips the step of original unity" — so the developmental plots of psychoanalysis (oedipal individuation) and Marxism (alienated labor restored to species-being) cannot bind it; it is illegitimate, unfaithful to its militarist-capitalist fathers, and "would not recognize the Garden of Eden."

## Expanded Explanation

For [[Donna Haraway]], a Western origin story is not merely a tale of where something came from; it is a *structure* — "the myth of original unity, fullness, bliss and terror, represented by the phallic mother from whom all humans must separate." That separation is then narrated as "the task of individual development and of history." The two great modern theories that run on this plot are, she argues following Hilary Klein, psychoanalysis and Marxism: both "depend on the plot of original unity out of which difference must be produced and enlisted in a drama of escalating domination of woman/nature." Psychoanalysis stages it as oedipal individuation out of a lost symbiotic wholeness; Marxism stages it as labor alienated from an original unity and destined to be reunified. The cyborg short-circuits the whole apparatus: it "skips the step of original unity, of identification with nature in the Western sense" (`Directly stated`, ch.01).

Because it has no original unity to fall from, the cyborg has nothing to recover and no Fall to mourn. Haraway makes the point through refused symbols. "Unlike the hopes of Frankenstein's monster, the cyborg does not expect its father to save it through a restoration of the garden"; "The cyborg would not recognize the Garden of Eden; it is not made of mud and cannot dream of returning to dust." The Edenic/biblical origin (mud, dust, the Garden) and the family-romance origin (the father who completes the monster) are both inapplicable. The cyborg "incarnation is outside salvation history," does not "mark time on an oedipal calendar," and "has no truck with bisexuality, pre-oedipal symbiosis, unalienated labor, or other seductions to organic wholeness" — a single list that names the psychoanalytic *and* the Marxist seductions in one breath and refuses both (`Directly stated`, ch.01).

The argument carries a "final" irony Haraway flags herself: the cyborg, lacking a Western origin, is nonetheless "the awful apocalyptic telos of the 'West's' escalating dominations of abstract individuation, an ultimate self untied at last from all dependency, a man in space." It is "the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism, not to mention state socialism" — but "illegitimate offspring are often exceedingly unfaithful to their origins. Their fathers, after all, are inessential." This is the ground of the cyborg's refusal of innocence: with no innocent origin there is no lost purity to ground politics in, and the lack of a faithful genealogy becomes "its illegitimate promise that might lead to subversion of its teleology as Star Wars" (`Directly stated`, ch.01).

## Reasoning Structure

1. A Western/humanist origin story is a specific structure: a myth of original unity from which a subject must separate, narrated as the work of individual development and of history.
2. Psychoanalysis (oedipal individuation) and Marxism (labor alienated from, then restored to, an original unity) are the two great modern theories built on this plot.
3. The cyborg "skips the step of original unity" — it has no prior wholeness, no symbiosis, no unalienated labor to lose or regain.
4. Therefore the developmental/redemptive plots of both psychoanalysis and Marxism have no purchase on it: it is "outside salvation history" and does not "mark time on an oedipal calendar."
5. Lacking an origin, the cyborg lacks a Fall and a Garden — it "would not recognize the Garden of Eden" and does not "expect its father to save it through a restoration of the garden."
6. Its actual parents (militarism, patriarchal capitalism, state socialism) are not redemptive origins but "inessential" fathers to whom it is "exceedingly unfaithful."
7. Conclusion: having no Western origin is precisely the cyborg's "illegitimate promise" — the ground of its refusal of innocence and its capacity to subvert the teleology it was born from.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The refusal of an origin story is itself a *story* — a counter-myth ("an ironic political myth") with its own genealogy (militarism, C3I, the integrated circuit). One could object that Haraway does not abolish the origin plot so much as replace it with an inverted one, trading the Garden for the Pentagon.
- The argument leans on Hilary Klein's interpretation of Marx and Freud rather than on Marx or Freud directly; readers who reject the premise that psychoanalysis and Marxism *must* run on "original unity" (e.g. structuralist or non-Hegelian Marxisms, post-Lacanian readings) would deny that the cyborg escapes anything by skipping a step those theories need not take.
- "Skipping" original unity may understate the cyborg's debt to its origins: Haraway concedes the cyborg is the *telos* of Western abstract individuation, so its freedom from the origin plot is asserted alongside an admission of deep continuity with it ("a final irony").
- The claim is normative as much as descriptive — Haraway *wants* the cyborg to be unfaithful and subversive; whether illegitimate offspring are in fact "exceedingly unfaithful to their origins" is asserted, not demonstrated.
