# The museum controls meaning not merely information

## Claim

Against the view that the museum's job is simply to control information, Ascott argues that it serves — and has always served — to control *meaning*: to disseminate ideology and to shape consciousness, a covert function its "cosmetic" education programmes only obscure.

## Expanded Explanation

In "The Mind of the Museum" (1996) Ascott reframes the debate over the museum's educational role around a sharper distinction. The museum's vast public and private funding, he contends, is "doubtless due to the efficacy of their ability to 'form' rather than inform the viewer." On the surface this looks like a quarrel over information management, but Ascott explicitly rejects that framing: "here I take issue with those who assert that the museum's function is simply to control information, since I believe that in truth it serves and has always served to control meaning, actually to assist in the dissemination of ideology, to shape consciousness." The museum is not a neutral conduit of facts; it is an apparatus of `Ideology` that produces subjects. (Directly stated.)

Ascott grounds this in the very materials of display. "Beauty and even decoration have never been semantically innocent, value-free or without significant form — every canvas, mural and architectural ornament being soaked in semiosis." Control of meaning is therefore exercised through "the semiology of [museum] architecture, the despotism of commission and omission of the paintings on the wall." He aligns this reading with `Michel Foucault` and Donald Horne, who "have both exposed the autocratic subtext of museum mediation," and distinguishes the French *formation* (forming/shaping consciousness, hidden in English as "training") from Maturana and Varela's `Autopoiesis` (self-creation). The traditional museum chooses formation: it tells us "there is a past to which we should attend" and that "we should read its authorized meanings." (Directly stated.)

Diagnosed this way, the museum is a casualty of "the values of the old industrial culture, a culture of exclusivity, compartmentalization, of the unified self, of anxiety, alienation, excessive individualism, privacy and secrecy, a society of paranoia." Its failure is not a lack of better content but its function as a meaning-control machine. Reform — "to rearrange the furniture of the museum or replace the fittings" or "the simple conversion of real estate to the virtual state" — cannot fix this. The remedy Ascott proposes is ontological: the museum "must become intelligent, must get smart," must "have a life of its own, that thinks for itself," embodying "what properly should be called its hypercortex." We must "stop thinking for the museum and allow it to think for itself" — the basis for [[The Museum of the Third Kind]]. (Strongly implied / Directly stated.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The museum receives its support because it can *form* (shape consciousness/behaviour), not merely *inform* — formation, not information, is its real product. (Directly stated.)
2. Therefore the claim that its function is "to control information" is too weak; what it actually controls is *meaning* — it disseminates ideology and shapes consciousness. (Directly stated.)
3. This control is structural and inescapable: beauty and decoration are never "semantically innocent"; it operates through architecture and the "despotism of commission and omission" on the wall. (Directly stated — corroborated by Foucault and Horne.)
4. The museum fell into "a society of paranoia" by adopting the values of industrial culture (exclusivity, the unified self, privacy, secrecy). (Directly stated.)
5. Because meaning-control is intrinsic to the institution, cosmetic reform cannot redeem it; it must instead become a self-thinking `Hypercortex` — a brain that "thinks for itself," celebrating connectivity (`Telenoia`) over paranoia. (Strongly implied.)

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The funding rationale: museums are supported "due to the efficacy of their ability to 'form' rather than inform the viewer." (Directly stated.)
- The "soaked in semiosis" argument: no canvas, mural, or ornament is semantically innocent; control runs through "the semiology of [museum] architecture, the despotism of commission and omission of the paintings on the wall." (Directly stated.)
- The *formation* vs `Autopoiesis` (Maturana and Varela) distinction — the museum chooses to "form" rather than enable self-creation. (Directly stated.)
- `Michel Foucault` (citing his *The Order of Things*) and Donald Horne, who "have both exposed the autocratic subtext of museum mediation" — the museum proposes there is a past with coherence, then supplies "its authorized meanings." (Directly stated.)
- Myron Krueger's "disenchantment with curatorial myopia" — the same blind spot let museums overlook a decade of [[Telematic Art]]. (Directly stated.)
- The "society of paranoia" diagnosis: the museum went wrong "when it adopted the values of the old industrial culture." (Directly stated.)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Ascott himself concedes exceptions — "With some well known and outstanding exceptions" and "there are exceptions in the museum world" — so the claim of universal meaning-control is hedged in practice. (Directly stated.)
- This is a keynote-address polemic ("recklessly speculating with irresponsible optimism!"), not an empirical study; the indictment rests on assertion and on the borrowed authority of Foucault and Horne rather than on a survey of institutions. (Interpretive synthesis.)
- The proposed cure (a self-thinking museum-brain, "a chip in my brain rather than a chip on my shoulder") relocates rather than dissolves the question of *who* shapes consciousness: a "bottom-up, distributed" Net "with no global controller" may still encode ideology, only diffusely — see [[Liberation vs Control (Telematics)]]. (Interpretive synthesis.)
- A defender of the museum could reply that authorized meaning and curatorial selection are conditions of intelligibility, not domination — that omission is unavoidable in any finite collection. Ascott does not engage this directly. (Speculative connection.)

## Tensions

- [[Visibility vs Understanding]] — the museum makes objects *visible* and authoritative while covertly governing what they *mean*; display is a technology of consciousness, not transparency.
- [[Liberation vs Control (Telematics)]] — Ascott opposes the paranoid control-museum to the telenoiac, connective museum-of-the-third-kind, yet a distributed Net is not automatically free of ideological control.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — meaning-control is tied to the museum's privileging of "material artifacts (concrete instances)"; Ascott's exit is into the immaterial, the "interspace between material and immaterial worlds."
