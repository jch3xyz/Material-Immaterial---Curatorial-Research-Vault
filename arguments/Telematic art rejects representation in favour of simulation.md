# Telematic art rejects representation in favour of simulation

## Claim

For Roy Ascott, "[t]elematic art rejects representation in favour of simulation." Representation belongs to "the old order": it presupposes the discrete art object, "a fixed separation of creator and observer," and "coherence in space and time" — and so produces "an art of assertion" and an "essentially passive" observing subject, while "mask[ing] the inherent instability, the chaos, of process in the world." Simulation, by contrast, seeks "to simulate in its digital space-time the unstable, chaotic, fleeting, and numinous quality of its subject," substituting "negotiation, participation, collaboration" for assertion. (*Directly stated*.)

## Expanded Explanation

This is the keystone argument of Ascott's 1990 essay "Beyond Time-Based Art: ESP, PDP, and PU." Having spent the chapter triangulating media, computer science, and the parallel-universes interpretation of quantum physics to "locate" a new, networked time, Ascott arrives at a single thesis about the *mode* of telematic art. The opposition is not between two styles but between two ontologies of the artwork. Representation is bound to the metaphysics of the stable object and the autonomous, sovereign viewer; it "makes demands on art as object," requires the work to be "determinate, coherent, and consistent," and "demands coherence in space and time between the work itself and the subject of its representation." Because it insists on "clarity and coherence," representation cannot admit "[p]rinciples of uncertainty, indeterminacy, flux and flow" — and so it falsifies a world that, on the account of the new physics Ascott has just rehearsed, *is* uncertainty, indeterminacy, flux and flow. Representation thus "always masks the inherent instability, the chaos, of process in the world."

Simulation is Ascott's name for the alternative. Where representation *re-presents* a stable subject to a passive receiver, simulation *reproduces the process itself* — its instability, its chaos, its numinous quality — in "the digital space-time of the computer." This is why the shift is simultaneously a shift in the subject's role: an art that simulates unstable process cannot be received passively but must be entered, negotiated, and co-constructed. Hence the substitution of "negotiation, participation, collaboration" for "the mode of assertion," and the matching collapse of Ascott's spectator into the "user" who is "neither autonomous creator nor passive viewer." The art of representation "led inexorably to representations of representation itself, a suicidal art of solipsistic inversion" — Ascott's verdict on "deconstructivist postmodernism"; simulation is offered as the constructive way out. Crucially, telematic art's simulation is not the imitation of appearances but the rendering-visible of the invisible: "telematic art, moving beyond object art and time-based art, uses simulation to render what is invisible visible, to bring the virtual, the potential, the unseen, and unrealised into view."

The argument is the network-and-participation inflection of two deeper spines in the vault. It is the participatory continuation of Jack Burnham's object→system thesis: Ascott's "moving beyond object art" carries Burnham's [[Object and System]] passage into the telematic, asynchronic register, so the argument anchors [[Object vs System]] and the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension from the immaterial, data-flow pole. (*Interpretive synthesis* — Burnham is not named in this chapter.) And the assertion/passivity-versus-negotiation/participation contrast restates Ascott's lifelong demand for a two-way, behaviourist art, mapping onto [[Reciprocal Communication]] and [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]]. The keystone opposition itself is carried by the [[Representation vs Simulation]] tension, and the positive pole by the concept [[Simulation (Ascott)]] — Ascott's *aesthetic* sense of simulation (a mode that reproduces numinous process), which must be kept distinct from the cosmological "we live in a computer simulation" thesis catalogued elsewhere in the vault as [[The Simulation Hypothesis]]. (*Directly stated* for the art-mode; the distinction is *Interpretive synthesis*.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Representation presupposes the art object, "a fixed separation of creator and observer," and "coherence in space and time between the work itself and the subject of its representation."
2. To meet these demands, representation must be "determinate, coherent, and consistent" — it "produces an art of assertion" and requires "the observing subject [to] be essentially passive."
3. But the world (as the new physics shows) *is* "uncertainty, indeterminacy, flux and flow," which "have no place in an art of representation"; so representation "always masks the inherent instability, the chaos, of process."
4. Telematic art's subject is precisely this "unstable, chaotic, fleeting, and numinous" process; representation cannot honour it.
5. Simulation can: it reproduces the unstable process itself "in its digital space-time," and so replaces assertion with "negotiation, participation, collaboration" and the passive observer with the active user.
6. Therefore telematic art "rejects representation in favour of simulation."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The simulation/representation opposition is asserted, not demonstrated.** Ascott offers two contrasting *definitions* and a strong rhetorical antithesis; a sceptic may hold that all media, including networked ones, ultimately re-present, and that "simulation" is itself a sophisticated mode of representation rather than its overcoming. (*Speculative connection*.)
- **"Simulation" is a loaded word elsewhere.** In Baudrillard's lineage simulation names the *loss* of the real (the precession of simulacra), a pessimistic verdict; Ascott reclaims it as a *constructive*, world-disclosing aesthetic. The same term carries opposite valences across the vault — see the disambiguation against [[The Simulation Hypothesis]]. (*Interpretive synthesis*.)
- **The "passive observer" charge against representation is a polemical caricature** of reception that much art theory would contest; Ascott deploys it as the foil for participation rather than defending it on its own terms.
- **Hope, not proof.** The argument's force is partly prophetic ("we can hope for the emergence of a radically constructive art"); whether networked simulation in practice escapes the assertive, "aggressive, consuming, demanding" culture it diagnoses is left as aspiration.
