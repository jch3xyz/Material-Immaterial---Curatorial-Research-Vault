# As art accepts technology it depends increasingly on the engineer

## Claim

As art accepts the technological scale of values and looks to electronic know-how to overcome its own inadequacy, it requires ever more outside engineering help — a new artist-engineer symbiosis in which the engineer may become "more than an equal partner."

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham argues that the kinetic and systems art of the twentieth century has committed Western art to a technological direction that artists themselves are not equipped to realize. The recurring weakness of Kinetic Art, on his account, is its makers' "comparative mechanical incompetence": kinetic artists have leaned on the contributed help of "sympathetic technicians," and even trained engineers have not, on their own, produced superior kinetic work. The consequence is structural rather than incidental — once art accepts "the technological scale of values," dependence on the engineer is built into the project itself. This realizes the precursor argument of [[Conceptual Art]] and [[New Media Art]]: the art object increasingly demands competence that belongs to the laboratory, not the studio.

Burnham frames this not as a deficiency to be lamented but as the founding condition of a "new and growing symbiosis." Reporting Billy Kluver's January 1966 remarks to the College Art Association of America, he records the claim that engineers are "not visionaries," so the artist still determines what must be done — yet "the artist will find it increasingly difficult to realize his visions without the aid of his practical advisers." The decisive move is Burnham's qualification: "there are already indications that the engineer may become more than an equal partner with the artist, depending upon the technical complexity of the projects under consideration." Authorship itself becomes distributed across the partnership.

This is the argumentative ground on which Burnham narrates the realization of [[New Media Art]] and its institutions. The "9 evenings: theatre and engineering" (fall 1966) is offered as the first large-scale pooling of the talents of engineers, artists, and dancers; the founding of E.A.T. (Experiments in Art and Technology, Inc., January 1967) institutionalizes the symbiosis as a "matching agency" between artists with feasible projects and engineers competent to solve them. The stated *raison d'être* of E.A.T. — "a work being created that was the preconception of neither the artist nor the engineer alone" — pushes the claim to its limit: the engineer is not merely an assistant to a prior artistic vision but a co-author of works neither party could have conceived alone. Burnham also notes the looming downside: a "technology gap" between subsidized and unsubsidized artists, as the rising cost of engineering favors those who can pay for it.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Kinetic and systems art commit Western art to producing work using "the parameters of real time and motion" and, increasingly, electronics — a technological scale of values.
2. Artists are characteristically "mechanically incompetent"; their kinetic facility "has usually been the result of technical assistance contributed by sympathetic technicians."
3. Therefore, the more art accepts the technological scale of values, "the more outside help it will require."
4. Because engineers are "not visionaries," the artist still determines what must be done — but cannot realize it without the engineer.
5. As technical complexity grows, the engineer "may become more than an equal partner"; in the limit (E.A.T.), the work is "the preconception of neither the artist nor the engineer alone."
6. This symbiosis is the condition of possibility for New Media Art and its institutions ("9 evenings," 1966; E.A.T., 1967), and it generates a "technology gap" stratifying artists by access to engineering.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself notes that "even the engineer with his superior training has so far not produced superior Kinetic Art, usually the opposite" — superior engineering does not guarantee superior art, complicating any claim that more engineering simply yields better work.
- The "9 evenings" was "pretty much written off as an avant-garde catastrophe by the popular press" (numerous technical breakdowns, lack of rehearsal); the symbiosis is asserted by participants more than demonstrated by the events' reception.
- E.A.T.'s own first-year findings were sobering: it "cannot grant materials or money," and there are "substantial blocks, both psychological and intellectual," among engineers and industry — so the symbiosis is aspirational and institutionally fragile.
- The "technology gap" is a self-limiting tendency: dependence on engineers risks restricting advanced art to the commercially successful, narrowing rather than broadening who can realize it.
