# Taming surveillance capitalism must begin with careful naming

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism can only be confronted once it is named precisely on its own terms, because the inherited mental models, vocabularies, and tools of the past obstruct understanding the way obsolete cures fail against a genuinely novel disease.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff frames the entire project of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* as an act of diagnosis before cure. She borrows the logic of vaccine research as an explicit analogy: a vaccine cannot be engineered until the pathogen is understood on its own terms, and decades of HIV research stalled precisely because scientists kept applying "the logic of earlier cures" to a virus that "defy[s] the patterns of other infectious diseases." The lesson she draws is epistemic, not merely tactical — the failure was a failure of naming, of forcing a new phenomenon into old categories.

Applied to surveillance capitalism, the argument is that piecemeal responses — "a fine paid here, an operational detour there, a new encryption package there" — amount to "hurling snowballs at a smooth marble wall," because they treat the new economic logic as a variant of familiar harms (a privacy breach, a monopoly, a software flaw) rather than as `The Unprecedented`. Old vocabularies make us "smell smoke and rush to close doors to rooms that are already fated to vanish." The remedy is conceptual precision: "we identify this new form of capitalism on its own terms and in its own words." Careful naming is therefore not preliminary to the political fight but constitutive of it; the book's coinages — `Surveillance Capitalism` itself, behavioral surplus, instrumentarian power — are the vaccine's first ingredient.

This positions the entire book as a work of nomenclature in service of contestation: to slow the action down, "enlarge the space for such debate," and make the new logic legible enough that "we who must make it so" can decide who decides. The claim that the digital future "is to be our home" only if we name and master its enemy disease is the throughline that justifies the book's taxonomic ambition.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Effective remedy (a vaccine) depends on a close understanding of the threat (the enemy disease) on its own terms.
2. Inherited mental models, vocabularies, and tools — "distilled from past catastrophes" — actively obstruct that understanding when the threat is unprecedented.
3. Surveillance capitalism is such an unprecedented threat, so applying old categories (privacy, monopoly, technology) yields only ineffective, glancing responses ("snowballs at a smooth marble wall").
4. Therefore taming it must begin with identifying it "on its own terms and in its own words" — with careful, original naming.
5. Naming the new logic precisely is the precondition for collective deliberation and democratic control: "We will need to know. We will need to decide. We will need to decide who decides."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The vaccine analogy casts surveillance capitalism as a pathogen to be eradicated; a critic could note that this framing presumes a healthy "host" (markets, democracy, the self) prior to infection, foreclosing the possibility that the conditions enabling it are endemic rather than alien.
- Naming is necessary but not sufficient: precise vocabulary does not by itself confer the regulatory, economic, or collective power to act, and the book's later chapters concede how asymmetric that power has become.
- The emphasis on the "unprecedented" risks underweighting genuine continuities with prior regimes of accumulation and control — a tension Zuboff manages but does not dissolve.

## Cross-note: the immunological figure (Zuboff and Han)

Both Zuboff and Han reach for the language of immunity, but to opposite analytic ends, so the link is drawn cautiously. Zuboff deploys the *immune system / vaccine* literally and prescriptively: surveillance capitalism is an "enemy disease" against which we must develop a precise countermeasure. Han's `Immunological Age`, by contrast, is a periodizing diagnosis — the immunological paradigm of self-versus-other defense belongs, for Han, to a *past* epoch superseded by a "neuronal" regime of excess positivity in which there is no longer an enemy/Other to repel. Zuboff thus invokes the immunological model as the right tool for the present moment; Han treats it as a model the present has outgrown. The shared figure (immunity, defense, the foreign agent) is therefore a point of resonance and divergence, not agreement — a `Speculative connection` between the two corpora rather than a claim that either author endorses the other's framing.
