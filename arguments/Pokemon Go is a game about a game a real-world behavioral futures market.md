# Pokemon Go is a game about a game a real-world behavioral futures market

## Claim

Pokémon Go is a game about a game: the visible augmented-reality treasure hunt herds players' bodies through the real world in order to constitute a hidden second game board whose real players are the paying merchants — the "sponsored locations" — to whom Niantic guarantees foot traffic on a cost-per-visit basis, extending surveillance capitalism's online logic into physical reality.

## Expanded Explanation

For Zuboff, Pokémon Go is the moment surveillance capitalism's online playbook is ported, intact, into the street. The players believe they are hunting virtual creatures; in fact they are the herd being driven across "real-world terrains to spend their real-world money in the real-world commercial establishments" of what she calls Niantic's "flesh-and-blood behavioral futures markets." There are two games stacked on top of each other. The first — the one you see — is a free treasure hunt running on GPS and the smartphone camera. The second — the one that pays — is a market in [[Prediction Products]] sold to merchants who bid for proximity to the herd. The first game exists to actuate the second. *(Confidence: Directly stated — "a higher-order game of surveillance capitalism, a game about a game.")*

The mechanism is [[Economies of Action]]. Where Facebook's contagion experiments proved that behavior could be *tuned* through suggestion, Pokémon Go proved it could be *herded* through space: the game's rules, rewards, and social dynamics "prod and herd people across real-world terrains," foreclosing alternatives and "moving behavior along a path of heightened probability that approximates certainty." Niantic founder John Hanke — previously of Keyhole, Google Earth, and Street View — built the game precisely to "turn the world into your game board," nudging players toward locations chosen not by them but by paying sponsors. Lure Modules, gyms, and Pokéstops are not game features so much as monetization checkpoints. *(Confidence: Directly stated.)*

What makes this an *argument* rather than a description is Zuboff's claim that the "sponsored locations" euphemism conceals a structural economic identity: this is the same logic that drove [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance capitalism]] online, now made physical. Hanke himself explained that sponsors "pay us to be locations within the virtual game board... the premise being that it is an inducement that drives foot traffic," charged on a "cost per visit" basis "similar to the 'cost per click' used in Google's search advertising." The migration from cost-per-click to cost-per-visit is the whole point: [[The Right to the Future Tense|the future tense]] of bodies in space is now auctioned exactly as clicks once were. The harm Zuboff names is not that players are tricked into walking around, but that their movement through the physical world has been quietly rendered as raw material and sold toward [[The Prediction Imperative|guaranteed outcomes]]. *(Confidence: Directly stated — the cost-per-visit/cost-per-click parallel is verbatim.)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. Economies of action require not just predicting behavior but *actuating* it — tuning, herding, and conditioning people toward profitable outcomes (premise, established earlier in Ch.10 via the Facebook experiments).
2. A real-world game can herd bodies through physical space the way Facebook's social cues herd attention online — Ingress (2012) was the test bed; Pokémon Go (2016) the proof at global scale.
3. The players believe the game's purpose is catching Pokémon (the visible game).
4. But the game's actual purpose is to drive players to locations whose owners pay Niantic to be on the board (the hidden game) — "sponsored locations" charged "cost per visit," explicitly modeled on Google's "cost per click."
5. Therefore the real players are the paying merchants, and the human players are the *game board* — "a wholly different kind of human game board for this second and more consequential game."
6. Conclusion: Pokémon Go is "a game about a game" — it transposes surveillance capitalism's online means of behavioral modification into physical reality, constituting a real-world behavioral futures market.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Efficacy / novelty question.** Location-based advertising and footfall-driving promotions predate Pokémon Go; a skeptic could argue Niantic merely repackaged loyalty-program nudges, not a new *species* of market. Zuboff's reply is structural: the difference is the automated, sub-awareness herding of bodies toward auctioned outcomes, not the existence of promotions per se. *(Limitation — Strongly implied.)*
- **The game faded.** "The zeal for Pokémon Go gradually diminished," which a critic might read as the model failing rather than proving itself. Zuboff treats the commercial decline as irrelevant to the *proof of concept*: the game "demonstrated that it was possible to achieve economies of action on a global scale." The claim is about the discovery of a capability, not the durability of one app.
- **Agency residue.** As with [[The goal is no longer to know our behavior but to automate us]], "herding" presumes targets who can still bolt; the argument is strongest as a description of an economic *imperative* and direction of travel, weaker as a claim that players are fully scripted.

## Tensions

- [[Knowledge vs Power]] — the asymmetry is the engine: players know the visible game, Niantic and its customers know (and shape) the hidden one; knowledge of where bodies go becomes the power to move them.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — players experience the game as free play and "adventures on foot," while the rapture of competitive social play is precisely the medium through which "the dreaded friction of individual will voluntarily gives way to game protocols."
- [[Production vs Extraction]] — the game extracts behavioral surplus (granular location graphs) and, beyond extraction, *produces* the foot traffic it sells, collapsing prediction into manufacture.
