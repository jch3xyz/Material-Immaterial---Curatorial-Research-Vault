# Rendition flattens world self and body into equivalent data objects stripped of moral meaning

## Claim

Rendition does not merely record the world; it translates world, self, and body into a single flat flow of equivalent data objects, and in that flatness each rendered bit is liberated from the moral reasoning, politics, norms, rights, relationships, feelings, and contexts that encumbered it in its lived social life — so that all things, animate and inanimate, share the same existential status as an indexable "it."

## Expanded Explanation

For Zuboff, [[Rendition]] is the foundational operation of surveillance capitalism's supply chain: the active process by which lived reality is claimed, computed, and rendered into behavioral data. Chapter 7's "[[The Reality Business|reality business]]" extends this operation from the screen out into the entire material world — bodies, rooms, streets, contact lenses, hospital beds, railway tracks — under the program of rendering "the entire world's actions and conditions as behavioral flows." The crucial move is not surveillance as such but what happens to a thing once it has been rendered: it enters a flow where "data are data, and behavior is behavior."

The argument's force is ontological and ethical at once. In ordinary social life a gesture, a feeling, or a body is inseparable from a thick web of moral and contextual meaning — it has rights attached, situations around it, norms governing it. Rendition severs that web. The rendered bit is "liberated from its life in the social," no longer "encumbered by moral reasoning, politics, social norms, rights, values, relationships, feelings, contexts, and situations." This liberation is precisely the stripping of moral meaning: what was a person in a situation becomes a measurable signal in a stream. The body is reduced to "a set of coordinates in time and space where sensation and action are translated as data" — the same flattening Zuboff's wider account names as [[The Body as Data|the body as data]].

The terminal consequence is an equivalence of existential status. Because the flow recognizes no qualitative difference between a feeling, a railway track, and a contact lens, "all things animate and inanimate share the same existential status in this blended confection, each reborn as an objective and measurable, indexable, browsable, searchable 'it.'" This is [[Dataism]] enacted at the level of metaphysics: the dissolution of the human subject — and of the moral distinctions that subjectivity carries — into an undifferentiated field of indexable objects.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism's instruments aim to render the entire world's actions and conditions as behavioral flows (the reality business extends rendition from screen to world).
2. To enter the flow, a thing must be detached from its lived context — "data are data, and behavior is behavior."
3. That detachment strips the rendered bit of moral reasoning, politics, norms, rights, values, relationships, feelings, contexts, and situations.
4. The body in particular is reduced to coordinates in time and space where sensation and action become data.
5. Therefore world, self, and body are flattened into equivalent data objects; all things, animate and inanimate, share one existential status as an indexable, searchable "it" — morality having been engineered out of the equation.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The IBM "Watson"/"internet of things" program: the explicit corporate ambition to render "everything in the world" — contact lens, hospital bed, railway track — as data (Ch. 7).
- The principle of the flow: "In the flatness of this flow, data are data, and behavior is behavior" (Ch. 7).
- The reduction of the body to "a set of coordinates in time and space" (Ch. 7).
- The terminal equivalence: animate and inanimate things "reborn" as an "objective and measurable, indexable, browsable, searchable 'it'" (Ch. 7).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- A defender of the data economy could argue that abstraction is the condition of all measurement and science, and that rendering the body as coordinates is no more morally corrosive than a doctor reading a chart; Zuboff's reply is that the issue is not abstraction per se but the economic imperative that makes the stripping of moral meaning into a business model and a property claim.
- The claim is descriptive of an ambition and a logic; it does not assert that every rendered bit in practice fully loses all human meaning — only that the flow, by design, recognizes none.
- Zuboff is presenting the surveillance-capitalist worldview at its own most expansive (the marketing rhetoric of "dark data" and ubiquitous sensors); the passage diagnoses an aspiration as much as an accomplished fact.
