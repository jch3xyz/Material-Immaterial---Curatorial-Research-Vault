# Surveillance capitalism reverts the diffusion of knowledge to a pre-Gutenberg order

## Claim

Gutenberg's printing press democratized knowledge by bypassing the priesthood and putting the word directly into ordinary hands, and the internet was believed to extend that emancipation; but surveillance capitalism drives a hidden countermovement that reverts the division of learning to a pre-Gutenberg order, re-concentrating learning behind a narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists, their privately owned machines, and the economic interests they serve.

## Expanded Explanation

For Zuboff, the printing press is the historical benchmark for the diffusion of knowledge: "More than six hundred years ago, the printing press put the written word into the hands of ordinary people, rescuing the prayers, bypassing the priesthood, and delivering the opportunity for spiritual communion directly into the hands of the prayerful." The modern faith in the internet is that it "exponentially realizes Gutenberg's revolution in the lives of billions of individuals" — a "mighty democratizing force" that promises "more knowledge for more people." This is the public-facing story.

The argument's force is that this celebrated achievement is a screen. Beneath the visible diffusion runs "a different historical development, one that moves out of range and out of sight, designed to exclude, confuse, and obscure." In that hidden movement, the competitive struggle over surveillance revenues "reverts to the pre-Gutenberg order." The vector is not technological but economic: machine intelligence, the talent that builds it, and the infrastructure that runs it are pooled inside a handful of firms because that is what the accumulation of surveillance revenue requires. The priesthood is reconstituted not from clergy guarding scripture but from a scarce caste of "privately employed computational specialists," operating "privately owned machines," learning on behalf of "the economic interests for whose sake they learn."

The result is a pathological division of learning in which the questions "Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?" are all answered by surveillance capital. The asymmetry is material as well as cognitive: as Zuboff stresses, "Only surveillance capital commands the material infrastructure and expert brainpower to rule the division of learning in society." Authority alone is insufficient; it is the privately held machines and the scarce expert labor — hyperscale compute and the world's concentrated AI talent — that make the new priesthood unanswerable. (The material/immaterial framing here is interpretive synthesis with the vault's wider cluster; Zuboff's own terms are infrastructure, brainpower, and ownership.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise: the printing press democratized knowledge by bypassing the priesthood and delivering the word directly to ordinary people. (Directly stated)
2. Premise: we take for granted that the internet extends Gutenberg's revolution, diffusing more knowledge to more people. (Directly stated)
3. Premise: this belief blinds us to a hidden, deliberately obscured countermovement driven by the competitive struggle over surveillance revenues. (Directly stated)
4. Premise: making sense of the information continent requires machine intelligence, expert specialists, and material infrastructure — all of which surveillance capital uniquely commands. (Directly stated)
5. Conclusion: the division of learning therefore reverts to the pre-Gutenberg order, captured by a narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists, their privately owned machines, and the capital they serve. (Directly stated)

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Gutenberg benchmark: the press "rescuing the prayers, bypassing the priesthood," delivering communion "directly into the hands of the prayerful" (Ch. 6, §IV–V). Source link below.
- The internet-as-Gutenberg faith: "a mighty democratizing force that exponentially realizes Gutenberg's revolution in the lives of billions." Source link below.
- Martin Hilbert's counsel to "fight fire with fire" — using "artificially intelligent computers" to "sift through the vast amounts of information" — which Zuboff reads as confirming "the privileged position of the surveillance capitalists and the asymmetrical power that enables them to bend the division of learning to their interests." Establishes the [[The New Priesthood]] as the necessary consequence of data volume meeting private machine intelligence.
- Pedro Domingos: "Whoever has the best algorithms and the most data wins.… whoever learns fastest wins." And the New York Times report that Google's CEO "now shares a floor with the company's AI research lab" — "a literal take on the concentration of power." Evidence that scarce talent and compute pool inside the firms.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The optimistic reading (which Zuboff names in order to refute) holds that the internet genuinely is a democratizing extension of Gutenberg — more knowledge for more people — and that open-source tooling, cheap publishing, and search access cut against any re-concentration thesis. Zuboff's response is that the visible "first text" is real but is exactly what conceals the privately owned "second text" and the [[The Two Texts|division of learning]] it commands.
- The "necessary and inevitable" framing — that machine concentration is just the consequence of computer-based technologies — is the view Zuboff rejects: research shows the pathological division of learning "reflects the strength of neoliberal ideology, politics, culture, and institutional patterns," not technical necessity (the moderated European case proves contingency).
- Limitation: the "priesthood" is a historical analogy, not an identity claim; Zuboff's medieval clergy guarded a fixed canon, whereas the computational specialists produce ever-new predictive knowledge. The analogy carries the asymmetry-of-access point, not a one-to-one institutional mapping.
