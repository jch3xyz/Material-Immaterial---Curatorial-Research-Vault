# Only the phonetic alphabet not writing in general created the detribalized civilized individual

## Claim

It is the phonetic alphabet alone — not literacy or writing in general — that detribalizes: its sudden breach between sight and sound dissolves the tribal web and produces the separate, uniform "civilized individual," whereas the non-phonetic scripts of ideogrammic cultures leave tribal cohesion intact across millennia of literacy.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan's argument in "The Written Word: An Eye for an Ear" turns on a distinction most accounts of literacy collapse: between *writing as such* and *the phonetic alphabet* specifically. Pictographic, hieroglyphic, syllabic, and ideogrammic writing are all extensions of the visual sense for storing experience, but they remain bound to meaning and to the oral world — "all of these forms give pictorial expression to oral meanings." Only the phonetic alphabet performs a radical surgery on perception: it is "a unique technology" in which "semantically meaningless letters are used to correspond to semantically meaningly sounds," a "stark divison and parallelism between a visual and an auditory world." This separation of sight from sound — and of both from semantic content — is what McLuhan isolates as the detribalizing agent. (`Directly stated`)

The decisive evidence is comparative and temporal. "Many centuries of ideogrammic use have not threatened the seamless web of family and tribal subtleties of Chinese society." Against that, "a single generation of alphabetic literacy suffices in Africa today, as in Gaul two thousand years ago, to release the individual initially, at least, from the tribal web." The contrast is the whole argument in miniature: millennia of one kind of literacy preserve tribal cohesion; a single generation of another kind dissolves it. McLuhan then explicitly rules out the obvious rival explanation — that it is being literate, or the things written, that matters: "This fact has nothing to do with the content of the alphabetized words; it is the result of the sudden breach between the auditory and the visual experience of man." This is the same logic as [[The medium not its content is the message]] applied to writing: the message of the alphabet is the sensory reorganization it imposes, not the meanings it transmits. (`Directly stated`)

The product of that breach is a new kind of person. When tribal man becomes literate, "Nearly all the emotional and corporate family feeling is eliminated from his relationship with his social group. He is emotionally free to separate from the tribute and to become a civilized individual, a man of visual organization who has uniform attitudes, habits, and rights with all other civilized individuals." From this McLuhan draws the strong form of the claim: "the phonetic alphabet, alone, is the technology that has been the means of creating 'civilized man' — the separate individuals equal before a written code of law," whose prime marks are "Separateness of the individual, continuity of space and of time, and uniformity of codes." Because the alphabet intensifies the visual at the expense of sound, touch, and taste, it dissociates the [[Sense Ratios]] that non-phonetic, ideogrammic cultures keep in an "inclusive gestalt" — which is why detribalization is a special case of [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] keyed specifically to phonetic, not all, literacy. (`Directly stated`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. There have been many writing systems (pictographic, hieroglyphic, syllabic, ideogrammic), but the phonetic alphabet is unique: it pairs *meaningless* signs with sounds, severing sight from sound and both from meaning (premise).
2. Non-phonetic scripts give "pictorial expression to oral meanings" and remain an "inclusive gestalt" — they do not breach the senses; "Many centuries of ideogrammic use have not threatened the seamless web of family and tribal subtleties of Chinese society" (evidence A).
3. The phonetic alphabet alone makes "a sharp division in experience, giving to its user an eye for an ear" — so "a single generation of alphabetic literacy suffices in Africa today, as in Gaul two thousand years ago" to release the individual from the tribal web (evidence B).
4. The cause is therefore not literacy or content: "This fact has nothing to do with the content of the alphabetized words; it is the result of the sudden breach between the auditory and the visual experience of man" (eliminating the rival cause).
5. Conclusion: "the phonetic alphabet, alone, is the technology that has been the means of creating 'civilized man'" — the separate, uniform individual equal before a written code of law.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The China/Africa-Gaul comparison: millennia of ideogrammic literacy leave Chinese tribal cohesion untouched, while one generation of alphabetic literacy detribalizes ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/13 Chapter 9 - The Written Word - An Eye for an Ear]]).
- The uniqueness of the phonetic alphabet: "semantically meaningless letters" corresponding to sounds, a "stark divison and parallelism between a visual and an auditory world" (Ch. 9).
- The transformation of tribal man into the "civilized individual" who loses "corporate family feeling" and gains "uniform attitudes, habits, and rights" (Ch. 9).
- The "American flag" thought-experiment: writing the words "American flag" on cloth conveys the same meaning as the Stars and Stripes but strips its "corporate image and of experience" — an illustration of the breach between visual abstraction and tribal richness (Ch. 9).
- The ideogram as "an inclusive gestalt, not an analytic dissociation of senses and functions like phonetic writing," which lets non-phonetic cultures "retain a rich store of inclusive perception" (Ch. 9).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- McLuhan brackets the question of value: "Tribal cultures like those of the Indian and the Chinese may be greatly superior to the Western cultures, in the range and delicacy of their perceptions," but "we are not here concerned with the question of values, but with the configurations of societies." The argument describes a structural effect, not a loss or a gain — yet his own language ("eroded," "represses," "purchased… at too high a price") leans evaluative.
- The "single generation" claim is asserted from two illustrative cases (modern Africa, ancient Gaul) and a contrast case (China); it is comparative-historical generalization rather than demonstration, and McLuhan supplies no mechanism for *why* one generation suffices beyond the sense-breach posited.
- The qualifier "initially, at least" hedges the release from the tribal web — detribalization may be partial or reversible, which McLuhan's own [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] confirms: electric speed can *re*tribalize the alphabetically detribalized individual.
- The strong word "alone" ("the phonetic alphabet, alone") may overstate: McLuhan elsewhere credits print and number as detribalizing agents too ("Phonetic letters and number were the first means of fragmenting and detribalizing man," Ch. 11), so the alphabet is the originating cause within a family of specialist technologies rather than a literally sole one.

## Tensions

- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the alphabet is the technology of detachment: it frees the individual from "the tribal trance of resonating word magic and the web of kinship," exchanging participational tribal involvement for the "cool and neutral eye" of visual organization.
- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — phonetic literacy is the founding *mechanical*, uniform, repetitive technology of detribalization; the chapter is written from the far side of the break, "As we move out of the Gutenberg era," where electric media begin to reverse its effects.
