# Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image

## Claim

Because a metaphor's source and target domains are interchangeable — "it is not so easy to distinguish the source domain from the target" — anthropomorphic projection ran the imago dei backward, so that the God said to have made us in his image is in truth the figure we made in ours, and the *same* reversibility now lets [[Mind as Machine|mind-as-machine]] flip into machine-as-mind.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the hinge argument of [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]'s first chapter, the place where her method [[Technology as Metaphor]] acquires its load-bearing structural premise. Following the anthropologist Stewart Guthrie, she takes the human tendency to anthropomorphize as an evolutionary perceptual strategy — when "confronted with an uncertain object," natural selection rewards those who "bet high," reading a dark mass in the woods not as a boulder but as a person. Guthrie's hypothesis is that "this habit of seeing our image everywhere in the natural world is what gave birth to the idea of God": early civilizations read earthquakes, famine, and design into "the blank vault of heaven" as signs of human-like agency. O'Gieblyn's move is to extract from this a claim about figuration as such. If God is the projection of the human, then the directional convention of the *imago dei* — Genesis's "make man in Our image" — has been running the wrong way the whole time. "For centuries we said we were made in God's image, when in truth we made him in ours."

The generalization is the title: **metaphors are two-way streets.** "It is not so easy to distinguish the source domain from the target, to remember which object is the original and which is modeled after its likeness. The logic can flow in either direction." This is not a remark about theology alone; it is the formal property O'Gieblyn needs for the rest of the book. Once it is granted that a comparison cannot police which term is original and which is copy, the chapter's other great metaphor — the computational theory of mind, in which the brain is "the hardware that 'runs' the software of the mind" — becomes structurally identical to the God case. Having spent centuries describing the mind in the idiom of the era's apex machine, we now find the traffic reversing: "as we increasingly come to speak of our minds as computers, computers are now granted the status of minds." The figure built to explain *us* by reference to the machine now confers personhood on the machine. Terms once "couched in quotation marks when applied to machines — 'behavior,' 'memory,' 'thinking' — are now taken as straightforward descriptions of their functions."

The two reversals are the same reversal. *Imago dei* flipped is anthropomorphism (we project the human onto heaven and call it divine); mind-as-machine flipped is the granting of minds to computers (we project the machine onto the mind, then read mind back into the machine). The interchangeability of source and target is what makes both flips not errors but the predictable behavior of metaphor — which is why O'Gieblyn's worry is not that the metaphor is *wrong* but that, once "so common that we forget it is a metaphor," its two-way traffic becomes invisible and "its meaning is taken to be literal." This grounds [[Technology as Metaphor]], the case study [[Mind as Machine]], and the inverted [[Imago Dei]] that the argument turns inside out.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Anthropomorphism is an inherited perceptual strategy: facing an uncertain object we "bet high" and read in human-like agency (Guthrie).
2. Applied to the natural world — earthquakes, famine, the appearance of design — this projection of the human "is what gave birth to the idea of God."
3. Therefore the *imago dei* runs backward: not man made in God's image, but God made in man's. "For centuries we said we were made in God's image, when in truth we made him in ours."
4. Generalize the mechanism: a metaphor cannot fix which domain is source and which is target — "the logic can flow in either direction." Metaphors are two-way streets.
5. The computational figure for the mind is a metaphor of exactly this kind (brain as hardware, mind as software, cognition as algorithm).
6. By the same reversibility, the traffic flips: "as we increasingly come to speak of our minds as computers, computers are now granted the status of minds."
7. Conclusion: the reversal that produced "God" from the human and the reversal that produces "minds" from machines are one and the same structural property of metaphor — which is why the dead metaphor (taken literally, its reversibility forgotten) is the danger to guard against.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The Guthrie genealogy is a hypothesis, not a proof.** O'Gieblyn flags it herself — "but this theory, *if true*, suggests a deeper truth" — so the inversion of the imago dei rests on an unverified (and unfalsifiable) evolutionary story about the origin of religion. The structural claim about metaphor's reversibility may survive even if the specific genesis-of-God account does not.
- **Reversibility may not be symmetric.** That a metaphor's logic *can* flow in either direction does not establish that both directions are equally warranted. Anthropomorphism is, in Guthrie's own frame, an "unfortunate side effect" and a perceptual *error* — so the flip from mind-as-machine to machine-as-mind might be precisely the mistake to resist, not a discovery to accept. O'Gieblyn's worry about the dead metaphor concedes as much.
- **It risks dissolving a real asymmetry.** The believer's claim and the engineer's claim are not obviously on a par: one term (the human, the mind) is given in first-person experience, the other (God, the computer) is constructed. Critics of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal-humanist]] retreats and of [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] enthusiasm alike could press that the "two-way street" elides which terminus is epistemically primary — exactly the [[Embodiment]] residue (the "hard problem," "what it is like") the computational metaphor cannot capture.
- **Scope.** The argument is made within Chapter 1's literary-philosophical register; it is asserted and illustrated rather than argued through the philosophy of metaphor (Lakoff, Black, Ricoeur). It is best read as O'Gieblyn's *framing premise* for the book, not a self-standing demonstration.
