# The dichotomy of artist and viewer resolves into a single participating user

## Claim

The telematic network dissolves the one-way artist/viewer — sender/receiver, producer/consumer — dichotomy of the perspectival era into a single "user" or "participant" who does not receive a finished work but completes and transforms the creative system by entering it.

## Expanded Explanation

For Roy Ascott the separation of artist from viewer is not a natural fact about art but a historical artefact of the Renaissance ordering of space. Perspective "underwrote a mode of visual discourse that separated out both the individual artist and the solitary viewer: a one-way view of the world calling for a one-way reading of the visual field" (ch. 15). The painting was a window through which one privileged maker depicted the world and one isolated observer received the depiction; the vanishing point fixed the viewer's place exactly as the social pyramid fixed his place in the world. The dichotomy of producer and consumer is, on this account, the aesthetic signature of a culture "in which information flows one way, from the apex of the social pyramid to the base" (ch. 17). This is the model Ascott calls the Renaissance paradigm: "the artist standing apart from the world and depicting it and the observer standing outside of the artwork and receiving this depiction."

Telematic systems break this open. Because the [[Telematic Art|telematic artwork]] is not an object carrying a fixed message but a [[Object and System|system]] — a "field of psychic probability" completed in interaction — the act of receiving is already an act of making (see [[The artwork is a probabilistic field where meaning is generated not transmitted]]). Ascott reads the twentieth-century tilt of the picture plane from vertical to horizontal — Duchamp's *Large Glass*, the flatbed picture-surface Leo Steinberg described, and above all Jackson Pollock's "all-at-onceness" — as a "convergence" toward this condition: Pollock's surface is an "arena, a meeting place" whose "anonymity of authorship ... embraces the viewer in the creation of meanings." What painting could only promise metaphysically, the network makes "technologically explicit": "the dichotomy of artist/viewer or sender/receiver of the earlier era is resolved into a unitary 'user' of the creative system" (ch. 15).

The 1991 essay states the same thesis as an obsolescence claim. "The primary effect of creative interaction within such networks is to render obsolete the distinction in absolute terms between the artist and viewer as producer and consumer, respectively. The new composite role becomes that simply of participant in a system creating meaning seen as art" (ch. 17). The new figure is the [[The Participator|participator]] — neither author nor audience but a node whose entry alters the field. This is the human correlate of Ascott's [[Reciprocal Communication|reciprocal]], two-way [[Telematics (Ascott)|telematics]]: where the old order stimulated a passive receiver in one direction, the network constitutes its meaning through the mutual transformation of everyone in it. It is the participatory pole of [[New Media Art]] and the experiential premise of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the work exists only in the act of use.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The artist/viewer (sender/receiver, producer/consumer) split is a product of the Renaissance perspectival paradigm, in which information and meaning flow one way from a privileged maker to a positioned, separated observer.
2. Telematic art is not an object that "carries" a fixed meaning but an open system — a probabilistic behavioural field — whose meaning is generated, not transmitted, and only in interaction.
3. In such a system the act of "receiving" is constitutively an act of making: to enter the network is to transform it, not to consume a finished product.
4. Therefore the categories of producer and consumer collapse: there is no fixed point from which to send and no passive point at which to receive.
5. Conclusion: the dichotomy resolves into a single composite role — the "user" or "participant" of the creative system, the network's correlate of the perspectival viewer.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- "Resolved into a unitary user" can flatten real asymmetries of skill, authorship, and control: someone still designs the system, sets its protocols, and owns its infrastructure. Ascott concedes a planning role — "that place can be defined properly only with the active involvement of the artist at the outset of the planning process" (ch. 17) — which re-introduces an artist who is not simply one user among others.
- The claim is partly programmatic and utopian: Pollock's "anonymity of authorship" is offered as metaphor, and the dissolution is asserted as the network's "primary effect" rather than empirically demonstrated. Many networked artworks in practice preserve a strong author/audience split.
- "Participation" is not automatically emancipatory. The same one-way pyramid Ascott describes can be reproduced inside a network; the producer/consumer collapse he predicts is a potential of the medium, not a guarantee — a tension he registers elsewhere as [[Liberation vs Control (Telematics)]].
- The argument risks equivocating "user" (a term with strong consumer-technology connotations) with "co-author"; the very word may smuggle back the consumption it claims to abolish.

## Related Arguments

- [[The artwork is a probabilistic field where meaning is generated not transmitted]] — the ontological premise: if meaning is generated in interaction rather than transmitted, there is no fixed sender and no passive receiver, so the producer/consumer roles have nothing to attach to.
- [[Art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour]] — the broader move of which this is the role-theoretic consequence: when art becomes behaviour rather than object, the people involved become participants in a behaviour rather than maker and beholder of a thing.

## Tensions

- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — the artist→viewer dichotomy is the human face of one-way stimulation; the unitary participating user is the human face of two-way, reciprocal communication. This argument is the role-level statement of that opposition.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the perspectival observer "standing outside of the artwork and receiving this depiction" is the figure of detachment; the participant "in a system creating meaning seen as art" is the figure of participation. The dichotomy resolves precisely by moving from one pole to the other.
