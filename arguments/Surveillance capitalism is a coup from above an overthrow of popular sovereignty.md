# Surveillance capitalism is a coup from above an overthrow of popular sovereignty

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism is best understood not as an economic phenomenon but as a political-constitutional one: a coup from above that overthrows not the state but the people's own sovereignty.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff's most consequential reframing is to lift surveillance capitalism out of the register of economics — markets, monopoly, privacy, data — and recast it in the register of politics and constitutional order. As the eighth and final clause of her dictionary [[Surveillance Capitalism|definition]], surveillance capitalism is "An expropriation of critical human rights that is best understood as a coup from above: an overthrow of the people's sovereignty." The figure of the *coup* is precise and deliberate. A coup is an illegitimate seizure of power, and what is seized here is not the apparatus of the state but the prior, more elemental thing the state is supposed to answer to — the sovereignty of the people themselves.

The mechanism of this overthrow is the expansion of [[Instrumentarian Power]] beyond the institutional terrain where economic power is conventionally exercised and contested. Surveillance capitalism, Zuboff argues, "claims dominion over human, societal, and political territories that range far beyond the conventional institutional terrain of the private firm or the market." Because it exceeds "the historical norms of capitalist ambitions" — demanding "both unimpeded freedom and total knowledge" and abandoning capitalism's reciprocities with people and society — it crosses from market actor into would-be sovereign. What it installs in place of popular self-rule is a collectivist order Zuboff calls life in [[The Hive]]: a "totalizing collectivist vision of life in the hive, with surveillance capitalists and their data priesthood in charge of oversight and control." Group pressure and computational certainty (see [[A Utopia of Certainty]]) replace politics and democracy.

The political stakes are therefore systemic, not incidental. Zuboff names surveillance capitalism "a prominent force in the perilous drift toward democratic deconsolidation that now threatens Western liberal democracies." The coup is antidemocratic by construction: it transfers the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning]] — who knows, who decides, who decides who decides — into private hands, voiding the citizen's standing as a co-author of the collective future and the bearer of [[The Right to the Future Tense|the right to the future tense]]. The remedy Zuboff proposes is correspondingly political rather than merely regulatory: only "we the people" can reverse the course, first by naming the unprecedented, then by mobilizing collaborative action that "reasserts the primacy of a flourishing human future."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism's power — [[Instrumentarian Power]] materialized in [[Big Other]] — extends beyond the firm and the market, claiming "dominion over human, societal, and political territories." *(Directly stated)*
2. In so doing it abandons capitalism's reciprocities with people and society and imposes a "totalizing collectivist vision of life in the hive," governed by surveillance capitalists and their "data priesthood." *(Directly stated)*
3. A power that seizes the human, societal, and political territories of self-rule, replacing politics and democracy with group pressure and computational certainty, has seized sovereignty itself — not the state's, but the people's. *(Directly stated)*
4. Such a seizure is by definition illegitimate and antidemocratic; Zuboff names it a "coup from above" and "an expropriation of critical human rights," and identifies it as "a prominent force in the perilous drift toward democratic deconsolidation." *(Directly stated)*
5. Therefore the adequate response is political — a reassertion of popular sovereignty by "we the people" — not merely the economic remedies (antitrust, privacy law) suited to a market problem. *(Strongly implied)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The word "coup" is a political metaphor applied to an economic actor; a critic could argue surveillance capitalism lacks the coercive monopoly on force that defines a genuine seizure of sovereignty, and that "coup" overstates a process that is partly consented to. Zuboff partly anticipates this: she stresses that the bondage is self-incurred — "we now pay for our own domination" and "its normalization leaves us singing in our chains" — which both strengthens the claim of an overthrow *of the people by the people's own participation* and complicates the clean image of an externally imposed coup. This is the productive divergence from Han, who foregrounds the *voluntary* (see [[The digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure]] and [[Auto-Exploitation]]) where Zuboff foregrounds the *unilateral*.
- "Democratic deconsolidation" is attributed to surveillance capitalism as "a prominent force," not the sole cause; the argument is one of contribution and intensification, not monocausality.
- The remedy ("only 'we the people' can reverse this course") is asserted as a normative-political horizon rather than demonstrated; the argument names the disease and prescribes collective action without specifying institutional mechanisms here.
