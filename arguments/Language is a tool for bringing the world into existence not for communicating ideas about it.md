# Language is a tool for bringing the world into existence not for communicating ideas about it

## Claim

For Roy Ascott, language is not merely a device for communicating ideas about a world that already exists, but a tool for bringing the world into existence — and because art is "language embodied in forms and behaviours," art is therefore world building, mind construction, and self-creation: the search for new language by which to reframe consciousness and redefine ourselves.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott calls the key to "this new state of being" language, but he immediately overturns the commonplace, representational picture of it. Language does not point at a pre-given reality and transmit reports about it; it constitutes reality. On this constructivist premise — which he draws explicitly from the philosopher Richard Rorty — the artist's task is not to describe the world more accurately but to make new worlds by making new language. Art becomes "a form of world building, of mind construction, of self-creation," carried out indifferently "through digital programming, genetic code, articulation of the body, imaging, simulation, or visual construction."

The argument cuts against any view of art (or of the artist's medium) as communication of ideas about something. To "reiterate received language, uncreatively and uncritically," Ascott writes, is "to renounce the idea that we can rethink ourselves and our world." Art's value is therefore measured not by the ideas it conveys but by the new language it produces: "Through the languages it creates, art serves to reframe consciousness. It can only be evaluated by the new language it produces." Self-creation and world-creation are the same act, because — quoting Rorty — "to create one's mind is to create one's own language."

For Ascott this is the philosophical ground of his larger program. The new language he seeks is not confined to text or image; it is embodied, "involving all the senses, going perhaps beyond the senses," and ultimately distributed across "moistmedia" at the convergence of the digital, the biological, and the spiritual. The constructivist account of language is what licenses treating art as a generative, world-making technology rather than a representational one — the conceptual root of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] as he frames them, and of the move from the art object toward the participatory, sense-constructed environment.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise (constructivist, after Rorty): language does not merely communicate ideas about a pre-existing world; it brings the world into existence.
2. Premise: art is "language embodied in forms and behaviours, texts and structures."
3. Therefore art is not communication about the world but world building, mind construction, and self-creation.
4. Premise: to create one's mind is to create one's own language (Rorty).
5. Corollary: art's task is the search for new language, new metaphors, new ways of constructing reality and of redefining ourselves; its sole valid measure is the new language it produces.
6. Conclusion: art's function is to reframe consciousness — refusing received language is refusing the passive acceptance that "in matters of reality, our minds are made up for us."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The framing thesis: "language is not merely a device for communicating ideas about the world but rather a tool for bringing the world into existence. Art is a form of world building, of mind construction, of self-creation" (ch. 31).
- The criterion of evaluation: "Through the languages it creates, art serves to reframe consciousness. It can only be evaluated by the new language it produces" (ch. 31).
- Richard Rorty, quoted directly: "To create one's mind is to create one's own language, rather than to let the length of one's mind be set by language other human beings have left behind" — Ascott's stated philosophical warrant (ch. 31).
- Rorty's pragmatism characterized as eschewing "the sanctity of philosophy in favour of the artist's visionary impulse and search for metaphor that leads to the continual construction of reality and of the self" (ch. 31).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The constructivist premise is asserted via Rorty rather than independently defended; a representationalist would hold that language can both construct and communicate, and that the dichotomy is overdrawn.
- "Bringing the world into existence" risks idealism — the world as wholly language-made — which sits in tension with Ascott's own insistence (same chapter) on the "re-materialisation of culture involving molecules and atoms, nanotechnology and neurons." The argument is constructivist about reality yet emphatically embodied and material in its media.
- Making "the new language it produces" the sole criterion of art's worth privileges novelty and risks deflating other values (craft, communication, critique) that the received-language tradition carries.
