# Traditional object-centred art education is in terminal decline

## Claim

Art education built on the material object cannot survive into the post-biological age, because art itself has ceased to be an object and become invisible, distributed, interactive, and emergent; the object-centred atelier therefore has nothing to offer the emerging telematic culture.

## Expanded Explanation

For Roy Ascott the icon of art has migrated. Where the standard scene of art was once "the artist and his model as the standard mise-en-scène," art now deals "essentially with the invisible, with transformative systems," and the defining metaphor of practice — first electronic flow, now emergence — "in recent years has replaced the aesthetic of the material object." Art is no longer a thing to be made and contemplated but a context, a system, a behaviour: connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence are its five defining features. An education whose curriculum, studio, and authority are all organised around producing and judging discrete objects is calibrated to an art that no longer exists.

The diagnosis is therefore not that the academy is merely outdated but that it is structurally mismatched to the culture it is meant to serve. Ascott says flatly that "traditional art education, at this point of its terminal decline, has shown itself to have nothing to offer the demands of the emerging culture." The institution is described as in tatters — "its insolent subjectivity turned in upon itself, its adoration of the object enfeebled and derided" — and no remedy internal to the old model (modularisation, bureaucratic tinkering, curricular meddling) can arrest "the attrition of the atelier." The cause is not curricular but ontological: the artist no longer provides content and meaning but "creates contexts and systems," and the viewer, once a passive observer, now "negotiates and navigates what is largely a world of her own making."

Crucially, the decline is also a matter of where the students actually are. They are "half in school and half in cyberspace," living "between the virtual and the real," more often on the Internet than out of it — the condition Ascott names inter-reality. The fixed academy, "art education in a box," is being abandoned for the network; its successor is the bottom-up, non-hierarchical, distributed learning organism Ascott calls the Planetary Collegium. The death of object-centred education is thus the obverse of an emergence: the old institution withers exactly as a networked, telematic pedagogy comes into being.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The icon of art has shifted from the canvas-on-easel / artist-and-model to the invisible, to transformative systems, to emergence — electronic flow has "replaced the aesthetic of the material object."
2. The emerging art is defined by connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence — it is a bottom-up, distributed system, not a discrete made object.
3. Object-centred art education is organised around producing, contemplating, and judging material objects ("its adoration of the object"), and so is calibrated to the superseded paradigm.
4. The mismatch is structural, not curricular: "no amount of modularisation, bureaucratic modification, or curricular meddling can stem the attrition of the atelier"; and the students themselves have already moved into inter-reality.
5. Therefore traditional object-centred art education is in terminal decline and "has nothing to offer the demands of the emerging culture"; its replacement is the networked, emergent Planetary Collegium.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Ascott is himself a participant-advocate (founder of the Planetary Collegium), so the "terminal decline" is asserted polemically from inside a competing institutional project; the verdict is normative as much as descriptive.
- The claim's force depends on accepting that art has in fact left the object behind. The vault's own [[Object vs System]] and [[Material vs Immaterial]] tensions register that this is contested — the object did not simply vanish (cf. [[Behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object]]), and object-making pedagogy has in fact persisted well past 1996.
- "Terminal decline" is a prediction with a timeline ("That may just be too late"); Ascott concedes the dissolution "will not occur overnight," which softens the literal terminality.
- The argument generalises from the digital-arts vanguard ("a more or less minority group, a terrorist group even") to the whole field; the rest of art education may not share the same icon-shift.
