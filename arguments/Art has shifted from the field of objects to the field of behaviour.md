# Art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour

## Claim

Modern art is unified by a single behavioural tendency — the move from the field of objects to the field of behaviour, from the one-way transmission of a fixed message to two-way feedback within a system — and this shift makes the spectator an active participant whose intervention completes the work.

## Expanded Explanation

[[Roy Ascott]]'s foundational thesis, voiced as early as 1966–67, is that what distinguishes modern art is not any property of the art *object* but a change in the location of art itself: art has migrated from things to *behaviour*. Where "the art of the past" sought to transmit "a clearly defined message to the spectator, as a more or less passive receptor," modern art "is concerned to initiate events" — it is purposive rather than descriptive, an open system rather than a closed composition (`Directly stated`). This is the cleanest art-side restatement of the vault's [[Object vs System]] and [[Material vs Immaterial]] tensions: art "resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system" (`Directly stated`).

The behavioural turn carries a built-in account of the viewer. Because art is now a transaction within a feedback system, "the viewer/observer must be a participator and is of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system" — meaning and value are *generated* only by the intervention of observers, not delivered ready-made by the artist (see [[The Participator]], [[Reciprocal Communication]]). This converts the one-way linearity of sender→message→receiver into the two-way reciprocity Ascott names cybernetic (the [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] tension; [[Detachment vs Participation]]).

Ascott generalizes the thesis into a programme. Against the "formalist modernist aesthetic," he proposes "a field theory that would replace [it]" that "takes as its focus not form but behaviour" (`Directly stated`). This is the conceptual root of [[Behaviourist Art]] and, downstream, of [[Telematic Art]], [[New Media Art]], and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]. It both predates and complements [[Jack Burnham]]'s 1968 object→system thesis in [[Systems Esthetics]] / [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] — Ascott reaches the same destination from the side of *cybernetic behaviour and the participant* rather than the side of the *dematerializing sculptural object* (`Strongly implied`).

## Reasoning Structure

1. The art of the past aimed to transmit a fixed message from a unique source to a passive receptor — a deterministic, descriptive, object-centred aesthetic.
2. Modern art instead initiates *events* and forms concepts of *existence*; it is purposive, open, and unresolved.
3. Therefore the locus of art is no longer the object or its surface but *behaviour* — the interaction among all parts of a system.
4. In such a system the viewer is structurally necessary: only the intervention of observers generates meaning and value, so the spectator becomes a participator.
5. Consequently the right paradigm for art is a field theory focused on behaviour rather than form — the basis for behaviourist, cybernetic, and (later) telematic art.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Editor Edward Shanken notes that Ascott's argument leans on binary oppositions (past vs. present, objects vs. events) and a faith in scientific progress that "suggest continuities with the liberal humanist values associated with modernism," so the object/behaviour split is less clean than the slogan implies (`Directly stated` — Shanken).
- The thesis privileges *participation* as inherently emancipatory; it does not, in these early texts, reckon with how feedback systems can also capture and condition the participant — a tension the vault's later surveillance/control authors press (cf. [[Reciprocal Communication]], [[Detachment vs Participation]]).
- "Behaviour" risks dissolving the artwork entirely: if art resides only "between the behaviours of all parts of the system," what remains object enough to be conserved, exhibited, or owned is left open.
