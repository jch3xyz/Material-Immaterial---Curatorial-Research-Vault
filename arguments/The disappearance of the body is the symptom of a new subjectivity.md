# The disappearance of the body is the symptom of a new subjectivity

## Claim

The postmodern orthodoxy that the body has vanished into discourse, signs, or information should be read not as a true report of the body's disappearance but as the symptom of a new kind of subjectivity — one constituted at the crossing of [[The Materiality of Informatics|the materiality of informatics]] with the immateriality of [[Information|information]], and whose very dream of dematerialization is materially and embodied-ly produced.

## Expanded Explanation

[[N. Katherine Hayles]] opens chapter 8 by naming a "postmodern orthodoxy that the body is primarily, if not entirely, a linguistic and discursive construction" — a belief she expects "to stupefy future generations." She catalogs its "ecstatic pronouncements and delirious dreams": Baudrillard's claim that "the human body, our body, seems superfluous"; the Krokers' "floating body parts" and rhetorical question "might this not be because the body no longer exists?"; Hardison writing the body into computers. The orthodoxy is the cultural face of the same move that, in cybernetics, stripped [[Information|information]] of its body. Both collaborate in "the postmodern ideology that the body's materiality is secondary to the logical or semiotic structures it encodes."

Hayles's reversal is a diagnostic one. Rather than asking whether the body has in fact disappeared, she asks what the *belief* in its disappearance is a symptom *of*. Her answer: "I believe they should be taken as evidence not that the body has disappeared but that a certain kind of subjectivity has emerged. This subjectivity is constituted by the crossing of the materiality of informatics with the immateriality of information." The pronouncements are not false descriptions to be refuted but cultural evidence to be excavated — symptoms whose conditions of plausibility are the real object of study. This is what distinguishes the argument from the concept [[The Materiality of Informatics]]: the concept names the constructive thesis and method; *this* note isolates the specific interpretive maneuver — the inversion that turns a claim about the world (the body is gone) into a claim about subjectivity (a new subject has emerged).

The reversal has a reflexive sting. "The very theorists who most emphatically claim that the body is disappearing also operate within material and cultural circumstances that make the claim for the body's disappearance seem plausible. The body's dematerialization depends in complex and highly specific ways on the embodied circumstances that an ideology of dematerialization would obscure." The dream of leaving the body behind is not weightless; it is manufactured by particular bodies in particular places — the high-tech facility, the printed page, the recording studio — whose materiality the dream must hide in order to be dreamed. To recover those circumstances is to denaturalize the dream without denying that something real has changed. What has changed is not that the body left but that a subjectivity arrived which experiences itself, falsely, as bodiless.

## Reasoning Structure

1. There is a widely held postmodern orthodoxy — running parallel to the cybernetic erasure of information's body — that the body is essentially a discursive construction and its materiality secondary to the codes it encodes.
2. This orthodoxy generates "ecstatic pronouncements" (Baudrillard, the Krokers, Hardison) that the body is "superfluous," "disappearing," or already nonexistent.
3. Taken as literal descriptions, these pronouncements would have to be either accepted (the body really is gone) or simply refuted; Hayles does neither.
4. Instead she reads them as *evidence* — symptoms — of an emergent subjectivity, not of an absent body: "evidence not that the body has disappeared but that a certain kind of subjectivity has emerged."
5. That subjectivity is specified as a crossing: "constituted by the crossing of the materiality of informatics with the immateriality of information" — it lives at the seam where material infrastructure and apparently weightless pattern meet.
6. The clinching reflexive step: the theorists who proclaim disembodiment "operate within material and cultural circumstances that make the claim for the body's disappearance seem plausible," so "the body's dematerialization depends in complex and highly specific ways on the embodied circumstances that an ideology of dematerialization would obscure."
7. Conclusion: the dream of dematerialization is itself materially produced; the proper analytic task is not to celebrate or mourn the body's disappearance but to excavate the embodied conditions that make its disappearance *seem* plausible — and thereby to read the symptom back to the new subject it indexes.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The reversal is interpretive, not empirical.** Hayles offers no proof that the body has *not* changed in some deep sense; she reframes the question. A reader committed to strong disembodiment (Moravec, Hardison) can grant that the dream has material conditions while still holding that those conditions are precisely what enable a real, coming transcendence of the body. Hayles's move blocks the inference from "the body is constructed as discourse" to "the body has disappeared," but it does not by itself settle the ontological dispute.
- **Symptom-reading risks circularity.** Treating a claim as a symptom rather than a description ("they say the body is gone *because* a new subject has emerged") can immunize the thesis: any pronouncement of disembodiment confirms the emergent subjectivity, and any denial confirms its repression. Hayles partly answers this by anchoring the symptom in specifiable "embodied circumstances" (media, infrastructures, contexts) that can be independently described.
- **The "new subjectivity" is named more than analyzed here.** In chapter 8 the crossing of materiality and immateriality is asserted compactly; its fuller characterization as the posthuman subject is deferred to later chapters. As stated, the claim diagnoses without yet specifying what kind of subject inhabits the crossing.
- **By Hayles's own warning, the diagnosis can leave the mechanism intact.** If one denaturalizes the *content* of disembodiment (this or that pronouncement) without dismantling the *mechanism of abstraction*, the reversal becomes another abstraction. Hayles flags this danger against Foucault; it applies reflexively to her own framework.
