# Games are counter-irritants to the stress of specialist labor and stave off automatism

## Claim

Games and technologies are counter-irritants that allow a society to adjust to the stress of its specialized actions and to release itself from the monopolistic tyranny of the social machine; a people deprived of games and the popular arts sinks into the zombie trance of automation and tends toward automatism.

## Expanded Explanation

This argument is the collective, social-scale application of McLuhan's [[Autoamputation]]/[[Narcosis]] apparatus. Where the autoamputation thesis describes how an individual numbs the central nervous system by extending an overstressed organ into a technology, here the same logic is run on the body politic: a culture under the strain of specialized, fragmented labor produces games as a popular art that both models and relieves that strain. Games "incorporate both the action and the reaction of whole populations in a single dynamic image," making them, in McLuhan's phrase, "faithful models of a culture."

The mechanism is one of compensation. The specialized job draws on "only a small sector" of a person's being, so the games door opening into the "free life" provides "a release from the monopolistic tyranny of the social machine." In this register games belong to the same family as [[Specialism as Counter-Irritant]]: a counter-irritant is not a cure but an antidote that creates a "countervailing force" against the numbing imposed by fragmented, delegated tasks. Art and games "enable us to stand aside from the material pressures of routine and convention, observing and questioning."

The negative pole completes the argument. A society that cannot devise this antidote — McLuhan's example is the tribal society "suddenly translated into industrial and specialist forms of mechanization" — "bog[s] down into grim earnest." Without the relief of popular art, the numbing of [[Narcosis]] is never broken: "Men without art, and men without the popular arts of games, tend toward automatism," and "a man or society without games is one sunk in the zombie trance of the automation." Games are therefore not a triviality but, for a highly specialized industrial culture, "a desperate need," because "real interplay is reduced to nothing in a specialist world of delegated tasks and fragmented jobs."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Specialized, fragmented labor stresses any social group, drawing on only a small sector of the whole person (the social-scale form of [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]]).
2. Games — like technologies, and like institutions — are extensions of social man that function as "counter-irritants or ways of adjusting to the stress of the specialized actions."
3. As popular art, games offer "an immediate means of participation in the full life of a society, such as no single role or job can offer," providing "a release from the monopolistic tyranny of the social machine."
4. This release lets people "stand aside from the material pressures of routine and convention, observing and questioning" — restoring awareness against the numbing of [[Narcosis]] (the social-scale stake of [[Numbness vs Awareness]]).
5. Conclusion: a society able to devise this antidote stays awake; a society without games and the popular arts is "sunk in the zombie trance of the automation" and "tend[s] toward automatism."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The structural definition: games, like institutions and technologies, are extensions of social man, and "Both games and technologies are counter-irritants or ways of adjusting to the stress of the specialized actions that occur in any social group" (the seed of [[Specialism as Counter-Irritant]]).
- The release function: "Do not our favorite games provide a release from the monopolistic tyranny of the social machine?" and games "offer to all an immediate means of participation in the full life of a society, such as no single role or job can offer to any man."
- The standing-aside function: "Art and games enable us to stand aside from the material pressures of routine and convention, observing and questioning."
- The negative pole (the automatism that results from absence): "a man or society without games is one sunk in the zombie trance of the automation," and "Men without art, and men without the popular arts of games, tend toward automatism."
- The case of failed adjustment: "Some backward or tribal societies suddenly translated into industrial and specialist forms of mechanization cannot easily devise the antidote of sports and games to create countervailing force. They bog down into grim earnest."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- McLuhan concedes that games, seen as live models of complex social situations, "may lack moral earnestness" — yet he turns this into a virtue, arguing it is "just for this reason" that highly specialized industrial cultures have "a desperate need" for them.
- The argument leans on McLuhan's contested ethnographic and historical generalizations (the "zombie trance," "tribal" vs. "individualist" societies, retribalization in the electric age); these are asserted impressionistically rather than demonstrated.
- The counter-irritant is palliative, not curative: it helps "both to teach us this kind of adjustment and also to provide a release from it," so games arguably reconcile people to the very specialized social machine they relieve them from — a tension McLuhan notes but does not resolve.
