# Foucault failed to make the turn to psychopolitics

## Claim

Foucault saw that [[Disciplinary Society]] no longer fit the late 1970s and turned to neoliberal government, but because he kept the categories of population and [[Biopolitics]] — which belong to disciplinary society — he failed to make the turn to [[Psychopolitics]], and so missed the engineering of freedom into [[Auto-Exploitation|self-exploitation]].

## Expanded Explanation

This is the hinge of *Psychopolitics*'s immanent critique of [[Michel Foucault]]. Han grants Foucault the decisive perception: "After *Discipline and Punish*, Foucault recognized that disciplinary society did not reflect the times in every respect," and "in the late 1970s, he devoted his attention to neoliberal forms of government." The diagnosis was right; the conceptual apparatus carried over to meet it was wrong. Foucault "stuck to the concepts of population and biopolitics" — categories Han identifies as "genuine categories of disciplinary society" and therefore "unsuited to describing the neoliberal regime." Having turned toward neoliberalism with disciplinary tools, Foucault "failed to do what the circumstances actually called for: to make the turn to [[Psychopolitics|psychopolitics]]." (`Directly stated`)

The reason the old tools fail is a change in the mode of production. [[Biopolitics]] "fundamentally concerns the biological and the physical" — it "constitutes a politics of the body in the fullest sense." But neoliberalism is a "mutated form" of capitalism that "has discovered the psyche as a productive force," organized around "immaterial and non-physical forms of production." Because "the body no longer represents a central force of production," productivity is enhanced "not... by overcoming physical resistance so much as by optimizing psychic or mental processes." The corporeal restriction that defines biopolitics is exactly what disqualifies it: a politics of bodies cannot reach the psyche that neoliberal power exploits. This is the [[Material vs Immaterial|material/immaterial]] axis on which Foucault's biopolitics becomes obsolete. (`Directly stated`)

What specifically "escaped Foucault" is the operative trick of neoliberal power: it "does not lay hold of individuals directly," but "ensures that individuals act on themselves so that power relations are interiorized — and then interpreted as freedom." In this regime "self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, fall into one." Foucault even held the right hinge in his hand — his early-1980s turn to "technologies of the self" — but read it as standing in opposition to power and domination. Han inverts this: the neoliberal regime "utterly claims the technology of the self for its own purposes," so that "perpetual [[Self-Optimization|self-optimization]]... represents nothing so much as a highly efficient mode of domination and exploitation." The "[[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]]" as "entrepreneur of himself" therefore "engages in [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploitation]] willingly — and even passionately." This is the [[Exploitation of Freedom|engineering of freedom]] Foucault could not see, and the [[Freedom vs Control|freedom-as-control]] paradox at the book's center. (`Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Foucault correctly diagnosed that [[Disciplinary Society]] no longer described the late-1970s present and turned to neoliberal government.
2. But he retained population and [[Biopolitics]] — categories Han classes as belonging to disciplinary society — to analyze that new terrain.
3. [[Biopolitics]] is "a politics of the body in the fullest sense"; it concerns "the biological, the somatic, the corporal," and so has no access to the psyche.
4. Neoliberalism, by contrast, turns to immaterial production and "has discovered the psyche as a productive force" — the body "no longer represents a central force of production."
5. Therefore biopolitical categories are structurally "unsuited to describing the neoliberal regime."
6. Conclusion: Foucault "failed... to make the turn to [[Psychopolitics|psychopolitics]]" — and what escaped him was the "engineering of freedom and exploitation... in order to effect self-exploitation."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Foucault's own admission, quoted by Han, that "in spite of everything, I really did intend to talk about biopolitics, and then, things being what they are, I have ended up talking at length, and maybe for too long, about neoliberalism" — and that the 1978-9 lectures, after invoking biopolitics, mention "neither biopolitics nor population again." ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/05 Foucault's Dilemma]])
- Foucault's "The Birth of Social Medicine," quoted to fix biopolitics as concerning "the biological, the somatic, the corporal" — "a politics of the body in the fullest sense."
- Foucault's turn to "technologies of the self" (*The Use of Pleasure*; *Technologies of the Self*), read by Han as the very hinge Foucault failed to follow into a critique of neoliberal power.
- Bernard Stiegler's observation that "Foucault's biopower... is no longer the force behind our age," recruited to corroborate biopolitics' obsolescence (though Han also faults Stiegler for over-weighting television and missing digital psychopolitics).
- Against [[Giorgio Agamben]]'s speculation that "Foucault's death kept him from showing how he would have developed the concept and study of biopolitics," Han counters that early death instead "deprived him of the chance to reevaluate his idea of biopolitics — and abandon it" for neoliberal psychopolitics.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The "technologies of the self" objection.** Han concedes Foucault "sensed the interrelation between the technology of the self and the technology of power" (the long Dartmouth note), which complicates the clean charge that the engineering of freedom wholly "escaped" him; the disagreement may be one of emphasis and follow-through rather than blind spot. (`Strongly implied`)
- **Does Han overbound biopolitics?** By tying biopolitics so tightly to the docile body, Han may underread how Foucault's own governmentality lectures already anticipate the entrepreneur-of-himself — making Han's "turn" less a missed discovery than an extension. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **The body's persistence.** Han's wager that the body "drops out" under immaterial production sits uneasily with his own examples — fitness, "sexiness," neuro-enhancement, cosmetic surgery — which re-inscribe a governed, marketed body the body/soul break may obscure. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **Successor vs. species.** Han insists psychopolitics is a *successor* to biopolitics, not a *form* of it, explicitly rejecting Alexandra Rau's and Thomas Lemke's framing of psychopolitics "as a biopolitical form of government." Whether the demography/psychogram distinction survives Big Data is exactly what the rejected reading contests. (`Directly stated`)
