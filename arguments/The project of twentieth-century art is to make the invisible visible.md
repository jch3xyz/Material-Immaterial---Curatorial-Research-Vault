# The project of twentieth-century art is to make the invisible visible

## Claim

The defining project of twentieth-century art has been to make the invisible visible — to be in touch with the unseen forces, fields, systems, relationships, connections, and transformations that constitute reality, and to render them perceptible — a task that exceeds the unaided eye and requires the technological extension of the senses.

## Expanded Explanation

In "Art and Education in the Telematic Culture" (1988), Roy Ascott states the thesis flatly: "the project of the art of our century has been essentially to make the invisible visible." Where a "nostalgic classicism" (marketed under a "(misunderstood) postmodernism") would return art to the appearance of things, Ascott insists that the real arc of modern and contemporary art runs the other way — toward what cannot be seen with the eye alone. Art "has progressively sought to be in touch with unseen forces and fields, systems, relationships, connections, and transformations and to make them visible." The visible object is not the destination; the invisible system behind it is.

The argument arises directly out of Ascott's polemic against the life drawing class, which he identifies as the central anomaly of the art academy. Life drawing, he charges, is "not where the living processes of the body are examined" but "where archaic codes of representation are rehearsed" — and there "we find the body immobilised, without mind; is that not the ideal of all repressive cultures?" The eye, drawing the still model, sees only the surface; "the human eye is insufficient to reveal the whole complexity of the living person." The body is in truth "a complex organism, made up of systems within systems, a subtle and continuous transformation of energy and matter" — i.e. precisely the invisible systems that art's true project must make visible. To reach them, observation "requires technological extensions of the senses to give access to the microscopic processes and macroscopic environments" that sustain the living model. The making-visible of the invisible is therefore not a matter of better looking but of extended perception.

This argument is the perceptual undercurrent of Ascott's larger telematic program: it is because reality is fundamentally a field of unseen systems and relationships that "art is all process, all system" rather than an object, and it anticipates the later Ascott arc of *cyberception* and *technoetics* — perception technologically extended into the domains (consciousness, networks, fields) that the unaided senses cannot reach. The claim thus carries both backward (the systems-esthetics lineage that already pushed art from object to system) and forward (the network/consciousness program) within the book.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The mature project of twentieth-century art is to make the invisible visible — to apprehend and render the unseen forces, fields, and systems behind appearances.
2. These unseen entities (e.g. the body as "systems within systems," "a subtle and continuous transformation of energy and matter") lie below the threshold of ordinary sight: "the human eye is insufficient to reveal the whole complexity of the living person."
3. The art academy's defense of life drawing — perfecting hand-to-eye coordination against the appearance of the model — therefore rehearses "archaic codes of representation" and yields only "the body immobilised, without mind."
4. To reach the invisible systems, perception must be extended: it "requires technological extensions of the senses to give access to the microscopic processes and macroscopic environments."
5. Conclusion: art's project of making the invisible visible is bound up with the technological extension of perception, not the cultivation of the unaided eye.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- "Invisible" is doing heavy and shifting work: it spans the literally sub-perceptual (microscopic and macroscopic processes accessible only via instruments) and the abstract or relational (systems, fields, relationships). The single thesis-sentence does not distinguish these registers, so "making the invisible visible" can read as either an empirical-instrumental claim or a metaphysical one.
- The historical generalization — that *the* project of *the* art of the century has *essentially* been this — is sweeping; it is asserted polemically (against "nostalgic classicism") rather than demonstrated across movements.
- The claim is normative as much as descriptive: Ascott deploys it to disqualify a rival practice (life drawing) and to license technological extension of the senses, so it functions partly as an argument *for* his telematic program rather than a neutral account of art history.
