# The posthuman is lethal only when grafted onto liberal humanism

## Claim

The posthuman is not dangerous as such; it becomes lethal only when it is grafted onto a [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist]] view of the self, which carries the autonomous subject's prerogatives — mastery, control, and the dream of immortality — into the posthuman rather than relinquishing them.

## Expanded Explanation

Hayles separates two things that the cultural imagination tends to fuse: the posthuman as a configuration of subjectivity, and the specific ideology onto which a given version of it is bolted. The terror provoked by the posthuman — visions of humans displaced or downloaded, the "end of humanity" — belongs not to the posthuman as such but to a particular construction of it. The decisive move in the Conclusion is to name the carrier of the danger precisely: "What is lethal is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self."

The exemplary case is Hans Moravec's downloading fantasy. On Hayles's reading, when Moravec imagines "you" choosing to download yourself into a computer and thereby securing immortality through technological mastery, he is not breaking with [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the autonomous liberal subject]] at all — he is *expanding its prerogatives* into the realm of the posthuman. The autonomous, will-exercising self does not die in the download; it is promised the ultimate privilege. This is why the apparently radical futurism of the cyberneticians is, structurally, deeply conservative: it preserves the very conception of the human (mastery, disembodied agency, control over nature) that the posthuman might otherwise dissolve. The posthuman here is recuperated back into liberal humanism.

Because the danger is located in the graft rather than in the posthuman itself, the diagnosis is also an opening. If the lethal element is detachable, then another posthuman is available — one "located within the dialectic of pattern/randomness and grounded in embodied actuality rather than disembodied information." This benign posthuman replaces autonomous will with [[Distributed Cognition|distributed cognition]], teleology with [[Emergence|emergence]], and a body-as-support-system with [[Embodiment|embodiment]]. The normative payload of the book turns on this single separation: the posthuman "need not be recuperated back into liberal humanism, nor need it be construed as antihuman."

## Reasoning Structure

1. The posthuman provokes terror — fantasies of human obsolescence, downloading, machines taking over — and this terror is widely treated as intrinsic to the posthuman itself.
2. But the terror traces to a specific construction: the posthuman *grafted onto* the liberal humanist self, not the posthuman as such.
3. Moravec's downloading scenario is the diagnostic case. It looks like the abandonment of the human, but it actually retains the autonomous liberal subject and merely *expands its prerogatives* — immortality through technological mastery — into the posthuman.
4. Therefore the lethal ingredient is the persistence of liberal-humanist assumptions (autonomous will, mastery, control, disembodied agency), not the posthuman frame.
5. If the lethal ingredient is detachable, a non-lethal posthuman becomes possible: one grounded in [[Embodiment|embodied actuality]] and the pattern/randomness dialectic rather than disembodied information.
6. Conclusion: the posthuman need not be antihuman or apocalyptic; the danger is contingent on the graft, and the graft can be refused.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The separation may be cleaner in theory than in practice.** Hayles concedes the benign reading is not guaranteed — it is "not necessarily what the posthuman will mean—only what it can mean if certain strands among its complex seriations are highlighted and combined." The argument states a possibility, not an inevitability; the lethal graft may be the default outcome of how the posthuman is actually being built.
- **"Lethal" is doing heavy and somewhat undefined work.** The word slides between literal scenarios (humans displaced as a species) and a normative-political sense (a self-conception that reinscribes imperialist mastery over nature). The argument's force depends on which sense is in play, and Hayles does not fully disentangle them.
- **One might object that the graft is constitutive, not contingent.** A critic could argue that the very desire to transcend the body — to become information — is already liberal-humanist mastery, so a posthuman "grounded in embodied actuality" is not the posthuman corrected but a different project altogether. On this view the separation Hayles needs may not hold.
- **It risks letting the embodied posthuman off too easily.** By concentrating the danger in liberal humanism, the argument can imply that an embodied, distributed posthuman is benign by default, understating the new forms of power and exclusion such systems can produce regardless of their stance on the autonomous subject.

## Tensions

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the lethal graft runs on disembodied information (downloading consciousness, leaving the "meat" behind); the benign posthuman is recovered by re-grounding it in embodied actuality. This argument is the normative edge of that opposition.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Moravec's immortality fantasy treats the self as immaterial pattern that can be ported; Hayles's counter insists on the material, evolutionary body as the limit that the graft tries to wish away.
