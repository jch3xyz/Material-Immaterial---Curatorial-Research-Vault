# Digital technology is an ideology of vacancy misread as the absence of philosophy

## Claim

Digital technology is not a philosophically neutral substrate but a full ideology whose central tenet is an "ontology of vacancy"; its conspicuous silence about beliefs and opinions is itself the content of its doctrine, not an absence of one, and through the viral/meme metaphor it reassigns agency from conscious subjects to self-propagating ideas.

## Expanded Explanation

O'Gieblyn opens the "Virality" chapter with a claim about how ideologies hide: "The most successful metaphors become invisible through ubiquity. The same is true of ideology, which, as it becomes thoroughly integrated into a culture, sheds its contours and distinctive outline and dissolves finally into pure atmosphere." Digital technology, she argues, has undergone exactly this dissolution — it "constitutes the basic architecture of the information age" yet "is rarely spoken of as a system of thought." The mistake is to read its refusal to hold "ideas or beliefs, preferences or opinions" as proof that it has no philosophy. That refusal *is* the philosophy. The invisibility born of ubiquity is misread as neutrality.

The doctrine has a structure. Its ontology is "a great emptying-out of qualities, content, and meaning" — being reduced to bare relation. This feeds an epistemology in which "knowledge lies not in concepts themselves but in the relationships that constitute them," discoverable by "artificial networks that lack any true knowledge of what they are uncovering." This is the same world-picture O'Gieblyn elsewhere traces to [[Cybernetics]] and the [[The Computational Universe|computational universe]] — and she names the lineage explicitly when she describes "that foundational creed of cybernetics: that information is a universal metaphor whose structures and models remain identical across multiple disciplines." Vacancy is what makes information feel substrate-independent: if being has no qualities, "Data is data" and "Numbers are universal."

The viral/meme metaphor is where the ontology of vacancy does its decisive work on agency. Borrowing Richard Dawkins's meme and Douglas Rushkoff's "viral media," the metaphor explains "how artifacts and ideologies reproduce themselves without the participation of conscious subjects." But this "entirely passive conception of human culture necessitates the awkward reassignment of agency to the ideas themselves — imagining that memes have 'goals' and 'ends'" — a reassignment "usually explained away as a figure of speech." The emptying-out of the subject and the animation of the idea are two faces of the same vacancy: once persons are reduced to "the nodes of this enormous brain," it becomes "increasingly difficult to speak of ourselves… as living agents with beliefs, preferences, and opinions." This continues O'Gieblyn's running engagement with the displacement of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] and with [[Posthumanism]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. Successful metaphors and integrated ideologies become invisible through ubiquity, dissolving into "pure atmosphere."
2. Digital technology has been thoroughly integrated and so is rarely recognized as a system of thought.
3. Its apparent inability to hold beliefs or opinions is therefore misread as an *absence* of philosophy.
4. But that very inability is a *description of its tenets*: the doctrine's central pillar is an ontology of vacancy — an emptying-out of qualities, content, and meaning.
5. From this ontology follows an epistemology in which knowledge is relation, not concept, computed by networks with no understanding of what they uncover (the cybernetic creed that information is universal).
6. The viral/meme metaphor extends this vacancy to agency: it explains cultural reproduction without conscious subjects and so must reassign agency to ideas themselves, which are imagined to have "goals" and "ends."
7. Conclusion: digital technology is an ideology of vacancy; its silence about beliefs is its central tenet, and it relocates agency from persons to self-propagating ideas.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The naive reading is precisely the counterargument O'Gieblyn anticipates: that digital technology is a neutral tool with no philosophy, and that talk of memes "having goals" is merely "a figure of speech." Her move is to deny that the figure of speech is innocent — but a critic could hold that personifying language is genuinely just convenient shorthand and carries no ontological commitment.
- "Ideology" and "ontology" are doing heavy interpretive lifting; the chapter asserts rather than systematically demonstrates that a coherent, singular doctrine underlies heterogeneous technologies.
- The early-internet optimism (Rushkoff's belief the network would benefit "people who lack traditional political power") shows the same architecture can be read as emancipatory before it is read as vacant; the vacancy thesis depends partly on which historical moment one privileges.
- The argument is essayistic and cultural rather than empirical: its evidence is metaphors, op-eds, and anecdotes, not a demonstration that engineers or systems in fact hold the metaphysics ascribed to them.
