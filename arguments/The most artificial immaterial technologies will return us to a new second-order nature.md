# The most artificial immaterial technologies will return us to a new second-order nature

## Claim

Ascott argues that the immateriality of the information society does not lead away from nature but back to it — the logical outcome of working in electronic space, joined by nanotechnology and artificial life, is the reinstatement of a natural materiality, returning us not to the old god-given nature but to a second-order, emergent "Nature II."

## Expanded Explanation

The chapter opens from the premise that we have lost touch with nature — not because nature retreated but because "the metaphors that it has long supported no longer hold." For Ascott, classical, pastoral, god-given nature has been irreversibly altered by twentieth-century physics and molecular biology and will, "by the twenty-first century," be "dead." Against the popular view that art's bond with nature lies in realistic landscape and figure painting — which he treats as a "cultural membrane" that kept us separated from the processes of nature — he holds that "technological and computerised systems" are what now provide "a threshold, an open doorway into the natural world."

The paradox at the chapter's core is deliberately staged as a question: "How is it that the most advanced technologies, electronic and molecular, the very epitome of the artificial, could bring us back to nature?" Ascott's answer is that "the logical outcome of our working in electronic space is to redefine our living in natural space," and, more pointedly, that "the logical outcome of the immateriality of the information society is the reinstatement in the twenty-first century of a natural materiality." This is not a return to the old metaphors of nature but the identification of "the metaphors of a new nature, second-order nature, emergent nature, Nature II," whose "engines of creation" will embrace [[Artificial Life]].

The mechanism is a convergence of two trajectories: an electronic one (computerised perception, [[Virtuality|virtualisation]], telematic communication) and a biological one (nanotechnology, molecular engineering, the organisation of matter and emergent behaviour). Their "marriage of the immaterial and the material" produces not pure disembodiment but a "rematerialization of art" — "new processes of materialisation" set beside the processes of virtualisation. The model for the relationship between observer and nature is second-order cybernetics, which folds the observer into the system; "second-order cybernetics and secondorder nature share in what can be called the connectivist paradigm," and through it we become "participants in the creative processes of nature" rather than outsiders looking in. This places the argument squarely in the lineage of [[New Media Art]] as [[Art as Experiential Technology]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. The old, anthropocentric nature has been dismantled by twentieth-century science and its metaphors no longer hold; by the twenty-first century it "will be dead."
2. Realistic painting acted as a membrane separating us from natural process; computerised and telematic systems instead open a doorway into it.
3. Working in immaterial electronic space therefore redefines, rather than abandons, our living in natural space.
4. The convergence of the electronic trajectory (virtualisation) with the biological trajectory (nanotechnology, molecular engineering, artificial life) yields not disembodiment but "new processes of materialisation."
5. Second-order cybernetics dissolves the boundary between observer and system, and between natural and artificial systems, making us participants in nature's creative processes.
6. Conclusion: the immateriality of the information society logically reinstates a natural materiality — a second-order, emergent Nature II.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is programmatic and predictive ("the early twenty-first century may see some success in this endeavour"); it forecasts a convergence rather than demonstrating an achieved one. Ascott himself concedes that much computer art "is little more than a replay of older art forms" and "not connectivist," so the Nature II he describes remains an aspiration.
- It leans on the speculative futurism of Drexler's nanotechnology and Moravec's robotics, and on contested holistic frameworks (Sheldrake's morphic resonance, Lovelock's Gaia, Bohm's implicate order) — "a science of metaphor, anti-reductionist, holistic, and intuitive" by his own description.
- "Natural materiality" risks equivocation: the chapter both pronounces nature "dead" and promises its reinstatement, resolving the contradiction only by redefining "nature" as a second-order construct, which a critic could read as relabeling rather than return.
