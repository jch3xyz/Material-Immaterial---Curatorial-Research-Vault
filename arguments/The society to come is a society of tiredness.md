# The society to come is a society of tiredness

## Claim

Against the burnout-producing society of activeness, Byung-Chul Han closes *The Burnout Society* with an affirmative, programmatic move: the society to come should be reimagined as a "society of tiredness" — a [[Community of Singularities]] founded on [[Negative Potency|the not-to]], an inspired, not-doing "Pentecostal company" that requires neither activity nor kinship nor function.

## Expanded Explanation

The book diagnoses the [[Achievement Society|achievement society]] as a "society of activeness" that, drained of [[Negativity]] and saturated by [[Positivity]], generates "excessive tiredness and exhaustion" — the solitary, divisive, world-destroying "I-tiredness" of the exhausted ego. Han's closing chapter does not stop at diagnosis; it offers a counter-figure. Reading Peter Handke's "Essay on Tiredness," Han distinguishes this isolating tiredness from a "trusting," "fundamental tiredness" that opens the I, makes room for the world, and founds a community needing neither belonging nor relation.

This is the affirmative core of the argument. [[Fundamental Tiredness|Fundamental tiredness]] is not a deficit, not a state of exhaustion in which one is unable to do anything; it is "a singular capacity" that "inspires," that "allows spirit/intellect" to emerge. Crucially, its inspiration is the inspiration of *not-doing*: it tells human beings "not so much what they should, as what they need not, be." This not-doing is the mark of [[Negative Potency|negative potency]] — the power of the not-to — as opposed to the positive potency whose excess produces collapse.

From this reframing of tiredness Han derives a reframing of society itself. The "Pentecostal company" Handke imagines — a gathering "tired to a man," inspired toward not-doing — stands opposed to the society of activity. Generalized, it names a [[Community of Singularities|community of singularities]] connected by a "friendly and," a community founded on the not-to that needs neither kinship nor function. If "Pentecostal company" is a synonym for the society of the future, then "the society to come might also be called a society of tiredness."

## Reasoning Structure

1. The society of activeness, poor in negativity and dominated by excess positivity, generates excessive, solitary, world-destroying tiredness and exhaustion (the burnout diagnosis).
2. But Handke's "Essay on Tiredness" names a second, opposite tiredness — "fundamental," "trusting," "we-tiredness" — that opens the I, makes room for the world, and founds a community requiring neither belonging nor relation.
3. This fundamental tiredness is not incapacity but a singular capacity that inspires; its inspiration is the inspiration of *not-doing*, telling people "what they need not, be."
4. Not-doing is negative potency (the not-to), opposed to the positive potency of "can" whose excess drives burnout.
5. The figure of a "Pentecostal company" — inspired, not-doing, "tired to a man" — names a community of singularities founded on the not-to, opposed to the society of activity.
6. Therefore the society to come should be reimagined as a society of tiredness.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Handke's account of "fundamental tiredness" as inspiration and as a singular capacity, not a state of exhaustion — [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/08 The Society of Tiredness]].
- The "Pindaric ode . . . to a tired man" and the "Pentecostal company that received the Holy Ghost as tired to a man," whose inspiration "tells them not so much what they should, as what they need not, be" — Handke, quoted by Han.
- The Dutch floral still life as Handke's image for the "all in one" — the community of singularities in which beetle, snail, bee, and butterfly "are all there together at the moment" without belonging or relation.
- The distinction between "the tiredness of positive potency" (which "makes one incapable of doing something") and "tiredness of negative potency, namely of not-to" (which "inspires").

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is programmatic and gestural rather than institutional: Han names what the society to come should *be* but supplies no mechanism by which a society of activeness becomes a society of tiredness. (`Strongly implied` — the chapter is hortatory in form.)
- It leans almost entirely on a single literary source (Handke's essay); the "society of tiredness" is borrowed and extended rather than independently theorized. (`Directly stated` — the chapter is structured as a reading of Handke.)
- The shift from an individual's "fundamental tiredness" to a collective "society of tiredness" is asserted by analogy with Handke's "we-tiredness" and the still-life image, not derived. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
