# Fulfilling humanism will destroy it by making humans irrelevant

## Claim

The same technologies that take humanism to its logical conclusion by upgrading humans into gods will simultaneously expose humanism's inherent flaws and render humans irrelevant, so that liberalism's very triumph contains the seeds of its ruin.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the spine argument of *Homo Deus* — the "gun in Act I" that Harari plants in the opening chapter and fires in the third part of the book. Its structure is dialectical rather than oppositional: the agenda that destroys humanism is not an external rival (a new totalitarianism, a hostile AI) but humanism *succeeding*. "While the attempt to upgrade humans into gods takes humanism to its logical conclusion, it simultaneously exposes humanism's inherent flaws." The catastrophe is the fulfilment of the dream, not its defeat. *(Directly stated)*

The mechanism is a single technological fact stated as a near-tautology. The drive to overcome ageing and death is the most humanist of projects — it follows from "an uncompromising humanist belief in the sanctity of human life." But the computational power required to achieve it is the same power that makes the achiever dispensable: "computers powerful enough to understand and overcome the mechanisms of ageing and death will probably also be powerful enough to replace humans in any and all tasks." There is no version of the upgrade that grants godlike power *over* death without granting the same machines godlike power to do everything humans do. The gift and the obsolescence are one capability. *(Directly stated)*

Harari ties this to liberalism specifically because liberalism is the humanist sect that won. By 2016 it "has no viable alternative," yet "its very success may contain the seeds of its ruin." The triumphant liberal ideals — "the voter knows best," "the customer is always right" — are precisely what push scientists and engineers, "egged on by the allegedly infallible wishes of customers and voters," toward immortality, bliss and divinity. And "what the scientists are discovering and what the engineers are developing may unwittingly expose both the inherent flaws in the liberal world view and the blindness of customers and voters." Once the technology exists "to calculate, design or outsmart their feelings," the free, feeling individual that liberalism sanctifies dissolves into "just another designable product." Liberalism is not refuted from outside; it is automated from within. *(Directly stated)* The claim that the resulting technologies are *post*-humanist rather than humanist — that this is humanism producing its own successor — is Harari's framing of why the move is fatal rather than merely transformative. *(Strongly implied)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. Humanism — and its winning branch, [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)|liberal humanism]] — sanctifies the life, happiness and power of the individual; carried to its conclusion this becomes [[The New Human Agenda|the agenda]] of immortality, bliss and divinity (the upgrade into [[Homo Deus|gods]]).
2. The upgrade requires computers powerful enough to understand and overcome ageing and death.
3. Computers that powerful are *also* powerful enough to replace humans "in any and all tasks" — the same capability does both.
4. Therefore the very project of fulfilling humanism produces the means to make humans irrelevant and to expose the liberal subject's free choices as calculable and designable.
5. Conclusion: humanism's triumph is the mechanism of its undoing — the gun in Act I fires in Act III; liberalism's success "may contain the seeds of its ruin."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Self-defeating by design.** Harari frames the whole *Homo Deus* forecast as "less of a prophecy and more a way of discussing our present choices" — "if the discussion makes us choose differently, so that the prediction is proven wrong, all the better." The argument is meant to be a warning that falsifies itself if heeded, which weakens it as a descriptive prediction.
- **From "can" to "will."** Step 3 turns on the assumption that a capability sufficient for one task is sufficient for "any and all tasks." This conflates the power to overcome ageing with general-purpose superintelligence; the two need not arrive together, and Harari himself is sceptical elsewhere that immortality is near ("the hopes of eternal youth in the twenty-first century are premature").
- **Irrelevance is not destruction.** Even granting that machines could replace humans in all tasks, it does not strictly follow that humanism (a system of *values*) is thereby refuted rather than merely outpaced — one might retain the sanctity of human experience while ceding economic and cognitive tasks.
- **Elite agenda, not humanity's.** The agenda is "what humankind as a collective will do," reached only by those "living in palaces" while billions "continue to deal with poverty, illness and violence" — so "humanity's" self-undoing may describe only a stratum.
