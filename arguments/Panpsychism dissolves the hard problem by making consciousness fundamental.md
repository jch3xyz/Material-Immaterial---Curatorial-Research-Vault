# Panpsychism dissolves the hard problem by making consciousness fundamental

## Claim

Panpsychism claims to dissolve [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|the hard problem of consciousness]] in a single move: because physics cannot say what matter *intrinsically* is, and because no one can explain how subjective experience arises from insentient stuff, the two unknowns can be identified — consciousness is the intrinsic nature of all matter — so that, as Russell and Eddington saw, "the two most notable 'gaps' in physicalism" could be "solved in one fell swoop." For [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] this is also a bid to *re-enchant* the universe; her reflexive worry is that the move may be the supreme anthropomorphism it claims to escape.

## Expanded Explanation

The argument is structurally a *double-gap* move. Modern materialism, [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] notes, "was a philosophical project, a thought experiment dreamed up in an armchair by the fire" — wildly successful at predicting the behavior of the physical world, yet unable to explain two things: consciousness and "the intrinsic nature of matter." Bertrand Russell and Arthur Eddington recognized that these are not two problems but one opportunity. Physics describes matter only relationally — what it *does*, never what it *is* — so the "problem of intrinsic natures" leaves a slot open; and the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]] leaves a phenomenon, subjective experience, that has nowhere to live in the physical ontology. Panpsychism slots one into the other: "Mental states were the intrinsic nature of physical states." Consciousness is not produced by matter; it *is* what matter, underneath the equations, fundamentally is.

This is why panpsychism, for its advocates, avoids the failure modes of both rival pictures. It escapes the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]] of materialism (there is no longer an explanatory gap between dead matter and felt experience, because there is no dead matter), and it escapes the *interaction problem* of dualism (there are not two substances that must somehow touch). It also disposes of the [[Emergence|emergence]] puzzle that O'Gieblyn singles out — "how consciousness somehow arose or emerged from matter during the blind march of evolution" — since "for the panpsychist, it was there from the beginning, in the smallest and earliest particles." Philip Goff presses the deeper point: physical science was "extremely successful when it ignores the sensory qualities," but that success "gives us no reason to think that it will be similarly successful if and when it turns its attention to the sensory qualities themselves." The exclusion of mind was the *condition* of physics' triumph, not evidence that mind can be recovered by it.

Beyond these "technical satisfactions," O'Gieblyn isolates the real draw: panpsychism is "primitively—perhaps even spiritually—appealing." It "essentially resurrects the Great Chain of Being," and Goff embraces the mysticism openly — it "offers a way of 're-enchanting' the universe. In the panpsychist view, the universe is like us; we belong in it." This is where O'Gieblyn's [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]] theme and her reflexive turn converge into the note's sting. Panpsychism is sold as an *escape from anthropocentrism* — Koch and Goff both "written passionately about the need to abandon anthropocentrism." But the early scientists who overthrew the enchanted worldview did so precisely because reading mind into nature is anthropomorphic: Bacon argued that attributing "ends" and "goals" to nature reflects "the nature of man rather than to the nature of the universe." So O'Gieblyn's question lands as a genuine aporia: "Is panpsychism in fact an escape from human centrality, as its advocates claim? Or is it merely another attempt to see all things in our image?" The book's recurring lesson — that [[Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image|metaphors are two-way streets]] — predicts the trap: the same projective reflex that made God in our image may now be making the cosmos conscious in our image.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Physics describes matter only relationally; it cannot say what matter *intrinsically* is — the "problem of intrinsic natures." (Gap 1)
2. Physicalism cannot explain how subjective experience arises from insentient matter — [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|the hard problem]]. (Gap 2)
3. Two unknowns invite identification: let consciousness *be* the intrinsic nature of physical states. "Mental states were the intrinsic nature of physical states."
4. Therefore both gaps close "in one fell swoop," and the [[Emergence|emergence]] and interaction problems dissolve — mind was fundamental from the start.
5. The payoff is not only technical but spiritual: the universe is "like us"; this "re-enchants" it and reconciles "the yearnings of faith and the rationality of science."
6. Reflexive counter-move (O'Gieblyn): seeing mind everywhere in nature is the textbook signature of anthropomorphism (Bacon, Galileo). So the re-enchantment marketed as an *escape* from human centrality may be its purest expression.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **O'Gieblyn's own reflexive worry — the anthropomorphism charge.** The decisive limitation, stated by the author: panpsychism advertises itself as an escape from human centrality, yet "seeing the world as broadly alive… is perhaps innate to us," and "humans are predisposed to believe all things have intelligence and agency." The framework purchased to dethrone anthropocentrism may simply be anthropomorphism with a metaphysics. This is the [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]]-as-projection thesis the chapter turns on. Label: `Directly stated`.
- **The combination problem.** O'Gieblyn flags panpsychism's "central problem": if neurons are conscious and a brain is billions of neurons, "why do I have only one mind and not billions?" Identifying mind with matter at the base does not yet explain how micro-experiences combine into the unified macro-experience of a self — Koch's integration-hierarchy answer is contested.
- **It re-describes rather than explains.** A skeptic can object that calling intrinsic nature "consciousness" relabels the mystery instead of dissolving it: positing proto-experience in quarks may be no more explanatory than positing it in brains, and is unfalsifiable.
- **Empirical underdetermination of the IIT route.** That [[Integrated Information Theory]] yields nonzero Φ for systems we take to be non-conscious (Watson, the internet — "it feels like something to be the internet") is, for critics, a *reductio* rather than a confirmation; it shows the threshold tracks integration, not felt experience.
- **Scope.** O'Gieblyn presents the case sympathetically ("I don't find panpsychism entirely absurd") but in a literary-philosophical register; the note reports advocates' arguments (Russell, Eddington, Goff, Strawson, Chalmers, Nagel) as she frames them, not a settled adjudication.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — panpsychism is the maximal collapse of this opposition: it does not spiritualize matter *against* the material, it declares the material to *be* mental at bottom, erasing "no clear boundary between the subjective mind and the objective world."
