# In conceptual art the form not the content carried the politics

## Claim

Although most Conceptual art "seems supremely apolitical," its political charge lived in its form — the de-commodification of the object, the breaking of the frame, the attack on originality and the "genius theory" — and not in its content.

## Expanded Explanation

[[Lucy Lippard]]'s standard puzzle, posed by younger students of the period, is why she insists on describing [[Conceptual Art]] in political terms when, "looking back, most of it seems supremely apolitical." Her answer relocates the politics. With a few exceptions the content of the work was apolitical; what was radical was the *form* — the genre's structural refusal of the art object as commodity, of the frame as boundary, and of originality as the seat of value. The political message rode the medium, not the message: "However, it was usually the form rather than the content of Conceptual art that carried a political message. The frame was there to be broken out of" (`Directly stated`). This is why an art that named no names and pictured no struggle could still read as insurgent: anti-establishment fervor "focused on the de-mythologization and de-commodification of art," on an "alternative" art "that could not be bought and sold."

The sharpest form of this formal politics is the attack on authorship. When [[Daniel Buren]], Olivier Mosset, and Niele Toroni invited critics to make or claim their identical striped paintings, or when Carl Andre's outdoor hay bales "never enters the property state," the target was the romance of the unique hand. Lippard reads this as a class attack: the assault on "originality, 'the artist's touch,' and the competitive aspects of individual style constituted an attack on the genius theory, the hitherto most cherished aspect of patriarchal, ruling-class art" (`Directly stated`). The politics is thus carried by [[Dematerialization]] itself — by what the work refuses to be (a saleable, signed, framed object), not by what it depicts. The genre tilts hard toward the immaterial pole of [[Material vs Immaterial]], and the tilt *is* the argument.

This thesis is deliberately held open, not resolved, by Lippard's own later self-criticism. The Postface diagnoses Conceptual art's political *failure*: it "has not, however, as yet broken down the real barriers between the art context and those external disciplines—social, scientific, and academic—from which it draws sustenance" (`Directly stated`). The de-commodifying wager collapsed — "the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums" and "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries" — and the movement stayed sealed inside a "ghetto mentality ... bound by invisible apron strings to the 'real world's' power structure" (`Directly stated`). The form carried the politics, but the form could not escape the market or the art world's own structural dependence on power. That is the tension this argument exists to keep open, not to close.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise: Most Conceptual art "seems supremely apolitical" in its content — it names no names, depicts no struggle.
2. Premise: Yet the genre's *form* enacts a structural refusal — de-commodification of the object, breaking the frame, and an attack on originality/the "genius theory."
3. Premise: Because authorship and ownership are intertwined, dismantling the unique signed object is itself a political act against "patriarchal, ruling-class art."
4. Conclusion (Lippard's): The political message of Conceptual art is carried by its form, not its content.
5. Self-correction (Postface): That formal politics nonetheless *failed* — it did not break the barriers to external disciplines, it was reabsorbed by the market, and the art world stayed bound by "invisible apron strings" to the power structure. The autonomy-vs-engagement tension therefore stays open.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The "supremely apolitical" framing and its relativization — radical only against a Greenbergian art world "that denied even the presence of political concerns." See [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]].
- The de-commodification program: an "independent (or 'alternative') art that could not be bought and sold" by "the greedy sector that owned everything." See [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]].
- The attack on authorship: [[Daniel Buren]]/Mosset/Toroni's interchangeable paintings, Jan Dibbets' refusal to sell, Carl Andre's hay bales that "never enters the property state" — culminating in the assault on "the genius theory." See [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]].
- The named exceptions whose *content* was political — [[Hans Haacke]] (the censored 1971 Guggenheim real-estate piece), the Guerrilla Art Action Group, Luis Camnitzer — prove the rule by contrast. See [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]].
- The Postface's failure diagnosis: barriers to external disciplines unbroken; reabsorption by the market; the "ghetto mentality" and "invisible apron strings." See [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/11 Postface]].

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Dematerialization did not escape the market — Lippard is self-critical.** The central wager (that an art of xerox sheets, photographs, and unrealized projects would be "forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status") proved "for the most part unfounded"; within three years the major conceptualists were selling for substantial sums in the world's most prestigious galleries. If the form's politics was de-commodification and the object was re-commodified, the political charge was at best contingent and at worst illusory.
- **The form/content split is porous.** Lippard's own named exceptions — [[Hans Haacke]], [[Daniel Buren]], [[Adrian Piper]], [[Douglas Huebler]], the Rosario group — are precisely the artists whose *content* carried politics, which suggests the most durable political work fused the two rather than relying on form alone.
- **The autonomy that made the form radical also sealed the ghetto.** The Postface concedes that "art that begins with other than an internal, esthetic goal rarely produces anything more than illustration or polemic" — the same aesthetic autonomy that let the form carry politics also kept the movement "confined to art quarters, usually by choice," unable to reach external disciplines or the "mythical 'masses.'"
- **"Apolitical" is relative.** Lippard grants the content was apolitical "with a few exceptions"; the radicalism was partly an artifact of a Greenbergian art world so depoliticized that any gesture looked like dissent. Measured against the actual activism of the sixties, the art "looks timid and disconnected."

## Tensions

- [[Autonomy vs Engagement]] — this argument holds the pole open: the form's politics depended on aesthetic autonomy (de-commodified, self-referential), yet that same autonomy is what the Postface blames for the failure to engage external disciplines and the world. (Forward-stub tension.)
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the political charge is carried by the tilt toward the immaterial (the refused, dematerialized object); the argument is an art-historical instance of how immateriality is made to bear a politics, and of how that politics fails when "a piece of paper... is as much an object" and as saleable as any other.
