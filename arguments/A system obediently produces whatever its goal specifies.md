# A system obediently produces whatever its goal specifies

## Claim

A system obediently produces exactly what its goal specifies, so a badly defined goal makes the system faithfully manufacture the wrong result; the remedy is to measure and report what you actually want.

## Expanded Explanation

For Meadows the goal is not a decorative statement of intention but the direction-setter of the whole system: the standard against which balancing [[Feedback Loops]] measure discrepancy and toward which they work. Because the loops are indifferent to meaning and only compare the actual state against the specified indicator, whatever indicator you install is what the system will pursue with full obedience. If the indicator is wrong — if it fails to measure the thing you truly care about — the system will still succeed at it, and in succeeding it produces the wrong world. This is why she likens systems to the three wishes of the fairy tale: they have "a terrible tendency to produce exactly and only what you ask them to produce."

The wrong-goal problem therefore does not look like malfunction. In the [[System Traps]] archetype [[Seeking the Wrong Goal]], the system is not evading or cheating its rule (that would be rule beating); it is dutifully obeying it. National security defined as military spending yields military spending; good education measured by standardized-test performance yields test performance; India's early family-planning program measured by the number of IUDs implanted yielded implantations without consent; a society whose goal is GNP produces GNP rather than welfare, equity, justice, or efficiency. In every case the failure is upstream of the machinery — in the specification, not the execution.

This same mechanism grounds Meadows's ranking of goals as leverage point #3 in [[Leverage Points]], a place to intervene superior to a system's self-organizing ability. Because the goal sits above the lower structural levels, everything beneath it — physical stocks and flows, information flows, [[Feedback Loops]], even [[Self-Organization]] — is bent into the service of that goal. A goal of engulfing more and more of the world under one central planning system will "twist to conform" every capacity below it. The corrective is to specify indicators that reflect the real welfare of the system, and never to confuse effort with result — or you will build a system that reliably produces effort and no result.

## Reasoning Structure

1. A system's goal is the indicator its balancing [[Feedback Loops]] work toward — the definer of discrepancy and the target of corrective action.
2. Feedback loops are obedient and semantically blind: they drive the system toward whatever indicator is specified, not toward whatever was actually intended.
3. Therefore, if the goal is defined "inaccurately or incompletely," the system will faithfully produce a result that no one really wants — a success at the wrong thing, not a malfunction.
4. Because the goal governs the levels beneath it, a wrong goal twists stocks, flows, information, and even [[Self-Organization]] into conforming to it — hence its outsized leverage.
5. Conclusion: fix the goal, not the machinery — measure and report what you actually want, and do not confuse effort with result.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Meadows notes goals are among the harder leverage points to move: even people inside a system rarely recognize the whole-system goal they are serving ("To make profits," she observes, is only a rule, a condition for staying in the game, not the goal).
- The prescription "measure and report what you want" presumes the real welfare of a system can be specified as an indicator at all; some intended goods may resist reduction to any single measurable proxy, which is precisely why the trap recurs.
- Goals of subsystem loops (a thermostat setting, an inventory level) are genuinely useful leverage points; the wrong-goal trap is most dangerous at the level of the whole system, where the goal is least obvious.

## Tensions

- [[Central Control vs Autonomy]] — the wrong-goal trap is sharpest under central control, where one dominating goal twists every subordinate loop, including self-organization, into conformity; distributing goal-setting is one hedge against a single mis-specified goal capturing the whole system.
