# Surveillance capitalism completes itself by adding actuation to prediction

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism completes itself by adding *actuation* to prediction: because a prediction built only on correlation is an extrapolation from the past, the system must intervene in the real world to approximate certainty, and so it crosses from knowing behavior to acting on it.

## Expanded Explanation

The first phase of surveillance capitalism extracts [[Behavioral Surplus]] and refines it into [[Prediction Products]] sold in behavioral futures markets. But Zuboff argues in Chapter 10 that prediction alone is structurally insufficient. Drawing on Google's own chief economist Hal Varian, she notes that data "can only measure correlation, not causality": data tell what happened but not why, so "even the best predictions are only extrapolations from the past." A merely predictive surveillance capitalism would remain a forecaster, never a guarantor — and it has promised its customers *guaranteed outcomes*.

The resolution is intervention. To close "the gap between prediction and observation in order to approximate certainty," the apparatus must move from observing behavior to shaping it. Engineers call this new capability *actuation* — "sensors can also be actuators" — and Zuboff treats it as the decisive, largely undiscussed turning point: the moment [[Economies of Action]] complete [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]]. Actuation is operationalized through three techniques — tuning, herding, and conditioning ([[Telestimulation]]) — that herd behavior "along a path of heightened probability that approximates certainty." This is the literal sense of the chapter's title: "we let the music make them dance."

The capstone of the argument is a recursion. The most predictive raw material of all is *behavior that has already been modified*. Once the system can intervene, the surplus it harvests is no longer found behavior but manufactured behavior, pre-oriented toward the guaranteed outcomes the firm has sold. Prediction and actuation fold into a single loop in which acting on behavior makes that behavior more predictable, which licenses more action — the same recursion captured in [[Prediction enacts the very fate it foresees]] and pushed to its limit in [[The goal is no longer to know our behavior but to automate us]]. This is what completes surveillance capitalism: not better knowing, but [[A Utopia of Certainty]] purchased through ubiquitous intervention in the real world.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism sells [[Prediction Products]] under [[The Prediction Imperative]], and its customers want certainty ("guaranteed outcomes").
2. But data yield only correlation, not causality; absent causal knowledge, predictions are mere extrapolations from the past — they cannot guarantee.
3. Causality is established only by *intervening* (running experiments); to approximate certainty the system must therefore act on behavior, not just observe it.
4. This intervention is *actuation* / [[Economies of Action]]: tuning, herding, conditioning that modify behavior in real time in the real world.
5. The most predictive surplus of all is behavior that has *already been modified* — so actuation also improves the raw material, closing a self-reinforcing loop.
6. Conclusion: the system completes itself by adding actuation to prediction, moving from knowing behavior to acting on it.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Engineers' own testimony that "sensors can also be actuators" and that the aim is now "ubiquitous intervention, action, and control" — actuation as the undiscussed turning point ([[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/12 Chapter 10 - Make Them Dance]]).
- Varian's concession that big data "can only measure correlation, not causality," and his prescription to "run experiments" continuously to "understand causality" — the explicit causal gap that actuation closes.
- The three approaches to economies of action — tuning, herding, conditioning ("nudge," the "uncontract," "make the music make them dance") — herding behavior toward "a path of heightened probability that approximates certainty."
- The recursion claim: "The most predictive source of all is behavior that has already been modified to orient toward guaranteed outcomes."
- The Facebook contagion experiments and Pokémon Go ("an experimental facsimile of surveillance capitalism's design for our future") as living laboratories of actuation at scale.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Zuboff concedes one can still imagine "automated behavioral modification without surveillance capitalism" (and benign feedback devices owned by their users); the harm is in the *marriage* of modification to the economic orientation, not in actuation as such.
- The measured behavioral effects (e.g., Facebook's emotional contagion) are real but described as "weak"; the argument concerns the structural imperative and trajectory, not the magnitude of any single experiment.
- Actuation works "just beyond the rim of individual awareness," which makes the claim hard to falsify by appeal to felt experience — but Zuboff treats covertness as part of the design, not a weakness in the analysis.
