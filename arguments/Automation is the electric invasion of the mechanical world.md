# Automation is the electric invasion of the mechanical world

## Claim

Automation is commonly mistaken for the culmination of the machine age — bigger, faster, more specialized mechanism. McLuhan's countervailing claim is that automation is not an extension of mechanical principles at all but their supersession: "the invasion of the mechanical world by the instantaneous character of electricity." Because electricity is "sheer information," automation belongs to the electric paradigm of instant synchronization, feedback, and organic interdependence, not to the mechanical paradigm of fragmentation, lineal sequence, and direct power-to-work coupling.

## Expanded Explanation

The argument is built as a sharp contrast between two technological logics. The **mechanical** logic works by fragmentation — it breaks "processes into homogenized but unrelated bits," sets up operations "in lineal sequence," and couples power directly to the work done (hand and hammer, water and wheel, horse and cart, steam and piston). The **electric** logic, by contrast, works by "instant synchronization of numerous operations," which "has ended the old mechanical pattern of setting up operations in lineal sequence." McLuhan's epigram for the death of the mechanical order is "The assembly line has gone the way of the stag line": the lineal, sequential arrangement of discrete operations is precisely what electric speed erases.

The structural hinge of the argument is that in any automatic machine "the generation and transmission of power is quite separate from the work operation that uses the power" — what McLuhan calls the property of "all servo-mechanist structures that involve feedback." Electric energy "can be applied indifferently and quickly to many kinds of tasks," "much as light itself illuminates a total field and does not dictate what shall be done." This separation of power from process is the mark of the electric, and it is "as basic" to automation "as tactility to the TV image." Where mechanical power was always in direct relation to the work done, electric power brings "a strange elasticity," because — and this is the identity that runs through the whole chapter — electricity is information, and "both energy and information can be applied in a great variety of ways."

McLuhan reinforces the claim with his recurring analogy of how a new technology retains only the residue of the old: "Automation retains only as much of the mechanical character as the motorcar kept of the forms of the horse and the carriage." To discuss automation as if it were merely more or faster mechanization is, in his mock-image, to behave "as if we had not passed the oat barrier, and as if the horse-vote at the next poll would sweep away the automation regime." This is why those involved in automation "insist that it is a way of thinking, as much as it is a way of doing" — it is a paradigm shift, not a quantitative increment. The argument is the industrial-economic register of the book's master opposition [[Mechanical vs Electric]] and supplies the structural basis for the chapter's concept [[Automation as Learning a Living]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. Mechanization works by fragmentation, lineal sequence, and direct coupling of power to work. *(Directly stated)*
2. Automation works by instant synchronization, feedback (an information loop), and the separation of power from the work operation. *(Directly stated)*
3. The separating property — that electric energy "can be applied indifferently... to many kinds of tasks" — is the signature of electricity, which is "sheer information." *(Directly stated)*
4. Therefore automation is not an extension of mechanical principles but "the invasion of the mechanical world by the instantaneous character of electricity"; it belongs to the electric, not the mechanical, paradigm. *(Directly stated)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The contrast is drawn schematically; McLuhan grants that automation "certainly assumes the servomechanism and the computer," i.e. it presupposes machines even as it supersedes the mechanical *principle*. The claim is about logic/paradigm, not about the literal absence of mechanism.
- The "way of thinking, not just a way of doing" formulation is asserted on the authority of "those involved in automation" rather than demonstrated, though it is consistent with the book's media-as-environment thesis.
