# Electric implosion must be met by decentralism not expansion

## Claim

A one-directional technological thrust calls for a countervailing thrust: the imploding energy of the electric age cannot be answered by enlargement or expansion but only by decentralism and the flexibility of multiple small centers.

## Expanded Explanation

In the strategic argument that closes Chapter 7, McLuhan converts his diagnostic schema of [[Challenge and Collapse]] into a prescription. The book's master claim — that [[Explosion vs Implosion|electric speed reverses explosion into implosion]] — has so far been descriptive. Here it becomes a rule of action: "When the technology of a time is powerfully thrusting in one direction, wisdom may well call for a countervailing thrust." Because the dominant thrust of the century is implosive, the wrong response is the reflex of the mechanical age — to meet pressure with more outward expansion. "The implosion of electric energy in our century cannot be met by explosion or expansion, but it can be met by decentralism and the flexibility of multiple small centers." The countervailing thrust to implosion is not bigness but plurality: many small autonomous centers rather than one enlarged plant.

McLuhan's governing example is the university. The crowding of students into higher education looks like growth, but it is misread if treated as expansion: "the rush of students into our universities is not explosion but implosion." Diagnosed correctly as implosion, it dictates its own remedy. "And the needful strategy to encounter this force is not to enlarge the university but to create numerous groups of autonomous colleges in place of our centralized university plant that grew up on the lines of European government and nineteenth-century industry." The centralized university is itself a relic of the explosive, industrial order; to enlarge it is to answer implosion with the very logic that implosion has rendered obsolete. The same diagnosis governs television: "the excessive tactile effects of the TV image cannot be met by mere program changes" but demand "a corresponding depth or structural approach to the existing literary and visual world."

The argument is buttressed by two historical precedents of decentralism McLuhan draws from Toynbee and Mumford. Toynbee "gives an example of Renaissance technology being effectively encountered and creatively controlled when he shows how the revival of the decentralized medieval parliament saved English society from the monopoly of centralism that seized the continent." Mumford, in *The City in History*, "tells the strange tale of how the New England town was able to carry out the pattern of the medieval ideal city because it was able to dispense with walls and to mix town and country." Both are cases in which a wall-less, distributed form withstood a new technological force that a centralized form could not. McLuhan reinforces the point by noting, against the cult of bigness, that Toynbee "not only abandons the concept of enlargement as a criterion of real growth of society" but holds that "geographical expansion is a concomitant of real decline." The cautionary counter-case is scholasticism: the oral Schoolmen "did not meet the new visual challenge of print," and "the resulting expansion or explosion of Gutenberg technology was in many respects an impoverishment of the culture" — a collapse that follows precisely from failing to mount the countervailing, structural response.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Each technology has a directional "thrust"; the dominant thrust of the electric century is implosion, not explosion (`Directly stated`).
2. A powerful one-way thrust calls not for amplification but for "a countervailing thrust" — wisdom answers a force with its opposite (`Directly stated`).
3. Therefore implosion "cannot be met by explosion or expansion" — answering it with enlargement applies the obsolete mechanical logic to a force that has already reversed it (`Directly stated`).
4. The countervailing thrust to implosion is "decentralism and the flexibility of multiple small centers" — plurality, not bigness.
5. Worked case: the student "rush" is implosion misread as explosion; its remedy is "numerous groups of autonomous colleges," not an enlarged centralized university plant (`Directly stated`).
6. Historical confirmation: the revived decentralized medieval parliament and the wall-less New England town survived new technological forces a centralized form could not; conversely, the centralized Schoolmen failed to meet print and were swept aside (`Directly stated` / `Strongly implied`).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The prescription rests on the prior descriptive claim that the age's thrust *is* implosion; if [[Explosion vs Implosion|the explosion-into-implosion reversal]] is a terminological schema rather than a measurable dynamic, the strategic rule inherits that softness.
- "Decentralism and the flexibility of multiple small centers" is stated as a principle but not operationalized — McLuhan offers historical analogies (parliament, town, autonomous colleges) rather than a mechanism for distinguishing genuine decentralization from mere administrative fragmentation.
- The university example assumes the "rush of students" is best read as implosion at all; a critic could hold it is straightforwardly demographic and economic expansion, in which case enlargement, not multiplication of centers, would be the fitting response.
- The historical precedents are read analogically; that decentralized forms survived past technological challenges does not establish that decentralization is the necessary or sufficient answer to electric implosion.
