# Digital co-production is a second order of reproduction beyond Benjamin

## Claim

Roy Ascott argues that the digital, hypermedia image inaugurates a "second order of reproduction" that supersedes Walter Benjamin's age of mechanical reproduction: mechanical reproduction multiplied copies of an image, whereas digital co-production lets the viewer transform the image itself, repositioning the spectator as a manipulator and distributing authorship across time.

## Expanded Explanation

In "Photography at the Interface" (1992) Ascott periodizes the history of the reproduced image. He takes Benjamin's "work of art in the age of mechanical reproduction" as the *first* order — the regime in which a technology multiplies copies of an already-made image — and names a *second* order that comes after it: "the stage of co-production." The break is not quantitative (more copies, faster, more ubiquitous) but ontological. Once the image is "flowing in data space," its meanings come unfixed, and the technology no longer merely *reproduces* the image but *transforms* it. This is Ascott's photographic instance of his wider thesis that interactive and telematic media convert spectatorship into participation — the logic that animates [[Reciprocal Communication]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]].

What changes "from the old economy of the image," for Ascott, is agency. The "processes of transformation" pass "into the hands of the viewer as much as the artist." The viewer is repositioned as "a manipulator of that image, as the one in whose hands the destiny of the image may lie" — able, with interactive media, to "distort, collage, dismember, fractionise, and in every way transform the image at will." Ascott distinguishes two layers of co-production stacked one on the other: an older co-production "between the photographer and the viewing subject in the realm of *meaning*," and now, on top of it, "a technology of co-production and transformation of the *image itself*." The second order is the second of these — co-production of the image as object, not merely of its interpretation.

Ascott also situates the shift in time as well as authorship. The "opening" of the image effects a "distribution, across time, of authorship at the public level," with — he predicts — "unprecedented effects both on the perceived legitimacy of the photograph and on the perceived authenticity and cultural status of the photographic artist." Authorship is no longer a single originating act but an ongoing, distributed, public process whose terminus is wherever the next manipulator takes the image. In this sense the second order finishes a demolition Benjamin's first order had only begun: it corrodes the very categories — authenticity, originality, the cultural status of the author — that mechanical reproduction had already started to destabilize (`Strongly implied`). The argument is a substantive *engagement* with Benjamin: he is the named prior term, cited directly ("[Benjamin 1969]") to mark the boundary Ascott is crossing; see [[The Second Order of Reproduction]] for the concept and [[Walter Benjamin]] for the anchoring figure.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Benjamin's mechanical reproduction is the *first* order: a technology that multiplies copies of an existing image (`Directly stated`).
2. In digital hypermedia, the image enters "data space," where stable, "stand-alone meanings and conceptual linearities disappear" — the image becomes permeable and manipulable rather than fixed (`Directly stated`).
3. The "processes of transformation" are therefore "in the hands of the viewer as much as the artist": the spectator becomes a manipulator/co-author (`Directly stated`).
4. This stacks a *second* co-production (of the image itself) on top of the older co-production of meaning between photographer and viewer (`Directly stated`).
5. The image's "opening" distributes authorship "across time" and "at the public level," eroding the legitimacy/authenticity/cultural status that mechanical reproduction had already destabilized (`Directly stated` for the distribution; `Strongly implied` for finishing Benjamin's demolition).
6. Therefore digital co-production is a qualitatively new, *second* order of reproduction — beyond, not merely a faster version of, Benjamin's first order (`Directly stated`).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Continuity, not rupture.** One could read the "second order" as a difference of degree, not kind — mechanical reproduction already loosened the image from a fixed original (Benjamin's loss of aura), so digital manipulability may extend rather than supersede the first order. Ascott himself cautions against total opposition ("It's simultaneously a case of both 'both/and' as well as 'either/or'").
- **Whose empowerment?** The "empowerment" of the viewer-manipulator can be read, against Ascott's optimism, as continuous with the participation/self-exposure logics other vault authors treat as control rather than liberation (cf. [[The digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure]]; [[Big Data is blind to the event and so blind to the future]]).
- **A coming third order.** Ascott concedes the human co-producer may soon be displaced: "just around the corner . . . is the artificial observer, the eye of the neural net, the artificial intelligence that will surely become a part of the observing system." If a non-human manipulator enters the loop, the "second order" framed around the viewing *subject* may itself be provisional.
- **Provenance caveat.** Benjamin was trimmed from the working book corpus; he appears here only as a figure Ascott cites by name, so [[Walter Benjamin]] is a forward-stub anchor, not a vault-ingested source.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the argument sits on the immaterial pole: the chemically fixed material print (silver grains) gives way to the manipulable, immaterial pixel "flowing in data space," and the stable object yields to a process; this is the same object→system / image→data spine that makes `Material vs Immaterial` an eight-author tension.
- [[Object vs System]] — Ascott's repositioning of the finished image as an open, manipulable field restates Burnham's object→system shift on the side of the image.
