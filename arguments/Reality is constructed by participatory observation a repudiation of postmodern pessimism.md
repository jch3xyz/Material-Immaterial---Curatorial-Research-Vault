# Reality is constructed by participatory observation a repudiation of postmodern pessimism

## Claim

From the quantum-physical premise that "reality is the production of observation," Ascott derives a constructivist, perspectival, indeterminate epistemology in which computer networking lets us construct and inhabit multiple coexisting realities — a *constructive* answer to the "deconstructive pessimism of late postmodern art."

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott takes the observer-dependence of quantum measurement not as a curiosity of physics but as the model for a general theory of reality. In the *Gesamtdatenwerk* essay (ch.18) he proposes that, since "the subject of computer science is the transformation of information, and data are its object," one can "replace the term 'quanta' with 'data'" and so invoke "a physics of information" — for "it is in the quantum world that reality is the production of observation." Reality, on this telling, is not a fixed ground that observation passively records but an outcome that observation *precipitates*. Immersion in electronic global networks therefore drives "a re-evaluation of the status of reality; to an understanding of its provisional nature, as one of many co-existing realities, all of which are constructed—'virtual' in a sense—and dependent upon our active participation for their construction."

What distinguishes Ascott's position from the relativism of his contemporaries is its affirmative charge. The dissolution of a single, stable, mind-independent reality is, for the late-postmodern sensibility, a cause for mourning — "aesthetic deprivation," "semantic nihilism," the "suicidal art of solipsistic inversion" that produces only "representations of representation." Ascott rejects this reading. Multiplicity and indeterminacy are not a loss but an opening: the network is the instrument through which we can "deal creatively with relativism and with pluralism," constructing and entering alternative realities rather than lamenting the loss of the one true one. He names this directly as "a radically constructive repudiation of the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought" (ch.18).

In "Beyond Time-Based Art" (ch.19) the same opposition is restated as a program for art itself. Telematic art "rejects representation in favour of simulation"; pursued in networked deep data space, the postmodern impulse to play with time and layer experience — "not entirely exhausted" — yields, in place of "the deconstructive pessimism of late postmodern art," the hoped-for "emergence of a radically constructive art" of "parallel time-worlds." The repudiation is thus *intra-postmodern*: Ascott does not reject the perspectival, anti-foundational insight of postmodernism but turns it from a deconstructive, nihilistic register into a constructive, world-building one. This is the epistemological engine beneath his larger program — if reality is constructed, plural, and participation-dependent, the networked interactive artwork is not a representation of the world but an apparatus for the collaborative *production* of new ones (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise (physics):** In the quantum world "reality is the production of observation" — measurement does not record a pre-given state but produces it (observer-created reality).
2. **Premise (transfer):** Replacing "quanta" with "data" yields "a physics of information"; the same observer-dependence governs the data field. Reality is therefore provisional, plural, "virtual," and dependent on active participation for its construction.
3. **Premise (instrument):** Computer networking is the medium through which we "deal creatively with relativism and with pluralism" — constructing and entering multiple coexisting realities.
4. **Premise (diagnosis):** Late postmodern (deconstructivist) art reads the loss of stable truth as "aesthetic deprivation and semantic nihilism," a "deconstructive pessimism."
5. **Conclusion:** Networked constructivism is not a deepening of that nihilism but its *repudiation* — "a radically constructive repudiation of the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought," issuing in "a radically constructive art."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The observer-dependence of quantum measurement: "For it is in the quantum world that reality is the production of observation" (ch.18); the eigenstate/wave-collapse read literally as observer-created reality elsewhere in the book.
- The data↔quanta analogy: "replace the term 'quanta' with 'data' and a physics of information is invoked" (ch.18).
- Philosophers named as pointing toward constructed, processual, perspectival reality — Henri Bergson and Friedrich Nietzsche; physicists named for indeterminacy/nonlocality — Heisenberg, Schrödinger, John Bell, Alain Aspect (ch.18).
- The diagnosis of "deconstructivist postmodernism" as "representations of representation," "aesthetic deprivation and semantic nihilism" (ch.19), against which "a radically constructive art" is set.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The physics may be metaphor, not argument.** Ascott himself elsewhere concedes the physicists' ideas are "scandalously metaphysical at heart"; the inference from observer-dependence at the quantum scale to a cultural epistemology of "co-existing realities" is an analogy whose warrant is contestable.
- **What constrains construction?** If reality is "built by us," it is unclear what prevents any model from being as real as any other; the affirmative "constructive" claim may smuggle in non-arbitrary criteria (coherence, solidarity) it does not name here.
- **Does networked multiplicity overcome relativism or merely automate it?** The "radically constructive repudiation" asserts that networking *answers* postmodern pessimism, but it may simply technologize the very relativism it claims to transcend.
- **The repudiation is partial.** Ascott keeps the perspectival, anti-foundational core of postmodernism and rejects only its "deconstructive" mood — so the claim is an intramural correction, not a refutation of postmodern epistemology as such.
