# All media must be studied together because any extension reorganizes the whole psychic and social field

## Claim

Because every extension of man reorganizes the entire psychic and social complex, media cannot be understood one at a time; the proper object of study is their interplay, since no medium has meaning or existence alone.

## Expanded Explanation

This is McLuhan's methodological thesis — the rule of procedure that governs how *Understanding Media* must be read. It follows directly from his master frame [[Media as Extensions of Man]]: if each medium is an extension of a body part, sense, or faculty, and if every such extension "affects the whole psychic and social complex," then no extension can be assessed in isolation. The effect of any one medium is a reorganization of the entire field — the sense ratios of the body and the relations of the social body alike — so the analyst who studies a medium alone is studying a fiction abstracted from the only place it actually has effects. McLuhan states the requirement plainly in the Introduction: "There is little possibility of answering such questions about the extensions of man without considering all of them together." `Directly stated`

In Chapter 2 the methodological claim is sharpened from a procedural caution into an ontological one. McLuhan ends the chapter's argument about hot and cool media by naming the "concentric" or "constant interplay" method as "the technique of insight, and as such is necessary for media study, since no medium has its i meaning or existence alone, but only in constant interplay with other media." The shift is from "you cannot answer the questions one at a time" to "the thing you would study does not exist one at a time." A medium's meaning is relational and its very existence is relational: a medium is what it is only in the configuration of media that surrounds it, the way a hot medium like print "fragments" the tribal field that a cool electric medium will later "retribalize." `Directly stated`

This is why McLuhan opposes the linear, fragmenting "single vision" of the mechanical age — the "old lineal and fragmentary procedures and tools of analysis" — to the "concentric" technique fit for the electric age. The fragmenting method treats subjects one plane at a time; the concentric method, modeled on the spiral and on oral cultures' "constant interplay," holds the whole configuration in view at once. Studying media singly is itself a residue of the typographic, specialist habit of mind (see [[Typographic Man]]); the interplay method is the form of thought the electric [[The Global Village|global village]] both requires and produces. The argument is thus self-exemplifying: the book's refusal to take any medium one at a time is the practical demonstration of the thesis it argues. `Strongly implied`

## Reasoning Structure

1. Every medium is an extension of man (the book's master frame, [[Media as Extensions of Man]]).
2. Any extension — "whether of skin, hand, or foot" — affects not a part but "the whole psychic and social complex."
3. Therefore an extension's effects can be located only in the whole field it reorganizes, not in itself; one cannot answer questions about the extensions of man "without considering all of them together."
4. Stronger still: a medium has no "meaning or existence alone, but only in constant interplay with other media" — its identity is relational.
5. Conclusion: the proper object of media study is the interplay of media, and the method must be "concentric" (the technique of insight), not the lineal, fragmentary procedure of the mechanical age.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The periodized statement that the mechanical age extended the body in space while the electric age "extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace" — establishing that the relevant unit is the whole field, abolishing "both space and time" ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/03 Introduction]]).
- The case of the phonetic alphabet "hotted up" into typography, which "burst the bonds of medieval corporate guilds and monasteries" and led to nationalism and the religious wars — a single medium reorganizing the entire social and political configuration ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/05 Chapter 2 - Media Cold and Hot]]).
- The contrast of "Specialist technologies detribalize. The nonspecialist electric technology retribalizes" — the same field reorganized in opposite directions by media of different temperature, intelligible only across their interplay ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/05 Chapter 2 - Media Cold and Hot]]).
- The Frank Lloyd Wright spiral / scriptural-scholarship analogy: subjects whose relations "subsist together, and act and react upon one another at the same time" demand the "concentric pattern" that is "the technique of insight" ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/05 Chapter 2 - Media Cold and Hot]]).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The holism is total to the point of being hard to falsify: if every medium reorganizes "the whole" field, it is unclear what observation could disconfirm a claim about any single medium's effect, since any effect can be redescribed as an interplay effect.
- McLuhan asserts the relational ontology ("no medium has its meaning or existence alone") but does not argue it independently of the extensions frame; a reader who rejects the premise that all technologies are extensions of the body need not accept that media have no existence alone.
- The book's own organization is in tension with the thesis: *Understanding Media* proceeds through a long sequence of single-medium case studies (Part II), so the "study all together" rule is carried more by cross-reference and the repeated extensions frame than by a method that ever treats the whole configuration at once.
- The polemic against "lineal and fragmentary" analysis risks circularity: the demand to study media "concentrically" is justified by the electric age's form, but the diagnosis of that form is itself a product of the method being recommended. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Related Arguments

- [[The world is being translated into a problem of coding]] — Haraway's later thesis shares the structural intuition that the field, not the isolated object, is the unit of analysis; for both, a single technology is intelligible only inside a total reconfiguration (for McLuhan the sensory/social complex, for Haraway the coded command-control network).

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — McLuhan's insistence that a medium has "meaning or existence alone" only "in constant interplay" treats the medium less as a discrete material object than as a position in a field of effects; the argument leans the medium toward the relational/immaterial pole even as the extensions frame keeps it rooted in the body.
- [[Hot vs Cool Media]] — the Chapter 2 distinction is the very setting in which the interplay thesis is stated: temperature is not a property of a medium in isolation but of how it reconfigures a given cultural field ("it makes all the difference whether a hot medium is used in a hot or a cool culture"), so the hot/cool tension is itself an instance of media having meaning only in interplay.
