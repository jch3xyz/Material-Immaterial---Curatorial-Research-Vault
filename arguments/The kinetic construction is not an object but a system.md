# The kinetic construction is not an object but a system

## Claim

The Kinetic construction is not an *objet d'art* in the conventional sense but a *systeme d'art* — "a system in the sense that any series of interacting components may need repair and adjustment from time to time," a description that "hardly fits" the traditional inert painting or sculpture. `Directly stated`

## Expanded Explanation

This sentence is the keystone object-to-system move of Part Two of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*. Where the book's Introduction defines the [[Object and System]] axis in the abstract, Chapter 6 supplies the first concrete medium in which the migration is forced: the moving artwork. A kinetic sculpture is no longer a finished, place-bound thing whose worth is the formal qualities it embodies; it is a working ensemble of interacting parts that runs, wears, breaks, and must be tended. The ontology of the work changes from product to process — the piece *is* its functioning, and its functioning is contingent and maintained over time. This is exactly the system pole as Burnham defines it elsewhere: an aggregate of mutually dependent components that may manifest the characteristics of life, including "eventual death."

Burnham reaches the claim not through theory but through the marketplace. Breakdown and resupply of parts, he writes, "is a major problem for the Kineticist, one only occasionally faced by the traditional artist." Because many kinetic works are deliberately built with imperfections — Tinguely's "cripples, or machines with a physical flaw" — and because complexity without precision raises the breakdown rate, the works fail in use. The decisive symptom is commercial: "Purchasers bring back broken machines expecting the artist to fulfill a lifetime guarantee." Buyers and sellers, conditioned by the inert art object, are unprepared for an artwork that behaves like an appliance, for which "post-sale services and stocked parts" would be "an indispensable aspect." The collector who returns a stalled machine has, without knowing it, recognized that he bought a system, not an object.

The contrast with the inert painting or sculpture is the load-bearing term. A canvas or a bronze is durable, stationary, and complete; it does not need to be kept running. The kinetic work, by contrast, exists only while its components interact, and interacting components fail. Maintenance is therefore not incidental upkeep but constitutive of the work's mode of being. This makes Chapter 6 the practical proof of the thesis the Introduction states and Chapter 1 stages at the level of the vanishing base: sculpture passes from the regime of the object into the regime of the system. `Strongly implied`

## Reasoning Structure

1. A kinetic work consists of interacting moving components driven and regulated by a mechanism.
2. Any series of interacting components wears, falls out of adjustment, and needs repair from time to time — empirically confirmed by the breakdown-and-resupply problem and by purchasers returning broken machines expecting a lifetime guarantee.
3. The inert painting or sculpture has no interacting components and so needs no such maintenance; it is durable, stationary, and complete.
4. Therefore what the kinetic work *is* cannot be the conventional inert art object; its mode of being is the maintained functioning of a set of parts — i.e. a system (*systeme d'art*), not an object (*objet d'art*).

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The defining sentence: the Kinetic construction "is not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art … a system in the sense that any series of interacting components may need repair and adjustment from time to time," and this "hardly fits the description of the traditional inert painting or sculpture" — [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/08 Chapter 6 - Kineticism - The Unrequited Art]]
- The breakdown/resupply problem: "Breakdown and resupply of parts is a major problem for the Kineticist, one only occasionally faced by the traditional artist," aggravated by works built with "built-in defects" that guarantee "self-augmented malfunction … leading to sure breakdown."
- The marketplace symptom: "Purchasers bring back broken machines expecting the artist to fulfill a lifetime guarantee," while neither artists nor customers have "adapted themselves to the realities of machines as art."
- The appliance analogy: "post-sale services and stocked parts remain an indispensable aspect of the commercial appliance business" — the model the kinetic work fits and the inert object does not.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself frames the kinetic project as a failure ("the present Kinetic revival is with very few exceptions a failure"); the system reading is drawn from works he judges aesthetically unrealized, so the claim describes a structural condition more than an achieved art.
- The argument turns on a contingent, period-specific market fact (collectors' and dealers' unpreparedness), not on an intrinsic property of motion; a culture organized like "the commercial appliance business" might absorb maintenance without conceding that the work has stopped being an object.
- "Repair and adjustment from time to time" is a weak criterion — durable objects also require conservation — so the line between an object that needs upkeep and a system that needs maintenance is one of degree, echoing Burnham's own admitted uncertainty about "where the object leaves off and the system begins."
