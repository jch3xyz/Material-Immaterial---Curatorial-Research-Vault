# The abolition and realization of art are inseparable aspects of a single transcendence of art

## Claim

For [[Guy Debord]], Dadaism sought to abolish art without realizing it and Surrealism sought to realize art without abolishing it; each was a one-sided half of a single act, and the Situationist position shows that the abolition and the realization of art are inseparable aspects of one transcendence (*Aufhebung*) of art. (`Directly stated`, thesis 191)

## Expanded Explanation

Thesis 191 is the culminating move of Chapter 8 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*, "Negation and Consumption Within Culture," and one of the most consequential single sentences in the book for a theory of art. Debord has spent the chapter arguing that "culture is the terrain of the quest for lost unity" and that, as a separate sphere, "culture as a separate sphere is obliged to negate itself" (th. 180) — that the entire "triumphant history of culture can be understood as a progressive revelation of the inadequacy of culture, as a march toward culture's self abolition" (th. 180). Art is the sensory branch of this self-abolishing culture. Its modern history, from Baroque through "an ever more individualistic art of negation which, from Romanticism to Cubism, continually renewed its assaults until it had fragmented and destroyed the entire artistic sphere" (th. 189), is the history of art dissolving itself. Art in dissolution is "at once an art of change and the purest expression of the impossibility of change… This art is necessarily avant-garde, and at the same time it does not actually exist. Its vanguard is its own disappearance" (th. 190). (`Directly stated`)

Onto this stage Debord places Dadaism and Surrealism as "the two currents that marked the end of modern art" (th. 191). They were, "though they were only partially conscious of it, … contemporaries of the last great offensive of the revolutionary proletarian movement, and the defeat of that movement, which left them trapped within the very artistic sphere whose decrepitude they had denounced, was the fundamental reason for their immobilization" (th. 191). This is the load-bearing historical premise: the two movements are read not as autonomous aesthetic programs but as symptoms of a *political* defeat. Because the revolutionary movement of which they were the cultural expression was crushed, both were thrown back inside the art-sphere they had wanted to leave, and there they froze — each stalled on one half of the necessary gesture. Debord's chiasmus states the split exactly: "Dadaism sought to abolish art without realizing it; Surrealism sought to realize art without abolishing it" (th. 191). Dada negated the art-object and the artistic institution but had no positive program to carry art's suppressed content over into life; Surrealism tried to release art's imaginative content into life (dream, desire, the marvelous) but preserved the artwork and the artist intact. Each is a "one-sided critique" whose "internal inadequacy" the very opposition between them reveals (th. 191). (`Directly stated`)

The Situationist resolution is a strictly Hegelian *Aufhebung* — a supersession that at once cancels, preserves, and lifts to a higher level. Abolition without realization (Dada) is empty negation; realization without abolition (Surrealism) is a preservation that changes nothing. Only if art is *both* abolished as a separate sphere *and* realized as the transformed content of everyday life is culture's "quest for lost unity" (th. 180) actually completed rather than merely represented. This is why Debord frames the supersession as the fulfillment of th. 187: the modern destruction of art means "the language of communication has been lost," and a common language "must now be found in a praxis that unifies direct activity with its own appropriate language. The point is to actually participate in the community of dialogue and the game with time that up till now have merely been *represented* by poetic and artistic works" (th. 187). The transcendence of art is the passage from representing life to living it — art's realization *is* its abolition as separate representation. The chapter's close names the same operation for culture at large: "The real values of culture can be maintained only by actually negating culture. But this negation can no longer be a cultural negation. It may in a sense take place within culture, but it points beyond it" (th. 210). (`Directly stated` for the quotations; the *Aufhebung* reading is `Strongly implied` — Debord's détournement of Hegelian supersession is explicit throughout the book though the word "Aufhebung" is Knabb's translation choice "transcendence.")

## Reasoning Structure

1. Separate culture — and art as its sensory branch — is "obliged to negate itself"; its whole history is "a march toward culture's self abolition." (th. 180)
2. Modern art is the terminal phase of that self-negation: an "art of negation" that "fragmented and destroyed the entire artistic sphere," whose "vanguard is its own disappearance." (th. 189–190)
3. Dadaism and Surrealism are the two currents that mark the end of modern art, and each seizes only one pole of the necessary act: Dada abolishes art without realizing it; Surrealism realizes art without abolishing it. (th. 191)
4. Their immobilization is caused politically, not aesthetically: the defeat of "the last great offensive of the revolutionary proletarian movement" trapped both inside the art-sphere they had denounced. (th. 191)
5. The opposition between them "revealed the internal inadequacy of their one-sided critiques" — proving that abolition and realization belong together. (th. 191)
6. Therefore abolition and realization are "inseparable aspects of a single transcendence of art": art must be at once suppressed as a separate sphere and carried over ("realized") into a praxis that lives rather than represents life. (th. 191, 187, 210)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The thesis is **historically self-serving**: it positions the Situationists as the dialectical truth of the entire avant-garde, the party that alone completes what Dada and Surrealism could only half-do. It is a partisan reading of art history designed to authorize a specific political program, not a neutral art-historical judgment. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- The reduction of Dada and Surrealism to "abolish-not-realize" versus "realize-not-abolish" is a schematic simplification; both movements are internally heterogeneous, and Surrealism in particular had explicit revolutionary-political commitments that the neat chiasmus flattens. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- The claim that art's "realization" *is* its "abolition" risks being unfalsifiable in practice: because Debord defers the transcendence to a revolutionary praxis that "does not actually exist" yet (the art whose "vanguard is its own disappearance," th. 190), the thesis can never be tested against an accomplished result — a mirror-image difficulty to Burnham's [[An art movement dies by realizing its goals not by self-destruction|claim that a movement dies precisely by realizing its goals]].
- The whole argument is conditional on the primacy Debord grants to the "revolutionary proletarian movement": if one rejects the premise that the two movements' fate was determined by that movement's defeat, the diagnosis of their "immobilization" loses its ground.

## Related Arguments

- [[An art movement dies by realizing its goals not by self-destruction]] — [[Jack Burnham]]'s inverse valuation of the same abolition/realization pair. Burnham holds that "the real way to kill an art movement is for it to realize its goals," so *unrealization* is a sign of life and the self-destroying (self-abolishing) machine is only a "Luddite solution." Debord holds the opposite: neither abolition nor realization alone suffices, and only their unity — a *transcendence* that is simultaneously both — fulfills art rather than killing it. The two arguments are the vault's sharpest contrast on what "realizing" an art movement means.
- [[Workers councils are the only undefeated form and negate the spectacle]] — supplies the positive political vehicle implied here: the "single transcendence of art" is not an aesthetic program but the same revolutionary praxis whose defeat immobilized Dada and Surrealism; the transcendence of art is bound to the resumption of the proletarian movement.
- [[Fascism is a technologically equipped primitivism and a founding force of the spectacle]] — the same chapter-cluster's account of how the "defeat of that movement" (th. 191) is bound up with the spectacle's consolidation; the political defeat that traps the avant-garde is the obverse of the spectacle's victory.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — this argument is the direct ancestor of the vault's later art-abolition attempts. Debord's demand to *abolish* the separate art-object prefigures Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] and [[Conceptual Art]]'s attack on the material commodity-object, and his demand to *realize* art as lived praxis prefigures the move from object to experience/behaviour that runs through Burnham and Ascott. The Situationist "transcendence of art" grounds one pole of the tension: art abolished *as material object* and realized *as immaterial lived relation*.
