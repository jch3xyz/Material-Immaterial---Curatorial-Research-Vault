# The artwork is a probabilistic field where meaning is generated not transmitted

## Claim

Art should be re-founded on behaviour rather than form: the artwork is not a fixed message transmitted from artist to viewer but a probabilistic, highly entropic field — a matrix between two behavioural systems — in which the participator is constitutive and meaning is generated, not received.

## Expanded Explanation

In "Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art" (a 1978 College Art Association paper, published in *Leonardo* in 1980), Roy Ascott proposes a paradigm "that would replace the formalist modernist aesthetic" and "takes as its focus not form but behaviour." The dominant modernist model treated the artwork as a discrete message dispatched from artist to spectator and "completing" itself in the spectator's passive act of closure. Ascott rejects the sender-to-receiver communications model wholesale. In its place he offers a [[A Field Theory of Art|field theory]]: art "is understood as a field of psychic probability, highly entropic, in which the viewer is actively involved." The artwork becomes "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer)," a field of psychic interplay "generative of multiple meanings, where the final responsibility for meaning lies with the viewer."

The decisive consequence is that the spectator is recast as [[The Participator|participator]]: the viewer is engaged "not in an act of closure in the sense of completing a discrete message from the artist (a passive process) but by interrogating and interacting with the system 'artwork' to generate meaning." Meaning is therefore not transmitted down a channel and decoded; it is gambled into existence in the transaction between two psychic systems — both, in Umberto Eco's phrase that Ascott imports, "gambling on the possibility of semiosis."

Ascott grounds this move not in art vocabulary but in [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] and the new physics. He recruits Norbert Wiener's redefinition of [[Information|information]] as a transmission of alternatives rather than of fixed content, and the quantum measurement insight that [[The Observer|the observer]]'s consciousness is part of what is observed. The art participator and the quantum observer become the same structural figure: the apparatus "includes the consciousness of the participators." This is the conceptual hinge of Ascott's whole oeuvre — from object to system, from spectator to participator — and the behavioural/participatory counter-pole to Jack Burnham's object-to-system systems esthetics.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The reigning aesthetic treats the artwork as a discrete, finished message and the viewer as a passive receiver who "closes" it by decoding the artist's intention.
2. But in both cybernetics and the new physics, the observer participates in producing what is observed — information is a "transmission of alternatives," and the physicist's "observational behaviour and consciousness are a part of what he is observing."
3. Therefore the sender-to-receiver model is the wrong model for art; the right model is a two-system matrix — a probabilistic field of psychic interplay between the behavioural system "artist" and the behavioural system "viewer."
4. In such a field meaning cannot be transmitted; it can only be *generated*, by the viewer interrogating and interacting with the system "artwork."
5. Hence the participator is not incidental but constitutive — "of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system" — and art must be re-founded on behaviour, not form.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The matrix-of-two-behaviours formulation: the artwork as "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer)," a field "generative of multiple meanings."
- Umberto Eco's "gambling on the possibility of semiosis" [Eco 1976] — meaning as a wager between two psychic systems, not a decoded message.
- Norbert Wiener's information theory: the "transmission of information as 'impossible save as a transmission of alternatives'" (see [[Information]], [[Cybernetics]]).
- The quantum observer / measurement problem: "The physicist knows that his observational behaviour and consciousness are a part of what he is observing"; "The apparatus includes the consciousness of the participators" (see [[The Observer]]).
- The "highly entropic" character of the field — probability and indeterminacy imported from the new physics rather than from formal art-criticism.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The equation of the art participator with the quantum observer is a structural analogy Ascott asserts rather than demonstrates; the measurement-problem sense of "observer" is technical and contested, and its transfer to aesthetics is interpretive.
- Ascott's "field of psychic probability" risks dissolving any criterion for a *wrong* reading: if "the final responsibility for meaning lies with the viewer," it is unclear what, if anything, constrains the generated meaning, or what role the artist's construction retains.
- The thesis is programmatic. Ascott concedes the essay is deliberately inconclusive — "Networking is the antithesis of reductionism" — so the field theory is offered as a set of threads rather than a closed account.
