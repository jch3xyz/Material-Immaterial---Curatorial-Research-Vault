# Biology machine theory and sculpture converge on understanding living matter through its creation

## Claim

Burnham's self-declared central idea is that the meaning of organicism for biology, machine theory, and sculpture has already begun to converge toward a single end — the understanding of living matter through its creation — so that the sculptor, by way of the technician, is approaching his ancient goal of bringing inert matter to life. *(Directly stated)*

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham splits "organicism" into two non-equivalent but overlapping senses: the awareness of the interrelation between systems and their components within larger systems (the engineer's, technician's, social-systems sense), and any arrangement possessing the attributes of life (the biologist's, sculptor's sense). He argues the second meaning is increasingly "the working objective of the first." On this reading, the twentieth-century technician's "unconscious obsession" — the "bringing to life" of machines, industrial and social systems — and the sculptor's ancient obsession — the "bringing to life" of wood and stone — are no longer separate projects but two faces of one historical movement. *(Directly stated)*

The convergence is staged as a redefinition of the word "life" across three disciplines at once. For the vitalist sculptor, "life" was a criterion of artistic success rooted in the ability to create a plastic unity; for the biologist, "life" remains a question of systematically understanding the nature of living matter; and machine theory (cybernetics, systems analysis, information theory) supplies the bridge by translating organic function into engineerable principle. Burnham's claim is that as biology approaches the literal synthesis of living matter — protein structure, the DNA molecule, the genetic code, near-synthesis of organisms in the laboratory — and as systems development edges toward "creating synthetic organisms," the sculptor for the first time "sees the virtual achievement of his goal through the technician." *(Directly stated / Strongly implied)*

This is the chapter's framing of the book's larger argument that the meaning of sculpture is being transformed by the convergence of organic and machine values — the move from sculpture as metaphor for life toward sculpture as an actual demonstration of, and ultimately participation in, the making of living matter. This connects Burnham's thesis to [[Artificial Life]] as a horizon: the program of understanding life by building it. *(Interpretive synthesis)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. "Organicism" has two senses that overlap: (a) the interrelation of systems within larger systems (machines, factories, social systems); (b) any arrangement possessing the attributes of life (biological life). *(Directly stated)*
2. The second sense is "often the working objective of the first" — the technician's task of "bringing to life" machines and systems aims, in effect, at the biologist's question of what life is. *(Directly stated)*
3. The sculptor's perennial obsession has been the "bringing to life" of inert matter (wood, stone); the technician's modern obsession is the "bringing to life" of machines and systems. *(Directly stated)*
4. Each discipline holds a distinct working definition of "life" (artistic plastic unity; systematic understanding of living matter; engineerable organic function). *(Directly stated)*
5. Therefore, in redefining "life," the meaning of organicism for biology, machine theory, and sculpture has begun to converge on one end: the understanding of living matter through its creation. *(Directly stated)*
6. Consequently the sculptor, for the first time, sees the virtual achievement of his ancient goal — but realized through the technician rather than through metaphor in lifeless materials. *(Directly stated)*

- The contrast of disciplinary definitions of "life" (the vitalist sculptor's plastic unity vs. the biologist's systematic understanding of living matter), declared "unquestionably one of the central ideas of this book." *(Directly stated)*
- Lewis Mumford's *Technics and Civilization* on the gravitation toward "an organic ideology," and Mumford's insistence that "the real destiny of the machine was to merge itself with natural organisms" — the historical premise that the machine's meaning has been changing toward the organic. *(Directly stated)*
- The advance of molecular biology cited as bringing the creation of living matter within reach: protein structure, isolation of genetic material, the DNA molecule, cracking the genetic code, and laboratory experiments (Miller, Nirenberg, Spiegelman, Abelson) "only the first few constructive stages toward reconstructing organisms." *(Directly stated)*
- Postwar systems analysis and development, where "some experts regard the organization of men and machines into optimally functioning systems as tantamount to creating synthetic organisms" — the machine-theory leg of the convergence (Burnham notes the analogy remains "more metaphorical than literal" for now). *(Directly stated)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself qualifies the machine-theory leg: men and machines working together "manifest many of the growth, stability, and continuity symptoms of organisms, but much remains to make the term 'organic' more than a design ideal," and "the analogy between the two remains more metaphorical than literal." The convergence is asserted as a *beginning*, not an accomplished fact. *(Directly stated)*
- Sculpture, on Burnham's own account, "with very few exceptions-has remained an art of metaphor and not an actual demonstration of structural principles." The convergence thesis runs ahead of the actual state of the art object as Burnham describes it. *(Directly stated)*
- The term "organic" in sculpture has "suffered a number of ambiguous and ill-defined applications"; Burnham concedes most "organic sculpture" implied only "a certain suprastylistic emphasis on biological characteristics," and that sculptors (per Henry Moore) often more accurately called the work "vitalistic." The convergence claim depends on a precise sense of "organic" that the discipline itself rarely held. *(Directly stated)*
- The deeper convergence is interpretive on Burnham's part — a prophetic reading of a tendency ("has already begun to converge") rather than a demonstrated identity among the three disciplines. *(Strongly implied)*
