# The post-industrial future will be carried by psychotechnology

## Claim

[[Roy Ascott]] forecasts in 1967 that technology alone will not carry the post-industrial society through — what will carry it is **psychotechnology**, a successor to technology that exceeds Skinner's behavioural engineering and reaches into ESP, out-of-body states, and "total telepathy" / "instant communications with no media."

## Expanded Explanation

The argument arrives in the closing movement of Ascott's 1967 poster-manifesto "Behaviourables and Futuribles" (reprinted in *Control 5*, 1970). Having spent the broadside recasting art as a cybernetic, feedback-driven form of behaviour, Ascott turns to the question of what force will succeed the machine age. His answer is a wager against technological determinism: "In the post-industrial society, it is not technology that will carry us through so much as psychotechnology." The operative agency of the coming culture is relocated from the information machine to the psyche and its (then) speculative capacities (Directly stated).

The program is stated plainly, but its *content* is visionary. Psychotechnology is defined by what it exceeds: it "may take us beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering into the shadow lands, the futuribles, the speculative, astrological, dreamed-up, out-of-body, future behaviours." This is a pointed move inside Ascott's own vocabulary — his art is avowedly *behaviourist*, borrowing the register of stimulus, response, and conditioning, yet he names Skinnerian behavioural engineering as the limit to be overshot rather than the destination. The frontier he reaches for is parapsychological: "We may not have reached the frontiers of parapsychology, but when we do—wham! Instant communications with no media. Total telepathy, waves of alternative behaviours surging on from creative impulses of the mind" (Directly stated as program; the parapsychological content is itself speculative).

This passage is the earliest documentable seed of what Ascott will later develop, across his 1990s essays, into [[Psychotechnology|technoetics]] — the psi / Shamantic Web / consciousness arc of his telematic theory. Embedded in a 1967 cybernetic-art text, the claim shows that Ascott's later mystical turn is not a late departure but a thread present from the start: the wager that the carrier of the post-industrial future is the mind's own (speculative) communicative reach, not the hardware of the machine age. The same manifesto looks forward to a cybernetics that disappears as hardware — "Cybernetics will have come of age when we no longer notice the hardware, where the interface is minimal" — so "instant communications with no media" is the limit case of an interface that has vanished entirely into mind.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The post-industrial diagnosis of the late 1960s assumes that information technology will steer the coming culture.
2. Ascott refuses this: technology alone "will [not] carry us through."
3. The carrying force is instead **psychotechnology** — the psyche and its capacities reframed as the operative medium.
4. Psychotechnology is defined by exceeding a known limit: it goes "beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering" into the speculative, astrological, out-of-body register.
5. Its limit case is a frontier of parapsychology — "total telepathy," "instant communications with no media": communication without any technological intermediary at all.
6. Therefore the post-industrial future is carried not by hardware but by the (speculative) communicative powers of mind.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The content is unverifiable and visionary.** The program is directly stated, but its substance — ESP, out-of-body communication, "total telepathy" — is parapsychological speculation with no empirical warrant. The argument's *claim about culture's carrier* is asserted; its *mechanism* is a wager, not a demonstration (confidence is therefore medium, with the content labelled speculative).
- **A single early aphoristic source.** This is a 1967 broadside in manifesto cadence ("Object hustlers! Reduce your anxiety!"; "Message ends."). The forecast is a seed, not a worked-out theory; its full development belongs to Ascott's later [[Psychotechnology|technoetic]] essays.
- **Tension with technological determinism it both refuses and relies on.** Ascott rejects technology as the carrier yet frames psychotechnology as the *successor* to a technological epoch and casts it in cybernetic/information-theoretic terms ("waves," "rerouted," "branch"). The immaterial carrier is still described in the vocabulary of the machine — a complication this argument shares with O'Gieblyn's critique that [[Re-enchantment]] remains "encoded with the assumptions of disenchantment."
- **"Beyond Skinner" is supersession, not refutation.** Ascott does not disprove behavioural engineering; he declares it surpassed. The behaviourist register he keeps (his art is "essentially behaviourist") sits in productive tension with the claim that psychotechnology leaves Skinner behind.

## Related Arguments

- [[A machine may be more responsive to human needs than another person]] — Burnham's contemporaneous (1968) cybernetic-art claim runs the opposite way: it locates the future in the responsive *machine*, where Ascott locates it in psychotechnology beyond the machine. The two are the poles of 1960s cybernetic art (interpretive contrast).

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott's wager pushes the carrier of culture from the material machine (hardware) toward the immaterial (mind, telepathy, "no media"), extending the vault's keystone material-vs-immaterial thread into a parapsychological register.
- [[Object vs System]] — the manifesto's "system supersedes structure" / "software predominates over hardware" sets up the same object-to-system migration that psychotechnology completes: a culture carried by process and mind rather than by the physical object or device.
