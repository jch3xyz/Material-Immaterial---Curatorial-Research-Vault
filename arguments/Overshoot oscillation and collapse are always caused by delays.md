# Overshoot oscillation and collapse are always caused by delays

## Claim

Because every stock is itself a delay and every consequential flow carries delays of perception, response, and physical throughput, a decision made on [[Delays|delayed]] information — or executed with a delay — arrives off target; the whole family of dynamic pathologies the vault cares about, [[Overshoot and Collapse|overshoot and collapse]] and [[Oscillation|oscillation]], is therefore always caused by delays, and since changing a delay's length can utterly change a system's behavior, the delays are among the most sensitive leverage points a policy has. *(Directly stated.)*

## Expanded Explanation

Meadows treats delay not as an incidental friction in a system but as one of its constitutive properties. "Delays are ubiquitous in systems. Every stock is a delay. Most flows have delays—shipping delays, perception delays, processing delays, maturation" — the stock of cars on a lot, the population maturing to breeding age, the pollutant percolating through an ecosystem, the emissions warming an ocean, each is a reservoir that holds the present apart from its own effects. To say every stock is a delay is to say that a system is built out of lags: the material accumulations that give a system inertia are exactly the things that keep its information and its consequences out of step with each other.

That desynchronization is the whole mechanism. Delays "determine how fast systems can react, how accurately they hit their targets, and how timely is the information passed around a system." When a decision point "is responding to delayed information, or responding with a delay, the decisions will be off target. Actions will be too much or too little to achieve the decision maker's goals." A [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] that could hold a stock steady if it acted instantly instead over- and under-corrects around the mark, because by the time the corrective action lands the situation it was correcting has already moved. Hence Meadows's flat causal claim: "Overshoots, oscillations, and collapses are always caused by delays." The always is the point — she is not saying delays are one contributing factor among several but that these behaviors have no other source. Overcapacity-then-brownout cycles in the electricity industry come from "long delays in building new power plants"; the climate changes "will not be fully revealed for a generation or two" because of "decades-long delays as the earth's oceans respond to warmer temperatures"; Gorbachev could transform the Soviet information system overnight but not its physical economy, which "takes decades," because a physical economy is a deeper stock and therefore a longer delay.

Two consequences follow. First, delay-length is a leverage point: "Delays are often sensitive leverage points for policy, if they can be made shorter or longer," and "Changing the length of a delay may utterly change behavior." The same lever can stabilize or destabilize depending on which way it is pulled — a caution Meadows develops in the car-dealer parable, where reacting *faster* to a delayed loop makes the oscillation worse (see [[Acting faster on a delayed loop worsens oscillation high leverage wrong direction]]). Second, because a delayed system cannot correct itself in real time, anticipation must substitute for reaction: "When there are long delays in feedback loops, some sort of foresight is essential. To act only when a problem becomes obvious is to miss an important opportunity to solve the problem." A system whose damage is separated from its cause by a generation cannot be governed by waiting for the damage. The leverage and the foresight are the same insight seen from two sides — the delay is where the system's behavior is decided, and it is decided before the behavior is visible.

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise (delay is constitutive, not incidental).** "Every stock is a delay" and most flows carry delays; a system is built out of lags between accumulation and consequence. *(Directly stated.)*
2. **Premise (delay puts decisions off target).** Responding to delayed information, or responding with a delay, means "the decisions will be off target" — actions are "too much or too little." *(Directly stated.)*
3. **Mechanism (off-target correction in a loop produces the pathologies).** A [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] correcting on lagged information over- and under-shoots the target rather than settling on it. *(Strongly implied.)*
4. **Central claim.** Therefore "overshoots, oscillations, and collapses are always caused by delays" — these behaviors have delay as their sole structural cause. *(Directly stated.)*
5. **Corollary 1 (leverage).** Because "changing the length of a delay may utterly change behavior," delays "are often sensitive leverage points for policy, if they can be made shorter or longer." *(Directly stated.)*
6. **Corollary 2 (foresight).** Because a delayed loop cannot correct its own present, "when there are long delays in feedback loops, some sort of foresight is essential"; waiting until the problem is obvious forfeits the chance to solve it. *(Directly stated.)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The prescription "shorten or lengthen the delay" is direction-dependent, not a blanket rule: the sibling argument [[Acting faster on a delayed loop worsens oscillation high leverage wrong direction]] shows that pulling this high-leverage lever the intuitive way can amplify the very oscillation it was meant to damp. The claim licenses *attending* to delays, not any fixed sign of intervention. *(Strongly implied.)*
- Which delays matter is scale-relative. "What is a significant delay depends—usually—on which set of frequencies you're trying to understand"; a delay that dominates weekly oscillations may be negligible for a decades-long trajectory, so "always caused by delays" identifies the class of cause without singling out which delay is decisive in a given case. *(Directly stated.)*
- The most powerful delay is often the least changeable — physical throughput delays (building plants, warming oceans, retooling economies) resist shortening — so the leverage the claim identifies can be real yet practically out of reach, which is exactly why Meadows falls back on foresight rather than intervention. *(Strongly implied.)*

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the material stocks (cars, power plants, oceans, economies) supply the inertia, but the decisive, leverageable variable is the immaterial-temporal one: how long information takes to arrive and how long a response is deferred. The cause of the collapse lives less in the matter than in the timing, placing this argument on the informational side of the vault's central tension.
