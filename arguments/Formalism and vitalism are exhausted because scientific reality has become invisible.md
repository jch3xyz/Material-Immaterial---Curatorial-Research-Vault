# Formalism and vitalism are exhausted because scientific reality has become invisible

## Claim

[[Formalism (Burnham)|Formalism]] and [[Vitalism]] are both exhausted because they were visual-geometrical programs and cannot mirror a world of wave mechanics, electronic circuitry and statistics; visual art that clings to them therefore shares the obsolescence of classical science itself.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the unifying claim of Chapter 4. Burnham argues that the twin nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century projects of modern sculpture — [[Formalism (Burnham)|formalism]] (analysis of pure visible form, "the prehistory of the visible") and [[Vitalism]] (the faith in an "inner life" dwelling within the object) — were not undone by any internal aesthetic failure but by a change in what science takes reality to be. Both were grounded in a directly perceivable, geometrical, causal picture of the world; both have been "nullified" because the science whose worldview they shared has itself been overtaken. `Directly stated`.

Burnham makes the parallelism explicit: just as classical [[Scientific Idealism|scientific idealism]] has declined under modern analysis — yielding a universe that "appears to be sensually irrational" and is "understandable only through scientific symbolism and computation" — so the art forms that mirrored that older, visible order have lost their vigor. The decline of formalism, he writes, "is the decline of a world fashioned to operate on a strictly visual-geometrical level." `Directly stated`.

The emblem of the argument is the transistor radio set against the spring-wound watch. The watch is a world of "causal, coherent relationships" you can see and follow; the radio's interior is "the orderly assembly of electronic coding and circuit miniaturization" — order, but invisible, statistical, non-mechanical. A formalism built for watches cannot survive in a world of radios. This is why Burnham can conclude that sculpture clinging to "artistic formalism had the same chance of survival as its scientific counterpart in a world ruled by invisible energy and statistical formulae." The exhaustion is shared because the obsolescence is shared. `Directly stated`.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Both formalism and vitalism are visual-geometrical programs — formalism is the analysis of pure visible form ("the prehistory of the visible," "the prehistory of scientific technique itself … made possible by visual study"); vitalism is the empathetic faith in an inner life perceived within the palpable object.
2. Each was the cultural counterpart of a classical scientific picture of reality: a world of visible, causal, geometrical relationships ([[Scientific Idealism|scientific idealism]]).
3. Modern science has dissolved that picture — wave mechanics, electronic coding, statistics — into a reality that is invisible and "sensually irrational," knowable only through symbolism and computation, not direct perception.
4. "In a world of invisible values formalism has quickly become a language with a gradually contracting spectrum of possibilities"; "advances in scientific conceptualization have nullified the continued vigor of formalism" — and, in the same chronology, "the gradual annihilation of scientific vitalism."
5. Therefore visual art that holds to formalism or vitalism is obsolete in exactly the way classical science is: it mirrors a world that no longer exists. ([[Form Exhaustion]] is the symptom, not the disease.)

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The transistor-radio-vs-watch emblem: the radio's interior is "the orderly assembly of electronic coding and circuit miniaturization, not causal, coherent relationships as in the watch" — see [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/06 Chapter 4 - Form Exhaustion and the Rise of Phenomenalism]].
- The decline of formalism stated as "the decline of a world fashioned to operate on a strictly visual-geometrical level."
- The parallel decline of classical [[Scientific Idealism|scientific idealism]]: modern analysis "has revealed a universe which appears to be sensually irrational … understandable only through scientific symbolism and computation."
- Husserl's "crisis in European science" — science producing "an increasingly abstract, if not artificial, picture of reality … derived less and less from sensory data and more by systems of formal logic" — and Merleau-Ponty on Einstein's failure to reconcile Cartesian rationalism with "the sensory irrationality of modern wave mechanics."
- The settling claim that formalist sculpture "had the same chance of survival as its scientific counterpart in a world ruled by invisible energy and statistical formulae."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham notes the formalist aestheticians (Bell, Fry, Osborne) held the opposite view — that "perhaps sculpture was just beginning to show its true potentialities" — and that "some critics speak of a sculpture renaissance based on color, new materials, and a synthesis of present formalistic principles"; he treats this as "unfounded optimism."
- The argument is a cultural-parallelism thesis ("One need not possess a Hegelian discernment for cultural parallelism…"), so the link between scientific and artistic obsolescence is analogical rather than strictly causal; Burnham concedes the connection "is by no means clear at this time."
- The chapter offers a successor — phenomenalism / [[Object Art]] (the shift "from material idealization … to … pure experience") — implying the exhaustion is generative, not terminal: the same crisis that kills formalism opens the post-formalist aesthetic.
