# Cyberception makes immaterial process perceptible as palpably as material objects

## Claim

Cyberception — the post-biological faculty that emerges when the human sensorium is fused with the [[The Cybernet|cybernet]] — lets us perceive immaterial process (transformative relationships, connectivity, emergence) "just as palpably and immediately" as we perceive material objects in material locations, thereby fulfilling the twentieth-century art project of making the invisible visible.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott's [[Cyberception]] is not a metaphor for thinking *about* the immaterial; it is a claim that the immaterial is *directly perceived*. Where ordinary perception divides the world into "objects with impermeable boundaries" laid out "one thing after another," cyberception apprehends the whole — pattern, flow, system, the "processes of emergence in nature." Crucially, Ascott insists this apprehension has the same perceptual immediacy and palpability that the senses give to solid things in space. Immaterial process is not inferred or imagined; it is cyberceived.

This collapses the usual hierarchy in which material objects are the paradigm of the real and immaterial relations are derivative or abstract. For Ascott the relations come first and are felt as fully as the objects. He frames the achievement as the completion of a long arc in modern art: "if, as many would hold, the project of art in the twentieth century has been to make the invisible visible," then cyberception is the faculty that finally delivers it — giving us "X-ray vision and the optics of outer space." Art accordingly shifts from appearance and representation to *apparition*, "the coming into being of what has never before been seen or heard or experienced" (see [[Appearance vs Apparition]]).

This is the strongest immaterial-process statement in Ascott's corpus and the natural anchor for his contribution to the vault's long-running [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension. It carries [[Jack Burnham|Burnham]]'s object→system thesis (see [[Object and System]], [[Object vs System]], [[Systems Esthetics]]) one step further: where Burnham showed sculpture dissolving the discrete object into a "physical system of forces," Ascott claims the dissolved, immaterial system is now *perceptually available* to us as such.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The cybernet — the sum of interactive computer-mediated systems and telematic networks — becomes part of the human sensory apparatus, redefining the body and connecting all bodies into a planetary whole.
2. The faculty this fusion produces, cyberception, is whole-seeing and pattern-seeing rather than linear and object-dividing; it apprehends process, connectivity, and [[Emergence|emergence]].
3. Therefore the immaterial (transformative relationships, connectivity, process) is perceived "just as palpably and immediately" as material objects in material locations — not merely conceived or represented.
4. Conclusion: cyberception fulfills the twentieth-century art project of "making the invisible visible," supplying "X-ray vision and the optics of outer space," and shifting art from appearance/representation to apparition.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The direct equation of cyberceived immaterial process with ordinarily perceived material objects: "just as palpably and immediately as we commonly perceive material objects in material locations." (See [[#Sources]].)
- The art-historical warrant: the twentieth-century project "to make the invisible visible," now delivered as "X-ray vision and the optics of outer space."
- The Cassini/Titan figure of distributed cyberceptive perception ("it will be our eyes and minds that are there") — perception literally extended into the immaterial reaches of outer space.
- The contrast with linear, categorical perception that splits the world into "objects with impermeable boundaries," against cyberception's "all-at-once perception of a multiplicity of viewpoints."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Asserted, not demonstrated.** Ascott states that immaterial process is perceived "just as palpably" as material objects; he does not give a perceptual or empirical account of how palpability transfers from the tactile/visible object to a relation or a flow. The claim is visionary and programmatic (an ISEA94/Leonardo manifesto), labelled `Directly stated` because Ascott explicitly makes it — not because it is independently established.
- **Possible equivocation on "perceive."** Critics could argue Ascott conflates perception (Ascott's gloss: "physical sensation interpreted in the light of experience") with conception, smuggling in as *perceived* what is really *modelled* or *visualised* by instruments and databases.
- **It does not abolish the material.** Even in this chapter the material persists — the city's "dense and intractable materiality," "its visible immateriality and its invisible construction." The argument makes the immaterial as palpable as the material; it does not claim matter disappears (the point of contact with the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension rather than its resolution).
- **Techno-optimism / access.** The faculty presupposes immersion in the cybernet; the claim universalizes a perceptual transformation that is, in practice, unevenly distributed.
