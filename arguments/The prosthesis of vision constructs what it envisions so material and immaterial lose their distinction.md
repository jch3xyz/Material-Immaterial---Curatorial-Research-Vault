# The prosthesis of vision constructs what it envisions so material and immaterial lose their distinction

## Claim

For Roy Ascott, when the instrument of seeing is at the same time the instrument of making — paradigmatically the scanning tunnelling microscope, which both images single atoms and manipulates them one by one to build structures from the bottom up — perception and construction fuse, and with that fusion the categorical distinction between the immaterial (conception, the mind's eye) and the material (the world built) loses its hold. *(Directly stated)*

## Expanded Explanation

This is the keystone material/immaterial-collapse argument of the book's late phase. Ascott builds it on top of his account of `[[Double Consciousness]]` and `[[Cyberception]]`: the "double gaze" that sees "at once both inward realities and the outward surfaces of the world." Historically, Ascott argues, our limited command of matter forced us to keep the worlds of double consciousness in "separate and distinct categories, such as the real, the imagined, and the spiritual." What changes the situation is a new "double technology" — the artificial-life sciences, both "dry (pixel)" and "moist (molecular)," together with nanotechnology — that erodes "the boundaries between states of mind, between conception and construction, between the internalisation and the realisation of our desires." *(Directly stated)*

The scanning tunnelling microscope (STM) is his concrete example. It does not merely *view* matter at the atomic level; it can "image individual, single atoms" and, "at the same time, manipulate them, one by one, atom by atom, to construct atomic structures of our own choosing from the bottom up." Seeing here is not receptive only — it is, in Ascott's words, "as much active and constructive as it is receptive and reflective." The same act that observes the atom also positions it. The "prosthesis of vision" therefore "can be at one and the same time instrumental in constructing what is envisioned," so that "to see in the mind's eye is to realize in the material world." When the gap between conceiving and constructing collapses, so does the ontological gap between the immaterial and the material: they "lose their categorical distinction." *(Directly stated)*

This is the art-and-technology lineage's strongest formulation of the vault's central `[[Material vs Immaterial]]` problem. Where Burnham (1968) traced sculpture's movement from object to system — see `[[Object vs System]]` and `[[Systems Esthetics]]` — Ascott (1998) pushes past the dematerialised art object to a condition in which the maker's vision is itself a material operation. The argument extends the committed art/new-media stubs Burnham realized: it is the telematic and bio-telematic horizon of `[[New Media Art]]` and the limit case of `[[Art as Experiential Technology]]`. *(Interpretive synthesis)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. The "double gaze" of cyberception already gives simultaneous access to inward (mental, virtual) and outward (material) fields — the precondition of double consciousness.
2. A "double technology" (artificial life, nanotechnology, the STM) now lets the same instrument both perceive and fabricate at the finest scale.
3. In the STM, imaging an atom and manipulating it are one operation: seeing *is* building, bottom-up.
4. Therefore the prosthesis of vision is "instrumental in constructing what is envisioned" — to see in the mind's eye is to realize in the material world.
5. With conception and construction no longer separable, the categories "immaterial" and "material" lose their categorical distinction.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The scanning tunnelling microscope as a device that simultaneously images and manipulates single atoms "to construct atomic structures of our own choosing from the bottom up" (Ch. 30, "Weaving the Shamantic Web").
- The "double gaze" / `[[Double Consciousness]]` framework, modelled on both the shaman's trance and immersion in virtual reality, and the faculty of `[[Cyberception]]` (Ch. 30).
- The "artificial life sciences" — "dry (pixel) and moist (molecular)" `[[Artificial Life|artificial organisms]]` plus nanotechnology — named as the technologies eroding the boundary between conception and construction (Ch. 30).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The strong claim rests on a single, dramatic instance (the STM) generalised to an ontological thesis; one could grant that the STM fuses observation and intervention without conceding that "the immaterial and material lose their categorical distinction" *as categories*. The conflation may be epistemic (our practices fuse) rather than ontological. *(Interpretive synthesis)*
- Ascott frames the collapse affirmatively, as an artistic and "technoetic" horizon; a critic in the surveillance/embodiment lineage might note that the very fusion of seeing-and-making is also a fusion of imaging-and-control, with the costs that `[[Material vs Immaterial]]`'s other authors attach to it. *(Speculative connection)*
- The phenomenological evidence Ascott braids in (the ayahuasca-induced "second body... made up of a mass of multicoloured particles") is offered as parallel testimony to double consciousness, not as proof of atomic-scale construction; the two registers — subjective experience and instrument capability — are joined rhetorically. *(Strongly implied)*

## Related Arguments

- [[We are not our bodies but a pattern of information that persists through material turnover]] — Kurzweil's patternist claim shares the move of subordinating the material substrate, but Ascott does not dissolve the body into information so much as make vision itself a material act; the two meet at the erosion of material/immaterial difference from opposite directions.
- [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]] — Hayles's corrective tempers Ascott: even a "prosthesis of vision" is an embodied, situated apparatus, so the "collapse" is a re-embodiment of seeing-as-building, not an escape from matter.
