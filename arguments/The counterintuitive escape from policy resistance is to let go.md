# The counterintuitive escape from policy resistance is to let go

## Claim

Because a [[Policy Resistance|policy-resistant]] system is held in place by symmetrical, mutually reinforcing effort, the way out is not to push harder but to withdraw one's own pull — "Let go" — since much of the behavior one was fighting was itself a response to that push, and the energy freed from enforcing and resisting can then be redirected toward goals everyone can share.

## Expanded Explanation

Meadows arrives at the counsel through a picture of a [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing]] standoff. Various actors pull a single system stock — her example is "drug supply on the city streets" — in incompatible directions: "Addicts want to keep it high, enforcement agencies want to keep it low, pushers want to keep it right in the middle." The result is not motion but deadlock: "Together, the countermoves produce a standoff, the stock is not much different from before, and that is not what anybody wants." The trap's signature is wasted symmetry — "everyone has to put great effort into keeping the system where no one wants it to be." Worse, the structure ratchets: "Intensification of anyone's effort leads to intensification of everyone else's," so the reflex to try harder only tightens the knot. `Directly stated`

Against this, the first instinct is to overpower the resistance, and Meadows concedes it can work "at the cost of monumental resentment and the possibility of explosive consequences if the power is ever let up." Her case study is the failed maximum of that strategy: "the formulator of the Romanian population policy, dictator Nicolae Ceausescu, who tried long and hard to overpower the resistance to his policy." Romania's 1967 abortion ban tripled the birth rate, whereupon "the policy resistance of the Romanian people set in" and drove it back down — "primarily though dangerous, illegal abortions, which tripled the maternal mortality rate" — filling orphanages with abandoned children. When Ceausescu's government fell he "was executed, along with his family," and the new government's first act was to repeal the ban. Overpowering did not defeat the resistance; it accumulated it until the pressure released catastrophically. `Directly stated`

The genuinely counterintuitive move is the opposite one: "The alternative to overpowering policy resistance is so counterintuitive that it's usually unthinkable. Let go." The rationale is that the resistance is not autonomous but reactive — "You won't get your way with the system, but it won't go as far in a bad direction as you think, because much of the action you were trying to correct was in response to your own action. If you calm down, those who are pulling against you will calm down too." Meadows' confirming instance is the end of American Prohibition: "This is what happened in 1933 when Prohibition ended in the United States; the alcohol-driven chaos also largely ended." Letting go is not surrender but the release of a self-sustaining standoff; the calm it produces "may provide the opportunity to look more closely at the feedbacks within the system" and, ultimately, to find "an overarching goal that allows all actors to break out of their bounded rationality" — the deeper cure the escape opens onto. `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. Policy resistance is a [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] standoff in which each actor, on its own [[Bounded Rationality|bounded rationality]], pulls a shared stock toward a different goal, and the countermoves cancel — "everyone has to put great effort into keeping the system where no one wants it to be."
2. Because the structure ratchets, pushing harder ("overpowering") only intensifies the counter-pull; it can hold only by continuous force, and releases explosively when the force lapses (Ceausescu).
3. But much of the counter-pull is a *response* to one's own pull, not an independent force — so if one lets go, the reason for the others' effort partly disappears and they calm down too.
4. Therefore the escape is to stop pushing: give up the ineffective policy, let the calming reveal the feedbacks, and redirect the freed energy toward goals all actors can pull toward together (Prohibition's end; the way out of the trap).

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The drug-supply standoff: addicts, enforcement, pushers, and citizens each pulling the stock differently until "the countermoves produce a standoff" — the anatomy of a policy-resistant [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing loop]] ([[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/09 Chapter 5 - System Traps and Opportunities]]).
- The Romanian abortion ban (1967) and Ceausescu's execution — the tragic maximum of overpowering, where accumulated resistance released catastrophically.
- The end of Prohibition (1933), when "the alcohol-driven chaos also largely ended" once the enforcing pull was withdrawn — the confirming case that letting go dissolves reactive resistance.
- The contrast set (Romania vs. Hungary's housing policy vs. Sweden's child-welfare goals) showing that beyond letting go lies the deeper cure of aligning subsystem goals under "an overarching goal."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Letting go is not costless or complete: Meadows is explicit that "You won't get your way with the system" — the abandoned goal is genuinely abandoned, only the *worst* of the resisted behavior abates.
- Overpowering is not always futile — Meadows grants it "can work, at the cost of monumental resentment," if one "can keep wielding" the power indefinitely; the argument is that this bet usually fails, not that it always does.
- The counsel presupposes that resistance is largely reactive to one's own action; where an unwanted behavior is driven by forces independent of the policy, withdrawing the policy would not calm it, and this remedy would not apply.
- Letting go is framed as the escape, but Meadows treats it as a *first* move, not the whole solution — "The most effective way of dealing with policy resistance is to find a way of aligning the various goals of the subsystems"; without that follow-through the freed energy may simply disperse.

## Tensions

- [[Individual Rationality vs System Welfare]] — the standoff is the tension deadlocked: each actor's locally rational pull holds the whole "where no one wants it to be," and letting go is the paradoxical move that lets system welfare improve by having one party stop optimizing its own goal.
