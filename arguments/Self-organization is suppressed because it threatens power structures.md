# Self-organization is suppressed because it threatens power structures

## Claim

Self-organization is routinely sacrificed and suppressed not only because it costs short-term productivity and stability but because it produces heterogeneity and unpredictability and demands freedom, experimentation, and disorder — conditions that frighten individuals and threaten power structures.

## Expanded Explanation

In *Thinking in Systems*, Donella Meadows treats [[Self-Organization]] — a system's capacity "to make its own structure more complex," to "learn, diversify, complexify, evolve" — as one of the three properties, beside [[Resilience]] and [[Hierarchy (Systems)]], that make systems "work so well." Having established it as the source of both hierarchy and heterogeneity and "the strongest form of system resilience," she then delivers a blunt political diagnosis: this most generative of properties is precisely the one that gets systematically destroyed. "Like resilience," she writes, "self-organization is often sacrificed for purposes of short-term productivity and stability" (`Directly stated`). The first half of the argument is thus continuous with her [[Resilience]] argument — a valuable but hard-to-see whole-system capacity is traded away for a measurable, immediately recognizable one.

But Meadows adds a second, sharper reason that the productivity argument alone does not cover. Self-organization is not merely invisible or slow-paying; it is actively *disturbing*. "Self-organization produces heterogeneity and unpredictability," and "it requires freedom and experimentation, and a certain amount of disorder" (`Directly stated`). These are exactly the conditions that legibility, order, and control cannot abide. "These conditions that encourage self-organization often can be scary for individuals and threatening to power structures" (`Directly stated`). Where resilience is sacrificed largely by *inadvertence* — nobody sees it — self-organization is suppressed partly by *design*, because the differentiation and unpredictability it generates are threats to whatever holds established power. The two motives compound: "productivity and stability are the usual excuses" that supply cover for a suppression whose deeper driver is the incompatibility of open-ended, disorderly generativity with centralized control.

Meadows illustrates the mechanism across registers. Under the productivity/stability excuse: "turning creative human beings into mechanical adjuncts to production processes," "narrowing the genetic variability of crop plants," "establishing bureaucracies and theories of knowledge that treat people as if they were only numbers." Under the fear-and-threat driver: "education systems may restrict the creative powers of children instead of stimulating those powers," "economic policies may lean toward supporting established, powerful enterprises rather than upstart, new ones," and "many governments prefer their people not to be too self-organizing" (`Directly stated`). She closes with a note of qualified hope that doubles as a description of the cost: because self-organization "is such a basic property of living systems … even the most overbearing power structure can never fully kill it, although in the name of law and order, self-organization can be suppressed for long, barren, cruel, boring periods" (`Directly stated`). The suppression is never total, but it is real, and its price is measured in barrenness.

This is the systems-theory native grounding of a suspicion the vault otherwise meets in its surveillance and control cluster: that the drive toward legibility, transparency, and prediction is, at bottom, a drive against a system's own generative capacity (`Interpretive synthesis`). What [[Byung-Chul Han]] frames as the smoothing away of negativity and the Other, and what [[Shoshana Zuboff]] frames as the rendition of the unpredictable self into behavioral certainty, Meadows names in the plainest systems vocabulary: power finds heterogeneity and unpredictability threatening, so it suppresses the very capacity that produces them.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Self-organization is a system's capacity to complexify, learn, diversify, and evolve its own structure — the source of hierarchy and heterogeneity and the strongest form of resilience.
2. Like resilience, it is hard to see and slow to pay off, so it is "often sacrificed for purposes of short-term productivity and stability" — productivity and stability being "the usual excuses."
3. But self-organization also "produces heterogeneity and unpredictability" and "requires freedom and experimentation, and a certain amount of disorder."
4. Heterogeneity, unpredictability, and disorder are frightening to individuals and threatening to whatever maintains established power.
5. Therefore self-organization is not only sacrificed by inadvertence but actively suppressed — through schooling that restricts creativity, policies that favor incumbents over upstarts, and governments that prefer un-self-organizing populations.
6. The suppression can never be total (self-organization is a basic property of living systems), but it can be sustained "for long, barren, cruel, boring periods" in the name of law and order.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Two motives are compressed into one claim.** Meadows names both a cost motive (productivity/stability) and a threat motive (fear of heterogeneity, threat to power). These may not always co-occur; some suppression is pure inadvertence (as with resilience) and involves no threatened power structure at all. The argument's strong form — that suppression is *because* it threatens power — is Meadows's emphasis but she also grants the blander productivity explanation as the more usual surface reason.
- **"Often," not "always," and never total.** Meadows explicitly limits the claim: self-organization "can never be fully killed," and the suppression is intermittent and defeasible. This is a tendency, not a law.
- **Threat is asserted, not demonstrated.** That heterogeneity is "threatening to power structures" is stated as a general truth and illustrated by example (schools, incumbents, governments) rather than argued from cases; a skeptic could read some of these examples (e.g., standardized education) as driven by cost and coordination, not by fear of self-organization as such.
- **Not all order is suppression.** Meadows elsewhere prizes [[Hierarchy (Systems)|hierarchy]] as itself a *product* of self-organization; order and self-organization are not simple opposites, so "a certain amount of disorder" is a claim about degree, and the argument does not license reading every stabilizing structure as anti-generative.

## Related Arguments

- [[The purpose of a system is the most crucial determinant of its behavior]] — the same immaterial-over-material logic: what governs a system (here, its generative capacity to evolve its own structure) is the least visible and most contested part, and misreading it as the visible, orderly surface is the characteristic systems error.
- [[Transparency abolishes the Other and produces compulsive conformity]] — Han's parallel diagnosis in a different vocabulary: the smoothing away of otherness, negativity, and difference is exactly the destruction of the heterogeneity Meadows says power finds threatening.
- [[Human consciousness itself is a threat to surveillance revenues]] — Zuboff's parallel: the unpredictable, self-authoring self is a threat to a power that requires behavioral certainty, so it is rendered and suppressed — the surveillance-capitalist form of Meadows's general claim.
