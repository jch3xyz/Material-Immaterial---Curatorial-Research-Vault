# The freedom of Can is more coercive than the disciplinary Should

## Claim

The neoliberal *You can* exercises greater constraint than the disciplinary *You should*, because the compulsion it generates is auto-compulsion — self-directed and therefore inescapable, "there is no way to resist oneself" — and because whoever fails under it bears the guilt alone, with no possibility of pardon, relief, or atonement.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the *Agony of Eros* statement of Han's central thesis about freedom, delivered in the chapter "Being Able Not to Be Able." Where *Psychopolitics* opens the same argument in a historical-structural register (freedom "switching over" into compulsion) and *The Burnout Society* stages it as a shift in the governing modal verb of a social formation, here the claim is sharpened to a single, almost clinical proposition about the *form* of the coercion: "You can exercises even greater constraint than You should. Auto-compulsion proves more fatal than allo-compulsion, because there is no way to resist oneself." The disciplinary *Should* comes from an Other — a commanding, exploiting authority external to the subject — and against an Other one can, in principle, push back. The neoliberal *Can* comes from the self. There is no exterior to it, no master to disobey, no distance from which to resist. Its inescapability is precisely what makes it more fatal than any allo-compulsion. (Directly stated.)

Han grounds this in the figure of the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] as "an entrepreneur of the self." Such a subject is free "insofar as he or she is not subjugated to a commanding and exploiting Other," and yet "still not really free because he or she now engages in self-exploitation — and does so of his or her own free will." The exploiter and the exploited coincide: "The achievement-subject is perpetrator and victim in one." This coincidence is the reason [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploitation]] "proves much more efficient than allo-exploitation" — it "is accompanied by a feeling of liberty," and so "makes possible exploitation without domination." Because the compulsion "appears as freedom," it "is not recognized as such"; the ruse of the neoliberal regime is that it "conceals its compulsive structure behind the seeming freedom of the single individual," who "no longer understands him- or herself as a subjugated subject ('subject to'), but as a project in the process of realizing itself." (Directly stated.)

The second half of the argument is what the *Can* does to failure. Under the *Should*, responsibility could be externalized: an authority commanded, and one could fail *against* it. Under the *Can*, "now, whoever fails is at fault and personally bears the guilt. No one else can be made responsible for failure." And there is no exit from this guilt: "Nor is there any possibility for pardon, relief, or atonement." Han reads this theologically, against Benjamin: capitalism, "counter to widespread belief," "is not a religion," because "every religion operates with both debt (guilt) and relief (pardon)," whereas "capitalism only works with debt and default." It "offers no possibility for atonement, which would free the debtor from liability." Depression and burnout are the somatic-psychic residue of this closure — an "unredeemable failure of ability," a "psychic insolvency," the impossibility of ever paying off the debt one owes to oneself. The unlimited *Can* not only compels without limit; it convicts without appeal. (Directly stated.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Achievement society is governed by the modal verb *Can*, in contrast to disciplinary society, which deploys *Should* through commandments and prohibitions.
2. The *Should* issues from a commanding, exploiting Other, so its compulsion is allo-compulsion — imposed from outside, and therefore, in principle, resistible.
3. The *Can* is internalized as self-exploitation undertaken "of one's own free will"; its compulsion is auto-compulsion, and "there is no way to resist oneself."
4. Auto-compulsion is inescapable in a way allo-compulsion is not; therefore *You can* exercises greater constraint than *You should*.
5. Because the compulsion appears as freedom, it goes unrecognized, and its efficiency ("exploitation without domination") exceeds that of external coercion.
6. Under the *Can*, failure can no longer be externalized: whoever fails "personally bears the guilt," and there is "no possibility for pardon, relief, or atonement."
7. Therefore the freedom of *Can* is the more total coercion — unlimited in its demand and unforgiving in its verdict — with depression and burnout as its pathological signs.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is diagnostic and largely asserted rather than demonstrated: the step from a structural feature of the *Can* (its self-directedness) to the clinical reality of depression and burnout ("psychic insolvency") is presented as self-evident. Han offers the maladies as illustration, not proof.
- "There is no way to resist oneself" is a strong claim that forecloses the possibility of internal critique, ascesis, or self-distance — capacities other traditions (and Han's own later appeals to contemplative [[Negative Potency|negative potency]], the power of the *not-to*) would treat as genuine forms of self-resistance. The argument's force depends on granting that the achievement-subject has no such interior distance from its own *Can*.
- It depends on the periodizing contrast between disciplinary and achievement society (the *Should*→*Can* shift). A reader who holds that disciplinary/allo-coercive power persists undiminished — or that Han's *Burnout Society* caveat that "the Can does not revoke the Should" undercuts the sharp asymmetry — would weaken the "greater constraint than" comparison on which the thesis rests.
- Han's turn against Foucault ("Foucault adopts a positive attitude toward neoliberalism") is a contestable reading of Foucault's late lectures; the argument's polemical framing is not required by its core structural claim.

## Tensions

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — this argument is the sharpest form of the tension: it asserts not that freedom is opposed to control but that freedom, as the neoliberal *Can*, *is* the more total control, because a self-issued compulsion cannot be resisted and its failure cannot be pardoned.
