# Money is a specialist visual technology underwritten by print literacy

## Claim

Money is a specialist, fragmenting, visual technology — of the same family as writing and the clock — and its modern statistical price-system is unthinkable without the psychological conditioning in uniformity and repeatability that print literacy supplies; nonliterate haggling cultures lack the psychic resources to sustain abstract pricing.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan refuses the psychoanalytic reading of money (the "anal" theory of "filthy lucre") as insufficient to "the nature and function of money in society," and instead places money inside his general media theory: money is a medium, "a staple, a natural resource," an "outward and visible form of the urge to change and to exchange." Like any extension of man it amplifies and accelerates human energies. But its specific *form* is what matters here. Money is "a specialist technology like writing," and McLuhan lines it up with the other great visual, fragmenting media of literacy: as writing "intensifies the visual aspect of speech and order," and as the clock "visually separates time from space," "So money separates work from the other social functions." Money is thus not a neutral token but a perceptual technology that fragments the social field into specialized, separable functions — the same operation print performs on knowledge and the assembly line performs on labor. *(Directly stated, Ch. 14.)*

The argument's sharpest move is to make modern money *parasitic on print*. McLuhan claims that "our Western use of money as store and translator of communal work and skill has depended upon long accustomation to the written word, and upon the power of the written word to specialize, to delegate, and to separate functions." The fixed, uniform price-system that the West takes for granted is, on this account, a print effect: "Uniformity of commodities, combined with a fixed-price system such as we now take for granted, does not become possible until printing prepares the ground." Print supplies the "psychological conditioning in the ways of uniformity and repeatability" without which whole populations cannot be trained to "translate their wishes and desires statistically" through "the visual technology of prices." This is why "the West is little aware of the way in which the world of prices and numbering is supported by the pervasive visual culture of literacy" — the literate eye cannot see the medium it is made of. *(Directly stated, Ch. 14.)*

The contrast case is the nonliterate economy. "Nonliterate societies are quite lacking in the psychic resources to create and sustain the enormous structures of statistical information that we call markets and prices." For peoples "for whom the exciting drama of price haggling occurs with every transaction," the "extreme abstraction and detachment represented by our pricing system is quite unthinkable and unusable." McLuhan even dates the threshold: only "in the eighteenth century" did the West "accept this form of extension of its inner life in the new Statistical pattern of marketing," a mechanism so strange that contemporaries called it a "Hedonistic calculus" and felt "the fragmentation of the inner life by prices" to be as mysterious as the fragmentation of space by the differential calculus a century earlier. Abstract pricing is not a human universal but a late, literacy-bred artifact. *(Directly stated, Ch. 14.)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. Money is a medium — an extension of man, a "metaphor, a transfer, and a bridge" — and like every medium it specializes and rechannels human energies and separates functions.
2. Among media, money belongs specifically to the *visual, fragmenting* family alongside writing and the clock: it separates work from the other social functions just as writing intensifies the visual and the clock separates time from space.
3. The fragmenting power that money exercises is "the fragmenting power of the visual sense, as literacy separates it from the other senses" — so money's specialist function presupposes the visual bias that literacy installs.
4. Therefore the Western use of money "has depended upon long accustomation to the written word"; and a uniform fixed-price system "does not become possible until printing prepares the ground" by conditioning populations in uniformity and repeatability.
5. Nonliterate societies, lacking that conditioning, "are quite lacking in the psychic resources" to sustain abstract markets and prices; among them, statistical pricing is "unthinkable and unusable" and exchange remains the drama of haggling.
6. Conclusion: modern abstract money — and especially the statistical price-system — is a specialist visual technology underwritten by print literacy, not a natural or universal feature of economic life.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Self-limiting historical scope.** McLuhan frames money's visual/print-bound character as a phase that the electric age dissolves: "electric technology puts the very concept of money in jeopardy," and money "begins to be less and less a means of storing or exchanging work and skill" as it "merges with the informational forms of credit and credit card." The argument therefore describes the literate-industrial era specifically, not money as such; the credit card "approaches once more the character of tribal money." This bounds the claim rather than refuting it, but it means "money is a visual technology" is true of a passing epoch.
- **The role of commodity money.** McLuhan concedes money "began in nonliterate cultures as a commodity" (whales' teeth, rats, tobacco, cigarettes) and that nonliterate peoples "easily accept" staples as money. So money *per se* predates literacy; what is print-bound is the *abstract, statistical, fixed-price* form, not exchange or value-storage in general. The claim must be read narrowly.
- **Technological determinism.** The argument leans on a strong causal direction (print conditioning → abstract pricing) and a contested developmental reading of "'backward' countries" and "economic 'takeoff,'" which risks both determinism and a periodizing of nonliterate cultures as merely prior. McLuhan's "psychic resources" claim is asserted by analogy more than demonstrated empirically.
- **Confidence.** The core formal claims are `Directly stated`; the inference that this constitutes a single unified *argument* (rather than a cluster of remarks across the chapter) is `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — money is McLuhan's hinge between the two: it specializes "away from the commodity form" (material staple) into "a specialist agent of exchange," and in the electric age dematerializes entirely as "the sheer movement of information" in credit and the credit card. The price-system is the immaterial, statistical translation of material work — an argument-level participation in the vault's central tension.
