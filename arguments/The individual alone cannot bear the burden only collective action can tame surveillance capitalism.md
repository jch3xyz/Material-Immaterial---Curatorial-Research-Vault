# The individual alone cannot bear the burden only collective action can tame surveillance capitalism

## Claim

The [[Asymmetries of Knowledge and Power|asymmetries of knowledge and power]] that surveillance capitalism produces are too vast for any individual to confront alone; just as an individual worker in the early twentieth century could not single-handedly win fair wages and had to organize, today's users must mobilize through collective action, because privacy, data-protection, and antitrust laws will not, by themselves, be enough to tame [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance capitalism]].

## Expanded Explanation

In the closing argument of "The Right to Sanctuary," Zuboff draws an explicit historical analogy between the twenty-first-century user and the early-twentieth-century worker. The worker of the first industrial era could not, acting alone, secure fair wages and humane working conditions against the concentrated power of capital; those gains required collective action — organization, mobilization, the building of countervailing institutions. Zuboff insists that the same structural logic governs the fight against surveillance capitalism. Effective contest "will require determined individuals," but the individual alone "cannot shoulder the burden of justice." The burden is not merely heavy; it is, in her word for the [[The Division of Learning in Society|pathological division of learning]], *insuperable* for any one person.

The reason is the radical asymmetry built into surveillance capitalism's [[The Two Texts|two texts]]. The user has access only to the "first text" — the page they experience — while the "shadow text," the proprietary second text in which behavioral surplus is queued for manufacture into prediction products, is deliberately withheld. Zuboff anchors this with the fifteen-month odyssey of Belgian mathematician and data-protection activist Paul-Olivier Dehaye, who in December 2016 requested his personal data from Facebook and, in March 2018, was finally told that the targeting analyses he sought were stored in "Hive" and "not available through our self-service tools." Dehaye knew more about Cambridge Analytica's operations than almost anyone outside the firm, and even he could not breach the shadow text. His experience is, for Zuboff, "but one illustration of the self-sustaining nature of a pathological division of learning and the insuperable burden placed on individuals moved to challenge its injustice." If the most expert and determined individual is defeated by the asymmetry, the ordinary user has no chance acting alone.

From this Zuboff draws the conclusion that defensive, individual, legal-procedural remedies are necessary but not sufficient. Individuals each wrestling with the complexities of their own data protection "will be no match for surveillance capitalism's staggering asymmetries of knowledge and power." Privacy law in the US has failed to keep pace; even the EU's GDPR will be decided not by "the wording of the regulations but rather the popular movements on the ground." A century ago workers organized for collective action and "ultimately tipped the scales of power," and today's users "will have to mobilize in new ways." Her flat warning: "If the past is a prologue, then privacy, data protection, and antitrust laws will not be enough to interrupt surveillance capitalism." What is required instead is the collective assertion of [[The Counter-Declaration and the Synthetic Declaration|synthetic declarations]] — institutionalized in new centers of democratic power, expertise, and contest.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism is structured around the [[The Two Texts|two texts]]: users see only the first text, while the proprietary shadow text is withheld, producing extreme asymmetries of knowledge and power. (Directly stated)
2. Dehaye's fifteen-month, ultimately rebuffed Facebook data request shows that even the most expert, determined individual cannot breach the shadow text — the burden on individuals is "insuperable." (Directly stated)
3. Therefore "the individual alone cannot bear the burden of this fight," and individuals each managing their own data protection "will be no match" for the asymmetries. (Directly stated)
4. The early-twentieth-century worker faced an analogous concentration of power and could not win fair wages alone — only collective action "tipped the scales." (Directly stated)
5. Privacy, data-protection (incl. GDPR), and antitrust laws are individual-and-procedural in their default operation and "will not be enough to interrupt surveillance capitalism." (Directly stated)
6. Therefore taming surveillance capitalism requires collective mobilization and synthetic declarations institutionalized in new centers of democratic power — "this quality of collective action will be required." (Directly stated)

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The worker analogy: the early-twentieth-century individual worker who could not bear the burden of fighting for fair wages and conditions alone, so that "those twentieth-century challenges required collective action, and so do our own." (Ch. 17, "The Right to Sanctuary")
- The Dehaye odyssey: a December 2016 data request answered only in March 2018, fifteen months later, with the targeting analyses locked in Facebook's "Hive" / shadow text and "not available through our self-service tools." (Ch. 17)
- The shadow-text asymmetry: "no surveillance capitalist will voluntarily provide data from the shadow text. Only law can compel this challenge to the pathological division of learning." (Ch. 17)
- The GDPR judgment: its impact "will not be the wording of the regulations but rather the popular movements on the ground that shape these interpretations." (Ch. 17)
- The explicit limitation on law: "privacy, data protection, and antitrust laws will not be enough to interrupt surveillance capitalism." (Ch. 17, "Prophecy")
- The emerging model of collective action: Max Schrems and None of Your Business (NOYB) as a nascent "stable European enforcement platform" uniting groups of users — pointing to "a social void that must be filled with creative new forms of collective action." (Ch. 17)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Zuboff does not dismiss individual effort or law: she stresses that "effective contest will require determined individuals" and that "only law can compel" disclosure of the shadow text. The claim is about *sufficiency* — the individual *alone*, and law *alone*, are inadequate — not about worthlessness.
- The collective-action prescription is, in this chapter, established more in form than in fully worked-out substance: NOYB is offered as an early, partial illustration of "new forms of collective action," and the concrete institutional design of the needed synthetic declarations is gestured toward rather than fully specified. (Strongly implied)
- The worker analogy is illustrative, not demonstrative: that labor organizing tipped the scales against industrial capital does not by itself prove that an analogous mobilization can constrain an economic logic embedded in proprietary machine intelligence and global infrastructure. (Interpretive synthesis)

## Tensions

- [[Knowledge vs Power]] — the argument turns on the concentration of knowledge as power in the shadow text, which no individual can redistribute alone.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — collective mobilization is Zuboff's wager for reclaiming freedom from a regime of remote behavioral control that individuals cannot resist singly.
- [[Division of Labor vs Division of Learning]] — the pathological division of learning, not merely a division of labor, is what places the insuperable burden on individuals and demands a collective response.
