# Freedom itself is producing coercion

## Claim

In the present phase of history, freedom — defined as the absence of constraint — switches over into compulsion, because the subject that takes itself to be a free, self-fashioning project subjugates itself to internal limits of compulsive achievement and optimization; depression and burnout are the pathological signs of this reversal.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the central thesis of *Psychopolitics*. Han opens not with technology or surveillance but with a structural paradox internal to freedom itself. The subject of the present no longer experiences itself as subjugated — as a *subject*, "the one who has been cast down" — but as a *project*, "always refashioning and reinventing" itself. The felt liberation of moving from subject to project is real, but it conceals a switch: the I that believes itself free of external, alien limitation has merely relocated limitation inward, where it returns as "compulsive achievement and optimization." Freedom does not so much disappear as *invert*.

The argument turns on the difference between two modal verbs. The disciplinarian *Should* issues commandments and prohibitions and therefore has a limit; the neoliberal *Can* has none, so the compulsion it entails is unlimited. This is why "the freedom of Can generates even more coercion than the disciplinarian Should." Freedom is technically the opposite of constraint — yet here "freedom itself, which is supposed to be the opposite of constraint, is producing coercion." Crucially, the coercion is more efficient precisely because it is self-administered and feels like liberty: it is inefficient to exploit people against their will, so only the [[Exploitation of Freedom]] maximizes returns.

The reversal is legible in the body. Han names depression and burnout as the symptoms by which the crisis becomes visible: they are not private misfortunes but "pathological signs that freedom is now switching over into manifold forms of compulsion." The [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] that deems itself free is, in reality, "an absolute slave" — one that exploits itself without a master, collapsing the Hegelian master and slave into a single self-combating figure who practises [[Auto-Exploitation]] voluntarily.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The contemporary subject experiences itself as a free, self-determining *project* rather than a subjugated *subject*.
2. In throwing off external constraint, the project does not escape constraint but internalizes it as self-imposed compulsion — "compulsive achievement and optimization."
3. The modality of freedom is *Can*, which (unlike the disciplinarian *Should*) has no limit; therefore the compulsion it generates is unlimited and exceeds that of discipline.
4. This self-administered coercion is more efficient than coercion against the will, because exploited freedom maximizes returns ([[Exploitation of Freedom]]).
5. Therefore freedom, the supposed opposite of constraint, is itself now producing coercion.
6. The evidence of this reversal is somatic and psychic: depression and burnout are the pathological signs that freedom is switching over into compulsion.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The etymological and structural reading of *subject* ("the one who has been cast down") versus *project* (the self-refashioning I), from [[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/01 The Crisis of Freedom]] — used to show that the felt shift to freedom is itself a more efficient mode of subjugation.
- The *Can* / *Should* contrast: *Should* "has a limit," *Can* "has none," so the compulsion of *Can* is unlimited. Realizes the committed concept [[Can vs Should]].
- Depression and burnout as named clinical-pathological signs of the reversal — the symptom that makes the otherwise invisible switch observable.
- The Hegelian master–slave dialectic, inverted: the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] "willingly exploits itself without a master," becoming "an absolute slave" — the totalization of labour through [[Auto-Exploitation]].

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is diagnostic and largely asserted rather than empirically demonstrated; the link from a structural account of *Can* to the clinical epidemiology of depression and burnout is presented as self-evident rather than evidenced. Han offers the maladies as illustration, not proof.
- It risks a totalizing reading in which all experienced freedom is unmasked as coercion, leaving little room for freedom that is not already a "ruse." Han himself gestures toward this when he asks whether freedom must be *redefined* — as relationship — to escape the dialectic, suggesting the reversal is not absolute but contingent on a deficient (isolating, neoliberal) concept of freedom.
- The claim depends on accepting the *Can*/*Should* periodization (a transition from disciplinary to achievement society); a reader who disputes that the disciplinary mode has actually been superseded would weaken the contrast on which the thesis rests.
