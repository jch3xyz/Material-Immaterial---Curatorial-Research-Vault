# Poststructuralism is the necessary correlative of telematic technology

## Claim

The deconstructive dissolution of the fixed object, the single original author, and literal meaning advanced by Barthes, Foucault, and Derrida is the "necessary (and perhaps inevitable) correlative" of networked computing — both replace fixity, causality, and stable identity with process, intertext, and field.

## Expanded Explanation

In "Concerning Nets and Spurs: Meaning, Mind, and Telematic Diffusion" (1985), Roy Ascott builds the keystone bridge between his theory of [[Telematic Art|telematic art]] and the poststructuralist criticism of his contemporaries. The essay's two halves announce the thesis structurally: "nets" names the ancient and quantum-physical metaphor of the world as a woven web of relations, while "spurs" is borrowed from Derrida's *Éperons: Les Styles de Nietzsche*, standing "metonymically for that corpus of texts by Roland Barthes, Michel Foucault, Derrida, and the poststructuralist, deconstructive practice they have advanced." Ascott then states the argument flatly: "The whole poststructuralist project can be seen as the necessary (and perhaps inevitable) correlative to the emergent communications technology that is so radically and rapidly affecting our ways of human interaction, the diffusion and integration of ideas, and our understanding and use of memory, language, and even human identity."

The correlation is structural, not stylistic. The rubric "telematics," Ascott writes, "qualifies a whole class of consciousness and culture in which new modalities of knowledge and the means of their distribution are being tested and extended"; telematic process "involves ambiguity, uncertainty, and incompleteness; meaning is not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined; truth, always relative, does not lie in an absolute location but is embedded in process." Each of these is a deconstructive commitment realized in hardware: the [[Distributed Authorship|dispersed authorship]] of the network embodies and amplifies Barthes's "death of the author" ("the text is not a line of words releasing a single 'theological' meaning . . . but a multidimensional space"); the associative, intertextual ranging of computer memory across previously walled-off categories enacts the deconstructive "intertext," the insight "that all texts and meanings are part of a continuous field"; and Derrida's *différance* — "difference" plus the "deferral of ultimate meaning and postponement of presence" — names exactly the flux in which telematic meaning is "telematically inscribed."

Against this Ascott sets the order being dissolved: a "rude fetishism" of "unity, identity, duration substance, cause, materiality, being," which he quotes from Nietzsche's *Twilight of the Idols* as the language-bound metaphysics that "create[s] the concept 'thing.'" Telematic networks, "celebrat[ing] the death of the 'individual,' 'original' author," substitute "a new 'dispersed authorship' generating massive variety within the virtual 'immateriality' of the networks." The argument thus folds the technological and the philosophical into one movement: the same "spur" that pushes the deconstructive ram into "the old order of art and knowledge" is the network that makes a "screen of transformations" replace "the mirror of reflections." Poststructuralism is what the technology means.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Telematic technology replaces the sender-to-receiver transmission of fixed content with a field in which meaning "is not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined" and truth "is embedded in process."
2. Poststructuralism independently dissolves the same three fixities — the stable object, the single original author, and literal/located meaning — replacing them with intertext, dispersed authorship, and the free play of sense.
3. The two movements are not merely coincident: the network materially "embodies and amplifies" the deconstructive insight (Barthes's multidimensional text, Derrida's *différance*, the continuous field of intertext), and the technology supplies the cultural ground that makes those insights livable rather than literary.
4. Therefore poststructuralism is the "necessary (and perhaps inevitable) correlative" of telematic technology — the philosophy adequate to, and called into being alongside, networked computing.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The flat thesis statement that the "whole poststructuralist project" is the "necessary (and perhaps inevitable) correlative" to "emergent communications technology" (see [[Telematic Discourse]]).
- Barthes's death of the author / "tissue of quotations": the network "embodies and amplifies Barthes's earlier insight" (see [[Distributed Authorship]]).
- Derrida's *Éperons* (the "spurs") and *Glas*, and the portmanteau *différance* carrying "difference" and the "deferral of ultimate meaning and postponement of presence."
- The deconstructive "intertext" — "all texts and meanings are part of a continuous field" — paralleled with Capra's quantum "cosmic web" of interconnection.
- Nietzsche's *Twilight of the Idols*: language as a "perpetual advocate" forcing us to "posit unity, identity, duration substance, cause, materiality, being," the metaphysics the telematic mode dissolves.
- Lyotard and Chaput's *Les Immatériaux* exhibition, which "registers the dissolution of the old orders of thought based on aesthetics, the legitimacy of the object and the fixity of individual identity" — organized literally as a "system of webbings."
- The 1983 project *La Plissure du Texte* (homage to Barthes's *Le Plaisir du texte*): hundreds of dispersed "users" across eleven cities weaving "a massive intertext" with no single author.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The relation is asserted as "necessary (and perhaps inevitable)" but the parenthetical "perhaps" concedes the inevitability is unproven; the convergence could be a shared cultural moment (both arising in the same post-Enlightenment ferment) rather than a causal or logical entailment — Ascott's own examples (Nietzsche, Duchamp) predate the technology.
- Ascott welds together heterogeneous sources — Barthes, Derrida, Lyotard, quantum physics (Capra, Bohm, Heisenberg), eastern mysticism, and ancient net-myth — by metaphorical resonance ("not causally but associatively") rather than argument; critics may judge the "cosmic web" parallel between deconstruction and quantum theory to be analogy stretched past its load.
- The celebration of "dispersed authorship" and dissolved identity sits uneasily with Ascott's own closing warning that the network's potential "will only be realised to the extent that artists resist the monopolisation and colonisation of electronic space by multinational business and the military" — the same immateriality can serve domination.
