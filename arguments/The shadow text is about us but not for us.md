# The shadow text is about us but not for us

## Claim

We author the public first text, but its [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral surplus]] fills a hidden shadow text — created, maintained, and exploited outside our awareness — whose learning is then turned back to shape the public text to surveillance capital's commercial interests, so that we are the objects of the second text and never its readers.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff names this the "problem of the two texts." The first text is public-facing and familiar: Google Search codifying the web, Facebook's News Feed binding the network, and above all the "great massing hubbub of our lives" that we ourselves inscribe — posts, photos, conversations, likes. Of this first text "we are its authors and readers." But under [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance capitalism]] the first text "does not stand alone; it trails a shadow close behind." Its real function is to be the *supply operation* for a second, hidden text. Everything we contribute, "no matter how trivial or fleeting," becomes a target for surplus extraction, and that surplus "fills the pages of the second text." The shadow text is "read only" for surveillance capitalists; it is "a burgeoning accumulation of behavioral surplus and its analyses, and it says more about us than we can know about ourselves."

The argument's sting is the loop's closure. The shadow text is not merely a hidden ledger about us — its learning is applied *back* to the first text we still believe we control. Google's algorithms, "derived from surplus, select and order search results," and Facebook's algorithms, "derived from surplus, select and order the content of its News Feed," and researchers have shown these manipulations "reflect each corporation's commercial objectives." The public text we read is thus quietly authored by lessons drawn from the shadow text we are forbidden to read. Frank Pasquale's account of the Googleplex supplies the evidence: behind closed doors, "the power to include, exclude, and rank" makes value-laden decisions, so that these firms "help create the world they claim to merely 'show' us."

Hence the asymmetry that gives the note its title. "We are the objects of its narratives, from whose lessons we are excluded." The second text "is about us, but it is not for us." This is the concrete mechanism of the larger [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning in society]] and of the [[Asymmetries of Knowledge and Power|asymmetries of knowledge and power]] it institutes: they know a great deal about us, while "our access to their knowledge is sparse: hidden in the shadow text and read only by the new priests, their bosses, and their machines." (Directly stated.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism's mechanisms compel the production of *two* electronic texts, not one (the "problem of the two texts").
2. The first text is public and authored by us; the second (shadow) text is hidden and "read only" for surveillance capitalists.
3. The first text functions as the *supply operation* for the second: our contributions become behavioral surplus that fills the shadow text.
4. Surveillance capitalists apply what they learn from the exclusive shadow text *back* to shape the public first text (search ranking, News Feed ordering — algorithms "derived from surplus") to their commercial interests.
5. Therefore we author the first text but are excluded from the second, of which we are merely the objects — it is "about us, but it is not for us."

- The closing of the loop named concretely: Google's and Facebook's algorithms "derived from surplus" that "select and order" what we see, with research showing the manipulations track commercial objectives. ## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The platform self-defense (Pasquale's foil): firms claim "objectivity and neutrality," presenting themselves as mirrors that merely *show* the world. Zuboff treats this as ideology, not description — "they help create the world they claim to merely 'show' us." This is the same neutral-intermediary posture critiqued in [[Section 230's neutral-intermediary logic collapses under surveillance capitalism]].
- A reformist reply: transparency or data-access rights could let us read the shadow text. Zuboff anticipates this — secrecy is not incidental but compelled by "surveillance capitalism's laws of motion" (see [[Euphemism and secrecy are structural to surveillance capitalism not incidental]]), so a right to read the second text would contradict the logic that produces it.
- Scope limit: the claim is grounded in the search/News-Feed cases of c. 2018; the *mechanism* (surplus → shadow text → reshaped public text) is asserted as general to the logic, but the specific manipulations cited are platform- and period-bound.
