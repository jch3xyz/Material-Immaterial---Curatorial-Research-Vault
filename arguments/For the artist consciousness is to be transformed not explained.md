# For the artist consciousness is to be transformed not explained

## Claim

For the artist, consciousness is something to be explored, transformed, and reframed rather than explained — and the very provenance of twentieth-century art, through its psychic, spiritual, and conceptual aspirations, already leads toward this technoetic condition.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott opens by setting art and science side by side on the same problem. Consciousness, he notes, is at once the most ubiquitous thing we possess and the least understood, and science — for all its effort — has made "distinctly limited success" in explaining it; it "seems to pose the most intractable of problems." Against the explanatory ambition of reductionist science, Ascott posits a different artistic relation to mind. The artist does not seek to *explain* consciousness but to *explore* it; not to *understand* it but to *transform* it; not to *report* it but to *reframe* it. The contrast is a contrast of stances, not merely of methods: where science brackets and objectifies, art enters, alters, and recasts.

Crucially, Ascott does not present this as a novel program. He argues that "the very provenance of art in the twentieth century leads, through its psychic, spiritual, and conceptual aspirations, towards this technoetic condition," pointing to Duchamp, Kandinsky, Klee, and Boccioni early in the century as "the roots of this tendency." The transformation-of-consciousness vocation of art is thus framed as a lineage being fulfilled, not invented — modern art was already reaching past the visible object toward mind, and telematic and digital media now extend that reach (see [[Technoetics]], [[Technoetic Aesthetics]], [[Network Consciousness]]). This is the conceptual ground on which Ascott's [[New Media Art]] and his account of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] stand: art's task is the creation, navigation, and distribution of mind.

The argument carries a polemical edge. Ascott warns that digital and communications technology has, "in many cases," reduced art "to a form of craft" in which "polished technique or skilful programming" and "dazzling special effects" displace "the creation of meaning and values" — a "dumbing down from art to craft" he likens to the dynamics of the Arts and Crafts movement. The transform-not-explain thesis is therefore also a standard against which to judge technological art: technique that does not serve the exploration and transformation of consciousness has missed art's vocation.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Consciousness is ubiquitous yet least understood; it is "high on the agenda" in both art and science.
2. Science attempts to *explain* consciousness and largely fails — it is "the most intractable of problems."
3. The artist's relation to consciousness is categorically different: to explore (not explain), to transform (not merely understand), to reframe (not report).
4. This is not a new imposition: twentieth-century art's psychic, spiritual, and conceptual aspirations (Duchamp, Kandinsky, Klee, Boccioni) already lead toward the technoetic condition.
5. Therefore the proper vocation of art — including new-media and telematic art — is the transformation of consciousness, against which mere technical virtuosity is a degradation of art into craft.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Ascott concedes science's direction is not fixed: "there are signs that science is becoming more subjective," which softens the art/science opposition into a convergence rather than a permanent divide.
- The claim that art *transforms* rather than *explains* consciousness is asserted as the artist's stance, not demonstrated; whether artistic "reframing" yields knowledge of consciousness in any sense science would accept is left open (it borders on [[Art vs Technics]] and the broader [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] problem).
- The appeal to Duchamp, Kandinsky, Klee, and Boccioni is a selective genealogy; it reads the "psychic and spiritual" strand of modernism as the mainline tendency, which a formalist or materialist account of the same period would contest.
- Ascott's own warning — that much technological art collapses into craft and special effects — is a limitation on his optimism: the technoetic condition is a potential of new media, not its guaranteed achievement.
