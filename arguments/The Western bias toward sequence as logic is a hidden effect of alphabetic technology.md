# The Western bias toward sequence as logic is a hidden effect of alphabetic technology

## Claim

The West treats sequence as the very form of reason — saying that one thing "follows" from another as if a cause were at work — but this conviction is not a discovery of logic; it is a perceptual habit trained into Western man by the phonetic alphabet, a media-effect that David Hume's and Immanuel Kant's analyses of causality both presuppose without ever detecting.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan grants the genealogy of the problem to philosophy. In the literate West "it is still plausible and acceptable to say that something 'follows' from something, as if there were some cause at work that makes such a sequence." Hume's eighteenth-century achievement was to dismantle exactly this: he "demonstrated that there is no causality indicated in any sequence, natural or logical. The sequential is merely additive, not causative." Kant credited Hume with waking him "from my dogmatic slumber." So the *content* of the question — whether sequence carries causal force — had already been worked over by the time McLuhan writes.

McLuhan's move is to shift from content to form, from the proposition to the perceptual ground on which the proposition becomes thinkable in the first place. The reason the Western mind even *expects* sequence to be causal, the reason "the chain of inference" feels like "the mark of logic and reason," is not argued but *acquired* — it is the residue of "all our centuries of phonetic literacy." This is the same analytic figure as [[The medium not its content is the message]]: the alphabet's "message" is not any sentence written with it but "the lineal structuring of rational life" it silently installs. Hence McLuhan's verdict on the two philosophers — that "neither Hume nor Kant, however, detected the hidden cause of our Western bias toward sequence as 'logic' in the all-pervasive technology of the alphabet." They were operating *inside* the alphabetic sensorium and so could examine the inference but not the medium that made inference feel like reason.

The phonetic alphabet is uniquely suited to do this because it "diminishes the role of the other senses" and trains "the breaking up of every kind of experience into uniform units," a habit McLuhan calls "the secret of Western power over man and nature alike." Sequence-as-logic is the cognitive face of that fragmentation. And because it is a *technological* effect rather than a law of thought, it is historically reversible: "Today in the electric age we feel as free to invent nonlineal logics as we do to make non-Euclidean geometries." The electric extension of the nervous system ([[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]]) returns the all-at-once configurational field of consciousness — "there is nothing lineal or sequential about the total field of awareness that exists in any moment of consciousness" — and so loosens the chain of inference that print had made to seem natural.

## Reasoning Structure

1. In literate Western culture it is taken as obvious that one thing "follows" from another, as if sequence implied a cause.
2. Hume showed this is false at the level of content: "there is no causality indicated in any sequence" — "the sequential is merely additive, not causative" — and Kant credited Hume with awakening him.
3. But Hume and Kant analyzed the *proposition* about causality; they never asked *why the West takes sequence for logic in the first place*.
4. McLuhan locates that hidden cause one level down, in form: "centuries of phonetic literacy" trained "the chain of inference as the mark of logic and reason," because the alphabet breaks experience "into uniform units" and extends "patterns of visual uniformity and continuity."
5. Therefore the sequence-bias is a media-effect of alphabetic technology, not a feature of mind as such — the same logic as [[The medium not its content is the message]] applied to reason itself.
6. Because it is technological, it is contingent and now dissolving: in the electric age "we feel as free to invent nonlineal logics as we do to make non-Euclidean geometries."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Correlation, not demonstrated causation.** McLuhan asserts the alphabet *caused* the sequence-bias but, by his own account of Hume, co-occurrence of literacy and lineal logic is "merely additive, not causative." The cross-cultural contrast with Chinese ideogrammic culture is suggestive but not a controlled demonstration. McLuhan concedes the point needs "a much more extensive inquiry than that of the present chapter."
- **Possible misreading of Hume/Kant.** One might argue Kant's transcendental analysis *did* address the conditions of the possibility of causal judgment — i.e., he sought a non-empirical "cause" of the sequence-bias — so McLuhan's claim that Kant "detected" nothing of the kind is at least contestable; McLuhan simply means neither located a *technological* cause.
- **Determinism.** The argument can read as technological determinism, treating a form of reason as a side-effect of a writing system; McLuhan softens this only by insisting the effect is now reversible in the electric age.
- **Scope of "logic."** McLuhan slides between "sequence as logic," "the chain of inference," and "cause and effect ... in the form of things in sequence and succession"; whether these are one bias or several is left undifferentiated.
