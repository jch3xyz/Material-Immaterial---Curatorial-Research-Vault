# Idealism is the return of God in philosophy of mind

## Claim

[[Idealism]] is the return of God in the philosophy of mind. The structure is a symmetry by reversal: [[René Descartes|Descartes]] invoked God to guarantee that the external world exists; Bernardo Kastrup, who does not believe in the external world, invokes a cosmic mind to guarantee that it does *not*. The same figure — God, "whether it goes by God, Brahman, or some other name" — is summoned to patch the load-bearing gap in two opposite metaphysics, and for [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] that recurrence, not either theory, is the chapter's real datum: "God keeps barging into philosophy long after we declared him dead."

## Expanded Explanation

The argument is built on an exact mirror. Descartes's *cogito* establishes that the only certainty is thought and perception, and from that island of certainty he must rebuild the outside world — a reconstruction that requires a non-deceiving God to vouch that our clear and distinct perceptions correspond to something real. Kastrup runs the *cogito* up to the same point — "all we can know for certain is thought and perception" — but, "unlike Descartes," refuses the reconstruction: he keeps mind as the only certainty and lets the material world go. Yet [[Idealism]] cannot simply stop at solipsism. To explain why there is "a shared, objective world that all of us experience," and why "trees still fall in the woods even when no one hears them," idealism needs a **universal mind** — Bishop Berkeley's God, "the infinite and omnipresent mind of God," who keeps the world "in perpetual existence simply by looking at it." So God reappears on the far side of the dichotomy. O'Gieblyn's compression is the heart of the note: "Descartes needed God to prove that the external world exists. Kastrup does not believe in the material world and yet needs God to prove it doesn't exist."

The force of the argument is that it does not resolve into a refutation. O'Gieblyn poses the disjunction herself: either the recurrence of God is "intellectual laziness, appealing to a 'God of the gaps' to patch over the chasms in a theory's logic," or "there something more telling—and more troubling—in the fact that God keeps barging into philosophy long after we declared him dead?" The "we declared him dead" is the Nietzschean-Weberian verdict of [[Disenchantment]] — the modern conviction that mechanism had emptied the heavens. That God should keep returning to do logical work in the most rigorous theories of mind is, on O'Gieblyn's reading, evidence that disenchantment never fully took. The pattern is the same one she traces across the book: the mind-uploading wager is [[The mind-uploading problem is the resurrection problem in technical dress|resurrection in technical dress]], the computational soul is [[The computer metaphor reintroduces the disembodied soul it was built to abolish|the disembodied soul]] reintroduced, and idealism is God re-entering by the back door of consciousness.

The second move converts the symmetry into a verdict about plausibility. If God recurs because every theory of mind is gapped — materialism cannot explain consciousness, dualism is inelegant, [[Panpsychism]] only deepens matter's inscrutability, idealism needs a cosmic dreamer — then no contender holds the high ground. O'Gieblyn grants that she finds Kastrup's idealism "highly implausible," but immediately adds the reversal that gives the argument its sting: "though not necessarily more implausible than the hypothesis that consciousness arises out of inert matter, or that the mind is software, or that observation can collapse a wave into a particle, or that the mind does not exist at all." Once the options and their limitations are exhausted, "the idea of God begins to seem just as crazy as anything else." The return of God is therefore not a sign of weak idealism but a symptom of the field: the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]] leaves every account equally short, and into that shortfall God walks.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Descartes used God instrumentally: the *cogito* secures only mind, so a non-deceiving God is needed to guarantee that the external material world exists.
2. Kastrup adopts the same Cartesian starting point — "all we can know for certain is thought and perception" — but, "unlike Descartes," does not rebuild the material world; for him matter is "an explanatory abstraction" and "consciousness is all that exists."
3. Idealism nonetheless requires a **universal mind** to avoid collapsing into solipsism — to explain a shared, stable world (Berkeley's God "looking at it") and to solve the **decomposition problem** of how one mind splits into many ("disassociated alters").
4. So God reappears, doing the opposite job: not proving the world *exists* but underwriting the claim that it does *not* (as the dreaming cosmic mind whose "vision" the inanimate world is).
5. Hence the exact reversal: "Descartes needed God to prove that the external world exists. Kastrup does not believe in the material world and yet needs God to prove it doesn't."
6. The recurrence admits two readings: a lazy "God of the gaps," or "something more telling—and more troubling—in the fact that God keeps barging into philosophy long after we declared him dead."
7. Because every theory of mind is exhausted by its own limitations, none is privileged; therefore "the idea of God begins to seem just as crazy as anything else," and idealism's return of God is a symptom of the whole field, not a defect peculiar to idealism.
8. Conclusion: idealism is the return of God in the philosophy of mind, and the recurrence of God patching a logical gap — the same gap, from opposite directions — is the chapter's real finding.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Kastrup himself resists the word "God."** O'Gieblyn is careful: "Kastrup mostly avoids the term 'God,' with or without the capital letter. He doesn't believe the cosmic mind is omniscient, omnipotent, or benevolent; most likely it is not even self-reflective, or self-aware." The "return of God" is O'Gieblyn's diagnostic framing, not Kastrup's self-description — the cosmic mind is a depersonalized stand-in, which weakens any claim that this is literally theism revived.
- **The "God of the gaps" charge cuts the other way.** If invoking a universal mind to plug an explanatory hole is illegitimate, then it is illegitimate for idealism too; O'Gieblyn raises this against herself ("Was this simply intellectual laziness...?") and does not settle it. The argument's interest is precisely that it withholds a verdict.
- **Symmetry is not equivalence.** That God appears on both sides of the Descartes/Kastrup mirror is rhetorically striking, but the two appeals do different work (epistemic guarantor vs. ontological substrate); reading them as the *same* recurrence is O'Gieblyn's interpretive bridge, not a logical identity.
- **Author's self-suspicion.** O'Gieblyn flags her own susceptibility — "I grew up religious and might not be capable of distinguishing compelling logic from simple familiarity" — and that Kastrup's "monist disposition" may have led him "to prefer the elegance of idealism for purely aesthetic reasons." The plausibility claim is hedged, not asserted.
