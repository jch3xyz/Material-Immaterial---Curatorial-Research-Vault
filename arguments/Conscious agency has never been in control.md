# Conscious agency has never been in control

## Claim

In the posthuman view, conscious agency has never been "in control"; the sense of mastery through autonomous will is only the story consciousness tells itself to explain results that actually come about through emergent processes and [[Distributed Cognition|distributed cognition]] — so the very feeling of control is evidence not of control but of ignorance about those processes.

## Expanded Explanation

This is [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s sharpest deflation of the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]]. The liberal subject takes conscious will to be the seat of identity and the engine of action: I decide, therefore I am, and therefore I master. Against this, Hayles advances a flat denial — not that conscious agency *has lost* control, but that it *never had it*. The reversal is total. Where the liberal subject reads the smooth coincidence of intention and outcome as proof of mastery, Hayles reads the same coincidence as a narrative consciousness manufactures after the fact, a "story" overlaid on processes it does not author and largely cannot perceive.

The crucial epistemic twist is that the feeling of being in control is not neutral but actively misleading. "The very illusion of control bespeaks a fundamental ignorance about the nature of the emergent processes through which consciousness, the organism, and the environment are constituted." The illusion is not a harmless byproduct; it is a symptom of not understanding how [[Emergence|emergence]], the body, and the environment co-constitute one another. The more confidently consciousness narrates its own sovereignty, the more it advertises how little it grasps of the [[Pattern and Randomness|chaotic dynamics and emergent structures]] from which its results actually emerge. Consciousness, in the book's recurring figure, is "a small subsystem running its program of selfconstruction and self-assurance while remaining ignorant of the actual dynamics of complex systems."

This argument is the agency-specific edge of the broader concept of [[Distributed Cognition]], and it should not be collapsed into it. Distributed cognition is the positive thesis — that cognition resides across human and nonhuman actors and "smarter environments." *This* argument is the negative, deflationary corollary aimed precisely at the will: it dethrones the autonomous subject by showing that the locus of control it claims for itself was never occupied. Hayles grounds the positive picture in Edwin Hutchins's ethnography of ship navigation (where a solution to a steering failure was "discovered by the organization [of the system as a whole] before it was discovered by any of the participants"), and she explicitly aligns the deflation with the feminist critique of mastery: the desire for control is bound up with "an objectivist account of science, and the imperialist project of subduing nature." Dethroning conscious agency is thus not only a metaphysical correction but an ethical and political one.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The liberal humanist subject identifies the human essence with conscious agency and treats autonomous will as the source of mastery over self and environment (Weizenbaum's "ethical imperative that humans keep control" is the explicit version Hayles targets).
2. But cognition and decision-making are in fact distributed across human and nonhuman actors and emergent systems; results "actually come about through chaotic dynamics and emergent structures," not through the directives of a sovereign will. (See [[Distributed Cognition]], Hutchins's navigation study.)
3. Therefore the experience of "being in control" cannot be a veridical report of how outcomes are produced; it is "merely the story consciousness tells itself to explain" results it did not author.
4. Worse, this story is diagnostic of error: "the very illusion of control bespeaks a fundamental ignorance" of the emergent processes constituting consciousness, organism, and environment. The feeling of mastery measures ignorance, not power.
5. Conclusion: conscious agency "has never been 'in control.'" Within the substitution list that defines Hayles's benign posthuman, "distributed cognition replaces autonomous will."
6. Corollary (feminist alignment): because the desire for mastery is entangled with objectivism and the "imperialist project of subduing nature," abandoning the illusion of control is also a way to "avoid reinscribing, and thus repeating, some of the mistakes of the past."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The accountability objection (Weizenbaum's).** If conscious agency was never in control, what becomes of responsibility and judgment? Weizenbaum holds that to cede judgment "is to abdicate their responsibilities as autonomous independent beings." Hayles's reply relocates rather than abolishes agency — into the distributed system — but the argument does not, on its own, supply an account of who or what is answerable when "thinking" is done by both human and nonhuman actors.
- **Self-undermining worry.** The claim is asserted *by* a conscious agent (Hayles) who presumably means it deliberately. If conscious control is illusory, the deliberate framing of the argument occupies an awkward performative position — the deflation is stated in the very register it deflates.
- **Hayles's own hedge.** She is careful that this is "not necessarily what the posthuman will mean—only what it can mean if certain strands among its complex seriations are highlighted and combined." The dethroning of agency is offered as a *usable* construction, not a proven fact about minds; it is a normative selection from the posthuman's "complex seriations," not a settled empirical result.
- **Risk of overreach.** Reading every "illusion of control" as "ignorance" risks proving too much: it can make any felt agency self-refuting, blurring the difference between coordinated, embodied skill and delusion. The argument leans on, but does not fully argue for, the strong claim that *all* such feeling is illusory rather than partial.

## Tensions

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — this argument detonates the pole of *control* from the inside: the subject's prized mastery is recast as the least controlled thing about it, and "freedom" understood as autonomous will dissolves into distributed, emergent process.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the deflation keeps agency *embodied and material* (distributed across enacted bodies and media) rather than relocating it to disembodied information; it inherits the book's governing tension over whether the posthuman frees mind from, or re-roots it in, the body.
