# Telematic art must remove the second observer and the phantom audience

## Claim

Gallery-installed interactive art is re-objectified by being viewed by a second observer, contradicting its open-ended meaning; invoking the pagé's non-performative ritual, Ascott prescribes removing the second observer and the phantom audience from the canon of [[Telematic Art]] — an audience-less enactment of mind.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott concedes that interactive art, as it is actually staged, betrays itself. By its "structure, placement, and presentation" — typically a museum or gallery — networked interactive work "presupposes, in spite of itself, an audience of more or less passive observers" even as it proposes a genuine participant engaged in open-ended interaction with its interface. The work therefore harbours a dichotomy: it aspires to the "open-ended evolution of meanings" yet remains sealed inside "the closure of an autonomous frame of consciousness." The whole apparatus, participant viewer included, "however dynamic a process it may be, is actually incarcerated within the very status it despises, that of pure object" — an envelope bracketed in space and time, there "to be viewed by a second observer." The second observer is what collapses an open system back into an object; it is the spectatorial position the gallery silently installs around any interactive piece.

To dissolve this contradiction Ascott invokes the shamanic tradition by way of contrast. All the activity of the pagé and those who interact with him — image-making, dancing, chanting, drumming — "is performative but is not intended as a public performance." It "is never played to an audience, actual or implicit"; no one watches or is expected to watch what is enacted. This is "not a public performance but a spiritual enactment," a structuring of psychic forces — what the [[Performance vs Enactment]] tension names. Transposed to interactive art, the lesson is to avoid "the double observer, the phantom audience" so that the work becomes "an enactment of mind," "an art without audience in its inactive mode." Eschewing "the passive voyeur, the traditional gallery viewer," this technoetic aesthetic substitutes a "widespread intimacy" on the planetary scale for the watching crowd.

The argument is thus the negative complement to Ascott's [[The shift from observer to participator signals a paradigm shift across art and science|shift from observer to participator]]: it is not enough to add a participant; the residual *outer* observer must be subtracted as well, or the work relapses into [[Object vs System|pure object]]. This is why telematic art, having already [[Telematic art moves beyond both object art and time-based art|moved beyond object art and time-based art]], must also move beyond the spectator who frames it.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Interactive art is normally placed in a museum/gallery, which presupposes an audience of passive observers alongside its active participant.
2. Being viewed by this "second observer" re-encloses even a dynamic, open process in "the very status it despises, that of pure object."
3. This produces a contradiction between the aspiration to open-ended meaning and the closure of an objectified frame.
4. The pagé's ritual shows a third option: activity that is performative yet played to no audience — enactment, not performance.
5. Therefore telematic art must remove the second observer and the phantom audience from its canon and become an audience-less "enactment of mind."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The model is normative and aspirational: Ascott admits gallery interactive art "in spite of itself" produces the observer it would abolish, but he does not specify an institutional or technical mechanism by which a networked, distributed work could actually be staged without *any* outer observer.
- The shamanic analogy is invoked "at a great distance from our current hypermediated culture," as Ascott himself concedes — its transposability to telematic art is asserted rather than demonstrated.
- An "art without audience" risks unfalsifiability and inaccessibility: it sits in tension with Ascott's own emphasis on [[Reciprocal Communication]] and [[Distributed Authorship]] across a network, and recalls Lippard's worry that dematerialized art achieved distribution but not accessibility.
