# Perpetual growth in a finite environment is impossible

## Claim

Every physical entity in a finite environment is surrounded by layers of limits, and growth itself keeps changing which limit is binding, so for any such entity perpetual growth is impossible — the only real choice is whether the limits that eventually stop growth are self-imposed or system-imposed.

## Expanded Explanation

Meadows arrives at this claim not as a slogan but as a structural consequence of how growth works. Anything that grows — a plant, a child, an epidemic, a product, a company, a city, an economy, a population — sits inside [[Layers of Limits|layers of limits]]. At any moment one factor is the binding constraint, the [[Limiting Factor|limiting factor]]; relieve it and growth resumes, but the growth then depletes or enhances the surrounding factors and shifts which one becomes limiting next. The growing entity and its finite environment are a single "coevolving dynamic system," not a mover and a passive backdrop.

Understanding this dynamic — keeping an eye on the next upcoming limit — improves one's grasp of the growth process, but Meadows is explicit that it is "not a recipe for perpetual growth." Because the environment is finite, the layers of limits are finite too; there is no limit behind which there is not, eventually, another. So the question is never *whether* growth stops but *when, at what, and by whose decision*. Either a growing entity chooses and enforces limits to keep itself within the capacity of its supporting environment, or the environment chooses and enforces those limits for it — through scarcity, price, collapse, disease, or decline.

This is the germ of *The Limits to Growth*, the world-modeling work Meadows is best known for, restated here in the compact language of systems structure. It is the direct antithesis of [[The Growth Imperative|the growth imperative]] that Harari finds at the core of the modern economic covenant, and it names one pole of the vault's [[Growth vs Limits|Growth vs Limits]] tension. Crucially, the argument concerns *physical* entities — things with material inputs and outputs — which is why it does not simply generalize to information, ideas, or love. The finitude is the finitude of matter and energy, not of everything.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Any physical entity with multiple inputs and outputs is surrounded by layers of limits; at any time one factor is binding.
2. Relieving the binding factor lets growth resume, but the growth itself changes the relative scarcity of factors, so a *different* factor soon becomes binding — the entity and its limits form one coevolving dynamic system.
3. The environment is finite, so the layers of limits are finite; there is no endless supply of "next" limits to relieve.
4. Therefore, for any physical entity in a finite environment, growth cannot continue forever.
5. The only remaining choice is *which* limits stop the growth and *who* imposes them: an entity can choose and enforce its own limits (self-imposed), or, if it does not, the supporting environment will enforce them (system-imposed).

## Evidence Used by the Author

- "There are layers of limits around every growing plant, child, epidemic, new product, technological advance, company, city, economy, and population" — establishing that the claim is not a special case but the general condition of physical growth (`Directly stated`).
- The interplay of "a growing plant and the soil, a growing company and its market, a growing economy and its resource base" as a *dynamic*, coevolving relationship rather than growth against a fixed ceiling (`Directly stated`).
- The perfect-product and the ideal-city thought experiments: a company with a perfect affordable product "will be swamped with orders until it grows to the point at which some limit decreases the perfection of the product or raises its price"; a city that meets all needs draws people "until some limit brings down the city's ability to satisfy peoples' needs" — showing that success itself summons the limiting factor (`Directly stated`).
- The stated conclusion that if managers, governments, and populations "do not choose and enforce their own... limits to keep growth within the capacity of the supporting environment, then the environment will choose and enforce limits" (`Directly stated`).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim is bounded to *physical* entities — "any physical entity with multiple inputs and outputs." It is not a metaphysical ban on all growth; the [[Material vs Immaterial|immaterial]] (knowledge, meaning, connection) is not obviously subject to the same material finitude, and Meadows does not extend the argument there.
- It is silent on *where* the limits sit. Technological advance can push a given limit far outward (raising, not removing, the ceiling), which is exactly what fuels [[The Growth Imperative|growth-imperative]] optimism; Meadows's rejoinder is that pushing one limit only reveals the next, never abolishes finitude — a claim about structure, not a forecast of any particular limit's location.
- "Impossible" is a statement about the infinite limit of a physical process, not a prediction that any specific growth curve ends soon; the argument fixes the eventual outcome, not its timing.
