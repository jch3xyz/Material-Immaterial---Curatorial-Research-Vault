# The history of automata is the aesthetic core of a Faustian drive to re-create life nonbiologically

## Claim

For Jack Burnham, what singles out Western culture among all the image-making traditions is not the making of human replicas as such but the *progressive mechanization* of automata — and in that mechanization art unknowingly converges with a Faustian desire to re-create life by nonbiological means.

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham opens Part Two ("Sculpture as System") by surveying a "continuum of images" — fetishes, idols, dolls, puppets, manikins, waxworks — that historians ranked below sculpture as "subsculpture." His thesis is that the branch of subsculpture that matters most, the one that "characterizes Western culture beyond these other forms," is the automaton, defined as "all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men." Because "the history of automata has always run close to that of technology," tracking the changing forms of automata also tracks "a parallel pulse in modern art and technology." The decisive variable is mechanization: as automata become more mechanically capable, the urge driving them shows itself for what it is.

That urge, in Burnham's reading by way of Spengler, is Faustian. The maker of anthropomorphic images ultimately wants to "bring to life" the human-shaped entity — but "not through natural biological creation." Where Apollonian sculpture stays cool, static, and within "a sense of well-defined limit," automata "possess a Faustian drive to transcend natural limitations," tying "the soul of the artist" to "mechanical fallibility" and to "the indefinite possibilities of technology." This is the structural pivot of the book: the static art object yields to the moving system.

A corollary follows about aesthetic register. Once sculpture is "willed to move," it forfeits the Apollonian "aloofness and serenity which surround the static object." Burnham aligns automata with the actor on the stage and with the machine: both "engage the mind on immediate terms," sacrificing the contemplative distance proper to sculpture for the immediacy of motion. Thus the gain in lifelikeness is also a loss — automata are "doomed to failure," carrying "the pathos" of disintegration and "a final sense of inconviction" that sculpture "would never tolerate."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Across ten thousand years, humans have made a continuum of anthropomorphic "subsculpture"; the ultimate concern of every image maker is to "bring to life" the human-shaped entity, "though not through natural biological creation."
2. What is distinctive about *Western* culture is not the image-making but the **progressive mechanization** of one branch of subsculpture — automata — whose history runs parallel to that of technology.
3. In that mechanization "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge shows itself": art converges with "a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically."
4. Sculpture (Apollonian) is static, serene, self-limiting; automata (Faustian) drive "to transcend natural limitations," binding the artist's soul to mechanical fallibility and "the indefinite possibilities of technology."
5. Therefore, once sculpture is "willed to move" it forfeits Apollonian aloofness for the immediacy of the machine and the actor — and the static object gives way to the moving system, the pivot from object to system that organizes Part Two.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The defining contrast: subsculpture in general vs. the mechanization specific to the West — "the progressive mechanization of automata" is where "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge shows itself." (Source 1)
- The Spenglerian frame: "sculpture, in its quietude arid static order, remains Apollonian while automata possess a Faustian drive to transcend natural limitations." (Source 1)
- The aesthetic cost of motion: "The machine, like the actor on the stage, engages the mind on immediate terms; both are devoid of that aloofness and serenity which surround the static object." (Source 1)
- The historical arc Burnham marshals as proof of "progressive mechanization": Egyptian servant figures as "fledgling automata"; Greek ritual and Hero of Alexandria's mechanical theatres; medieval clockwork "jacks" and the Strasbourg Cathedral clock; Descartes's mechanism, Pascal's and Leibniz's calculators; Vaucanson's flute player and *Canard*; nineteenth- and twentieth-century robots, Čapek's *R.U.R.*, and the cybernetic "pseudo-organisms" of Grey Walter, Ross Ashby, and Claude Shannon "tenuously linked with life itself."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham flags his own thesis as contestable: re-creating life nonbiologically as the convergence point of art is "an assumption which may be disputed." (Source 1, `Directly stated`)
- He concedes a long-standing counter-tradition, citing Toynbee's view "that mechanics cannot help but erode the soul of art" — so the move to motion may be decay, not advance, of the aesthetic.
- Automata "have assumed a very secondary role to sculpture" in emotional resonance; they carry "pathos" and "a final sense of inconviction" — the Faustian drive succeeds technically while failing the contemplative register sculpture commands.
- The argument is Eurocentric by design (it isolates "the Western development of one particular branch of subsculpture") and brackets the non-Western "history of human images" Burnham says "has yet to be written."
