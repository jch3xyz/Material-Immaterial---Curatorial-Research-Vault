# The greater good of the hive is never ours

## Claim

When surveillance capital owns the means of behavioral modification, the "greater good" promised by instrumentarian society arrives already oriented toward the owners' interests, so the question Alex Pentland never asks — "Whose greater good?" — reveals a collective good that is someone's but is never ours.

## Expanded Explanation

The first principle Zuboff distills from Pentland's vision of an instrumentarian society is "behavior for the greater good." Pentland presents the continuous rendering of human behavior as a path to "a world without war or financial crashes," to coordinated collective action governed by "social universals" — a [[Social Physics]] in which a new "collective intelligence" serves everyone. But Zuboff's intervention is to interrogate the grammatical possessive hidden inside that promise. A "greater good" is never an ownerless abstraction; it is always *someone's* good. The decisive question is therefore not whether a good is collective but *whose* collective good is being engineered, and by whom.

Pentland, Zuboff argues, systematically avoids this question. He laments that "privacy concerns" and the absence of consensus over "the trade-offs between personal and social values" are the "main barriers" to his engineered future — treating individual moral and political bearings as friction to be overcome rather than as the very ground on which "whose good?" could be contested. But once surveillance capitalism owns the machines and the [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|means of behavioral modification]], the answer is structurally pre-decided. "Goodness" does not arrive neutral, awaiting democratic determination; it arrives "already oriented" toward the interests of the owners and the clients whose [[Guaranteed Outcomes|guaranteed outcomes]] they sell. The hive's good is computed by, and for, whoever owns the math.

This is where the argument fuses Zuboff's reading of B. F. Skinner with her analysis of contemporary instrumentarian power. Skinner's planned society "keeps intelligence on the right track, for the good of society rather than of the intelligent individual." [[Applied Utopistics|Pentland's applied utopistics]] realize Skinner's behaviorist dream in practice — but with the proprietary twist Zuboff names elsewhere: the knowledge that displaces our freedom is theirs, while the lost freedom is ours. The "greater good of the hive" is thus a euphemism. It launders the [[Radical Indifference|radical indifference]] of surveillance capital — its formal indifference to the *content* and *meaning* of our lives — into the language of universal benefit, so that domination can present itself as care.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Instrumentarian society is sold as "behavior for the greater good": continuous behavioral data yielding coordinated collective action and a frictionless, optimized world (Pentland; echoing Skinner).
2. A "greater good" is never ownerless — it is always someone's good, determined by some agent through some apparatus.
3. The apparatus that determines and imposes this good is the means of behavioral modification, and under surveillance capitalism those means are *owned* by surveillance capital.
4. Pentland never poses "Whose greater good?"; he reframes the question (privacy, contested values) as mere friction obstructing the engineered future.
5. Therefore the good is not determined democratically but arrives "already oriented" toward the owners' and clients' interests — pre-decided by who owns the machines.
6. Conclusion: the greater good of the hive is real — it is *someone's* — but it is not ours; the collective benefit is a euphemism for the proprietary interests that author it.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Pentland's promise of "a world without war or financial crashes" and a "collective intelligence" serving the greater good through "social universals" — [[Social Physics]] as the engine of coordinated collective action (Ch. 15).
- Pentland naming "privacy concerns" and the lack of "consensus around the trade-offs between personal and social values" as the "main barriers," thereby treating moral/political bearings as friction rather than as the site where "whose good?" is decided (Ch. 15).
- Zuboff's direct counter-question — "How is the greater good determined when surveillance capitalism owns the machines and the means of behavioral modification?" — and her answer that "Goodness" arrives "already oriented" toward the owners and clients (Ch. 15).
- The Skinnerian lineage: the "planned society" that serves "the good of society rather than of the intelligent individual," realized in practice as [[Applied Utopistics|applied utopistics]] (Ch. 15).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- A defender of Pentland would reply that *any* collective decision procedure (markets, states, democracies) also produces a "someone's good," so singling out instrumentarian society begs the question of what a legitimate determination would look like.
- Zuboff's force is rhetorical-diagnostic: she shows the question is *suppressed*, not that a perfectly neutral "greater good" is available. The argument indicts the foreclosure of contestation more than it specifies an alternative procedure.
- The claim depends on Zuboff's inference of "the theory behind the practice" from Pentland's writings; Pentland himself frames social physics as descriptive science, not as a normative program of ownership — though Zuboff reads that disavowal as itself part of the foreclosure.
