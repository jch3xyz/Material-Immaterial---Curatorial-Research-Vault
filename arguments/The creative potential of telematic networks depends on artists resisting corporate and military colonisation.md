# The creative potential of telematic networks depends on artists resisting corporate and military colonisation

## Claim

The emancipatory creative potential Roy Ascott ascribes to the network is **conditional, not guaranteed**: it "will only be realised to the extent that artists resist the monopolisation and colonisation of electronic space by multinational business and the military."

## Expanded Explanation

Most of Ascott's telematic writing is utopian — the network dissolves the solitary author into [[Network Consciousness]], turns the spectator into a participator, and makes [[Reciprocal Communication]] the form of art itself. But Ascott himself supplies the dark counter-reading. In "Concerning Nets and Spurs" (1985) he closes a passage celebrating "multiple authorship and ownership of images and forms in the telematic process" with a single sharp caveat: that creative potential "will only be realised to the extent that artists resist the monopolisation and colonisation of electronic space by multinational business and the military" (`Directly stated`). The emancipation is not a property of the medium; it is a stake to be fought for.

The caveat is grounded in a sober factual premise that Ascott states a year earlier in "Art and Telematics" (1984): "At present, most timesharing systems are in the hands of the multinational corporations and the military, in any case" (`Directly stated`). The infrastructure on which [[Telematic Art]] depends is *already* owned by exactly the powers Ascott warns against. Electronic space is not a neutral commons awaiting artists; it is a colonised territory, and the artist's role is therefore adversarial — to occupy and re-purpose the medium before its proprietors close it. This is why Ascott folds the network into an ethical mission rather than treating it as a technical given: the liberatory outcome is a wager won by resistance, not a destiny conferred by the technology.

This conditional framing is the hinge between Ascott's optimism and the vault's surveillance-and-domination cluster. The same networks Ascott hopes artists can hold open are the ones Donna Haraway reads as [[The Informatics of Domination]] (the historic shift from organic hierarchies to coded command-control-communication networks) and the ones Byung-Chul Han later diagnoses as the [[Digital Panopticon]]. Ascott differs from both not in his picture of the threat — corporate and military command of coded space — but in keeping the fork genuinely open: he names colonisation precisely in order to argue it can be resisted.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Telematic networks have real emancipatory potential — multiple authorship, negotiated meaning, distributed creation (the utopian premise of [[Network Consciousness]] and [[Telematic Art]]).
2. But electronic space is not neutral or freely available: "most timesharing systems are in the hands of the multinational corporations and the military."
3. Powerful institutions therefore have both the means and the motive to monopolise and colonise that space, foreclosing its participatory uses.
4. Hence the emancipatory potential is **conditional** — realised "only to the extent that artists resist" this colonisation.
5. Therefore resistance to corporate/military capture is not a side concern but the precondition of telematic art's creative promise.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The explicit conditional caveat from "Concerning Nets and Spurs" (1985): the creative potential "will only be realised to the extent that artists resist the monopolisation and colonisation of electronic space by multinational business and the military." (See Sources.)
- The factual premise from "Art and Telematics" (1984): "most timesharing systems are in the hands of the multinational corporations and the military" — the ownership state of the infrastructure. (See Sources.)
- The wider context in ch. 15: a "growing and close interest in telematics on the part of the controlling institutions in our society," the exponential growth of data banks, and the "telematic imperative" — the network's build-out is driven by commercial, state, and military priorities, not artistic ones.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **A single-paragraph caveat, not the essay's thesis.** In both chapters the dominant register is celebratory; the colonisation warning is a hedge within a broadly utopian argument. Its weight as a standalone claim is interpretive (hence `medium` confidence).
- **The optimism may be naive.** Read against the [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[The Informatics of Domination]], "artists resisting" looks like a thin lever against multinational and military control of the infrastructure — the same networks that promise the embrace are the ones that afford the surveillance, and there is no guarantee resistance can prevail.
- **The threat is already actual, not merely possible.** Because the timesharing systems are *already* corporate/military-owned (ch. 15), the framing arguably understates the asymmetry: artists begin not on neutral ground but inside an already-colonised space.
- **Resistance is left undertheorised.** Ascott names what to resist but offers little account of *how* — beyond building participatory, decentralized forms (see [[The decentralized structure of telematic networks is subversive]]) and trusting [[Decentralization]] itself to be subversive of centralized control.
