# The algorithmic God is the modern God of Calvin and Luther

## Claim

The opaque superhuman deity that technology critics imagine in machine learning is not a regression to the Dark Ages but the specific God of the Reformation: the radically other, voluntarist, inscrutable God of nominalism, Calvin, and Luther — "a thoroughly modern entity." Submission to a black-box algorithm therefore reproduces the position of Job, who is permitted to question a sovereign whose verdicts come without reasons; deep learning delivers "objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension."

## Expanded Explanation

This is the organizing argument of the "Algorithm" chapter of [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]'s *[[God Human Animal Machine]]*, and the historical correction that anchors the concept [[The Algorithm as Providence]]. The chapter opens on Chris Anderson's 2008 call to abandon scientific theory in the age of big data — "Correlation is enough... We can stop looking for models" — and on the writers who read deep learning as a return to premodern enchantment: David Weinberger's "premodern epistemology," James Bridle's "New Dark Age," a regress "to a time when knowledge could be obtained only through revelation, without true understanding." O'Gieblyn accepts the religious diagnosis but rejects the *period*. The doctrine forming around AI, she insists, "is not in fact a holdover from the Dark Ages. The boundless alien deity that technology critics have in mind is the God of Calvin and Luther—a thoroughly modern entity."

The correction matters because the two Gods are opposites. The medieval, Scholastic God of Aquinas was *comprehensible*: theology was "inflected with Platonism," ideas corresponded to "eternal, transcendent realities" perceivable through the *lumen naturalis*, and for Aquinas the Book of Job illustrated "how humanity gradually becomes enlightened to the ultimate truth." It was *nominalism* — the late-medieval doctrine associated with [[William of Ockham]] that "God was radically other than his creation and limited neither by morality nor by rational laws" ("The reason is that He willed it, and no other reason is to be expected") — that eradicated universals and made the world "radically contingent." This doctrine "reached its apotheosis in the work of the Protestant reformers": Calvin's God is "completely other," "incomprehensible" and "remotely hidden from human understanding," who "does whatever he pleases"; Luther's divine justice is "entirely alien to ourselves." Drawing on [[Hans Blumenberg]]'s *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age*, O'Gieblyn argues this voluntarist God is precisely what produced modernity — disenchantment, [[René Descartes|Descartes]], the scientific revolution — so the deity is *modern*, not archaic (see [[The true trauma of disenchantment is the loss of intrinsic meaning]]).

The algorithm reincarnates this God structurally. Black-box deep-learning models build internal representations that "correspond to [no] terms in human language"; printed out, they would be "billions of arithmetic operations—an 'explanation' that would be impossible to understand" (see [[The Black Box]]). So "these algorithms have fulfilled Anderson's call for objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension. To obtain the superior knowledge the machines possess, we must give up our desire to know 'why' and accept their outputs as pure revelation." O'Gieblyn corrects [[Norbert Wiener]], who in *God & Golem, Inc.* cast the learning machine as the *Devil* of the Job wager: "These algorithms are not the sly devil that has outsmarted its creator. They have become instead the absolute sovereign who demands blind submission." The subjects of predictive systems — denied a loan, fired, sentenced — "now find themselves in a position much like Job's," "free to question the algorithm in the same way that Job was welcome to question the justice of Jehovah." The chapter closes on the genealogical irony: "AI began with the desire to forge a god. It is not coincidental that the deity we have created resembles, uncannily, the one who got us into this problem in the first place."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Critics describe machine learning in religious terms — opaque, oracular, beyond appeal — and read it as a *premodern* regress: Weinberger's "premodern epistemology," Bridle's "New Dark Age," knowledge "only through revelation, without true understanding."
2. But the Scholastic, medieval God was *comprehensible*: God and nature were knowable through the light of reason, and Job, for Aquinas, was a parable of enlightenment, not of human limitation.
3. The inscrutable, will-only God arrives later, with *nominalism* ([[William of Ockham]]): God is "radically other," bound by no rational or moral law; "He willed it, and no other reason is to be expected." This makes the world "radically contingent."
4. Nominalism "reached its apotheosis" in Calvin and Luther: a God who is "completely other," "incomprehensible," who "does whatever he pleases," whose justice is "entirely alien to ourselves," and who hides the status of salvation (predestination).
5. Per [[Hans Blumenberg]], this voluntarist God *generated* modernity — disenchantment, Descartes, empiricism, the scientific revolution. The deity is therefore "a thoroughly modern entity," not a Dark-Ages holdover.
6. The black-box algorithm has the same structure: it yields "objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension," issues verdicts with no accessible reasons, and demands that we "give up our desire to know 'why' and accept their outputs as pure revelation."
7. Therefore the algorithmic "god" is specifically the modern nominalist/Reformation God; submission to the algorithm is the position of Job before a sovereign whose decrees come without reasons — Wiener miscast it as the Devil; it is God.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The "human brain is also a black box" symmetry.** The chapter records the technologists' rebuttal that human judgment is no more transparent — "The human brain is also a black box," says Richard Berk; judges' reasoning is "a black box that provides inconsistent error-prone decisions." If true, trusting the algorithm is no worse than trusting a person, blunting the theological alarm. O'Gieblyn answers that the symmetry is "remarkably specious," because humans "evolved as a species to have similarities in our method of reasoning," whereas machines reach conclusions "totally unlike human reason" ("That's not a human move"). The counter is registered, not conceded (see [[The Black Box]]).
- **The analogy may overreach.** The structural identity between a neural network and Calvin's God is an *interpretive* mapping; a skeptic could hold that opacity-plus-authority is a thin resemblance, not a genuine theological lineage. O'Gieblyn's defense is historical (the Blumenberg genealogy), but the claim that we "raised this God from the dead" is a reading, not a demonstrable causal chain — confidence on the deep continuity is `Strongly implied` rather than `Directly stated`.
- **Opacity is often legal, not metaphysical.** Even within the chapter's logic, much algorithmic secrecy is engineered (COMPAS "was protected by proprietary law"), which makes the "inscrutable God" framing partly a mystification of ordinary corporate power — a tension the next chapter develops and that complicates the purely theological reading.
- **Some in the field embrace the alien God as a feature.** Yann LeCun ("the value of smart machines is that they are quite alien from us") and Weinberger ("alien" does not mean "wrong") treat inscrutable intelligence as the *goal*, not a danger. For them the Calvinist comparison is not a critique but a recommendation — the argument's normative sting depends on a humanist premise they reject.

## Tensions

- [[Visibility vs Understanding]] — the argument's core fault line: the algorithm makes the world maximally *predictable* while making it maximally *unintelligible* ("give up our desire to know 'why'"), exactly the gap Calvin demands the believer accept before an incomprehensible God.
- [[Transparency vs Opacity]] — the algorithmic God's authority rests on constitutive opacity; the demand to "open the black box" is the modern echo of Job's demand that God answer for himself, and meets the same refusal.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the chapter's later cases (Loomis, Gates: "How do you stop something that predetermines your fate?") show prediction shading into decree, the predestination problem returned as algorithmic determinism.
