# The spectacle fulfills both materialism and idealism as pseudo-concreteness

## Claim

Against the project of the "Theses on Feuerbach" — the realization of philosophy in a praxis that transcends the opposition between idealism and materialism — the [[Spectacle]] betrays that project by preserving the ideological features of *both*, imposing them together in "the pseudo concreteness of its universe" (thesis 216).

## Expanded Explanation

Thesis 216 is the keystone of Debord's `Material vs Immaterial` position, and it is structured as a direct inversion of a Marxian promise. The young Marx's eleventh thesis on Feuerbach had announced that philosophy would cease to merely *interpret* the world and would instead be *realized* — dissolved into a practical activity that abolished the old philosophical opposition between an idealism that made mind primary and a materialism that made matter primary. Debord grants that this was the project. He then argues that the spectacle is precisely what happens when that project is *not* carried through: instead of transcending the two poles in praxis, the spectacle keeps them both alive as ideology, and stages their reconciliation as a false, "pseudo" concreteness.

The betrayal is symmetrical, and Debord spells out each half. The **contemplative old materialism** — the standpoint that "conceives the world as representation and not as activity," and which in doing so "ultimately idealizes matter" — is "fulfilled in the spectacle, where concrete things are automatic masters of social life" (thesis 216). Here matter does not liberate; it rules. Commodities and concrete things have become "automatic masters," so the materialist's world-as-representation is realized as a regime of [[Reification]] where the object dominates the subject. Conversely, the **"dreamed activity of idealism"** — the fantasy of a pure activity of mind — is "also fulfilled in the spectacle, through the technical mediation of signs and signals — which ultimately materialize an abstract ideal" (thesis 216). The immaterial dream of idealism is not defeated; it is *given a body* through the technical apparatus of media, signs, and signals, so that an "abstract ideal" acquires material force.

The result is a chiasmus that resists resolution into either pole: materialism ends by *idealizing matter*, and idealism ends by *materializing an abstract ideal*. Neither material nor immaterial is transcended, and neither is simply victorious; each is preserved as ideology and folded into the other. Debord names the product of this folding "pseudo concreteness" — a universe that feels solid and given precisely because the genuine opposition that praxis was meant to resolve has been embalmed rather than overcome. This is why the chapter is titled "Ideology Materialized": the spectacle is "the materialization of ideology brought about by the concrete success of an autonomized system of economic production" (thesis 212).

## Reasoning Structure

1. The "Theses on Feuerbach" set the project of realizing philosophy in a praxis that *transcends* the opposition between idealism and materialism (thesis 216, directly stated).
2. The spectacle does not transcend this opposition; instead it "preserves the ideological features of both materialism and idealism" (thesis 216, directly stated).
3. On the materialist side: the old contemplative materialism conceives the world as representation, not activity, and thereby "ultimately idealizes matter"; this standpoint is "fulfilled in the spectacle, where concrete things are automatic masters of social life" (thesis 216, directly stated).
4. On the idealist side: the "dreamed activity of idealism is also fulfilled in the spectacle, through the technical mediation of signs and signals — which ultimately materialize an abstract ideal" (thesis 216, directly stated).
5. Because both poles are preserved and each collapses into the other (matter idealized, ideal materialized), the spectacle imposes them "in the pseudo concreteness of its universe" — a false reconciliation, not the promised transcendence (thesis 216, directly stated).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument presumes the Marxian "Theses on Feuerbach" as its normative baseline; a reader who rejects the premise that philosophy *ought* to be realized in a class praxis will not read the spectacle's preservation of both poles as a "betrayal" so much as a description. Debord's claim is diagnostic and polemical at once (interpretive synthesis).
- "Pseudo-concreteness" is asserted rather than demonstrated within the thesis itself; the force of the term depends on the surrounding argument — especially thesis 215's account of the spectacle as "the material 'expression of the separation and estrangement between man and man'" — for its content (strongly implied).
- The symmetry ("matter idealized" / "ideal materialized") is elegant but compressed: Debord does not fully separate the *technical* mediation of signs and signals (the idealist half) from the *economic* dominance of concrete things (the materialist half), leaving the mechanism by which the two halves interlock to be reconstructed by the reader (interpretive synthesis).

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — this is Debord's keystone contribution to the tension.
