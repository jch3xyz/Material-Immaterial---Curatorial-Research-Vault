# Regulation aimed at privacy or monopoly cannot constrain surveillance capitalism

## Claim

Because surveillance capitalism continuously discovers and secures new supply routes and there is not enough relevant law to stop it, regulation framed through the inherited lenses of *privacy* or of *monopoly* cannot grasp its dangers or constrain its fundamental operations.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff argues that the twentieth-century regulatory categories we instinctively reach for — antitrust against monopoly, privacy protections against intrusion — are aimed at the wrong target. They are valid as far as they go, but they "omit the most-salient elements of the new order." What looks like a monopoly is, under surveillance capitalism, actually a [[Cornering|cornering]] of "user-derived raw-material supplies": practices "defined as monopolistic actually function as means of cornering" the unregulated commodity of behavioral surplus, "not primarily to fix prices." Breaking up the firm therefore leaves [[The Extraction Imperative|the extraction imperative]] untouched. (`Directly stated`)

The deeper reason regulation cannot bite is structural: supply is fungible and the supply *routes* are plural and replaceable. "New supply routes are continuously constructed and tested," and "if one route is blocked, another is found." Zuboff renders this as the river metaphor — "Like a river running to the sea, if one route is blocked, another is found" — so that any single regulatory blockage is routed around rather than stopped. Dispossession activities "are compelled to circumvent every obstacle and will continue to do so, short of a genuine existential threat." (`Directly stated`)

A reform job *inside* such a firm is, by the same logic, futile. The privacy czar's brief — repair the privacy reputation "not to dismantle the extraction imperative and its relentless demand for economies of scale in the supply function" — is, Zuboff writes flatly, "a logical impossibility." And the historical record confirms that the law's silence, not society's consent, is what protected Google: it "did not evade legal consequences because society agreed with its practices, but because there was not enough relevant law to protect citizens from its incursions." The conclusion Zuboff draws for the whole book is that "the dangers of surveillance capitalism cannot be fully grasped through either the lens of privacy or of monopoly" — the real stakes lie in who commands knowledge, authority, and power (`Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?`), the questions the [[Knowledge vs Power]] tension names. (`Directly stated`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The harm of surveillance capitalism is a *market form* — the capture of behavioral surplus and the acquisition of decision rights — not a single firm's price-fixing or a single intrusion on privacy.
2. Monopolistic-looking practices are really [[Cornering|cornering]] of raw-material supply routes; antitrust aimed at competition and prices misses this.
3. Supply routes are plural and replaceable: "if one route is blocked, another is found" ("Like a river running to the sea"), so any localized intervention is circumvented.
4. Reforming the firm from within fails too: a privacy czar tasked with fixing privacy without ending the extraction imperative is "a logical impossibility."
5. Where Google did escape, it escaped for *want of law*, not by consent — "there was not enough relevant law."
6. Therefore neither the privacy lens nor the monopoly lens can grasp the danger, and regulation framed in those terms "is likely to have little effect on the fundamental operations of this market form."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The cornering analysis: monopolistic practices "actually function as means of cornering user-derived raw-material supplies" to protect "the unregulated commodity that is behavioral surplus." (Ch. 5) — see [[Cornering]].
- The continuous-supply-route mechanism and the river metaphor: "New supply routes are continuously constructed and tested… if one route is blocked, another is found." (Ch. 5)
- Android and the EU antitrust case: "governmental opposition to Google's monopolistic activities emphasized traditional competitive harms at the expense of surveillance capitalism's new harms." (Ch. 5)
- The Verizon/FCC settlement: a "$1.35 million settlement" after which "Verizon's burgeoning new supply routes would remain unchallenged" — a public institution "outmatched by the velocity and resources of a determined surveillance capitalist." (Ch. 5)
- The privacy-czar episode: Alma Whitten's job "was a logical impossibility." (Ch. 5)
- The Street View / wiretap outcome: Google "did not evade legal consequences because society agreed with its practices, but because there was not enough relevant law." (Ch. 5)
- The framing conclusion: "the dangers of surveillance capitalism cannot be fully grasped through either the lens of privacy or of monopoly." (Ch. 5 close)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Zuboff does not claim regulation is useless in principle — only that *these* frames (privacy, monopoly) are mismatched to the harm; she leaves open the possibility of new law adequate to the new facts (the chapter ends by promising "a new way of thinking about danger"). (`Strongly implied`)
- The river-metaphor inevitability could be read as overstated: it assumes regulators will always be slower than supply-route innovation, an empirical claim that future, surveillance-specific law (e.g., banning behavioral-surplus markets outright rather than policing competition) might falsify. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- The "want of law" diagnosis is U.S.-and-period-specific; jurisdictions with stronger data-protection regimes complicate the claim that no relevant law exists. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
