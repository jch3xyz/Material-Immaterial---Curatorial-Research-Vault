# Science strengthens myths rather than replacing them with facts

## Claim

Modern science did not replace myths with facts; it changed the rules of the game in a way that lets the [[Intersubjective Reality|intersubjective]] myths dominate more completely than ever, reshaping objective and subjective reality to match our fictions.

## Expanded Explanation

The standard secularization narrative holds that the rise of science was a struggle in which "the light of science dispelled the darkness of religion, and the world became increasingly secular, rational and prosperous." Harari rejects this story. He concedes that science genuinely changed the human game — antibiotics, unlike Sobek's prayers, "cure infections whether you believe in them or not" — but denies that this displaced myth with fact. On the contrary, science *amplifies* myth. "Myths continue to dominate humankind. Science only makes these myths stronger" (`Directly stated`).

The crucial move is that science does not destroy the [[Intersubjective Reality|intersubjective reality]] of gods, nations, money and corporations; it equips that reality to *act on* the objective and subjective worlds. "Instead of destroying the intersubjective reality, science will enable it to control the objective and subjective realities more completely than ever before." As computers and bioengineering blur the line between fiction and reality, "people reshape reality to match their pet fictions" (`Directly stated`). The myth is no longer merely a belief in the collective imagination — it becomes a blueprint that physically remakes the world. This is the same mechanism by which [[Writing Reshapes Reality|writing reshapes reality]], scaled up by twenty-first-century technology.

Harari dramatizes the difference between ancient impotence and modern capability with the priests of Sobek. They "imagined the existence of divine crocodiles, while pharaoh dreamt about immortality," but the sacred crocodile "was a very ordinary swamp reptile dressed in golden fineries," and the mummified pharaoh "was as lifeless as one can get." Where the ancient myth could only dress up reality in gold leaf, modern science can realize it: "twenty-first-century scientists might be able to really engineer super-crocodiles, and to provide the human elite with eternal youth here on earth" (`Directly stated`). The fiction that once needed only to be *believed* can now be *built* — and so "the rise of science will make at least some myths and religions mightier than ever."

## Reasoning Structure

1. The secular thesis says modern systems abandoned intersubjective myths in favour of objective scientific knowledge (the apparent fruit of overcoming famine, plague and war).
2. But science is not merely another myth either: unlike prayers to Sobek, scientific theories work whether or not you believe in them (antibiotics vs. God). So science did change the rules of the game.
3. Yet changing the rules is not the same as replacing myth with fact: myths continue to dominate, and science makes them *stronger*, not weaker.
4. Mechanism: science does not destroy the intersubjective reality but lets it control the objective and subjective realities more completely; computers and bioengineering blur fiction and reality so people reshape reality to match their fictions.
5. Illustration: where Sobek's priests could only dress a swamp reptile in gold and mummify a mortal pharaoh, modern science can really engineer super-crocodiles and grant the elite eternal youth.
6. Conclusion: the rise of science makes at least some myths and religions mightier than ever — the secularization story is false.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Sobek-vs-antibiotics contrast: prayers to Sobek "didn't raise or lower the Nile's water level by a millimetre," whereas "Antibiotics, unlike God, help even those who don't help themselves" — establishing that science really does change the rules (source below).
- The super-crocodile / eternal-youth image: ancient myth could only gild an ordinary reptile and mummify a mortal pharaoh, but science could engineer super-crocodiles and grant "the human elite ... eternal youth here on earth" (source below).
- The historical reframing of modernity as "the process of formulating a deal between science and one particular religion — namely, humanism," in which society "uses science not in order to question these dogmas, but rather in order to implement them" — see [[The Modern Covenant]] and [[Religion as Social Order]].

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Harari himself voices the secular counter-thesis at length ("the light of science dispelled the darkness of religion") before rejecting it; a reader could hold that disease, drought and earthquakes are now *explained* without myth, narrowing what "domination" by myth can mean.
- The claim leans on Harari's expansive definition of religion as "anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures," under which liberalism, communism and Nazism all count as religions. If one rejects that definition, the thesis that "myths continue to dominate" weakens (`Strongly implied`).
- The super-crocodile and eternal-youth examples are forward-looking ("might be able to"), so the strongest form of the claim is partly predictive rather than established fact (`Directly stated` that the prediction is made; the prediction's truth is open).
