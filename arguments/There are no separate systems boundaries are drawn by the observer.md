# There are no separate systems boundaries are drawn by the observer

## Claim

The world is a continuum; there are no separate systems in it, and every boundary drawn around a "system" is invented by the observer for the purpose of a particular discussion — so forgetting that a boundary is artificial produces surprise, and the right boundary rarely coincides with a discipline, a profession, or a political border. *(Directly stated.)*

## Expanded Explanation

Meadows treats boundaries as the systems thinker's most seductive convenience and most reliable source of error. Her governing principle — [[No Separate Systems]] — is that the world is unbroken, so the problem she names in [[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/08 Chapter 4 - Why Systems Surprise Us|Chapter 4]] is not that we draw boundaries but that we forget we drew them. "There is no single, legitimate boundary to draw around a system. We have to invent boundaries for clarity and sanity; and boundaries can produce problems when we forget that we've artificially created them." The invention is unavoidable — the mind cannot hold a continuum without cutting it up — but the cut has no authority in the world. It is a property of the discussion, and of [[The Observer]] conducting it, not of nature. This is why the fault line she keeps returning to is the gap between the map and the territory, between the [[Mental Models|model of the system]] and the system: a boundary is a feature of the model, and the model is always a simplification we have chosen.

From this follows her most compressed statement of the point, which recurs verbatim in the book's summarizing [[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/13 Appendix|Appendix]] of system principles: "There are no separate systems. The world is a continuum. Where to draw a boundary around a system depends on the purpose of the discussion—the questions we want to ask." The boundary is downstream of the question. Change the question and the legitimate boundary changes with it; there is no boundary that is correct in the abstract, only one that is appropriate to a purpose. Meadows makes this concrete with a boundary that seems obvious and then dissolves it: a population's "cradle to grave" limits look like natural edges, yet they become "unserviceable" the moment the problem shifts to in- and out-migration, or to limited cemetery space. The same physical population is bounded differently depending on what you are trying to understand.

The argument has two failure modes, and Meadows insists both are real. Draw the boundary too narrowly and "the system surprises you": urban traffic planned without settlement patterns produces highways that attract housing that puts more cars on the highways; a sewage problem "solved" by throwing waste into a river forces the boundary to expand to "the whole river," and perhaps the soil. A national park managed to its "physical boundary" is crossed by migrating wildlife, nomadic peoples, flowing water, economic development at the edges, acid rain, and now a changing climate — "to manage a park you have to think about a boundary wider than the official perimeter." But the opposite error is just as damaging: systems analysts "often fall into the opposite trap: making boundaries too large," producing "'my model is bigger than your model'" diagrams "connecting everything with everything" whose piles of information "only serve to obscure the answers to the questions at hand." Neither too-narrow nor too-broad is safe; the discipline is to fit the boundary to the question and hold it lightly. And because the appropriate boundary tracks the problem rather than any inherited category, it "rarely coincides with the boundary of an academic discipline, or with a political boundary" — the continuum does not respect the lines the observer's professions and nations have already drawn. Meadows extends the same logic to time in [[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/12 Chapter 7 - Living in a World of Systems|Chapter 7]]: "In a strict systems sense, there is no long-term, short-term distinction. Phenomena at different time-scales are nested within each other." The temporal boundary between "short-term" and "long-term" is as observer-imposed as the spatial one.

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise (the continuum).** The world is not pre-divided into systems; it is continuous. "There are no separate systems. The world is a continuum." *(Directly stated.)*
2. **Inference (the observer draws the line).** Because reality is continuous, any boundary must be supplied by the observer, not discovered: "We have to invent boundaries for clarity and sanity." "There is no single, legitimate boundary to draw around a system." *(Directly stated.)*
3. **The boundary is a function of the question.** Which boundary is appropriate "depends on the purpose of the discussion—the questions we want to ask." Change the purpose and the right boundary changes. *(Directly stated.)*
4. **Failure mode A (too narrow).** "When you draw boundaries too narrowly, the system surprises you" — traffic, sewage, and park examples show relevant structure lying just outside the imposed edge. *(Directly stated.)*
5. **Failure mode B (too broad).** The "opposite trap" of "making boundaries too large" produces analyses that "obscure the answers to the questions at hand." *(Directly stated.)*
6. **Corollary (boundaries betray our categories).** Because the fit is to the problem, the appropriate boundary "rarely coincides" with a discipline or a political border; the same holds in time, where there is "no long-term, short-term distinction." *(Directly stated.)*
7. **Conclusion.** A system boundary is a feature of the observer's [[Mental Models|model]], not of the world; its artificiality must be remembered, or it will generate surprise. *(Strongly implied.)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Anti-relativist caveat.** That boundaries are observer-drawn does not make them arbitrary or all equally good. Meadows is explicit that a boundary can be *wrong for a purpose* — too narrow or too broad — so the claim is about the source of boundaries (the observer), not a license to draw them anywhere.
- **Physical edges still bite.** Some boundaries feel forced by matter (a cell membrane, a watershed). Meadows would answer that even these are chosen relative to a question — a watershed is the right frame for runoff but not for acid rain or greenhouse gases — but a critic could hold that certain physical discontinuities constrain, rather than merely inform, the observer's choice.
- **Practical indeterminacy.** If the correct boundary depends on the question, and questions can always be widened, the doctrine offers no stopping rule on its own; Meadows supplies only the pragmatic counsel to fit the boundary to the problem "at hand" and resist both traps.
