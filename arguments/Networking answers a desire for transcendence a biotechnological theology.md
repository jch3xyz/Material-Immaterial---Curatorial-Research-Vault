# Networking answers a desire for transcendence a biotechnological theology

## Claim

For Roy Ascott, computer networking is not driven by utility but by a "deep psychological desire for transcendence—to reach the immaterial, the spiritual—the wish to be out of body, out of mind, to exceed the limitations of time and space, a kind of biotechnological theology"; the virtual and data spaces it opens occupy "the domain, previously provided by myth and religion." (*Directly stated*.)

## Expanded Explanation

This is Ascott at his most explicitly theological. Having argued that telematics is a [[Telematic networking is a quantum leap in human consciousness|paradigm change in human consciousness]], he names the *motive* underneath the technology: not communication, not productivity, but transcendence. The network is wanted because it promises release from the body, from the located mind, from the constraints of space and time — the perennial wishes of mystical and religious longing, now routed through fiber and silicon. Ascott does not deflate this as mere metaphor; he gives it a name — "biotechnological theology" — that fuses the biological substrate, the technological apparatus, and the divine aspiration into a single drive. The desire is old; the apparatus is new.

In the later "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" Ascott restates the same drive in barer terms — "the product of a profound human desire for transcendence: to be out of body, out of mind, beyond language" — and then makes the decisive functional claim: [[Data Space|virtual space and data space]] "constitute the domain, previously provided by myth and religion." This is a claim about *succession*, not mere analogy. The space of imagination, desire, and will — the space where humans have always "reengage[d] the forces of space, time, and matter in the battle for a new reality" — was historically furnished by myth and religion; telematic [[Virtuality|virtuality]] now furnishes it. The network does not abolish the religious function; it inherits it. What was once cosmology and ritual is now cyberspace and data wear.

This argument is the keystone bridge between Ascott's networked-art program and the vault's [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]] cluster. It makes the immaterial turn of [[Telematic Art|telematic art]] continuous with a spiritual rather than merely an aesthetic motive — the dematerialized artwork as a vehicle for an out-of-body, out-of-mind reaching toward the immaterial. And it locates Ascott squarely on the religious-technology axis the vault tracks through O'Gieblyn: where O'Gieblyn diagnoses transhumanism and the computational soul as *secularized Christian eschatology*, Ascott avows the same fusion from the inside, as a hope rather than a critique. The two readings — celebrant and diagnostician — should be cited together as the two faces of one phenomenon. (*Interpretive synthesis*.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The desire for transcendence — to be out of body, out of mind, beyond the limits of space, time, and language — is a deep, perennial human longing, historically expressed through myth and religion.
2. Computer networking promises exactly this: to "reach the immaterial, the spiritual," to "exceed the limitations of time and space."
3. Therefore networking "responds to" that desire — it is wanted *for* its transcendence-affording power, not merely its communicative utility ("a kind of biotechnological theology").
4. Virtual space and data space, accessed through the network, are the domain in which imagination, desire, and will reengage reality — the same function "previously provided by myth and religion."
5. Therefore telematic virtuality succeeds myth and religion as the medium of transcendence: the network inherits the theological function.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The naming itself — networking as a response to a "deep psychological desire for transcendence … a kind of biotechnological theology" (ch. 18, "Gesamtdatenwerk").
- The restatement of the drive — the telematic process as "the product of a profound human desire for transcendence: to be out of body, out of mind, beyond language" (ch. 20).
- The succession claim — virtual and data space as "the domain, previously provided by myth and religion, where imagination, desire, and will can reengage the forces of space, time, and matter in the battle for a new reality" (ch. 20).
- The phenomenology of immersion that grounds the claim experientially — the headset, data glove, and "data wear" make a "personal connection to cyberspace—socialisation in hyper reality"; the "passage from real to virtual will probably be seamless," interaction with remote others "experienced as 'real'" (ch. 20).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Theology as overreach.** Calling networking a "theology" may dignify an infrastructure of data exchange with a metaphysics it cannot bear; the "transcendence" on offer may be only distributed reach, not spiritual release. The line between [[Data Space|data space]] and a genuinely sacred domain is precisely where the claim is most contestable.
- **The critical inversion.** The same out-of-body, out-of-mind reach the vault reads elsewhere as a *loss* — Hayles's erasure of [[Embodiment|embodiment]], O'Gieblyn's [[Disembodied Information as the Soul|disembodied information as the soul]], Han's [[Hell of the Same]] — so the "transcendence" register should be cited apart from those embodiment-critical readings. To be "out of body" is, for Hayles, not transcendence but a dangerous fantasy of [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment|leaving the body behind]].
- **Diagnosis vs. avowal.** O'Gieblyn argues that the religious furniture of techno-spirituality is unacknowledged inheritance — [[Transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology|secularized eschatology]] mistaking itself for science. Ascott avows the inheritance openly, which is more honest but also less guarded against the consolations religion is accused of offering. (*Interpretive synthesis*.)
- **Pre-commercial vision.** Written before the network became a commercial and surveillance apparatus, the redemptive framing presumes a network whose dominant tendency turned out, for other vault authors (the [[Digital Panopticon]]), to be capture rather than transcendence.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott's transcendence is a reaching *toward* the immaterial pole: "to reach the immaterial, the spiritual," out of the material body. This is the spiritual, theological inflection of the eight-author tension's immaterial side — the immaterial as not merely informational but redemptive.
- [[Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment]] — by making data space the heir to myth and religion, Ascott stages a [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]] *through* technology rather than against it: the network re-sacralizes the world the disenchanted modern lost, locating the marvelous inside the machine.
