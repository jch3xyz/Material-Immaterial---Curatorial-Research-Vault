# Repeatability is the core of the mechanical principle that has dominated the West

## Claim

Repeatability — the precise, indefinite reproduction first achieved by the pictorial print and then generalized by movable type into the mechanizing of any handicraft "by the process of segmenting and fragmenting an integral action" — is the core of the mechanical principle that has dominated the West.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan locates the deep structure of Western mechanization not in any particular machine but in a single formal achievement: the capacity to reproduce something *precisely and indefinitely*. He first names this in the woodcut-and-print chapter, where the print's "supreme quality" is "lost on us, since it has so casual and obvious an existence." That quality is bare repeatability: "It is simply that it is a pictorial statement that can be repeated precisely and indefinitely —at least as long as the printing surface lasts. Repeatability is the core of the mechanical principle that has dominated our world, especially since the Gutenberg technology. The message of the print and of typography is primarily that of repeatability" (Directly stated, Ch. 16). The print is, before it is anything else, a *repeatable statement* — and that formal fact, not its devotional or informational content, is its message. See [[The medium not its content is the message]] and [[The Medium Is the Message]].

The argument's force comes from the move that immediately follows: movable type *generalizes* the pictorial print's repeatability into a universal method of mechanization. "With typography, the principle of movable type introduced the means of mechanizing any handicraft by the process of segmenting and fragmenting an integral action" (Directly stated, Ch. 16). What the print does to an image — fix it as a precisely repeatable unit — typography does to *action* itself: it breaks an integral, continuous handicraft into discrete, uniform, recombinable pieces, the very pieces (movable types) whose rearrangement is the work. The print contributes the pictorial-reproduction facet; typography contributes the segmenting-and-fragmenting facet. Together they are one principle, and that principle is mechanism.

Chapter 18 carries the same thesis into the social order and supplies its historical and political scale. Movable type is not one mechanization among others but the *first* and the model for all the rest: "Printing from movable types was the first mechanization of a complex handicraft, and became the archetype of all subsequent mechanization" (Directly stated, Ch. 18). Because print "presented an image of repeatable precision," it "inspired totally new forms of extending social energies" — and McLuhan generalizes repeatability into the explanatory key for Western dominance itself: the astonishment of Margaret Mead's Pacific islanders at "the identical character of several books" is a response "to what is after all the most magical and potent aspect of print and mass production. It involves a principle of extension by homogenization that is the key to understanding Western power" (Directly stated, Ch. 18). The same uniformity and repeatability that made the printed book "the first teaching machine" and "the first mass-produced commodity" became, by extension, "the basis of calculus and of marketing, as of industrial production, entertainment, and science." The political product of this homogenizing repeatability — nationalism, the open society "open by virtue of a uniform typographic educational processing that permits indefinite expansion of any group by additive means" — is developed in [[Print as the Architect of Nationalism]]; here the point is the underlying mechanical principle of which nationalism is one consequence. See also [[Typographic Man]] for the human type this principle produces.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The print's supreme, overlooked quality is that "it is a pictorial statement that can be repeated precisely and indefinitely" (premise, Ch. 16).
2. This repeatability — not the print's content — *is* "the core of the mechanical principle that has dominated our world, especially since the Gutenberg technology" (the thesis, Ch. 16).
3. Movable type generalizes the print's repeatability beyond images into a method: it provides "the means of mechanizing any handicraft by the process of segmenting and fragmenting an integral action" (generalization, Ch. 16).
4. Therefore movable-type printing "was the first mechanization of a complex handicraft, and became the archetype of all subsequent mechanization" (Ch. 18).
5. Because print "presented an image of repeatable precision," repeatability + homogenization scales up into "the key to understanding Western power" — the basis of mass production, marketing, calculus, science, and nationalism (Ch. 18).
6. Conclusion: one principle — precise, indefinite, segmenting repeatability — runs from the woodcut through movable type through the whole mechanical West.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The thesis is a sweeping single-cause history: McLuhan derives mechanism, mass markets, nationalism, calculus, and "Western power" from one formal property (repeatability), asserting the lineage more than demonstrating each link. The "archetype of all subsequent mechanization" claim is a strong inductive generalization from the print case.
- The two facets are unified by McLuhan's own framing rather than argued as identical: the pictorial print's *image*-repeatability (Ch. 16) and movable type's *action*-segmentation (Ch. 16 and Ch. 18) are presented as one principle, but a critic could treat reproduction and fragmentation as distinct operations that print merely happens to combine.
- The same chapter contains a countervailing thread McLuhan does not reconcile here: the low-definition woodcut, "like the modern comic strip and comic book," *resists* perfect precision and "compelled" the viewer "to participate in completing and interpreting the few hints provided" — repeatability and "low definition" pull against one another in the same print medium (Ch. 16; cf. [[Hot vs Cool Media]]).
- It proves the form-over-content reading: if repeatability is "the message of the print and of typography," the doctrinal or informational *content* of what is printed drops out of the account of historical effect — the limitation already flagged for [[The medium not its content is the message]].
