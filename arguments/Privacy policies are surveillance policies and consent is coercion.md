# Privacy policies are surveillance policies and consent is coercion

## Claim

What the industry calls a "privacy policy" is more accurately a surveillance policy — a document that authorizes, conceals, and normalizes the extraction of personal experience as data — and the "consent" it secures is coercion, extracted through dense, unread contracts whose only alternative is a degraded or disabled product.

## Expanded Explanation

Shoshana Zuboff argues that the instruments meant to protect the person against extraction have been inverted into the legal machinery of extraction itself. The "privacy policy" presents itself as a safeguard, but empirically it does the opposite work: it discloses (when it discloses anything at all) that sensitive information is shared with unnamed personnel, other devices, and third parties for trade in behavioral futures markets. The protective name is therefore a misnomer that should be discarded. Zuboff's renaming — *surveillance policies* — is not rhetorical flourish but a corrective that makes the document's true function legible.

The second, inseparable move targets consent. The standard defense of [[Surveillance]] capitalism is that users *agree* — they opt in. Zuboff dismantles this on two fronts. First, the contracts are not read: a study of 543 participants familiar with privacy law found 74 percent took the "quick join" path and the median reading time among those who did look was fourteen seconds, against the forty-five minutes the documents required for comprehension. Second, refusal is penalized. In what Zuboff calls Requirimiento-style relationships — after the absurd Spanish legal proclamation read to indigenous peoples to legitimate conquest — the smart-product message is "Bend the knee or we degrade your purchase." Opt-in/opt-out is meaningless when the only alternative to consent is a crippled device.

Together these moves show that the legal form of consent launders coercion. The diabetes-app study is the keystone evidence: privacy policies, where they existed at all, did not actually protect privacy, and apps with and without them shared sensitive health data at nearly the same rates. This is why Zuboff treats the corporate privacy violation not as an aberration but as the designed operation of the model — directly connecting this argument to [[The pattern of corporate privacy violations is a feature not a bug]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. Privacy policies are presented as protections of the person against data extraction.
2. Empirically, they instead authorize and disclose (incompletely) the sharing of sensitive data with third parties and behavioral futures markets; apps with privacy policies shared data at roughly the same rate as those without.
3. Therefore the protective name is false; the documents are more aptly called *surveillance policies*.
4. The defense of extraction rests on consent ("the user agreed"), but the contracts are effectively unread (median fourteen seconds; 74 percent skip them entirely).
5. Refusing consent triggers degraded or disabled product functionality ("Bend the knee or we degrade your purchase").
6. Consent obtained under a renaming that hides the function, that no one reads, and that punishes refusal is coercion, not agreement.
7. Therefore privacy policies are surveillance policies and consent is coercion.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The diabetes-app study: a 2016 Journal of the American Medical Association report examined 211 Android diabetes apps and sampled 65 for close analysis; 81 percent had no privacy policy, and among those that did "not all of the provisions actually protected privacy"; 76 percent of apps without policies and 79 percent of apps with policies shared sensitive data — only about half of the latter disclosing it ([[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/10 Chapter 8 - Rendition - From Experience to Data]]). `Directly stated`
- Merely downloading the diabetes software "authorized collection and modification of sensitive information"; backstage actions included modifying/deleting data (64 percent), reading phone status and identity (31 percent), gathering location (27 percent), and (4–6 percent) activating the microphone to record speech. `Directly stated`
- The 543-participant reading study: 74 percent chose "quick join," bypassing the terms; the documents required at least forty-five minutes for adequate comprehension, but the median time spent by those who looked was fourteen seconds. `Directly stated`
- The Nest thermostat terms-of-service and EULA as a Requirimiento-style contract sharing sensitive information with other devices, unnamed personnel, and third parties for trading in behavioral futures markets; courts generally upheld no-liability claims absent demonstrated economic harm. `Directly stated`

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The industry's own counter is the consent defense itself — that users opt in freely; Zuboff's argument is precisely that this "agreement" is manufactured by unread, coercive contracts, so the rebuttal is internal to the argument.
- The empirical claims rest on specific studies (diabetes apps in 2016; one 543-participant reading study); generalization to all data products is `Strongly implied` rather than exhaustively demonstrated.
- Legally, courts have *upheld* the companies' position absent demonstrated economic harm, so the "coercion" claim is moral and structural rather than (yet) a settled legal finding.
