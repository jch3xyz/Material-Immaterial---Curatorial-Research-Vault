# Technology is a mirror that returns us to ourselves in a different disguise

## Claim

The harder we try to eliminate ourselves and our interests from our instruments, the more those instruments deliver our own bias and prejudice back to us in a different disguise—an inverted [[Imago Dei]] in which, reaching for the non-human, we meet only ourselves.

## Expanded Explanation

O'Gieblyn builds this argument on a paradox that Arendt, after Heisenberg, identifies at the heart of the modern scientific project. We construct instruments meant to be perfectly objective—to let us climb to the Archimedean point and see the world from no one's vantage at all. But the instruments are made in our image and built in a particular historical context, so they keep returning us to ourselves. "Whenever man attempts to transcend his own point of view," Heisenberg argued, he "confronts himself alone." The reach for the wholly other collapses into a mirror. This is the inversion of the [[Imago Dei]]: instead of finding that we are made in the image of something greater, we discover that everything we build is made in the image of us, faults and all.

The argument's force is that this is not a contingent flaw to be engineered away but a structural condition. The very gesture of self-elimination produces the self in a new costume. "The more we try to rid the world of our image, the more we end up coloring it with human faults and fantasies," O'Gieblyn writes; "the more we insist on removing ourselves and our interests from the equation, the more we end up with omnipotent systems that are rife with human bias and prejudice." Arendt's term, borrowed from Kafka and Heisenberg, is exact: the modern technological subject is unlikely "ever to meet anything but himself and man-made things," and the world fills up with what is, "in the last analysis, he himself in a different disguise."

Applied to machine learning, the abstraction becomes literal. Because these systems are trained on historical data—using the past to predict the future—they inherit the biases that have "long colored our social and political life." They do not target race or gender; they "zero in on other information—zip codes, income, previous encounters with police—that are freighted with historic inequality." So the "alien" superintelligence turns out to be "prone to errors that are all too human": Tay, the chatbot that learned to deny the Holocaust within sixteen hours; the COMPAS sentencing tool that assigned higher recidivism scores to Black defendants; the neural net that "knew us better than we knew ourselves" by predicting Trump's victory on the basis of the relevance of gender. The mirror is two-sided in a sense O'Gieblyn turns against its boosters: the reflection these machines return is "deeply unflattering," and it is ours. This connects to [[Algorithmic Determinism]] and [[The Algorithm as Providence]]—the bias is laundered into the authority of objectivity, and the feedback loop makes it harder to escape.

## Reasoning Structure

1. We build instruments to reach the Archimedean point—to see the world objectively, eliminating our anthropocentric vantage (Arendt, *The Human Condition* and "The Conquest of Space and the Stature of Man").
2. But the instruments are made in our image and in a historical context, so per Heisenberg, transcending one's point of view means confronting oneself alone.
3. Therefore the gesture of self-elimination is self-defeating: the more anthropocentric considerations we try to remove, the less likely we are to meet anything but ourselves "in a different disguise."
4. Machine learning makes this literal: trained on the past, it encodes past prejudice; the "objective" output is our bias returned with the authority of impersonal data.
5. Evidence (Tay, COMPAS, the predicted election) shows that the more we insist on neutrality, the more we get "omnipotent systems that are rife with human bias and prejudice."
6. Conclusion: technology is a mirror—an inverted imago dei. To blame the algorithm is to refuse to read the reflection. Kafka's epigram seals it: man "found the Archimedean point, but he used it against himself."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The "better algorithm" rejoinder.** The obvious response is to fix the bias—use more representative data sets, make the models less error-prone. O'Gieblyn (via Jackie Wang and Hamid Khan) argues this often backfires: better models require *more* data, which "justifies dragnet surveillance and the expansion of policing." There may be no unbiased algorithm for policing "even mathematically."
- **The mirror is not a perfect mirror.** Bias can also be amplified or distorted, not merely reflected; the feedback loop (predictive policing finding crime where its own data sent officers) means the instrument partly *manufactures* the reality it claims to find, blurring "mirror" into "engine."
- **Scope.** The paradox is strongest for systems trained on the human past. It does not obviously extend to every instrument, and Arendt herself notes the unsettling counter-case (Ch10): machines that "do what a human brain cannot comprehend," suggesting the tool sometimes exceeds rather than merely reflects us.
- **Determinism caveat.** O'Gieblyn cautions in Ch12 that the analytics "were precise only at a very large scale"—the mirror operates statistically, not over the individual, so the reflection is of a population, not a person.
