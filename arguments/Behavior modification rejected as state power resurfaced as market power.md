# Behavior modification rejected as state power resurfaced as market power

## Claim

The very means of behavioral modification that 1970s US society investigated, condemned, and drove from civilian life as an illegitimate extension of *state* power — yielding the Common Rule, institutional review boards, and the Belmont Report — did not die but resurfaced "as a creature of the market," now turned on "us" rather than "them" and freed of the constitutional constraints that democracy had imposed on government.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff reconstructs a forgotten history. In 1971 the Senate Subcommittee on Constitutional Rights, led by Sam Ervin, opened a multiyear investigation into "a variety of programs designed to predict, control, and modify human behavior," subjecting behavior modification "to intense constitutional scrutiny for the first time, questioning and ultimately rejecting the use of behavioral modification as an extension of state power." The CIA's MKUltra revelations exposed behavior modification as something its own practitioners regarded as "professionally unethical" and frequently illegal; B. F. Skinner's *Beyond Freedom & Dignity* (1971), which openly prescribed a population-scale "technology of behavior," became a bestseller even as the public "just as surely rejected" its argument against individual freedom. The political and cultural backlash had institutional teeth: the National Research Act (1974) created institutional review boards and laid the foundation for the Common Rule, and the resulting commission's "Belmont Report" became the professional standard for the ethical treatment of human subjects in all federally funded research. (This historical record is **Directly stated** throughout the chapter.)

The argument's pivot is interpretive. Zuboff observes that during the same years US democratic forces were resisting behavior modification *as state power*, "the energies of capitalist counterinsurgency were already at work within society," producing the corporation that "was to enjoy the rights of personhood but be free of democratic obligations, legal constraints, moral calculations, and social considerations." The project therefore "resurface[d] in a wholly unexpected incarnation as a creature of the market," and "in its latest incarnation" comes to life as "a global digital market architecture unfettered by geography, independent of constitutional constraints." This state-to-market migration is Zuboff's synthesis: the constitutional machinery built to bind government simply does not reach a private actor wielding the same means. (The parallel is **Interpretive synthesis**.)

The third move sharpens the stakes. The mid-twentieth-century means "were aimed at individuals and groups who were construed as 'them': military enemies, prisoners, and other captives of walled disciplinary regimes." Today's means "are aimed unabashedly at 'us'" — "everyone is swept up in this new market dragnet." What was once an exceptional instrument of coercion against marginal subjects has become an ambient condition of ordinary life, and the protections won against the state offer no defense, because "even the most stringent application of the 'Common Rule' would be unlikely to change these facts." (The them→us contrast is **Directly stated**; its bearing on the obsolescence of the Common Rule is **Strongly implied**.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. In the 1970s, US society mobilized — through the Ervin subcommittee, the MKUltra exposures, and the popular rejection of Skinner — to investigate and reject behavior modification as an illegitimate extension of state power. *(Directly stated.)*
2. That mobilization produced durable institutional safeguards: institutional review boards, the Common Rule, and the Belmont Report, all binding on government-funded research. *(Directly stated.)*
3. Simultaneously, a "capitalist counterinsurgency" was constructing a corporate form that enjoyed the rights of personhood but was free of democratic, legal, and moral constraints. *(Directly stated, attributed.)*
4. The same means therefore resurfaced "as a creature of the market" — a global digital market architecture "independent of constitutional constraints." *(Directly stated for the resurfacing; the structural state→market parallel is Interpretive synthesis.)*
5. Whereas the old means targeted "them" (enemies, prisoners), the new means target "us" (everyone), so the safeguards designed against the state cannot constrain a private actor. *(them/us Directly stated; the inadequacy of the safeguards Strongly implied.)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The historical facts are firmly documented, but the *equivalence* between Cold-War state behavior modification and present-day market behavioral modification is Zuboff's interpretive bridge; a skeptic could argue the digital "tuning," "herding," and "conditioning" of [[Economies of Action]] differ in kind, not merely in sponsor, from MKUltra or prison behavior-mod regimes.
- The chapter itself notes (via legal scholar James Grimmelmann) that even the most rigorous extension of the Common Rule "would do little to curb the immense power" of a firm like Facebook — so the historical safeguards are offered less as a usable remedy than as evidence of a forgotten democratic will.
- The "creature of the market" framing risks downplaying continued state-corporate entanglement; Zuboff elsewhere argues the state still leans on private firms (see Related Arguments), which complicates a clean state→market succession.
