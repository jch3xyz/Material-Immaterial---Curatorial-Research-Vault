# The achievement-subject is master and slave at once so there is no end of history

## Claim

The auto-exploiting achievement-subject is at once the Hegelian slave who clings to [[Bare Life]] and the master who exploits him — an "enslaved master" — and because the master/slave dialectic is the history of freedom and we remain unfree, there can be no Kojèvian end of history.

## Expanded Explanation

Han rereads Hegel's dialectic of master and slave through the figure of today's [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]]. In the original dialectic the master is the one who does not fear death and so raises himself above concern for [[Bare Life]]; the future slave, preferring servitude to the threat of dying, "clings to bare life" and becomes "a vassal who labors." The decisive faculty is not physical superiority but the "ability to die" — a [[Negative Potency|capacity for death]] the slave lacks. Because the slave holds fast to life and labors, he is "incapable of erotic experience," incapable of the excess and transgression that Han calls [[Eros]].

The novelty of the present, Han argues, is that the achievement-subject is equal to the Hegelian slave "in all respects except for the fact that he does not work for the master, but rather exploits himself of his own volition." As an entrepreneur of the self he collapses the two positions of the dialectic into one: he is "master and slave at once." This is the "fateful unity that Hegel did not consider" — [[Auto-Exploitation|self-exploitation]] is exactly as unfree as [[Allo-Exploitation|allo-exploitation]], only now the coercion is self-administered, which is why it feels like freedom (see [[Freedom itself is producing coercion]]).

From this folded figure Han draws his verdict on Kojève's thesis of the end of history. If the master/slave dialectic is understood as the history of freedom, then the persistence of unfreedom means the dialectic is unresolved and history is not over. We are not the free subjects who "will achieve reality at the end of history"; we are "enslaved masters or slaves who think themselves masters." History would be over only if we were "neither master nor slave" — genuinely free. The "enslaved master" is therefore Han's counter-image to the post-historical Last Man, and it doubles Nietzsche's "last human beings" who have made health "the great goddess" and blink, "We invented happiness."

## Reasoning Structure

1. In Hegel's dialectic the master transcends [[Bare Life]] through the ability to die, while the slave clings to bare life and labors, lacking the [[Negative Potency|capacity for death]].
2. Today's achievement-subject matches the slave exactly — holds fast to life, labors, absolutizes health and survival — except that he is not compelled by an external master.
3. Instead he exploits himself of his own volition ([[Auto-Exploitation]]), so master and slave are folded into one figure: the "enslaved master."
4. [[Auto-Exploitation|Self-exploitation]] is no less unfree than [[Allo-Exploitation|allo-exploitation]] — the entrepreneur of the self is still a slave, only to himself.
5. If the master/slave dialectic is the history of freedom, and we are still unfree, then the dialectic is not resolved.
6. Therefore there is no end of history in Kojève's sense: history would be over only if we were "neither master nor slave," and we are both at once.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Hegel's dialectic of master and slave, read as a "battle for life and death" in which the master fears not death and the slave "clings to bare life" — [[Byung-Chul Han and Hegel]], `raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/04 Bare Life.md`.
- Nietzsche's "last human beings," who make health the "great goddess" and say "We invented happiness" while they blink — the therapeutic hallowing of [[Bare Life]] that marks the modern slave.
- Aristotle's *Politics* on those who accumulate "without limit" because "they are preoccupied with living, not with living well" — [[Capital as Religion|capitalism]] as the absolutization of bare life against death.
- The structural contrast with [[Eros]] as "excess and transgression," which "denies both work and bare life" and requires the surrender of the self to [[The Other]].

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Kojève's own "end of history" was a claim about the universal recognition achieved in the modern state, not about literal freedom from labor; Han reinterprets the thesis through the lens of self-exploitation rather than refuting Kojève on his own terms — his is a diagnostic redeployment of the dialectic.
- The claim depends on Han's own idiosyncratic reading of Hegel as the thinker "receptive to the Other like no other," against the Derrida/Deleuze/Bataille reading of the Hegelian "Absolute" as violence and totality; readers who reject that reading may not follow the move from unfreedom to "no end of history."
- Treating the achievement-subject as *identical* to the Hegelian slave "in all respects" but self-exploiting risks flattening real differences between coerced and voluntary labor; the folding of master and slave into one figure is asserted more than argued.
