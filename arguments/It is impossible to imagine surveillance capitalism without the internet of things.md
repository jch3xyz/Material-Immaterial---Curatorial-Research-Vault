# It is impossible to imagine surveillance capitalism without the internet of things

## Claim

Because high-quality prediction products require behavioral surplus that is vast, varied, and shaped at its source, the pervasive real-world apparatus — the everywhere, always-on instrumentation glossed as the "internet of things" — is not an optional technological accessory but a structural necessity of surveillance capitalism's business model, which is therefore structurally compelled to migrate out of the online world and into physical life.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff frames the "internet of things" as the **material expression of the prediction imperative**. The argument is deliberately asymmetrical: one can imagine ubiquitous or ambient computing arising for other ends, but one cannot imagine surveillance capitalism existing *without* something like it. The apparatus is not a contingent product line that happened to attract surveillance interests; it is the necessary infrastructure that the [[The Prediction Imperative|prediction imperative]] demands once prediction products must approximate observation. (`Directly stated`)

The necessity follows from the economics of prediction. To improve, prediction quality must approach the certainty of actual observation, and this drives competition along three fronts: scale (volume of surplus), **scope** (variety — extraction reaching from the virtual world into "real life on the roads, among the trees, throughout the cities," and downward into the intimate self), and **action** (intervention at the source — machine processes that "nudge, tune, herd, manipulate, and modify behavior"). Scope demands surplus drawn from the whole physical world; action demands a real-world architecture that can not only *know* but *do*. Both vectors can only be satisfied by a pervasive, sensate, actuating material presence in the world. (`Directly stated`)

Zuboff names the consequence: the apparatus combines an extraction architecture with a new **execution architecture**, and as these "begin to function as a coherent whole, they produce a twenty-first-century 'means of behavioral modification.'" The migration into the physical world is thus not opportunism but compulsion — the business model has no other way to manufacture the certainty its markets require. The convergence of the "early ideals of ubiquitous computing" with "the economic imperatives of surveillance capitalism" "signals the metamorphosis of the digital infrastructure from a thing that we have to a thing that has us." (`Directly stated`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Prediction products must approximate the certainty of observation to remain competitive in [[Behavioral Futures Markets|behavioral futures markets]].
2. Approximating observation requires surplus that is not only vast (scale) but **varied** ([[Economies of Scope|economies of scope]]) and **shaped at its source** ([[Economies of Action|economies of action]]).
3. Scope can only be sourced by extending extraction from the online world into physical life; action can only be performed by machine architectures planted in and permeating the real world.
4. Therefore surveillance capitalism requires a pervasive real-world "knowing and doing" presence — an apparatus combining extraction with execution.
5. That apparatus is precisely what is glossed as the "internet of things"; hence surveillance capitalism is unimaginable without it, even though the "internet of things" is imaginable without surveillance capitalism.
6. Conclusion: the apparatus is a structural necessity of the business model, and surveillance capitalism is structurally compelled to migrate from the online world into the physical world.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Inevitability rhetoric vs. economic compulsion.** Industry voices present ubiquitous computing as technologically "inevitable." Zuboff's interviews undercut this, treating inevitability talk as "a Trojan horse for powerful economic imperatives" — but this means the *driver* is a contingent business model, not a law of technology, so a different economic order could in principle build the same hardware to other ends. The argument is about surveillance capitalism's dependence on the apparatus, not the apparatus's dependence on surveillance capitalism. (`Directly stated`)
- **Scope and reach of the empirical base.** Magnitude is uncertain — the domain is "plagued by hyperbole, where projections frequently outrun actual results"; Zuboff concedes "no one knows what the real magnitude is or will be," so the apparatus is described partly as program and ambition, not only as accomplished fact.
