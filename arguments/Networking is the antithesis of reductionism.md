# Networking is the antithesis of reductionism

## Claim

A connective, multidimensional network of ideas cannot be closed without betraying its own form, so inconclusiveness is not a failure of art theory but the constitutive essence of the postmodernist condition.

## Expanded Explanation

Roy Ascott closes his 1980 essay "Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art" by turning the form of the essay itself into its final argument. Having proposed a [[A Field Theory of Art|field theory of art]] that "takes as its focus not form but behaviour," he refuses to tie the essay up. "I have not sought to tie up loose ends," he writes, "so much as to lay out the threads for a multidimensional network of ideas." The methodological commitment follows directly: "Networking is the antithesis of reductionism, and to be inconclusive in this sense is the essence of the postmodernist condition." To reduce — to close a system down to a single resolving thesis — is to deny the very connectivity that defines the network. Inconclusiveness is therefore structural, not a shortcoming.

This is the procedural counterpart to Ascott's substantive thesis that the artwork is not an object delivering a fixed message but a [[Object and System|system]] — "a field of psychic probability, highly entropic" — completed by the [[The Participator|participator]]. If meaning is generated in the open interaction of artist and observer rather than encoded once and decoded, then any theory of that art must remain as open as its object. The claim thus binds Ascott's account of the artwork, of the viewer, and of theory into one connective figure: reductionism closes; networking holds the threads open.

The same logic governs Ascott's account of criticism. Because "Art is an open system," criticism cannot deliver a verdict on a finished thing; "A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms" (ch. 11, "Connective Criticism," 1977). And in ch. 12 ("Network as Artwork," 1978) the network paradigm is named as the destination of art's emerging participatory mode — "a connective, network paradigm for art and art theory, calling for a connective criticism." The 1980 statement is the sharpest formulation of a position Ascott was building across these short programmatic texts: the network is anti-reductive by definition, and openness is the price and the proof of connectivity.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Postmodernist art is connective — "connective, inclusive, transactional, associative, referential, interactive" — and so "presents a connective paradigm." (ch. 13)
2. A connective paradigm is a multidimensional network of interlinked ideas whose value lies in the relations it holds open, not in a single resolving point.
3. Reductionism, by definition, closes such a network down to fewer terms and a fixed conclusion — it severs the very threads connectivity depends on.
4. Therefore networking and reductionism are opposites: to network is to refuse reduction; to reduce is to dissolve the network.
5. A theory (or critique) adequate to a connective art must itself remain connective, hence inconclusive; to "tie up loose ends" would betray the form it describes.
6. Conclusion: inconclusiveness is not a defect of postmodernist art theory but constitutive of the postmodernist condition.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The essay's self-description as method-as-form: laying out "the threads for a multidimensional network of ideas" rather than tying up loose ends. (ch. 13) — `Directly stated`
- The definition of postmodernist art by what formalist modernism "was not" (connective, transactional, associative, etc.), yielding "a connective paradigm, which in turn demands a connective criticism." (ch. 13) — `Directly stated`
- The corroborating principle from criticism: art is "an open system," so "A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms," because "there are no limits to the experience and ideas an artwork can generate for the observer when he can participate in its evolution." (ch. 11) — `Directly stated`
- The destination-claim from the 1978 abstract: the emerging participatory mode of art leads "to a connective, network paradigm for art and art theory, calling for a connective criticism." (ch. 12) — `Directly stated`

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- A connective network and a reductive analysis are not always strictly opposed: a reductive model can be one node within a larger network of ideas, used instrumentally without "closing" the whole. Ascott treats the opposition as categorical; one might hold it to be a difference of emphasis.
- "Inconclusiveness" risks becoming a license against rigor — a defense of vagueness as if it were principled openness. Ascott guards against this by tying inconclusiveness to a specific structural claim (the artwork-as-system completed by the participator), but the slogan can be invoked without that grounding.
- The argument is asserted programmatically and performed by the essay's own open form rather than demonstrated; its force is partly rhetorical. The 1977 and 1978 texts are brief, informal statements (a conference statement and an abstract), so the corroboration is consistent rather than independently developed.

## Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — reductionism is the closure proper to the bounded object; networking is the openness proper to the system. The argument is the epistemic/methodological face of Ascott's object→system move.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the connective network is an immaterial relation of ideas and behaviours rather than a closed material thing; anti-reductive openness aligns with Ascott's "transition from materialist culture ... towards the spiritual in art."
