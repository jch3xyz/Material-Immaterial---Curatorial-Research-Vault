# The shift from observer to participator signals a paradigm shift across art and science

## Claim

Ascott argues that the art-historical shift from observer to [[The Participator|participator]] parallels the identical shift in quantum physics (Wheeler's participatory universe, with "data" substituted for "quanta") and in second-order [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] (from analyzing observed systems to the cybernetics of observing systems) — and that the web of connections binding these moves across art and science is "so pervasive as to suggest a paradigm shift in our worldview."

## Expanded Explanation

In chapter 20 Ascott reproduces John Wheeler's formulation that the act of measurement forces us to "cross out that old word 'observer' and put in its place 'participator'" — that "in some strange sense the universe is a participatory universe." Ascott then performs a single substitution: replace "quanta" with "data." In a telematic network the user who logs on does not receive a finished reality but, by intervention and choice, transforms the data flow and constructs image, sound, and meaning. The structural move is the same as Wheeler's: the apparatus and the act of observation help bring the observed into being. Thus the demotion of the detached spectator to an active [[The Participator|participator]] is not a local fact about interactive art but an instance of a pattern that recurs across physics, biology (von Uexküll's species-specific Umwelt), and linguistics (Whorf).

The same shift recurs in the genealogy of cybernetics. Ascott names it precisely: "from the analysis of observed systems to the (second-order) cybernetics of observing systems." First-order cybernetics studies a system from outside; second-order cybernetics folds the observer back into the system being described. This is the cybernetic form of the observer-to-participator move, and Ascott files it under what he calls "the canon of the immaterial and participatory" — coupling the participatory turn directly to the dematerialization of the art object into the interface, the data space, the network.

Because the same redescription appears independently in quantum theory, in cybernetics, and in the practice of telematic art, Ascott reads it as more than a "pleasant game" of analogy. The pervasiveness of the correspondence — over "a wide domain" — is itself the evidence: a worldwide convergence on chance, indeterminacy, transformation, and "the immaterial and the numinous" amounts to "a paradigm shift in our worldview, a redescription of reality and a recontextualization of ourselves." This grounds his broader program ([[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]]) in a claim about the structure of knowledge across the disciplines, not merely about an artistic style.

## Reasoning Structure

1. In quantum physics, the act of measurement forces the replacement of "observer" with "participator" (Wheeler's participatory universe). *(Directly stated, via Ascott's reproduction of Wheeler)*
2. Substituting "data" for "quanta," the telematic user likewise becomes a participator who transforms the data flow rather than passively receiving it. *(Directly stated)*
3. The genealogy of cybernetics shows the same move: from analyzing observed systems to the second-order cybernetics of observing systems. *(Directly stated)*
4. The same observer-to-participator redescription thus appears independently across art, physics, and cybernetics, "over a wide domain."
5. The pervasiveness of this convergence is so great that it indicates not a set of loose analogies but a paradigm shift in the worldview itself. *(Directly stated)*

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Wheeler's account of quantum measurement — crossing out "observer" and substituting "participator"; "the universe is a participatory universe" (Wheeler and Zurek 1983), reproduced verbatim by Ascott in ch. 20.
- The parallel-universes / observer-creates-reality account drawn from Fred Alan Wolf on the work of Wheeler, Hugh Everett III, and Bryce DeWitt — "it is the observer who creates the reality of the act by observation and choice of measuring apparatus or viewing frame" (ch. 19).
- Cross-disciplinary corroboration: Whorf's linguistic relativity and von Uexküll's species-specific Umwelt, cited as further cases of observer-relative worlds (ch. 19).
- The cybernetics genealogy: "from the analysis of observed systems to the (second-order) cybernetics of observing systems" (ch. 20) — see [[Cybernetics]].
- The naming of telematic content-creation as participatory: "content is created rather than received" at the interface (ch. 20).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Wheeler himself ("in some strange sense") hedges the participatory reading of measurement; the standard collapse-vs-decoherence debate over the [[The Measurement Problem|measurement problem]] is unsettled, so the physics premise Ascott leans on is itself contested rather than established fact.
- The argument rests on an analogy ("substitute 'data' for 'quanta'") that Ascott concedes could be dismissed as "a pleasant game"; the leap from a structural resemblance to a shared "paradigm shift" is an inference about worldview, not a demonstrated causal or logical identity.
- "Paradigm shift" is invoked in a looser, cross-disciplinary sense than Kuhn's intra-disciplinary usage; whether independent shifts in separate fields constitute a single paradigm shift "in our worldview" is interpretive.
