# Telematic networks can create a consciousness greater than the sum of its parts

## Claim

Edward A. Shanken's central thesis about Roy Ascott: the potential exchange of information among all nodes of a [[Telematic Art|telematic]] network yields an emergent, decentralized yet collective state of mind — a [[Network Consciousness|networked consciousness]] "greater than the sum of its parts" — and this conceptual idea is not a side-effect of the technology but an integral part of the artwork itself.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the keystone claim of the whole Ascott volume, stated by its editor as the thread running through every essay. Shanken's formulation has two moves. The first is an emergence claim: when a network permits "the potential exchange of information among all nodes," what emerges is not faster transmission but a new register of mind — "a decentralized yet collective state of mind," "the emergence of a collective consciousness." The whole exceeds its parts because awareness is the emergent property of the distributed exchange, not a possession of any one node. The second move is the aesthetic claim that makes this an *argument about art* rather than about telecommunications: the "conceptual idea" of the networked consciousness "is an integral part of his work." The idea is not illustrated by the artwork; it is constitutive of it. The telematic artwork is a process — a web of reciprocal exchanges completed by whoever enters the network — so the collective mind it generates is itself the work. (*Directly stated* — Shanken states it as the book's organizing thesis; Ascott develops it across the essays.)

Shanken carefully distinguishes this from cyberspace: "Whereas cyberspace emphasizes the phenomenology of individual experience, telematics emphasizes the emergence of a collective consciousness." Cyberspace names what one mind feels inside the network; telematics names what many minds become together. The argument is therefore irreducibly social and distributed — a single immersed user is not yet network consciousness; the consciousness is the field that arises *between* nodes, in [[Reciprocal Communication|reciprocal]] two-way exchange rather than one-way broadcast.

The claim is scaffolded by two speculative consciousness theories Shanken names as Ascott's frame: Pierre Teilhard de Chardin's *noosphere* — a planetary "thinking layer, enveloping the biosphere of the earth" — and Peter Russell's *global brain*, "the emergence of a cyborgian superorganism integrating human consciousness and global computer networking technology." These give Ascott's network consciousness its cosmic, evolutionary register. It is also explicitly the networked successor to McLuhan's [[The Global Village|global village]]: where McLuhan described the electric retribalization of the planet through extensions of the central nervous system, Ascott projects a web-mind with "no centre, or hierarchy, no top nor bottom." The same global connectivity that here reads as convivial expansion of mind is read elsewhere in the vault as domination — Han's [[Hell of the Same]] and the [[Digital Panopticon]] — so the descriptive and the prophetic registers of this argument should be cited separately. (*Interpretive synthesis*.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. A telematic network permits the potential exchange of information among *all* its nodes (not point-to-point broadcast but many-to-many reciprocal exchange).
2. From that distributed exchange there emerges a "decentralized yet collective state of mind" — a property of the whole network that no single node possesses.
3. Therefore the networked consciousness is "greater than the sum of its parts" — an emergent collective mind.
4. In Ascott's telematic art this conceptual idea is not depicted but enacted: the artwork *is* the networked process, so the emergent consciousness is "an integral part of his work and the history of the genre."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Attribution.** This thesis statement is **Shanken's** (the editor, writing *about* Ascott in his introduction), not Ascott's own prose. Ascott develops the idea in his essays; ch. 04 frames it. The claim is reported here with that provenance.
- **Metaphor vs. mechanism.** "Consciousness" may be a metaphor projected onto an infrastructure of information exchange. Where is the line between [[Distributed Cognition|distributed cognition]] (a defensible cognitive-science claim) and a mystified "collective mind"? Ascott deliberately courts both registers.
- **Utopian charge.** The argument is dated 1984–1990, before the commercial internet. The same all-nodes architecture that Ascott reads as emergent collective consciousness can equally produce homogenization (Han's [[Hell of the Same]]) and control (the [[Digital Panopticon]], Haraway's informatics of domination) — connectivity does not entail a *richer* shared awareness. (*Interpretive synthesis*.)
- **Whose consciousness?** If the work is "greater than the sum of its parts," who or what is the subject of the resulting consciousness, and can such a thing be the subject of an artwork at all?

## Related Arguments

- The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour — the prior move: once art is behaviour/process rather than object, the network's distributed mind can *be* the work; network consciousness is the consciousness-register of the object→behaviour shift.
- [[The decentralized structure of telematic networks is subversive]] — the political corollary: the same "no centre, no hierarchy" architecture that produces collective consciousness subverts solitary authorship and centralized institutions.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the emergent collective mind is the immaterial-of-process pole; the artwork is no longer a physical object but a distributed field of consciousness. Ascott adds an art-side pole to this eight-author tension.
- [[Object vs System]] — Burnham's object→system spine restated for consciousness: network consciousness is what the art-system *is for*, the mind that the system of reciprocal exchanges produces.
- [[Distributed vs Centralised Processing]] — the claim depends on genuinely distributed, all-nodes processing; a centralized broadcast network would not yield collective consciousness, only audience.
