# Extraction is migrating from the online world to the real world from knowledge to influence to control

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism's dispossession cycle does not stay on the screen: it migrates off the browser and into physical space, advancing through a series of escalating relationships to behavior — from an online data source, to a real-world monitor, to an advisor, to an active shepherd — which Zuboff names as the progression "from knowledge to influence to control."

## Expanded Explanation

The Street View narrative supplies Zuboff's exemplary case. What begins as an online map — a way to describe and represent the world — is "redirected" into something far more ambitious. The decisive turn is a shift in the program's grammar: "it would no longer be only about routes, but about routing." To know the routes is to hold a description of the world; to do the routing is to intervene in it. This is the hinge on which the larger thesis of [[The Reality Business]] swings — the move from harvesting data *about* behavior to operating *on* it in the real spaces of everyday life. (`Directly stated`)

Zuboff makes the escalation explicit as a four-term sequence. Street View "and the larger project of Google Maps illustrate the new and even more ambitious goals toward which this cycle of dispossession would soon point: the migration from an online data source to a real-world monitor to an advisor to an active shepherd—from knowledge to influence to control." Each term names a tighter grip on conduct: the *data source* observes; the *monitor* watches in real time; the *advisor* recommends; the *shepherd* herds. The compression of the four into three — knowledge, influence, control — is the seed of [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]]: the apparatus does not merely predict behavior, it is built to *modify* it. (`Directly stated`)

The 2016 Maps feature "Driving Mode" is Zuboff's concrete instance of the shepherding stage. It "suggests destinations and travel times without users even selecting where they want to go," and can route a person who searched for a hammer to a hardware store the moment they buckle the seat belt — a "push" technology folded into the search app. The "copilot" then "prompts an individual to turn left and right on a path defined by its continuously accruing knowledge of the person and the context," issuing real-time, real-world prompts ("Eat here." "Buy this.") matched to a profile and timed to proximity. Zuboff's escalating list of verbs — "Push and pull, suggest, nudge, cajole, shame, seduce" — registers the move from describing behavior to shepherding it, culminating in the ambition that "Google wants to be your copilot for life itself." Each human response to each prompt "yields more data to refine into better prediction products," and the prompts themselves are "bought and paid for" — "real-time, real-world trading in behavioral futures. Your future." (`Directly stated`)

This is the operation Han diagnoses, in a different idiom, as [[Smart Power]]: a power that does not forbid or command from outside but works *with* freedom, prompting and seducing the subject into desiring what serves the system. Where Han names the psychic mechanism, Zuboff supplies its economic engine and its migration into physical space — the same passage from prohibition to nudging that Deleuze's [[Society of Control]] anticipates as the successor to the disciplinary enclosure. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The dispossession cycle's redirection phase reorients Street View from *routes* (a description of the world) to *routing* (an intervention in it).
2. This reorientation is generalizable as a migration through four relationships to behavior: online data source → real-world monitor → advisor → active shepherd.
3. Compressed, those four name a passage from *knowledge* (observing) through *influence* (advising) to *control* (shepherding).
4. "Driving Mode" instantiates the shepherd stage: real-time prompts that route conduct in physical space without the user choosing a destination.
5. Each prompt both modifies behavior and feeds back fresh surplus, refining the next prediction; the prompts are themselves traded in behavioral futures markets.
6. Conclusion: extraction is leaving the screen for the world, and its end is no longer to *know* behavior but to *shepherd* it — the seed of the reality business and the means of behavioral modification.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The chapter establishes the trajectory by example (Street View, Driving Mode) and by Zuboff's naming of it; the fuller demonstration that the real-world operation succeeds at scale belongs to the later chapters on the reality business and economies of action, which this passage forward-references rather than proves. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- "Suggest" and "seduce" sit at different ends of Zuboff's own verb-string; a defender could argue that recommendation is not control and that users retain refusal. Zuboff's reply is structural — the prompts are engineered, traded, and fed back as surplus — but the line between *influence* and *control* is asserted as a continuum here, not resolved.
- The Han/Deleuze cross-reading is an interpretive bridge: Zuboff does not cite either, and the convergence is at the level of mechanism (nudging power that works through, not against, freedom), not direct lineage.
