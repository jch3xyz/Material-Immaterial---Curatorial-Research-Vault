# The neutral-instrument view of technology is the numb stance of the technological idiot

## Claim

The widely held belief that a technology is in itself neither good nor bad — that only its use determines its value — is, for McLuhan, "the numb stance of the technological idiot," a "somnambulism" that ignores the nature of the medium and so fails to study media at all.

## Expanded Explanation

In Chapter 1 of *Understanding Media* McLuhan stages his thesis [[The Medium Is the Message]] against its most respectable opponent: the neutral-instrument view of technology. He takes the view in its crispest period form from General David Sarnoff, who, accepting an honorary degree at Notre Dame, declared that "the products of modern science are not in themselves good or bad; it is the way they are used that determines their value." McLuhan flatly names this "the voice of the current somnambulism" — a sleepwalk, the trance of one who never wakes to the medium itself. `Directly stated`

His method of refutation is reduction by substitution. If Sarnoff's principle is sound, it must hold for any product whatever, so McLuhan runs it through three substitutions that expose its emptiness: "Suppose we were to say, 'Apple pie is in itself neither good nor bad; it is the way it is used that determines its value.' Or, 'The smallpox virus is in itself neither good nor bad; it is the way it is used that determines its value.' Again, 'Firearms are in themselves neither good nor bad; it is the way they are used that determines their value.'" The deadpan extensions — "if the slugs reach the right people firearms are good. If the TV tube fires the right ammunition at the right people it is good" — make the point that the formula is indifferent to the nature of the thing it is about. "There is simply nothing in the Sarnoff statement that will bear scrutiny, for it ignores the nature of the medium." `Directly stated`

The deeper charge is diagnostic, not merely logical. The neutral-instrument view is for McLuhan a symptom: the [[The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium|content of a medium is always another medium]], and content "is like the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind" (see [[The Burglar's Meat]]). Fixating on use and content is precisely what keeps the medium's restructuring of the [[Sense Ratios|sense ratios]] invisible and unresisted; it is "how people fail to study media at all." McLuhan therefore restates his reflex-response indictment as a verdict: "Our conventional response to all media, namely that it is how they are used that counts, is the numb stance of the technological idiot." The word *numb* is load-bearing — it ties this argument forward to the [[Narcosis]] / [[Autoamputation]] material of Chapter 4, where every extension of the body anesthetizes the very sense it amplifies, so that the user becomes a numbed [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions|servomechanism]] of his own technology (the [[Narcissus as Narcosis]] figure). The "technological idiot" is numb in exactly that clinical sense: narcotized by the medium he refuses to perceive. `Directly stated` (Ch. 1) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the cross-link to the Ch. 4 numbness account)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The respectable common sense is the neutral-instrument view: products of science are in themselves neither good nor bad; only their use determines their value (Sarnoff).
2. If true, the principle must apply to any product — so substitute apple pie, the smallpox virus, firearms: the formula yields absurd or vacuous results ("if the slugs reach the right people firearms are good").
3. The formula yields nothing because it abstracts away the *nature of the medium* — the change of scale, pace, and pattern a technology introduces regardless of content.
4. The reflex to judge media by use is therefore not neutrality but blindness: it is how people "fail to study media at all," a "somnambulism."
5. Conclusion: the neutral-instrument view "is the numb stance of the technological idiot" — numb because content (the burglar's meat) distracts the mind from the medium's real, subliminal effect.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Sarnoff quotation as the exemplary statement of the neutral-instrument view — "The products of modern science are not in themselves good or bad; it is the way they are used that determines their value" — labeled "the voice of the current somnambulism" ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/04 Chapter 1 - The Medium Is the Message]]).
- The three reductio substitutions — apple pie, the smallpox virus, firearms — each carrying the Sarnoff formula to absurdity ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/04 Chapter 1 - The Medium Is the Message]]).
- The diagnosis that "it ignores the nature of the medium," and the metaphor of content as "the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind" — content as the lure that keeps the medium unseen (see [[The Burglar's Meat]]).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The neutral-instrument view is itself the standing counterargument, and it remains the popular default; McLuhan never refutes the moral intuition that *uses* can be evaluated, only the claim that use is *all* there is to evaluate.
- The reduction by smallpox and firearms trades on cases where the "instrument" plainly has a determinate character; a defender could grant these as special cases while holding that many technologies really are use-neutral.
- McLuhan's countervailing claim — that content is "ineffectual in shaping the form of human association" — arguably overshoots, leaving little room for the politics or ethics of *what* a medium carries (a tension the [[The Medium Is the Message]] note raises directly).
