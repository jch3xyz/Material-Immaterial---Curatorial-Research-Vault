# The computer metaphor reintroduces the disembodied soul it was built to abolish

## Claim

The computer metaphor was expressly invented to dispense with the metaphysical soul and the "long, inelegant history of mind-body dualism," yet the picture it bequeathed us — mind as software, brain as hardware — makes the mind an immaterial pattern of information that supervenes on matter, which is structurally Descartes's soul/body split under a technical disguise; the cybernetic decoupling of information from matter did not abolish the soul but produced "the materialist's substitute" for it.

## Expanded Explanation

O'Gieblyn's argument turns on a precise irony. The [[Mind as Machine|computer metaphor]] was built to do a job the seventeenth century left undone: to give a fully physical, third-person account of cognition that needs "no mysteries, no ghostly agents, no élan vital." The cyberneticists "made every effort to scrub their discipline of any trace of subjectivity." And yet, she argues, "the soul keeps slipping back in." The reason is structural, not rhetorical. The canonical popular form of the metaphor — mind = software, brain = hardware — assigns the mind to the side of *immaterial pattern* and the brain to the side of *inert physical substrate*. The mind becomes "a pattern of information — an algorithm, or a set of instructions — that supervenes on the hardware." That mapping is the Cartesian division redrawn: *res cogitans* renamed software, *res extensa* renamed hardware. So "the popular notion that the mind is software running on the brain's hardware is itself a form of dualism." The metaphor is "arguably appealing," she adds, "because it reiterates the Cartesian assumption that the mind is something above and beyond the physical." (See [[The Mind-Body Problem]], [[René Descartes]].)

The deeper mechanism that lets the soul return is, on O'Gieblyn's account, the decoupling of information from matter, which she narrates through [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s history of the Macy Conferences in *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*. As cybernetics' ambitions grew, "context began to seem like a hindrance," and information "became decontextualized" until it seemed "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates." [[Claude Shannon]]'s theory "stripped information of meaning," and "during the Macy Conferences, it became decoupled from matter," ending as something "practically immaterial, 'a mathematical quantity weightless as sunshine.'" The decisive sentence is O'Gieblyn's verdict on that abstraction: "It became, in other words, the materialist's substitute for the soul." Once mind is identified with this weightless, substrate-independent [[Information|information]], it has all the metaphysical properties of the soul — immateriality, transcendence of the body, portability — while claiming the prestige of materialism. (See [[The Body as Data]], [[Pattern and Randomness]], [[Patternism]].)

This is the through-line linking the book's chapters named in the scope. Chapter 2 establishes the *crypto-dualism* — the supposedly anti-metaphysical figure secretly Cartesian. Chapter 4 shows the consequence: the same metaphor, "expressly designed to avoid the notion of a metaphysical soul," has "returned to us these ancient religious ideas about physical transcendence and the disembodied spirit." Functionalism's claim that mental states are "multiply realizable," "instantiated by any medium, biological or mechanical," carries the soul further than Descartes dared — to [[Mind Uploading|mind-uploading]] and the promise that "the patterns of our minds are not only abstract and irreducible but potentially immortal." The disembodied [[Embodiment|body]] thus comes back as a disembodied spirit, and what was sold as the abolition of the soul becomes its [[Transhumanism|technological reincarnation]]. As [[The Mind-Body Problem|O'Gieblyn's broader thesis]] holds, the metaphor "has not solved our most pressing existential problems; it has merely transferred them to a new substrate."

## Reasoning Structure

1. The computer metaphor was *invented to abolish* the soul — to "get around the metaphysical notion of a soul and the long, inelegant history of mind-body dualism."
2. Its canonical form splits mind from brain: mind = software (immaterial pattern), brain = hardware (inert substrate).
3. That split is *structurally identical* to Descartes's *res cogitans* / *res extensa* — so "the mind is software running on the brain's hardware is itself a form of dualism."
4. The split was made possible by the cybernetic *decoupling of information from matter* (Macy Conferences): information stripped of meaning, then of materiality, became "weightless as sunshine."
5. Substrate-independent information has exactly the soul's properties (immaterial, transcendent, portable): it "became, in other words, the materialist's substitute for the soul."
6. Conclusion: the metaphor did not eliminate the disembodied soul but *reintroduced* it — returning "ancient religious ideas about physical transcendence and the disembodied spirit" under a materialist alias.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The physicalist reply.** O'Gieblyn fairly states the proponents' defense: the metaphor "is compatible with physicalism: the mind cannot exist without the brain, so it is ultimately connected to and instantiated by something physical." A defender can argue that *supervenience on* matter is precisely *not* Cartesian substance dualism — the pattern does not float free, it has no separate existence. O'Gieblyn's rejoinder is that the metaphor's *appeal* nonetheless trades on treating mind as "above and beyond the physical," so the Cartesian intuition does the persuasive work even where the official metaphysics is monist.
- **Property vs. substance dualism.** The argument is strongest as a claim about *structure and appeal*, not about a literal commitment to an immaterial substance. Most functionalists would call themselves property pluralists, not Cartesians; the note is on firm ground only because O'Gieblyn's claim is about the *return* of dualist intuitions and religious longings, "Strongly implied" rather than a confession the theorists would sign.
- **The opposite reading: the metaphor erases the body, it does not re-ensoul it.** [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s diagnosis (which O'Gieblyn borrows) is the *erasure* of embodiment, not the *return* of the soul. The two are complementary, not identical: O'Gieblyn adds the theological register (soul, transcendence, resurrection) that Hayles's posthumanism brackets. The contrast is real and is the productive tension between the notes (see [[The disappearance of the body is the symptom of a new subjectivity]]).
- **Risk of over-reading the metaphor.** The eliminativists (Dennett, see [[Eliminativism]]) would deny there is any soul-shaped residue to smuggle: "subjectivity… that's an illusion." On their view the metaphor abolishes the soul completely. O'Gieblyn counters that even denial is Cartesian — "to place it outside the material world, deeming it something — much like Descartes's soul — that does not exist within time or space."
