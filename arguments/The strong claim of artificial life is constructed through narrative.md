# The strong claim of artificial life is constructed through narrative

## Claim

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], the "strong claim" of [[Artificial Life]] — that self-evolving computer codes "do not merely simulate life but are themselves alive" — is not established by the programs but *constructed through a multilayered system of narratives*: anthropomorphic naming and imagery at the level of the program, rhetorical positioning of AL as theoretical biology at the level of the field, and speculative stories about our "silicon cousins" at the level of evolution, through which "life," "nature," and the "human" are redefined and the machine becomes the model for the human.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the demystifying methodological move of Hayles's chapter, and it should be distinguished from the concept [[Artificial Life]] itself. The concept names the research program; this argument names Hayles's *reading* of how that program's most ambitious claim is made to seem plausible. Her wager is that the question "How is it possible in the late twentieth century to believe . . . that computer codes are alive—and not only alive but natural?" cannot be answered by inspecting the scientific content of the programs alone, because the belief lives partly outside the machine: "the program operates as much within the imagination as it does within the computer." The right object of analysis is therefore "not only . . . the scientific content of the programs but also . . . the stories told about and through them." Those stories, she argues, are "a multilayered system of metaphoric and material relays."

Hayles structures the chapter as three nested levels of narrative. The **first level** is the program itself — Thomas Ray's Tierra — and the descriptions Ray and others give of it, in which "authorial intention, anthropomorphic interpretation, and the operations of the program are so interwoven that it is impossible to separate them." Bit-flips become "mutations," memory blocks become a primeval "soup," 80-byte strings become "ancestors," "parasites," and "hyperparasites," and a spectrum of bar graphs becomes "the high drama of a Darwinian struggle for survival and reproduction." The crucial point is that this is not a belated overlay (as with Dawkins's selfish genes) but design intent: Ray built the system "precisely so the 'creatures' would be able to manifest these qualities," so that naming functions "not so much as an overlay as an explication of an intention that was there at the beginning." Image and plot complete the work: the Santa Fe Institute video translates "material base → functionality → representational code," giving the codes three-dimensional ant-like bodies and a Genesis plot, eliding the difference between "behavior, properly restricted to an organism, and execution of a code." This is where the [[The Observer|observer]] re-enters — "It's in the eye of the beholder. It's not the system, it's the observer."

The **second level** is the rhetoric by which AL practitioners "position Artificial Life as a valid area of research within theoretical biology." Here the strong claim does political work: as long as AL programs are "considered to be simulations," their results are "artifactual and therefore nonsignifying with respect to the natural world"; but "these difficulties disappear . . . if AL programs are themselves alive." [[Christopher Langton]]'s definition — that "'aliveness' will be found to be a property" of logical form, "not of the . . . material basis" — is, Hayles notes, partly tautological (it defines life so the programs qualify) but more deeply a *reinscription* of the Platonic privileging of form over matter, which is why it persuades. Recast as "a second instance of life," AL even "affect[s] the definition of biological life as well." The **third level** is the speculative narrative of terrestrial evolution — stories of "our silicon cousins" told by [[Hans Moravec]] (consciousness as essence, to be downloaded) and [[Rodney Brooks]] (consciousness as a "cheap trick" emerging from robust embodied interaction). At this level the relay reverses direction: where Artificial Intelligence took "the human . . . [as] the measure" against which the machine was judged, AL "evolve[s] intelligence within the machine," so that "human intelligence is itself reconfigured in the image of this evolutionary process." This is the culmination Hayles names: "In the AL paradigm, the machine becomes the model for understanding the human. Thus the human is transfigured into the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]." The argument thus exposes the strong claim as a narrative achievement that, by routing the human through the machine, naturalizes [[The Computational Universe|the computational universe]] and sides decisively with information against [[Embodiment|embodiment]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. The belief that "computer codes are alive—and not only alive but natural" rests on assumptions "not explicitly articulated" and cannot be answered by the programs' scientific content alone; it must be "approached through indirection," by examining "the stories told about and through them."
2. These stories form "a multilayered system of metaphoric and material relays through which 'life,' 'nature,' and the 'human' are being redefined" — i.e., the work of the claim is done in narrative, not in the machine.
3. *First level (the program):* anthropomorphic naming, image, and plot interweave intention and operation so tightly that "it is impossible to separate them"; the codes are translated "into the high drama of a Darwinian struggle," and the "creatures" are imaged as bodies, eliding code-execution with organism-behavior.
4. *Second level (the field):* the strong claim is rhetorically necessary — simulations yield only "artifactual" results, but "these difficulties disappear . . . if AL programs are themselves alive," so AL is positioned as theoretical biology via Langton's form/matter separation, which persuades "precisely because [it] reinscribe[s]" the Platonic hierarchy of form over matter.
5. *Third level (evolution):* speculative stories about "silicon cousins" interpolate the creatures' story into ours; AL inverts the AI relation so that "human intelligence is itself reconfigured in the image of this evolutionary process."
6. Across all three levels "the implication of the observer in the construction of all three narratives becomes explicit" — the strong claim is "in the eye of the beholder."
7. *Conclusion:* "the machine becomes the model for understanding the human. Thus the human is transfigured into the posthuman" — the strong claim is a narrative construction whose stakes are the redefinition of the human in favor of disembodied information.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The "it's only a visualization" defense.** Hayles concedes that practitioners "would no doubt want to remind us" that the video "is merely an artist's visualization and has no scientific standing." Her reply is that the same anthropomorphic transformations "are also inscribed in published articles and commentary" and "are essential to the strong claim" — but a skeptic could hold that the load-bearing scientific argument is Langton's formal definition, not the imagery, so the narrative analysis targets the popularization rather than the science.
- **Narrative is not refutation.** Showing that a claim is *constructed* through narrative does not show it is *false*; AL's defenders (Fredkin, Langton) could grant the narratives and still maintain that, on an informational metaphysics, the codes really are alive. Hayles's move demystifies and denaturalizes the claim rather than directly disproving it — her counter ultimately rests on the separate, normative insistence that "bodies can never be made of information alone."
- **The argument is selective about the field.** Hayles notes that "not everyone who works in the field agrees that disembodied 'organisms' are the best way to construct Artificial Life" — [[Francisco Varela]] and the embodied roboticists complicate the picture. The narrative-construction critique bites hardest on the disembodied wing; the embodied wing (Brooks's "the world is its own best model") partly resists it, which Hayles uses but which also limits the claim's generality.
- **Interpretive load.** The reading that anthropomorphic naming is "an explication of an intention that was there at the beginning" is an interpretive synthesis of Ray's design rationale; a reader could grant the naming while disputing that intention determines ontology.
