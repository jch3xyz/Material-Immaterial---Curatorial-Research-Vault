# In an industrial society abstraction is psychic preparation for the re-creation of life

## Claim

In an industrial society artistic abstraction is no longer a form of psychic protection from an indifferent environment, as Worringer held, but becomes "psychic preparation for the entire re-creation of society, including remaking the biological composition of its inhabitants."

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham opens his introduction by explicitly departing from Wilhelm Worringer's account of why abstraction recurs in art. For Worringer, abstraction was a defensive reflex: it appeared "when a culture was confronted with existential doubt," a means by which man "periodically sought relief from 'cosmic anguish,'" while naturalism expressed human confidence before an ordered world. Burnham concedes this may hold for a "pre-scientific society," where abstraction "served as a form of psychic protection from the dangers of an indifferent environment." But he insists the function inverts once a society industrializes. Abstraction stops being shelter and becomes a program. It is no longer adjunct to a now-hollow ritual; it is the rehearsal of a world about to be re-engineered — including the biological re-engineering of human beings themselves.

The pivot is a "threshold of scientific understanding." Burnham argues that this threshold has, "for the first time in the known history of the world," actually been crossed, marked by "Newton's proofs for universal gravitation in his Principia, the equations of Clerk Maxwell for light and electromagnetism, and Einstein's famous ratio for the interconversion of mass and energy" — each "a delicate web of symbols" on which "the present technology" was built. Once this threshold is passed, abstraction can no longer remain "latent and decorative." To "remain culturally potent" it "must gravitate toward a merger with the types of symbolic reasoning employed by science." Formalist art in a technological culture therefore "seeks and finds its raison d'etre in more pervasive types of formalism" — the formalism of scientific theory itself.

This argument grounds Burnham's larger thesis that modern sculpture is "a preparatory stage representing steps toward the simulation of biological life." Abstraction is the artistic limb of a single, irreversible drive — what he elsewhere calls [[The Scientific Demiurge]] — to bring "inert matter to life" by non-biological means. It connects the vault's art-historical material directly to its [[Posthumanism]] / [[The Body as Data]] cluster: the abstract artwork is read not as flight from the world but as imaginative groundwork for remaking the body and recreating life through [[Technics]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. Worringer: abstraction recurs as psychic protection from an indifferent, disordered environment; naturalism expresses confidence before an ordered one.
2. This may be true of a pre-scientific society, where abstraction shelters against cosmic anguish.
3. But an industrial society has crossed a "threshold of scientific understanding" (Newton / Maxwell / Einstein) — symbolic reasoning that actually masters "the invisible energies which pervade the universe."
4. Past that threshold, abstraction can no longer remain decorative or merely ritual-adjacent; to stay culturally potent it must merge with scientific symbolic reasoning.
5. Therefore, in an industrial society abstraction's role is not protection but preparation — "psychic preparation for the entire re-creation of society, including remaking the biological composition of its inhabitants."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham presents the inversion of Worringer as asserted history of art, not demonstrated; whether industrial abstraction *causes* or merely *accompanies* the technological re-creation of life is left as an "assumption" he himself flags ("an assumption which eliminates the pretense that contemporary art is tied to the art history karma of stylistic cycles").
- The "threshold of scientific understanding" is admittedly "unspecified," which weakens the precision of the claim that a single dateable crossing reorients all abstraction.
- The teleology is strong: art "becomes a dwindling part of an unstoppable scheme of events," moving "in a single irreversible direction." A critic could read this as technological determinism imposed on a far messier history of abstract art.
