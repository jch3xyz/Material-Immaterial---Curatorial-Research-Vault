# Fix the system not the individual because rational actors produce outcomes no one wants

## Claim

Because [[Bounded Rationality|bounded rationality]] makes every actor's local decisions reasonable on the incomplete, delayed information available from their place in the system, separate rational actions aggregate into results no one wants — so change comes from redesigning the information, incentives, and goals acting on a role, not from replacing the person who occupies it.

## Expanded Explanation

Meadows opens the argument with a catalogue of self-inflicted collective failures — tourists ruining the resorts they flock to, farmers gluttering their own markets, "Fishermen overfish and destroy their own livelihood," corporations timing investment so as to manufacture business-cycle downturns. These are "examples of people acting rationally in their short-term best interests and producing aggregate results that no one likes." Her diagnosis names two sources for the pattern: "what World Bank economist Herman Daly calls the 'invisible foot'" and "what Nobel Prize-winning economist Herbert Simon calls bounded rationality." The "invisible foot" is the pointed inversion of Adam Smith's invisible hand — where Smith's market coordinates private interest into public good, the invisible foot kicks it into public harm. `Directly stated`

The engine of the pattern is not stupidity or malice but structural position. "Bounded rationality means that people make quite reasonable decisions based on the information they have. But they don't have perfect information, especially about more distant parts of the system." Fishermen do not know how many fish there are or how many others will catch that day; businessmen do not know rivals' plans, consumer demand, or their own market share. Information is "incomplete and delayed, and their own responses are delayed," so they "systematically under- and overinvest." We are, in Simon's phrase, not "omniscient, rational optimizers" but "blundering 'satisficers.'" The rationality is genuine; its horizon is set by where in the structure the actor stands. This is why the same self-aware understanding blocks the reflex to blame: "Seeing how individual decisions are rational within the bounds of the information available does not provide an excuse for narrow-minded behavior. It provides an understanding of why that behavior arises." `Directly stated`

From this follows the argument's anti-heroic core, and its practical payoff. Because the constraint lives in the role and not the person, swapping the occupant changes almost nothing: "Taking out one individual from a position of bounded rationality and putting in another person is not likely to make much difference. Blaming the individual rarely helps create a more desirable outcome." Meadows dramatizes the point with the thought experiment of being "lifted out of your accustomed place in society" into a role you never understood — critic-turned-official, laborer-turned-manager, environmentalist-turned-corporate-decision-maker. Inside the new position you inherit "the information flows, the incentives and disincentives, the goals and discrepancies, the pressures — the bounded rationality — that goes with that position," and you behave as its previous occupant did: "If you become a manager, you probably will stop seeing labor as a deserving partner in production, and start seeing it as a cost to be minimized." The Stanford prison experiment is her extreme illustration — students who "in an amazingly short time" took on "the attitudes and behaviors of prison guards and prisoners." The lever, then, is never the actor: "What makes a difference is redesigning the system to improve the information, incentives, disincentives, goals, stresses, and constraints that have an effect on specific actors." Restructure the field and "separate, bounded, rational actions do add up to results that everyone desires." `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. Actors decide reasonably, but only on the incomplete and delayed information their position affords them ([[Bounded Rationality]]).
2. Because each actor optimizes a different local goal on a partial picture, their separate rational actions aggregate into outcomes no one wants — the "invisible foot," overfishing, and (in Ch. 5) [[Policy Resistance|policy resistance]], where "everyone has to put great effort into keeping the system where no one wants it to be."
3. Since the constraint is a property of the *position* — its information, incentives, and goals — not of the *person*, replacing the occupant leaves the outcome essentially unchanged; blame is a category error.
4. Therefore the effective intervention is to redesign the information, incentives, disincentives, goals, stresses, and constraints acting on whoever holds the role — fix the system, not the individual.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The catalogue of "acting rationally… producing aggregate results that no one likes": ruined resorts, farm surpluses, fishermen who "overfish and destroy their own livelihood," self-inflicted business cycles ([[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/08 Chapter 4 - Why Systems Surprise Us]]).
- Herman Daly's "invisible foot" and Herbert Simon's [[Bounded Rationality|bounded rationality]] — the explicit inversion of Adam Smith's optimizing *homo economicus*, whose "assumptions" Meadows says "Neither… stands up long against the evidence."
- The "lifted out of your accustomed place" thought experiment — manager, financier, the poor, the mortgaged fisherman who "will overfish" — and the Stanford prison experiment as the boundary case of role over character.
- The [[Policy Resistance|policy-resistance]] structure from Ch. 5 (drug-supply stock, Romanian abortion ban): divergent, each-locally-rational goals lock a system "where no one wants it to be," the collective analogue of the individual-level surprise.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Meadows is explicit that the argument "does not provide an excuse for narrow-minded behavior" — it explains behavior without excusing it, which leaves the location of moral responsibility genuinely unresolved (a question she raises but does not close).
- Some systems already absorb bounded rationality well: her Dutch electric-meter interlude shows that merely relocating the meter to the front hall cut household use by a third — "even slight enlargement of bounded rationality" via "better, more complete, timelier information" can help before any deeper redesign of incentives.
- The remedy for entrenched [[Policy Resistance|policy resistance]] — "providing an overarching goal that allows all actors to break out of their bounded rationality" — risks concentrating exactly the power to define and surveil the whole system that other authors in the vault treat as domination; Meadows does not weigh that cost.
- The claim runs against two hundred years of Smithian economics and, as Meadows concedes, produced "controversy… far from over"; the argument is a wager on the satisficer, not a settled result.

## Related Arguments

- [[The tragedy of the commons is a failure of feedback not of morality]] — the commons is the paradigm case: individually rational herdsmen destroy a shared resource, and the fix is restructured feedback, not better people.
- [[A system causes its own behavior through its structure]] — the general Meadows principle this argument specializes to human actors: behavior (including "rational" misbehavior) flows from structure, so intervene on structure.

## Tensions

- [[Individual Rationality vs System Welfare]] — this argument is the tension's central statement: what is rational for each actor "may not lead to decisions that further the welfare of the system as a whole," and only a redesign of the system can reconcile the two poles.
