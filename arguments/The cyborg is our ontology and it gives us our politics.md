# The cyborg is our ontology and it gives us our politics

## Claim

Because by the late twentieth century "we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism," the [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] is not a chosen image or rhetorical device but our actual ontological condition — and from that condition a politics directly follows.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the keystone descriptive-to-prescriptive move of *A Cyborg Manifesto*. Haraway first establishes a state of affairs (a description of what we now *are*), then derives from it an imperative (a politics we must now *do*). The two are welded together in a single, deliberately compressed pair of sentences: "we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism—in short, cyborgs. The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics." The semicolon does the philosophical work — ontology on one side, politics on the other, the second presented as flowing necessarily from the first (`Directly stated`).

The force of the argument depends on the cyborg being *real* rather than figural. Haraway is explicit that "the boundary between science fiction and social reality is an optical illusion," and that "A cyborg is a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction." It is a "creature of social reality" precisely because "Social reality is lived social relations, our most important political construction, a world-changing fiction." The cyborg is therefore not offered as a *better way to think about* late-twentieth-century life; it is named as the condition we already inhabit, evidenced by the cyborgs of "contemporary science fiction," "modern medicine," "modem production," and "modern war" — the latter being "a cyborg orgy, coded by C3I, command-control-communication-intelligence" (`Directly stated`).

Because the condition is not optional, neither is the politics. Haraway gives the bridging premise that makes the inference non-trivial: "The cyborg is a condensed image of both imagination and material reality, the two joined centers structuring any possibility of historical transformation." The cyborg joins the imaginative and the material at a single site, and that joint is what "structur[es] any possibility of historical transformation." So whoever controls or inhabits the cyborg controls the conditions of historical change. The political question is then unavoidable: "Who cyborgs will be is a radical question; the answers are a matter of survival." The cyborg is "an argument for the cyborg as a fiction mapping our social and bodily reality and as an imaginative resource suggesting some very fruitful couplings" — that is, simultaneously a map of what is and a resource for what could be (`Directly stated`; the necessity of the prescriptive step is `Strongly implied`).

## Reasoning Structure

1. A cyborg is "a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction" — real, not merely imagined, because "the boundary between science fiction and social reality is an optical illusion."
2. By the late twentieth century this condition is universal: "we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism—in short, cyborgs." It is descriptive of our actual state, not a metaphor we may decline.
3. The cyborg uniquely "condense[s]" imagination and material reality into "the two joined centers structuring any possibility of historical transformation" — it is the site where historical change is decided.
4. Therefore the cyborg is "our ontology" (premise about what we are) and "gives us our politics" (conclusion about what we must do): contesting who cyborgs will be becomes "a matter of survival."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The descriptive-to-prescriptive move is asserted by juxtaposition (the semicolon) rather than argued step by step; that the cyborg condition *entails* a particular politics is `Strongly implied` more than logically demonstrated. Haraway herself frames the whole as "an ironic political myth" and "blasphemy," foregrounding rhetoric over deduction.
- The cyborg is double-edged: "Cyborg unities are monstrous and illegitimate," "the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism." The same ontology underwrites "the final imposition of a grid of control on the planet" as readily as emancipation — so "our ontology" does not by itself fix which politics follows; the political struggle is "to see from both perspectives at once."
- The universality of the claim ("we are all … cyborgs") flattens differences of position; Haraway elsewhere insists on "permanently partial identities and contradictory standpoints," which sits in tension with a single shared ontology.

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the argument's load-bearing premise that the cyborg is "a condensed image of both imagination and material reality" places it exactly on this axis: the cyborg is the figure where the immaterial (fiction, imagination, the coded text) and the material (lived social and bodily reality) are not opposed but joined.
