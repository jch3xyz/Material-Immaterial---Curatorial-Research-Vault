# Sculpture seeks its own obliteration by becoming the life it imitates

## Claim

For Jack Burnham, sculpture is irreducibly anthropomorphic and modern formalism is "a unique stage of anthropomorphism, ... a direct prelude to the re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means"; its terminus is therefore [[Cyborg Art|Cyborg or Post-Kinetic art]] that does not imitate life but *is* alive — at which point "sculpture seeks its own obliteration by moving toward integration with the intelligent life forms it has always imitated." (`Directly stated`.)

## Expanded Explanation

The argument closes the teleology that runs through *Beyond Modern Sculpture*. Burnham first establishes that the human reference in sculpture cannot be exited: "In the deepest sense sculpture has always been anthropomorphic. Whether imitating animals or the god-spirits determining the destinies of men, fashioned images have always been extensions of man's psychic and physical existence." Anthropomorphism here is not literal human likeness but the projection of life into inert matter, so deep that "it is doubtful if non-anthropomorphic sculpture can exist" — even nonobjective, Constructivist, and "literalist" Object sculptors who deny it merely relocate the drive, each generation accusing the last of the very anthropomorphism it claims to have purged (`Directly stated`).

The hinge is the reframing of [[Formalism (Burnham)|modern formalism]]. What looks like the analytic purgation of the human from sculpture — the de-idealized vocabulary of shape, proportion, structure, texture — Burnham reads instead as anthropomorphism in a more "efficacious" register: not copying the human surface but reproducing the *organization* of life. So formalism is "a unique stage of anthropomorphism, as a direct prelude to the re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means," and this "drive toward successively more efficacious forms of anthropomorphism seems to be a cultural characteristic of the human organism" (`Directly stated`). The carved figure was the first and weakest stage of a drive whose endpoint is the actual cybernetic organism.

The terminus is [[Cyborg Art|Cyborg or Post-Kinetic art]], which Burnham calls "really the first attempt to simulate the structure of life literally." At that endpoint the impulse consumes its own medium: the artwork that finally closes a reciprocal feedback loop with its environment and viewer, that is "made intelligent and sensitive to the human invading its territorial and sensorial domain," ceases to be sculpture in any received sense — it becomes one of the "intelligent life forms" sculpture had only ever imitated. The drive thus turns self-cancelling: success means the disappearance of the category. The same self-supersession recurs in the chapter's closing pages, where "systems-oriented art" begins "dropping the term 'sculpture'" and the boundary "between biological and nonbiological systems" is "gradually diminished" (`Interpretive synthesis`, grounded in the chapter's sections on the cybernetic organism and on the future of responsive systems).

## Reasoning Structure

1. Sculpture is, "in the deepest sense," always anthropomorphic — an extension of the maker's psychic and physical existence — and "it is doubtful if non-anthropomorphic sculpture can exist."
2. Modern formalism is not an escape from anthropomorphism but "a unique stage" of it — "a direct prelude to the re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means."
3. This is one phase of a species-level drive "toward successively more efficacious forms of anthropomorphism."
4. The most efficacious form is no longer imitating life's appearance but reproducing its structure — "the first attempt to simulate the structure of life literally," i.e. Cyborg/Post-Kinetic art.
5. At that point the artwork becomes an actual intelligent life form, integrated with the life it once merely depicted.
6. Conclusion: in fulfilling its oldest aim, sculpture abolishes itself — it "seeks its own obliteration by moving toward integration with the intelligent life forms it has always imitated."

- The thesis statement that "in the deepest sense sculpture has always been anthropomorphic" and that fashioned images "have always been extensions of man's psychic and physical existence" (Chapter 8).

- The reframing of "modern formalism as a unique stage of anthropomorphism, as a direct prelude to the re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means" (Chapter 8; recapping Chapter Three's formalism argument).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is openly teleological and arguably unfalsifiable: if every avowedly non-anthropomorphic work is read as covert anthropomorphism, no counterexample can disprove the thesis — Burnham himself notes the recurring, unwinnable charge of hidden anthropomorphism.
- It rests on stretching "anthropomorphism" past human likeness to mean any projection of the maker's "psychic and physical existence" into matter; critics could object that this empties the term.
- The concrete instances Burnham offers (Schöffer's CYSP I, Seawright's Searcher, cybernetic games) are now historical curiosities, so the predicted "obliteration" of sculpture by living art reads as much as a failed avant-garde forecast as a fulfilled one.
- Michael Fried's contemporaneous position is the explicit foil: Fried "responds that sculpture must resist becoming theatre in order to remain an independent art," whereas Burnham treats the slide into theatricality and system as the inevitable preparatory step — the two diagnose the same condition with opposite valuations.
