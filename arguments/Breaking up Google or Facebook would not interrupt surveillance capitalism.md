# Breaking up Google or Facebook would not interrupt surveillance capitalism

## Claim

Because surveillance capitalism is a *logic of accumulation* distributed across firms rather than a property of any one company, antitrust remedies like breaking up Google or Facebook would not interrupt it — they would only multiply surveillance-capitalist firms at a smaller scale.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff's argument is a direct corollary of her central thesis that surveillance capitalism "is a logic in action and not a technology" and, by the same token, "cannot be reduced to any single company or group of companies." If the offending thing is neither a device nor a corporation but a *market form* — a way of converting human experience into behavioral data for prediction and sale — then the standard tools of twentieth-century reform are aimed at the wrong target. We reach for the inherited categories of "monopoly" and "privacy," but these "fall short in identifying and contesting the most crucial and unprecedented facts of this new regime."

The breakup remedy is the clearest illustration. Dissolving a dominant firm on monopoly grounds treats market concentration as the harm. But the harm in surveillance capitalism is the logic of extraction itself, which every successor firm would inherit. Splitting Google or Facebook would therefore "clear the way for more surveillance capitalist competitors" — and redistributing the online-advertising duopoly accomplishes nothing "if online advertising market share is simply spread over five surveillance capitalist firms or fifty, instead of two." More firms practicing the same logic is not less surveillance capitalism; it is more of it.

This is why Zuboff insists the unit of analysis is the logic, not the technology and not the company (`Interpretive synthesis`). Where Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and Harari's [[Dataism]] locate the danger in a diffuse condition — voluntary self-exposure, the worship of data — Zuboff locates it in an *economic order* whose imperatives any firm can adopt. The implication is that contestation must target the operations of extraction and prediction directly, not merely the size or number of the firms performing them.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism is a logic of accumulation, not a technology and not a single company.
2. Antitrust remedies (breakup, reducing a duopoly) treat the harm as market concentration within particular companies.
3. Therefore such remedies redistribute the logic rather than ending it — they produce more surveillance-capitalist firms at smaller scale.
4. Conclusion: breaking up Google or Facebook would not interrupt surveillance capitalism; only contesting the logic's unprecedented operations could contain it.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The thesis statement that "surveillance capitalism is a logic in action and not a technology," because "surveillance capitalists want us to think that their practices are inevitable expressions of the technologies they employ" — source link below.
- The companion distinction that "this new logic of accumulation cannot be reduced to any single company or group of companies," refusing to treat Apple, Google, Amazon, Microsoft, and Facebook as "a single entity."
- The breakup hypothetical and the "five surveillance capitalist firms or fifty, instead of two" advertising example — source link below.
- The diagnosis that inherited categories ("monopoly," "privacy") "fall short" against this regime.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- An antitrust advocate could reply that scale itself is a precondition of surveillance capitalism's predictive power — the value of behavioral surplus rises with the breadth of data, so fragmenting the giants might genuinely weaken the logic, not merely redistribute it. Zuboff grants that monopoly and privacy harms are "vital" but treats them as necessary-not-sufficient.
- The argument is programmatic rather than empirical here: Zuboff asserts the logic would survive fragmentation but offers the breakup scenario as a hypothetical, not a demonstrated case.
- It leaves open what *would* interrupt the logic; the chapter promises only to "focus on the unprecedented aspects... that must be contested and interrupted," deferring the remedy.

## Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — the argument's spine: the harm is not an *object* (a company, a technology) but a *system* of accumulation that persists when its objects are rearranged.
