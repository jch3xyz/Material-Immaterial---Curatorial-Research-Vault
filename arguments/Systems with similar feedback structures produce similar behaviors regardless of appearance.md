# Systems with similar feedback structures produce similar behaviors regardless of appearance

## Claim

Systems built from the same feedback-loop topology produce the same characteristic dynamic behavior over time even when they look nothing alike, because behavior is generated by structure, not by surface appearance — so structural isomorphs are behavioral isomorphs.

## Expanded Explanation

This is one of the two central insights Donella Meadows names in *Thinking in Systems*, standing beside the claim that [[Structure Determines Behavior|systems largely cause their own behavior]]. In her own phrasing it is "as central as the observation that systems largely cause their own behavior." Where that first insight looks *inward* — behavior is latent in a system's own internal wiring rather than imposed by outside events — this second insight looks *across*: once you learn to read a system by its [[Feedback Loops|feedback-loop structure]], you can recognize the same dynamic playing out in bodies of matter that share nothing on the surface. The lesson of "A Brief Visit to the Systems Zoo" is that the zoo's specimens are sorted not by what they are made of but by the shape of their loops.

The argument dissolves the apparent gap between wildly unlike things. "A population is nothing like an industrial economy, except that both can reproduce themselves out of themselves and thus grow exponentially. And both age and die." A herd of animals and a stock of steel mills, lathes, and turbines are, in Meadows's words, "dissimilar in many ways" but "have one important thing in common: their feedback-loop structures." Both carry a stock governed by a reinforcing growth loop and a balancing death loop, plus an aging process — and so both trace the same curve. The industrial economy is a structural isomorph of the population, and therefore a behavioral one.

The same holds for the simplest balancing case. "A coffee cup cooling is like a warmed room cooling, and like a radioactive substance decaying, and like a population or industrial economy aging and dying. Each declines as the result of a balancing feedback loop." A cup of coffee, a heated room, and a lump of uranium have no material resemblance whatever; they are alike only in that each is governed by the goal-seeking, "homing in" behavior of a single balancing loop, and that shared structure is enough to make them decline along the same path. This is why the systems lens is portable: a diagnostic learned on one system transfers to any other with the same loop skeleton, regardless of scale, substance, or appearance (`Directly stated`).

## Reasoning Structure

1. A system's behavior over time is produced by its internal structure — its stocks, flows, and above all its feedback loops — not by its material composition or outward form (see [[Structure Determines Behavior]]).
2. Two systems can therefore be *structurally identical* (same configuration of reinforcing and balancing loops) while being *materially and visually unrelated*.
3. If structure generates behavior, then structural identity entails behavioral identity: same loop topology, same characteristic dynamic (exponential growth, goal-seeking decline, oscillation).
4. Concrete cases confirm it — a population and an industrial economy share a growth-plus-death-loop structure and both grow exponentially then age and die; a coffee cup, a warmed room, and a decaying radioactive substance share a single balancing loop and all decline the same way.
5. Therefore: to know how a system will behave, read its feedback structure, not its appearance — and a model built for one system applies to every other system that shares its structure.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The population and the industrial-economy models, drawn side by side in [[The Systems Zoo]], are "dissimilar in many ways" yet "have one important thing in common: their feedback-loop structures" — a reinforcing growth loop, a balancing death loop, and an aging process — and so behave alike ([[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/06 Chapter 2 - A Brief Visit to the Systems Zoo|Ch. 2]]).
- The one-balancing-loop family: "A coffee cup cooling is like a warmed room cooling, and like a radioactive substance decaying, and like a population or industrial economy aging and dying. Each declines as the result of a balancing feedback loop" — three materially unrelated systems collapsed into one behavior class by shared structure.
- The explicit statement of the principle as a named insight of [[Systems Theory|systems theory]]: "systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors, even if the outward appearance of these systems is completely dissimilar."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Structural isomorphism is not total identity.** Two systems that share a loop skeleton can still diverge once *delays*, nonlinearities, or additional loops enter — Meadows immediately follows the isomorphism claim with the inventory/car-dealership system, whose delays produce oscillation absent from the simpler cases. Shared loop topology predicts a *family* of behavior, not every detail.
- **Recognizing the shared structure is the hard part.** The claim licenses transferring insight only *after* one has correctly abstracted the feedback structure from the surface; misreading the loops (or missing a hidden one) transfers a wrong prediction just as confidently.
- **Behavioral likeness can be read backward into false unity.** The insight warrants "these behave alike because their structures are alike," not "these are alike in every respect" — a coffee cup and a radioactive nucleus remain, in every way but their decline curve, utterly different things.
