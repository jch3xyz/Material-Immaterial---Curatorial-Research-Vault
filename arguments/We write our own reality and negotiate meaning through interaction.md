# We write our own reality and negotiate meaning through interaction

## Claim

Against the "hermeneutic error" that the world is a pre-given text to be interpreted, Ascott argues that in the telematic era we write our own reality and negotiate for meaning through interaction — a constructivist epistemology in which reality is made, not read.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott names the older, interpretive model of meaning a *hermeneutic error*: the belief, characteristic of "the pre-telematic era," that the world is "full of meaning, a text to be interpreted, a great book waiting to be read." On this view meaning is antecedent and external — embedded in the world by some author (God, nature, history) and awaiting the reader who will decode it. The interpreter is passive before a finished text. Ascott treats this not as a partial truth but as a category mistake about where meaning lives. `Confidence: Directly stated.`

In its place he asserts a constructivist and connectivist epistemology: "Now we see that we write our own reality and negotiate for meaning through interaction." Meaning is no longer found but produced, and it is produced *together* — "negotiate" makes it transactional and intersubjective rather than private. This is the epistemology that underwrites his coupled program of *radical constructivism / radical connectivism*, which he defines as the conjunction that "can provide a scenario of living in which we take on responsibility for the construction of our own identity and the reality it inhabits." The telematic network is the medium of that joint authorship: reality is rewritten at the interface, in the back-and-forth of interaction, not contemplated from outside it. `Confidence: Directly stated.`

This reframing is structural to Ascott's aesthetics. If the world is a text to be read, art's job is representation and interpretation; if reality is written through interaction, art's job becomes the building of *contexts* and *systems* in which users co-author meaning. Hence his recurring move from "the appearance of things and their representation" toward "processes of interaction, transformation, and emergence," and his insistence that "reality is renegotiated and the new consciousness is embodied" in the interspace between the virtual and the actual. The argument thus grounds his broader claim that telematic art is participatory by epistemological necessity, not by stylistic choice. `Confidence: Strongly implied.`

## Reasoning Structure

1. The pre-telematic worldview held meaning to be pre-given — the world as a full text, a book to be read — so the human task was interpretation (the "hermeneutic error").
2. In the telematic condition, networked interaction makes participants co-producers, not decoders; there is no finished text standing behind the exchange.
3. Therefore reality is *written* (constructed) and meaning is *negotiated* (produced jointly through interaction), not discovered.
4. Conclusion: we take on responsibility for the construction of our own identity and the reality it inhabits — the constructivist/connectivist program of telematic art.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim is asserted as a periodizing observation ("Now we see…") rather than argued; it presupposes the very telematic break it describes, so a reader who rejects the periodization is given little independent ground to accept the epistemology.
- A constructivist "we write our own reality" risks collapsing the distinction between intersubjective construction and mere subjectivism; Ascott's "negotiate … through interaction" is meant to forestall this by making meaning transactional, but the entry does not specify what constrains the negotiation (cf. his "Cyberpower" entry: "Virtual reality corrupts and absolute reality corrupts absolutely" when its constraints are "preordained, predefined, or pre-set").
- Compared with Harari, who treats constructed realities (money, nations, gods) as *fictions* that nonetheless have real causal force, Ascott affirms the constructed reality without the ironic distance — closer to a normative program than a descriptive thesis.
