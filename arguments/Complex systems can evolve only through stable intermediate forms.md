# Complex systems can evolve only through stable intermediate forms

## Claim

A complex system can be assembled — and therefore can evolve — only if it can pass through stable intermediate forms along the way; because non-modular assembly collapses whenever it is interrupted, the only complex forms that have ever had time to come into being are the hierarchically buildable ones. Hierarchy is thus not one design choice among many but very nearly the *only* arrangement complexity could have arrived by.

## Expanded Explanation

Meadows makes the argument not as a theorem but as a fable — an "INTERLUDE" she titles "Why the Universe Is Organized into Hierarchies." Two watchmakers, Hora and Tempus, both make fine thousand-part watches and both have busy phones. Tempus assembles his watches as one continuous piece, "in such a way that if he had one partly assembled and had to put it down—to answer the phone, say—it / fell to pieces." Every interruption wipes out all accumulated work, so the busier he gets, the less he can finish, and he grows poorer and poorer. Hora, by contrast, "put together stable subassemblies of about ten elements each," then combined ten subassemblies into a larger assembly, and ten of those into the whole watch. When the phone rings, "he lost only a small part of his work." He prospers.

The moral generalizes past watches: **"Complex systems can evolve from simple systems only if there are stable intermediate forms. The resulting complex forms will naturally be hierarchic."** Evolution, like a busy watchmaker's day, is a process constantly "interrupted" — by perturbation, by the sheer improbability of assembling a thousand parts in one uninterrupted stroke. A form that must be built all-at-once, with no stable resting points, is effectively unbuildable; the assembly never survives long enough to complete. Only forms that can be locked in as durable subassemblies — cell, organ, organism, family, town, nation — accumulate. Hence the survivorship conclusion Meadows states outright: "That may explain why hierarchies are so common in the systems nature presents to us. Among all possible complex forms, hierarchies are the only ones that have had the time to evolve."

The fable is **Meadows's retelling of a parable that originates with Herbert Simon** (his "The Architecture of Complexity" / *The Sciences of the Artificial*). In this raw chapter Meadows does not name Simon, so the retelling is attributed to her while Simon is flagged as the parable's source. The claim connects [[Self-Organization]] (the tendency of systems to elaborate their own structure from simple rules) to [[Hierarchy (Systems)]] (the particular structure that elaboration reliably yields): "In the process of creating new structures and increasing complexity, one thing that a self-organizing system often generates is hierarchy." Hierarchy is what self-organization builds because it is the only thing self-organization *can* build to completion.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Assembling a complex form is a process that can be interrupted before completion (the ringing phone; in nature, perturbation and improbability).
2. Non-modular assembly (Tempus) loses *all* accumulated work on interruption, so it rarely, if ever, reaches completion.
3. Modular assembly through stable subassemblies (Hora) loses only a small increment on interruption, so it reaches completion far more efficiently — the accumulated intermediate forms are preserved.
4. Therefore only forms buildable through stable intermediate forms can actually get built; the resulting forms are, by construction, nested and hierarchic.
5. Across the vast time of evolution, this is a survivorship filter: "Among all possible complex forms, hierarchies are the only ones that have had the time to evolve." Hierarchy in nature is thus explained, not merely described.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Hora/Tempus watchmaker fable — modular vs. non-modular assembly of a thousand-part watch, where interruption destroys unfinished non-modular work but spares completed subassemblies (`[[The Two Watchmakers]]`; `[[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/07 Chapter 3 - Why Systems Work So Well]]`).
- The empirical ubiquity of nested subsystems: "A cell in your liver is a subsystem of an organ, which is a subsystem of you as an organism, and you are a subsystem of a family... These groups are subsystems of a town or city, and then a nation." "Corporate systems, military systems, ecological systems, economic systems, living organisms, are arranged in hierarchies. It is no accident that that is so."
- The functional payoff of hierarchy that makes the surviving forms robust as well as buildable: hierarchies "reduce the amount of information that any part of the system has to keep track of," because "relationships within each subsystem are denser and stronger than relationships between subsystems," so "no level is overwhelmed with information."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The argument explains prevalence, not necessity in the strict sense.** Meadows hedges — "That *may* explain why hierarchies are so common." It is a survivorship/probabilistic account (hierarchies are what had *time* to evolve), not a proof that non-hierarchic complexity is logically impossible.
- **Attribution.** The "stable intermediate forms" thesis and the watchmaker parable are Herbert Simon's; Meadows retells them without citation in this chapter, so the reasoning is inherited, not original to her. A dedicated Meadows–Simon reference note is deferred to a chapter where Simon is named explicitly.
- **Hierarchy has failure modes Meadows immediately flags.** Right after this passage she notes that hierarchies "can be taken apart" (partial decomposability) and that treating a subsystem in isolation invites [[System Traps]]-style surprises; elsewhere she warns that misdesigned hierarchies over-optimize for the sub-unit at the expense of the whole. Buildability does not guarantee good behavior.
- The claim concerns *assembly under interruption*. It says little about complex forms that might arise through means other than incremental assembly, which the fable brackets out.

## Related Arguments

- [[A system persists as itself through complete turnover of its elements]] — the companion Meadows theme: hierarchy explains how complexity gets *built*; turnover-through-structure explains how the built system *maintains* its identity over time. Both locate a system's endurance in its architecture, not its parts.

## Tensions

- [[Distributed vs Centralised Processing]] — Meadows's decomposability argument (hierarchy works because "no level is overwhelmed with information" and each subsystem "can largely take care of" itself) is a general-systems statement of why distributed, locally self-regulating architectures out-compete a single overloaded central processor. It grounds Harari's political-economy version of the same contrast in its native discipline.
