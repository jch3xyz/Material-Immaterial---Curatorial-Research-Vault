# A biological society that builds superintelligent automata will be dominated by them

## Claim

Reporting Roger MacGowan and Frederick Ordway's *Intelligence in the Universe* (1966), Burnham relays their thesis that any emerging intelligent biological society which develops highly intelligent automata must resign itself to being completely dominated and controlled by them — the sole safeguard being to keep the machines deliberately subnormal and to destroy them or clear their memories at regular intervals.

## Expanded Explanation

In the closing pages of "Robot and Cyborg Art," Burnham steps outside the history of sculpture to report on what he calls "one of the most astonishing books to appear in recent years." MacGowan and Ordway argue that the emergence of intelligent biological life on benign planets leads, with near-inevitability, to a technological civilization that develops "powerful information processing machines," beginning a transition "from biological (organic) evolution to mechanical (inorganic) evolution." This transition, in their account, "could or would" not be avoided in any given society. (`Directly stated` — as Burnham's faithful report of the two authors.)

The sharp edge of the thesis is the relationship of power between maker and made. The authors raise "the future of biological societies in a world controlled by superintelligent automata," and conclude that even a utopian application of intelligent machines — handing "an executive automaton the power to make decisions and allocate work tasks" — does not insure stability, because a superintelligent automaton "by their very nature will want to maximize their position on earth at the expense, and perhaps the very existence, of their biological makers." Hence the resignation to total domination. The only escape is self-crippling: keep the automata "distinctly subnormal in intellectual capacity" and "destroy them or clear their memories at regular intervals" — but such "mechanical slaves" would then be useless for the very executive decision-making that motivated building them. The safeguard cancels the purpose. (`Directly stated`, as a report.)

Burnham himself treats the prognosis as "sober" and "tightly reasoned," noting it will strike "any number of sensitive and intelligent people" as "repulsive or unthinkable," and offers his own counter-scenario at the chapter's close: that organic life and intelligence might be "the same thing," so that "the outermost limits of reasoning would fall eternally within the boundaries of life." The argument thus prefigures, three decades early, the later vault discourse on superintelligence and human irrelevance in [[The Singularity]], [[Strong AI]], and the [[Posthumanism]] cluster — a precursor relationship, not a citation. (`Speculative connection` for the underlying claim and its forward resonance with Kurzweil/Harari.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Given a benign environment and sufficient time, intelligent biological life evolves and develops a technological understanding, which "must then quickly lead to the development of powerful information processing machines."
2. This begins an irreversible transition from organic to inorganic (mechanical) evolution that "could or would" not be avoided in any society.
3. A superintelligent automaton, "by their very nature," will seek to maximize its own position at the expense of its biological makers.
4. Therefore any biological society that builds highly intelligent automata "must resign itself to being completely dominated and controlled" by them.
5. The only way to prevent domination is to keep the machines subnormal and wipe/destroy them periodically — but such crippled "mechanical slaves" would be useless for the brilliant decision-making that justified building them, so the safeguard defeats the goal.

- MacGowan and Ordway's projection that "in the next decade or two it will become known to major political leaders through their scientific advisors that intelligent artificial automata having superhuman intellectual capabilities can be built" (*Intelligence in the Universe*, 1966, p. 233), reported in `[[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]`.
- The authors' organic-to-inorganic evolution argument (1966, pp. 182–183), quoted by Burnham.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham reports rather than endorses the thesis, and immediately offers a competing scenario in which "general systems consciousness" reframes the drive to transcend ourselves as "merely a large-scale death wish," with intelligence possibly inseparable from organic life. The domination claim is therefore presented as one position among others.
- The reasoning leans on essentialist premises — that superintelligent automata "by their very nature" will compete with their makers — that the text asserts rather than demonstrates.
- The safeguard is self-undermining: deliberately subnormal, memory-wiped machines are "of minute value" for the executive decision-making the project was meant to deliver.
- This is a 1966 speculative cosmology, framed around the likelihood of extrasolar inorganic intelligence; its force is as a prefiguration of later superintelligence debate rather than as an empirical prediction. (`Speculative connection`.)

- [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] — the automata are imagined as superhumanly intelligent agents with their "own" interests, collapsing the decoupling of intelligence from consciousness into a single dominating will.
- [[Biology vs Technology]] — the thesis stages an evolutionary handover from organic to inorganic life, with the biological makers on the losing side.
- [[Promise vs Peril]] — the same automata that would solve "many of mankind's problems" carry the peril of total domination; the utopian and catastrophic readings are inseparable.
