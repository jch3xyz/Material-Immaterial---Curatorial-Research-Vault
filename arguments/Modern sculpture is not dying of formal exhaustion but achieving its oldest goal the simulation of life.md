# Modern sculpture is not dying of formal exhaustion but achieving its oldest goal the simulation of life

## Claim

For Jack Burnham, the apparent "death" of modern sculpture — Herbert Read's diagnosis of its reduction to a "scribble in the air" — is not exhaustion but transformation: sculpture faces "time extinction through the attainment of goals as old as sculpture itself," becoming a preparatory stage toward the re-creation of biological life by nonbiological (technological) means.

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham opens *Beyond Modern Sculpture* against the elegiac reading of postwar three-dimensional art typified by Herbert Read, for whom "extension rather than containment, dynamicism instead of mass" had carried sculpture so far from its original stability that it threatened to become "totally unrelated — both materially and psychically — to the functions of all past sculpture." Burnham accepts the premonition of an ending but rejects its cause. The end is not internal collapse (formal exhaustion) but the fulfillment of a goal sculpture has always pursued: the conferral of life upon inert matter.

On Burnham's account, "the beauty and potency of sculpture was connected with an obsession for the free-standing human form and its life-emitting properties." When sculptors abandoned naturalism and the human figure, they did so not from a desire to destroy the biological replica but from the realization that "if he was serious in his quest to bring inert matter to life anthropomorphism would not accomplish it." The machine — and beyond it the scientific model, cybernetics, and artificial intelligence — becomes the only means by which "man would eventually reconstruct intelligent life, or what might be called life-bearing artifacts." Formalist and vitalist abstraction are thus reframed as "two preparatory tendencies which symbolically anticipate the re-creation of life through nonbiological means." This is a teleological history: sculpture is "a preparatory stage representing steps toward the simulation of biological life."

The argument reframes [[Dematerialization]] from loss to directional metamorphosis. Where Lippard charts the dematerialization of the conceptual-art object, Burnham reads sculpture's flight from solid mass as motion toward [[The Cyborg|cybernetic organism]] and "life-simulating systems through the use of technology" — the trajectory he names from object to system, culminating in "Cyborg Art."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Critics of Read's generation correctly sense that modern sculpture is ending — losing its traditional relation, "both materially and psychically," to all past sculpture.
2. But the cause is misdiagnosed as formal exhaustion (death from within the abstract idiom).
3. Sculpture's oldest aim was always to confer "life-emitting properties" on inert form via the human figure.
4. The figure was abandoned not to destroy the biological replica but because anthropomorphism cannot actually bring matter to life; the machine and scientific model can.
5. Therefore abstraction (formalism, vitalism) is preparatory, not terminal — a rehearsal for re-creating life by nonbiological means.
6. Conclusion: sculpture meets "time extinction through the attainment of goals as old as sculpture itself," passing from object to system to "life-bearing artifacts."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Herbert Read's characterization of postwar sculpture's dematerialization as a "scribble in the air," and his sense that the art could become "totally unrelated" to all past sculpture (Burnham, Introduction).
- The historical claim that for "nearly twenty-five centuries" sculpture's potency was bound to "the free-standing human form and its life-emitting properties" (Introduction).
- The thesis that "formalist and vitalist sculpture represent two preparatory tendencies which symbolically anticipate the re-creation of life through nonbiological means, that is, through technology" (Introduction).
- The book's structural arc from object to system, ending in the final chapter on "Cyborg Art (the art of cybernetic organisms)" and an anticipated "aesthetics of artificial intelligence" — see [[The Cyborg]], [[Cybernetics]], [[Artificial Life]] (Introduction).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is openly teleological — it presupposes that sculpture "always" sought to bring matter to life, a retrospective reading that Read and other critics of his generation do not share (they read the change as decline).
- Burnham hedges the timeline: he concedes that "science cannot indefinitely maintain its exponential rate of growth," and offers alternative futures (a stabilizing ecology in which "the need for totemic artifacts would have disappeared," or a man-precipitated reversion to a "pre-scientific state" in which sculptural images reappear) — so the "extinction" is contingent, not guaranteed.
- The claim depends on equating diverse abstract idioms (biomorphic vitalism, Constructivism, Surrealist and Object sculpture) as a single directional trend toward life-simulation, which flattens their distinct historical motives.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Burnham's sculpture migrates from palpable mass ("the prime attribute of palpable form") toward immaterial systems, light, and cybernetic process, yet remains "slavishly attached to the idea of physical mass"; his object-to-system arc is a primary-source instance of this tension in the sculptural register.
