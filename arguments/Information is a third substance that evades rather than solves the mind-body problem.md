# Information is a third substance that evades rather than solves the mind-body problem

## Claim

By positing information as a third substance that explains both mind and matter, [[John Wheeler]]'s informational cosmology does not resolve the [[The Mind-Body Problem|mind-body problem]] but evades it — a sleight of hand that discards the original dichotomy rather than reconciling it.

## Expanded Explanation

O'Gieblyn traces a circular history. [[Cybernetics]] arose at midcentury, from the "annihilated foundations" left by quantum physics and Gödel's incompleteness theorem, as "an all-encompassing system that would restore the world to its original order and comprehensiveness." Its founding move was to privilege relationship and behavior over content and essence: cybernetics "privileged relationships over content in the first place so that it could explain things like consciousness purely in terms of classical physics, which is limited to describing behavior but not essence—'doing' but not 'being.'" This was the discipline's escape from essence-talk. It is also why [[Information]] became, in O'Gieblyn's phrase, "the ultimate metaphor, an umbrella wide enough to extend across forests and cities, insect colonies and highway systems, computers and human minds" — a single abstraction wide enough to cover every system precisely because it described none of them in their substance. `Directly stated`.

The circle closes with [[John Wheeler]]. Wheeler exploited "a rarely acknowledged problem that lies at the heart of physics: it's uncertain what matter actually is." [[Materialism]] claims that matter is all there is, "and yet, ask a physicist to describe an electron or a quark, and he will speak only of its properties, its position, its behavior—never its essence." Matter has a missing essence — a hole at the center of the material worldview. Wheeler filled that hole with information: he declared that "all things physical are information-theoretic in origin," that "matter itself does not exist," that it "is an illusion that arises from the mathematical structures that undergird everything, a cosmic form of information processing." This is the move O'Gieblyn calls "closing the circle." Cybernetics had invented information *to avoid* talking about essence; Wheeler now offers that same abstraction *as* the missing essence of matter — "proposing that the hole in the material worldview—intrinsic essence—could be explained by information itself." `Directly stated`.

The result is not a solution but a substitution. Descartes' dualism left two substances — *res cogitans* and *res extensa* — and the unanswered question of how they interact. Wheeler's answer "explains, or attempts to explain, how the mind is able to interact with matter" by collapsing both into a single third kind of stuff: for Wheeler, "the mind itself was nothing but information," so that when mind meets world it is merely "code" manipulating "code," "the same sort of mathematical exchange that might take place between two machines." But O'Gieblyn's verdict is that this "is a somewhat evasive solution to the mind-body problem, a sleight of hand that discards the original dichotomy by positing a third substance—information—that can explain both." The dichotomy is not resolved; it is *discarded*. Information does not bridge mind and matter so much as dissolve the question by declaring there were never two things to begin with — only one, conveniently named after the very metaphor cybernetics had built to avoid the metaphysics it now claims to settle. This grounds the O'Gieblyn treatment of [[Information]] and extends [[The Computational Universe]] from Hayles' cosmos-as-computer into Wheeler's stronger, ontological claim that the cosmos *is* information. `Directly stated`.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Materialism claims matter is the sole substance, yet physics can describe matter only by its behavior and properties — "never its essence." There is a hole at the center of the material worldview where intrinsic essence should be.
2. Cybernetics had earlier invented information precisely as a behavioral, relational abstraction — a way to "explain things like consciousness purely in terms of classical physics," describing "'doing' but not 'being.'" Information was designed to *avoid* essence-talk.
3. Wheeler merges information theory with quantum physics and fills materialism's missing essence with this very abstraction — "closing the circle" by making information the essence of matter.
4. This appears to solve the mind-body problem, because if both mind and matter are information, their interaction is just code meeting code.
5. But the solution is evasive: it does not reconcile the Cartesian dichotomy, it discards it by positing a third substance. The problem is dissolved, not answered. `Directly stated` / `Strongly implied`.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- A defender of Wheeler would say this is not evasion but ontological parsimony: if mind and matter are both information, then there never was a genuine dichotomy, and dissolving a pseudo-problem is a legitimate result, not a dodge. O'Gieblyn registers the explanatory ambition — the theory "explains, or attempts to explain, how the mind is able to interact with matter" — before judging it evasive.
- The charge of "sleight of hand" trades on the missing-essence claim about matter; a physicist might reply that demanding "essence" beyond properties and behavior is a metaphysical hangover, not a real gap to be filled. On that view Wheeler answers a confused question rather than evading a real one.
- O'Gieblyn writes as an essayist and self-described "layperson," explicitly avoiding "the speculative fringe of physics"; the verdict is philosophical-critical rather than a technical refutation of informational physics.
- The "third substance" framing is O'Gieblyn's interpretive gloss on Wheeler's program; Wheeler himself would likely reject "substance" language, since for him information is not a third thing alongside mind and matter but the only thing.
