# The computer is not a thing but a set of behaviours and must be made invisible

## Claim

If the project of twentieth-century art and science is to render the invisible visible, then the "currently very visible computer" must itself be made invisible — recognised not as a screen-and-keyboard object, the "apparatus of the accountant," but as pure system and behaviour: a system of systems, a universal transformative matrix whose lingua franca is data.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott's argument turns the period's defining ambition for art — making the invisible visible — back onto the computer that art now uses to pursue it. The computer "as thing — as object, as apparatus, as machine — is too much with us, too dominant"; it is "all too solidly there, a computational block to poetry and imagination." So long as it is encountered as a discrete physical object, it cannot perform its real function, which is not computation but transformation, not storage but dissemination. It is, properly understood, "the agent of the data field, a construct of data space," and what it processes are exactly the processes, systems, forces, and fields that "lie outside our vision." The thing must therefore become invisible so that the behaviour can become apparent.

This is why Ascott reclassifies the computer ontologically: "The computer is not primarily a thing, an object, but a set of behaviours, a system, actually, a system of systems. Data constitute its lingua franca." The category error he attacks is treating the machine as a screen ("the pages of an illuminated book," "an internally lit painting") or as a keyboard that forces "the user into the posture of a clerk." To do so is to mistake the interface for a membrane separating object from self rather than designing it as "a doorway into data space, a synaptic interval in a human-computer symbiosis" (see [[The Interface as Doorway]], [[Data Space]]).

The aesthetic stakes are explicit and absolute. To "use it as no more than the apparatus of the accountant — is to be condemned to produce art of the 'bottom line'; that is to say, an art of finalities, of completion." Such use "deny[s] ... the essential qualities of open-endedness, nonlinearity, and fecund incompleteness that are the great distinguishing potential of computer-mediated art practice." Invisibility, then, is not concealment but immanence: "as we come to see more, we shall see the computer less and less. It will become invisible in its immanence." This is the keystone bridge from Burnham's 1968 object→system thesis ([[Object and System]], [[Systems Esthetics]]) into the telematic era — Ascott extends the dematerialisation of the art object into the dematerialisation of the apparatus itself.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The overarching ambition of both art and science this century is to make the invisible visible — the processes, systems, forces, and fields that elude the senses.
2. The computer is the instrument capable of redescribing exactly these invisible patterns ("The computer deals invisibly with the invisible").
3. But as currently encountered it is "too much with us": a dominant material object, screen and keyboard, "not transparent, ... not understood as pure system."
4. Ontologically, the computer "is not primarily a thing ... but a set of behaviours, a system ... a system of systems" whose medium is data.
5. To treat it as object/screen/keyboard ("the apparatus of the accountant") is to produce "art of the 'bottom line'" — finality and completion — denying open-endedness, nonlinearity, and "fecund incompleteness."
6. Therefore the visible computer must be made invisible — dissolved into system, interface-as-doorway, immanent behaviour — so that the invisible it processes can become visible.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The reframing of the project of art and science as making the invisible visible, applied reflexively to the computer (ch. 18, ch. 20).
- The ontological redefinition: thing/object → "set of behaviours," "system of systems," with "Data ... its lingua franca" (ch. 20).
- The negative examples that fix the computer as a thing: the screen-as-illuminated-book or internally-lit-painting; the keyboard/mouse that reduces the user to a clerk; the "apparatus of the accountant" (ch. 18, ch. 20).
- The cardiac metaphor: the computer "is at the heart of this circulation system, and, like the heart, it works best when it becomes invisible" (ch. 20).
- The closing figure of immanence: "as we come to see more, we shall see the computer less and less. It will become invisible in its immanence" (ch. 18).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is programmatic and prophetic (written ca. 1989–90); the "invisibility" it demands is partly a critique of the then-current GUI/desktop paradigm (the keyboard, the "mouse," "the electronic palette, the light pen") rather than a settled technical proposal. Whether ubiquitous, "invisible" computing realises Ascott's open-endedness or instead intensifies surveillance is left open here — a tension the later vault authors press (cf. [[Digital Panopticon]], [[Dataism]]).
- "Invisible in its immanence" risks naturalising the apparatus: a machine that "we shall see ... less and less" is also one whose operations become harder to audit. Ascott treats invisibility as liberatory; he does not weigh the opacity it produces. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- The disqualification of "art of the 'bottom line'" is a value judgement, not a demonstration; screen-and-keyboard practices are dismissed as "artistically valueless" / "of no artistic value" rather than argued out of contention.
