# There is no natural basis for the unity of women

## Claim

There is nothing about being female that naturally binds women; gender, race, and class consciousness is an achievement forced on us by the terrible historical experience of patriarchy, colonialism, and capitalism, not a natural fact — therefore feminist unity must be built through coalition, not discovered in a shared essence.

## Expanded Explanation

Haraway's anti-essentialist thesis dismantles the founding assumption of much feminist politics: that "woman" names a natural kind whose members share an essence that grounds their solidarity in advance. She denies this twice over. First, there is no natural bond among the female: "There is nothing about being 'female' that naturally binds women." Second, and more radically, there is no stable substrate beneath the bond either — "There is not even such a state as 'being' female, itself a highly complex category constructed in contested sexual scientific discourses and other social practices." The category is an effect of discourse and practice, not a pre-given fact (`Directly stated`).

If unity is not given by nature, where does shared consciousness come from? For Haraway it is forged in suffering: "Gender, race, or class consciousness is an achievement forced on us by the terrible historical experience of the contradictory social realities of patriarchy, colonialism, and capitalism." Consciousness is wrested from history, not read off biology (`Directly stated`). This is the hinge that turns a negative claim (no natural unity) into a positive program: because unity cannot be found, it must be constructed — through "coalition—affinity, not identity." The argument is thus the ground beneath [[Affinity Not Identity]], [[Oppositional Consciousness]], and [[Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity]], the alternative models of political belonging Haraway endorses under [[Fractured Identities]] (`Strongly implied`).

The same move underwrites Haraway's critique of the two dominant feminisms of her moment. Both Marxist/socialist-feminism and Catharine MacKinnon's radical feminism, she argues, refused the modest status of a partial account and instead claimed to be total explanations — each annexing other forms of domination "by analogy, simple listing, or addition." The cost of this totalizing drive was the erasure of difference, most damagingly the "embarrassed silence about race among white radical and socialist-feminists." The denial of a natural matrix of unity is therefore simultaneously an epistemological demand: feminist theory must embrace partiality and "making partial, real connection," because "'Epistemology' is about knowing the difference" (`Directly stated`).

## Reasoning Structure

1. Identities are socially and historically constituted, so gender, race, and class "cannot provide the basis for belief in 'essential' unity."
2. There is no natural bond among women — and not even a stable state of "being" female, the category itself being a discursive construction.
3. The shared consciousness that does exist is an *achievement* "forced on us by the terrible historical experience" of patriarchy, colonialism, and capitalism — produced, not natural.
4. A unity that is constructed rather than natural can only be built deliberately, "on the basis of conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship."
5. Feminisms that instead posited a natural or single ground of unity (Marxist/socialist and MacKinnon's radical feminism) became totalizing, erasing the very differences — above all race — they failed to name.
6. Therefore feminist unity must be a built coalition that embraces partiality and difference, not a found essence.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The direct denial of natural binding and of any "being" female as a stable state — the discursive constitution of the category itself (`02 [Fractured Identities]`).
- The example of Chela Sandoval's "women of color" / oppositional consciousness: an identity built "by conscious appropriation of negation," affirming "no naturalization of the matrix" and acting "only on the basis of conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship" — see [[Oppositional Consciousness]] and [[Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity]] (`02 [Fractured Identities]`).
- The schematic "caricature" of Marxist/socialist-feminism (class // wage labor // alienation; labor → reproduction → sex → race) and radical feminism (gender // sexual appropriation // objectification; sex → labor → reproduction → race), showing both annexing other dominations "by analogy, simple listing, or addition" (`02 [Fractured Identities]`).
- MacKinnon's radical feminism as a "caricature of the appropriating, incorporating, totalizing tendencies of Western theories of identity grounding action," whose totalization achieves "the unity of women" only "by enforcing the experience of and testimony to radical nonbeing" (`02 [Fractured Identities]`).
- The political consequence of totalizing unity: "Embarrassed silence about race among white radical and socialist-feminists was one major, devastating political consequence" (`02 [Fractured Identities]`).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Haraway concedes the *need* the essentialist position answers: "I do not know of any other time in history when there was greater need for political unity." The argument does not dissolve the demand for unity; it relocates it from nature to construction, which is harder, "confusing," and "permanently unclosed."
- She names the opposite danger of her own anti-essentialism: "in the consciousness of our failures, we risk lapsing into boundless difference and giving up on the confusing task of making partial, real connection." Refusing natural unity must not collapse into pure pluralism or relativism.
- MacKinnon's countervailing point is granted as partly correct: Haraway agrees "MacKinnon correctly argues that no Marxian version of identity can firmly ground women's unity" — the dispute is over MacKinnon's even more totalizing remedy, not over the failure of the natural/Marxian ground.
- The argument is itself situated, not universal: Haraway marks her own "historical location in white, professional middle-class, female, radical, North American, mid-adult bodies," and calls her taxonomy "a reinscription of history" — by its own logic it is a partial explanation, not a total one.
