# The mind-uploading problem is the resurrection problem in technical dress

## Claim

The question that puzzled the early Church fathers — whether identity survives when the bodily parts that compose it are consumed, decayed, or wholly replaced — is, by O'Gieblyn's account, *essentially the same problem* now confronting proponents of mind-uploading, and Origen's third-century notion of the *eidos* (a pattern that organizes the flux of matter) is the closest ancient analogue to [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s claim that consciousness is a pattern of information.

## Expanded Explanation

O'Gieblyn does not offer this as a loose analogy but as an identity of problems. The patristic debates over the [[Resurrection]] turned on a question about whether matter is intrinsic to selfhood: if a martyr's flesh has been consumed and digested by an animal (Bynum's "chain consumption argument"), or if a corpse has decayed, can the *same* person be reassembled, or is the reconstituted body merely a new entity? This is structurally the question that the [[Ship of Theseus]] poses, and it is the same question mind-uploading raises when it proposes replacing neurons "one by one with synthetic implants." In both registers the issue is: *to what extent are parts of a body — individual organs or neurons — intrinsic to identity, and will identity survive if those parts are replaced.*

The argument's capstone is Origen. Confronting the same difficulty — the body's matter is never the same for even two days, so which body would God resurrect, the child's or the old man's? — Origen relocated identity away from matter and into a persisting *eidos*, "a kind of Platonic form or plan," which Bynum glosses as "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter." O'Gieblyn's claim is that this *eidos* resembles, "more than anything," Kurzweil's [[Mind as Machine|patternism]]: consciousness conceived as "a pattern of information" that persists while "the actual molecules of water change every millisecond." The river metaphor that Kurzweil borrows from [[Norbert Wiener]] ("we are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves") is, in Origen's hands, already there — "a river is not a bad name for the body."

The convergence matters because it exposes the theological structure inside an ostensibly secular [[Posthumanism|posthumanist]] project. Both Origen and Kurzweil dissolve the threat that material change poses to selfhood by abstracting identity into an immaterial *pattern* — the move [[N. Katherine Hayles]] diagnoses as making "disembodied information" into "the ultimate Platonic Form." The promise of immortality through mind-uploading is, on this reading, [[The Body as Data|the resurrection of the body]] in technical dress: the same wager that a self can be carried, intact, across the destruction of its substrate.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Early Christian theology held body and soul inseparable, so resurrection required the survival of *bodily* identity — raising the problem of how identity persists when the body's matter is consumed (chain consumption) or decays.
2. Aristotle had located identity in the body's organization rather than its matter, yet still denied that a decayed body could be reconstituted as the same person; the Church fathers needed identity to survive precisely that.
3. Their proposed solutions — sprouting wheat, melted-and-recast statues, Tertullian's ship restored with new planks — all sought a *continuity of pattern or structure* across a change of matter.
4. Mind-uploading faces the identical structural problem in its "ship of Theseus" method: neurons replaced one by one, asking whether identity survives the replacement of every part.
5. Therefore the patristic and transhumanist debates are "essentially the same problem."
6. Origen's most refined solution — identity as a persisting *eidos*, "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter" — maps directly onto Kurzweil's patternism (consciousness as "a pattern of information"). The *eidos* "resembles more than anything" Kurzweil's view.
7. Conclusion: mind-uploading reinstates the resurrection problem, and its solution (patternism) reinstates Origen's solution (the *eidos*).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Functionalism is not theology.** O'Gieblyn notes that patternism is "essentially a functionalist account of the mind," and that "most transhumanists insist that this understanding of personal identity is fully compatible with physicalism." On their own terms the *eidos* parallel is a resemblance of structure, not a confession of religious content — a transhumanist could grant the structural identity of the *problem* while denying any continuity of *project*.
- **The continuity-of-identity objection cuts both ways.** Susan Schneider argues that even a perfect functional copy "will be a zombie with no subjective experience," and that "the most mind-uploading will ever be able to achieve is functional similarity to the original." If she is right, mind-uploading does not solve the resurrection problem any better than the Church fathers did — both may be preserving a *pattern* while losing the person.
- **Scope of the identity claim.** O'Gieblyn marks the strongest link cautiously: the *eidos* "resembles more than anything" Kurzweil's patternism — a claim of resemblance, not derivation. There is no assertion that Kurzweil drew on Origen.
- The parallel is O'Gieblyn's framing; the patristic authors and the transhumanists are not in dialogue, and the equivalence is an interpretive bridge she builds across seventeen centuries.
