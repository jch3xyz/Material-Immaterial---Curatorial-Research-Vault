# Urbanization was explosion not implosion

## Claim

Where Lewis Mumford calls the rise of the ancient city "implosion," McLuhan corrects him: urbanization "was really an explosion" — the centralizing, center-to-margin fragmentation of pastoral life advanced by the wheel and the road — because specialism and explicitness are explosion, while genuine implosion is the signature of the electric age and of prehistoric tribal cultures.

## Expanded Explanation

The argument is an explicit terminological dispute, conducted in Chapter 19 ("Wheel, Bicycle, and Airplane") against Lewis Mumford's *Technics and Civilization* and *The City in History*. Mumford had described the gathering of population and supplies into the ancient city as an "implosion" — a drawing-inward, a concentration. McLuhan rejects the word. For him "implosion" names a specific dynamic — contraction, simultaneity, decentralism, the recovery of tribal interplay — that belongs properly to the electric age and, before it, to prehistoric oral cultures. Ancient urbanization runs the opposite way: "Cities were made by the fragmenting of pastoral modes," and "The wheel and the road expressed and advanced this explosion by a radiational or center-margin pattern." The city is therefore not a contraction but a centrifugal radiation of specialized functions outward from a citadel core to its accessible margins (`Directly stated`, Ch. 19).

The hinge of the dispute is McLuhan's equation of *specialism* with *explosion*. Quoting Lyman Bryson — "technology is explicitness" — he reasons that "explicitness, or specialist extension of functions, is centralism and explosion of functions, and not implosion, contraction or simultaneity." The urban center forms precisely by separating and fragmenting human functions in space and architecture: "Acceleration created larger and larger centers more and more specialism." Power radiates from the citadel along road and wheel — and, with the alphabet, along written record — to the margins of empire. This is "the direct force, outside and external, to which men do not necessarily submit their minds." It is the antithesis of the implosive: implosion "is the spell and incantation of the tribe and the family, to which men readily submit." To build the city at all, "men must explode the inner unity of their beings into explicit fragments" — the very opposite of the tribal integral being.

McLuhan thus stakes out a clean reservation of terms. "It is too bad that Mr. Mumford has chosen the term 'implosion' for the urban specialist explosion. 'Implosion' belongs to the electronic age. as it belonged to the prehistoric cultures. All primitive societies are implosive, like the spoken word." The word is double-anchored: implosion describes both the acoustic, simultaneous, all-at-once field of the spoken word in tribal life *and* the contracting electric field of the present. Between those two implosive poles lies the long mechanical "explosion" — three thousand years of wheel, road, alphabet, and print radiating power from centers to margins. Only "Today, in an age of implosion, we are playing the ancient explosion backward, as on a film" — the same reversal image McLuhan uses throughout Part II. The dispute with Mumford is therefore not a quibble but a defense of the master schema: misnaming ancient urban concentration as "implosion" would collapse the very distinction (center-margin explosion versus decentralized electric implosion) on which the book turns.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Mumford calls the rise of the ancient city "implosion" — a concentration, a drawing-inward.
2. But the city is built by *specialism*: "Cities were made by the fragmenting of pastoral modes," and acceleration "created larger and larger centers more and more specialism."
3. Specialism is explosion: "technology is explicitness" (Bryson), and "explicitness, or specialist extension of functions, is centralism and explosion of functions, and not implosion, contraction or simultaneity."
4. The wheel and road carry this explosion outward "by a radiational or center-margin pattern"; "Centralism depends on margins that are accessible by road and wheel." This is centrifugal, not implosive.
5. True implosion is contraction, simultaneity, and tribal interplay — "the spell and incantation of the tribe and the family" — and belongs to two ages only: the prehistoric/oral ("All primitive societies are implosive, like the spoken word") and the electric ("'Implosion' belongs to the electronic age").
6. Therefore Mumford has misnamed the dynamic: ancient urbanization "was really an explosion," not an implosion.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The dispute is partly a *terminological territory-claim* rather than a self-evident physical distinction. Mumford and McLuhan may be tracking the same phenomenon — the dense gathering of people into cities — and disagreeing only about which metaphor (inward concentration vs. outward radiation) best captures it.
- McLuhan's own schema strains the binary: he concedes that even "a merely mechanical form" like the typewriter "acted in some respects as an implosion," so the mapping of ancient-mechanical = explosion is not airtight, and a defender of Mumford could argue that the citadel's gravitational pull of population *is* an implosive moment within the larger explosion.
- The argument leans on McLuhan's pattern-recognition (radiational center-margin form, the spoken word as implosive) rather than on demonstrated empirical or archaeological measure; it reads the historical record analogically.
- McLuhan does not engage Mumford's substantive critique of urban "death-oriented myths" — which he actually quotes approvingly — but only his choice of the single word "implosion," leaving the deeper Mumford argument about urban power untouched.
