# The science of tracking free-roaming animals became the template for tracking human beings

## Claim

The telemetry science developed in the mid-twentieth century to render unrestrained animals as data outside their awareness was, in a metamorphosis its inventor never foresaw, refurbished by surveillance capitalists into the template for tracking human beings—"now we are the animals."

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff dates the genealogy of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] not to Silicon Valley but to a 1964 expedition to the Galapagos Islands, where the physicist-engineer-biologist R. Stuart MacKay pioneered biomedical telemetry: sensors so compact they could "disappear into the body without triggering the animal's awareness." The scientific problem MacKay solved was the obstinacy of free-roaming creatures—elk, sea turtles, geese—who could not be studied in a zoo without destroying the very behavior under observation. Telemetry let scientists render animals as information "even when they believed themselves to be free." This is the structural ancestor of [[Rendition]]: the conversion of living behavior into data flows that operate outside the subject's awareness. (`Directly stated`)

The argument's force is in MacKay's two-part design and its later corporate inheritance. MacKay insisted that monitoring was only half the story—"the route was not enough; it had to be the routing"—and proposed a "reverse process" of telestimulation that would not merely observe but modify and optimize behavior. Zuboff reads this directly into the present: MacKay's framework anticipates the [[Economies of Scope]] (rendering whole populations and the details of individuals, reaching "inaccessible regions") and his telestimulation is "resurrected in the [[Economies of Action]]," now aimed "not to save the human herd from catastrophe but rather to heighten the predictability of its behavior." The mechanism MacKay built to know animals is, in [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]], the mechanism by which surveillance capital shapes people. (`Directly stated`)

The political sting is the inversion of freedom. MacKay's animals were "innately uncooperative because they felt themselves to be free." Zuboff transposes the predicate onto us: "the un-self-conscious, easy freedom enjoyed by the human animal—the sense of being unrestrained that thrives in the mystery of distant places and intimate spaces—is simply friction on the path toward surveillance revenues." The felt experience of being free is no longer the dignity of a subject but an obstacle to a revenue stream—the same reduction by which industrial capitalism treated nature as raw material, now applied to human nature. What had been an instrument of disinterested discovery becomes, dressed as [[Inevitabilism]], "an inevitable feature of twenty-first-century life on Earth." (`Directly stated`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Free-roaming animals resist study precisely because, feeling free, they behave naturally; locking them up destroys the behavior to be observed.
2. MacKay's telemetry solved this by rendering animals as data through sensors that operate *outside the animal's awareness*, even at population scale and in inaccessible regions.
3. MacKay's design was never merely observational: "the reverse process" of telestimulation aimed to modify and optimize behavior—a "remote dialogue" between subject and experimenter.
4. Surveillance capitalists refurbished this exact architecture—rendition outside awareness ([[Rendition]]), population-plus-individual scope ([[Economies of Scope]]), behavior-shaping ([[Economies of Action]])—and applied it to humans.
5. Therefore the same logic now treats the human animal's felt freedom as mere friction: "now we are the animals," and surveillance is presented as inevitable.

- MacKay's "reverse process" of telestimulation, "a remote dialogue between the subject and the experimenter," read as the ancestor of behavior modification.
- The 2014 University of Washington "super GPS" assembled from municipal cameras to visualize humans walking the streets — Zuboff's marker that "the threshold has already been crossed."
- The auto-insurance telematics chapter: drivers tracked and behaviorally shaped "as MacKay advised, outside the driver's awareness while she still thinks that she is free."
- IBM's "dark data" rhetoric, with its "echoes of MacKay" and his "determination to penetrate the secrets of unrestrained animals and inaccessible regions."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The continuity is partly analogical: Zuboff narrates a genealogy ("if this were a novel … MacKay's brilliant work … would serve as foreshadowing"), and the historical *causal* links from animal telemetry to Google-era surveillance are asserted by resemblance and intellectual descent (MacKay → Paradiso → Weiser → Sidewalk Labs) more than by a documented chain of transmission.
- MacKay's own stated purpose was "enhancing and protecting the well-being of animal populations"; the transformation into a logic of accumulation is, Zuboff concedes, "a metamorphosis that MacKay did not foresee"—so the template thesis indicts the refurbishers, not the original science.
- A critic might argue the "now we are the animals" framing is rhetorical leverage rather than a precise claim; the disanalogy is that humans, unlike tortoises, can in principle become aware and contest the rendition—which is the whole stake of Zuboff's call to "name" the phenomenon.
