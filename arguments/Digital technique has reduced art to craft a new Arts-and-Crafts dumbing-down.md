# Digital technique has reduced art to craft a new Arts-and-Crafts dumbing-down

## Claim

The impact of technology — especially digital and communications technology — has in many cases reduced art to a form of craft, in which polished technique, skilful programming, and dazzling special effects have come to replace the creation of meaning and values; Ascott reads this as a resonance with William Morris's nineteenth-century Arts and Crafts movement, where the same dumbing-down from art to craft occurred once the authoring of technique took primacy over the authoring of ideas.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott's polemic appears as the negative counter-image to his own program for a [[New Media Art]] grounded in consciousness rather than execution. Having argued that "the very provenance of art in the twentieth century leads, through its psychic, spiritual, and conceptual aspirations, towards this technoetic condition," he warns that much art made *with* technology has gone the opposite way: it mistakes facility for art. Where the work's center of gravity shifts from "the creation of meaning and values" to "polished technique or skilful programming, leading to dazzling special effects," the medium's virtuosity becomes an end in itself, and the art empties out. This is the failure mode of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] when the technology is foregrounded and the experience hollowed.

The historical analogy is exact and deliberate. Ascott invokes William Morris's Arts and Crafts movement not as praise but as a cautionary precedent: there too "the authoring of technique took primacy over the authoring of ideas," and there too the result was a "pandering to the luxury market covered by a veneer of social conscience." The recurrence is the point — digital art risks becoming "computer arts and crafts," repeating Morris's regression a century later in a new material register. The opposition is therefore not technique-versus-no-technique (Ascott is no technophobe) but technique-as-authorship versus idea-as-authorship: skill in the service of meaning, or skill as a substitute for it.

This makes the argument a clarifying limit on the rest of Ascott's optimism. The same digital and telematic apparatus that he elsewhere celebrates as the carrier of a planetary consciousness can, in the wrong hands, collapse into mere effect. The diagnosis aligns Ascott with the broader vault suspicion that the digital is read as neutral or empty when it is in fact ideologically loaded (the "ontology of vacancy"), and with Burnham's worry that the ultra-rational ethos of technics "reduces every art form to technique."

## Reasoning Structure

1. The proper end of art is the creation of meaning and values (for Ascott, ultimately the authoring of consciousness — the technoetic condition).
2. Digital and communications technology offer powerful means: polished technique, skilful programming, dazzling special effects.
3. In many cases these means displace the end: technique and effect come to *replace* the creation of meaning and values rather than serve it.
4. Historical precedent: in Morris's Arts and Crafts movement the same displacement occurred — the authoring of technique took primacy over the authoring of ideas, producing a dumbing-down from art to craft (and a pandering to the luxury market under a veneer of social conscience).
5. Therefore digital art that foregrounds technique over idea repeats this regression — it is "computer arts and crafts," a dumbing-down from art to craft.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- "Technique" and "idea" are not cleanly separable: in much new-media practice the programming *is* the conceptual work, so "polished technique" need not be opposed to "the authoring of ideas." Ascott's binary risks devaluing material/computational craft as such.
- The phrase "in many cases" makes the claim a tendency, not a law; it does not specify a criterion for telling meaningful technical art from mere effect, leaving the judgment to taste.
- The Morris analogy is asserted by resonance ("springs to mind"), not argued historically; whether the parallel holds — and whether Arts and Crafts was in fact a "dumbing down" — is contestable.
- It sits in tension with Ascott's own enthusiasm for the digital and telematic apparatus elsewhere in the book; the same tools he warns can hollow art are the ones he credits with raising consciousness.

## Tensions

- [[Art vs Technics]] — this argument is Ascott's instance of Burnham's worry that the "ultra-rational, self-augmenting" ethos of technics "reduces every art form to technique"; the craft-art he describes is technics having won out over art.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the digital "special effects" pole is the immaterial/processual medium being mistaken for the work; meaning and values are the immaterial *content* it should carry, not the slickness of its surface.
