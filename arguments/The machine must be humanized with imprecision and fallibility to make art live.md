# The machine must be humanized with imprecision and fallibility to make art live

## Claim

Because the determinate machine — the [[The Closed Kinematic Chain|closed kinematic chain]] — forbids the psychic involvement and not-always-rational order that art demands, the [[Kinetic Art|kinetic]] artist must inject imprecision, fallibility, even self-destruction so the machine can "begin to live, in doubt and indecision, as human beings do."

## Expanded Explanation

The chapter "Kineticism: The Unrequited Art" turns on a contradiction Jack Burnham states flatly: the machine is "a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies," built up by progressively *reducing* the freedom between its parts, and so it is constitutively hostile to art. Art, Burnham writes, "demanded a certain degree of psychic involvement, an autonomous capitulation to a not always rational visual order-and the machine, as Reuleaux made clear, could not allow that to happen." A determinate, frictionless mechanism can deliver controlled motion and power, but not the doubt, ambiguity, and irrationality that make a thing feel alive. This is why an "art of machines" struck the century as "a self-contradiction, like an 'art of advertising' or an 'art of scientific, optics.'" `Directly stated`

The argument's resolution is that the machine must be *humanized*. Burnham's keystone formulation, on Duchamp: "Yet to function in actuality-and artistically-it had to be injected with elements of imprecision and irrationality. Then, perhaps, it could begin to live, in doubt and indecision, as human beings do." To live, in Burnham's account, is to be uncertain and fallible; precision is precisely what must be undone. The kinetic artist therefore works *against* the perfected machine — diverting "the essential restrictive nature of the machine," seeking "to allow the machine new degrees of freedom—even, on occasion, by destroying it." Three moves recur: humanize (Tinguely's "joyous machine," "one that is, in fact, free"; Duchamp's stipulation that mechanisms be "tamed or humanized"), randomize (the machine "primarily as a power source and as a 'randomizing' device"), and destroy (Man Ray's *Destructible Object*, Tinguely's self-annihilating spectacles). `Directly stated`

Burnham's fullest gloss on what "human" means here strips it of anthropomorphic precision and reattaches it to vulnerability: "A precise definition of 'human' is illusive. It is not an extension of that anthropomorphic precision which characterizes the automata collection at Neuchatel. Rather to be 'human' is to expose oneself through animal vulnerability and fallibility." Tinguely, "without the advantages of cybernetics," "has come closest to 'humanizing' the machine" — not by making it more capable, but by making it break, falter, and fail. The argument thus inverts the engineer's value system: the determinate, defect-free machine is dead; only the imprecise, fallible, mortal machine can live. `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. The machine is, by definition (Reuleaux), "a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies" — a determinate [[The Closed Kinematic Chain|closed kinematic chain]] developed by reducing the freedom between its elements.
2. Art "demanded a certain degree of psychic involvement, an autonomous capitulation to a not always rational visual order," which "the machine... could not allow."
3. Therefore a straightforwardly determinate machine cannot make living art; an "art of machines" appears a self-contradiction.
4. To "function in actuality-and artistically-it had to be injected with elements of imprecision and irrationality."
5. So humanized — given imprecision, fallibility, "doubt and indecision," even self-destruction — the machine "could begin to live... as human beings do." To be "human" here is "to expose oneself through animal vulnerability and fallibility."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- **Tinguely's "joyous machine."** "If you respect the machine, if you enter into a game with the machine, then perhaps you can make a truly joyous machine-by joyous, I mean free" — the chapter's epigraph, and Burnham's emblem of humanizing the machine by granting it freedom. - **Tinguely's "cripples."** "Tinguely created many of his anthropomorphic constructions with the thought in mind that they would not be smoothly functioning machines but cripples, or machines with a physical flaw" — built-in defects, "a self-augmented malfunction... leading to sure breakdown." Fallibility designed in.
- **Tinguely as the closest "humanizer."** "Without the advantages of cybernetics Tinguely has come closest to 'humanizing' the machine," where to be "human" is "to expose oneself through animal vulnerability and fallibility."
- **Duchamp's "unartistic."** Duchamp "did not believe in the use of mechanics in art... they were 'unartistic'"; he stipulated "that the artist must establish a relationship with his mechanisms whereby they are tamed or humanized." His withdrawal at "the brink of raw technology" — choosing to "draw back" rather than "plunge into a rational world of impersonally controlled effects" — dramatizes the same diagnosis.
- **Man Ray's *Destructible Object*.** Listed among the parodies of machines — "Man Ray's metronome entitled Destructible Object" — an instance of the destroy-the-machine move that grants it "new degrees of freedom."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Self-defeat / Luddism.** If the only ways to make the machine artistic are to corrupt, randomize, or destroy it, the kinetic project may be self-contradictory at its root: it cannot succeed *as machine art*. Burnham himself treats Tinguely's self-destruction elsewhere as "only a Luddite solution," and pronounces the kinetic revival "with very few exceptions a failure."
- **Determinism merely relocated.** Burnham's own escape route — from the [[The Closed Kinematic Chain|closed kinematic chain]] to open, self-organizing [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] systems — raises the question whether the move from imprecision-as-life to feedback-as-life escapes determinism or only reinstalls it inside the program.
- **"Human" defined by deficiency.** Equating the human with "animal vulnerability and fallibility" makes *malfunction* the criterion of life; a critic could object that this romanticizes failure and confuses the look of life with life.
- **Scope.** The claim is anchored in pre-cybernetic Kineticism (Tinguely, Duchamp, Man Ray); Burnham's later chapters argue that genuine life-simulation requires not imprecision but cybernetic [[Object and System|system]] — so the "humanize by imprecision" thesis is, in his arc, a transitional rather than final answer.

## Tensions

- [[Mechanism vs Vitalism]] — the argument is a primary instance: the determinate mechanism must be infused with the appearance of vital indeterminacy to read as alive; precision (mechanism) is set against fallible aliveness (a vitalist sensibility) on aesthetic, not factual, grounds.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — humanizing the machine through "doubt and indecision" pulls the work away from the inert, determinate object toward something whose value lies in unpredictable behavior, on the vault's keystone axis. The same chapter's New Tendency makes "immateriality" its rallying cry.
