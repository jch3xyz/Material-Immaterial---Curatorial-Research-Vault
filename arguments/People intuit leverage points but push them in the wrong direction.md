# People intuit leverage points but push them in the wrong direction

## Claim

People deeply involved in a system usually know, intuitively and accurately, where its [[Leverage Points|leverage points]] lie — but more often than not they push the change in the wrong direction, so that a correct instinct about *where* to intervene is coupled to a reliable error about *how*, and the intervention systematically worsens the problem it was meant to solve. *(Directly stated.)*

## Expanded Explanation

Meadows opens Chapter 6 by crediting ordinary intuition with far more accuracy than a folk-view of expertise would grant it. Reporting Jay Forrester's experience of "years of working with corporations on their systems problems," she notes that "the average manager can define the current problem very cogently, identify the system structure that leads to the problem, and guess with great accuracy where to look for leverage points—places in the system where a small change could lead to a large shift in behavior." The wish for such points is old and universal — "it’s embedded in legend: the silver bullet; the trimtab; the miracle cure; the secret passage; the magic password" — and the wish is not naive: leverage points are real, and "leverage points are points of power." The failure is not in locating them. It is in the sign of the push. "But Forrester goes on to point out that although people deeply involved in a system often know intuitively where to find leverage points, more often than not they push the change in the wrong direction."

The empirical spine of the claim is Forrester's modeling. Asked by the Club of Rome to show how poverty, hunger, environmental destruction, resource depletion, urban decay, and unemployment interrelate, "Forrester made a computer model and came out with a clear leverage point: growth." The world's leaders had already found this point — "The world’s leaders are correctly fixated on economic growth as the answer to virtually all problems" — which is exactly the intuition being praised. But the model showed the fix inverted: growth "has costs as well as benefits, and we typically don’t count the costs—among which are poverty and hunger, environmental destruction, and so on—the whole list of problems we are trying to solve with growth!" So the leaders are right about the lever and wrong about the direction: "they’re pushing with all their might in the wrong direction," when "what is needed is much slower growth, very different kinds of growth, and in some cases no growth or negative growth." Forrester's earlier *Urban Dynamics* (1969) is the sharper illustration, because there the correct direction offends common sense outright: the model "demonstrated that subsidized low-income housing is a leverage point," but that "the less of it there is, the better off the city is—even the low-income folks in the city." Published when national policy dictated massive housing projects, the finding was derided; "since then, many of those projects have been torn down in city after city." Meadows lets Forrester's word carry the moral: "Counterintuitive—that’s Forrester’s word to describe complex systems," which she immediately generalizes into the argument's headline: "Leverage points frequently are not intuitive. Or if they are, we too often use them backward, systematically worsening whatever problems we are trying to solve."

This is the general leverage-direction claim of which the Chapter 2 inventory model is the specific, mechanically worked case (see [[Acting faster on a delayed loop worsens oscillation high leverage wrong direction]]): there a car dealer trying to damp oscillations reaches for a genuinely strong lever — her reaction delay — and shortens it, amplifying the very oscillations she meant to stop, before the correction turns out to be *lengthening* the same delay. Chapter 6 states as a system-wide regularity what that episode proves in miniature. It also seeds Meadows's constructive turn: because our directional intuitions are so unreliable, she offers a whole ordered list of leverage points and, crucially, warns that the higher a lever's power, the more resistant a system will be to having it moved — so the argument is not a counsel of despair but a case for learning the counterintuitive discipline of systems thinking (see [[Systems Thinking]]) over trusting the reflex to push harder in the familiar direction.

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise (people locate leverage points well).** Managers "guess with great accuracy where to look for leverage points"; those deeply involved in a system "often know intuitively where to find" them. *(Directly stated.)*
2. **Premise (the levers are genuinely powerful).** "Leverage points are points of power" — a small change there "could lead to a large shift in behavior." *(Directly stated.)*
3. **Observation (the direction is reliably inverted).** "More often than not they push the change in the wrong direction"; if the points are intuitive at all, "we too often use them backward." *(Directly stated.)*
4. **Evidence A (economic growth).** Leaders are "correctly fixated on economic growth" (right lever) but "pushing with all their might in the wrong direction," since growth generates the very costs it is meant to cure; the fix is slower, different, or negative growth. *(Directly stated.)*
5. **Evidence B (subsidized housing).** *Urban Dynamics* found subsidized low-income housing a leverage point whose *reduction* helps the city — a correct lever whose beneficial direction is the opposite of the policy consensus, later vindicated by demolitions. *(Directly stated.)*
6. **Conclusion (empirical generalization).** Complex systems are "counterintuitive"; leverage points, even when correctly identified, are typically used backward, "systematically worsening whatever problems we are trying to solve." *(Directly stated.)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim is an empirical generalization ("more often than not," "frequently," "too often"), not a law; Meadows does not quantify the frequency, and it rests on Forrester's modeling judgment, so a skeptic could contest whether the models' "correct" directions were truly correct or merely artifacts of their assumptions.
- Forrester's housing and growth conclusions were and remain politically contested; Meadows presents the derision-then-vindication arc as confirmation, but that narrative is itself an interpretation of a contested policy history.
- The argument identifies the error (wrong direction) without yet supplying a method to avoid it — Meadows concedes "no quick or easy formulas for finding leverage points," so the diagnosis outruns the cure.
