# Inevitability is an illusion and resistance is possible

## Claim

Technological inevitability is a manufactured ideology — an "existential narcotic prescribed to induce resignation" — not a law of history; because capitalism is plastic and public opinion eventually hardens into durable law, the future of surveillance capitalism remains contingent and open to human refusal. (Directly stated.)

## Expanded Explanation

The closing argument of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* is a direct assault on the rhetoric of inevitability that the book elsewhere diagnoses as a tool of dispossession (see [[Inevitabilism]]). Where the inevitability thesis presents surveillance capitalism's expansion as the unstoppable unfolding of technology itself, Zuboff insists that this is "the mantra on which we are trained," a narcotic of resignation rather than a description of reality. The function of the rhetoric is to produce passivity; naming it as rhetoric — rather than fact — is the first step toward agency. (Directly stated.)

The positive case for openness rests on three supports. First, **historical plasticity**: capitalism "has survived the longue durée less because of any specific capability and more because of its plasticity," periodically renewing its roots in the social. Zuboff marshals Thomas Piketty ("There is no single variety of capitalism") and the Harvard philosopher Roberto Unger (market forms "can take any number of distinct legal and institutional directions") to show that no particular form of capitalism — least of all the surveillance form — is mandated by history. (Directly stated.)

Second, **public opinion drives durable law**: from Milton Friedman and Friedrich Hayek, Zuboff takes the lesson that "legislative and judicial action invariably reflect the public opinion of twenty to thirty years earlier," and that "even the most destructive 'ages' do not last forever." Healthy democratic institutions can shift the tide of opinion against new oppressions, "with legislation and jurisprudence eventually to follow." Third, **the faculty of freedom**: George Orwell's 1946 evisceration of James Burnham, and Hannah Arendt's claim that "every beginning, seen from the perspective of the framework that it interrupts, is a miracle," together establish that to predict mere continuation of the present is a "worship of power... not fully separable from cowardice," while the human capacity to begin anew is "the source of all freedom." (Directly stated.) This is the conviction behind the book's program, [[Be the Friction]].

> Note: the James Burnham whom Orwell eviscerates here is **James Burnham, the political theorist** and author of *The Managerial Revolution* (1940) — NOT the art critic Jack Burnham (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*) who appears elsewhere in this vault. They are unrelated; James Burnham is rendered as plain text throughout.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The claim that surveillance capitalism is inevitable is an ideology of resignation ("an existential narcotic"), not a fact about technology or history.
2. Capitalism is plastic: it has survived by taking many different forms, so no single form — including the surveillance form — is historically mandated (Piketty, Unger).
3. In a democracy, public opinion eventually hardens into durable law and jurisprudence (Friedman's twenty-to-thirty-year cycle); therefore destructive "ages" can be ended once people refuse them.
4. To predict merely "a continuation of the thing that is happening" is, as Orwell showed against James Burnham, a failure of moral effort rooted in "cowardice and... the worship of power."
5. The human "faculty of freedom" — Arendt's "sheer capacity to begin" — means every new beginning is possible; the present is not foreordained.
6. Therefore inevitability is an illusion, resistance is both warranted and possible, and "we are at the beginning of this story, not the end."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The naming of inevitability as "an existential narcotic prescribed to induce resignation: a snuff dream of the spirit" (Ch. 18, §IV).
- Capitalism's "plasticity" across the longue durée; Piketty ("There is no single variety of capitalism") and Roberto Unger (market forms "can take any number of distinct legal and institutional directions") (Ch. 18, §VI).
- Friedman and Hayek on the primacy of public opinion: "the primary thing is to change opinion"; legislation reflects "the public opinion of twenty to thirty years earlier" (Ch. 18, §VI).
- Orwell's 1946 review of James Burnham's *The Managerial Revolution*: Burnham "is predicting a continuation of the thing that is happening... a major mental disease... cowardice and... the worship of power" (Ch. 18, §VI).
- Hannah Arendt on the "faculty of freedom itself, the sheer capacity to begin," and on every beginning as "a miracle" (Ch. 18, §VI).
- Tom Paine on each generation's renewed right to be free; the fall of the Berlin Wall ("the people of East Berlin said, 'No more!'") as proof that refusal ends regimes (Ch. 18, §VI).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Zuboff concedes that "surveillance capitalism and its instrumentarian power appear to be invincible," and documents a "democratic recession" and weakening attachment to democracy (the 2017 Pew survey) — the very conditions that make resistance hard. Her argument is therefore a wager on democracy, not a guarantee. (Directly stated.)
- The public-opinion mechanism she leans on is the same one that Friedman and Hayek used to entrench neoliberalism; the cycle that can dismantle surveillance capitalism is the one that built it. The argument supplies a method, not an outcome.
- The "faculty of freedom" and "the miracle of beginning" are normative-existential commitments (via Arendt) rather than empirical predictions; a critic could read them as exhortation rather than evidence.
- The historical plasticity of capitalism cuts both ways: plasticity is also how capitalism absorbs and survives challenges, so a renewed capitalism need not be a more humane one.
