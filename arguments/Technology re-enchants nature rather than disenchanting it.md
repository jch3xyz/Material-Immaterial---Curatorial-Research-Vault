# Technology re-enchants nature rather than disenchanting it

## Claim

Against the modern disenchantment thesis — the Weberian story in which scientific-technical rationalization strips the world of mystery, soul, and spirit — Ascott argues the opposite movement: technology *re-enchants* nature, supplying the tools and insights to recognise its sentience and to see how intelligence and consciousness pervade its every living part, so that the emergent moist domain is not merely instrumental but "spiritually numinous."

## Expanded Explanation

In the "Moist Manifesto" essay (ch. 31, "Art @ the Edge of the Net," 2000), Ascott describes a convergence of the digital, the biological, and the spiritual into a third, "moist" domain. The decisive ontological move is relational: nature ceases to be an external object held at a contemplative distance. "We recognise that nature is no longer to be thought of as over there, to be viewed in the middle distance, benign or threatening as contingency dictates." Technology is the instrument of this re-seeing — not what desacralizes nature but what lets us "see more deeply into its richness and fecundity, and above all to recognise its sentience" (Directly stated).

The enchantment is therefore not a residue technology has yet to dissolve but something technology actively *discloses*: intelligence and "indeed, consciousness, pervade[s] its every living part." This is a panpsychist register — consciousness as a pervasive feature of the living world rather than a late, local emergent of the human brain — and it is what underwrites the manifesto's signature definition: "MOIST ART is digitally dry, biologically wet, and spiritually numinous" (Directly stated). The third predicate, *spiritually numinous*, is doing the anti-disenchantment work: a thoroughly technological art is here also a sacred, mind-saturated one. Ascott names the combined faculty "technoetic" — "techne and gnōsis combined into a new knowledge of the world" — fusing the technical and the spiritual-cognitive into a single category rather than opposing them.

The word "re-enchantment" is not used in the chapter; Ascott does not frame his claim explicitly against Weber's "disenchantment of the world." But the structure of the argument — technology as the agency that restores soul, sentience, and the numinous to a nature previously held at instrumental distance — is precisely the re-enchantment thesis that O'Gieblyn names and debates from the skeptical side. The bridge to [[Re-enchantment]] and [[Disenchantment]] is therefore Strongly implied, not stated by the author (Strongly implied).

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise: Under the modern (disenchantment) picture, nature is "over there" — an inert, contingent object to be viewed, used, mistreated, feared, or pitied at a distance.
2. Premise: New technology (the Net, artificial life, nano- and bio-engineering, the convergence into moistmedia) gives us tools and insights to see "more deeply" into nature.
3. Premise: What that deeper seeing reveals is not mechanism but *sentience* — intelligence and consciousness pervading nature's every living part (a panpsychist disclosure).
4. Premise: The domain so revealed is not merely useful but "spiritually numinous"; the new knowledge is "technoetic," fusing techne and gnōsis.
5. Conclusion: Therefore technology re-enchants rather than disenchants — it is the very agency by which the living world is restored as ensouled, conscious, and sacred.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The "over there" reversal: nature is "no longer to be thought of as over there, to be viewed in the middle distance" and "no longer to be seen as victim or ecology" — see ch. 31.
- The sentience/consciousness disclosure: technology lets us "recognise its sentience and to understand how intelligence, indeed, consciousness, pervades its every living part" — the central evidentiary sentence (Directly stated).
- The numinous predicate: "MOIST ART is digitally dry, biologically wet, and spiritually numinous" — the manifesto verse fixes spirit as a co-equal term with the digital and the biological (Directly stated).
- The "technoetic" coinage: "techne and gnōsis combined into a new knowledge of the world" — the methodological warrant that technical knowing and spiritual/gnostic knowing are one.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- O'Gieblyn's caution: the re-enchantment that technology offers may be a *projection* — the mind, having drained meaning from the world, reads its own pattern back into the machine and the cosmos. Ascott's "consciousness pervades its every living part" is asserted as disclosure but is closer to a metaphysical commitment ([[Panpsychism]]) than a finding; whether technology *reveals* sentience or *attributes* it is exactly the contested point of [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]].
- The argument inherits the standard objection to [[Panpsychism]]: ascribing consciousness to "every living part" lacks an account of how, or whether, such micro-experience combines into the unified sentience Ascott invokes.
- Genre limitation: this is a visionary manifesto, not an empirical or analytic case. The disenchantment thesis it overturns (Weber) is never named or engaged on its own terms, so the reversal is asserted programmatically rather than argued against the strongest version of disenchantment.
- "Re-enchantment" is the system's label for the move, not Ascott's word; the bridge to the O'Gieblyn vocabulary is interpretive (Strongly implied), and should not be read as Ascott citing or answering Weber.
