# Without a religious framework human worth collapses into economic logic

## Claim

When Covid-19 forced American public discourse to put a number on a human life, the secular op-eds defending shutdown all began with "some vague appeal to the intrinsic value of human life" and then "quickly devolv[ed]" into cost-benefit math — a maneuver that, far from rescuing intrinsic worth, "only confirmed, in the end, the opposing view that human life was reducible to economic logic"; the lone defense of priceless life that did not collapse came from outside the secular frame entirely — Russell Moore's *imago dei* — making the pandemic the live test case for [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]'s decade-long question of "how human life can have value outside a religious framework."

## Expanded Explanation

This argument is the theological payoff of the [[Virality]] chapter's reading of the early pandemic. O'Gieblyn has already established that the algorithmic stock market behaved as a transcendent, indifferent deity — it "resided like the nominalist God in some transcendent realm, following its own whims, its own logic, with little concern for us," yet "was often appealed to as a force that we had to appease — one that required the sacrifice of human life." Against that demand for sacrifice, the shutdown's defenders had to argue that the human life saved was worth more than the economic activity lost. The trouble, for O'Gieblyn, is the *form* their defense took. The op-eds "all seemed to follow the same formula, beginning with some vague appeal to the intrinsic value of human life and then quickly devolving into profitability algorithms and affordability assessments." By trying to win on "both moral and economic fronts," they conceded the very ground they meant to defend: once you agree to weigh a life on the economic scale at all, you have admitted it belongs there. The tactic "only confirmed, in the end, the opposing view that human life was reducible to economic logic."

The trend "reached its logical end" in a Paul Krugman op-ed that "flatly debunked the truism that human life was 'priceless,'" noting that "the statistical cost of life was calculated all the time in transportation and environmental policy" and came to "roughly $10 million"; *Forbes* got "more granular," contrasting the value of a statistical life (VSL) with quality-adjusted life years (QALYs) "to arrive at a more precise figure." This is the pandemic as proof-of-concept for the book's larger anxiety. O'Gieblyn frames herself explicitly as the test subject: "As someone who has spent the past decade trying to understand how human life can have value outside a religious framework, I was dismayed to find that the most cogent and unqualified argument for shutdown was made by Russell Moore, of the Southern Baptist Convention." Moore's case is not cost-benefit at all; it is *imago dei*: humans "were made in the image of God," and "any attempt to weigh the economy against public health will 'turn human lives into checkmarks on a page rather than the sacred mystery they are.'" The dismay is the argument. A secular survey of the available defenses of priceless life finds that the only one that does not collapse into accounting is the one grounded in [[Imago Dei]] — the framework O'Gieblyn, an ex-fundamentalist, has spent a decade trying to do without.

The stakes are sharpened by the chapter's countervailing motif, [[The Human as Error]]: the recasting of the human as virus, glitch, and Elon Musk's "biological bootloader" — "the bug slowing down a system that would function better, in practically every sense, without us." If the human is already coded as the defect in an optimizing system, then VSL/QALY pricing is not an aberration but the consistent expression of a worldview in which the human has no value the system is bound to honor. The imago dei is the precise inverse: where the economic-computational view makes the human a checkmark to be optimized away, Moore's framework makes it a "sacred mystery." O'Gieblyn does not endorse the theology; she reports, with dismay, that nothing in her secular toolkit did the same work. The argument is therefore diagnostic, not confessional — it identifies a gap (a secular ground for unqualified human worth) and observes that, in the one case where the question was forced, the gap held.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The pandemic forced a literal valuation: is a saved human life worth the economic cost of shutdown? The system demanding the trade-off behaved as a "nominalist God" requiring "the sacrifice of human life."
2. Secular pro-shutdown op-eds tried to defend the life by appealing to its "intrinsic value," then "devolving into profitability algorithms and affordability assessments" to win on economic grounds too.
3. But to argue a life is worth *more* than the economy on the economy's own scale is to concede that the life belongs on that scale — so the tactic "only confirmed... the opposing view that human life was reducible to economic logic."
4. The trend reaches its endpoint: Krugman debunks "priceless" (a statistical life ≈ $10 million); *Forbes* refines it (VSL vs. QALYs). Intrinsic value has been fully cashed out as a number.
5. The only defense that does *not* collapse is non-secular: Russell Moore's imago dei — life as "sacred mystery," not "checkmarks on a page."
6. Therefore the pandemic is the test case for the book's question — how can human life have value outside a religious framework? — and the dismaying answer is that, when forced, the secular frameworks could not supply an unqualified one.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **A small, possibly unrepresentative sample.** The verdict rests on the op-eds O'Gieblyn happened to read in spring 2020 (Krugman, *Forbes*, the unnamed shutdown pieces). A secular philosopher could object that Kantian dignity, capabilities theory, or a rights-based account offers a non-religious ground for unconditional worth that simply did not appear in the pandemic's hot-take genre — so the failure may belong to op-ed discourse, not to secularism as such.
- **"Most cogent and unqualified" is an aesthetic judgment, not a proof.** O'Gieblyn reports being *dismayed* that Moore's argument was the strongest; she does not demonstrate that imago dei *succeeds* (it inherits all the problems of [[Theodicy]] and of grounding value in a contested God). The argument shows that secular defenses *collapsed under the conditions observed*, not that the religious one is *valid*.
- **The reduction may be a feature of the medium, not of valuation.** Public policy *does* price statistical lives routinely (transportation, environmental rule-making), and arguably must, to allocate finite resources. A defender of cost-benefit analysis would say pricing a *statistical* life is not the same as denying that any *particular* life is priceless — so the op-eds may not concede as much as O'Gieblyn claims.
- **It leans on the genre's own framing.** The essay's force is rhetorical and autobiographical (the ex-fundamentalist "dismayed" to find the theologian right). As with [[The true trauma of disenchantment is the loss of intrinsic meaning]], the felt necessity of a religious or quasi-religious ground for worth may itself be a post-religious hangover rather than a universal requirement — a possibility the chapter does not foreclose.
- **Scope.** This is a literary-essayistic diagnosis embedded in a pandemic memoir, not a worked-out meta-ethics; it functions as the empirical pressure-test of the book's question, not as a demonstration that worth *cannot* be secularly grounded.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the argument turns on the residue of the seventeenth-century divorce of "matter from meaning": once worth (the immaterial, the "sacred mystery") is exiled from the materially measurable, only the material — the dollar value, the QALY — remains sayable in public, and "intrinsic value" has no non-economic vocabulary to fall back on. Moore's imago dei is an attempt to reassert the immaterial side; its being "the most cogent" defense available is the tension's verdict.
