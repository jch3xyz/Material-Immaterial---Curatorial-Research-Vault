# Automation liberates men into the role of artist

## Claim

Automation, by separating the source of energy from the work operation and projecting fragmented mechanical labor onto the plane of entertainment as the motorcar "released the horse," releases men from specialist servitude into a freedom that — because it "taxes our inner resources of self-employment and imaginative participation" — amounts to a summons to the role of artist in society.

## Expanded Explanation

This is McLuhan's most utopian claim and the redemptive close of *Understanding Media*. The argument turns on an analogy he has built across the book: as the machine and the motorcar made the horse economically obsolete and thereby "released" it onto the plane of leisure and entertainment, so electric automation makes the fragmented, specialized human worker obsolete and releases men into a comparable condition. The mechanical age fragmented work into "homogenized but unrelated bits" and demanded a uniform, specialist workforce; the electric age, by contrast, separates energy and information from the specific work operation, so that "all aspects of production, consumption, and organization become incidental to communications." Labor ceases to be a fixed slot and becomes a matter of learning, of "paid learning" as both the dominant employment and the source of new wealth — what the chapter's own concept names [[Automation as Learning a Living]].

The freedom this produces is not idleness. McLuhan inverts the mechanical-age equation of leisure with the absence of work: in the electric age "we are most at leisure when we are most intensely involved, very much as with the artists in all ages." Released from repetitive specialist routine, men are thrown back on "inner resources of self-employment and imaginative participation in society" — precisely the resources of the artist. This connects directly to McLuhan's broader figure of [[The Artist as Integral Awareness]]: the artist is the one who perceives and works with the whole field rather than a fragment, and automation, by demanding "depth approaches in all fields," generalizes that integral, non-specialist stance across society. The "social and educational patterns latent in automation are those of self-employment and artistic autonomy."

The claim is prescriptive and optimistic rather than demonstrated, which is why its confidence is **medium** and its optimism must be flagged. McLuhan himself names the dark side — "the specter of joblessness and propertylessness in the electric age" — but folds it into the redemptive arc as "a liberation that taxes our inner resources," dismissing alarm about unemployment as "folly." The vault preserves the disagreement rather than endorsing the prophecy: the same separation of work from wealth that McLuhan reads as liberation is read by his critics as new domination (see Counterarguments).

## Reasoning Structure

1. In any automatic or servomechanist structure, the generation and transmission of power is separate from the work operation that uses the power; electricity is "sheer information" that can be applied indifferently to any task. *(Directly stated)*
2. This separation dissolves the fixed, fragmented specialist "job" of the mechanical age: "Wealth and work become information factors, and totally new structures are needed." *(Directly stated)*
3. By analogy, just as the motorcar made the horse economically obsolete and projected it onto the plane of entertainment, automation does the same with men. *(Directly stated)*
4. The resulting condition is not idleness but a freedom that taxes "inner resources of self-employment and imaginative participation" — the condition McLuhan identifies with the artist in all ages. *(Directly stated)*
5. Therefore automation "calls men to the role of artist in society"; the patterns latent in it are "those of self-employment and artistic autonomy." *(Directly stated, but as prophecy / prescriptive synthesis — Interpretive)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Optimism flag.** This is a prescriptive, redemptive synthesis, not a demonstrated empirical claim. McLuhan asserts the artist-fate as something that "would seem to be a fate" and dismisses unemployment alarm as "folly" without engaging the distribution of the new wealth. The vault preserves the disagreement rather than endorsing the prophecy.
- **Han's critique (auto-exploitation / immaterial labour).** Where McLuhan reads the dissolution of the fixed job into self-directed "imaginative participation" as liberation, Byung-Chul Han reads the same condition — the worker as their own boss, freely and ceaselessly self-optimizing — as [[Auto-Exploitation]], and the shift from material to communicative-affective labor as [[Immaterial Labour]] that intensifies rather than relieves coercion. The freedom McLuhan celebrates is, for Han, freedom that produces its own compulsion. *(Interpretive synthesis)*
- **Harari's critique (the useless class).** Where McLuhan claims automation makes "the folly of alarm about unemployment" obvious because labor becomes learning, Yuval Noah Harari projects that the same decoupling of intelligence from economic need produces not a society of artists but [[The Useless Class]] — masses rendered economically superfluous with no compensating artistic vocation. *(Interpretive synthesis)*
- **Lippard's critique (dematerialization and the market).** McLuhan's hope that "self-employment and artistic autonomy" follow from the dissolution of material production parallels the conceptual-art wager that dematerializing the art object would free art from commodity status; Lippard's verdict that [[Dematerialization failed to escape the market]] is a cautionary precedent — autonomy from material labor did not deliver autonomy from the market. *(Speculative connection)*
- **Internal tension.** McLuhan concedes "the specter of joblessness and propertylessness" and that "totally new structures are needed," yet the argument does not specify those structures; the liberation is asserted to coincide with, rather than to require solving, the propertylessness problem.

## Tensions

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the keystone disagreement: McLuhan reads automation's release as freedom; Han reads the identical decoupling of work from fixed labor as a new mode of control (auto-exploitation).
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the argument hinges on labor and wealth migrating from material goods to "information factors"; whether that migration liberates or merely reconfigures domination is the cross-author stake (Han, Harari, Lippard, McLuhan).
