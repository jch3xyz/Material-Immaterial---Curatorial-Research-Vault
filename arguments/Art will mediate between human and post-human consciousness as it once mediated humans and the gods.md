# Art will mediate between human and post-human consciousness as it once mediated humans and the gods

## Claim

Art will come to constitute a form of mediation between human and post-human consciousness, just as in past cultures it was used to mediate between humanity and the gods. (`Directly stated`)

## Expanded Explanation

In the closing arc of *Telematic Embrace*, Ascott returns art to a frankly sacred function. The telematisation of consciousness, he argues, is carrying the species "forward to another level of evolutionary development," and the research surrounding the sciences of mind, AI, artificial life, molecular engineering, robotics, and complex systems holds the potential for consciousness "to evolve beyond the human organism" — to a degree "that perhaps only silicon-based life could accommodate." Quoting Hans Moravec, he entertains the prospect of entities "as complex as ourselves, and eventually into something transcending everything we know—in whom we can take pride when they refer to themselves as our descendants." At that threshold a gap opens between human and post-human consciousness, and Ascott assigns art the role of mediating across it.

The analogy is deliberate and re-enchanting. Where archaic and traditional cultures used art (the icon, the temple, the ritual image) to mediate the unbridgeable distance between mortals and the gods, Ascott projects that office onto the coming encounter between human consciousness and whatever consciousness succeeds or exceeds it. This is the destination of his recurring claim that telematic technology is the technology of consciousness, spiritual before it is utilitarian — see [[Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness spiritual before utilitarian]]. Art is not decoration on this transition; it is the interface through which one order of mind addresses another.

The supporting "Mind of the Museum" passage grounds the mediation in Ascott's refusal to separate science from spirituality: "The threads of science and spirituality, technology and consciousness, are often so closely intertwined that clear differentiation of them is not possible." Because the technological and the spiritual are not cleanly separable for Ascott, art that works at the technological frontier (his [[New Media Art]] of interactivity and connectivity) is already operating in the register where the old mediation between humanity and the gods belonged. This is the re-enchantment terminus of the book: cybernetic and telematic art recovers, rather than abolishes, the sacred-mediating vocation of art.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Telematic culture is telematising consciousness, carrying the species to "another level of evolutionary development."
2. The convergence of AI, artificial life, molecular engineering, robotics, and complex systems makes it possible for consciousness "to evolve beyond the human organism," possibly into silicon-based "descendants" (Moravec).
3. This opens a gap between human and post-human consciousness analogous to the old gap between mortals and the gods.
4. In past cultures, art was the practice that mediated the mortal–divine distance.
5. Science/spirituality and technology/consciousness are so intertwined that they cannot be cleanly differentiated, so technological art already operates in the sacred-mediating register.
6. Therefore art will come to mediate between human and post-human consciousness, just as it once mediated between humanity and the gods.

- The non-differentiability of science/spirituality and technology/consciousness, from "The Mind of the Museum," which licenses treating technological art as already sacred-mediating.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim is hedged in the source ("Art *may* come to constitute..."): it is a projection / hope, not a demonstrated outcome — its force is visionary rather than evidential.
- It depends on the Moravec premise that consciousness can migrate to or arise in silicon "descendants," a contested transhumanist conjecture; if `Mind Uploading` and substrate-independent mind are incoherent (the embodiment critiques the vault catalogues elsewhere), the "post-human consciousness" pole of the mediation may never materialise.
- The "gods" analogy re-enchants art at the cost of empirical specificity: it tells us art's *office* (mediation) but not its *means* — what such mediating works concretely look like is left to the reader.
- A disenchantment reading (Weber via O'Gieblyn) would treat the sacred-mediation framing as nostalgic projection onto secular technology rather than a genuine recovery of the sacred.
