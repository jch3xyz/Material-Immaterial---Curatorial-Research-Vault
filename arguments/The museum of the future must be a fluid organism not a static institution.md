# The museum of the future must be a fluid organism not a static institution

## Claim

Because the electronic arts embody open-endedness, immersion, and emergence, the institution required to host them cannot be a fixed museum but must be a fluid, flowing system — a dynamic organism, a matrix — distinct from the museum, school, workshop, studio, production facility, or art centre.

## Expanded Explanation

In his 1993 conceptual brief for the proposed Ars Electronica Center (AEC) in Linz, Roy Ascott argues that the existing forms of cultural institution have become incompatible with the work they are now asked to hold. The electronic arts of the 1990s are not finite objects to be displayed and judged from a distance; they are processes and systems — "a fluid, moving stream of data configurations" — that depend on the public's interactive participation to evolve. A building designed around the static contemplation of fixed objects cannot support an art whose defining qualities are connectivity, sensory immersion, intensive interactivity, and emergence. The mismatch is structural, not a matter of degree.

The remedy Ascott proposes is therefore not a better museum but a different *kind* of entity altogether. The institution he calls for "must constitute a fluid and flowing system," and to describe such a thing is "to describe a dynamic organism rather than a fixed and static institution." He is explicit that this will "not be in any way the same as a traditional museum, or school, workshop, studio, production facility, or art centre" — it functions instead "as a matrix within which artistic work can evolve through public interactivity." The AEC is to *be* this organism — "a museum of the twenty-first century" in name only, since its identity is closer to a living, permeable system than to a repository (Directly stated).

His master image for it is the **datapool**: a pool "into which and within which data in all their modes flow, endlessly transformed through human interaction, and from which they emerge." The institution-as-organism is thus the institutional face of the broader object→system thesis the vault tracks from Burnham forward — the cultural container itself dematerializes from a fixed structure into a flowing, emergent process. This argument is the curatorial/institutional pole of Ascott's electronic-arts program; it carries the [[Object and System]] spine of the vault into the design of the museum itself.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The electronic arts of the 1990s embody open-endedness, sensory immersion of the viewing subject, and intensive interactivity from which new material and new art emerge.
2. A fixed, static institution — the traditional museum and its cognates — is organized around the display and distanced judgment of finite objects, and so cannot support emergence, immersion, or full interactivity.
3. Therefore the institution required must instead be a fluid and flowing system that supports open-endedness and emergence and encourages connectivity at all levels.
4. To describe such a system is to describe a dynamic organism — a matrix — rather than a fixed and static institution; the AEC is to be that organism.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The defining qualities of the electronic arts — "open-endedness, of sensory immersion of the viewing subject, and of intensive interactivity from which new material emerges" — which a static institution cannot host (see [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]]).
- The explicit contrast with every existing institutional form: "not be in any way the same as a traditional museum, or school, workshop, studio, production facility, or art centre."
- The redefinition of the artwork as process and system rather than finite object — "art not as finite object but as process and system, a fluid, moving stream of data configurations" — which makes the static container obsolete (extends [[Systems Esthetics]], [[Reciprocal Communication]]).
- The datapool metaphor as the positive model of the required organism: data that flow, are transformed through human interaction, and emerge as "art-in-flux."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- This is a programmatic brief for a specific proposed building, not a completed institution; the claim is a design thesis whose feasibility the document asserts rather than demonstrates.
- Ascott's "fluid organism" still requires, by his own admission, "precise" engineering and construction — the immaterial, flowing output rests on a fixed material and infrastructural base (the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension that the brief reframes as "complementary" and "energising" rather than resolving).
- The argument generalizes from the electronic/networked arts of the early 1990s; it does not address whether object-based or historical art still needs the conservation and stability functions a "static institution" provides — Ascott largely brackets that question rather than answering it.
- The organism metaphor is itself drawn from the new-biology / artificial-life vocabulary Ascott elsewhere foregrounds; critics could note that it imports a normative biological model of the institution rather than arguing for it on institutional grounds.
