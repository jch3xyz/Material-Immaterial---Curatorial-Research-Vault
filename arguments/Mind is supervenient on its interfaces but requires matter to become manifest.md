# Mind is supervenient on its interfaces but requires matter to become manifest

## Claim

Mind does not reside in matter as a property of the brain alone; it is supervenient on the activity of its interfaces — organic or telematic — and although mind is ubiquitous and always present, it requires matter to become manifest.

## Expanded Explanation

This is a compressed philosophy-of-mind position that Ascott folds into his [[Technoetics|technoetic]] program. It appears in the glossary chapter "Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions" as the definition of the term *Supervenience*. The claim does two things at once. First, it makes mind a function of interface activity rather than a substance localized in the organic brain: mind arises wherever an interface — a nervous system, a network, a telematic apparatus — is active. By placing organic and telematic interfaces on the same footing, Ascott extends mind out of the skull and into the [[Reciprocal Communication|networks]] that his work treats as the new medium, the same move that underwrites his account of [[Cyberception]] and the planetary [[Telematic networks constitute a planetary network consciousness|network consciousness]].

Second, the claim is a near-panpsychist ontology. Mind is "ubiquitous and always present" — consciousness pervades the field rather than being switched on only in brains — yet it "requires matter to become manifest." Mind is everywhere latent but appears only when matter and an active interface give it a site of expression. This is the technoetic analogue of [[Panpsychism]]: consciousness is fundamental and pervasive, but it needs an interface to actualize. The position thus refuses both the eliminativist reduction of mind to matter and the dualist separation of mind from matter; mind and matter are two phases of one process, the immaterial requiring the material to appear.

The argument bears directly on the vault's central [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension. Mind is the immaterial pole — ubiquitous, distributed, supervenient — but it is non-manifest without the material pole. For an artist whose medium is the network, this licenses an art that works with consciousness directly: if mind is supervenient on interface activity, then building new interfaces (telematic, technoetic, [[Moistmedia|moist]]) is a way of building new manifestations of mind.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Mind is not a substance located in the organic brain but a phenomenon supervenient on the activity of interfaces.
2. Interfaces may be organic *or* telematic; the same supervenience relation holds for both, so mind extends into networks.
3. Mind is ubiquitous and always present (the panpsychist premise): consciousness pervades the field.
4. Yet what is ubiquitous is latent; mind requires matter — an active material interface — to become manifest.
5. Therefore mind and matter are not opposed substances but co-dependent phases: the immaterial is everywhere, but it appears only through the material.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The glossary definition of *Supervenience* itself, stated as a flat ontological proposition (raw ch. 32). It sits inside Ascott's wider technoetic lexicon, beside entries such as *World-mind* ("Planetary self-awareness and cognition arising from the technoetic Web") and *Technoetic aesthetics* ("the technoetic aesthetics of creative consciousness and artificial life"), which presuppose that mind can be carried by non-organic interfaces.
- The companion glossary entries on *Telepresence* ("out-of-body and reembodied, dematerialised, and reconfigured at one and the same time") and *Telemadic* ("our minds traversing the vast interspaces of the worldwide networks") supply the telematic-interface case of the same supervenience relation (raw ch. 32; Supporting context, `Strongly implied`).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim is asserted, not argued — it is a glossary definition, so it states the position without defending it against the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]]. Why interface activity should yield, rather than merely correlate with, experience is left open.
- "Supervenience" is used loosely. In analytic philosophy supervenience is a dependence relation (no change in the mental without a change in the physical); Ascott appears to mean something stronger and more emanationist — mind as a ubiquitous field that *manifests* through matter — which is closer to [[Panpsychism]] or [[Idealism]] than to the technical term.
- The panpsychist premise ("ubiquitous and always present") is unsupported within the entry; it inherits whatever burden panpsychism carries — the combination problem, the lack of an empirical test.
- A materialist would reverse the priority: matter does not merely *manifest* a pre-existing mind; matter *produces* mind, and absent the right material organization there is no mind to manifest.
