# All prior sculpture is a one-way message that does not respond to man

## Claim

From the paleolithic through Kinetic Art, all sculpture shares the same inherent defect — it does not respond to man intelligently; it is a one-way message, a dead soul made alive only through art and prearranged mechanized motion.

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham opens chapter eight by enlisting Norbert Wiener's music-box analogy. Wiener describes the little figures that dance atop a music box according to a pattern "set in advance, and in which the past activity of the figures has practically nothing to do with the pattern of their future activity." A message reaches them, "indeed [one way] but it goes from the machinery of the music box to the figures, and stops there." The figures are "blind, deaf, and dumb." Burnham generalizes the plight of these automata to the entire sculptural tradition: the defect "in principle... has in common all free-standing sculpture beginning with the paleolithic age."

The defect is that sculpture "do[es] not respond to man in any intelligent fashion." A statue, a Calder mobile, a symphonic score, a Tinguely machine, a piece of Kinetic Art — each can serve as part of a "message" for a receptive observer, but the message runs in one direction only. Burnham distinguishes this *one-way stimulation* from the *reciprocated message* that Wiener identifies as "the prime means by which intelligent beings communicate with one another." Living creatures, even at the level of food-gathering or combat, communicate two ways with their environment; sculpture does not. (`Directly stated`.)

The Kinetic Art that appeared to break the impasse — the "newer myth [that] sees mechanical kinetic sculpture 'act' as a spectator participates with it" — does not escape the defect. "Yet this sculpture no more 'acts' than does a ball of yarn when it is batted and chased by a kitten." The motion is real but the agency is illusory: the work is moved, it does not act. To say that sculpture "does act," Burnham warns, only "sets the scene psychologically for the eventuality that it will" — naming the threshold beyond which a genuinely responsive Cyborg Art would lie. (`Directly stated`.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise (via Wiener): the music-box figures receive a one-way message that originates in the machinery and "stops there"; they cannot vary their activity from a pre-set pattern.
2. Premise (Burnham's generalization): this plight is not isolated — it characterizes all free-standing sculpture from the paleolithic onward, including automata and present-day Kinetic Art.
3. Premise: intelligent beings communicate by the *reciprocated* (two-way) message; living creatures communicate with their environment, inert objects do not.
4. Premise: a statue, mobile, symphony, or Kinetic work delivers only one-way stimulation to the observer, not communication.
5. Premise: apparently "acting" Kinetic sculpture acts no more than a ball of yarn batted by a kitten — the motion is prearranged or pseudo-random, not responsive.
6. Conclusion: therefore all prior sculpture is defective in the same way — a dead soul made alive through art and prearranged motion, not a partner in intelligent intercourse; only a future Cyborg Art that possesses some degree of intelligence could supply the missing return message.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Wiener music-box epigraph (1950, p. 9): the dancing figures are "blind, deaf, and dumb," receiving a one-way message that "stops there." Used as the controlling image for the whole chapter.
- The catalogue of one-way works: a Rubens nude, a Henry Moore bronze, a Tinguely exhibition, a Rauschenberg combine, motion pictures, symphonic music, Kinetic Art — "all one-way messages."
- The cybernetic re-description of the past: a relationship "between a complex, self-stabilizing, goal-seeking system (man) and an inert object," yielding "not communication but one-way stimulation for the human party involved."
- The ball-of-yarn figure (FIG. context, the "newer myth" of kinetic sculpture): the work "no more 'acts' than does a ball of yarn when it is batted and chased by a kitten."
- Julio Le Parc's call for "active participation" of the art work — which, Burnham notes, "never touches the consideration that art, to engage in a series of interactions with a human observer, must itself possess some degree of intelligence."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The Kinetic Artists' own claim (the "newer myth") that mechanical sculpture *does* act when a spectator participates with it. Burnham concedes a *form of interaction* occurs — "just as a bow interacts harmoniously with the movements of a skilled violinist" — but denies it is communication "of the kind... of two men talking." (`Directly stated`.)
- The argument is teleological: it judges all historical sculpture by a standard (intelligent reciprocity) that only an as-yet-unrealized Cyborg Art could meet, framing thousands of years of work as a defective "pre-stage." That the defect is a *defect* rather than simply the nature of the medium is Burnham's interpretive premise. (`Strongly implied`.)
- The "dead soul made alive" language imports a vitalist/animist frame (the artist-magician who would "literally replace" the animal) into a cybernetic argument; whether responsiveness is the right measure of a sculpture's life is left as an assumption. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
