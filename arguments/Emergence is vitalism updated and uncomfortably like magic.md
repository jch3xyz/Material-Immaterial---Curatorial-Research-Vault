# Emergence is vitalism updated and uncomfortably like magic

## Claim

[[Emergence|Emergentism]] is structurally identical to discredited vitalism — both try to get "something from nothing" by hypothesizing an additional, invisible animating power that exists within the mechanism, "like a ghost in the machine," which makes strong emergence (in Mark Bedau's phrase) "uncomfortably like magic" — and yet, scratch the mystical surface, and emergentism turns out to be covert reductive materialism that dissolves the very interior consciousness it claims to explain.

## Expanded Explanation

[[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] came to emergence hopefully, "because it seemed like a compelling alternative to reductive materialism — the view that any phenomenon can be explained by breaking it down to its most basic, elemental building blocks." Where reductive materialism led "too often to the absurd position that because consciousness cannot be observed in the brain, it does not exist," emergentists hold that "complex, dynamic systems cannot always be explained in terms of their constituent parts": the mind is "a kind of structural pattern that emerges from the complexity of the entire network." This is the appeal that links emergence to [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] and [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] accounts of distributed, decentered cognition (forests, beehives, traffic, Rodney Brooks's insect robots) — consciousness as [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] rather than substance, distributed across the body and its environment rather than seated in a central control.

But Chapter 5 turns this hope against itself. "Although emergentism is rooted in physicalism, critics have often claimed that there is something inherently mystical about the theory, particularly when these higher-level patterns are said to be capable of controlling or directing physical processes." The AI philosopher Mark Bedau "has argued that emergence, in its strongest iterations, 'is uncomfortably like magic,' as it assumes that a nonmaterial property (consciousness) is capable of somehow acting causally on a material substance (the brain)." From here O'Gieblyn draws the genealogy: "Some critics have argued that emergentism is just an updated version of vitalism — the ancient notion that the world is animated by a life force or energy that permeates all things." Vitalism was the default ontology before Descartes; forced to articulate itself scientifically against the view that "humans were simply machines," it insisted "that an organism was more than the totality of its parts — that there must exist, in addition to its physical structure, some 'living principle,' or *élan vital*." The diagnostic line: "the theory is vulnerable to the same criticism that has long haunted vitalism: it is an attempt to get 'something from nothing.' It hypothesizes some additional, invisible power that exists within the mechanism, like a ghost in the machine." Watching Brooks's team, she "often got the feeling that they were engaged in a kind of alchemy, carrying on the legacy of those vitalist magicians who inspired Victor Frankenstein."

The sharper turn comes in Chapter 6, and it inverts the magical charge. "In fact, to dismiss emergentism as sheer magic is to ignore the specific ways in which it differs from this folklore — even as it superficially satisfies the same desire. Scratch beneath the mystical surface and it becomes clear that emergentism is often not so different from the most reductive forms of materialism, particularly when it comes to the question of human consciousness." Far from adding a ghost, emergentism *subtracts* the self: "We are not rational agents but an encasement of competing systems that lack any sort of unity or agency." Marvin Minsky described the mind as "a sort of tangled-up bureaucracy" of agents that, at the lowest level, "cannot think at all"; Brooks called consciousness a "cheap trick," "an illusion that exists only in the eye of the beholder." So the same theory is at once *too much* (an invisible animating power, vitalism redux) and *too little* (dumb agents all the way down, reductive materialism in disguise). The science historian Jessica Riskin states the cost exactly: "emergent theories of mind end up dismissing the very thing they are supposed to explain." "Arriving…at the level of these dumb, foundational agents, one will have lost all sense of intelligence as a feature of mind." The ghost promised by the vitalist reading and the corpse delivered by the reductive reading are the same machine, viewed from two ends — which is why emergence "uncomfortably" both is and is not magic.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Emergentism markets itself as the cure for reductive materialism: complex systems "cannot always be explained in terms of their constituent parts," so the mind is a structural pattern of the whole network, not a substance found in the brain.
2. But when emergent patterns are said to *control or direct* physical processes, the theory posits "a nonmaterial property (consciousness) … acting causally on a material substance (the brain)" — which "is uncomfortably like magic" (Bedau).
3. This is exactly vitalism's move: positing "some 'living principle,' or *élan vital*" — "an attempt to get 'something from nothing,'" "some additional, invisible power that exists within the mechanism, like a ghost in the machine."
4. So emergence inherits vitalism's discredit — and its alchemical lineage (Galvani's frog legs, the vitalist magicians behind Victor Frankenstein, Brooks's MIT "alchemy").
5. Yet "scratch beneath the mystical surface" and emergentism is "not so different from the most reductive forms of materialism": Minsky's mind is "a tangled-up bureaucracy" whose foundational agents "cannot think at all"; Brooks calls consciousness a "cheap trick."
6. Therefore the theory dissolves what it set out to explain: "Arriving…at the level of these dumb, foundational agents, one will have lost all sense of intelligence as a feature of mind" (Riskin) — "emergent theories of mind end up dismissing the very thing they are supposed to explain."
7. Conclusion: emergentism is structurally double — too much (a ghost in the machine) and too little (no self at all). Both readings are objections, and they are the same objection seen from opposite ends.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **O'Gieblyn concedes weak emergence is legitimate.** The charge targets the *strong* claim (emergent consciousness in machines). She grants that "mystical overtones should not discredit emergence as such — it is a useful enough way to describe complex systems like beehives and climates." The vitalism/magic critique bites only where higher-level patterns are said to exert downward causal control, not where emergence merely redescribes self-organization.
- **The two objections may be in tension with each other.** "Vitalism updated" says emergence adds an illicit *extra* (a ghost); "covert reductive materialism" says it subtracts the self entirely (dumb agents). A defender could reply that these cancel: a theory cannot be both spookily additive and ruthlessly eliminative. O'Gieblyn's resolution is that they are the *same* structure seen from two ends, but that synthesis is interpretive, not forced by the texts.
- **Disanalogy with vitalism.** Vitalism concerned *life*; emergentism "is focused specifically on consciousness." O'Gieblyn flags this herself — the inherited criticism transfers by analogy ("vulnerable to the same criticism"), not by identity of subject matter, so the structural parallel does some load-bearing work the historical record does not directly supply.
- **The reductive reading is a critics' construal.** That emergentism "is often not so different from the most reductive forms of materialism" rests on reading Minsky and Brooks as representative; a strong-emergence theorist (e.g. a downward-causation realist, or a [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard-problem]] dualist like David Chalmers) would reject the agents-all-the-way-down picture and so escape the dissolution charge while keeping the "magic."
- **Scope.** The case is built through MIT-lab narrative (Brooks, Breazeal, Minsky) and a handful of philosophers (Bedau, Riskin, Dennett), in O'Gieblyn's essayistic register — illustrated and quoted rather than argued through the analytic philosophy-of-mind literature on emergence.

## Related Arguments

- [[Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image]] — the prior chapter's mechanism: the [[Mind as Machine]] metaphor flips into machine-as-mind. Emergence is where that reversed metaphor cashes out as a *theory of consciousness*, and where its quasi-religious residue (the ghost, the *élan vital*, the golem's animating breath) becomes visible.
- [[The strong claim of artificial life is constructed through narrative]] — [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s parallel: the leap from simulation to "life itself" rides on story, not demonstration. O'Gieblyn's "something from nothing" is the same unearned leap, here from complexity to consciousness.
- [[Conscious agency has never been in control]] — the eliminativist underside the two arguments share: Hayles's distributed, decentered subject and O'Gieblyn's Minskyan "tangled-up bureaucracy" both dethrone the unified self, but O'Gieblyn reads the dethroning as the *cost* of emergence, not its liberation.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — emergence sits astride it uncomfortably: rooted in physicalism (material), yet smuggling a nonmaterial pattern with causal power (immaterial). The vitalism charge says it cannot have both; the reductive charge says it secretly has neither.
- [[Visibility vs Understanding]] — Brooks's "consciousness is a cheap trick" and "intelligence is in the eye of the observer" collapse interiority into observable behavior. Emergence explains the *appearance* of mind (visible performance) precisely by giving up on mind as something *understood* from within (first-person experience).
