# Networking re-materialises the body it does not dematerialise it

## Claim

For Ascott, the telematic network erases the Cartesian boundary of the material body, but this erasure produces not disembodiment or immateriality but a *rematerialisation* — the reinvention of the human being as a new, almost wholly artificial, bionic, prosthetic, post-biological corporeality.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott makes a deliberate and emphatic distinction. Networking, telepresence, and artificial life do dissolve "the established boundaries of the material body" as that body was defined "within a Cartesian, medical orthodoxy specific to Western cultures." The obvious conclusion — the one drawn by transhumanist uploading and by disembodiment readings of cyberculture — would be that the body falls away into pure information. Ascott explicitly refuses that conclusion. The erasure "leads, however, not to a disembodiment of the person, nor to an immateriality, but to the rematerialisation, the redescription, reconstruction— in short, the reinvention of the human being." The body is not deleted; it is rebuilt.

Crucially, this is the inverse of [[Dematerialization]]. Where Lippard's conceptual artists sought to dissolve the art *object* toward the immaterial, Ascott insists that leaving "the old, classical, earthly body" is "not in order to dematerialise but to inhabit a new corporeality, which is almost totally artificial, bionic, prosthetic." The brain seeks its hypercontext "in the hyperbody"; this is "the post-biological body as interface." Far from disappearing, the body in the networked condition is hyper-present: "In one sense, we were never as aware of the body as we are now. It is glorious, we celebrate it... Our technological prostheses extend it, amplify it, enrich it." What shifts is not the body's existence but its frame — "we are moving not so much out-of-body as out-of-frame."

This positions Ascott as a high-value counterpoint within the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension. He shares Hayles's refusal of disembodiment (see [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]]) — there is no escape into pattern without a substrate — but he parts company with both the Lippard immaterialist reading and the Kurzweil/Moravec uploading reading by naming the new substrate as a *materially reconstructed*, engineered body: "human presence is now a technological matter." Notably, Ascott invokes [[Hans Moravec]] approvingly ("his vision of a consummate downloading of personality from wetware to hardware") yet his own thesis is not pattern-migration off the flesh but the engineering of new flesh — bionics, nanotechnology, "born-again bionics" — a re-materialisation, not a dematerialisation.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Telematic networking, telepresence, and artificial life erase the boundaries of the Cartesian material body.
2. One might expect this erasure to yield disembodiment (mind freed from body) or immateriality (existence as pure information).
3. Ascott explicitly denies both: the erasure yields instead rematerialisation — redescription, reconstruction, reinvention of the human being.
4. The new corporeality is "almost totally artificial, bionic, prosthetic" — a post-biological body that serves as interface, the "hyperbody" sought by the brain.
5. Therefore the networked condition does not dematerialise the body; it re-materialises it into a new, engineered form.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Tension with the Moravec citation.** Ascott approvingly cites [[Hans Moravec]]'s "downloading of personality from wetware to hardware" — a paradigm that *is* a form of dematerialisation/substrate-independence (see [[We are not our bodies but a pattern of information that persists through material turnover]] and [[Mind Uploading]]). His own anti-dematerialisation thesis sits in unresolved tension with this endorsement; he wants both the migration off the biological body and the re-materialisation into a new engineered one.
- **"Erasure of the body" can read as rhetorical.** Ascott also speaks of "the longing to escape the earth, to escape the body" and of being "out-of-frame," which a [[Posthumanism|posthumanist]] critic could read as a residual escape-from-the-flesh fantasy dressed up as re-materialisation.
- **Scope.** The thesis is asserted within an art/consciousness manifesto (the "Telenoia" address); it is a visionary claim about cultural transition, not an argued ontology of embodiment. Its evidentiary weight is programmatic.
- **Counterpoint to Lippard.** Against [[Dematerialization (Lippard)]], Ascott's "rematerialisation" is a genuine reversal of vocabulary: the same network condition Lippard's lineage would call a move toward the immaterial, Ascott names a move toward a new materiality. The two are describing adjacent phenomena (the loss of the discrete object/body) under opposite signs.

## Related Arguments

- [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]] — Hayles's thesis that information always has a material substrate; Ascott converges (no disembodiment) but specifies the new substrate as an *engineered, reconstructed* body rather than a different but given one.
- [[We are not our bodies but a pattern of information that persists through material turnover]] — Kurzweil's patternist uploading thesis; Ascott's re-materialisation is the sharpest available *counter*-pole, insisting the new corporeality is itself material (bionic, prosthetic), not pattern abstracted from matter.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott enters this multi-author tension on the *material* side at the precise point most readers expect the immaterial: he takes the network's dissolution of the Cartesian body and routes it not to immateriality but to a new materiality (re-materialisation). A direct counterweight to the Lippard dematerialisation pole.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — Ascott refuses the clean migration from embodiment to information; the network produces a new [[The Body as Data|body]] that is both informational interface *and* engineered flesh.
