# The machine is us an aspect of our embodiment

## Claim

The machine is not an external "it" to be animated, worshipped, and dominated; it is us — our processes, an aspect of our [[Embodiment]] — because high-tech culture has dissolved the ontological boundary between organism and machine, leaving us responsible for boundaries we ourselves are.

## Expanded Explanation

Haraway closes the *Manifesto* by inverting the inherited relation between human and machine. The dualist tradition she catalogues (self/other, mind/body, maker/made, agent/resource, active/passive) cast the machine as a dominated object on one side and the human as autonomous subject on the other. Under late-twentieth-century "high-tech culture," that arrangement collapses: "It is not clear who makes and who is made in the relation between human and machine. It is not clear what is mind and what is body in machines that resolve into coding practices." The categories that once kept organism and machine apart no longer hold.

From this collapse Haraway draws her affirmative conclusion. The machine ceases to be an alien thing requiring either reverence or mastery and becomes a constituent of the self: "The machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment." This is the affirmative complement to the anxious cyborg of Hayles — where [[The Cyborg]] in Hayles registers a threat to the liberal subject, in Haraway the [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] is embraced as a hopeful figure that releases pleasure in skill and dissolves antagonistic dualisms. The argument is grounded in a strong ontological claim: "There is no fundamental, ontological separation in our formal knowledge of machine and organism, of technical and organic."

The consequence is one of responsibility rather than helplessness. Because the boundary between us and our tools is not given but made, we are answerable for it: "We can be responsible for machines; they do not dominate or threaten us. We are responsible for boundaries; we are they." The machine/organism opposition that animated both technophobic and technophilic politics is declared "obsolete, unnecessary." This is why Haraway can ask, "Why should our bodies end at the skin, or include at best other beings encapsulated by skin?" — the body's edge is itself a contingent construction, not a natural limit.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Western traditions rest on a set of persistent dualisms (self/other, mind/body, maker/made, agent/resource) that organize the logics and practices of domination. (Directly stated)
2. High-tech culture challenges these dualisms: in machines that "resolve into coding practices" it is no longer clear who makes and who is made, or what is mind and what is body. (Directly stated)
3. Therefore there is "no fundamental, ontological separation in our formal knowledge of machine and organism, of technical and organic." (Directly stated)
4. If organism and machine are not ontologically separate, then the machine is not an external object to be animated, worshipped, or dominated. (Strongly implied)
5. The machine is instead "us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment"; the old machine/organism relationships are "obsolete, unnecessary." (Directly stated)
6. Because the boundary is constructed rather than given, responsibility shifts to us: "We are responsible for boundaries; we are they." (Directly stated)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The radical-feminist and ecofeminist positions Haraway names (Griffin, Lorde, Rich) "insist on the organic, opposing it to the technological" — for them embracing the machine as part of the self forfeits a needed ground of resistance. (Directly stated — Haraway presents these as the opposing organicist commitments)
- Haraway concedes the cyborg world is "a matrix of complex dominations" as well as a source of "great human satisfaction"; the dissolution of boundaries does not by itself guarantee liberation, only the possibility of "taking responsibility." (Directly stated)
- The claim of "no fundamental, ontological separation" is asserted at the level of "our formal knowledge" — it is a claim about how high-tech culture has *textualized* bodies and machines, not an uncontested metaphysical proof. (Interpretive synthesis)

## Related Arguments

- [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]] (Hayles) — both arguments refuse the erasure of the body, but Haraway goes further: the machine is itself folded *into* embodiment rather than opposed to it. Haraway's affirmative cyborg is the hopeful counterpart to Hayles's anxious one.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Haraway holds the poles together: the machine is immaterial "coding practice" yet "an aspect of our embodiment," refusing the choice between material body and informational pattern.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — where the cybernetic tradition (per Hayles) tends to subtract the body, Haraway makes information-saturated machinery a constituent of the embodied self rather than its replacement.
