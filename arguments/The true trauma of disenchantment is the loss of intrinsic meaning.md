# The true trauma of disenchantment is the loss of intrinsic meaning

## Claim

The standard objection to the [[Disenchantment]] narrative — that scientific and technical mastery breeds its own magic, mystery, and awe — misses the point, because what disenchantment actually destroyed was not enchantment but *intrinsic meaning*: the conviction that the world has a telos and that understanding it brings us closer to the good, a loss that no quantity of smartphone-magic can repair.

## Expanded Explanation

This is [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]'s rebuttal of what might be called the re-enchantment objection, the line of argument associated with Bruno Latour and Charles Taylor that the modern world has never really been disenchanted at all. The objection is genuinely forceful, and O'Gieblyn states it at full strength before turning on it: "technical mastery of the world does not necessarily strip it of all magic, mystery, and awe; only an impoverished imagination will fail to find beauty in the revelations of science or ignore our capacity to discover, or invent, new sites of wonder." Latour, in *We Have Never Been Modern*, asks how we could possibly have disenchanted the world "when every day our laboratories and our factories populate the world with hundreds of hybrids stranger than those of the day before," and presses the case with a question — "Is Boyle's air pump any less strange than the Arapesh spirit houses?" O'Gieblyn updates the illustration to our own technological idiom: "Is the smartphone any less magical than Moses's staff? Are our home assistants... a lesser enchantment than the ancient spirits who were capable of manifesting in rocks and shrubbery and trees?"

Her move is not to deny that wonder persists but to relocate the wound. "These objections ultimately miss the point." For [[Max Weber]], from whom O'Gieblyn takes the term, "disenchantment was not merely an ontological hollowing-out... nor was it the simple fact that the universe could be reduced to causal mechanisms." The loss of literal spirits — souls in bodies, gods in rocks — is the surface phenomenon the objection answers, and answering it leaves the real injury untouched. "The true trauma of disenchantment is that the world, as seen through the lens of modern science, is devoid of intrinsic *meaning*." The human mind, O'Gieblyn writes after Weber, "has religious, ethical, and metaphysical needs in addition to its hunger for knowledge"; it is driven by "an inner compulsion to understand the world as a meaningful cosmos and to take up a position towards it." Re-enchantment by technological marvel cannot satisfy that compulsion, because marvel is not meaning. A smartphone may be as strange as Moses's staff and still answer no question about how one should live.

The historical engine of the loss is the seventeenth-century mechanistic philosophy, which "divorced not only body from mind but also matter from meaning." Once Aristotelian final causes — "the Aristotelian notion that nature has intrinsic purposes and goals" — were banished from science, "the spiritual and the ethical were no longer bound up with the physical processes of life but were relegated to that ghostly and uncertain realm of the subjective mind." Where Aristotle and Aquinas alike held that "understanding the world brought us closer to God, or the absolute," so that natural philosophy was "necessarily bound up with questions of virtue, ethics, and ultimate meaning," disenchanted science abandons telos for description. Its self-undermining consequence is O'Gieblyn's clinching point, drawn from Weber's 1917 lecture *Science as a Vocation*: science "is so committed to describing the world objectively... without presuppositions, that it cannot even affirm its own intrinsic value; it can't explain why technical mastery of the world is desirable, or why knowledge itself is worthwhile." A worldview that cannot say why its own pursuits matter has not merely lost magic — it has lost the ground of meaning, and that is precisely the loss the re-enchantment objection cannot touch. This argument is the load-bearing premise beneath O'Gieblyn's reading of [[Transhumanism]] and the broader [[Re-enchantment]] thesis: she diagnoses transhumanism as a *failed* re-enchantment because, promising to "restore through science" the hopes science obliterated, it offers technical mastery and immortality — more marvel — where the disenchanted longing was for meaning and telos all along.

## Reasoning Structure

1. State the strongest objection: technical mastery does not strip the world of magic; science and technology generate their own wonders (Latour, Taylor). The smartphone is no less magical than Moses's staff.
2. Concede the factual point — wonder does persist — but deny its relevance: "these objections ultimately miss the point."
3. Distinguish two things the objection conflates. Disenchantment is *not* (a) the ontological hollowing-out (no spirits in rocks, no souls in bodies) nor (b) the bare reduction of the universe to causal mechanism.
4. Identify the real loss: the world "as seen through the lens of modern science, is devoid of intrinsic *meaning*."
5. Ground it in a need re-enchantment cannot meet: the mind has "religious, ethical, and metaphysical needs," an "inner compulsion to understand the world as a meaningful cosmos."
6. Trace the historical cause: mechanistic philosophy "divorced... matter from meaning" by banishing final causes; ethics and spirit were exiled to "the subjective mind."
7. Clinch with the self-undermining consequence (Weber): disenchanted science "cannot even affirm its own intrinsic value" — it cannot say why knowledge or mastery is worthwhile.
8. Conclusion: because the loss is meaning/telos, not magic, technological wonder ("smartphone-magic") is the wrong currency; it cannot pay the debt. This grounds the reading of transhumanism as re-enchantment that fails on its own terms.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The objection it rebuts is not refuted, only relocated.** O'Gieblyn grants that technological wonder is real; she does not show that meaning and wonder are cleanly separable. A Latourian could reply that "intrinsic meaning" was always a projection (a theme O'Gieblyn herself develops in [[Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image]]), in which case nothing was *lost* — only an illusion withdrawn — and the "trauma" is grief for a fiction.
- **It leans on Weber as authority rather than argument.** The claim that the mind has an "inner compulsion to understand the world as a meaningful cosmos" is asserted on Weber's say-so; whether that compulsion is a universal feature of mind or a culturally specific (post-religious) hangover is left open. O'Gieblyn writes partly from the autobiography of leaving fundamentalism, which may overweight the felt necessity of telos.
- **The self-undermining point cuts both ways.** That science "cannot even affirm its own intrinsic value" is, for Weber, a feature — the discipline of "intellectual integrity," not a defect to be cured. Weber himself "was not arguing that the sciences should return to the business of values," and warned that the worst thing science could do "was to take up the mantle of reenchantment." So the argument diagnoses a lack without licensing any restoration of meaning *through* science — which is exactly O'Gieblyn's verdict on transhumanism, but it leaves the meaning-hunger with nowhere legitimate to go.
- **Scope.** The argument is made in the literary-essayistic register of Chapter 3 and turns on O'Gieblyn's reading of Weber rather than on a worked-out philosophy of meaning or value; it functions as a framing premise for the [[Re-enchantment]] critique, not a free-standing demonstration.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the mechanist divorce that drives the argument, "matter from meaning," is a partition of the immaterial (meaning, telos, the ethical) from the material (causal mechanism); disenchantment is precisely the verdict that only the material side is real, exiling meaning to "that ghostly and uncertain realm of the subjective mind."
