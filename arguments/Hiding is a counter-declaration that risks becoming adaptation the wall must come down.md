# Hiding is a counter-declaration that risks becoming adaptation the wall must come down

## Claim

The art and science of hiding from surveillance is a brilliant artistic vanguard, but as a defensive counter-declaration it leaves the offending facts intact and risks becoming an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage; it is the wall, not the tunnels under it, that requires attention.

## Expanded Explanation

For Zuboff, [[The Hiding Strategy]] names the proliferating tactics by which a new generation of activists, artists, and inventors seeks human invisibility from the lawless machines of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — signal-blocking phone cases, anti-facial-recognition visors and masks, radio-wave-blocking garments, route-scrambling apps. She honors this work: "the need to make ourselves invisible is the theme of a brilliant artistic vanguard," a "message in a bottle cast from the front lines of mourning and revulsion." The artists, like the young, are "canaries in the coal mine" — their genius is itself diagnostic of intolerable conditions.

But honoring the vanguard is not endorsing hiding as a remedy. Hiding belongs to the genus of the *counter-declaration*: the defensive move that responds to surveillance capitalism's unilateral declarations without unseating them (see [[The Counter-Declaration and the Synthetic Declaration]]). A counter-declaration accepts the offending facts on the ground and merely seeks respite from them; it engineers tunnels under the wall while leaving the wall standing. Worse, it tends to *normalize* what it resists: "Like every counter-declaration, hiding risks becomes an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage." When evasion becomes a way of life, the outrage that should be mobilized against the conditions themselves is privatized into individual technique, and the conditions are quietly conceded.

Hence the closing escalation: "These conditions are unacceptable. Tunnels under this wall are not enough. This wall must come down." The corrective is not better hiding but collective political action — a *synthetic* declaration that abolishes the conditions rather than dodging them (see [[Only a synthetic declaration not defensive measures can transform surveillance capitalism]]). Zuboff sharpens the stakes with a second danger: the greatest peril "is that we come to feel at home in glass life or in the prospect of hiding from it." Either alternative — total exposure, or perpetual evasion — "rob[s] us of the life-sustaining inwardness, born in sanctuary, that finally distinguishes us from the machines," the well "from which we draw the capacities to promise and to love." So even psychologically, hiding is no sanctuary; it is the negative image of glass life, not its overcoming. Only [[The Right to Sanctuary]], secured collectively, restores the inwardness that both glass life and the hiding response destroy.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The art and science of hiding is a genuine, brilliant artistic vanguard — a true diagnostic signal that present conditions are intolerable.
2. But hiding is structurally a *counter-declaration*: a defensive response that accepts the offending facts and seeks only respite, leaving the imposing structure (the "wall") intact.
3. Every counter-declaration carries the risk of becoming an *adaptation* — normalizing the conditions it resists and dissipating the outrage that should target those conditions.
4. Moreover, feeling "at home" in hiding, like feeling at home in glass life, robs us of the inwardness born in sanctuary that distinguishes us from machines.
5. Therefore tunnels under the wall are not enough: the conditions must be named unacceptable and the wall itself brought down through collective declaration, not individual evasion.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- One could argue defensive hiding has real protective value in the interim and that dismissing "tunnels" understates the safety they provide to vulnerable people now; Zuboff's point is comparative and strategic (necessary but insufficient), not a denial that evasion ever helps.
- The line between a counter-declaration that "rallies outrage" and one that "becomes adaptation" is not sharp; the same artwork may do both depending on how it circulates — Zuboff treats the vanguard as ambivalent (genuine signal *and* latent anesthetic) rather than condemning it.
- The transcribed text reads "hiding risks becomes an adaptation" (a grammatical artifact of the source); the sense is unambiguous — *risks becoming*.
