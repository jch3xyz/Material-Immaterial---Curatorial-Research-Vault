# Telematic networks constitute a planetary network consciousness

## Claim

The worldwide spread of computer communications networks constitutes a planetary network consciousness — what the esoteric traditions would call a "subtle body" and what Teilhard de Chardin called the noosphere — emerging from the symbiosis of the human mind and the artificial thought of parallel distributed processing.

## Expanded Explanation

For Roy Ascott the telematic network is not merely an instrument for moving messages but the material substrate of an emergent planetary mind. He gathers a deliberately heterodox lineage to name it: the "subtle body" of Eastern and Western esoteric traditions, Teilhard de Chardin's noosphere, Gregory Bateson's "mind at large," and Peter Russell's "global brain." These are not loosely poetic gestures but a single chained argument — that the ubiquitous web of computer communications across the face of the earth is a "psychic envelope for the planet," a thinking layer wrapped around the globe. (Directly stated — Ascott explicitly invokes each of these four figures by name in chapter 20.)

The mechanism Ascott borrows from Russell is quantitative-becoming-qualitative: as the sheer number of information exchanges shuttling through the networks increases, the system crosses a threshold of connectivity at which coherence — a brain-like ordering of activity — spontaneously arises, "similar to those found in the human brain." (Directly stated.) Crucially, this consciousness is not the human's alone nor the machine's alone. Ascott insists it requires "a redescription of human consciousness as it emerges from the developing symbiosis of the human mind and the artificial thought of parallel distributed processing (PDP)." The neural-net architecture of PDP — connectionist, distributed, non-serial — is for Ascott the technical form that makes a *distributed* planetary mind thinkable, not a centralized one. (Directly stated.)

This argument is the conceptual core of Ascott's larger project: the telematic network as the new medium of art and consciousness alike. It extends the vault's `Network Consciousness` concept (Ascott's own 1984 coinage, "Art and Telematics: Towards a Network Consciousness") into an explicit cosmology, and it converges with McLuhan's `The Global Village` and `The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System` while pushing past them: where McLuhan described the electric extension of the nervous system, Ascott describes the network *becoming* a nervous system — a `Distributed Cognition` no longer metaphorical but planetary in scale. (Interpretive synthesis on the McLuhan convergence; the noosphere/subtle-body/global-brain claim itself is directly stated.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Computer communications networks are spreading ubiquitously across the entire surface of the earth.
2. Many esoteric traditions, Teilhard (noosphere), Bateson (mind at large), and Russell (global brain) each name a planet-scale enveloping consciousness.
3. The growth of network information exchanges crosses a threshold at which brain-like coherence emerges across the global system (Russell's mechanism).
4. The connectionist architecture of parallel distributed processing supplies a *distributed* (not centralized) substrate for this emergent mind, in symbiosis with human cognition.
5. Therefore the telematic network constitutes a planetary network consciousness — a subtle body / telematic noosphere — requiring a redescription of human consciousness itself.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Ascott himself raises and names the objection: "that such a global vision of an emerging planetary art is uncritically euphoric, or that the prospectus of a telematic culture ... is too grandiose." His reply appeals to the "political, economic, and social sensibilities of those who laid the conceptual foundations" of interactive systems — a rebuttal by genealogy rather than by empirical demonstration. (Directly stated.)
- The chain mixes registers — esoteric tradition (subtle body), Catholic teleology (Teilhard's noosphere converging on an Omega Point), systems-theoretic emergence (Bateson, Russell), and engineering (PDP) — without arguing that these name the same thing; the synthesis is asserted by juxtaposition. (Interpretive synthesis.)
- The emergence of "coherence" is borrowed from Russell as prediction, not observed; the threshold is stipulated ("we will eventually reach a point"), so the claim is programmatic and futural rather than demonstrated. (Strongly implied.)
- Against the vault's surveillance authors (Han's `Digital Panopticon`, Harari's `Dataism`), Ascott's planetary mind reads the very pervasiveness of networked information exchange as transcendence and harmonisation rather than domination — the same global connectivity, opposite valuation. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the network consciousness is "immaterial" (a psychic envelope, pure data space) yet wholly dependent on the material spread of cables, satellites, and processors; Ascott's planetary mind is the immaterial telos of the object→system spine.
- [[Object vs System]] — the argument is the apotheosis of Burnham's object→system shift: the artwork dissolves entirely into a planet-scale distributed system whose "content" is a shared consciousness.
- [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] — Russell's threshold claim asserts that scaling intelligence (information exchanges) yields consciousness (coherence), the precise move Harari's decoupling thesis contests.
