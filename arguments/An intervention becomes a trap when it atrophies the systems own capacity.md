# An intervention becomes a trap when it atrophies the system's own capacity

## Claim

Shifting a burden to an intervenor can be beneficial, but it becomes a trap precisely when the intervention undermines the system's own corrective loop; once that self-maintaining capacity atrophies, dependency ratchets and ever more intervention is required.

## Expanded Explanation

In [[Donella Meadows]]'s account of [[System Traps]], the archetype [[Shifting the Burden to the Intervenor]] is not a bad thing in itself — it is a good thing that curdles. Meadows is emphatic that the move can be desirable and is often done on purpose: "Shifting a burden to an intervenor can be a good thing. It often is done purposefully, and the result can be an increased ability to keep the system in a desirable state." She names a case where the intervention is unambiguously worth it — "Surely the 100 percent protection from smallpox vaccines, if it lasts, is preferable to only partial protection from natural smallpox immunity" — and adds, flatly, "Some systems really need an intervenor!" The argument is therefore not against intervention. It is about a specific side effect that converts a helpful intervenor into a trap.

That side effect is *atrophy of the system's own corrective capacity*. A [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] is doing a "poor (or even so-so) job" of maintaining the system; an intervenor steps in and quickly brings the system to the desired state; but because "nothing has been done to solve it at its root cause," the original problem reappears, more "solution" is applied, and the real state of the system is disguised again. Meadows's diagnostic line marks the exact threshold at which help becomes a trap: "The trap is formed if the intervention, whether by active destruction or simple neglect, undermines the original capacity of the system to maintain itself. If that capability atrophies, then more of the intervention is needed to achieve the desired effect. That weakens the capability of the original system still more." At that moment the governing balancing loop has been captured and overwhelmed by a [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]]: "If the intervention designed to correct the problem causes the self-maintaining capacity of the original system to atrophy or erode, then a destructive reinforcing feedback loop is set in motion. The system deteriorates; more and more of the solution is then required. The system will become more and more dependent on the intervention and less and less able to maintain its own desired state."

The claim's force comes from its structural indifference to content. Because the trap is a structure and not a substance, the vaccine that is a genuine good and the price support that is a trap are told apart not by *what* the intervention is but by *what it does to the system's own capacity*. Smallpox vaccination adds protection the body could not muster on its own without degrading anything the body was already doing; the fixed oil price, pesticide monoculture, and chronic subsidy each substitute for — and thereby let wither — the system's own adjustment (fuel efficiency and fuel switching, soil fertility and natural predators, an effective enterprise). Meadows lists the oil case as pure atrophy-by-substitution: "Rather than acknowledge the inevitable depletion of a nonrenewable resource and increase fuel efficiency or switch to other fuels, we can fix the price... making the depletion problem worse." And she makes pesticide monoculture the same loop: pesticides "will bring back the bugs in greater outbursts, requiring more pesticides in the future." The test the argument proposes is therefore prospective and structural — does this intervention build the system's own capacity, or hollow it out? — and the exit follows directly: "The problem can be avoided up front by intervening in such a way as to strengthen the ability of the system to shoulder its own burdens," restoring the system's capacity first and only then withdrawing.

## Reasoning Structure

1. A system maintains a stock via its own [[Balancing Feedback Loop]]; an intervenor can take over some of that corrective load, and often should — "shifting a burden to an intervenor can be a good thing."
2. Whether this is benign turns on a single hinge: does the intervention leave the system's *self-maintaining capacity* intact, or cause it "to atrophy or erode"?
3. If the intervention (by destruction or neglect) undermines that capacity, a destructive [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] takes over: atrophy demands more intervention, more intervention deepens atrophy.
4. This loop is content-blind — the same structure whether the "drug" is heroin, subsidy, fertilizer, or a fixed price — so interventions are sorted into good (smallpox vaccine, which does not erode a needed capacity) and traps (oil price-fixing, pesticide monoculture, chronic subsidy, which do).
5. Therefore an intervention becomes a trap exactly when it atrophies the system's own capacity; the remedy is to strengthen that capacity first, then withdraw — "the longer you wait, the harder the withdrawal process will be."

The intoxication wears off. The subsidy is spent. The fertilizer is consumed or washed away."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The distinction may be hard to draw in advance.** Meadows insists "some systems really need an intervenor," but her own test (does capacity atrophy?) is diagnosed after the loop is running; the smallpox-vs-subsidy sorting is clean in hindsight but offers no guaranteed ex-ante signal, so a well-meaning intervenor can enter the trap without foreseeing it — "the intervenor may not foresee that the initial urge to help out a bit can start a chain of events that leads to ever-increasing dependency."
- **The account is value-neutral by design.** Treating heroin, subsidy, fertilizer, and price supports as one structure relocates the moral difference into *how* an intervention is designed and withdrawn rather than resolving it; a critic could argue this flattens cases (a drug of despair vs. a public health program) that differ in more than their feedback topology.
- **Withdrawal has real costs.** The remedy — rebuild the system's capacity, then remove the intervention — imposes the "pain" of withdrawal (an oil-price increase, a pest invasion while predators recover), so the argument does not by itself tell you when the transition cost is worth paying, only that deferring it compounds.

## Tensions

- [[Stability vs Resilience]] — the trap buys short-term stability (the intervenor "quickly brings the system to the state everybody wants") at the direct cost of resilience, since the system's own capacity to recover atrophies.
- [[Central Control vs Autonomy]] — shifting the burden centralizes the corrective load in the intervenor and erodes the system's autonomous self-maintenance; the way out is to restore autonomy before removing control.
- [[Autonomy vs Heteronomy]] — the argument is a systems-native statement of the same pole: an intervention that hollows out self-governance leaves the system heteronomously run from outside, "less and less able to maintain its own desired state."
