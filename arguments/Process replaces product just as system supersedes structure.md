# Process replaces product just as system supersedes structure

## Claim

Once art is understood as a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware in the creative sphere: process becomes more important than product, and system supersedes structure, so that the informational and processual layer of the artwork becomes primary and the material artifact secondary.

## Expanded Explanation

In his 1967 poster-manifesto "Behaviourables and Futuribles," Roy Ascott programmatically inverts the modern art object. His diagnosis is that "the world is all process, constant change," that process at the human level is recognized as behaviour, and that art therefore "is essentially behaviourist." From this premise he draws the manifesto's keynote consequence in a single compressed sentence: "When art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware in the creative sphere. Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure." The terms are paired deliberately — hardware/software, product/process, structure/system — and in each pair the second, immaterial, processual term is given precedence.

This is the behaviourist counterpart to Jack Burnham's contemporaneous object-side argument in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968). Where Burnham theorizes the migration from object to system through sculpture's own internal history and states that "for Object Art presence is everything, for Systems Art information is the key" ([[For Object Art presence is everything for Systems Art information is the key]]), Ascott arrives at the same primacy of the informational/processual layer from the side of behaviour, feedback, and the participating spectator. The two are the two poles of 1960s cybernetic art — Burnham from the object, Ascott from behaviour — converging on the displacement of the material artifact by the system. See the keystone tension [[Object vs System]] and the cross-author tension [[Material vs Immaterial]] (the Ascott "software over hardware" section extends this to a ninth author).

Crucially, Ascott does not simply abolish the object. He reassures the "object hustlers" that "process culture and behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object, as long as it means the beginning of new values for art." The shift is one of *importance* and *value*, not of literal elimination: the artifact survives but is now read as behaviour ("read like the palm of your hand"), demoted beneath the process and the system it serves. This nuance distinguishes Ascott from a naive reading of dematerialization (see [[Dematerialization failed to escape the market]]) and keeps his position tied to a feedback-completed, participatory artwork ([[Reciprocal Communication]], [[Feedback Loops]]).

## Reasoning Structure

1. The world is now understood as all process and constant change.
2. Process at the human level is recognized as behaviour.
3. Therefore art, mirroring the world, is essentially behaviourist — "art is now a form of behaviour."
4. When art is behaviour, software predominates over hardware in the creative sphere.
5. Conclusion: process replaces product in importance, and system supersedes structure — the informational/processual layer becomes primary and the material artifact secondary (though not abolished).

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The cybernetic / post-industrial diagnosis of the world as process: "Now that we see that the world is all process, constant change, we are less surprised to discover that our art is all about process too." ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/08 Behaviourables and Futuribles]])
- The paired inversion of hardware/software, product/process, structure/system in the keynote sentence.
- The "hardline software culture, always being rerouted, conditioned only to branch" — the immaterial, reprogrammable substrate framed as the creative medium.
- The hot/cool "thermodynamics of an artwork": an over-determined "hot" work is a one-way channel, while an ambiguous "cool" work opens the artifact/observer system to feedback and participation — the systemic, informational frame in which "software" operates. ([[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]], [[Media Hot and Cold]])
- The object-retaining caveat: "Process culture and behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object, as long as it means the beginning of new values for art."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Ascott explicitly preserves the object ("need not mean the end of the object"), so the argument asserts a hierarchy of *importance* rather than the literal disappearance asserted by some dematerialization claims — the "supersedes" is one of value, not of being.
- The 1967 hardware/software metaphor is programmatic and aphoristic (a poster manifesto), not a worked-out ontology; "software" here names process/behaviour/program broadly rather than computer code as such.
- The pairing software/hardware presupposes that art's "behaviour" can be cleanly separated from its physical substrate — a separation that the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension keeps open rather than settles.
