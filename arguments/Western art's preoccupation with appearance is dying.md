# Western art's preoccupation with appearance is dying

## Claim

For Roy Ascott, the art of appearance — the millennial Western concern "with the look of things, with surface reality," with representation — "is dying," not from formal exhaustion but because "it is no longer relevant to a culture that is progressively concerned with the complexity of relationships and subtlety of systems, with the invisible and immaterial, the evolutive and the evanescent, in short, with [[Apparition|apparition]]." Representation itself is, for Ascott, "a fraud": "essentially mendacious, illusory, and counterfeit. The mirror always lies." `Directly stated`

## Expanded Explanation

This is the title polemic of Ascott's 1993 essay "From Appearance to Apparition," delivered at the Fourth International Symposium on Electronic Arts. Ascott frames the death of appearance-art as a *paradigmatic shift* rather than a stylistic episode. The art of appearance, he argues, has always been complicit with the metaphysics it served: "[i]ts concern with appearance has kept it in line with classical science, looking no further into things than their outward forms allow, making of the world a clockwork machine of parts whose movements are regulated by rigid determinism, and seeing man as little more than a material object." Bound to surface, representation, and the determinist clockwork worldview, appearance-art "made [art] the servant of ideologies of both church and state." It survives, Ascott observes acidly, only as commodity: "the art of appearance that is purveyed in boutiques, galleries, museums, and on the pages of chic art magazines. It is international art." And it is dying because the culture that gave it sense has been displaced.

The displacing culture is the systems-based, networked culture of the cybersphere, whose concerns are precisely those that surface and representation cannot reach: complexity, relationship, system, "the invisible and immaterial, the evolutive and the evanescent." Twentieth-century science "has been based largely on what is invisible to human retinal vision, since it has always attempted to comprehend the forces, fields, and relationships underlying 'our' visual world" — and an art still dedicated to appearance therefore "simply gives the lie to whatever is the case, since the retinal gaze can penetrate very little of the material state and almost nothing of the spiritual state of things. The surface of the world hides more than it discloses." This is why representation is not merely outmoded but *false*: "the processes of apparition are authentic and … appearance is a fraud." Ascott poses the choice as a flat antithesis — "Can representation co-exist with constructivism?" — and answers it against representation: "Questions of representation no longer interest us. We find no value in representation … We do not wish to keep up appearances." `Directly stated`

The argument is the cultural-relevance face of Ascott's program; its modal counterpart is [[Telematic art rejects representation in favour of simulation]] (ch. 19), where the same rejection is argued at the level of the *artwork's mode* rather than its cultural fate. Both rest on Ascott's [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)|radical constructivism]] — the position that reality is constructed in consciousness, so that an art of disclosed surfaces gives way to an art of constructed becoming. As a death-of-the-object thesis it is the participatory, telematic continuation of Jack Burnham's object→system spine, so it anchors [[Object vs System]] and the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension from the immaterial pole; appearance belongs to "the material object" and the retinal surface, apparition to "the invisible and immaterial." (*Burnham is not named in this chapter; the bridge is* `Interpretive synthesis`.) Ascott reads the disarray of late-century art — "the whole miserable confusion, demoralisation, and splintering of art at the fag end of this century" — as itself "evidence of the major paradigm shift that we are undergoing." `Strongly implied`

## Reasoning Structure

1. Western art has "always" been concerned with appearance — the look of things, surface reality, representation.
2. That concern bound it to classical science (the clockwork-determinist worldview) and to the ideologies of church and state; the retinal gaze "can penetrate very little of the material state and almost nothing of the spiritual state of things."
3. Twentieth-century science, and the emerging cyberculture, are concerned instead with the invisible — forces, fields, relationships, systems — "the invisible and immaterial, the evolutive and the evanescent."
4. In a culture so reoriented, an art of appearance "give[s] the lie to whatever is the case" and "is no longer relevant."
5. Because reality is constructed in consciousness (radical constructivism), representation is not just irrelevant but fraudulent — "mendacious, illusory, and counterfeit; the mirror always lies."
6. Therefore the art of appearance is dying, displaced by an art of apparition — emergence, becoming, transformation, construction.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The historical indictment: appearance-art as "the servant of ideologies of both church and state," kept "in line with classical science … seeing man as little more than a material object" (ch. 24).
- The institutional polemic: appearance-art surviving only as commodity "in boutiques, galleries, museums, and on the pages of chic art magazines … international art" (ch. 24).
- The epistemic premise from twentieth-century science: that it "has been based largely on what is invisible to human retinal vision … the forces, fields, and relationships underlying 'our' visual world," so "[t]he surface of the world hides more than it discloses" (ch. 24).
- The constructivist warrant: "our insight into the ways in which reality is constructed in our consciousness leaves us in no doubt that the processes of apparition are authentic and that appearance is a fraud" (ch. 24) — cross-reference [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]].
- The Kuhnian framing: the "confusion, demoralisation, and splintering of art at the fag end of this century" as "evidence of the major paradigm shift" (ch. 24).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **"Dying" is prophecy, not demonstration.** Ascott pronounces appearance-art dying at the very moment painting and the commercial gallery were thriving; the claim is a polemical/eschatological wager about cultural relevance rather than an empirical finding. (*Speculative connection*.)
- **The "mirror always lies" charge presupposes constructivism.** The verdict that representation is fraudulent follows only if one already accepts that reality is constructed in consciousness; a realist about representation would reject the premise and so the conclusion. (*Interpretive synthesis*.)
- **Equates a tradition with one of its modes.** "Western art has always been" concerned with appearance is a sweeping generalisation that flattens iconoclastic, conceptual, abstract, and non-mimetic strands (which Ascott himself partly exempts — Kandinsky, Duchamp, Pollock "attempt[ing] to reveal the invisible"); the all-or-nothing antithesis is rhetorically sharpened.
- **The institutional polemic is valuation, not argument.** Locating appearance-art "in boutiques … chic art magazines" is a charge of complicity with the market, not a proof of irrelevance.
