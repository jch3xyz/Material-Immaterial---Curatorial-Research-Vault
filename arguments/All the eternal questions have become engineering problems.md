# All the eternal questions have become engineering problems

## Claim

The old theological questions—how the soul is connected to the body, whether there is any room for free will, whether the self can survive death—did not disappear when the West became disenchanted; they were absorbed by artificial intelligence and information technology, where they recur "in different guises" and "go by different names" but persist like the dead metaphors that still lurk in the syntax of contemporary speech.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the organizing thesis of [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]'s *[[God Human Animal Machine]]*—the historical-migration claim that grounds [[Technology as Metaphor]] and the religious double of [[Posthumanism]]. O'Gieblyn arrives at it autobiographically. Trained in theology at a fundamentalist college, she once studied "the kinds of questions that have not been taken seriously since the days of Scholastic philosophy: How is the soul connected to the body? Does God's sovereignty leave any room for free will? What is our relationship as humans to the rest of the created order?" Having lost her faith, she now lives "with the rest of modernity in a world that is 'disenchanted'"—[[Max Weber]]'s word for "the mechanism of a world robbed of gods." The disenchantment narrative says these questions were dissolved by science. O'Gieblyn's counter-claim is that they were merely *relocated*.

The relocation is precise and named. "Today artificial intelligence and information technologies have absorbed many of the questions that were once taken up by theologians and philosophers: the mind's relationship to the body, the question of free will, the possibility of immortality." The mind-body problem reappears as the "hard problem" of consciousness (she cites [[David Chalmers]]) and as the computational theory of mind first proposed by [[Warren McCulloch]] and Walter Pitts; free will reappears in debates over whether a determined neural mechanism leaves room for agency; immortality reappears as mind-uploading and the dream of running the self on new hardware (see [[Ray Kurzweil]], [[Hans Moravec]], [[The Body as Data]]). The questions are old; only the idiom is new—"which are for the most part technological." This is why O'Gieblyn insists that "to discover truth, it is necessary to work within the metaphors of our own time."

The phrase that gives the note its title—"All the eternal questions have become engineering problems"—does double work. It states the migration, and it echoes [[Claude Shannon]]'s founding gesture of information theory, which the same chapter quotes: messages may have meaning, Shannon wrote, but "these semantic aspects of communication were 'irrelevant to the engineering problem.'" The convergence is not accidental. The eternal questions become engineering problems *because* the discipline that re-housed them—[[Cybernetics]]—was built by stripping meaning and embodiment out of [[Information]] (see [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]]). The simile that closes the passage is load-bearing: the old problems "persist in conversations about digital technologies much like those dead metaphors that still lurk in the syntax of contemporary speech." Just as "the soul" survives as a dead metaphor "long after a culture has lost faith in the concept," the theological questions survive inside the engineering vocabulary that claims to have superseded them—present, structuring, but unrecognized as theological.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Before secularization the world was "enchanted" ([[Max Weber]]); the realms of spirit and matter were porous, and theology took seriously the questions of soul-body connection, free will, and the relation of humans to the created order.
2. Modern science "disenchanted" the world, reducing everything "from our minds to the rotation of the planets" to "the causal mechanism of physical laws"; the soul became "a dead metaphor," a word that survives "long after a culture has lost faith in the concept."
3. The disenchantment narrative implies the old metaphysical questions were dissolved. But the questions did not vanish—they were absorbed by a new discipline.
4. Artificial intelligence and information technology have "absorbed many of the questions that were once taken up by theologians and philosophers": the mind-body relation, free will, immortality.
5. These are "old problems"; "although they now appear in different guises and go by different names, they persist in conversations about digital technologies much like those dead metaphors that still lurk in the syntax of contemporary speech."
6. To seek truth one must therefore "work within the metaphors of our own time, which are for the most part technological"—not nostalgically outside them—while recognizing them *as* metaphors.
7. Conclusion: "All the eternal questions have become engineering problems." Theology has not been refuted but re-encoded; its questions live on, unrecognized, in the syntax of technology.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **A migration claim is not yet a vindication of theology.** O'Gieblyn is careful that the questions *persisting* does not mean the religious *answers* were right. She is "wary of nostalgia and wishful thinking" and rejects the temptation "to believe that the religious life I left behind was richer and more satisfying than the materialism I subscribe to today." The argument shows the questions endure; it does not license a return to the soul.
- **Continuity vs. genuine novelty.** A critic could argue that the "hard problem" or mind-uploading are not the *same* questions in new dress but genuinely new problems posed by genuinely new objects (neural networks, large-scale computation), so that the resemblance to theology is loose analogy, not migration. O'Gieblyn's reply is structural—the questions "go by different names" but are recognizably the soul-body, free-will, and immortality problems—but the strength of the identity is interpretive.
- **The "dead metaphor" simile is double-edged.** If the theological questions persist only as *dead* metaphors—forgotten as metaphors and taken literally—then their persistence is a symptom to be diagnosed, not a wisdom to be recovered. The same chapter warns that a metaphor "can also die when it becomes so common that we forget it is a metaphor"; the migrated questions risk being mistaken for solved engineering tasks precisely because their theological lineage is invisible (see [[Mind as Machine]]).
- **Scope.** The claim is stated in Chapter 1 as a programmatic thesis; its full evidentiary weight is distributed across the whole book (immortality in the posthuman chapters, free will in the predestination/algorithm chapters). Read alone, Chapter 1 *asserts* the migration more than it *demonstrates* it.

- [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]] — supplies the historical mechanism: the eternal questions could become *engineering* problems only after [[Cybernetics]] stripped meaning and body out of information, a contestable choice rather than a necessity.
- [[The disappearance of the body is the symptom of a new subjectivity]] — the immortality question re-housed: the dream that the self is informational pattern separable from its substrate is the engineering form of the soul's survival of the body.
- [[Conscious agency has never been in control]] — the free-will question re-housed: cybernetics and neuroscience relocate "Does God's sovereignty leave any room for free will?" into a debate about whether the conscious subject directs the mechanism at all.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the migrated mind-body question *is* this tension; the soul/body split returns as the dispute over whether mind is immaterial pattern or embodied matter.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the engineering reformulation of "How is the soul connected to the body?": once thought is recast as substrate-independent information, the body becomes incidental, and the theological question reappears as the dispute over whether information can be disembodied at all.
