# The bourgeoisie made irreversible time known but prevents society from using it

## Claim

The class of owners, inextricably tied to economic history and threatened by any other irreversible use of time, made irreversible historical time known and imposed it on society but forbids its use — freezing history into "a new immobility within history"; the proletariat, "for the first time not materially estranged from history," can negate this by demanding to live the historical time it produces.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the keystone argument of Chapter 5 ("Time and History") — the thesis on which [[Guy Debord]]'s whole account of the [[Reification]] of history turns. The chapter has traced a long historical arc: from the [[Pseudo-cyclical Time]] of static, agrarian societies, through the [[Irreversible Time]] of the rulers ("it alone possesses the irreversible time of the living," th. 128), to the bourgeoisie's own new type of irreversible time, which is "the time corresponding to an economic production that continuously transforms society from top to bottom" (th. 141). The bourgeoisie is "the first ruling class for which work is a value" (th. 140), and its victory is the victory of "a profoundly historical time" (th. 141). It thus universalizes irreversible time — it makes it *known* — in a way no prior ruling class had.

But that same universalization is a betrayal. The triumph of irreversible time is simultaneously "its metamorphosis into a time of things, because its victory was brought about by the mass production of objects in accordance with the laws of the commodity" (th. 142). History becomes the single most-consumed commodity — "but only in the form of the history of the abstract movement of things that dominates all qualitative aspects of life" (th. 142). So the very economy that makes historical time general also socially *eliminates* lived historical time: "the irreversible time of production tends to socially eliminate such lived time" (th. 142). Thesis 143 states the resulting contradiction as a single sentence — the bourgeoisie has made irreversible historical time known and imposed it, "but it has prevented society from using it." The class of owners must repress every other irreversible use of time "because it is directly threatened by them all," and being "specialists in the possession of things who are themselves therefore possessed by things," it is "forced to link its fate with the preservation of this reified history, that is, with the preservation of a new immobility within history" (th. 143). The owners of history, having freed time, must now freeze it — this is the [[Reification]] of history in its temporal form, and it is what later, under fully developed capitalism, becomes [[Spectacular Time]] and "the time of the global spectacle" (th. 145).

The argument does not end in paralysis. Its second movement is the proletarian counter-thesis: because the irreversible movement is "now generated from that base," the worker "at the base of society is for the first time not materially estranged from history" (th. 143). Where every earlier ruling class had held history as its "private property" and its illusory-mythical possession (th. 132), and where the earlier producing classes were, like "the peasant masses who... never change" (th. 132), locked out of history, the modern worker *produces* the very historical time from which capital excludes him. Hence the revolutionary project is not to abolish irreversible time but to reclaim its use: "By demanding to live the historical time that it produces, the proletariat discovers the simple, unforgettable core of its revolutionary project" (th. 143). This is the seam that binds the reification argument to Debord's theory of revolutionary agency in [[The Proletariat as Subject and Representation]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. The bourgeoisie is the ruling class of *labor* and economic production, whose irreversible time "continuously transforms society from top to bottom" (th. 141); its victory therefore universalizes irreversible historical time — makes it *known* and imposes it on all society.
2. But this victory occurs *through* the mass production of commodities, so irreversible time metamorphoses into "a time of things" (th. 142) and history itself becomes a consumed commodity — the abstract movement of things that eliminates lived time.
3. The class of owners is "inextricably tied to economic history" and is "directly threatened" by any *other* irreversible use of time; to survive, it must repress every such use and preserve "this reified history... a new immobility within history" (th. 143).
4. Therefore the same class that made irreversible time known must *prevent society from using it* — "Once there was history, but not any more" (th. 143). Freeing time and freezing it are one act.
5. But the irreversible movement is now generated from the base, so the worker is "for the first time not materially estranged from history" (th. 143) — the producer of historical time is no longer its exile.
6. Conclusion: the proletariat's revolutionary project is to *live the historical time it produces* — to convert the reified, contemplated history of the owners back into lived, used time.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is normative-Hegelian-Marxist in form: it *presupposes* that the proletariat is history's rightful subject and that "living the historical time one produces" is emancipatory. A reader who rejects the labor theory of value, or the identification of the working class with historical agency, can grant Debord's diagnosis of reified time while denying his prescription. Debord himself concedes the proletariat has so far only *defeated* attempts — "each previously defeated attempt to carry out this project" (th. 143) — leaving the revolutionary conclusion aspirational rather than demonstrated.
- The claim that the worker is "for the first time not materially estranged from history" is in tension with the rest of the book, where the spectacle names precisely the *deepening* of estrangement (the shift from having to appearing). Debord's own solution — [[Spectacular Time]], "time cut up into equal abstract fragments" (th. 145) — describes an ever more total capture of lived time, which cuts against the optimism of th. 143. The two moments (reification as near-total; the base as the point of departure) sit in unresolved dialectical tension.
- "Once there was history, but not any more" is Debord quoting (the annotated edition attributes the aphorism); the strong claim that the bourgeoisie *ends* history collapses the difference between suppressing the *use* of historical time and ending historical time as such — a slippage the thesis rides deliberately but which invites the objection that history plainly continues, only under a different management.

## Related Arguments

- [[The spectacle is capital accumulated to the point that it becomes images]] — the spatial/ontological form of the same reification that th. 143 describes temporally: the frozen "time of things" (th. 142) is the spectacle read as time.
- [[The proletariat can only take power by becoming the class of consciousness]] — the fuller development of the counter-thesis; "living the historical time it produces" is the temporal name for the proletariat becoming subject.
- [[Abstract time was created by literacy reinforced by the clock not by the clock alone]] — a converging (McLuhan-side) account of how abstract, uniform time is *made*, not natural; both treat measured time as an imposed social form, not a given.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the worker is "for the first time *not materially* estranged from history" (th. 143): reified history is the *immaterial* abstraction ("time of things," the consumed commodity) hardened over a *material* base of production that actually generates the movement. The argument locates the point where immaterialized history can be re-materialized as lived time.
- [[Unity vs Separation]] — reified history is history held *separately*, as the owners' possession ("this history develops and perishes separately, leaving the underlying society unchanged," th. 132); the proletarian project of using the time it produces is the reunification of the separated — the [[Separation]] that is "the alpha and omega of the spectacle" negated in its temporal register.
