# The spectator completes the artwork in a feedback loop

## Claim

Because feedback binds artist, artwork, and observer into one integral system, the work is never finished by the artist but is perpetually completed by the spectator, who interrogates the open system to generate its meaning.

## Expanded Explanation

Roy Ascott's [[Behaviourist Art]] reverses the deterministic model in which "the dominant feature of art of the past was the wish to transmit a clearly defined message to the spectator, as a more or less passive receptor, from the artist, as a unique and highly individualised source." In its place he proposes a participational art whose "basic principle" is feedback — the cybernetic loop of retroaction that "makes an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork / observer." Once that loop is closed, the three terms can no longer be ranked as source, channel, and receiver; they become subsystems of a single self-organising system, and the locus of meaning shifts from the finished object to the ongoing transaction. This is the participatory mechanism behind the vault's [[Reciprocal Communication]] and the wider claim of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] that the work is constituted in interaction rather than delivered as a product.

The artist accordingly relinquishes the privilege of closure. Ascott figures the relation through the metaphor of the game: in the past "the artist played to win and so set the conditions that he always dominated the play," while "the spectator was positioned to lose." Now "the game is never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play." The artist still sets the general context — "the general context of the art experience is set by the artist" — but "its evolution in any specific sense is unpredictable and dependent on the total involvement of the spectator." The work exists "in a perpetual state of transition, where the effort to establish a final resolution must come from the observer." Completion is therefore not an act the artist performs and the viewer receives; it is an act the viewer is responsible for performing, repeatedly and irreversibly, each time the work is engaged.

What the spectator contributes is not interpretation of a fixed content but the generation of information through participation. "Deep involvement and interplay produces information"; the artwork "has variety only in so far as the observer participates." In the later field-theoretical statement Ascott sharpens the point against the communication-theory reflex: the viewer is involved "not in an act of closure in the sense of completing a discrete message from the artist (a passive process) but by interrogating and interacting with the system 'artwork' to generate meaning." Meaning is not decoded from the work; it is produced in the loop. This is why, on the table-top of the telematic transaction, "only the intervention of observers can generate meaning and value," and why a "definitive critique is a contradiction in terms" — the open system has no terminal reading because the observer is, in Ascott's phrase, responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Feedback (retroaction) is the basic principle of participational art, so output from one term of the artist/artwork/observer triad re-enters as input to the others.
2. A system bound by feedback is an integral whole, not a one-way transmission line from a privileged source to a passive receptor.
3. Therefore the artist cannot close the work: he sets the initial program or context, but its specific evolution is unpredictable and depends on the observer's involvement.
4. The observer's involvement is not the decoding of a fixed message but the generation of meaning and information through interrogation of the open system ("deep involvement and interplay produces information").
5. Conclusion: the work is "a game that is never won" — a perpetual state of play in which the spectator completes (and re-completes) the artwork, becoming responsible for the extension of its meaning.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The cybernetic principle of feedback / retroaction, taken as the defining structural feature of the new art (ch. 07, "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision").
- The game metaphor: the artist who "played to win" versus the modern situation in which "the game is never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play" (ch. 07).
- The "behavioural Tarot pack" — coordinates "endlessly reshuffled by the spectator, always to produce meaning"; "the active involvement of the spectator can be thought of as removing uncertainty about a set of possibilities" (ch. 07).
- Cézanne as the historical hinge: the late watercolours "as if not yet come to rest," so that "the spectator becomes involved, he can construct a final equilibrium for the composition out of a variety of possibilities" — glossed by Brion-Guerry's "le spectateur achève" (ch. 07).
- The field-theory restatement that meaning is generated by "interrogating and interacting with the system 'artwork'" rather than by closure (ch. 13, "Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art").
- The telematic table-top as "an open system, in which only the intervention of observers can generate meaning and value" (ch. 10, "Table").
- The corollary for criticism: "A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms" because the observer participates in the artwork's evolution (ch. 11, "Connective Criticism").

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The artist's role is reframed, not abolished: Ascott still grants that "the general context of the art experience is set by the artist." The loop is asymmetrical — the artist supplies "the initial programme or code" and the matrix of constraints — so "completion by the spectator" operates inside conditions the artist established, not from a blank field.
- Ascott concedes a technical limit current to 1966–67: "the artwork is not usually at present homeostatic." The fully self-organising, self-controlling artwork is projected, not yet realized; in conventional painting and sculpture the feedback "operates within the conventions" only insofar as the work displays "low definition, multiple associations, and indeterminate content."
- The claim risks idealizing participation as automatically emancipatory. Ascott's optimism about the "cybernated society" and "creative leisure" frames the completing spectator as liberated, but offers little account of how the same feedback architecture could be used to channel or capture that participation — a tension the vault's later authors (e.g. Han's voluntary self-exposure) press on directly.
- "Generating meaning" is asserted rather than demonstrated to be unbounded; Ascott leans on Eco's "gambling on the possibility of semiosis," which still presupposes a shared code that could constrain, not merely license, the observer's productions.

## Related Arguments

- [[For Object Art presence is everything for Systems Art information is the key]] — Burnham's parallel diagnosis: where the object solicits presence, the system solicits information. Ascott's feedback loop is the participatory mechanism by which that information is produced, locating it in the observer's transaction rather than in the work's physical presence.

## Tensions

- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — this argument is the core statement of the two-way pole: the deterministic artist "communicates, but by a one-way channel," whereas the behaviourist artwork establishes a reciprocal loop in which the observer answers back and the work changes.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the spectator moves from McLuhan-style passive reception to the "deep participative involvement" that constitutes the work; completion is the act of participation itself.
- [[Object vs System]] — to be completed in a feedback loop the work must cease to be a closed object and become an open system; the spectator's intervention is what an object cannot accommodate and a system requires.
