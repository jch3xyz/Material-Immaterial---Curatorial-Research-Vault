# Surveillance capitalism founds itself by declaration a conquest pattern that fabricates legitimacy from thin air

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism founds itself the way Columbus founded the conquest of the New World — by *declaration*: asserting new facts out of thin air, then engineering others' acceptance of those facts through "faux-legitimation," with terms-of-service agreements substituting for the monarchical edict.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff borrows the philosopher of language John Searle's theory of the *declaration* — a speech act that does not merely describe the world or command a change in it, but does both at once, "asserting a new reality by describing the world as if a desired change were already true." For Searle, declarations are not a marginal category: "All of institutional reality, and therefore… all of human civilization is created by… declarations." A declaration is therefore *inherently invasive*: it imposes new facts on the social world while its declarers "devise ways to get others to agree to those facts." This is the mechanism by which legitimacy is fabricated from nothing — a reality "where there was nothing" before. (Directly stated.)

Zuboff fuses this with the historians' "conquest pattern" she finds in Columbus's 1492 landfall on Bohio: a three-phase design — "the invention of legalistic measures to provide the invasion with a gloss of justification, a declaration of territorial claims, and the founding of a town to legitimate and institutionalize the conquest." The Spaniards' instrument of faux-legitimation was the *Requirimiento*, the Monarchical Edict of 1513 read aloud — often mumbled "into their beards in the dead of night" — to indigenous villagers "in a language they could not fathom" before attacking them, so that any resistance could be reframed as "revolt" and the violence as legitimate "retaliation." Conquest, in short, is declaration backed by a legalistic veneer. (Directly stated.)

Google, on Zuboff's account, follows this template exactly. The facts of surveillance capitalism "have been carried into the world on the strength of six critical declarations pulled from thin air when Google first asserted them" — escalating claims to *take* human experience as free raw material, to *translate* it into behavioral data, to *own* that data, to *know* what it discloses, to *decide* how the knowledge is used, and finally to secure "the conditions that preserve" all these rights. Each builds on the one before, "If one falls, they all fall." The age was thus "inaugurated with six declarations that defined it as an age of conquest." Where the conquistador had the edict, the surveillance capitalist has the terms-of-service agreement — "just as obscured and incomprehensible" — and where the conquistador founded a town, Google builds "towns in intricate ecosystems of commerce, politics, and culture that declare the legitimacy and inevitability of all that they have accomplished." The genius of the maneuver is that it needs no consent: as Zuboff notes, Eric Schmidt asked for trust, "but Google's 'declarations' ensured that it did not require our trust to succeed." (Directly stated.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. A declaration (Searle) creates institutional reality by representing a desired change as already accomplished — "We make something the case by representing it as being the case" — and such reality "rise[s] or fall[s] on the strength of others' acceptance of the new facts."
2. Declarations are therefore inherently invasive: they impose facts and then manufacture agreement to them.
3. The historical "conquest pattern" is exactly this — a declaration of claims wrapped in a "legalistic veneer" (the *Requirimiento*) that pre-empts and delegitimizes resistance.
4. Google's six declarations enact the same three-phase pattern: legalistic gloss (terms-of-service), declaration of claims (the right to take/own/know/decide), and the founding of "towns" (commercial-political ecosystems that naturalize the result).
5. Conclusion: surveillance capitalism's legitimacy is not earned but *fabricated from thin air* by declarative conquest — it does not ask permission and does not require trust; it forges ahead and engineers acceptance after the fact.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Searle's theory of the declaration as the speech act that makes "All of institutional reality… created by… declarations" — including the wordless example of the waiter and the two soup bowls. (`08 Chapter 6 - Hijacked - The Division of Learning in Society`)
- The Columbus / Bohio conquest pattern and Matthew Restall's account of Spaniards depicting conquests "as faits accomplis." (`08 Chapter 6`)
- The *Requirimiento* of 1513 — read in an unfathomable language before attack — as the historical analogue of the terms-of-service agreement; Bartolomé de las Casas's witness to the atrocities it licensed. (`08 Chapter 6`)
- Google's six escalating declarations and Schmidt's admission that platforms "scale as quickly, efficiently, or aggressively as these technology platforms." (`08 Chapter 6`)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The conquest-pattern analogy is an interpretive frame Zuboff imposes: Google issued no literal edict and claimed no physical territory, so the "declaration" is reconstructed from corporate practice and terms-of-service rather than from a single proclaimed text. (Interpretive synthesis — though Zuboff presents the six declarations as faithful reconstructions of asserted facts.)
- A defender of the platforms would argue that users *do* accept the terms (clicking "I agree"), so consent, however thin, is real rather than fabricated. Zuboff's reply is built into the argument: the agreements are "obscured and incomprehensible," and acceptance is engineered, not informed — declarations "take without asking."
- The historical parallel risks flattening enormous moral differences between physical colonial violence and data extraction; Zuboff uses it for structural illumination, not moral equivalence.
