# We are not using technology but becoming a new post-biological kind of being

## Claim

For Ascott the decisive technological change is not what we *do* with machines but what we are *becoming* through them: prosthetics and genetic engineering are secondary, the real transformation is to consciousness and to human presence, such that the bounded liberal individual gives way to a "set of selves" — an interface — inhabiting the real and the virtual at once. This is a qualitative, not quantitative, change: a new post-biological kind of being.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott opens "The Architecture of Cyberception" (1994) by distinguishing two registers of technological change. The first is the obvious one — "the prosthetics of implant organs, add-on limbs or surgical face-fixing" — which he explicitly demotes ("however necessary and beneficial such technologies of the body may be"). What matters is "a matter of consciousness": we are "acquiring new faculties and a new understanding of human presence." The capacity "to inhabit both the real and virtual worlds at one and the same time, and to be both here and potentially everywhere else at the same time" is what gives "a new sense of self." Technology here is not used; it is constitutive of a new mode of being.

The argument culminates in the dissolution of the bounded individual. "As for the sanctity of the individual, well, we are now each of us made up of many individuals, a set of selves." The figure of the unitary subject is replaced by the figure of the interface: "the sense of the individual is giving way to the sense of the interface... We are all interface. We are computer-mediated and computer-enhanced." Ascott elsewhere names this dispersed condition "telepresence, the disseminated self" and "the distributed self" — "the collaborative mind and the connected consciousness replacing the unitary mind and solitary consciousness of the old order of Western thought."

Crucially, Ascott marks the change as *qualitative*: the new ways of perceiving "involve more than simply some sort of quantitative change in how we see, think, and act in the world. They constitute a qualitative change in our being, a whole new faculty, the postbiological faculty of 'cyberception.'" Genetics and post-biological modelling appear in the argument, but as accelerants of a being-change already underway, not its cause: "Cyberception defines an important aspect of the new human being whose emergence is further accelerated by our advances in genetic engineering and post-biological modelling." This is what aligns the claim with [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] as Ascott conceives them — art whose subject and medium is the transformation of the human itself.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise: The visible technologies of the body (prosthetics, implants, surgery, genetics) are real but secondary — they modify the organism without changing what kind of being it is.
2. Premise: The decisive change is to consciousness and to "human presence" — the new capacity to inhabit real and virtual simultaneously and to be "here and potentially everywhere else at the same time."
3. Premise: This capacity dissolves the sanctity of the bounded individual: each of us becomes "a set of selves," and "the sense of the individual is giving way to the sense of the interface."
4. Premise: Such a change is not a matter of degree (quantitative) but of kind (qualitative) — a "whole new faculty," cyberception.
5. Conclusion: Therefore we are not *using* technology but *becoming* a new, post-biological kind of being.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Visionary, not empirical. Ascott writes as an artist-theorist; the claim is a programmatic manifesto for a [[New Media Art|new media art]], asserted (1994) more than demonstrated. The "new being" is a prospect being declared into existence.
- The "post-biological" framing risks the disembodiment thesis that [[N. Katherine Hayles]] critiques — treating presence as detachable from the specific flesh. Ascott keeps the body in play ("We inhabit material forms with psychic dimensions set in the limitless boundaries of cyberspace"), but the rhetoric of transcending "the limitations of our bodies" leans toward erasure of [[Embodiment]].
- It universalizes ("we are all interface") a condition that is, in 1994 and after, unevenly distributed — access to the cybernet is not a human universal but a material and economic one.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the post-biological being is constituted in the immaterial flow of the cybernet ("immaterial process") while still inhabiting "material forms"; Ascott's "new human being" sits on the immaterial pole of this eight-author tension.
