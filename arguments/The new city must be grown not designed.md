# The new city must be grown not designed

## Claim

Because buildings and cities are living, feedback-rich systems rather than static surfaces, the new city must be seeded and grown rather than designed — with horticulture replacing warfare as architecture's guiding metaphor and the [[Cyberception|cyberception]]-equipped artist as its sower.

## Expanded Explanation

In "The Architecture of Cyberception," Ascott argues that Western architecture has failed because it treats the city as a problem of surfaces, structures, and style — "the physics of space" — when the city is in fact "a zone of negotiation made up of a multitude of networks and systems." Once architecture is reconceived as the design of living, cybernetic systems rather than the styling of objects, its entire logic of production must change: a building or a city is not an object to be composed and finished, but an organism to be cultivated and left to develop. The horticultural metaphor (seeding, growing, pollination, grafting) replaces both the formalist metaphor of composition and the territorial metaphor of warfare in which "the city is seen as a battle zone" of competing styles.

This is the architectural application of Ascott's wider systems esthetic. Just as [[Telematic Art|telematic art]] dissolves the discrete art object into a participatory process and [[The Distributed Self|the distributed self]] dissolves the unitary mind into a networked one, the city dissolves from an "amalgam" of inert edifices into "dynamic, evolving zones of transformation" criss-crossed by high-speed feedback. The city's infrastructure must be both "intelligent" and "publicly intelligible," "comprising systems that react to us as much as we interact with them" — making rapid, visible [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] "the very heart of the city's development." Architecture's task is no longer to make surfaces speak ("Buildings will behave in ways consistent with their announced function, rather than speaking their role by semiological implication"); it is to "fuse together material structures and cyberspace organisms into a new continuum," importing "the paradigms and discoveries of artificial life science."

The agent of this growth is the artist. Because the new city must be "seeded with imagination and vision," and because only [[Cyberception|cyberception]] supplies "the global awareness and conceptual dexterity to re-see, rethink, and rebuild our world," Ascott concludes that "It is artists who can become the sowers of these seeds." This makes the argument a direct architectural extension of his claim that the artist, as a systems-conscious participant, is the one who can author the immaterial environment — the same move by which Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics|systems esthetics]] had already relocated the work of art from the object to the system.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Western architecture is "too much concerned with surfaces and structures and too little concerned with living systems" — "There is no biology of building, simply the physics of space." (Directly stated)
2. But "a city is not just a pretty façade. It's a zone of negotiation made up of a multitude of networks and systems," i.e. a living, cybernetic system, not an object. (Directly stated)
3. A living system is grown and tended, not composed and finished — so design (the imposition of finished form) is the wrong operation; "buildings must be planted and 'grown.'" (Directly stated)
4. Therefore "seeding replaces designing," and architecture's guiding metaphor must shift from warfare (the city as battle zone of styles) to horticulture (growth, pollination, grafting). (Directly stated)
5. Growth requires a sower equipped to perceive systems and apparitions — cyberception — which the artist uniquely possesses; therefore "It is artists who can become the sowers of these seeds" of the new city. (Directly stated)

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The diagnosis that "The problem with Western architecture is that it is too much concerned with surfaces and structures and too little concerned with living systems. There is no biology of building, simply the physics of space" — establishing the object-vs-system frame ([[Object vs System]]).
- The redefinition of the city as "a zone of negotiation made up of a multitude of networks and systems" and "an amalgam of systems interfaces and communications nodes," whose development should make "rapid and effective feedback at all levels" central ([[Feedback Loops]]).
- The injunction to "fuse together material structures and cyberspace organisms into a new continuum" and to bring "the paradigms and discoveries of artificial life science" into planning ([[Artificial Life]]).
- The culminating horticultural metaphor — "It's a growbag culture that is needed, in which seeding replaces designing… horticulture, rather than in warfare… pollination and grafting" — and the pedagogical corollary that "classes in consciousness and gardening should replace the study of classical orders."
- The designation of the artist as sower: "It is artists who can become the sowers of these seeds, who can take the chances needed to allow new forms and features of the new city to grow."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is programmatic and metaphorical rather than demonstrated: Ascott offers no built example of a "grown" city, and concedes that "the traditional architect and urbanist have no idea whatsoever of what we are proposing" — the case rests on the prior acceptance of cyberception as a real new faculty.
- The horticulture/warfare opposition is rhetorical; cultivation can be as controlling and instrumentalised as design (the "growbag" and "grafting" images are themselves technological, not innocent of intention).
- Ascott's demand that the city "be transparent in its structures, its goals, and its systems of operation at all levels," and that "Feedback should not only work but be seen to work," sits in tension with critiques of the surveilled, totally legible environment — the same pervasive feedback he celebrates is, for other authors in the vault, the architecture of control.
- The reliance on the artist as the privileged seeder risks substituting one elite author (the cyber-visionary) for another (the modernist master planner) while retaining a top-down origination of form.
