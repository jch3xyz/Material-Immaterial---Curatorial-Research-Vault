# Inevitabilism is an ideological fraud that erases human agency

## Claim

Inevitabilism — the doctrine that ubiquitous computing and total information are simply the autonomous, unstoppable destiny of technology — is not an observation but a "cunning fraud": it dresses a chosen economic logic as a fated natural force in order to delete human agency and absolve power of responsibility; but because surveillance capitalism was made by particular people in a particular place, it can also be controlled. `Directly stated`

## Expanded Explanation

In Chapter 7, [[Shoshana Zuboff]] turns from documenting the apparatus to anatomizing its protective ideology. Among the data scientists and Silicon Valley leaders she studies, the conviction that everything "will be connected, knowable, and actionable" is "an article of faith," so pervasive that "within the tech community it can be considered a full-blown ideology of inevitabilism." `Directly stated` Google's leaders are "passionate inevitabilists" — Schmidt and Cohen open *The New Digital Age* with "Soon everyone on Earth will be connected" and invoke "predictive 'laws'" like Moore's Law as "this new iron law of necessity." `Directly stated` The rhetoric works by *historical framing*: the apparatus is cast as a new "age," "era," "wave," "phase," or "stage," lending "a veneer of gravitas" that "conveys the futility of opposition to the categorical inevitability of the march toward ubiquity." `Directly stated`

The fraud is structural, not incidental. Inevitabilist messages present the apparatus "as the product of technological forces that operate beyond human agency and the choices of communities, an implacable movement that originates outside history." `Directly stated` Zuboff's two governing images make the alibi visible. First, Victor Frankenstein: "The image of technology as an autonomous force with unavoidable actions and consequences has been employed across the centuries to erase the fingerprints of power and absolve it of responsibility. The monster did it, not Victor Frankenstein. However, the ankle bracelet does not monitor the prisoner; the criminal justice system does that." `Directly stated` Second, Steinbeck's bank in *The Grapes of Wrath*, whose agents tell the dispossessed farmers that "The bank is something more than men… It's the monster. Men made it, but they can't control it." `Directly stated` In both, an institution built by people is reframed as an autonomous beast so that no one can be held to account.

This is why Zuboff names inevitabilism an ideological weapon rather than a forecast: "Every doctrine of inevitability carries a weaponized virus of moral nihilism programmed to target human agency and delete resistance and creativity from the text of human possibility. Inevitability rhetoric is a cunning fraud designed to render us helpless and passive in the face of implacable forces." `Directly stated` Its aim is to make us "succumb to the naturalistic fallacy" — to slide from *is* to *ought*, concluding that "because surveillance capitalism is successful—its rules must obviously be right and good." `Directly stated` The decisive counter-move is a single restoration of agency: "Men and women made it, and they can control it. They merely choose not to do so." `Directly stated` Inevitability is thereby exposed not as a property of the world but as a story told to forestall the politics that could unmake it.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The purveyors of ubiquitous computing share a near-universal faith that total connection and information are coming — a "full-blown ideology of inevitabilism." `Directly stated`
2. This rhetoric casts the apparatus as an autonomous force "beyond human agency," originating "outside history" and marching toward an unavoidable future. `Directly stated`
3. Attributing autonomy to technology "erase[s] the fingerprints of power and absolve[s] it of responsibility" — *the monster did it, not Victor Frankenstein*; *Men made it, but they can't control it* (Steinbeck's bank). `Directly stated`
4. The mechanism is the naturalistic fallacy: from "surveillance capitalism is successful" the doctrine smuggles "its rules must obviously be right and good." `Directly stated`
5. But the responsibility is real and locatable: "the ankle bracelet does not monitor the prisoner; the criminal justice system does that." `Directly stated`
6. Conclusion: inevitabilism is "a cunning fraud" engineered to "delete resistance"; since "Men and women made it, and they can control it," the future is contingent and contestable, and the only thing inevitable is the rhetoric insisting otherwise. `Directly stated`

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The 52-practitioner interview study (19 companies, 586 combined years): "nearly every interviewee regarded inevitability rhetoric as a Trojan horse for powerful economic imperatives," and each lamented the absence of any critical discussion of it (Ch.7).
- The marketing director's candid admission: "You can say 'exponential' and 'inevitable' as much as you want. The bottom line is that the Valley has decided that this has to be the next big thing" (Ch.7).
- The "hammer and nails" parable and the systems architect's "manifest destiny" speech ("The IoT is inevitable like getting to the Pacific Ocean was inevitable") as raw specimens of inevitabilist self-justification (Ch.7).
- Schmidt and Cohen's *The New Digital Age* ("Soon everyone on Earth will be connected"; "Many of the changes we discuss are inevitable. They're coming") as the corporate canon of the doctrine (Ch.7).
- Two literary-historical figures: Mary Shelley's Frankenstein (the monster as the disowned creation) and Steinbeck's bank-monster in *The Grapes of Wrath* (Ch.7).
- Langdon Winner on the unexamined acceptance of technology "as given or inevitable simply because no one bothered to ask whether there were other possibilities" (Ch.7).
- The historical parallel to Marxist inevitabilism ("Its fall and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable") to show that inevitability is "the opposite of politics and history" (Ch.7).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is normative as much as descriptive: showing that the apparatus "was made" establishes contingency *in principle*, but the closing concession — they "merely choose not to" control it — concedes that the *capacity* for control may be far from the *political will or leverage* to exercise it against entrenched power. Exposing the fraud does not by itself supply the institutions that would act on it. `Interpretive synthesis`
- A determinist could rejoin that not every appeal to technological momentum is bad faith: some trajectories (network effects, compounding compute) really do constrain choice, so the charge of "fraud" risks flattening genuine path-dependence into pure ideology. Zuboff anticipates this by locating the fingerprints — the *interests* the rhetoric serves — rather than denying that technologies have affordances. `Interpretive synthesis`
- The Frankenstein/Steinbeck framing personalizes responsibility ("Men and women made it"), which clarifies accountability but can understate how impersonal market structures and the [[Dispossession proceeds through a predictable cycle that normalizes the unthinkable as inevitable|dispossession cycle]] reproduce the apparatus even against the wishes of individual makers. `Interpretive synthesis`
