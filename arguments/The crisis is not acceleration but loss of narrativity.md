# The crisis is not acceleration but loss of narrativity

## Claim

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], the diagnostic error of the acceleration literature is to mistake the symptom for the cause: the actual crisis of contemporary temporality is the **scattering and dissociation of temporality** through the loss of [[Narrativity]], and because speed is merely a consequence of denarrativization, deceleration cannot repair what only **narrative gravity** can.

## Expanded Explanation

Han inverts the standard critique of modern speed (associated with thinkers such as Hartmut Rosa and Paul Virilio) by arguing that acceleration is not a primary force acting on time but a feature of a particular *kind* of time — additive, denarrativized time. Only a process that has already been emptied of [[Narrativity]] *admits* acceleration; a sacrificial act, a [[Ritual]], or a [[Procession]] cannot be sped up because they possess their own rhythm and tact. Where there is no narrative arc, no threshold, no scenography of meaning, time collapses into a "mere series of punctual, atomized presences" that buzz without direction.

The remedy, therefore, cannot be slowing down. Deceleration applied to atomized time merely yields slower atomization. Han uses an explicit chemical-fragrance metaphor (see [[Atoms and Molecules of Narration]]) to describe what is required instead: isolated atoms have no fragrance; only when a "figurative attraction, a narrative gravity" binds them into "fragrant molecules" does time acquire tact, rhythm, and the phosphorescence of duration. The diagnosis is thus structural, not kinetic — it concerns the *form* of time, not its tempo.

This places Han in pointed disagreement with theorists who treat speed itself as the pathology of late modernity. For Han, accelerationism and decelerationism share the same error: both treat tempo as primary. The real lost dimension is the [[Narrative Form of Time]], which alone can produce conclusions, thresholds, and meaning.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Narrative processes (rituals, ceremonies, processions, pilgrimages) possess their own internal temporality — tact and rhythm — that resists acceleration.
2. Additive processes (the processor, computation, information accumulation) are transparent and lack narrativity; only such processes *admit* acceleration.
3. Therefore acceleration is not an autonomous force but a property that emerges only once time has been denarrativized.
4. What is experienced as the crisis of speed is in fact the prior condition: temporality has been "scattered and dissociated" into atomized, punctual presences.
5. Atomized time, like isolated atoms, has no fragrance — no [[Aura]], no afterglow, no completion.
6. Deceleration applied to atomized time produces only slower atoms; it cannot generate the "figurative attraction" or "narrative gravity" that binds atoms into fragrant molecules.
7. Therefore the cure is not slower time but renarrativized time — the restoration of thresholds, transitions, selection, and the [[Negativity]] that constitutes narrative tension.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The contrast between the [[Procession]] and the processor: both derive from *procedere* ("to step forward"), but only the procession is harnessed by narration and so cannot be meaningfully accelerated; the processor "tells nothing — it simply counts" (see chapter quote below).
- The contrast between the [[Pilgrim vs Tourist|Pilgrim and the Tourist]]: the pilgrim's path is "rich in significance" and charged with meanings of atonement, healing, and thanksgiving; the tourist sticks to the present and traverses empty passage. Speed is irrelevant to the pilgrim because the way itself is narrative.
- The contrast between [[Memory]] and storage: memory is narrative, subject to constant rearrangement; storage is additive, accumulating undisturbed. Contemporary memory becomes a "junkshop" that "can neither remember nor forget."
- The Proustian observation that beauty is "a late bloomer" — the "phosphorescence of time" requires temporal layers and deposits that additive, transparent time cannot accumulate.
- The molecules-of-narration metaphor: "A figurative attraction, a narrative gravity must first unite atoms into fragrant molecules" (see [[Atoms and Molecules of Narration]]).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The chicken-and-egg objection.** One could argue, against Han, that acceleration *causes* denarrativization rather than presupposing it: the demand for ever-faster circulation strips processes of ritual elaboration. Han's text does not rule this out; it asserts the priority of denarrativization but does not fully argue against the reverse causation.
- **Romanticism of ritual.** Han's positive examples — sacrificial acts, pilgrimages, processions — are pre-modern and religious. The argument risks nostalgia for sacral temporalities that are not straightforwardly available to recover, a tension explored in [[Ritual versus Modernity]].
- **Fragrance as criterion.** The metaphor of "fragrance," "phosphorescence," and "tact" does aesthetic-evaluative work without explicit defence. A critic could object that these terms substitute mood for argument.
- **Underdetermination of the cure.** Han diagnoses the loss of narrative gravity but does not prescribe institutional or political mechanisms for restoring it. The note's claim is therefore strong as critique, weaker as program.
- **Possible overlap with deceleration positions.** Some decelerationists (e.g. slow-movement advocates) already insist that slowing down must be coupled to meaning-making practices. Han's argument cuts more cleanly against pure-tempo accounts than against thicker decelerationist positions.
