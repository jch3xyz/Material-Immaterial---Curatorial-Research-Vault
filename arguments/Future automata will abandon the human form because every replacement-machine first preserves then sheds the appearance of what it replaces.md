# Future automata will abandon the human form because every replacement-machine first preserves then sheds the appearance of what it replaces

## Claim

Future automata will abandon the human form because every replacement-machine first preserves, then sheds, the appearance of the thing it replaces — so the coming adaptive, role-playing automata will be "regarded as machines, first and foremost, with no attempt to preserve anthropomorphic appearances." (`Directly stated`)

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham's argument turns on a recurring developmental law in the history of technology: a new machine, when it first replaces an older machine or an animal, clings to the look of the obsolete thing before it dares to look like itself. He offers a small museum of transitional fossils — "'clock watchers' that struck the time, steamboats with sails, and trains that looked like stagecoaches" — and, in the case of robotics specifically, the first automobiles "looked like and were called 'horseless carriages,'" while "the first industrially devised robots were clumsy caricatures of the human form." (`Directly stated`) The human shape, on this reading, is not the destiny of the thinking machine but merely the borrowed skin of its infancy, retained out of habit and reassurance until the new form's own logic asserts itself.

Behind this developmental law lies a deeper structural split that Burnham dates to the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. Since the calculating engines of Pascal and Leibniz, "the problem of designing automata… has been divided into two branches": "the traditional objective of constructing amusing facsimiles of men and animals" and "the designing of logic systems and programming techniques for the solution of problems put into mathematical form." (`Directly stated`) Pascal's calculator, Burnham notes, "performed the human act of abstract problem solving" but "did so without looking human" — the first non-anthropomorphic automaton. The figural, humanoid branch (the [[Automata]] proper, and later [[The Robot]]) wanes; the logical, programming branch becomes "progressively more important for modern automata." The historical-analogy argument is thus the visible symptom of this much older bifurcation: the machine that thinks was never essentially man-shaped, and the man-shaped phase is the residue of an aesthetic, not a functional, requirement.

The decisive evidence is postwar. After 1945, man-machine research "was coordinated under the title of [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]], resulting in electronic pseudo-organisms quite unlike robots in appearance" — Grey Walter's tortoises, Ross Ashby's homeostat, Shannon's maze-running mice — and the abstract "theory of automata… has nothing to do with mechanical men." (`Directly stated`) Already "power amplifiers" exist that "look nothing like human beings but enable a man to perform the work of ten or twenty men." The clanking anthropomorphic shell, Burnham argues, is being shed exactly as the sails fell off the steamboat: not by aesthetic choice but by the same law of obsolescence that governed every prior replacement-machine.

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise (developmental law).** "Whenever a new machine is invented to replace either an older machine or an animal the tendency is to preserve the appearances of the obsolete object" — clock watchers, steamboats with sails, trains like stagecoaches, the automobile as "horseless carriage," the early robot as a "caricature of the human form." (`Directly stated`)
2. **Premise (the appearance is transitional, not essential).** Preserving the obsolete appearance is a phase, not a permanent condition; the new form eventually sheds the borrowed skin and is built to its own logic, "like the factory machines of the nineteenth century." (`Strongly implied`)
3. **Premise (the thinking-machine lineage was never essentially human-shaped).** Since Pascal and Leibniz the design of automata has split into a humanoid-facsimile branch and a non-anthropomorphic logic/programming branch; Pascal's calculator already "performed the human act of abstract problem solving" without "looking human," and the logical branch is "progressively more important." (`Directly stated`)
4. **Premise (the postwar evidence confirms the shedding).** Postwar cybernetic "pseudo-organisms" are "quite unlike robots in appearance," and "power amplifiers" that "look nothing like human beings" already outperform men. (`Directly stated`)
5. **Conclusion.** Therefore the coming "adaptive, complex, role-playing automata will be regarded as machines, first and foremost, with no attempt to preserve anthropomorphic appearances" — the human form will be abandoned. (`Directly stated`)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **It is a prediction by historical analogy.** The argument generalizes from horseless carriages and steamboats to "the coming generation" of automata; the inductive law could fail if the human form turns out to serve a genuine function (social interface, manipulation in human-built environments) rather than mere transitional habit. Burnham does not test this counter-case.
- **The anthropomorphic branch persists alongside the logical one.** Burnham himself preserves both branches since Pascal and Leibniz; the facsimile tradition wanes but is not extinguished, so "abandon the human form" is a claim about dominance and direction, not total disappearance.
- **Burnham relies on Cleator's prophecy as much as on his own demonstration** ("there is no reason why the coming generation of industrial robots should resemble men"), which is an argument from absence-of-reason rather than positive necessity.
