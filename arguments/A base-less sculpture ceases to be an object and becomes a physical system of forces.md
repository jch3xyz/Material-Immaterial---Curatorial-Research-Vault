# A base-less sculpture ceases to be an object and becomes a physical system of forces

## Claim

When levitation and feedback (Alberto Collie's titanium disk) remove the base — sculpture's last physical support — the artwork's reason for being shifts from embodying formal qualities to existing "as a physical system including invisible forces," and the duality between matter and energy enters a new phase. `Directly stated`

## Expanded Explanation

Chapter 1 traces a single long arc — the steady withering of the sculpture base, from Degas's hardwood plinth and Rosso's fused tomb-slab through Brancusi's animistic piles, the Constructivists' near-invisible struts, and postwar floorbound and air-borne work — toward one terminal event. Alberto Collie's titanium disks are held aloft not by a thread but by a self-correcting electromagnetic system: "Any attempt to dislodge the disk relays a feedback signal which strengthens the magnetic field opposite the force pushing the disk out of equilibrium. Hence, the base has vanished altogether!" The base, which Burnham has presented throughout the chapter as the convention "for rooting his art to surrounding reality," the device that "physically defined the aesthetic distance," and the "biological reaffirmation that man is constructed to walk the ground," is the last thing the artwork lets go of.

Its disappearance is not a stylistic refinement but an ontological switch. As long as a work sat on a base it was an *object* whose worth lay in the formal qualities it embodied — mass, contour, finish, proportion. Once the base is gone and the work hangs in a field of forces, those formal qualities are no longer its raison d'être. The piece now *is* the equilibrium of forces holding it there; the invisible field is constitutive, not incidental. This is the chapter's keystone and, stated at the level of the base, the thesis of the whole book: sculpture passes from the regime of the object to the regime of the system.

The closing sentence — "The duality between matter and energy enters a new phase" — generalizes the local event into a historical claim. Where traditional sculpture was matter arranged to be looked at, the levitating, feedback-stabilized work is matter sustained by energy and information; the boundary between the solid thing and the field around it dissolves. Burnham positions this as the hinge between Part One ("Sculpture as Object") and the later investigation "of sculpture as system" he repeatedly forecasts within the chapter.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The base is the convention that constitutes sculpture as an object: it roots the work to reality, sets aesthetic distance, and biologically orients it (top/bottom, gravitational dependence).
2. The modern history of sculpture is the progressive withdrawal of that support — incorporating the base into the work, then freeing the work from "all earthly contact or means of visible support."
3. Collie's electromagnetic levitation with a feedback signal removes the *last* physical contact: "the base has vanished altogether."
4. With no base, the work can no longer be an object whose value is the formal qualities it embodies; it can only be the system of forces that sustains it: "it exists as a physical system including invisible forces."
5. Therefore the ontology of the artwork shifts from object to system, and "the duality between matter and energy enters a new phase."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim rests on a single, then-novel laboratory work (Collie's titanium disks, fabricated with a Cambridge laboratory's "systems technology"); Burnham himself notes the "technological significance" is what is impressive, leaving open whether one engineered piece can carry a thesis about the whole field.
- Burnham distances the result from its supposed lineage: he insists the point is "not because of its association with Brancusi's sculpture," cautioning against reading levitation as merely "the creation of better Brancusis" — the shift is to a different problem-realm, not a refinement of the old one.
- "Invisible forces" and "a physical system" are asserted at the level of the artwork's *raison d'être*; the chapter does not yet specify what counts as the system's boundary, which is deferred to the later "sculpture as system" investigation.
