# The proletariat can only take power by becoming the class of consciousness

## Claim

The bourgeoisie won because it was already the class of the developing economy and could therefore take power through the state and through ideology; the proletariat holds no partial reality of its own and can "make use of" no ideology, so it can embody its own new form of power *only* by becoming the class of consciousness — a total, self-transparent revolution, not a Jacobin-style seizure of the state.

## Expanded Explanation

At thesis 88 of [[The Society of the Spectacle]], [[Guy Debord]] draws the decisive asymmetry between the only two classes he grants full historical reality: the bourgeoisie and the proletariat. They are "the only two revolutionary classes in history, but operating under different conditions." The bourgeoisie is the sole revolutionary class that has ever *won*, and it won for a specific structural reason — "the development of the economy was both the cause and the consequence of its taking control of society" (thesis 87). Before it ever held political power, the bourgeoisie was already the rising class of an emerging economic order; it possessed a *partial reality that was truly its own* (its property, its markets, its mode of production) that expanded regardless of who held the state. Taking power was therefore a matter of ratifying an economic fact — it could be accomplished through the state, and it could clothe its partial class interest in a universal ideology (liberty, the rights of man) because that ideology masked interests it genuinely possessed.

The proletariat's situation is the exact inverse, and this inversion is the argument's whole force. The proletariat owns no partial reality — it is the dispossessed class, defined by having nothing of its own to preserve within the existing order. Debord's two premises follow directly: it cannot rely on the automatic "growth of productive forces" to deliver power, and it cannot "make use of any ideology designed to disguise partial goals as general goals, because the proletariat cannot preserve any partial reality that is truly its own." An ideology works by passing off a particular interest as a universal one; the proletariat has no particular interest to disguise, so ideology has nothing to grip. What remains is the one route left open: the proletariat "cannot embody its own new form of power except by becoming the class of consciousness." Its revolution cannot be a change of who administers the state but must be a change in the form of social existence itself — conscious, unitary, self-transparent. This is why the argument grounds Debord's later insistence (theses 116–121) that a "Jacobin-style seizure of the state" is a dead end that only breeds a new bureaucratic ruling class, and that the sovereign [[Representation|councils]] — federating delegates "answerable to their base and revocable at any moment" — are the only adequate form: they are the terrain where "the objective preconditions of historical consciousness are brought together," marking "the end of specialization, hierarchy and separation." The class of consciousness cannot be represented by a party standing above it; it must be its own self-conscious activity or it is nothing.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The bourgeoisie and the proletariat are the only two genuinely revolutionary classes, but under opposite conditions (thesis 88).
2. The bourgeoisie won because it was already "the class of the developing economy": its power was a preexisting economic fact that the seizure of the state merely ratified (thesis 87).
3. Because it *had* a partial reality of its own, the bourgeoisie could disguise its partial interest as a general one — it could "make use of" ideology — and take power through the existing state form.
4. The proletariat, by contrast, "cannot preserve any partial reality that is truly its own"; it is defined by dispossession.
5. Therefore (a) the mere "growth of productive forces will not in itself guarantee the emergence of such a power," and (b) the proletariat "cannot make use of any ideology designed to disguise partial goals as general goals."
6. Therefore a "Jacobin-style seizure of the state" — the bourgeois route — "cannot be a means to this end."
7. Conclusion: the proletariat "cannot embody its own new form of power except by becoming the class of consciousness" — a total, self-transparent revolution whose only adequate form is the sovereign workers' councils, not the party or the state (theses 116–121).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The premise that the proletariat owns no partial reality is a theoretical stipulation, not an empirical finding.** Actual workers plainly do hold partial interests (wages, trade-union gains, national identity); Debord's claim is that these are precisely the terrain of the proletariat's *false consciousness* and integration into the [[Spectacle]], not a reality "truly its own." A critic can hold that this makes the argument circular — the class is defined so that only a total revolution counts as its own act. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **The rejection of ideology-as-such doubles as a rejection of the Leninist vanguard**, and Debord knows the reply: without a disguising universalism or a directing party, spontaneity may never cohere into power. His answer is not that organization is unnecessary but that a revolutionary organization "cannot claim to represent the working class" (thesis 121) — a wager on self-organization whose historical record (the councils were "immediately opposed and defeated," thesis 116) he himself concedes. `Directly stated`.
- **Scope.** Thesis 88 states the asymmetry and its two disqualifications; the positive account of *how* consciousness becomes a form of power — the councils, the fusion of theory and practice (thesis 90), the [[Representation|representation]] that must not become "externalized and alienated in the form of a working-class party" — is developed across theses 89–121 rather than in the single passage. `Strongly implied`.

## Related Arguments

- [[Spectacular oppositions conceal the unity of the capitalist system]] — the reverse face of this claim: because the bourgeoisie's power is total and self-justifying, only a total (conscious, self-transparent) negation can answer it; a merely partial or statist opposition is reabsorbed as another spectacular pole.
- [[The spectacle is a social relation mediated by images not a collection of images]] — if the spectacle is a social relation and not a set of objects, then overturning it requires the class of consciousness transforming those relations, not a Jacobin transfer of the state apparatus.

## Tensions

- [[Subject vs Object]] — the argument turns the proletariat from the mere *object* of an economic process (which is how the "growth of productive forces" model treats it) into the conscious *subject* of history; "becoming the class of consciousness" is precisely the passage from object to self-acting subject.
- [[Unity vs Separation]] — bourgeois power is compatible with separation (the state above society, ideology above interest, representatives above the represented); proletarian power, having no partial reality to defend, can exist only as unitary self-transparent activity, "the end of specialization, hierarchy and separation."
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the proletariat cannot be *emancipated* by a controlling apparatus (party or state) acting on its behalf; Debord's contrast makes conscious self-emancipation the only form its freedom can take, since any representative power reintroduces the domination it was meant to abolish.
