# Broad relinquishment is unworkable and would require totalitarianism and make the dangers worse

## Claim

Broad relinquishment of GNR technologies would require a totalitarian global regime to enforce, would drive development underground to the least responsible actors, and would disarm the very defensive technologies we need — making the dangers worse, not better.

## Expanded Explanation

Kurzweil directly engages the relinquishment position associated with Bill Joy and, more aggressively, with Bill McKibben and Ted Kaczynski. His target is specifically *broad* relinquishment — the proposal to ban entire fields (nanotechnology, biotechnology, robotics) rather than specific high-risk practices. He does not dismiss the dangers that motivate the call; he shares the fear of self-replicating pathogens, gray goo, and unfriendly AI. What he disputes is the proposed remedy. *(Directly stated)*

The core structural objection is that technology is too deeply integrated to ban selectively. Modern medicine depends on chemistry, physics, biology, and computer science; nanotechnology is the "inevitable end result of the ongoing miniaturisation of technology of all kinds." Forgoing one field means forgoing all the fields that feed it — and those fields are what produce food security, medicine, and economic growth. The only coherent enforcement of broad relinquishment would be a comprehensive ban on technological progress as such, which would require the dismantling of the open, decentralised communication infrastructure that is itself democracy's greatest ally. In Kurzweil's formulation this means a "Brave New World style of totalitarian government" — precisely the kind of regime that would be most likely to *misuse* the technologies it was suppressing. *(Directly stated)*

The second, and in some ways more devastating, structural objection is that relinquishment makes the dangers worse rather than eliminating them. Kurzweil's argument here is an analogy from the software industry: defensive technologies against computer viruses have worked precisely *because* knowledge was widely available to responsible practitioners. Restriction would have slowed defensive responses while doing nothing to stop malicious actors, who are by definition outside any voluntary regulatory agreement. The analogy extends directly to GNR: "the surest way to prevent development of effective defensive technologies would be to relinquish the pursuit of knowledge in a number of broad areas." ([[Defensive Technologies]] are not a luxury; they are the only durable answer.) The responsible scientists who would develop [[Defensive Technologies]] would be driven out of the field; "rogue states" and underground actors would inherit the expertise. *(Directly stated)*

Kurzweil does acknowledge fine-grained relinquishment as appropriate: targeted moratoria (such as Asilomar 1975), bans on specific high-risk practices (self-replicating nanotechnology in a natural environment, publication of pathogen genomes), and ethical guidelines like the Foresight Institute's broadcast architecture. The argument is not that all restraint is wrong but that the *granularity* matters decisively. Coarse-grained bans are both unenforceable and counterproductive; fine-grained targeted relinquishment combined with aggressive investment in [[Defensive Technologies]] is the only workable path. *(Directly stated)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. Broad relinquishment targets entire fields (nanotechnology, biotechnology); but technology is an integrated system — eliminating one field requires eliminating the whole technological substrate.
2. Enforcing such a ban globally would require a totalitarian government capable of suppressing all technical development — which is inconsistent with democratic values and decentralised communication.
3. Even if enforceable, such a regime would be exactly the kind of actor most likely to misuse suppressed technologies; responsible practitioners would be sidelined while rogue states and underground actors would retain and exploit the expertise.
4. The software-virus analogy: defensive technologies succeed when knowledge is open and widely held by responsible actors; restriction slows the defenders without stopping the attackers.
5. Therefore broad relinquishment makes the dangers worse; the correct response is fine-grained, targeted relinquishment of specific high-risk practices plus massive investment in [[Defensive Technologies]].
6. [[Self-Replication Risk (Gray Goo)]] and engineered pathogens are real existential threats — but the answer to them is a nanotechnology immune system and antiviral investment, not abandonment of the fields that would produce those defences.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Misreads Joy.** Kurzweil himself acknowledges that Joy says broad relinquishment was "never his intent" — Joy advocates targeted prohibition (no self-replicating nanotechnology, no pathogen genome publication), not blanket technology bans. Kurzweil's strongest rhetorical ammunition is aimed at McKibben and Kaczynski, but the label "relinquishment" gets attached to Joy's position in public debate. The argument is sharper against the McKibben/Kaczynski wing than against Joy's actual published position.
- **The underground assumption.** The claim that broad relinquishment "drives technology underground to rogue states" assumes that absent restraint, responsible actors *will* develop effective defences in time. That is precisely what relinquishment advocates doubt. The argument is partly circular: "open development produces better defences" is true only if the defence race keeps pace with the offence race, which is not guaranteed.
- **Software analogy is imperfect.** Computer viruses are not existential threats; Kurzweil concedes this, then argues that the very absence of lethality explains why the defensive community mobilised with less urgency. Extending the analogy to GNR — which *are* potentially existential — requires believing that more is-at-stake produces proportionally better defensive investment, which is an empirical bet, not a logical necessity.
- **Fine-grained/broad distinction is unstable.** Kurzweil offers no principled criterion for where fine-grained relinquishment ends and unworkable broad relinquishment begins. The Foresight Institute ban on self-replicating nanotechnology in natural environments is itself a fairly broad prohibition. The argument risks being tautological: "relinquishment works when it is targeted enough" — but "targeted enough" is defined by what works.
- **Democratic decentralisation as defence.** Kurzweil treats the spread of decentralised electronic communication as a structural barrier to totalitarianism, therefore as a structural barrier to enforced relinquishment. This is optimistic about the relationship between technology and political freedom; the same surveillance tools that enable open communication enable state surveillance and control.
