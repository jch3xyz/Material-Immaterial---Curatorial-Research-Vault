# We have leased our central nervous systems to private corporations

## Claim

Because electric technology is a literal extension of our central nervous systems, the Narcissus attitude of treating those extensions as external lets commercial interests take a lease on our eyes, ears, and nerves — leaving us, McLuhan says, with no rights left.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan's argument runs directly from his thesis that media are [[Media as Extensions of Man|extensions of man]]. In the electric age the relevant extension is not a hand or a wheel but the whole nervous system: "Electric technology is directly related to our central nervous systems" (see [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]]). From this premise he draws a consequence about ownership and rights. If the public's senses *are* the medium, then "what the public wants" is an absurd question — like asking people what sights and sounds they would prefer around them in a city. The senses are not a market to be polled; they are the very thing being played over.

The corruption enters through the [[Narcosis|Narcissus attitude]]: as long as we regard the extensions of our own bodies as "really out there and really independent of us," we treat our own nerves as external real estate that can be rented out. Commercial interests then "take a lease on our eyes and ears and nerves." McLuhan escalates the analogy: this is like handing the common speech over to a private corporation, or giving the earth's atmosphere to a company as a monopoly — and, he notes, something like it has already happened with outer space. The surrender is total because the thing surrendered is the perceptual ground of having any rights at all; once it is leased, "we don't really have any rights left." This is the vault's clearest statement of McLuhan's media-ownership and surveillance claim, the diagnosis underneath [[Leasing the Central Nervous System]].

He closes by recasting Archimedes as the corporate world-mover. Where Archimedes asked only for a place to stand to move the world, McLuhan's updated Archimedes stands on our eyes, ears, nerves, and brain and moves the world "in any tempo or pattern I choose" — and we have leased him those "places to stand." The fulcrum of power is no longer external leverage but the colonized interior of human perception.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise: technology is an extension of our own bodies and senses, and the demand for it is a closure or psychic consequence of that extension, not a free preference ("Nobody wants a motorcar till there are motorcars").
2. Premise: in the electric age the extension is the central nervous system itself — electric technology is directly related to it — so "what the public wants" played over its own nerves is a ridiculous question.
3. Premise (the error): under the Narcissus attitude we regard these extensions as external and independent of us, and so treat our nerves as alienable property.
4. Step: this lets those who would benefit "take a lease" on our eyes, ears, and nerves — analogous to monopolizing the common speech, the atmosphere, or outer space.
5. Conclusion: once senses and nervous systems are surrendered to private manipulation, "we don't really have any rights left"; the recast Archimedes stands on our nerves and moves the world to his chosen pattern.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The closure/demand argument: technology creates its own world of demand because it is first an extension of our bodies and senses; the urge to keep radio and TV going continuously is "testimony to the fact that technology is part of our bodies." (Source 1)
- The "what the public wants" reductio — asking people what sights and sounds they would prefer around them in an urban metropolis. (Source 1)
- The chain of monopoly analogies: leasing eyes/ears/nerves to commercial interests is "like handing over the common speech to a private corporation, or like giving the earth's atmosphere to a company as a monopoly," with outer space already gone the same way. (Source 1)
- The recast Archimedes who stands on our eyes, ears, nerves, and brain to move the world in any tempo or pattern he chooses. (Source 1)
- The framing premise that ties it all together: "As long as we adopt the Narcissus attitude of regarding the extensions of our own bodies as really out there and really independent of us, we will meet all technological challenges with the same sort of banana-skin pirouette and collapse." (Source 1) — the [[Narcosis|Narcissus]] mechanism.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- McLuhan asserts the literal continuity of electric media with the central nervous system rather than demonstrating it; the "lease" and "no rights left" claims inherit whatever force that physiological metaphor has. (Confidence on the rights claim as *stated*: `Directly stated`; on its literal truth: `Interpretive synthesis`.)
- The argument offers a diagnosis but, within this chapter, little remedy beyond dropping the Narcissus attitude and the broader chapter's appeal to the artist's "integral awareness"; it does not specify what reclaiming the lease would look like institutionally.
- It treats "the public" as a single nervous system, eliding differences in who actually owns the media and who is leased — a flattening that the political-economy traditions (and Haraway's [[The Informatics of Domination]]) would contest.
