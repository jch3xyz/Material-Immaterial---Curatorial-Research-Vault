# Technology's logic of duplication has dealt a death blow to the unique art object

## Claim

Because technology's strategy of duplication and interchangeability treats unique, separate entities as a cardinal sin, scarcity value collapses and artistic uniqueness migrates from the material object to the generative power of the artist's ideas.

## Expanded Explanation

Writing in 1968, Burnham reads modern technology not as a set of new tools for the sculptor but as a *strategy* whose internal logic is hostile to the very ground sculpture once stood on. Traditional sculpture "had been dependent on the inviolable separateness of objects," but technology binds materials, energy, objects, events, and signals into "a unified network above and beyond natural order," within which the discrete object becomes "a cumbersome element." The unique, bounded entity — the thing that cannot be substituted for another — is, in technological terms, "a cardinal sin against the technological strategy of duplication and interchangeability." This is the first move: the singular object is not merely outmoded but actively offensive to the system's operating principle.

The second move is economic and is where the argument bears on the art world directly. Mass-produced "protosculpture" (Burnham's examples run from cement garden dwarfs to Rodin's *Thinker* reborn as bronzed bookends) and the gallery-and-museum trade in "multiples" — "the serial production of simple works by 'name' artists" — erode the scarcity on which the object's value depended. Once "all articles are made commonly accessible," ownership and "scarcity value derived from a single human hand no longer hold meaning in a technological society." Sculpture, in terms of uniqueness, "has been handed a death blow."

The third move is the constructive consequence. If material singularity can no longer carry artistic value, value must relocate. Burnham concludes that "at this stage of technical evolution the uniqueness of an artist can only be proven by the generative power of his ideas, not by material output." This is a striking anticipation, from a 1968 sculpture history, of the conceptualist turn: the locus of art shifts from the fabricated object to the idea that generates it. The argument thus runs parallel to Lippard's account of [[Dematerialization]] and [[Conceptual Art]], reached here through technology's logic of reproduction rather than through art-internal critique.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Technology integrates materials, objects, and signals into a unified network in which the discrete object is merely "cumbersome." (Directly stated)
2. That network's operating principle is duplication and interchangeability, which makes "separate, unique entities a cardinal sin." (Directly stated)
3. Mass-produced protosculpture and gallery "multiples" extend this logic into art, eroding scarcity. (Directly stated)
4. When all articles are commonly accessible, scarcity value "derived from a single human hand no longer hold[s] meaning" — uniqueness has "been handed a death blow." (Directly stated)
5. Therefore artistic uniqueness can only be proven "by the generative power of [the artist's] ideas, not by material output." (Directly stated)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself notes the "multiples" trade was pitched on "a seemingly more ethical level" — an attempt to democratize access. The same mechanism that erodes uniqueness is also a deliberate art-world strategy, so the "death blow" is partly self-inflicted by galleries and museums, not purely an external technological force.
- The argument is asserted on the threshold of conceptual art (1968) rather than demonstrated across it; whether ideas in fact resist commodification is contested. Lippard's later finding — that [[Dematerialization failed to escape the market]] — suggests the migration of value to ideas did not free art from the scarcity economics Burnham describes; the market re-attached scarcity to documents, certificates, and authorship.
- "Generative power of ideas" reintroduces a romantic locus of artistic uniqueness (the singular mind) even as it dissolves the unique object — the scarcity of the hand becomes the scarcity of the idea, which the argument does not fully reckon with.
