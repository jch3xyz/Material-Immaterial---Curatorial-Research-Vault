# Surveillance capitalism does not destroy the self it authors it

## Claim

Because our wills threaten the flow of surveillance revenues, surveillance capitalism does not aim to destroy the self but to *author* it and profit from that authorship; the [[The Right to the Future Tense|right to the future tense]] is therefore not extinguished but **usurped** — commandeered by surveillance capital's exclusive claims on our futures. (`Directly stated`)

## Expanded Explanation

This is the pivotal distinction that separates [[Surveillance Capitalism]] from the totalitarianism it is so often mistaken for, and it is the bridge from Part II's account of foundational mechanisms to Part III's analysis of [[Instrumentarian Power]]. Zuboff has spent the chapter establishing the [[The Right to the Future Tense|right to the future tense]] as a condition of a fully human life — the freedom of will, [[The Will to Will|the will to will]], by which we make promises and so author our own futures across an "ocean of uncertainty." The threat she names is not annihilation. "In the future that the surveillance capitalism prepares for us, my will and yours threaten the flow of surveillance revenues. Its aim is not to destroy us but simply to author us and to profit from that authorship." (`Directly stated`)

The logic is economic, not ideological. Surveillance capitalism's [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|means of behavioral modification]] exist to manufacture certainty — "guaranteed outcomes" — out of human behavior, and the unpredictable eruptions of human will are precisely the friction that its drive toward certainty must eliminate. It does not want our souls or our deaths; it wants our futures rendered as reliable supply. So the will is not killed (which would end the supply) but *commandeered*: kept alive and producing, yet redirected to others' ends. Zuboff drives the point home with the grammar of dispossession she has used throughout the book: "With the construction and ownership of the new means of behavioral modification, the fate of this right conforms to a pattern that we already have identified. It is not extinguished, but rather it is usurped: commandeered and accumulated by surveillance capital's exclusive claims on our futures." (`Directly stated`)

The stake of the distinction is practical. To call this power "destruction" or "digital totalitarianism" is to misname it and so to disarm resistance. What is actually lost is *authorship* — the first-person standing to "speak in the first person as the author of our futures." In its absence "the future collapses into an infinite present of mere behavior, in which there can be no subjects and no projects: only objects." The self persists, but as something written rather than writing — an object tuned and herded rather than a subject willing. (`Strongly implied`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The freedom of will founds the [[The Right to the Future Tense|right to the future tense]] — our capacity to make and keep promises and so author a future not yet real (the [[The Will to Will|will to will]]).
2. Surveillance capitalism's economic imperatives require *certainty* — guaranteed behavioral outcomes — and uncertain human will is the friction those imperatives must eliminate.
3. But the will cannot simply be destroyed: a dead will produces no behavior, and behavior is the supply. So the will must be *kept and redirected* rather than killed.
4. Therefore the aim "is not to destroy us but simply to author us and to profit from that authorship" — the self is commandeered, not abolished.
5. Consequently the right to the future tense "is not extinguished, but rather it is usurped" — accumulated by surveillance capital as an exclusive claim on our futures.
6. Naming this correctly matters: misreading it as destruction or totalitarianism (the wrong-aim lens) impedes resistance, because what is at risk is authorship and subjecthood, not survival.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The book-writing parable: a promise is "my flag planted in the future tense," the product of "my will to will," by which the author transforms uncertainty into fact — Zuboff's working model of what authorship of one's future means (`raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/13 Chapter 11 - The Right to the Future Tense.md`).
- Hannah Arendt on will as the "organ for the future" and promises as "islands of predictability… in an ocean of uncertainty" — the philosophical grounding for treating the future tense as a right (`raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/13 Chapter 11 - The Right to the Future Tense.md`).
- The "uncontract" and Hal Varian's market of guaranteed outcomes: the algorithm "tasked to eliminate the messy, unpredictable, untrustworthy eruptions of human will" — the operational form of commandeering, not destroying, the will (`raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/13 Chapter 11 - The Right to the Future Tense.md`).
- The closing inventory of what the right to the future tense "comprises… our will to will, our autonomy, our decision rights, our privacy, and, indeed, our human natures" — naming exactly what is usurped (`raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/13 Chapter 11 - The Right to the Future Tense.md`).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The distinction between "destroy" and "author" can read as rhetorical reassurance: if the will is fully commandeered and redirected to others' ends, the lived difference from destruction of a free will may be slight — Zuboff concedes the future then "collapses into an infinite present of mere behavior… only objects."
- "Author us" presumes a coherent prior self there to be authored; the more constructivist accounts in the vault — that the self is itself a fabricated story ([[The self is an imaginary story like nations gods and money]]) or the symptom of a passing subjectivity ([[The disappearance of the body is the symptom of a new subjectivity]]) — would question whether there is an authentic authorship being usurped, or only one authorship displacing another.
- The claim is normative as much as descriptive (the right to the future tense is asserted as a "hard-won achievement," not derived); a reader who rejects free will as foundational ([[Free Will vs Determinism]]) may grant the mechanism while disputing that anything *rightful* is lost.

## Related Arguments

- [[Instrumentarianism is a new species of power not a form of totalitarianism]] — the companion ch.1/ch.12 statement of the same distinction at the level of *power*: totalitarianism wanted total possession of the soul; instrumentarianism is indifferent to the soul and wants total certainty. This argument is its ch.11 form at the level of the *self*.
- [[Freedom itself is producing coercion]] — Han's parallel: in the achievement/neoliberal regime power no longer represses freedom but works through it; here too domination operates by enlisting rather than crushing the will.
- [[The self is an imaginary story like nations gods and money]] — Harari's account of the fictive self; sets up the limitation about whether there is an authentic self to author.

## Tensions

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the argument depends on freedom of will as a real, foundational capacity that can be usurped; it is the strongest pro-will pole in the vault.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — control here is exercised *through* a still-functioning freedom (the will is kept alive and producing), not by negating it.
- [[Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism]] — the "author, not destroy" formula is the self-level expression of this power-level opposition.
