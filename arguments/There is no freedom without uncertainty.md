# There is no freedom without uncertainty

## Claim

The future's basic uncertainty is not a defect to be engineered away but the very medium in which human will, freedom, and promising are exercised — so a power that deletes uncertainty does not perfect the human subject but eliminates it, leaving "only objects."

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff grounds the [[The Right to the Future Tense|right to the future tense]] in a philosophical argument about the structure of will and freedom. Drawing on Hannah Arendt, she names will the "organ for the future" — the faculty by which we deal with "visibles and invisibles, that have never existed at all." Where the past presents itself to the mind in the guise of certainty, the future's defining characteristic is "its basic uncertainty, no matter how high a degree of probability prediction may attain." Will is therefore not the enemy of uncertainty but its inhabitant: it operates precisely in the open space where outcomes are not yet fixed.

The mechanism that converts an uncertain future into a willed one is the promise. "To make a promise is to predict the future; to fulfill a promise through the exercise of will turns that prediction into fact." Zuboff reinforces this with John Searle's account of the "causal gap" between the reasons for our actions and their enactment — the gap whose "traditional name in philosophy is 'the freedom of the will.'" Promising presupposes this gap; it is the act by which a free agent reaches across it. Freedom, then, is not the absence of uncertainty but the labor of "steadily will[ing] ourselves to close the gap between making promises and keeping them."

This yields the argument's sharp edge. If uncertainty is the medium of will, and will is the [[The Will to Will|will to will]] that founds the right to the future tense, then a [[A Utopia of Certainty|utopia of certainty]] — surveillance capitalism's guaranteed outcomes secured through [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|behavioral modification]] — does not merely constrain freedom; it dissolves its precondition. "In the absence of this freedom, the future collapses into an infinite present of mere behavior, in which there can be no subjects and no projects: only objects." The deletion of uncertainty is the deletion of the subject.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Will is the "organ for the future" (Arendt), and the future's basic characteristic is its irreducible uncertainty.
2. Promising is the act that converts an uncertain prediction into willed fact by reaching across Searle's "causal gap" — the gap whose traditional name is the freedom of the will.
3. Therefore uncertainty is not freedom's obstacle but its medium: "there is no freedom without uncertainty."
4. To engineer guaranteed outcomes is to eliminate uncertainty, hence to eliminate the space in which will and promising operate.
5. Conclusion: deleting uncertainty does not perfect the subject but abolishes it — "no subjects and no projects: only objects."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Arendt on will as the "organ for the future" and on the future's "basic uncertainty"; "A will that is not free is a contradiction in terms" (ch. 11).
- Searle on the "causal gap" between reasons and enactment, whose "traditional name in philosophy is 'the freedom of the will'" and which "the notion of making and keeping promises presupposes" (ch. 11).
- The Roman origin of "contract" as the joining of wills and promises toward collective action — the institution that uncertainty makes both necessary and possible (ch. 11).
- The contrast term: uncertainty as "the necessary habitat of the present tense," chosen over "the certain tyranny imposed by a dominant power or plan" (ch. 11).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The technocratic position Zuboff attributes to Hal Varian and others treats freedom-of-contract principles as "quaint accessories" to be superseded by data-driven certainty; on that view, eliminating uncertainty is an improvement, not a loss. Zuboff counters that these are "hard-won achievements that have crystallized over millennia," not inefficiencies.
- The argument leans on a normative, humanist conception of the subject as a willing author of its future; a determinist or behaviorist (the very stance surveillance capital operationalizes) might deny the causal gap exists at all. Zuboff concedes via Searle that "even if the gap is an illusion it is one we cannot shake off" — a pragmatic rather than metaphysical defense.
