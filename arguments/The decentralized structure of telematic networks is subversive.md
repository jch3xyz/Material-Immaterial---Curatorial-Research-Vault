# The decentralized structure of telematic networks is subversive

## Claim

Because telematic communication is "at once everywhere and nowhere," with no centre and no hierarchy, its decentralized structure is subversive — it dissolves solitary authorship, individual ownership, and the bricks-and-mortar institutions of culture, replacing them with an invisible college and a floating museum.

## Expanded Explanation

In "Art and Telematics" (1984), quoted by editor Edward Shanken in his introduction to *Telematic Embrace*, Roy Ascott argues that the network has no fixed location and no governing centre. To enter telematic communication is to occupy every node and no node at once; meaning is not transmitted from a source to a passive receiver but produced across the whole distributed field. From this topology Ascott derives a political consequence: a structure with "no centre, or hierarchy, no top nor bottom" cannot sustain the institutions that depend on centre and hierarchy.

The three things Ascott names as subverted are precisely the load-bearing pillars of the modern art world — the solitary author, the private owner, and the physical institution (the museum, the academy). In their place he offers two figures of dispersal: the "invisible college" (a community of minds defined by participation rather than by walls or membership) and the "floating museum" (a collection without a building, "the reach of which is always expanding"). This is the [[Decentralization]] thesis applied to culture, and it underwrites Ascott's larger program of [[Distributed Authorship]] and [[Reciprocal Communication]] — see [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]].

Shanken frames the statement as "distinctly political" — a call not merely for expanded consciousness but for the "replacement of traditional cultural and social institutions." He also enters an important caveat that keeps the argument from triumphalism: "hierarchies are not necessarily evil," and it remains "unclear to what extent telematics has challenged the conventional hierarchical structure of society." The fundamental structure of power, he notes, "arguably remains the same."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Telematic communication has no fixed centre and no hierarchy — to engage in it is to be "at once everywhere and nowhere."
2. The institutions of culture (authorship, ownership, the museum/academy) are organized around a centre and a hierarchy.
3. Therefore a centre-less, hierarchy-less medium is structurally incompatible with those institutions — it subverts them.
4. What replaces them is not nothing but a different, distributed form: an invisible college and a floating museum, continually expanding.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Ascott's own formulation from "Art and Telematics" (1984): the network has "no centre, or hierarchy, no top nor bottom," and telematic communication is "at once everywhere and nowhere." (Quoted by Shanken — see Sources.)
- The naming of three concrete targets — solitary authorship, individual ownership, the "bricks and mortar of institutions of culture and learning" — replaced by the "invisible college and a floating museum."
- Shanken's contextualizing read: an "inherited model of hierarchical, discrete, centralized, individualistic systems of communication" against which the telematic model is "essentially different."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Shanken's caveat: "hierarchies are not necessarily evil," and "often serve vital functions of creating order and differentiation." The subversion is not self-evidently a good.
- Empirical doubt (Shanken): "the fundamental structure of power arguably remains the same"; telematics "has not proven the ability to bring repressive regimes to their knees."
- Gene Youngblood (cited in the same chapter) warns that artists merely repeating "what had become common commercial practices" reveal "nothing... that is not already given, obvious, routine."
- Robert Adrian (cited): the decentralized network "ought not to be seen as originating with the technology but rather as the refinement of an existing network" of artists — the structure remediates prior relationships rather than inventing emancipation.
- Eduardo Kac (cited): telematic artists risk restoring "the same hierarchy they seem to negate" by presenting themselves as "the central figure from which meaning irradiates."

## Tensions

- [[Distributed vs Centralised Processing]] — this argument is a strong claim for the distributed pole as politically subversive of centralized cultural authority.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — Ascott reads decentralization as freedom; Shanken's caveat (and Han's later diagnosis) warns that decentralized networks can also be the perfect medium of control.
