# Most of what goes wrong in systems goes wrong because of missing information

## Claim

Because decision makers can only act on the information they actually possess, that is accurate, and that reaches them in time, the corruption of a system's information streams — through bias, delay, or withholding — is the largest and most easily remediable cause of system malfunction.

## Expanded Explanation

For Meadows, information is not a supplement to a system but the medium that holds it together: [[Feedback Loops]] can only correct a stock toward its goal if a signal about that stock is sensed, transmitted, and acted upon. She isolates three ways that medium fails a decision maker — it can be *absent*, *inaccurate*, or *late* — and each maps onto a distinct pathology of response: "Decision makers can't respond to information they don't have, can't respond accurately to information that is inaccurate, and can't respond in a timely way to information that is late." From this she draws the sweeping empirical wager of the chapter: "most of what goes wrong in systems goes wrong because of biased, late, or missing information" (`Directly stated`).

The claim is diagnostic and prescriptive at once. Diagnostically it reframes many apparent structural failures as information failures — the stock-and-flow architecture may be sound while the signal about it is muddied, so that "you can drive a system crazy by muddying its information streams." Prescriptively it identifies the cheapest available repair: "you can make a system work better with surprising ease if you can give it more timely, more accurate, more complete information." Because information is one of Meadows's more *immaterial* — and therefore higher-leverage — places to intervene, restoring an honest information flow costs little relative to re-engineering the physical system, which is why she elevates it to an "eleventh commandment": "Thou shalt not distort, delay, or withhold information" (`Directly stated`).

Her paradigm case is the 1986 Toxic Release Inventory. New federal legislation required U.S. companies to report their hazardous air emissions, and the Freedom of Information Act — "from a systems point of view, one of the most important laws in the nation" — made that record public. No lawsuits, fines, or mandated reductions followed; the emissions were not even illegal. Yet "within two years chemical emissions nationwide ... had decreased by 40 percent," with some firms targeting 90 percent cuts, "just because of the release of previously withheld information." Meadows closes the loop with a warning about who controls the flow: "Information is power," and those who "filter and channel information" — media, PR, politicians, advertisers — "often ... do so for short-term, self-interested purposes," which is why "our social systems so often run amok" (`Directly stated`).

## Reasoning Structure

1. A system is regulated by feedback loops, and a feedback loop can only act on a signal it receives.
2. That signal fails a decision maker in exactly three ways: it is missing, it is biased/inaccurate, or it is late — and each produces a corresponding failure of response (no response, wrong response, untimely response).
3. Therefore corrupted information, not merely corrupted physical structure, is a — and in Meadows's estimate the leading — cause of malfunction.
4. Conversely, restoring timely, accurate, complete information is unusually cheap and high-leverage: the Toxic Release Inventory cut emissions ~40% through disclosure alone, with no penalties.
5. Hence a normative rule ("Thou shalt not distort, delay, or withhold information") and a political corollary ("Information is power"): whoever controls the flow can either heal or derange the system.

- The three-part diagnosis of information failure ("can't respond to information they don't have ... inaccurate ... late") — raw Ch. 7, [[raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/12 Chapter 7 - Living in a World of Systems]].

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Meadows hedges the magnitude herself — "I would guess" — so the claim that missing information is *the* leading cause is offered as a seasoned estimate, not a measured result (`Directly stated` that it is a guess).
- The Toxic Release Inventory case contains its own caveat: emissions fell "at least as reported, and presumably also in fact" — disclosure can improve the *signal* (reporting) without a fully verified change in the underlying *stock*, exactly the accuracy problem the argument warns about.
- Information is necessary but not always sufficient: where the deeper fault lies in a system's rules, goals, or paradigm (higher leverage points still), better information may be received and ignored. Meadows treats honoring information as one intervention among several, not a universal cure.

## Tensions

- [[Visibility vs Understanding]] — the Toxic Release Inventory made emissions *visible* and thereby corrective, yet Meadows also warns that gatekeepers who channel flows can make a system *less* legible; disclosure is only healing when the visible signal is accurate and complete.
- [[Transparency of the Watched vs Secrecy of the Watcher]] — Meadows's "eleventh commandment" against withholding information is an argument for symmetrical transparency; [[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s work shows the modern asymmetry where the powerful watch while withholding, the pathology Meadows names when she says information gatekeepers act "for short-term, self-interested purposes."
