# In an instrumentarian society computation replaces politics

## Claim

In the instrumentarian society modeled on [[The Hive]], **computation replaces the political life of the community**: statistics replace meaning, the plan replaces democratic deliberation, and politics itself — the human work of establishing and asserting our bearings — is reframed as friction to be engineered away. The behaviorist Planners of B. F. Skinner's *Walden Two* return as Alex Pentland's "Tuners," administering [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]] in place of self-government. (`Directly stated`)

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff builds the chapter around Pentland's "social physics," which she reads as the twenty-first-century fulfillment of Skinner's behaviorist utopia, now equipped with the instruments Skinner lacked. The displacement of politics by computation is the chapter's most explicit thesis. Pentland, she writes, discards the inherited categories of the Enlightenment and political economy — "status, class, education, race, gender, and generation" — as obsolete; in their place "Pentland prefers 'populations' to societies, 'statistics' to meaning, and 'computation' to law." Where the old vocabulary described "societies through the lens of history, power, and politics," the new vocabulary dissolves society into a measurable population whose behavior can be predicted and steered. This is not a refinement of governance but its replacement by a different ontology — the social rendered as data, then as math, then as guaranteed outcome. (`Directly stated`)

The mechanism of replacement runs through Skinner's *Walden Two*, where, as Zuboff notes, "a 'plan' replaces politics, overseen by a 'noncompetitive' group of 'Planners' who eschew power in favor of the dispassionate administration of the schedules of reinforcement aimed at the greater good." Skinner viewed "the creative and often messy conflicts of politics, especially democratic politics, as a source of friction" threatening the rational efficiency of the community-as-superorganism. Pentland inherits this exactly: "computational truth must necessarily replace politics as the basis for instrumentarian governance," and so "Computation thus replaces the political life of the community as the basis for governance." The Skinnerian Planner becomes Pentland's Tuner — "'Tuners' fill the role of Pentland's 'we'" — a new social class that "tweak[s] Big Other's operations" to herd behavior "back into the fold of harmonious confluence." The continuity Zuboff insists on is precise: Skinner's *Planners* and Pentland's *Tuners* are the same role under two regimes, the difference being only that the certainty machines of [[Big Other]] now exist to carry the plan out. (`Directly stated`)

The deepest stake is what happens to politics as such. For Zuboff, politics is not one administrative function among others but the activity by which a people orients itself: "There is no room for politics in this instrumentarian society because politics means establishing and asserting our bearings." Once orientation is delegated to computation, the human work of deliberation — contesting ends, weighing values, choosing a direction together — registers only as cost. "Individual moral and political bearings are a source of friction that wastes precious time and diverts behavior from confluence." This is the keystone of the [[Knowledge vs Power]] inversion that organizes the whole book: under instrumentarianism, perfect knowledge held by the few substitutes for the freedom of the many ([[Surveillance capitalism may realize Skinners behavioral utopia but the knowledge is proprietary]]), and the certainty produced by computation is offered in place of the uncertainty proper to a self-governing people. Politics does not lose an argument; it is redefined out of existence as noise in an optimization. (`Directly stated`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Pentland's "social physics" treats the inherited categories of politics, history, and power as obsolete, preferring "populations" to societies, "statistics" to meaning, and "computation" to law.
2. Skinner's *Walden Two* already supplied the template: a "plan" replaces politics, administered by "Planners" who treat democratic conflict as friction threatening the efficiency of the superorganism.
3. Pentland completes Skinner with the missing instruments; his "Tuners" inherit the Planners' role, tweaking Big Other to herd behavior toward confluence.
4. Therefore "computational truth must necessarily replace politics as the basis for instrumentarian governance" — "computation thus replaces the political life of the community."
5. Because politics *means* "establishing and asserting our bearings," and computation now supplies the bearings, there is no room left for politics.
6. What remains of deliberation, moral judgment, and individual bearing is reclassified as friction — waste to be minimized — so democratic self-government is not defeated but engineered away.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Pentland himself "round[s] the square": he disclaims the charge that social physics makes people "machines without free will," conceding a "capacity for independent thought" while insisting his models "do[] not need to try to account for it." A defender could argue Zuboff overstates the abolition of politics, since Pentland claims only to bracket independent thought as statistically negligible, not to deny it.
- The argument treats "computation replaces politics" as inherently a loss; an advocate of algorithmic governance who regards democratic conflict as genuinely wasteful (the "despotism of democracy" Skinner names) could accept the mechanism while denying that what is replaced — slow, low-trust, friction-laden deliberation — was worth defending.
- Zuboff's case rests substantially on reading Pentland *through* Skinner ("though Pentland never mentions the old behaviorist"); the equivalence of Planners and Tuners is her interpretive synthesis across two texts, not a self-description Pentland offers. The strength of the indictment depends on accepting that resonance as identity.
- Locating the harm in the loss of "bearings" and democratic self-authorship presupposes a normative account of the human as a being who must orient itself; a thoroughgoing behaviorist who rejects free will as foundational ([[Free Will vs Determinism]]) may grant that computation governs while disputing that anything *political* is thereby lost.

## Tensions

- [[Knowledge vs Power]] — the keystone: instrumentarianism offers perfect computational knowledge in exchange for the freedom and deliberation that constitute political life, so the replacement of politics by computation *is* the conversion of power into knowledge held by the few.
- [[The Individual vs The Hive]] — politics-as-bearings is the activity of self-authoring individuals; its replacement by computation is the dissolution of the individual into the tuned confluence of the hive.
- [[Meaning vs Power]] — Pentland's preference for "statistics" over "meaning" is the concise form of this tension: computation governs by quantification, displacing the interpretive, meaning-making work that politics requires.
