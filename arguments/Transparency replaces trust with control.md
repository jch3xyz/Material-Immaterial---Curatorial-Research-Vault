# Transparency replaces trust with control

## Claim

Because [[Trust]] requires a gap between knowing and not-knowing, the abundance of information characteristic of [[Byung-Chul Han|Han]]'s [[The Transparency Society]] abolishes the conditions for trust and substitutes [[Control]] in its place.

## Expanded Explanation

[[Byung-Chul Han|Han]] inverts the prevailing slogan that "transparency creates trust." His structural point is that trust is not a relation of full knowledge but a relation across ignorance: it is a positive bond with the [[The Other|Other]] precisely *in* the absence of certainty. If everything is already disclosed, the wager that constitutes trust becomes impossible — there is nothing left to trust *across*. What looks like an enhancement of trust through more information is, on closer inspection, its abolition.

The compensating mechanism for vanished trust is [[Control]]. In the Preface, [[Byung-Chul Han|Han]] frames this as a switch in the "social system": when information becomes cheap and ubiquitous, the social system trades trust for control. Chapter 9 extends this into a moral-historical diagnosis: the loud public demand for transparency is itself a symptom — it signals that "the moral foundation of society has grown faulty," that honesty and uprightness are eroding, and that an institutional [[Surveillance]] apparatus is being installed as a "moral instance" in trust's stead.

This argument is structural, not merely cautionary. Han is not saying transparency *can* erode trust under bad conditions; he is saying transparency and trust are logically incompatible, because transparency is defined as "a state in which all not-knowing is eliminated" and trust is defined as a relation "in a state between knowing and not knowing." The two cannot coexist by definition.

## Reasoning Structure

1. *Definition of trust.* Trust is "establishing a positive relationship with the [[The Other|Other]], even in ignorance"; it "makes actions possible despite one's lack of knowledge" (Ch. 9, `Directly stated`).
2. *Definition of transparency.* Transparency is "a state in which all not-knowing is eliminated" (Ch. 9, `Directly stated`).
3. *Logical incompatibility.* If trust requires not-knowing, and transparency eliminates not-knowing, then where transparency prevails "no room for trust exists" (Ch. 9, `Directly stated`).
4. *Substitution by control.* When information is easy to obtain, "the social system switches from trust to control" (Preface, `Directly stated`). The transparency society is therefore "a society of control," not "a society of trust" (Preface, `Directly stated`).
5. *Symptomatic reading of the demand.* The loud public demand for transparency is itself evidence that trust has already collapsed: "The demand for transparency grows loud precisely when trust no longer prevails" (Ch. 9, `Directly stated`).
6. *Moral diagnosis.* Strident calls for transparency therefore "point to the simple fact that the moral foundation of society has grown faulty" — transparency is taking the structural place of a moral instance (Ch. 9, `Directly stated`).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- *The David Brin counterposition.* Han himself stages an opposing view in Chapter 9: the futurologist David Brin's call for "the surveillance of all by all" as a "democratization of surveillance" — the idea that mutual transparency would dissolve asymmetries of power and produce a freer society. Han rejects this as collapsing into "an inhuman society of control: everyone controls everyone."
- *Conceptual scope.* The argument depends on a specific, narrow definition of trust as a relation across ignorance. Other traditions (e.g. trust as well-founded confidence, or trust as institutional reliability under verification) would not be threatened by transparency in the same way. Han does not engage these alternative definitions, which is a limitation of the argument's reach.
- *Empirical specification absent.* Han does not specify the threshold at which "easy to obtain" information triggers the switch from trust to control. The claim is structural, not measured, so it is hard to falsify against concrete cases.
- *Conflation risk.* The argument moves quickly between (a) interpersonal trust, (b) political-institutional trust, and (c) the trust of citizens in rulers (cited via the line that rulers must be granted a "measure of freedom to act without constant auditing"). These are different registers, and the inference that what holds for one holds for the others is not separately defended.
- *Productive transparency.* One could object that some institutions — courts, audits, scientific replication — function precisely because they convert opaque claims into checkable ones. Han would presumably reply that these are control regimes, not trust regimes, which is consistent with his thesis but concedes that "control" is not in every case pathological.

## Related Arguments

- [[Transparency eliminates Otherness]] — the Preface argues transparent communication is "smoothing and leveling," producing "synchronization and uniformity"; the same logic that dissolves the Other dissolves the not-knowing that trust requires.
- [[The digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure]] — the substituting "control" is not classical surveillance but voluntary self-illumination, where inhabitants "actively collaborate in its construction."
- [[Auto-exploitation is more efficient than allo-exploitation]] — Chapter 9 makes the structural homology: just as the achievement-subject self-exploits under the sensation of freedom, the inhabitant of the control society self-illuminates under the sensation of freedom.
- [[Transparency is a neoliberal dispositive]] — the Preface frames transparency as economically motivated; Chapter 9 confirms that "today's compulsive transparency no longer has an explicitly moral or biopolitical imperative; above all, it follows an economic imperative."

## Tensions

- [[Transparency versus trust]] — this argument is the canonical statement of that tension: the two are not complementary but mutually exclusive.
- [[Freedom vs Control|Freedom versus control]] — Han closes Chapter 9 with the line "Freedom turns out to be a form of control." The same dialectic underwrites the trust-to-control switch: the freedom of information produces the control it was supposed to prevent.
- [[Knowing versus not-knowing]] — the conceptual hinge of the argument; trust lives in the interval that transparency abolishes.
