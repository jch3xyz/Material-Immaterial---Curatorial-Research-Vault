# Capitalism is summoned by conditions of existence not the drive to produce more

## Claim

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], capitalism and its market forms are summoned by the human quest to live in changing "conditions of existence" — not by the drive to produce more — so a market form succeeds when, and only insofar as, it is shaped by an allegiance to the actual demands and mentalities of people.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the methodological keystone of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*. Against the economist's reflex of reading economic change as a story of "greater production," Zuboff adopts a Durkheimian thesis: production is a "necessary consequence" of something prior, the "perennial human quest to live effectively in our 'conditions of existence.'" The division of labor, technologies, work organization, "capitalism, and ultimately civilization itself" are, on this view, summoned by changing human need — "the invisible causal power" that economists are "systematically blind to." Capitalism is not an autonomous engine of accumulation that pulls populations along behind it; it is a *response* — "Capitalism evolves in response to the needs of people in a time and place." *(Directly stated)*

From this causal inversion Zuboff derives the book's operative principle: "new market forms are most productive when they are shaped by an allegiance to the actual demands and mentalities of people." Henry Ford is the first illustration — he "stood alone in his recognition of a nation of newly modernizing individuals" whose "'demand' issued from the same conditions of existence that summoned Ford and his men," and his mass production "rested upon a thriving population of mass consumers." Apple is the second — the eager customers for "the new consumers of iPods and iPhones" are likewise "expressions of the conditions of existence that characterized their era." Both market forms answered a felt human need, located in two distinct phases of [[Individualization]]: Ford's were members of the "first modernity," the Apple consumers of the [[Second Modernity]]. *(Directly stated)*

The argument is load-bearing for the whole book because it sets up Zuboff's indictment of [[Surveillance Capitalism]]. If a market form is productive only when allied with people's actual demands, then surveillance capitalism — which "unilaterally claims human experience as free raw material" and reverses reciprocity into extraction — represents a *break* with that alignment, not its fulfillment. The "second modernity" did "summon the likes of Google and Facebook into our lives," but in "an unexpected twist" the very needs that called them forth "helped to enable the surveillance capitalism that would follow." The Durkheimian lens thus both legitimates the new tech firms' origin (they answered a real need) and measures their betrayal (they no longer serve the demands that summoned them). *(Strongly implied)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. Economists describe economic change but mistake its cause, reading the division of labor as essentially "greater production."
2. Durkheim's correction: greater productivity is "only a necessary consequence, a repercussion"; people specialize "not to produce more, but … to enable us to live in the new conditions of existence."
3. Generalized: the human quest for effective life under given "conditions of existence" is the "invisible causal power" that summons the division of labor, technology, work organization, and capitalism itself.
4. Therefore a market form is most productive when "shaped by an allegiance to the actual demands and mentalities of people" (the reciprocity principle).
5. Cases: Ford's mass producers answered the "first modernity" demand of newly modernizing individuals; Apple answered the "second modernity" demand of the psychologically individualized self.
6. Implication for the book: surveillance capitalism arises from a real second-modernity need yet abandons the allegiance/reciprocity that makes a market form legitimate — so it is a "rogue mutation," not an organic answer to demand.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The classical economic counterview Zuboff names directly: for economists the division of labor "essentially consists in greater production." On that account productivity is the cause, not the "repercussion" — Zuboff dismisses this as a systematic blindness to "social facts," but she asserts the Durkheimian causal order rather than demonstrating it against the economists' case.
- The thesis risks circularity as a criterion: if every successful market form is, by definition, one "shaped by allegiance to the actual demands … of people," then market success is its own proof of alignment — which makes surveillance capitalism's commercial success awkward for the argument. Zuboff partly answers this by distinguishing reciprocity-based productivity from extraction, but the line between "answering a need" and "manufacturing/exploiting one" is not sharply drawn here.
- The cases are selective (Ford, Apple) and celebratory of an idealized mid-century reciprocity; the "many failings and … many violent facts" of the Fordist order are acknowledged but bracketed, so the baseline against which surveillance capitalism's betrayal is measured may be romanticized.
- Whether "conditions of existence" can do genuine *causal* work, or whether it is a redescription that relocates rather than explains the cause, is a standard objection to Durkheimian "social facts."

## Tensions

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the "conditions of existence" of the second modernity are lived as the right to author one's life set against an economics that thwarts it; surveillance capitalism's break with reciprocity converts the freedom that summoned it into control.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the thesis sits on the vault's object→system spine: the cause of capitalism is relocated from material output ("greater production") to the immaterial domain of human need, mentality, and "conditions of existence," and surveillance capitalism intensifies that immaterial pole by trading lived experience as data.
