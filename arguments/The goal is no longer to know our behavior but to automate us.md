# The goal is no longer to know our behavior but to automate us

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism's competitive drive for ever-more-predictive behavioral data reorients it from knowledge to power, so that it no longer merely predicts behavior but intervenes to shape it at scale — the goal is no longer to know us but to automate us.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff stages this as an *evolution* internal to surveillance capitalism's own logic, not a moral choice. The behavioral futures markets that buy and sell predictions about human conduct reward accuracy, and accuracy improves the closer prediction approaches certainty. The most certain prediction is one that helps cause its own outcome. So the competitive pressure for predictive power pushes inexorably past observation toward *intervention*: surveillance capitalists discover that the richest behavioral data come not from watching but from "intervening in the state of play in order to nudge, coax, tune, and herd behavior toward profitable outcomes." Machine processes that once only *knew* our behavior now *shape* it.

This is the pivot Zuboff names "from knowledge to power." Automating information flows *about* us was the prior phase — the extraction of [[Behavioral Surplus]] and its refinement into [[Prediction Products]] sold in [[Behavioral Futures Markets]]. The new phase automates *us*: the [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|means of behavioral modification]] are scaled up until behavior itself becomes a manufactured output. Crucially, this subordinates the older industrial telos to a new one — "the means of production are subordinated to an increasingly complex and comprehensive 'means of behavioral modification.'" Ownership of those means, Zuboff argues, eclipses ownership of the means of production as the fountainhead of capitalist wealth in the twenty-first century.

The argument anchors Zuboff's concept of [[Instrumentarian Power]] — a species of power that "knows and shapes human behavior toward others' ends," working not through armies but through the automated medium of ubiquitous "smart" networked devices. It is the structural hinge of the whole book: the asymmetry of knowledge ("they know everything about us, whereas their operations are designed to be unknowable to us") matters precisely because that knowledge has become a means of control rather than understanding. *(Confidence: Directly stated — the reorientation, the subordination of the means of production, and the instrumentarian conclusion are all stated verbatim in Chapter 1.)*

## Reasoning Structure

1. Behavioral futures markets reward ever-more-predictive data about human conduct (premise — competitive dynamics).
2. The most-predictive data come not from observing behavior but from *intervening* to "nudge, coax, tune, and herd" it toward profitable outcomes.
3. Therefore automated machine processes that once only knew our behavior now also shape it at scale.
4. This is a reorientation *from knowledge to power*: automating information flows *about* us gives way to automating *us*.
5. In this phase the means of production are subordinated to the means of behavioral modification.
6. Conclusion: surveillance capitalism births a new species of power — instrumentarianism — that knows *and shapes* human behavior toward others' ends.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Efficacy question.** That firms *attempt* to nudge and herd does not establish that behavior is reliably *automated*; the claim's force depends on the actual predictive/causal power of these interventions, which Chapter 1 asserts programmatically and defers to "the coming chapters" for demonstration. *(Limitation — Strongly implied by the chapter's own forward-pointing structure.)*
- **Agency residue.** The framing risks the very determinism Zuboff elsewhere resists; "herding" presumes targets who can still bolt. The argument is strongest as a description of an economic *imperative* and *direction of travel*, weaker as a claim that the human is already fully scripted.
- **Contrast with Han.** Where Zuboff locates the engine in market competition and *external* instrumentarian power, Han locates it in the subject's *own* freedom — coercion that "calls itself freedom." A reader could object that "automate us" overstates external compulsion and understates voluntary self-exposure (see [[The digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure]]). The two diagnoses are complementary more than rival.

## Related Arguments

- [[Freedom itself is producing coercion]] — Han's parallel reorientation: like Zuboff, he holds that contemporary power no longer disciplines from outside but works *through* the subject; Zuboff's "automate us" is the market-economic counterpart to Han's psychopolitical "free" self-exploitation.
- [[Big Data is blind to the event and so blind to the future]] — Han argues big-data power can only count, not narrate, and is blind to the unforeseeable; Zuboff sharpens this into a *productive* aim — not to foresee the future but to *foreclose* it by manufacturing it.
- [[The digital panopticon runs on voluntary self-exposure]] — the Han/Deleuze account of post-disciplinary visibility that Zuboff's instrumentarianism economizes and automates.

## Tensions

- [[Knowledge vs Power]] — this argument *is* the hinge of the tension: Zuboff's thesis turns on the moment knowledge ceases to be an end (understanding) and becomes a means (control). The reorientation "from knowledge to power" names the pathological *fusion* of the two in private hands.
- [[Meaning vs Power]] — resonant with Harari's framing: when systems optimize for power over behavior rather than for meaning or understanding, prediction collapses into production.
