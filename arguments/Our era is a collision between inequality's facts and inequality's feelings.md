# Our era is a collision between inequality's facts and inequality's feelings

## Claim

The pain, anger, and violence that define our era erupt from a collision between two opposing historical forces: economic and social inequality that has reverted to a preindustrial feudal pattern, and a population that has not — hundreds of millions of self-determining [[Second Modernity|second-modernity]] individuals who "know ourselves to be worthy of dignity."

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff frames the present as a poisonous mismatch between facts and feelings. The *facts* are economic: under the neoliberal paradigm, inequality has been driven back toward a premodern shape. The *feelings* are existential: the people subjected to that inequality are no longer the illiterate peasants, serfs, or slaves of the feudal order, but individualized persons with complex experiences, opinions, and an "entitlement to self-determination." The reversion of the economy and the non-reversion of the population are incompatible, and the friction between them detonates.

The economic side of the collision Zuboff grounds in Thomas Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* — the law of accumulation r > g, in which the rate of return on capital exceeds the rate of economic growth, producing ever-more-extreme income divergence. Many scholars, she notes, describe the resulting conditions as "neofeudalism"; Piketty himself calls it a return to "patrimonial capitalism," a reversion to a premodern society in which life chances depend on inherited wealth rather than meritocratic achievement. This is the "feudal pattern" half of the collision.

The human side is the irreversible achievement of the [[Second Modernity]]. Zuboff calls the new sense of psychological sovereignty "existential toothpaste that, once liberated, cannot be squeezed back into the tube." When self-determining individuals are nonetheless "treated as invisible" and as "ciphers," the losses they experience are not merely economic — they "slice us to the quick" because the population knows its own worth. The 2011 London riots are her concrete detonation: 270 interviews with participants reflected "a pervasive sense of injustice," a "denial of dignity," and a "sense of being invisible." Zygmunt Bauman names the underlying structure as "the yawning gap between the right of self-assertion and the capacity to control the social settings which render such self-assertion feasible." This argument anchors the concepts [[The Collision]] and [[Second Modernity]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. The modernization process has produced, since the mid-twentieth century, a second-modernity population born to a sense of psychological individuality, feeling both the right and the requirement to choose their own lives. `Directly stated`
2. The neoliberal paradigm, documented economically by Piketty's r > g, has reverted economic and social inequality to a preindustrial "feudal" pattern (neofeudalism / patrimonial capitalism). `Directly stated`
3. But the population has *not* reverted: "economic and social inequalities have reverted to the preindustrial 'feudal' pattern but that we, the people, have not." `Directly stated`
4. Therefore the era's pain and anger arise from the collision between inequality's facts (the feudal economic reality) and inequality's feelings (the dignity-conscious second-modernity self). `Directly stated`
5. This collision empirically detonates into anger and violence — the London riots as Zuboff's case. `Directly stated`

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is diagnostic rather than predictive: Zuboff herself poses the open question of whether "the instability of the second modernity [can] give way to a new synthesis: a third modernity that transcends the collision," leaving the resolution undetermined. `Strongly implied`
- The framing treats "second-modernity" psychological self-determination as a near-universal historical condition; it is most clearly developed from a Western and affluent vantage, and Zuboff acknowledges the second modernity was unevenly distributed and "slated to become a hard place to live." `Interpretive synthesis`
- A counter-reading would attribute the riots to material deprivation alone rather than to a facts/feelings mismatch; Zuboff's own cited interviews mix economic grievance ("the lack of a job, money, or opportunity") with the dignity/invisibility grievance, so the affective component is corroborated but not isolated. `Strongly implied`
