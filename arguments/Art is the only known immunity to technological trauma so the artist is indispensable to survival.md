# Art is the only known immunity to technological trauma so the artist is indispensable to survival

## Claim

Because the numbing [[Narcosis]] of new technology shuts the gates of judgment and perception, no society can consciously adjust to its own extensions by ordinary means; only the artist perceives the present and anticipates the impact decades early, so art is precise advance knowledge of how to cope with the next technology and the artist is therefore indispensable to survival.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the keystone argument of Chapter 7. McLuhan frames the insertion of any new technology into a culture as a wounding operation — "massive social surgery" performed on the social body — accompanied by a built-in anesthetic that numbs the very faculties that might judge the change ([[Media Change as Collective Surgery]], [[Narcosis]]). The logical consequence is that immunity to one's own extensions has never been achieved through normal social or political channels: to be aware of the blow is precisely what the narcosis prevents. McLuhan defines immunity by analogy to disease: "To havea disease without its symptoms is to be immune." The new technology is the disease; its self-administered anesthetic is what keeps the symptoms — and so the chance of immunity — out of reach.

Against this, McLuhan advances a single exception. In all of cultural history the only conscious adjustment to new extensions has come from "the puny and peripheral efforts of artists." The artist is the [[The Artist as Integral Awareness|man of integral awareness]] who, alone, registers the cultural and technological challenge "decades before its transforming impact occurs" and can "correct the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures" ([[Sense Ratios]]). Where the percussed victim takes the blow on the chin, the artist rides with the punch. Art, on this account, is not ornament or "frill" but "exact information of how to rearrange one's psyche in order to anticipate the next blow from our own extended faculties."

From this McLuhan draws his strongest practical conclusion: the artist must move "from the ivory tower to the control tower of society," and knowledge of the artist's predictive function "is now needed for human survival." If the analysis is even remotely true, it "would warrant a global armistice and period of stock-taking." The note's title compresses two of McLuhan's own sentences: art may provide the immunity no society has otherwise found, and "the artist is indispensable in the shaping and analysis and understanding of the life of forms."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Inserting a new technology into a culture is collective surgery on the social body, and it carries a built-in numbing apparatus that "lulls attention while the new form slams the gates of judgment and perception" ([[Media Change as Collective Surgery]], [[Narcosis]]).
2. To be immune is to have the disease without its symptoms; but the narcosis is exactly what suppresses the symptoms, so ordinary perception cannot yield immunity — "No society has ever known enough about its actions to have developed immunity to its new extensions or technologies."
3. The sole historical exception is the artist, whose integral awareness lets him pick up the challenge "decades before its transforming impact occurs" and "correct the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures" ([[The Artist as Integral Awareness]], [[Sense Ratios]]).
4. Therefore art is "precise advance knowledge of how to cope with the psychic and social consequences of the next technology" — immunity in the form of perception.
5. Conclusion: the artist is "indispensable" to survival and must move from the ivory tower to the control tower; the situation "would warrant a global armistice and period of stock-taking."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The disease/immunity analogy: "To havea disease without its symptoms is to be immune" — defining immunity as awareness without the anesthetized blow ([[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/10 Chapter 7 - Challenge and Collapse - The Nemesis of Creativity]]).
- Gustave Flaubert: "The war of 1870 need never have been fought had people read my Sentimental Education" — art as squandered advance warning.
- Wyndham Lewis: "The artist is always engaged in writing a detailed history of the future because he is the only person aware of the nature of the present."
- Emile Durkheim's observation "that the specialized task always escaped the action of the social conscience," set against the artist as "the social conscience."
- The Balinese saying "We have no art... we do everything as well as possible," offered as the integral, unfragmented alternative to specialism.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- McLuhan presents the claim hedged: "If this is true," "If there were even a remote likelihood of this analysis being true" — he frames it as a hypothesis warranting stock-taking, not a demonstrated law. Confidence here is in the *argument as stated*, not in its empirical proof.
- The mechanism rewarding artists can neutralize the very function it honors: "To reward and to make celebrities of artists can, also, be a way of ignoring their prophetic work, and preventing its timely use for survival." The "world and bureaucracy of 'art appreciation'" risks being "a conspiracy to make die artist a frill, a fribble." So the argument is partly self-undermining in practice.
- "Artist" is defined functionally and very broadly — "the man in any field, scientific or humanistic, who grasps the implications of his actions and of new knowledge in his own time" — which both extends and blunts the thesis: nearly anyone with integral awareness qualifies.
- The claim assumes the narcosis can be sidestepped by some (the artist) but not others; it does not explain why the artist is exempt from the numbing it treats as near-universal.
