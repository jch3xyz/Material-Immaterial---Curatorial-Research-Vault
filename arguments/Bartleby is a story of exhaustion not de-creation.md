# Bartleby is a story of exhaustion not de-creation

## Claim

Melville's "Bartleby" is not Agamben's tale of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung] but a story of exhaustion [Erschöpfung]: its pervasive negativity — Bartleby's "dim eyes," the "dead wall," the Tombs, the Dead Letter Office, his "negative being-unto-death" — contradicts the ontotheological reading that makes Bartleby the herald of a second Creation.

## Expanded Explanation

Against the long tradition of metaphysical and theological readings of "Bartleby" — culminating for Han in Giorgio Agamben's interpretation — Han submits the tale to a *pathological* reading. Agamben elevates Bartleby to "a metaphysical position of the highest potency": the scrivener who has stopped writing becomes "the extreme figure of the Nothing from which all creation derives" and "the most implacable vindication of this Nothing as pure, absolute potentiality." On this reading Bartleby is an angelic messenger, the Angel of Annunciation, the herald of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung] that "undoes the border between what has been and what has not, between Being and Nothingness." This is precisely what Han denies. Bartleby's "I would prefer not to" expresses "neither the negative potency of not-to nor the instinct for delay and deferral that is essential for 'spirituality'" — it is not [[Negativity|negative potency]] but "a lack of drive and for apathy, which seal Bartleby's doom." The [[Negative Potency]] Agamben attributes to him is exactly the capacity Han says the tale denies him.

Han's counter-reading is grounded in the *facts of the narrative*, which he reads as saturated with negativity rather than messianic possibility. The office is "hostile to life," choked by a "lofty brick wall, black by age and everlasting shade"; "wall" is "one of the most frequently used words" and "always associated with death"; Bartleby works behind a screen and stares at the "dead brick wall." He ends in the thick-walled prison called the "Tombs," where "all life is extinguished," and dies "in complete isolation and solitude." He had formerly worked at the Dead Letter Office — "Dead letters! does it not sound like dead men?" — so that "on errands of life, these letters speed to death." This is why "Bartleby's Dasein is a negative being-unto-death." Even the small "imprisoned turf" in the Tombs, which Agamben reads as a messianic sign, "only underscores the negativity of the realm of the dead."

The argument is clinched by a German pun. The tale, Han writes, "is not a tale of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung], but rather a story of exhaustion [Erschöpfung]." The near-homophony of *Ent-Schöpfung* (un-creation, the undoing of creation) and *Er-schöpfung* (exhaustion, depletion, being used up) folds Han's whole quarrel with Agamben into a single word: where Agamben hears a theology of creative potentiality, Han hears the diagnosis of a depleted, used-up life. The closing exclamation — "Ah Bartleby! Ah humanity!" — is "both a lament and an indictment," not an annunciation. Note that Bartleby still belongs to the [[Disciplinary Society]] of "walls and partitions," "an obedience-subject" who "does not develop symptoms of depression," and so he is not yet the achievement-subject of late modernity either; Agamben's reading, Han charges, "fails to take note of the change of mental structure [psychischer Strukturwandel] in the present day."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Agamben gives "Bartleby" an ontotheological reading "which pays no attention to pathological elements," making the scrivener "a being of pure potentiality" — the herald of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung].
2. But the narrative is pervaded by negativity: dead walls, the Tombs, dim eyes, the Dead Letter Office, refusal of every errand. Bartleby's "I would prefer not to" expresses apathy and lack of drive, not negative potency.
3. Therefore Bartleby's Dasein "is a negative being-unto-death," and "this negativity contradicts Agamben's ontotheological interpretation."
4. Conclusion: the tale "is not a tale of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung], but rather a story of exhaustion [Erschöpfung]" — a lament and an indictment ("Ah Bartleby! Ah humanity!"), not a messianic annunciation.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- This is explicitly a rebuttal of Giorgio Agamben's reading, which Han quotes at length; Agamben's own account treats Bartleby's "I would prefer not to" as the very figure of *potentia absoluta* — "pure being without any predicate." Han does not refute Agamben on Agamben's metaphysical terms but reframes the question as pathological/historical, so the disagreement turns on which interpretive register the tale demands.
- Han concedes Melville "allows for a tiny sprout of life to appear in the Tombs" (the "imprisoned turf"); his reading depends on construing that sign as augmenting "the hopeless emptiness" rather than as the messianic gesture Agamben sees — an interpretive choice, not a textual given.
- The thesis is period-bound: Bartleby is "still an obedience-subject" of the disciplinary society, so the tale figures *exhaustion* but not yet the depressive "I-tiredness" of the achievement society proper.
