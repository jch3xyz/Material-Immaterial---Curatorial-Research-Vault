# Naming an unprecedented power is the first act of resistance

## Claim

Because a genuinely new species of power defies the comprehension that inherited vocabularies afford, the work of naming it must begin anew — and that naming, modeled on the postwar scholars who first comprehended totalitarianism, is itself the first and indispensable act of effective resistance.

## Expanded Explanation

In the closing arc of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, Zuboff pivots from the *economic* logic she has spent two parts dissecting to the *species of power* that logic produces, which she names **instrumentarianism** — "the instrumentation and instrumentalization of behavior for the purposes of modification, prediction, monetization, and control" (`Directly stated`). Her structural worry is not only that this power is new but that it is *indiscernible*: when critics "speak out courageously against this new power, invariably we look to Orwell's Big Brother and more generally the specter of totalitarianism as the lens through which to interpret today's threats." Reaching for that familiar lens, she argues, "impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist." A power for which "the past offers no adequate compass" cannot be defeated with borrowed names; mistaking it for `Surveillance` of the Orwellian, soul-engineering kind leads to conclusions that "are necessarily incorrect" — the "syndrome of the horseless carriage." (`Directly stated`)

The argument's force comes from its historical model. Totalitarianism, too, "was also unprecedented. It literally defied human comprehension." For decades it "eluded systematic study," lionized in *Look* and on the cover of *Time* even as the Great Terror unfolded, because "the actual facts were so 'improbable' that it was difficult even for specialists to grasp their truth." Only after the Nazi defeat did "the program of naming" begin in earnest — in the work of Carl Friedrich (totalitarianism had "burst upon mankind… unexpected and unannounced"), Robert Conquest (the Stalin epoch "replete with what appear as improbabilities to minds unfitted to deal with the phenomena"), and above all Hannah Arendt, for whom "**Comprehension**… is the necessary response to the 'truly radical nature of Evil' disclosed by totalitarianism." (`Directly stated`) Arendt's *The Origins of Totalitarianism* is Zuboff's template: comprehension as the response to radical novelty, and naming as the act that breaks "speechless outrage and impotent horror" into the possibility of telling, understanding, and therefore resisting. (`Strongly implied`)

The conclusion she draws is methodological and prescriptive: "The work of naming a strange form of power unprecedented in the human experience must begin anew for the sake of effective resistance and the creative power to insist on a future of our own making." Naming is not a preliminary to politics but the opening move of it — without an adequate name there is no object to oppose, only a fog of mislabeled dangers. (`Directly stated`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. A genuinely unprecedented power "defies human comprehension" — totalitarianism did, and instrumentarian power, though "not murderous," is "as startling, incomprehensible, and new to the human story as totalitarianism was to its witnesses and victims." (premise)
2. Faced with the incomprehensible, witnesses reach for "familiar vernaculars of twentieth-century power like lifesaving driftwood" — Orwell, Big Brother, "digital totalitarianism." (premise)
3. But those borrowed names misdescribe the new power's "specific inner logic," yielding conclusions that "are necessarily incorrect" and so disabling resistance. (premise)
4. History shows the remedy: totalitarianism could be resisted and "vanquished" intellectually only once Arendt, Friedrich, Conquest and others *comprehended* and *named* it — comprehension being, in Arendt's words, "the necessary response to the 'truly radical nature of Evil.'" (premise / model)
5. Therefore the naming of each unprecedented power "must begin anew," and that naming is the first act of effective resistance and of "the creative power to insist on a future of our own making." (conclusion)

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The coinage and definition of **instrumentarianism** as a power with "no historical precedent," distinct from totalitarianism in aim and means (Ch. 12, §I) — source link below.
- The diagnosis that equating the new power with totalitarianism "impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist," and the "horseless carriage" / "lifesaving driftwood" figures for borrowed vocabulary (Ch. 12, §I).
- The historical case study of totalitarianism's long invisibility: Walter Duranty's 1939 *Look* article, Stalin's ten *Time* covers, Conquest's "improbabilities to minds unfitted to deal with the phenomena," and the postwar onset of "the program of naming" (Ch. 12, §II).
- The Arendtian model of **comprehension** as "the necessary response to the 'truly radical nature of Evil'," with Friedrich's "burst upon mankind… unexpected and unannounced" and Arendt's move from "speechless outrage" to telling and understanding (Ch. 12, §II).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Naming is necessary but not sufficient.** Zuboff casts naming as the *first* act, not the whole of resistance; comprehension equips us "to resist, neutralize, and ultimately vanquish," but the vanquishing remains to be done. The note should not overstate naming as itself emancipatory.
- **The totalitarianism analogy cuts both ways.** Zuboff insists instrumentarian power is "utterly distinct" from totalitarianism — "no interest in murder or the reformation of our souls" — so the very comparison she models her method on is one she spends the chapter warning against as a *content* analogy. The argument is that we should borrow Arendt's *method* (comprehend the new), not her *object* (totalitarian terror).
- **A neologism can obscure as well as clarify.** A skeptic might hold that "instrumentarianism" risks the opposite failure — coining a term so novel it detaches the phenomenon from the concrete, already-named harms (monopoly, manipulation, privacy loss) that existing law can address. Zuboff does not entertain this objection in this chapter.
