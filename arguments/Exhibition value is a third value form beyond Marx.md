# Exhibition value is a third value form beyond Marx

## Claim

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], [[Exhibition Value]] constitutes a third order of value that cannot be reduced to either [[Use Value]] or [[Exchange Value]]; under the fulfilled condition of capitalism, this third form becomes ontologically primary — things must be displayed in order to be.

## Expanded Explanation

Han extends [[Walter Benjamin]]'s pairing of [[Cult Value]] and [[Exhibition Value]] into an economic-theoretical claim. Where Benjamin tracked the historical migration of artworks from ritual concealment toward display, Han argues that under the [[Positivity|Society of Positivity]] the exhibition mode has so fully colonized objecthood that it now produces a value form irreducible to the Marxian categories. Use value belongs to the sphere of utility; exchange value crystallizes congealed labor. Exhibition value satisfies neither criterion. It is generated by, and only by, the [[Attention]] an object captures — what Han elsewhere calls [[Exhibition Value|Attention Capital]].

The claim is ontological as well as economic. If value accrues "only insofar as objects are seen," then bare existence — the object resting in itself — is voided of meaning. To be is to be displayed. This is why Han calls exhibition value the "fulfillment of capitalism": capitalism here completes itself by collapsing the distinction between being and appearance, between commodity and image. The remainder that Benjamin still located in the "human countenance" — the last refuge of [[Aura]] — has, for Han, also been absorbed: the face becomes a "visage on display," the commodity form of the countenance (see [[The face as commodified countenance]]).

The consequence is a regime in which [[Dwelling]] (Heidegger's *bei sich verweilen*) becomes impossible. What rests in itself, withholds itself, or remains hidden has no value within the exhibition economy and is therefore experienced as a deficit to be eliminated. The third value form is thus not an additive expansion of Marx's schema but a displacement of its frame: utility and labor recede behind the production of visibility itself.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Marx's analysis of the commodity rests on the opposition between use value (utility) and exchange value (congealed labor).
2. Benjamin identifies a different polarity within the artwork — cult value (existence, concealment) versus exhibition value (display, visibility).
3. In the [[Transparency|Society of Transparency]], exhibition value becomes generalized to all things, not just artworks: "things become commodities; they must be displayed in order to be."
4. This generalized exhibition value cannot be use value, because the exhibited thing "stands removed from the sphere of utility."
5. Nor can it be exchange value, because it "does not reflect any labor" — its substance is not crystallized labor time but captured attention.
6. Therefore exhibition value names a third value form, generated by attention, that lies outside Marx's categories.
7. Because this third form has become the dominant mode under fulfilled capitalism, the ontological condition of objects shifts: what does not appear does not exist; "value accrues only insofar as objects are seen."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Han directly states the non-derivability of exhibition value from the Marxian pair, in `[[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/05 Ch2 - Exhibition]]`.
- Han grounds the ontological consequence ("must be displayed in order to be") in the same chapter, linking exhibition value to the collapse of [[Cult Value]] under [[Positivity]].
- The claim that "value accrues only insofar as objects are seen" is presented as a direct corollary, marking the eclipse of Heideggerian [[Dwelling]] (*bei sich verweilen*).
- Benjamin's account of the migration from cult value to exhibition value (Benjamin, *The Work of Art in the Age of Its Technological Reproducibility*) is the historical backdrop Han extends — see [[Walter Benjamin on cult and exhibition value]].

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- A Marxist rejoinder: exhibition value may be reducible to exchange value once one accepts that attention itself is a form of labor (cf. [[Immaterial Labor]], Lazzarato; the "attention economy" literature). Han does not engage this objection; he asserts that exhibition value "does not reflect any labor," which presupposes a narrow, productivist definition of labor.
- A Benjaminian rejoinder: Benjamin himself already treated exhibition value as continuous with the commodity form rather than orthogonal to it. Han's elevation of exhibition value to a *third* category may overstate its independence from exchange.
- The claim that exhibition value "exists thanks only to the attention it produces" risks circularity if attention is itself measured by exhibition; Han does not offer an independent metric.
- The argument is articulated at a high level of generality and is not supported by empirical economic analysis; it functions as a phenomenological-diagnostic claim, not a quantitative one. Treat as `Directly stated` for the theoretical thesis, but `Interpretive synthesis` where it is taken as an economic theory proper.
- Han's identification of "fulfillment of capitalism" with the exhibition regime is a strong periodizing claim; readers committed to alternative periodizations (post-Fordism, platform capitalism, [[Surveillance Capitalism]]) may find it underspecified.
