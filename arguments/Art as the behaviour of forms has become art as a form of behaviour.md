# Art as the behaviour of forms has become art as a form of behaviour

## Claim

The locus of the artwork moves from the object's formal arrangement to the participant's behaviour within a system the artist designs — interaction becomes the very form of art, so that the viewer no longer receives a finished work but manipulates her own universe of experience.

## Expanded Explanation

This is [[Roy Ascott]]'s most compressed, chiastic statement of the behavioural turn, delivered in "The Planetary Collegium" (1994) as one of the five defining features of telematic art: "interaction: as the very form of art, such that art as the behaviour of forms has become art as a form of behaviour" (`Directly stated`). The chiasmus is the argument. In the old dispensation, *forms behaved* — they composed, balanced, and resolved on a surface, and the work was the well-arranged object the spectator received. Ascott inverts the relation: *behaviour becomes the form* of the work. Art no longer resides in the internal formal order of an object but in the conduct of participants inside a system the artist has set up.

The reformulation carries a redefinition of the artist's role with it. "Instead of providing content and meaning, directing passion and pre-empting feeling, the artist now creates contexts and systems, within which the viewer is instrumental in manipulating his own universe of experience and insight" (`Directly stated`). The artist withdraws from the position of message-sender — the one who directs passion and pre-empts feeling — and becomes [[The Artist as Designer of Context|a designer of conditions]]. Correlatively, the formerly passive observer becomes active: "the viewing subject, hitherto a passive observer of art, now negotiates and navigates what is largely a world of her own making" (`Directly stated`). The work is not delivered; it is *generated* by [[The Participator|the participator]] through [[Reciprocal Communication|two-way, mind-to-mind interaction]].

This is the mature 1994 restatement of the position Ascott first set out in the 1960s and which the vault records as [[Art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour]]. The earlier thesis located the shift "from the field of objects to the field of behaviour"; the later slogan tightens it into the chiastic claim that behaviour *is* the form. It belongs to a longer genealogy in the same essay, where "the metaphor of emergence" displaces "the aesthetic of the material object," so that art's defining quality becomes "the perpetual coming into being of meaning, matter, and mind" (`Strongly implied`). Behaviour-as-form is the participatory face of the vault's material→immaterial spine: the work is not a thing that exists but a behaviour that happens, open and unresolved, completed differently by each participant.

## Reasoning Structure

1. In the classical aesthetic, the artwork is an object whose value lies in the arrangement of its forms; the spectator passively receives a finished message.
2. In telematic/cybernetic art, interaction is the defining feature — the work exists only in the loop between artist's system and participant's conduct.
3. Therefore the carrier of art shifts from the object's formal order ("the behaviour of forms") to the participant's activity within the system ("a form of behaviour").
4. This requires the artist to stop supplying content and instead design contexts and systems.
5. And it requires the viewer to stop being a passive observer and instead manipulate "his own universe of experience" — negotiating a world "largely of her own making."
6. Consequently the artwork is not a thing that exists but a behaviour that happens: emergent, participatory, and completed differently by each participant.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The participant's freedom is framed celebratorily — "a world of her own making" — but the artist still *designs the system within which she acts*. Behaviour that unfolds inside a context another has built is not unconditioned; this is the seam the vault's later Han/Harari surveillance-and-control cluster presses, where feedback systems capture and condition the very participation they invite (cf. [[Reciprocal Communication]], [[Detachment vs Participation]]).
- If the work resides only in behaviour "between the behaviours of all parts of the system," it is unclear what remains *authored* or conservable — and how quality is to be judged when each participant produces a different outcome.
- Editor Edward Shanken notes that Ascott's behavioural thesis leans on binary oppositions (object vs. event, past vs. present) and a faith in progress that "suggest continuities with the liberal humanist values associated with modernism," so the clean object/behaviour split the slogan asserts is less clean than it sounds (`Directly stated` — Shanken, on the parent thesis).
