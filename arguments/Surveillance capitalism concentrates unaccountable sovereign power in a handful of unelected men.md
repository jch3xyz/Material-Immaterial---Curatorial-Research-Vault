# Surveillance capitalism concentrates unaccountable sovereign power in a handful of unelected men

## Claim

The net effect of surveillance capitalism's fortifications is a radical concentration of sovereign power: two men at Google and one man at Facebook — none of whom enjoys the legitimacy of the vote, democratic oversight, or the demands of shareholder governance — exercise control over the organization of the world's information and over a universal means of social connection.

## Expanded Explanation

Chapter 4, "The Moat Around the Castle," catalogs the [[Fortifications]] that protect surveillance capitalism from external interference — the rhetoric of inevitabilism and "the dispossession cycle," the strategic capture of regulators, the revolving door between firm and state, the funding of academics and civil-society groups, and the broader regime of [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]] underwritten by [[Neoliberalism]] and corporate [[Self-Regulation]]. Zuboff's chapter closes by stating the political *result* of these defenses in plain numbers. The fortifications are not an end in themselves; their function is to "keep democracy at bay," and the consequence is the delivery of control over the world's most consequential information infrastructures into a tiny circle of unaccountable hands.

The argument's force lies in Zuboff's enumeration of the three sources of legitimacy that these men conspicuously lack. They do not hold power by *the vote* (they are not elected and cannot be voted out); they are not subject to *democratic oversight* (no public institution governs their decisions about what the world sees and knows); and they are not even disciplined by *shareholder governance* (dual-class share structures concentrate voting control in the founders, so the ordinary mechanism by which capitalism's owners check its managers does not apply). What remains, by Zuboff's accounting, is sovereign power without any of capitalism's or democracy's accountability circuits — a private dominion over "the organization and presentation of the world's information" (Google) and over "an increasingly universal means of social connection along with the information concealed in its networks" (Facebook).

This is the vault's clearest primary-text link between the surveillance apparatus and the dismantling of popular sovereignty. Where the broader thesis ([[Surveillance capitalism is a coup from above an overthrow of popular sovereignty]]) names the *form* of the seizure — a coup not against the state but against the people's self-rule — this argument supplies its *concrete face*: the named, unelected individuals in whom the seized power now resides. It foreshadows the book's culminating chapter on [[The Coup from Above]] and grounds the otherwise abstract category of [[Instrumentarian Power]] in a question of governance: who, exactly, decides, and by what right.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism has erected fortifications — political and cultural capture, exceptionalism, the neoliberal habitat — designed "to protect supply chain operations from scrutiny." *(Directly stated)*
2. The purpose and effect of these fortifications is to "keep democracy at bay." *(Directly stated)*
3. Behind them, control over the world's information (Google) and over a universal means of social connection (Facebook) has come to rest with two men and one man respectively. *(Directly stated)*
4. These men hold that power without the three accountability circuits that would otherwise legitimate or constrain it — the vote, democratic oversight, and shareholder governance. *(Directly stated)*
5. Therefore the fortifications produce, as their "stark facts," a concentration of sovereign-scale power that is structurally unaccountable to the public it governs. *(Strongly implied — Zuboff frames the enumeration as the demonstrable result of "surveillance capitalism's ability to keep democracy at bay")*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The "two men / one man" framing personalizes a structural condition. A critic could note that the concentration is a feature of [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance capitalism's]] *logic* and ownership structures, not of these particular individuals' intentions — and Zuboff would largely agree, since elsewhere she insists the threat is a [[Surveillance capitalism is a logic not a technology|logic, not a technology]] (or a person). The named men are the current occupants of an unaccountable seat, not its cause.
- Removing or replacing the men — or even breaking up their firms — would not dissolve the underlying power. This is the explicit lesson of [[Breaking up Google or Facebook would not interrupt surveillance capitalism]]: the dominion would simply redistribute among successor surveillance capitalists. The concentration is the symptom; the economic logic is the disease.
- "Sovereign power" is, strictly, a political-theoretical term applied to corporate actors who lack a monopoly on legitimate force. Zuboff's claim is that they nonetheless exercise a governing dominion over knowledge and connection — but the analogy to state sovereignty is interpretive, and a skeptic could argue these are still, formally, market firms subject to law.
- Dual-class share structures (the reason shareholder governance does not bind the founders) are a legal arrangement that shareholders accepted at IPO; the absence of that check is, in a narrow sense, consented to — complicating the image of a purely unilateral seizure.
