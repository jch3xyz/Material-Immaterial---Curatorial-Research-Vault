# Telematic networking is a quantum leap in human consciousness

## Claim

For Roy Ascott, the convergence of computers and telecommunications — what Nora and Minc named [[Telematic Art|telematics]] — is not merely a powerful new technology but "a paradigm change in our culture" and what "may amount to a quantum leap in human consciousness." (*Directly stated*.)

## Expanded Explanation

This is the master thesis of Ascott's 1984 essay "Art and Telematics: towards a Network Consciousness" — the opening move on which the rest of his telematic program rests. Ascott refuses to treat the marriage of computing and telecommunications as an incremental media advance. The convergence of "these two, in themselves vastly powerful media" is, in his words, a *paradigm change*: a transformation not of the tools we use but of the kind of beings, and the kind of mind, that use them. The strong form of the claim is the consciousness register — telematic networking is poised to deliver "a quantum leap in human consciousness," a discontinuous shift in awareness rather than a faster channel for the awareness we already have.

The claim is consciousness-first and only secondarily about art and technology. Ascott immediately hedges it ("without being foolishly optimistic, I hope") and then defends it not by technical argument but by *chorus*: "I am certainly not alone in this, and I propose to cite a variety of writers from a variety of disciplines who collectively contribute to a vision of the future of the human condition that is rather hopeful." Across the essay that chorus is Teilhard de Chardin's noosphere, Peter Russell's global brain, Hazel Henderson on networks as the "next revolutionary step in developing human consciousness," Gregory Bateson's ecology of mind, and the holistic physics of Capra and Bohm — a convergent, cross-disciplinary intimation of an emerging planetary consciousness. The leap, in other words, is not Ascott's idiosyncratic hope but a pattern he reads as already legible across many fields. This is the paradigm-change / consciousness-leap claim proper; its emergent-whole corollary — that the network itself generates a mind greater than the sum of its parts — is developed in the sibling argument [[Telematic networks can create a consciousness greater than the sum of its parts]].

This argument extends the just-realized art clusters [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] into the networked register, and it positions Ascott directly downstream of [[Marshall McLuhan]]: Ascott's "paradigm change" is the consciousness-transforming successor to McLuhan's [[The Global Village|global village]] and electric implosion, now routed through computer-mediated networking rather than broadcast. The same global connectivity Ascott reads as a hopeful expansion of mind is read elsewhere in the vault as homogenization and domination — Han's [[Hell of the Same]] and the [[Digital Panopticon]], Haraway's informatics of domination — so the prophetic register here should be cited apart from those critical readings. (*Interpretive synthesis*.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Telematics is the convergence of two already "vastly powerful media" — computers and telecommunications networks.
2. The marriage of two such media is not additive but transformative: it "constitutes a paradigm change in our culture."
3. A change at the level of culture's organizing paradigm registers, ultimately, at the level of mind — "what may amount to a quantum leap in human consciousness."
4. This is corroborated, not asserted alone: a chorus of writers across disciplines (Teilhard, Russell, Henderson, Bateson, Capra/Bohm) independently sees a planetary consciousness emerging.
5. Therefore telematic networking is best understood as a transformation of consciousness rather than merely a new technology.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The convergence thesis itself — telematics as a "paradigm change in our culture" and a possible "quantum leap in human consciousness" (ch. 15).
- The "engagement of creative minds in telematic systems will affect human consciousness and transform our culture" (ch. 15) — the art-specific restatement.
- The cross-disciplinary chorus Ascott marshals: Teilhard's noosphere, Russell's global brain, Bateson's ecology of mind, and especially Hazel Henderson, quoted at length, that networking "may even augur the next revolutionary step in developing human consciousness" [Henderson 1978] (ch. 15).
- Shanken's editorial corroboration that Ascott's telematic art "results in the transformation of consciousness on a global scale," and that Ascott reads telematics as "a subversive paradigm shift" (ch. 04).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Self-flagged optimism.** Ascott himself hedges — "without being foolishly optimistic, I hope" — marking the claim as hopeful projection rather than demonstrated fact. Shanken notes Ascott's theories are "subject to criticism for their deterministic and utopian attitude regarding technology and the future" (ch. 04).
- **Metaphor vs. mechanism.** "Consciousness" may be a figure projected onto an infrastructure of information exchange; the line between [[Distributed Cognition|distributed cognition]] and a mystified planetary mind is exactly where the claim is most contestable (see ch. 04: a global brain "may never achieve the type of consciousness manifested in humans").
- **The counter-reading.** The same converging networks can equally produce a leap *downward* — toward the [[Digital Panopticon]], the [[Hell of the Same]], and Haraway's informatics of domination. Convergence does not entail a richer or freer consciousness; Shanken warns one "must remain vigilant that its hugs do not squeeze too tightly" (ch. 04). (*Interpretive synthesis*.)
- **Access.** Written before the commercial internet and when "only a fraction of the world's population had even a telephone" (Shanken, ch. 04), the "quantum leap" presumes a planetary access that did not — and may not — obtain.
