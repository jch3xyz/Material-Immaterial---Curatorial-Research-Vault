# We see only what we can talk about

## Claim

Language does not merely report perception but conditions it — "we see only what we can talk about" — so that reshaping a society's vocabulary, measurements, and communication systems reshapes its perceptions, actions, and ultimately its reality at the most fundamental level. *(Strongly implied / endorsed.)*

## Expanded Explanation

Late in *[[Thinking in Systems]]*, among the fourteen "system wisdoms" Meadows offers for living inside complexity, the ninth is a directive: **"Make Feedback Policies for Feedback Systems... Stay Humble—Stay a Learner," "Honor and Protect Information," and in particular, "Expand the Boundary of Caring."** Under the heading of honoring information she folds a claim about language that runs deeper than communication hygiene. Information, for Meadows, is the connective tissue of a system — "the largest [[Leverage Points|leverage point]]" she never quite ranks — and the most human information system is language itself. "Our mental models are mostly verbal," she writes, so to honor information "means above all avoiding language pollution... [and] expanding our language so we can talk about complexity." The claim is not that clearer words describe the world better; it is that the words are constitutive of the world we can see at all.

To make the point she quotes the systems theorist Fred Kofman at length, and the quotation is the argument's hinge. Language "can serve as a medium through which we create new understandings and new realities as we begin to talk about them. In fact, we don't talk about what we see; we see only what we can talk about." Perception runs backward from the naive picture: the sayable precedes and bounds the visible. Kofman then supplies the causal mechanism — "Our perspectives on the world depend on the interaction of our nervous system and our language—both act as filters through which we perceive our world" — and draws the systemic consequence Meadows is after: "The language and information systems of an organization are not an objective means of describing an outside reality—they fundamentally structure the perceptions and actions of its members. To reshape the measurement and communication systems of a [society] is to reshape all potential interactions at the most fundamental level." Language, Kofman concludes and Meadows endorses, "as articulation of reality is more primordial than strategy, structure, or... culture." Because [[Mental Models|mental models]] are mostly verbal, the vocabulary a society possesses is a [[Leverage Points|leverage point]] on what that society is able to do.

Meadows makes the abstraction bite with a series of one-line diagnoses. "A society that talks incessantly about 'productivity' but that hardly understands, much less uses, the word 'resilience' is going to become productive and not resilient. A society that doesn't understand or use the term 'carrying capacity' will exceed its carrying capacity." A missing word is a missing capacity; the absence in the lexicon becomes an absence in the world. The inverse holds too — she notes that the words she herself had to add to her spell-checker while writing the book were "feedback, throughput, overshoot, self-organization, sustainability," and that "the industrial society is just beginning to have and use words for systems, because it is only beginning to pay attention to and use complexity." The corruption of language is therefore not a stylistic failing but an epistemic and moral one. Following Wendell Berry, she names its worst form: a society that says "'Peacekeeper' missile" or "'collateral damage,' a 'Final Solution' or 'ethnic cleansing,' is speaking what Wendell Berry calls 'tyrannese'" — language "used conscientiously to refer to nothing in particular," whose "only practical utility is to support with 'expert opinion' a vast, impersonal technological action already begun." Tyrannese perceives, and permits, atrocity precisely by naming it in words that cannot be stood on or acted from.

This is where Meadows converges, from within the native discipline of [[Systems Theory|systems theory]], on a thesis the vault meets elsewhere as media theory. Her "both act as filters through which we perceive our world" is the same structure McLuhan gives when he casts every medium — and language is his first and deepest medium — as an active reshaper of the [[Sense Ratios|sense ratios]] through which experience is organized: [[All media are active metaphors that translate experience|all media are active metaphors that translate experience]], and [[The medium not its content is the message|the medium, not its content, is the message]]. It is also O'Gieblyn's terrain in [[Technology as Metaphor]], where the metaphors a culture reaches for do not describe the mind or the world so much as retroactively remake them. Meadows's contribution is to hold the claim at the scale of a whole society's [[Information|information]] and measurement systems, and to give it a lever: change the words and the metrics, and you change what the society can perceive, hence what it can become.

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise (mental models are verbal).** "Our mental models are mostly verbal," so the models through which we understand any system are built largely out of language. *(Directly stated.)*
2. **Premise (perception is language-bounded).** "We don't talk about what we see; we see only what we can talk about" — the sayable precedes and bounds the visible. *(Directly stated, via Kofman quoted approvingly.)*
3. **Mechanism (language as filter).** The nervous system and language "both act as filters through which we perceive our world," so what reaches perception is already shaped by the available vocabulary. *(Directly stated, via Kofman.)*
4. **Systemic escalation.** Because language and information systems "fundamentally structure the perceptions and actions" of a system's members, "to reshape the measurement and communication systems of a [society] is to reshape all potential interactions at the most fundamental level." *(Directly stated, via Kofman.)*
5. **Empirical corollary (missing word, missing capacity).** A society fluent in "productivity" but not "resilience" "is going to become productive and not resilient"; one lacking "carrying capacity" "will exceed its carrying capacity." *(Directly stated.)*
6. **Moral corollary (tyrannese).** Corrupted language — Berry's "tyrannese" — permits impersonal technological violence by referring "to nothing in particular," making atrocity sayable and therefore doable. *(Directly stated.)*
7. **Conclusion.** Honoring and enlarging language is a systems intervention: keep information streams clear and "enlarge language to make it consistent with our enlarged understanding of systems," because the vocabulary is a leverage point on reality. *(Directly stated.)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The strong reading — that we see *only* what we can talk about — courts linguistic determinism (a Sapir–Whorf-style overclaim). Meadows's own examples are softer: a missing word makes a capacity *unlikely* ("is going to become," "will exceed"), not strictly impossible, leaving room for pre-verbal or newly-coined perception. Indeed her whole prescription — invent new words, enlarge language — presupposes that perception can outrun current vocabulary enough to demand it. *(Interpretive synthesis.)*
- The claim is endorsed rather than independently derived: its sharpest formulations are Kofman's and Berry's, quoted approvingly, so the argument rests on Meadows's judgment in selecting them as much as on argument of her own. *(Strongly implied.)*
- Enlarging vocabulary is offered as high leverage but is not sufficient on its own; Meadows pairs it with the adjacent wisdoms of paying attention to "what is important, not just what is quantifiable" and keeping information streams clear — signaling that new words without new attention would be empty. *(Strongly implied.)*

## Related Arguments

- [[All media are active metaphors that translate experience]] — McLuhan's media-theoretic form of the same claim: language (his deepest medium) does not transmit a pre-given world but translates and reshapes it, so Meadows's "filters through which we perceive" is his "active metaphor" at the scale of society.
- [[The medium not its content is the message]] — the parallel move that it is the *form* of a communication system, not its content, that restructures perception; Meadows locates the "medium" in a society's measurement and communication systems.

## Tensions

- [[Metaphor vs Literalism]] — the argument sits on the metaphor side: language "as articulation of reality" is constitutive rather than a literal, "objective means of describing an outside reality," bridging Meadows to [[Technology as Metaphor]].
- [[Visibility vs Understanding]] — Meadows inverts the naive priority of the two: understanding (the sayable) governs visibility (the seen), so a society sees only what its language lets it understand.
