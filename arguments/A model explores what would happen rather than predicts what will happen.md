# A model explores what would happen rather than predicts what will happen

## Claim

A dynamic-systems model is not a forecasting device. Its purpose is to explore *what would happen if* a set of driving factors unfolded in various ways, not to predict *what will happen* — and its quality is measured by whether it reproduces a realistic *pattern of behavior*, not by whether its numbers come out exactly right.

## Expanded Explanation

In the systems zoo (see [[The Systems Zoo]]), Meadows runs simple stock-and-flow models to generate population scenarios — growth, decline, stabilization — and pauses to make a methodological point about what such models are for. Every scenario you meet in ordinary life (an economic prediction, a corporate budget, a weather forecast, a stockbroker's tip) rests on an underlying model, and the way to judge that model is not to ask whether it foretells the future correctly. The first question a model raises — will the driving factors actually unfold this way? — "can’t be answered factually. It’s a guess about the future, and the future is inherently uncertain."

What *can* be judged is the second question: given some assumed driving factors, does the system react the way the model says it would? This is "a question about how good the model is" — whether, in Meadows's words, it "capture[s] the inherent dynamics of the system." The model earns its keep not by nailing the numbers but by responding with a realistic *pattern*. Meadows's own population model is deliberately crude, yet "basically this model responds the way a real population would" — growing when a real population would grow, declining when it would decline. "The numbers are off, but the basic behavior pattern is realistic."

This reframes what a model is *for*. It is an instrument for asking "what if" questions and exploring possible futures, not a crystal ball. A [[Feedback Loops|feedback]]-structured model can only test scenarios — it "can test a number of scenarios to see what happens if the driving factors do different things" — while the analyst, not the model, must judge which of those futures should be taken seriously as genuinely possible. This is the core of the [[Prediction vs Exploration]] tension.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Every scenario about the future rests on an underlying model, whose quality we need a way to judge.
2. Whether the *driving factors* will actually unfold a given way "can't be answered factually" — it is a guess about an inherently uncertain future, so a model cannot be validated as a *prediction*.
3. What *can* be judged is whether the *system* responds correctly to assumed driving factors — i.e., whether the model captures the inherent dynamics of the system.
4. A model captures those dynamics when it reproduces a realistic *pattern of behavior*, even if the exact numbers are wrong.
5. Therefore the value of a dynamic-systems model lies in exploring "what would happen" as the driving factors are varied, not in predicting "what will happen" — and a crude model with off numbers can still be a good model.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Meadows concedes that the analyst, not the model, must supply the judgment the model cannot: "you have to be the judge of which scenario, if any, should be taken seriously as a future that might really be possible." Exploration relocates rather than removes the uncertainty.
- The claim is scoped to *dynamic* systems modeling ("usually"), not to all quantitative forecasting; short-horizon, well-bounded systems may legitimately support prediction.
- A "realistic pattern of behavior" is itself a judgment call — a model that reproduces a plausible-looking curve can still mis-specify the underlying feedback structure.
