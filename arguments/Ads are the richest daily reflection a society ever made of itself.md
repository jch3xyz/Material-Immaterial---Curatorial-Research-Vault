# Ads are the richest daily reflection a society ever made of itself

## Claim

Because the advertising industry out-researches every other institution and can build only on the tested foundations of public stereotypes, an acceptable ad cannot depart from the center of communal experience without collapsing — so the ads of an era constitute the richest and most faithful daily reflection a society ever made of its entire range of activities.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan inverts the conventional moralizing view of advertising. Critics treat ads as deception — "false and misleading ad copy" to be protested. McLuhan argues the protesters miss the point: ads are not arguments to be evaluated for truth, they are icons assembled from the actually-shared feelings of a community. Far more thought, care, testing, and skill go into a prominent ad than into the features and editorials that surround it, and the budgets dwarf any sociological study. The ad teams have "billions to spend annually on research and testing of reactions," so their products are "magnificent accumulations of material about the shared experience and feelings of the entire community."

The crucial mechanism is a constraint, not an intention. An ad that strayed from the communal center would lose its hold on collective feeling and "collapse at once." This is why ads are diagnostic: they are forced toward the statistical and emotional center of a society, and they record it without the distortions of explanation or point of view. The ad is "a vigorous dramatization of communal experience" — built on tested public stereotypes "as any skyscraper is built on bedrock."

From this McLuhan draws his historical conclusion: "The historians and archeologists will one day discover that the ads of our time are the richest and most faithful daily reflections that any society ever made of its entire range of activities. The Egyptian hieroglyph lags far behind in this respect." The ad surpasses the older pictorial and inscriptional records of civilizations precisely because it is researched, tested, and constrained to the communal center. This connects to McLuhan's broader account of the ad as a corporate icon that fuses producer and consumer into a single compressed image, and to his treatment of media as the public registration of collective life — see [[Media as Extensions of Man]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. Ad teams command research budgets ("billions") that no group of sociologists can approximate, and gather/process exploitable social data more thoroughly than any other institution.
2. Any expensive ad is built on the tested foundations of public stereotypes and established attitudes — it cannot be improvised away from the communal center.
3. If an ad departed from that center of shared experience, it would lose its hold on collective feeling and collapse at once.
4. Therefore any acceptable ad is a vigorous dramatization of communal experience — a compressed, tested record of what a society actually shares.
5. Conclusion: the ads of an era are the richest and most faithful daily reflection a society ever made of its entire range of activities, surpassing even the Egyptian hieroglyph as a historical record.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The advertising budget ("twelve-billion-dollar annual budget") approximating the national school budget — advertising as a vast "educational enterprise."
- More thought and care go into a prominent ad than into the features and editorials of the same publication.
- The skyscraper-on-bedrock analogy: ads are built on tested public stereotypes and established attitudes.
- The "collapse at once" claim: ads that depart from the center of shared experience lose all hold on feeling.
- The post-war army officer in Italy: a society's ad-consciousness (worrying about cornflakes and cigarettes rather than cabinet ministers) is read by McLuhan as a measure of homogenized communal life.
- The closing comparison to the Egyptian hieroglyph as an inferior historical record.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- McLuhan concedes ads use the most basic and tested human experience "in grotesque ways" and are "incongruous, if looked at consciously" — a faithful reflection can still be a distorting funhouse mirror. The fidelity is to communal *feeling*, not to literal fact.
- Ads reflect a *homogenized* society: McLuhan ties their reach to the literate world's readiness to "homogenize its social life," so the "daily record" is also a record produced by, and biased toward, the consumer culture it serves (oral/tribal cultures resist it).
- Ads are "subliminal pills for the subconscious," designed for "semiconscious exposure" — so the record is one a society makes of itself without consciously avowing it, which complicates calling it the society's own faithful self-portrait.
- The claim that no group of sociologists can rival the ad teams is asserted, not demonstrated; it rests on budget and testing volume rather than on validated representativeness.
