# The unprecedented eludes contest because existing categories make it invisible

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism escapes systematic contest because, being genuinely unprecedented, it is automatically interpreted through familiar categories that render it invisible — so fresh observation and new naming are the necessary prelude to effective opposition.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the methodological premise of Zuboff's whole book, and the reason it coins so much vocabulary. The single explanation that "floats above" surveillance capitalism's many triumphs is that "it is unprecedented," and "the unprecedented is necessarily unrecognizable." When confronted with something new, we reach reflexively for inherited lenses; those lenses "illuminate the familiar, thus obscuring the original by turning the unprecedented into an extension of the past." The category does not merely fail to capture the new thing — it actively hides it, contributing to "the normalization of the abnormal." `Directly stated`

The failure mode has two emblems. The "horseless carriage" names the automobile by what it replaced — a carriage minus its horse — keeping perception tethered to the superseded object and blind to a new kind of thing and a new social order. The Taínos of the pre-Columbian Caribbean, "unable to imagine their own destruction," took the armored Spanish conquerors for gods and "welcomed them with intricate rituals of hospitality." Zuboff places the contemporary public in the Taínos' position before [[Surveillance Capitalism]]: we "risk catastrophe when we assess new threats through the lens of old experience." `Directly stated`

The political payoff is precise. Because surveillance capitalism "cannot be adequately grasped with our existing concepts," it "has enabled it to elude systematic contest." We rely on categories such as "monopoly" or "privacy," and although those issues are "vital," the existing categories "fall short in identifying and contesting the most crucial and unprecedented facts of this new regime" — so much so that breaking up Google or Facebook on monopoly grounds could simply "clear the way for more surveillance capitalist competitors." The same syndrome later afflicts the analysis of power: [[Instrumentarian Power]] "has defied comprehension in part because it has been subjected to the 'horselesscarriage' syndrome," misread "through the old lenses of totalitarianism." Hence Zuboff's prescription, framed as a vaccine: "fresh observation, analysis, and new naming are required if we are to grasp the unprecedented as a necessary prelude to effective contest." Naming is not ornament; it is the precondition of resistance. `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism is genuinely unprecedented — *sui generis*, not a variant of an existing market form.
2. The unprecedented is necessarily unrecognizable: we automatically interpret the new through familiar categories.
3. Those categories actively render invisible precisely what is new ("horseless carriage"; the Taínos mistaking conquerors for gods), normalizing the abnormal.
4. Therefore the inherited categories available for contest — "monopoly," "privacy," "totalitarianism" — though real and vital, are insufficient: they miss the crucial facts and so let the regime elude systematic contest.
5. Conclusion: fresh observation and new naming are the necessary prelude to effective contest — which is why the book mints its own vocabulary ([[Behavioral Surplus]], the prediction imperative, [[Big Other]], instrumentarianism, [[Surveillance Capitalism]]) to make the invisible visible.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The "horseless carriage" — the lens "to which people reverted when confronted with the unprecedented facts of the automobile" (Ch.1).
- The Taínos of the pre-Columbian Caribbean, who took the Spanish conquerors for gods (Ch.1).
- The "vaccine" framing: "Every vaccine begins in careful knowledge of the enemy disease," and the book is the journey to that knowledge (Ch.1).
- The monopoly counter-example: breaking up Google/Facebook on monopoly grounds "could easily result in establishing multiple surveillance capitalist firms" (Ch.1).
- The lineage of theorists who "struggled to name the unprecedented" — Durkheim, Marx, Weber on industrial capitalism; Arendt, Adorno, Polanyi and others on totalitarianism (Ch.1).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Self-referential trap.** If the unprecedented is "necessarily unrecognizable," what licenses Zuboff's own claim to have named it correctly rather than supplying a more sophisticated "horseless carriage"? The argument must exempt its own act of naming from the diagnosis it levels at everyone else's.
- **Names can be absorbed.** Fresh naming may be necessary but not sufficient: a regime can metabolize its own new vocabulary ("surveillance," "data," even "privacy") without losing power, so naming alone need not enable contest.
- **Overclaiming novelty.** The line between a genuinely unprecedented phenomenon and a familiar one (monopoly, dispossession) that merely *looks* new is contestable; the "horseless carriage" diagnosis can itself overclaim, dismissing apt older categories as mere old lenses.
- **Fatalism in the analogy.** The Taínos framing risks casting the public as fated victims, which sits awkwardly with the book's call to contest the unprecedented through understanding.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the "horseless carriage" error is a category-mismatch about *what kind of thing* a new object is; surveillance capitalism is misperceived partly because its raw material (lived human experience converted into immaterial behavioral data) has no settled name on the vault's material→immaterial spine.
- [[Object vs System]] — naming the new by the old object ("carriage minus horse") is precisely the failure to see that the unprecedented is a new *system*, not a modified object; Burnham's object→system shift names the same perceptual hurdle in the art domain.
