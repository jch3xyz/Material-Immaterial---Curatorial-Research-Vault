# The liberal subject and the posthuman both erase embodiment

## Claim

Because the cybernetic posthuman recodes the body as the instantiation of thought/information, it inherits rather than overturns the liberal humanist privileging of mind over body; the erasure of [[Embodiment]] is the deep continuity that runs beneath the apparent rupture between the human and the posthuman.

## Expanded Explanation

This is [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s **continuity-amid-rupture** thesis, and it is the argument that blocks any easy celebration of [[Posthumanism|the posthuman]] as liberation. On its surface, the posthuman looks like the deconstruction of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]]: its autonomy is breached by [[Feedback Loops|feedback loops]] that flow "between the subject and the environment," its self-possession dissolved into a "distributed cognition" ([[Distributed Cognition]]) of heterogeneous components. Yet for Hayles this break is partial and treacherous. The liberal subject, "identified with the rational mind," already "possessed a body but was not usually represented as being a body." Its prized universality was bought only by "erasing markers of bodily difference, including sex, race, and ethnicity." The posthuman, when it construes the body as a portable informational pattern — Gibson's "data made flesh" ([[The Body as Data]]) — does not repudiate that contempt for the flesh; it perfects it, this time under the sign of [[Information]].

Hence the load-bearing sentence: "To the extent that the posthuman constructs embodiment as the instantiation of thought/information, it continues the liberal tradition rather than disrupts it." The mechanism is the prior separation of information from materiality — the story of "how information lost its body." Once information is theorized as a pattern indifferent to its substrate, "the materiality in which the thinking mind is instantiated appears incidental to its essential nature," and the body becomes one more medium the pattern merely happens to occupy. The liberal subject located the self in the mind and treated the body as property to be mastered; the posthuman locates the self in the code and treats the body as a substrate to be transcended. Different vocabularies, same suppressed term. This is why the relation between the two is not clean opposition but **supersession-with-inheritance**, distinct from the framing in [[Embodiment vs Information]]: that axis names a tension between two poles, whereas this argument names a specific *shared erasure* across what look like opposed regimes.

The stakes are critical, not merely descriptive. Hayles is careful that she does "not mourn the passing" of the liberal subject, "a concept so deeply entwined with projects of domination and oppression." Her point is not to rescue the human but to warn that the supposed successor smuggles the predecessor's worst assumption forward. If both regimes erase the body, then "posthuman" risks naming not a break from liberal humanism but its intensification — disembodiment "rewritten, once again, into prevailing concepts of subjectivity."

## Reasoning Structure

1. The liberal humanist subject is "identified with the rational mind"; it "possessed a body but was not usually represented as being a body" — the self is located in mind, not flesh.
2. This location of the self in the mind is what lets the liberal subject claim its "notorious universality," a claim that "depends on erasing markers of bodily difference, including sex, race, and ethnicity."
3. The cybernetic posthuman rests on the prior separation of information from materiality ("information had lost its body"), which "implied that embodiment is not essential to human being."
4. So the posthuman "constructs embodiment as the instantiation of thought/information" — the body becomes a derivative manifestation of a pattern, an "accident of history rather than an inevitability of life."
5. Therefore the posthuman repeats, rather than reverses, the liberal subject's relegation of the body: "the erasure of embodiment is a feature common to both the liberal humanist subject and the cybernetic posthuman."
6. Conclusion: "To the extent that the posthuman constructs embodiment as the instantiation of thought/information, it continues the liberal tradition rather than disrupts it" — the rupture is partial, and the posthuman cannot be celebrated as embodied liberation.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The structural claim about the liberal subject: "Identified with the rational mind, the liberal subject possessed a body but was not usually represented as being a body" — and its universality "depends on erasing markers of bodily difference, including sex, race, and ethnicity." (See [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]].)
- The contrast with *other* critiques of the liberal subject: "Embodiment has been systematically downplayed or erased in the cybernetic construction of the posthuman in ways that have not occurred in other critiques of the liberal humanist subject, especially in feminist and postcolonial theories." The shared-erasure charge is specific to the *cybernetic* posthuman, not to deconstruction of the liberal subject as such.
- Gillian Brown's study of humanism and anorexia: the anoretic's "fight for self-control" is possible "precisely because the body is understood as an object for control and mastery rather than as an intrinsic part of the self" — a "material testimony that the locus of the liberal humanist subject lies in the mind, not the body."
- William Gibson's *Neuromancer*: the posthuman body figured as "data made flesh" ([[The Body as Data]]) — the literary icon of embodiment recoded as the instantiation of information.
- The genealogy of disembodiment Hayles assembles elsewhere in the chapter — [[Hans Moravec]]'s downloading fantasy, Wiener's telegraphing of a human being, Minsky's memories "imported, intact and unchanged, to computer disks" — all presuppose that "information can circulate unchanged among different material substrates." (See [[The Body as Data]].)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Hayles concedes the posthuman genuinely does break from the liberal subject in other respects.** It "deconstructs the liberal humanist subject" — dissolving its self-possession into distributed cognition and breaching its autonomous boundaries via feedback loops. The continuity is specifically about embodiment, not about the whole apparatus; the title *How We Became Posthuman* is meant to "connote multiple ironies," and the thesis is continuity *amid* rupture, not pure continuity.
- **The charge is scoped to the *cybernetic* posthuman.** Hayles explicitly exempts feminist and postcolonial deconstructions of the liberal subject, where the erasure of embodiment "ha[s] not occurred." The argument is therefore not that every critique of liberal humanism erases the body, only that the informatic one does — and the claim is hedged ("one could argue," "to the extent that").
- **There is a posthuman that does *not* erase embodiment.** Hayles holds open "a version of the posthuman that... recognizes and celebrates finitude as a condition of human being." So the erasure is a contingent feature of how the posthuman has been constructed, not a necessity of the concept; "the disembodiment of information was not inevitable."
- **The reading of "data made flesh" is interpretive.** Hayles builds the continuity claim partly on a single elliptical phrase from a novel; a skeptic could read Gibson's line as ironic or as describing one fictional regime rather than as evidence about the cybernetic posthuman in general.
- **Hayles is not defending the liberal subject.** A possible misreading is that she wants to recuperate the human; she explicitly does not — which means the argument is wielded *against* the posthuman's self-image as liberation, not in nostalgia for what it replaced.
