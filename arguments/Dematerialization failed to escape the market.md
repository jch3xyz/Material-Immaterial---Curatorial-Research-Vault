# Dematerialization failed to escape the market

## Claim

The 1969 hope that a dematerialized, non-object art could not be bought and sold proved unfounded — by 1973 the major conceptualists were "selling work for substantial sums" in "the world's most prestigious galleries," because the commodity form attaches to art's social status under capitalism, not to the materiality of the object.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the signature self-critical argument of *[[Six Years]]*, and Lippard stages it as a wager she lost. The hope was structural and almost syllogistic: if conceptual art is "a xerox sheet referring to an event past or never directly perceived, a group of photographs documenting an ephemeral situation or condition, a project for work never to be completed, words spoken but not recorded," then no one "would actually pay money, or much of it" for it; and if no one would pay, then "these artists would therefore be forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status and market-orientation." [[Dematerialization]] of the art object was meant to be, by the same stroke, a de-commodification of art. The premise that grounded the whole "decommodified 'idea-art'" — the idea that "some of us (or was it just me?) thought we had in our hands the weapon that would transform the art world into a democratic institution" — was that the market's grip was a grip on *matter*: on the unique, hangable, garden-able, ownable object.

The 1973 verdict is that the premise was false. "Three years later, the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums here and in Europe; they are represented by (and still more unexpected—showing in) the world's most prestigious galleries." The market did not need the object; it recuperated the documents, the photographs, the certificates of ownership, the words. Lippard's conclusion is flat and total: "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries." The escape metaphor that runs through "Escape Attempts" encodes the same verdict in its final sentence — "The escape was temporary. Art was recaptured and sent back to its white cell, but parole is always a possibility" — figuring the market not as a buyer but as a carceral institution from which the artwork is paroled, never freed.

The recuperation is *doubled and self-illustrating*. The book that documented and championed dematerialized art suffered the same fate as its subject: "Then the book went out of print for over a decade and became, ironically, a very expensive collector's item, paralleling the fate of the art it espoused." The critique of commodification became a commodity. This is the structural lesson the argument carries beyond its art-historical occasion: the commodity form is not a property of an object's bulk or permanence. A paper, a photograph, a documentary trace, even a *book about non-objects* can all "enter the property state" (the phrase Carl Andre used, quoted by Lippard, for what his disappearing hay bales would *not* do). The thinning of matter does nothing to the social relation that makes art saleable — which is exactly why Han's [[Exhibition Value]] can attach to anything displayed, and why the [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy, taken as a strategy of liberation, fails on its own terms.

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise (1969 hope).** The market's hold on art runs through the object's materiality — uniqueness, permanence, the hangable/garden-able thing that "can be bought and sold." (Stated in the Preface: "their work unfortunately lends itself more easily to capitalist marketing devices.")
2. **Premise (the inference).** Therefore an art that is "inexpensive, ephemeral, unintimidating" — documents, photographs, words, unbuilt projects — cannot be a commodity, and its makers will be "forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status."
3. **Observation (1973 outcome).** "The major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums ... in the world's most prestigious galleries." The market recuperated the dematerialized work.
4. **Sub-observation (the doubling).** The book itself "became, ironically, a very expensive collector's item, paralleling the fate of the art it espoused" — the critique was commodified along with its object.
5. **Conclusion (verdict).** "Art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries"; the escape was "temporary," art "recaptured and sent back to its white cell."
6. **Structural lesson.** The commodity form is not a property of the object's materiality; de-materializing the object does not de-commodify the art. The hope inverted cause and effect.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Lippard's own self-correction is the strongest counter, and she makes it herself.** This is not a thesis defended but a hope retracted; the argument *is* the limitation. She had even predicted the failure in 1969 — "the art world is probably going to be able to absorb conceptual art as another 'movement' and not pay too much attention to it" — and reprints the prediction unrevised, so the recuperation is, by her own framing, foreseeable rather than surprising.
- **The escape was never total to begin with.** Lippard concedes that "however rebellious the escape attempts, most of the work remained art-referential, and neither economic nor esthetic ties to the art world were fully severed (though at times we liked to think they were hanging by a thread)." The market did not have to *recapture* what had never left.
- **"Parole is always a possibility."** The metaphor refuses a purely defeatist reading: the verdict is recuperation, not futility. Lippard balances it against real, surviving gains — "minor revolutions in communication," cooperative galleries, artists' books, an "international performance art and video network," and "a model that remains flexible enough to be useful today." The failure is the failure to escape the *market*, not the failure of the work.
- **The mechanism is asserted more than analyzed.** Lippard states *that* the work was recuperated and *that* art remains a "luxury"; she gestures at the "invisible apron strings" binding the small art world to the "'real world's' power structure," but does not give a worked theory of *why* the commodity form survives dematerialization. The structural lesson — that commodification is a social relation, not a material attribute — is `Interpretive synthesis` drawn from her evidence, not a thesis she formalizes.
- **Selection bias in the verdict.** The claim concerns "the major conceptualists." The work that genuinely resisted sale (a "secret," an "invisible Magnetic Field," On Kawara's telegram) tends to be the work that also stayed marginal; the verdict is partly a statement about which dematerialized art the market chose to notice.

## Related Arguments

- [[Exhibition value is a third value form beyond Marx]] — supplies the missing mechanism. If, as Han argues, displayed things acquire a value form beyond use and exchange, then the commodity status of art was never anchored in the object's materiality, and dematerialization could not dislodge it. Lippard documents the failure; the exhibition-value argument names why it was structurally inevitable. (`Speculative connection` — cross-author, drawn by this vault.)
- [[The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable]] — the interpretive parallel to Hayles. Lippard's dematerialization and Hayles's "[[The Materiality of Informatics|information lost its body]]" are the same gesture in different domains: both presume value/meaning can be detached from a material substrate, and both are shown to fail — for Hayles because information is always instantiated, for Lippard because the commodity form rides the social relation, not the matter. The "immaterial" never stands free of the conditions that produce it. (`Speculative connection`.)

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — this argument is the art-historical *test case* of the vault's titular axis, and it fails in the same direction Hayles and Han predict. Dematerialization treated immateriality as a route of escape (thin the matter, lose the market); the outcome shows the immaterial is parasitic on the material conditions — galleries, prestige, ownership, the property state — that an "ideology of dematerialization would obscure." The "immaterial" pole did not free art from the market; it relocated the market onto documents, certificates, and the book itself.
