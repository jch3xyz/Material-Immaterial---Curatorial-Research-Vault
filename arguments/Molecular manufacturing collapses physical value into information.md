# Molecular manufacturing collapses physical value into information

## Claim

When molecular nanotechnology makes it possible to assemble any physical object from inexpensive raw atoms via a software description, the cost and value of physical goods collapse to essentially zero—leaving information (the design) as the only remaining locus of value.

## Expanded Explanation

Kurzweil argues that the mature nanotechnology assembler—a tabletop device capable of building almost any physically possible product from raw atomic feedstock—will reduce the incremental cost of making physical things to "pennies per pound." The atoms are cheap; the assembly energy is cheap; the only scarce ingredient is the software description that tells the assembler what to build. At that point, physical objects become instantiations of information: a computer, a coat, a meal, or a house are all equivalent to a downloadable file executed on a universal fabricator.

This is not merely a prediction about future manufacturing economics. For Kurzweil it is a logical completion of a trajectory already visible in the present: the information content of products is already "rapidly increasing, gradually approaching an asymptote of 100 percent of their value." The material substrate does not disappear—atoms are still assembled—but the substrate is demoted to a commodity input with near-zero marginal cost, while the pattern (the design file) becomes the entire product.

The argument has a second register beyond economics. If value is information and information is copyable, transmissible, and storable independently of any particular instantiation of matter, then the conceptual boundary between the material and the immaterial dissolves at the level of production itself. Making a thing and downloading a file become economically and ontologically equivalent operations.

## Reasoning Structure

1. A mature molecular assembler can manufacture almost any physically possible product from raw atomic feedstock.
2. The incremental cost of the raw feedstock and assembly energy approaches zero (Drexler's estimate: ten to fifty cents per kilogram regardless of product).
3. Therefore the only non-trivial cost is the software description (design information) that controls the assembly.
4. Therefore the value of a physical product is entirely the value of its information content.
5. Corollary: the material/immaterial distinction collapses at the level of production—making is downloading.

(`Directly stated`)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The atoms still matter.** The argument elides the logistics of raw material supply and the energy required for feedstock processing. Even at ten cents per kilogram, global-scale atomic feedstock is not free; scarcity and distribution of carbon-rich source materials could re-ground value in the material.
- **Intellectual property and access inequality.** Collapsing manufacturing cost into information cost does not democratize access if design files are proprietary or paywalled. The value of information shifts, not disappears; monopoly over design information would reproduce and potentially intensify existing inequalities. Kurzweil addresses this only obliquely.
- **Safety prohibitions may prevent realization.** Kurzweil acknowledges that the Foresight Institute's ethical guidelines and safety concerns about self-replication may constrain precisely the universal-assembler architecture that the argument requires. A world of licensed, restricted assemblers is not a world where physical value has collapsed.
- **Lippard's precedent.** Conceptual art in the 1960s–70s attempted a related dematerialization—collapsing the art object into idea or documentation—and failed to escape the market (see [[Dematerialization failed to escape the market]]). The recapture of value by information regimes (IP, design patents, platform fees) may follow the same logic here.
- **Hayles's embodiment critique.** [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]] — the move to treat matter as mere substrate while privileging the pattern repeats the disembodiment fantasy that Hayles argues is never actually achieved; some material configuration always carries the information, and the properties of that configuration are never neutral.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — This argument is one of the most direct statements in the vault of the claim that the material can be fully translated into (and superseded by) the immaterial. It sits at the extreme immaterial pole and provides a technological grounding for what Lippard, Hayles, and Han approach from cultural and philosophical directions.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — Kurzweil explicitly demotes the material body to a changeable, optional instantiation of an informational pattern; the tension here is with Hayles's insistence that embodiment is not optional but always operative.
