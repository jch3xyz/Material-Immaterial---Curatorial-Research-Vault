# Big Data is blind to the event and so blind to the future

## Claim

Because Big Data is statistical — it calculates only average values and what is probable — it has no access to the singular, so it is wholly blind to the event, and since it is the unlikely, singular event that shapes history, Big Data is blind to the future too.

## Expanded Explanation

This is Han's decisive limit-argument against predictive dataism, and the structural counterpart to his concept of [[The Event]]. The whole euphoria of [[Big Data]] rests on the promise of foretelling the future — Brooks's dataist creed includes the assumption "that data will help us do remarkable things - like foretell the future." Han grants Big Data its predictive power over [[Gleichschaltung|collective behaviour]] but draws a sharp boundary around it: "Data-mining does not differ from statistics in any fundamental way. The correlations it lays bare represent only what is likely in statistical terms. It calculates average values." Being statistical, the apparatus can model the herd, the regular, the recurrent — but the regular is precisely not what makes history. (`Directly stated`)

Han reaches this conclusion through a genealogy of the affect against statistical reason. He sets today's Big-Data euphoria beside the eighteenth-century euphoria over statistics, then summons Romanticism and Nietzsche as the resistance that "mounted against statistical reason." Romanticism's "fundamental affect" is "horror at everything average and normal," and it "cultivated the outlandish, abnormal and the extreme in order to counter statistical normality." Nietzsche's abhorrence is the hinge: for him the figures of statistics "prove only that man is a herd animal" and that the masses are "increasingly growing equal" — *die Menschen zunehmen im Gleichwerden*. This is the same logic of sameness Han names [[Gleichschaltung]] and diagnoses as the [[Hell of the Same]]: "Dataism is augmenting Zunehmen im Gleichwerden, or hypertrophied sameness." Statistics is rigorous exactly to the degree that the population is uniform; "way up at the top, where the great minds are, you no longer can make any calculations at all." (`Directly stated`)

From this Han draws the future-blindness as a strict entailment. What shapes history is not the statistically likely but the singular, the unlikely, the event — and the event is by definition outside the average that statistics extrapolates. Hence the apparatus that knows only averages cannot, in principle, see what is coming: "Big Data has no access to what is unique. Big Data is wholly blind to the event… Thus, Big Data is blind to the future too." The predictive instrument is blind to precisely the thing prediction would most need to see. This is why, for Han, the event is the basis of resistance: the singular is the constitutive blind spot of total data-knowledge, the gap predictive psychopolitics cannot foreclose. (`Directly stated` for the entailment; `Interpretive synthesis` for its function as the basis of resistance, which Han develops in "Beyond the Subject," not this chapter.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise: Data-mining "does not differ from statistics in any fundamental way"; the correlations it produces "represent only what is likely in statistical terms" — it "calculates average values."
2. Premise: As a statistical operation, Big Data therefore "has no access to what is unique"; it captures the herd and the average (*Zunehmen im Gleichwerden*), not the singular.
3. Premise: It is "not what is statistically likely, but what is unlikely - the singular, the event" — that "will shape history, in other words, the future of mankind."
4. Conclusion: Therefore Big Data "is wholly blind to the event," and since the event is what shapes the future, "Big Data is blind to the future too."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The dataist reply (which Han stages only to reject) is that with enough data prediction becomes exhaustive — Anderson's "With enough data, the numbers speak for themselves." Han's argument depends on the singular being irreducible to any volume of past data; a critic could hold that rare events are themselves statistically modelable as low-probability tail events, which would blunt the "no access to what is unique" claim.
- The argument is conceptual, not empirical: Han establishes the blindness by definition (statistics = the average; the event = the singular), so it stands or falls on the equation "data-mining ≈ statistics." Machine-learning methods that depart from classical statistics are not addressed in the chapter.
- "Blind to the future" is a claim about the *historically decisive* future (what "shapes history"), not about all prediction; Big Data may still forecast aggregate, average outcomes well. The thesis concerns the singular rupture, not routine extrapolation.
