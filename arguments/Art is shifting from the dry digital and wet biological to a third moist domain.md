# Art is shifting from the dry digital and wet biological to a third moist domain

## Claim

By historical analogy — twentieth-century interactive media "replaced the cult of the objet d'art with a process-based culture" — Ascott argues that as silicon and pixels merge with molecules and matter, a third, **moist** interspace, [[Moistmedia]], becomes the substrate of twenty-first-century art.

## Expanded Explanation

The argument is structured as a one-step extrapolation from an accomplished art-historical fact. Ascott takes as settled that interactive media in the twentieth century already broke the "cult of the objet d'art" and installed in its place a [[Process replaces product just as system supersedes structure|process-based culture]] — the very shift the vault traces from [[Jack Burnham|Burnham]]'s 1968 object→system thesis (see [[Object and System]], [[Systems Esthetics]]) through Lippard's [[Dematerialization]]. He then projects the same logic one register deeper: where the twentieth-century shift was from object to immaterial process, the twenty-first-century shift is the convergence of the *dry* (digital, virtual) and the *wet* (biological, vegetal) into a *moist* third domain. The "moist" is not a return to the object but a re-materialisation of the immaterial digital into "bits, atoms, neurons, and genes" — the substrate Ascott names [[Moistmedia]].

The keystone passage frames the moist as "a new interspace of potentiality and promise" lying *between* virtuality and biology, and identifies its art as "a transformative art concerned with the construction of a fluid reality." Crucially, the convergence is triple, not double: in the manifesto litany "MOIST ART is digitally dry, biologically wet, and spiritually numinous." The spiritual/numinous third pole is what makes the moist domain [[Technoetics|technoetic]] (techne + gnōsis) rather than merely a hybrid of computation and biology, and what links the argument to the consciousness-and-[[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]] cluster rather than to engineering alone.

This is the keystone Ascott statement of the Burnham object→system / process-culture bridge translated into the moist register. It does not abolish matter or the object — it claims a new substrate (moistmedia) in which the dry-immaterial and the wet-material are synthesised, which is why it belongs to the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension as a *re-materialisation* move rather than a continuation of dematerialisation. It updates Ascott's own [[Reciprocal Communication|interactive]], behaviour-driven account of [[New Media Art|new-media art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] with the biological and spiritual dimensions the digital-only telematic chapters lacked.

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise (accomplished fact):** Twentieth-century interactive media transformed print and broadcasting and "replaced the cult of the objet d'art with a process-based culture."
2. **Premise (present development):** At the start of the twenty-first century "silicon and pixels merge with molecules and matter" — the digital converges with the biological (and, in the manifesto, the spiritual).
3. **Inference (the analogy):** Just as the prior media shift produced a new (process) culture, this convergence produces a new domain — neither the dry digital nor the wet biological but a *moist* interspace between them.
4. **Conclusion:** Moistmedia — "comprising bits, atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination" — "will constitute the substrate of the art of our new century, a transformative art concerned with the construction of a fluid reality."

- The art-historical warrant — interactive media already "replaced the cult of the objet d'art with a process-based culture" (the premise the analogy extrapolates from). See [[#Sources]].
- The "Moist Manifesto" litany defining the new domain: "MOIST SPACE is where dry pixels and wet molecules converge"; "MOIST ART is digitally dry, biologically wet, and spiritually numinous"; "MOIST MEDIA comprises bits, atoms, neurons, and genes."
- The dry / wet / moist tripartition stated directly: the moist domain as "a new interspace of potentiality and promise" lying "between the dry world of virtuality and the wet world of biology."
- The definition of moistmedia as the "substrate of the art of our new century, a transformative art concerned with the construction of a fluid reality."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Argument by analogy, not demonstration.** The whole case rests on the claim that the twenty-first-century convergence will repeat the structure of the twentieth-century media shift. It is a programmatic, visionary forecast (a manifesto), labelled `Directly stated` because Ascott explicitly asserts it — not because the moist culture is established.
- **The premise is itself contestable.** That interactive media *replaced* (rather than supplemented) the cult of the objet d'art is the strong reading of the Burnham–Lippard arc; the unique art object and the market persisted (cf. [[Dematerialization]] failing to escape the market), so the analogy may overstate the completeness of the prior shift.
- **Speculative substrate.** "Bits, atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination" presumes a maturity of nanotechnology, artificial life, and genetic intervention that, in 2000, was largely projective.
- **The "spiritually numinous" third pole is asserted.** The move from a dry/wet (digital/biological) duality to a triple digital/biological/spiritual convergence carries Ascott's [[Technoetics|technoetic]] commitments; readers who do not grant the spiritual pole get only a digital–biological hybrid, not the "moist" as Ascott defines it.
