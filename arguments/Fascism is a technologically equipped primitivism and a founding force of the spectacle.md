# Fascism is a technologically equipped primitivism and a founding force of the spectacle

## Claim

Fascism is not fundamentally an ideology but a violently modern staging of archaic myth: it resurrects "race, blood, leader" through "the most modern means of conditioning and illusion," and because it also destroyed the old working-class movement it stands as both a form of the [[Spectacle|spectacle]] and "one of the founding forces of present-day society."

## Expanded Explanation

The argument occurs at thesis 109 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*, in the chapter where [[Guy Debord]] narrates the defeat of the workers' movement as a genealogy of the modern [[Spectacle|spectacle]]. Debord frames fascism first as an economic emergency measure — "a desperate attempt to defend the bourgeois economy from the dual threat of crisis and proletarian subversion," a "state of siege in which capitalist society saved itself by giving itself an emergency dose of rationalization in the form of massive state intervention." But this rationalization, he insists, "is hampered by the extreme irrationality of its methods." The crucial move is Debord's refusal to read fascism as *primarily* ideological. Although it rallies to the conservative icons of a bourgeois order in crisis — "family, private property, moral order, patriotism" — "it is not itself fundamentally ideological." It "presents itself as what it is-a violent resurrection of myth calling for participation in a community defined by archaic pseudo values: race, blood, leader."

The compressed formula — "Fascism is a technologically equipped primitivism" — captures the contradiction Debord wants to name: a *regression* in content (mythic, archaic, tribal) carried by an *advance* in form (the technological apparatus of mass conditioning). The two halves are not in tension but in collusion. "Its factitious mythological rehashes are presented in the spectacular context of the most modern means of conditioning and illusion." The myth is fake — "factitious … rehashes" — and precisely because it is fabricated it needs the modern machinery of illusion to be believed. This is why fascism belongs to the theory of the spectacle rather than merely to a theory of ideology: the spectacle is Debord's name for a regime in which lived reality has receded into staged appearance, and fascism is that regime's earliest total, mythic staging.

From this two Debordian consequences follow, and together they make fascism *foundational* rather than aberrant. First, "It is thus a significant factor in the formation of the modern spectacle" — fascism is one of the historical laboratories in which the modern spectacle's techniques of mass conditioning were developed. Second, "its role in the destruction of the old working-class movement also makes it one of the founding forces of present-day society." The spectacle, for Debord, is what fills the void left when the proletariat failed to become the conscious subject of its own history; by helping to destroy the workers' movement (alongside "the Stalinist bureaucracy and offascist totalitarianism"), fascism cleared the ground on which the spectacular organization of society was built. Debord closes the thesis by noting fascism's obsolescence-in-victory: "since it is also the most costly method of preserving the capitalist order, it has generally ended up being pushed to the back of the stage and replaced by the major capitalist states, which represent stronger and more rational forms of that order" — the spectacle survives its fascist prototype and diffuses into the more efficient, "diffuse" forms of the liberal-capitalist democracies.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Fascism arises as an economic-political emergency: a "state of siege" defending the bourgeois economy against crisis and proletarian subversion through massive state intervention (thesis 109).
2. Its content is not fundamentally ideological but *mythic regression*: a "violent resurrection of myth" organized around the "archaic pseudo values" of race, blood, and leader (thesis 109).
3. Its form is maximally modern: the archaic myth is "presented in the spectacular context of the most modern means of conditioning and illusion" (thesis 109). Hence "technologically equipped primitivism."
4. Because the myth is factitious, the modern apparatus of illusion is not incidental to it but constitutive — this is what makes fascism a phenomenon of the [[Spectacle|spectacle]], not merely of ideology.
5. Fascism therefore contributes twice to the modern spectacle: positively, as a laboratory of mass conditioning ("a significant factor in the formation of the modern spectacle"); and negatively, by "the destruction of the old working-class movement," clearing the ground for spectacular society.
6. Conclusion: fascism is "one of the founding forces of present-day society" — even though, being "the most costly method of preserving the capitalist order," it is later superseded by "stronger and more rational forms" of that order (thesis 109).

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The direct definitional statements of thesis 109: fascism as "a violent resurrection of myth" and "a technologically equipped primitivism" whose "factitious mythological rehashes" are staged through "the most modern means of conditioning and illusion" ([[raw/Debord - The Society of the Spectacle/07 Ch4 - The Proletariat as Subject and Representation]]).
- The historical claim opening thesis 109: "Between the two world wars the revolutionary working-class movement was destroyed by the joint action of the Stalinist bureaucracy and offascist totalitarianism" — placing fascism's spectacular function inside the larger genealogy of the workers' movement's defeat (Ch. 4, theses 95–109).
- The structural parallel with Stalinism developed in the same chapter — "this crude form of the spectacle" that "simply used police-state methods to transform people's perception of the world" (thesis 105) — against which fascism is presented as the mythic-nationalist rather than bureaucratic-ideological variant of the same spectacular formation.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Fascism as ideology.** Standard accounts treat fascism precisely as an ideology (of nation, race, hierarchy). Debord's claim that "it is not itself fundamentally ideological" is a deliberately narrow, Situationist use of "ideology" (a coherent false consciousness with theoretical pretensions): fascism, for him, dispenses with the theoretical scaffolding and presents naked myth. Read against a broader definition of ideology, the claim is more a repositioning than a denial.
- **Historical scope.** Debord writes in 1967 and treats classical interwar fascism as a superseded prototype "pushed to the back of the stage." The thesis is a diagnosis of fascism's *founding* contribution to the spectacle, not a prediction; whether later authoritarian-populist "spectacular" politics vindicate or complicate the "technologically equipped primitivism" formula is a question the thesis raises but does not settle.
- **Compression.** The argument is delivered in a single aphoristic thesis. Its force depends on accepting Debord's prior definition of the [[Spectacle|spectacle]] as a materialized worldview and on the chapter's larger claim that "the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class" (thesis 100); detached from that frame, "founding force of present-day society" can read as overstatement.

## Tensions

- [[Reality vs Appearance]] — the founding opposition of the book: fascism is the case where "archaic pseudo values" (a fabricated mythic *appearance*) are made to stand in for and organize social *reality* through modern illusion.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the immaterial mythic content (race, blood, leader) is "technologically equipped," i.e. materialized as an apparatus of mass conditioning; fascism instances the spectacle's characteristic hardening of a worldview into objective social fact.
