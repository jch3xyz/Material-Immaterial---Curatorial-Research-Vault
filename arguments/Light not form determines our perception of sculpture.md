# Light not form determines our perception of sculpture

## Claim

Because we perceive forms only through their light-emitting properties, it is light—not form—that determines our perception of sculpture; reflection, transparency, and color therefore become the sculptor's most evocative stimuli, and the development culminates in works "solely designed as modulators of light."

## Expanded Explanation

This is the hinge argument at the close of Chapter 3 ("Optical Properties of Sculpture"), where Burnham, having tracked the exhaustion of the formalist vocabulary — proportion, scale, mass, structure — names what has quietly replaced it. The replacement is not a fresh repertoire of forms but a change in the *perceptual basis* of the art altogether: "In the past decade the tacit conviction has arisen that light-not form-determines our perception of sculpture" (`Directly stated`). Writing about the "optical properties" of sculpture, he notes, would ten years earlier "have seemed a very curious, if not oblique, approach to an art based primarily on the ordering of physical mass" — and it no longer does.

The argument's pivot is a claim about perception itself, stated parenthetically but doing all the causal work: "for we perceive forms in terms of their light-emitting properties." If form is only ever given to us *through* light, then controlling light is the prior and decisive act, and form becomes a downstream effect. From this it follows that "light, controlled by color, transparency, or reflection-or a combination of the three-is one of the sculptor's most evocative stimuli," and that "as the need to control light ... gained priority, the tendency grew to construct works solely designed as modulators of light" (`Directly stated`). The sculptural object is thereby reconceived as an optical instrument — a distributed array of reflective and emitting surfaces (Read's "glitter principle") rather than a coherent inner mass.

The stakes are metaphysical, not merely technical. The optical conviction is, "in the broadest sense ... a reaction against the vitalist myth that the psyche of sculpture is buried within the 0bject itself"; instead, "what is important about sculpture lies on its surface for optical examination" (`Directly stated`). Burnham presses this to its dematerializing limit: the refutation of vitalism makes "form and matter lose their substantiality. Form, in effect, is what we make it appear to be." Perception, not the object, becomes the locus of the work — which is exactly why this argument tilts the dense, autonomous sculptural object of Part I toward the optical and light *system* of Part II. It grounds [[Art as Experiential Technology]] and is the argument-form of the concept [[Light as a Sculpture Medium]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. We never apprehend form directly; we perceive forms only "in terms of their light-emitting properties." (Premise — the perceptual claim.)
2. Therefore the variable that actually governs our perception of a sculpture is its light — its reflection, transparency, and color — not its underlying form or mass. (Inference.)
3. Therefore light, "controlled by color, transparency, or reflection," is the sculptor's most evocative stimulus, and form is demoted to an effect of light. (Inference.)
4. Therefore, as controlling light gains priority, the rational endpoint is works "solely designed as modulators of light." (Conclusion — the genre claim.)
5. This refutes the vitalist myth that the work's significance ("psyche") is buried *inside* the object: significance instead "lies on its surface for optical examination," and "form and matter lose their substantiality." (Corollary — the metaphysical payoff, relocating the work from object to perception/system.)

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The historical-perceptual claim itself: "In the past decade the tacit conviction has arisen that light-not form-determines our perception of sculpture" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/05 Chapter 3 - Formalism - The Weary Vocabulary]]).
- The reflective tradition — Brancusi's *Bird in Space* designed for the natural light of his studio clerestory ("a climax of downward reflected light"), Schlemmer's mirrored *Metall-Tanz*, Arp's polished *Concretions* — read not for their volumes but for their handling of light.
- Read's "glitter principle": postwar American sculptors (Lippold, Lassaw, Bertoia) who derive reflection not "from a continuous, coherent mass of metal" but from "fragmented ... semi-random, reflective surfaces positioned in spatial configurations" — light treated as a distributed optical system. See [[Light as a Sculpture Medium]].
- The vitalism refutation: optical sculpture "is a reaction against the vitalist myth that the psyche of sculpture is buried within the 0bject itself," locating significance on the surface. See [[Vitalism]], [[Form Exhaustion]].
- The dematerializing conclusion: "form and matter lose their substantiality. Form, in effect, is what we make it appear to be."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself flags the risk: the trend toward "more color and reflectivity" can collapse into "a cult devoted to optical deception and excitation" — perception untethered from any object may be mere spectacle rather than a deeper truth about sculpture (`Directly stated`).
- The claim is period-bound and partly sociological: Burnham grants it is "the tacit conviction" of "the past decade" and concedes the shift may be read "rightly or wrongly ... as the salvation of sculpture" — a diagnosis of taste, not a proof.
- "We perceive forms in terms of their light-emitting properties" is asserted, not argued; a formalist could reply that light merely *reveals* a form whose order remains the carrier of meaning, so light is the medium of perception without being its determinant.
- The argument privileges the optical at the expense of touch, scale, and bodily mass — the very attributes Burnham elsewhere treats as sculpture's province (cf. [[A base-less sculpture ceases to be an object and becomes a physical system of forces]]).
