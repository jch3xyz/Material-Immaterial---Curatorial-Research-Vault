# Euphemism and secrecy are structural to surveillance capitalism not incidental

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism's ability to dispossess depends on language—euphemism, omission, and secrecy—as much as on its technical methods, making the concealment structural to the project rather than an incidental byproduct.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff argues that the dispossession at the heart of surveillance capitalism—the unilateral taking of human experience as free raw material—could not survive open description. The rights grab is therefore wrapped in a vocabulary that drains it of moral and legal weight. "Digital exhaust" and "digital breadcrumbs" recast deliberately constructed behavioral data as worthless waste left lying around for the taking; "targeted" advertising connotes precision and competence while concealing the override of users' decision rights. These are not loose figures of speech but load-bearing instruments of legitimation. (Directly stated)

The decisive move in the argument is the colonial-map analogy. Zuboff aligns Google's industry jargon with the labels on the earliest maps of North America—"heathens," "infidels," "idolaters," "primitives"—that deleted native peoples and their claims from the invaders' moral and legal equations, legitimating acts of taking and breaking. The euphemism does the same work in both cases: it makes the prior occupant unreadable as a rights-bearing party, so the appropriation can proceed without contest. This is why she insists the concealment is structural. The taking is invisible not because anyone failed to notice, but because the language was engineered so that there would be nothing visible to object to. (Directly stated)

Language is one element in a larger repertoire of hiding—"rhetoric, omission, complexity, exclusivity, scale, abusive contracts, design, and euphemism." Together these explain why, during Google's breakthrough to profitability, few grasped the foundational mechanisms of its success. The corollary is Zuboff's anti-functionalist thesis: commercial surveillance "is not merely an unfortunate accident or occasional lapse," nor a necessary product of digital technology, but "a specifically constructed human choice." If the dispossession is a choice rather than a destiny, then the rhetoric that hides the choice is part of the construction, not a side effect of it. (Directly stated)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The dispossession of behavioral surplus is a "rights grab"—the taking of decision rights without permission.
2. An open description of that taking would invite contest, regulation, and refusal.
3. Therefore the taking must be hidden; hiding "depends on language as much as it does on technical methods or corporate policies of secrecy."
4. Euphemisms ("digital exhaust," "digital breadcrumbs," "targeted") perform this hiding by recoding expropriation as recycling, waste, or precision.
5. This is the same mechanism by which colonial cartographic euphemisms ("heathens," "primitives") deleted native peoples from the invaders' moral and legal equations.
6. Conclusion: because the rhetoric is doing the legitimating work that makes the construction possible, euphemism and secrecy are structural to surveillance capitalism, not incidental.

- George Orwell's observation that euphemisms in politics, war, and business are instruments that "make lies sound truthful and murder respectable." (Ch. 3) (Directly stated)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- A functionalist objection holds that jargon like "digital exhaust" is ordinary technical shorthand, not deliberate concealment. Zuboff anticipates and rejects this: she frames it as "intentional work" and "a fog of euphemism" the company "learned" to deploy. (Directly stated)
- The argument rests substantially on the Orwell maxim and the colonial analogy, which are rhetorical and historical rather than empirical demonstrations of intent; the strongest direct evidence (the patent's frankness "before" euphemism) is one case. (Interpretive synthesis)
- The claim concerns Google's early period; whether the same rhetorical strategy generalizes across all surveillance-capitalist firms is asserted by Zuboff's wider thesis rather than proven here. (Strongly implied)
