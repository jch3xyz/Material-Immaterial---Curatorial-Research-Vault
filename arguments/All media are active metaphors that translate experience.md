# All media are active metaphors that translate experience

## Claim

Every medium is an active metaphor: just as a metaphor transforms and transmits experience by carrying it across from one form into another, a technology translates experience into a new, amplified, and explicit form.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan's Chapter 6 argument fuses two ideas the title holds together — that media are *translators* and that they are *metaphors*. A metaphor works by "getting at one thing through another"; it carries experience across (the literal sense of *meta-pherein*) from an immediate, implicit register into an uttered, "outered" one. Technology does the same: "technology is explicitness," and "translation is thus a 'spelling-out' of forms of knowing." To mechanize nature, or our own natures, is to translate them "into amplified and specialized forms." Because every medium performs this carrying-across, "all media are active metaphors in their power to translate experience into new forms" — and conversely, "just as a metaphor transforms and transmits experience, so do the media."

The metaphor is "active" because translation is never neutral transcription; it restructures what it transmits. McLuhan unpacks the colloquial "I'll take a rain-check on that" as a four-part ratio — "Your invitation is to ordinary invitations as ball games are to conventional social life" — to show that a figure of speech stores and amplifies experience by "seeing one set of relations through another set." Money works the same way ("money is also a metaphor"), as does Bacon's emblem of Pan choosing Echo for his wife — "that alone is true philosophy which doth faithfully render the very words of the world." The translation is realized even by the literal machine: the Mark II translator stands ready "to render the masterpieces of literature from any language into any other language."

This is the engine beneath McLuhan's larger claims. If every technology is a translation of experience, then in the electric age "we see ourselves being translated more and more into the form of information, moving toward the technological extension of consciousness" — the argument links directly to [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]] and underwrites the reading of [[Media as Extensions of Man]] as extensions that re-form, and do not merely relay, what they extend.

## Reasoning Structure

1. A metaphor transforms and transmits experience by carrying it across from one form into another ("getting at one thing through another").
2. A technology does exactly this: "technology is explicitness"; mechanization is "a translation of nature, and of our own natures, into amplified and specialized forms."
3. Therefore every medium *is* an active metaphor — its defining power is "to translate experience into new forms."
4. Because the translation is active (a four-part ratio, as in "rain-check" or money), the medium reshapes experience rather than passively conveying it.
5. Generalized to the electric age, this means we are being "translated more and more into the form of information," extending consciousness itself outward.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- McLuhan offers the metaphor-translation thesis as a direct assertion rather than a proved theorem; the supporting cases (rain-check, money, Pan/Echo, Mark II) are illustrative analogies, not a demonstration that *all* media share the structure.
- "Active metaphor" risks circularity: if any carrying-across counts as metaphor, the claim that media are metaphors becomes definitional rather than empirical.
- The chapter also concedes that translation can fail or numb — what is not translated into conscious art is "repressed," and the unified-image promise sits beside the Narcissus illusion — so the translating power of media is double-edged, not simply enlarging.
