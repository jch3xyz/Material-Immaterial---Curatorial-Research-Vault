# Eros and depression are opposites the achievement-subject cannot love

## Claim

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], [[Eros]] and depression are structural opposites: eros pulls the subject out of itself toward [[The Other]], whereas depression plunges the subject into itself — and because the narcissistic [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] validates itself through success, robbing the Other of its otherness and degrading it into a mirror, it can neither reach the Other nor love, and its corollary is success-induced depression.

## Expanded Explanation

The argument opens the *Agony of Eros* proper (chapter 2, "Melancholia"). Han's diagnosis is that "the crisis of love does not derive from too many others so much as from the erosion of the Other" — an explicit correction of [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz|Eva Illouz]], whose sociology traces the end of love to the rationalization of choice. Eros, by contrast, "concerns the Other in the strong sense, namely, what cannot be encompassed by the regime of the ego." It presupposes "the asymmetry and exteriority of the Other" — the placeless, *atopos* Other who cannot be compared, consumed, or assimilated. In the [[Hell of the Same|inferno of the same]] that contemporary society is becoming, this atopic negativity is flattened out, and so "erotic experience does not exist."

The affective mechanism is narcissism. "Narcissism is not the same as self-love": the self-loving subject draws a boundary against the Other, but the narcissistic subject dissolves that boundary, so "the world appears only as adumbrations of the narcissist's self." Depression is then "a narcissistic malady" — a "pathologically distorted self-reference" in which the subject, "abandoned by the Other," worn out and worlds, "wallows in its own shadow everywhere until it drowns—in itself." Against this Han sets the antithesis that titles this note: "Eros and depression are opposites. Eros pulls the subject out of itself, toward the Other. Depression, in contrast, plunges the subject into itself."

The achievement-subject is the figure in whom the two collapse together. Because it "seeks out success above all," and "finding success validates the One through the Other," the Other is instrumentalized into a mirror that only reflects the One's image back to it. This "logic of recognition ensnares the narcissistic achievement-subject more deeply in the ego," and its "corollary is success-induced depression: the depressive achievement-subject sinks into, and suffocates in, itself." Eros would be the cure precisely because it is not an achievement but a receptivity — "freely willed self-renunciation, freely willed self-evacuation," a weakening "accompanied by a feeling of strength" that "is not the achievement of the One, but the gift of the Other." Chapter 4 ("Bare Life") restates the same incapacity through Hegel's master–slave dialectic: the slave, who "clings to bare life" and labors rather than risking death, "proves incapable of erotic experience—of erotic desire," and "today's achievement-subject is equal to the Hegelian slave in all respects except for the fact that he does not work for the master, but rather exploits himself of his own volition." (See [[Bare Life]], [[Auto-Exploitation]].)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Eros is the relation to the Other "in the strong sense" — the *atopos*, "what cannot be encompassed by the regime of the ego"; it "presumes the asymmetry and exteriority of the Other."
2. Eros and depression are opposite motions: eros "pulls the subject out of itself, toward the Other"; depression "plunges the subject into itself."
3. The narcissistic subject dissolves the boundary between self and Other, so it "is incapable of recognizing the Other in his or her otherness"; "the world appears only as adumbrations of the narcissist's self."
4. The achievement-subject "seeks out success above all"; success "validates the One through the Other," and "the Other is robbed of otherness and degrades into a mirror of the One."
5. Robbed of its otherness, the Other can no longer be reached or loved — eros, which requires the Other's exteriority, is foreclosed; the achievement-subject cannot love.
6. The corollary is "success-induced depression": ensnared "more deeply in the ego," the depressive achievement-subject "sinks into, and suffocates in, itself."
7. Restated in chapter 4: the achievement-subject is the self-exploiting Hegelian slave who "clings to bare life" and so "proves incapable of erotic experience." Eros, "as excess and transgression," "denies both work and bare life."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument rests on a strong, quasi-definitional premise — that eros *requires* the exteriority of a non-assimilable Other. A reader who holds a more reciprocal or immanent account of love (mutual recognition rather than self-loss) could grant Han's phenomenology of narcissism while denying that success and love are mutually exclusive.
- Han inherits the equation of depression with narcissism largely from a Freudian frame ("Depression is a narcissistic malady" evokes Freud's *Mourning and Melancholia*), but Freud is not named in this chapter; the clinical claim is asserted, not argued from the psychiatric literature, and elides depression's biological and social etiologies.
- The master–slave restatement in chapter 4 imports Hegel's schema wholesale; whether the "self-exploiting slave who is master and slave at once" is really "incapable of erotic experience," or merely differently capable, is asserted rather than demonstrated.
- The claim is an identity claim, not a tendency claim: the achievement-subject *cannot* love, not merely tends not to. This forecloses gradations — the possibility of eros surviving within, or against, an achievement-oriented life.
