# The state outsources surveillance to private firms to evade constitutional constraints

## Claim

Because the Constitution slows government actors with warrants, due process, and oversight while imposing few privacy restrictions on the business records people give to third parties, the state relies on private firms to collect and generate information for it — outsourcing surveillance to inherit the lawlessness it is itself denied.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the legal-structural mechanism beneath [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]]. Zuboff reports the diagnosis through three legal scholars she cites in *The Moat Around the Castle*. The constitutional order, by design, binds the state but not the firm. Intelligence agencies are "bound to democratic constraints" — the "time-wasting practices of due process, evidence, warrants, and law" — whereas a company like Google "enjoyed" an "absence of law." The two are not symmetrically regulated, and that asymmetry is itself the incentive: the agencies "craved the lawlessness that a firm such as Google enjoyed" (`Directly stated`).

Law professor Jack Balkin supplies the constitutional reasoning. The Constitution "inhibits government actors from high-velocity pursuit of their surveillance agenda, and this creates incentives for the government 'to rely on private enterprise to collect and generate information for it.'" The doctrinal gap is specific: the Supreme Court "has imposed few privacy restrictions on business records and information that people give to third parties," and e-mail held on private servers has protection "limited if not nonexistent." Private firms therefore become "attractive partners for government actors" precisely because the firms are not bound by what binds the state (`Directly stated`). Legal scholar Jon Michaels extends this: the government's "need to evade constitutional oversight" produces secret public-private collaborations "orchestrated around handshakes rather than legal formalities, such as search warrants," arrangements that may be made this way "to evade oversight and, at times, to defy the law" (`Directly stated`).

The consequence is the engine of surveillance exceptionalism. The outsourcing does not merely let the state see more; it shelters the firm's extraction from regulation, because the data the state covets is the same [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral surplus]] the firm monetizes. Zuboff's counterfactual sharpens the point: "Had it not been for surveillance exceptionalism, it is possible that these data would not even exist, at least not in their current volume and detail" (`Directly stated`). The lawlessness the firm enjoys and the lawlessness the state borrows are one and the same — the moat around the castle protects both.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The Constitution constrains government surveillance — warrants, due process, oversight, the "slow by design" tempos of democracy.
2. The Constitution imposes few limits on third-party business records and information people give to firms; e-mail on private servers has protection "limited if not nonexistent."
3. Therefore private firms enjoy an "absence of law" — a lawlessness the agencies lack and crave.
4. This asymmetry creates an incentive for the state "to rely on private enterprise to collect and generate information for it" (Balkin).
5. The state thus outsources collection to evade its own constitutional oversight, through "handshakes rather than legal formalities" (Michaels).
6. Conclusion: the private firm becomes the state's surveillance proxy — the legal-structural engine of [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]], which shelters the firm's extraction in return.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Zuboff concedes the historical claim is not controlled: "history offers us no control groups, and we cannot know with certainty whether information capitalism might have developed in a different direction." The outsourcing thesis is one of several conditions she names (alongside the neoliberal inheritance and second-modernity needs), not a sole cause.
- The argument describes an American constitutional structure; the Cate/Dempsey observation that "essentially every government in the world" compels such disclosure suggests the mechanism may be a global template rather than a U.S. exception — which weakens any framing of it as a single jurisdiction's loophole.
- A defender of the security state would invoke the [[Privacy vs Security]] tension directly: in an emergency of "milliseconds," the third-party doctrine is not an evasion but a legitimate adaptation. Zuboff treats this defense as the very rationalization that naturalizes the exception.
- The claim is about *incentive and affinity*, not a proven conspiracy; Michaels's "handshakes" and Zuboff's "elective affinity" (Weber) describe a magnetism of shared interest, leaving the precise causal weight of intent versus structure open.

- [[Privacy vs Security]] — this argument is the institutional resolution of that tension *against* privacy: the third-party doctrine lets the security imperative route around the privacy protections that would otherwise bind the state.
