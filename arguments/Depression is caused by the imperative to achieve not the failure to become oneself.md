# Depression is caused by the imperative to achieve not the failure to become oneself

## Claim

Depression is the pathology of the [[Achievement Society]], caused by the imperative/pressure to achieve, by impoverished attachment [Bindungsarmut], and by the systemic violence of [[Auto-Exploitation]] — not, as Alain Ehrenberg argues in *The Weariness of the Self*, by the failure to become oneself.

## Expanded Explanation

This is Han's central polemic against Alain Ehrenberg. Han accepts Ehrenberg's historical placement of depression — its ascent tracks the transition from disciplinary society to achievement society, as the rule of authority and observance of taboos yields to the injunction to "be ourselves." What Han rejects is Ehrenberg's *diagnosis*: that depression is an "economy of the self," the late-modern subject's failure to measure up to the demand to belong to oneself. For Han this misses the cause. Depression is not produced by the imperative *only to belong to oneself* but by the *pressure to achieve* — the new commandment of late-modern labor society — together with the impoverished attachment of an atomized social life and the systemic violence that achievement society inflicts. Burnout, on this account, "does not express the exhausted self so much as the exhausted, burnt-out soul."

Ehrenberg's error is to consider depression "only from the perspective of the economy of the self" — psychologically, in terms of the pathology of the self, with no attention to economic and political context, to Bindungsarmut, or to the systemic violence that provokes "psychic infarctions." He also wrongly equates the depressive with Nietzsche's "sovereign man"; for Han the achievement-subject is rather Nietzsche's "last man," who lacks all sovereignty and exploits himself voluntarily as predator and prey at once.

In ch. 9 Han sharpens the point into a claim about method: depression eludes psychoanalysis itself. The conflict model, the unconscious, and transference — all presuppose the negativity of repression and prohibition that constituted the disciplinary society where psychoanalysis was founded. Achievement society, dominated by excessive positivity, has dismantled that negativity, so its characteristic maladies (depression, burnout, ADHD) "indicate an excess of positivity, that is, not negation so much as the inability to say no." Ehrenberg recognizes that depression is marked by an absent relation to conflict yet still clings to the conflict model — he "attempts to rescue psychoanalysis even though its conditions have disappeared."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Han accepts Ehrenberg's placement of depression in the disciplinary→achievement transition (continuity holds at the level of the drive to maximize production).
2. But Ehrenberg considers depression *only* through the economy of the self: the imperative to belong to oneself, the failure to become oneself.
3. This overlooks (a) impoverished attachment [Bindungsarmut] from the atomization of social life and (b) the systemic violence of achievement society.
4. Therefore the true cause is the *pressure to achieve* — auto-exploitation under compulsive freedom — not the failure to become oneself; burnout is the exhausted soul, not the exhausted self.
5. Methodologically, depression lacks the negativity (conflict, the unconscious, transference, repression) that psychoanalysis requires; so depression eludes psychoanalysis, and Ehrenberg's attempt to retain the conflict model is misplaced.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Ehrenberg's own thesis quoted at length — that depression rises as the disciplinary model breaks against norms "enjoining us to be ourselves," and that "the depressed individual is unable to measure up; he is tired of having to become himself" — which Han then turns against him (source 1).
- The achievement-subject as Nietzsche's "last man," not "sovereign man": "The depressive human being is an animal laborans that exploits itself—and it does so voluntarily, without external constraints. It is predator and prey at once" (source 1).
- The disappearance from the depressive psyche of negation, repression, the unconscious, transference, and conflict — the conditions of psychoanalysis (source 2).
- Bindungsarmut and depression's severance of attachment: "Depression severs all attachments"; depression is "objectless and therefore undirected," distinct from both mourning and melancholy (source 2).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Ehrenberg's position is itself the standing counterargument: depression as the "economy of the self," the failure to become oneself — a psychological, self-pathology account that Han charges with ignoring economic and systemic context.
- Ehrenberg also notes that depression involves an absent relation to conflict, yet preserves the conflict model by positing a hidden conflict at its basis (further obscured by antidepressants) — a defense of psychoanalysis Han rejects as anachronistic.
- The argument depends on Han's strong positivity/negativity periodization; a reader who doubts that the unconscious or repression have genuinely "disappeared" would resist the conclusion that depression simply eludes psychoanalysis.
