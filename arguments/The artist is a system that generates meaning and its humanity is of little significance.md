# The artist is a system that generates meaning and its humanity is of little significance

## Claim

The artist is not a sovereign human author but a system — human plus artificial, coupled through the interface to networks — by and in interaction with which meaning is generated; the degree to which that system is "human" is of very little significance, because the *human* value of the meaning resides not in the maker but in the intervention of the viewer or user.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott's late telematic position dissolves the romantic figure of the artist-as-genius into a distributed cognitive arrangement. Once the artwork ceases to be a residual object and becomes an interactive system — an interface to networks of connectivity and interactivity — the locus of meaning-making shifts off the individual maker and into the loop that runs between human, machine, and audience. The artist is reframed as a node, a meaning-generating system, rather than as the unitary source of content. This is why Ascott can say the system's "humanity" barely matters: what generates meaning is the *interaction*, not the biological status of any one participant in it.

Crucially, the claim is not a nihilistic erasure of the human. Ascott relocates human value rather than abolishing it: "The human value of the meaning generated resides in the intervention of the (human) viewer or user of the system." The human re-enters at the moment of participation. The artist may be a system of indeterminate humanity, but meaning becomes *human* meaning through the viewer's act of engaging it. The argument therefore couples a post-individual, post-biological account of authorship (the artist constructs context, not content — a "hypercontext"; see [[Reciprocal Communication]]) with a participatory ethics that grounds value in reception.

This is the place where Ascott's [[New Media Art]] / [[Art as Experiential Technology]] program meets two existing strands of the vault. It extends Burnham's 1968 move from object to system into the network era: where Burnham argued that for Systems Art *information* not presence is the key, Ascott completes the trajectory by making the artist *itself* a system. And it instances Hayles's [[Distributed Cognition]]: cognition and meaning are spread across a human-plus-artificial assemblage, "structurally coupled" to its environment, rather than housed in a bounded human mind.

## Reasoning Structure

1. In telematic, interactive art the artwork is no longer a residual object but an interface to networks — a system of connectivity and interactivity.
2. The artist's task accordingly shifts from creating *content* to constructing *context* (a "hypercontext"); the maker becomes a node in the meaning-generating loop, not its sovereign origin.
3. Meaning is generated *by and in interaction with* this system, i.e. across the human-machine-viewer assemblage, not by an individual human author.
4. Therefore the degree to which the system is "human" is of very little significance to whether meaning is generated.
5. Yet the *human value* of that meaning is real, and it resides in the intervention of the (human) viewer or user.
6. Conclusion: the artist is a (possibly largely non-human) meaning-generating system whose humanity is incidental to production but whose output acquires human value through participatory reception.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The post-biological body recast as interface: "the post-biological body as interface… it is the interface to networks, to systems of connectivity and interactivity, that is our primary concern" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/23 Telenoia]]).
- The content→context shift: "the artist is concerned no longer with the creation of content but with the construction of a context (the goal is a hypercontext)" — establishing the artist as a constructor of systems rather than a maker of objects.
- The structural-coupling claim later in the chapter: "The human organism is structurally coupled to its 'environment,' and communication… will always be a process of negotiation, of modelling, and of shared construction" — grounding meaning in interaction rather than authorship.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim risks self-undermining: if the system's humanity is "of very little significance," the reservation that human value resides in the human viewer smuggles human exceptionalism back in. Ascott decentres the *artist* but re-centres the *viewer* — the human is displaced from production only to be reinstated at reception.
- A more humanist art theory would object that authorial intention and embodied skill are not incidental but constitutive of art's meaning; reducing the artist to an interchangeable "system" can flatten the difference between art and any meaning-generating apparatus.
- The argument is programmatic and forward-looking (1990s telematic utopianism, "telenoia replacing paranoia"); it asserts the artist-as-system as a cultural destiny more than it demonstrates it through analysis of specific works.

## Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — the argument lands decisively on the system side: the artist ceases to be an object-maker and becomes a system.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — meaning migrates from the material residue (the artwork-as-object) to the immaterial process of interaction across the network.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the system's "humanity" (embodiment) is rendered incidental to meaning, which is generated informationally through interaction; yet human value re-enters at the embodied point of reception.
