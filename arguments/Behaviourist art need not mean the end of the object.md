# Behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object

## Claim

For Ascott, process culture and behaviourist art reframe the art object around "new values" rather than abolishing it — so the 1960s turn to behaviour and system need not entail the disappearance of the material object.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott's 1967 manifesto *Behaviourables and Futuribles* arrives at a moment when the rhetoric of process, system and behaviour is being read by many as a programme for the elimination of the object — the trajectory Lucy Lippard would name [[Dematerialization]]. Ascott explicitly refuses that inference. Addressing "Object hustlers" directly, he tells them to "reduce your anxiety": the shift to seeing "art now as being essentially behaviourist" does not require that the object be left behind. The condition he attaches is generative rather than negating — process culture is acceptable "as long as it means the beginning of new values for art." The object is to be *revalued*, not voided.

This positions Ascott pointedly against a naive reading in which all 1960s [[Systems Esthetics|systems-oriented]] and behaviour-oriented art tends toward immateriality. Where Lippard's account leans on the object's recession into documentation and idea, Ascott keeps the object in play as a site of changed reading: "Maybe the behaviourist art object will come to be read like the palm of your hand." The object persists, but its mode of legibility migrates — from a fixed image to be contemplated toward a behavioural surface to be read, interpreted, even divined ("Pictomancy—the palmistry of paintings"). The work becomes a locus of [[Reciprocal Communication|interaction]] and prefiguration ("the delineation of futuribles") rather than a finished, autonomous form.

The claim is rhetorically hedged — it is delivered in the polemical, aphoristic register of a poster-manifesto, and the qualifier "Maybe" softens the prediction about how the object will be read. It is best understood not as a doctrinal thesis but as Ascott's deliberate correction of a slide from "behaviour" to "no object," reserving for the [[Behaviourist Art|behaviourist]] object a transformed but continuing existence.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The world, and therefore art, is now grasped as process; at the human level process is recognised as behaviour, so art is becoming essentially behaviourist.
2. A widespread (anxious) inference holds that behaviourist / process art spells "the end of the object."
3. Ascott rejects this inference: process culture and behaviourist art "need not mean the end of the object."
4. The acceptable form of the shift is one that inaugurates "new values for art" — i.e. revaluation, not elimination.
5. Conclusion: the object survives the turn to behaviour and system, but in a changed register — "read like the palm of your hand," as a behavioural / prefigurative surface rather than a static image.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The statement is rhetorically hedged: it appears in a polemical poster-manifesto, and "Maybe the behaviourist art object will come to be read like the palm of your hand" is offered as conjecture, not assertion. Confidence: `Directly stated` (but rhetorically hedged).
- It stands in tension with Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] thesis, which charts much of the same systems/behaviour-era art toward the recession of the object — see [[Dematerialization failed to escape the market]]. Whether Ascott and Lippard genuinely disagree, or are describing different strands of the period, is interpretive.
- Ascott does not specify what the "new values" are in this passage; the object's persistence is asserted more than demonstrated.
