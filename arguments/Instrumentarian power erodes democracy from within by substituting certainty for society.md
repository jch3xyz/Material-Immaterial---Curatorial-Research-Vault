# Instrumentarian power erodes democracy from within by substituting certainty for society

## Claim

[[Instrumentarian Power]] does not confront democracy by suspending institutions; it **erodes democracy from within**, growing through "declaration, self-authorization, rhetorical misdirection, euphemism, and the quiet, audacious backstage moves specifically crafted to elude awareness," and it fills the void opened by collapsing social trust by "substituting machines for social relations, which amounts to the substitution of certainty for society." (`Directly stated`)

## Expanded Explanation

The chapter's analytic move is to deny instrumentarianism the recognizable face of tyranny. Unlike totalitarianism — which seized power "through terror, murder, the suspension of democratic institutions, massacre, or expulsion" — [[Instrumentarian Power]] "operate[s] remotely and move[s] in stealth." It does not need to abolish elections, courts, or the press, because its medium of conquest is not force but procedure: the same conquest-by-declaration pattern Zuboff tracks across the whole book ([[Surveillance capitalism founds itself by declaration a conquest pattern that fabricates legitimacy from thin air]]), here turned against democratic life itself. The result is corrosion rather than confrontation: "It does not confront democracy but rather erodes it from within, eating away at the human capabilities and self-understanding required to sustain a democratic life." The threat is not to the *institutions* of democracy in the first instance but to the human capacities — autonomy, the will, the standing to author one's own future ([[The Right to the Future Tense]], [[The Will to Will]]) — that a self-governing people requires. (`Directly stated`)

The second half of the argument supplies the mechanism by which this corrosion advances: a void. Zuboff documents an empirical collapse of social trust in the United States across exactly the decades surveillance capitalism flourished — interpersonal trust falling from roughly 46 percent (1972) to 30 percent (1995–2014), and trust in government from over 75 percent (1958) to 18 percent (2017). "Social trust is highly correlated with peaceful collective decision making and civic engagement. In its absence, the authority of shared values and mutual obligations slips away. The void that remains is a loud signal of societal vulnerability. Confusion, uncertainty, and distrust enable power to fill the social void. Indeed, they welcome it." Into precisely this vacancy instrumentarian power moves: "In the age of surveillance capitalism it is instrumentarian power that fills the void, substituting machines for social relations, which amounts to the substitution of certainty for society." Where human beings once produced order through reciprocity, obligation, and the friction of shared deliberation, the certainty machines of [[Big Other]] promise "guaranteed outcomes" — order without society, prediction in place of trust. (`Directly stated`)

The decisive point is that this power does not *heal* the instability it exploits. Zuboff is explicit that the offer instrumentarianism makes to institutions — "omniscience, control, and certainty" — is parasitic on social breakdown, not a cure for it: "The idea here is not to heal instability—the corrosion of social trust and its broken bonds of reciprocity, dangerous extremes of inequality, regimes of exclusion—but rather to exploit the vulnerabilities produced by these conditions." This is what makes the substitution of certainty for society an erosion *from within*: the conditions that make a people ungovernable by trust become the very conditions that invite their governance by machine. The deeper the distrust, the more attractive the certainty machine; and the more society is replaced by certainty, the further the bonds of reciprocity that might rebuild trust decay. This is the [[A Utopia of Certainty]] arriving not as a coup against democratic institutions ([[Surveillance capitalism is a coup from above an overthrow of popular sovereignty]]) but as their slow hollowing. (`Strongly implied`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Tyranny is recognized by terror, murder, the suspension of institutions, massacre, expulsion — instrumentarian power uses none of these; it "operate[s] remotely and move[s] in stealth."
2. It grows instead "through declaration, self-authorization, rhetorical misdirection, euphemism, and the quiet, audacious backstage moves specifically crafted to elude awareness."
3. Therefore it "does not confront democracy but rather erodes it from within," eating away at the human capabilities and self-understanding a democratic life requires.
4. Independently, social trust — the substance of those capabilities at the collective level — has empirically collapsed across the decades surveillance capitalism flourished, leaving a "social void."
5. Confusion, uncertainty, and distrust "enable power to fill the social void" — the void invites occupation.
6. Instrumentarian power fills that void by "substituting machines for social relations, which amounts to the substitution of certainty for society."
7. Crucially, it does not heal the instability but exploits it; so the substitution deepens the very corrosion that summoned it — democracy is hollowed from within rather than overthrown from without.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The trust-collapse data is correlational, and Zuboff treats it as such ("flourished during the same period"); she does not establish that surveillance capitalism *caused* the decline rather than merely coinciding with it — the causal arrow between neoliberal disciplines, connectivity, and distrust is asserted as affinity, not proven.
- "Substituting certainty for society" frames machine-mediated coordination as inherently a loss; a reader who regards algorithmic prediction as a genuine public good (the position Zuboff caricatures as "a thin utopian gruel") could grant the mechanism while denying that what is replaced — friction-laden, low-trust social relations — was worth preserving.
- Locating the harm in eroded "human capabilities and self-understanding" rests on a normative account of what a democratic life requires (autonomy, the will to will); a determinist or behaviorist who rejects free will as foundational ([[Free Will vs Determinism]]) may accept that institutions persist while disputing that anything *rightful* is being eaten away.
- The argument concedes its own ambiguity about the state: instrumentarian power is described as both summoned *by* a void and as actively *deepening* it — whether market or weakened public institutions are the prime mover is left as "a crucial frontier for study and democratic debate," not resolved.

- [[Instrumentarianism is a new species of power not a form of totalitarianism]] — the foundational distinction this argument applies to *democracy specifically*: because instrumentarianism is not totalitarianism, it does not need to suspend institutions, which is exactly why it can erode them from within.
- [[Surveillance capitalism is impelled to lawlessness and must keep democracy at bay]] — the companion claim about surveillance capitalism's *economic* antagonism to democratic oversight; this argument is its *political-anthropological* form, locating the antagonism in eroded human capacities and substituted social relations.
- [[Euphemism and secrecy are structural to surveillance capitalism not incidental]] — supplies the operational means ("euphemism," "stealth," "backstage moves") by which erosion-from-within proceeds without confrontation.
- [[There is no freedom without uncertainty]] — the inverse normative claim: if freedom requires uncertainty, then "the substitution of certainty for society" is by that measure the substitution of unfreedom for freedom.
- [[Surveillance capitalism is a coup from above an overthrow of popular sovereignty]] — the contrasting register: a coup is a seizure *of* democratic power; this argument names the slower hollowing of the human substrate *beneath* it.

## Tensions

- [[Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism]] — the argument turns entirely on this opposition: democracy is eroded rather than confronted precisely because the power at work is instrumentarian, not totalitarian.
- [[Certainty vs Uncertainty]] — "the substitution of certainty for society" is the keystone instance of this tension; certainty here is not a good but the medium of democratic corrosion.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — control is exercised by occupying the social void and reorganizing collective life around guaranteed outcomes, not by overt repression of a free citizenry.
