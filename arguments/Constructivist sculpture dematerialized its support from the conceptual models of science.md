# Constructivist sculpture dematerialized its support from the conceptual models of science

## Claim

The Constructivists (Gabo, Pevsner) released sculptural mass from gravity by nearly invisible means — fragile pedestals, the thinnest wooden struts, and tension wires — not chiefly in imitation of heavier-than-air flight but because of "the conceptual models of science," whose form-world had reshaped the sculptural sensibility itself.

## Expanded Explanation

For Burnham this is a clean instance of his master thesis: the decisive cause of a formal transformation in modern sculpture is not another art-historical precedent and not even the most obvious contemporary technology, but the form-world of science. He poses the question directly — what motivated the Constructivists to support masses by nearly invisible means? — and rules in the most tempting explanation only to subordinate it. Recent successes with heavier-than-air flight "might have been influential," but the more important cause was the conceptual models of science. The Constructivists were not imitating airplanes; they were thinking in a vocabulary — tension and compression, structural members, fields and forces, self-contained systems — that science had made available.

The chapter shows this concretely. During the "heroic" period of the Russian Revolution the Constructivists "had visions of revising art according to the technological and scientific landscape," and their bases bore a relation less to abutments and foundations than "to dirigible moorings." Gabo's later constructions become "creations of pure imagination (though ruled by a host of subconscious images culled from the realm of engineering and science)," shedding all biotic orientation — no up or down, no front, back or sides. The dematerialization of the support is thus not an aesthetic whim but the migration of a scientific form-world into sculpture.

This argument grounds the broader narrative of [[The Vanishing Base]]: the secularization and withering of the base, the move from earthbound mass to gravity-defying construction, is propelled by science's conceptual models displacing the older anthropomorphic, architectural, and animistic functions of [[The Sculpture Base]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. The Constructivists supported masses by nearly invisible means (fragile pedestals, thin struts, tension wires).
2. A candidate explanation — the contemporary excitement over heavier-than-air flight — might have been influential.
3. But the more important cause was "the conceptual models of science": tension/compression, fields, structural members, self-contained systems.
4. Therefore the dematerialization of the sculptural support is driven by science's form-world reshaping the sculptural sensibility, not primarily by mimicry of a single technology.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The flight explanation is not denied but ranked second; Burnham concedes heavier-than-air flight "might have been influential," so the claim is one of relative weight, not exclusion.
- "The conceptual models of science" is asserted rather than argued in detail at this point; the chapter offers illustrative cases (the dirigible-mooring analogy, engineering models) rather than a documented chain from a specific scientific theory to a specific sculptural form.
- The Vkhutemas constructions were teaching models "intended to teach students the properties of materials for engineering purposes," "not for pure sculptural experimentation," so their evidentiary value for an aesthetic thesis is partly inferential.
