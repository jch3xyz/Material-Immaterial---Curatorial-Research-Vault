# Digital and shamanic plant technology are parallel technologies of consciousness

## Claim

Digital/telematic technology (virtual reality, the Net) and the ancient "plant technology" of shamanism (ayahuasca, the Santo Daime ritual) are parallel technologies of consciousness, both yielding a [[Double Consciousness|double consciousness]] — a shared phenomenology of moving, at one and the same time, between two distinct fields of experience.

## Expanded Explanation

In "Weaving the Shamantic Web" (1998), Ascott structures his late, technoetic argument around a sustained parallelism between two poles. At one pole stands "an extremely powerful computer" — the immersive virtual reality of advanced computer systems; at the other, "a particularly potent plant," ayahuasca, "the 'vine of the soul.'" Crucially, the argument is grounded not in theory but in **first-person fieldwork at both poles**: Ascott reports immersion in VR labs in Seattle (the Human Interface Technology Laboratory) and Chapel Hill (the University of North Carolina Virtual Reality Laboratory), and initiation into the shamanic reality of the Kuikuru *pagés* of the Xingu River Region of the Mato Grosso and the Santo Daime community in Brasília.

From this parallel he derives a shared phenomenology. The shaman in trance is "both in the everyday world and at the same time navigating the outermost limits of other worlds"; the VR user, "aided by computer technology," moves "effortlessly through the infinities of cyberspace, while at the same time accommodating ourselves within the structures of the material world." Ascott names this simultaneous access to two fields of experience **double consciousness**, its perceptual correlate **the double gaze** ("seeing at once both inward realities and the outward surfaces of the world"), and the shamanic, ayahuasca-enhanced analogue of technologically aided [[Cyberception|cyberception]] **psi-perception**. The two technologies are thus instruments of one and the same emergent faculty.

The conclusion folds the parallel into Ascott's signature coinage: through bio-telematic art "we are weaving what I would call a 'shamantic' web, combining the sense of shamanic and semantic." See [[The Shamantic Web]]. The argument is the consciousness-side hinge of his [[Technoetics|technoetic]] program — "technology plus mind" — and it is the experiential ground on which his later claim that [[Material vs Immaterial|the immaterial and material lose their categorical distinction]] rests.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The shamanic "trance" is a state of double consciousness: simultaneous presence in the everyday world and navigation of "other worlds," accessed via "plant technology" (premise, from anthropology and fieldwork).
2. The post-biological VR user mirrors this exactly: aided by computer technology, moving through cyberspace while accommodating the material world (premise, from VR fieldwork).
3. First-person research at both poles — VR labs and the ayahuasca/Santo Daime ritual — reveals the same phenomenology of moving between two fields of experience (evidence).
4. Therefore the two are parallel technologies of consciousness, and technology is integral to double consciousness, the double gaze, and cyberception alike (conclusion); the shamanic analogue of cyberception is "psi-perception."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- First-person VR immersion at the Human Interface Technology Laboratory (Seattle) and the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill Virtual Reality Laboratory — the "computer" pole.
- First-person initiation with the Kuikuru *pagés* (shamans) of the Xingu River Region of the Mato Grosso, and into the Santo Daime ritual community in Brasília, drinking ayahuasca — the "plant technology" pole.
- The classical-anthropological account of the shamanic trance (the shaman both in the everyday world and navigating other worlds).
- The post-biological account of the VR user moving through cyberspace while remaining in the material world.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The parallel is an **analogy of phenomenology, not of mechanism** — a psychoactive alkaloid and a head-mounted display produce double consciousness by wholly different means; the argument asserts a shared structure of experience, not a shared cause.
- "Psi-perception" imports parapsychological vocabulary (psi) that reductionist science would reject; Ascott concedes that science treats consciousness as "the most intractable of problems," and the spiritual register (the Sufi injunction, "psychic instrumentality") is one "neither reductionist science nor the postmodern aesthetic could possibly countenance."
- The evidence is anecdotal and autobiographical (one researcher's reported states), not controlled or replicable.
