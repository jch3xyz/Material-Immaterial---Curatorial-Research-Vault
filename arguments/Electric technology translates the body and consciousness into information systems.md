# Electric technology translates the body and consciousness into information systems

## Claim

By putting our physical bodies inside our own outered nervous systems, electric technology translates all prior bodily extensions—and ultimately consciousness itself—into information systems, with the projected limit of transferring consciousness to the computer and fusing the globe into a single consciousness.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan's sixth chapter recasts every technology as a *translator*: media "translate experience into new forms," and "all media are active metaphors in their power to translate experience." Within this framework he stages the decisive break of the electric age. Earlier technologies extended discrete organs—"hands and feet and teeth and bodily heat-controls"—but with electric media the human "puts our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems," wearing "its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide." Once the central nervous system itself is outered, the relationship inverts: instead of the body extending into tools, the body is enclosed by an extension, and "all such extensions of our bodies, including cities—will be translated into information systems." (`Directly stated`)

The argument's force lies in a difference of *kind*, not degree. McLuhan insists that "previous technologies were partial and fragmentary, and the electric is / total and inclusive." Mechanical extension specialized and fragmented; electric extension is total, so the translation it performs is not of one organ but of the whole human situation—body, city, economy ("all forms of wealth result from the movement of information")—into circulating information. This is the chapter's pivot from the merely instrumental to the ontological: when the nervous system is the thing extended, *information* becomes the universal solvent into which solid goods, labour, and bodies are resolved. (`Directly stated`)

From this McLuhan extrapolates the limit case. Having "extended or translated our central nervous system into the electromagnetic technology, it is but a further stage to transfer our consciousness to the computer world as well." The chapter closes by imagining "our entire lives" translated "into the spiritual form of information," which would "make of the entire globe, and of the human family, a single consciousness." McLuhan phrases this as a question, but the trajectory—nervous system outered, then consciousness transferred, then the species unified as one mind—is asserted as the logical next stage; hence the final single-consciousness claim is `Strongly implied` at high confidence. This is the keystone bridge from McLuhan's 1964 media theory to the later vault cluster on [[Mind Uploading]], [[Patternism]], and [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]]. (`Strongly implied`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. All media are translators: technology "spells out" and translates experience into new forms (premise).
2. Electric media extend the central nervous system itself, so the body is now *inside* its extension rather than extended by it.
3. Once the nervous system is outered, everything previously extended—organs, cities, the economy—is re-described as, and translated into, information.
4. Unlike mechanical technologies, which were "partial and fragmentary," the electric is "total and inclusive," so the translation is complete rather than local.
5. Therefore the next stage is to transfer consciousness itself "to the computer world," and the terminal stage is the globe fused into "a single consciousness" (conclusion / projected limit).

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The puzzle of neurotic children losing traits by telephone and stutterers losing the stutter in a foreign language—media translating one mode of knowing into another—raw file [[raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/09 Chapter 6 - Media as Translators]].
- The economic restatement: under electric technology "all solid goods can be summoned to appear as solid commodities by means of information circuits," "all forms of employment become 'paid learning,'" and "all forms of wealth result from the movement of information."
- The physiological image of the organism that "now wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide," demanding "servo-mechanistic fidelity."
- The programmability argument: "it is now possible to program ratios among the senses that approach the condition of consciousness," licensing the further step of transferring consciousness to the computer.
- Literary/figurative warrant: Shakespeare's Forest of Arden as "an advance model of the age of automation" and Mallarmé's "the world exists to end in a book," capped by transferring "the entire show to the memory of a computer."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **It is rhetorical, not demonstrated.** The transfer of consciousness to the computer and the global "single consciousness" are offered as extrapolation and as a closing question ("might not our current translation... seem to make of the entire globe... a single consciousness?"), not as established fact. McLuhan asserts a tendency, not a mechanism.
- **"Translation into information" may be a category mistake.** Hayles' counter-argument [[Disembodiment is impossible there is only different embodiment]] holds that information never actually loses its material substrate; calling a body "translated into an information system" can elide the embodiment that persists (see [[Embodiment vs Information]], [[Material vs Immaterial]]).
- **It conflates extension with replacement.** That the nervous system is *extended* electronically does not entail that consciousness can be *transferred*; the inference from outering to uploading is exactly what the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] and the [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]] put in question.
- **Numbness/Narcissus risk.** McLuhan himself warns that extensions "numb" us (the Narcissus illusions), so the very docility he describes may foreclose the critical awareness his projection assumes.
