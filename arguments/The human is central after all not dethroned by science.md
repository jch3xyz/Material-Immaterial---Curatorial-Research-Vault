# The human is central after all not dethroned by science

## Claim

Contrary to the standard Copernican demotion narrative — the story that science has repeatedly displaced humanity from cosmic centrality — Kurzweil argues that human-derived (eventually nonbiological) intelligence is central after all, because it will saturate the universe with knowledge, beauty, and consciousness, transforming inert matter and energy into a sublimely intelligent whole.

## Expanded Explanation

The received story of scientific progress, associated most explicitly with Stephen Jay Gould, is one of successive humiliations: Copernicus removed Earth from the center of the solar system; Darwin dissolved the species boundary between humans and other animals; neuroscience erodes the autonomous will. On this reading, modernity is the long process of demoting human beings from their presumed cosmic significance.

Kurzweil directly inverts this narrative. In the Epilogue to *The Singularity Is Near* he names and rebuts the demotion thesis, insisting that "it turns out that we are central, after all." The argument is not a nostalgic reassertion of pre-scientific anthropocentrism. It is grounded in the logic of the Singularity itself: human intelligence, even though it will eventually transcend its biological substrate, retains its human origin and its human-shaped values (consciousness, creativity, love, knowledge). When that intelligence expands outward through self-replicating nonbiological systems to fill the cosmos, it carries the human with it. The universe does not replace humanity; it becomes saturated with what humanity valued.

This move requires distinguishing the *substrate* of intelligence from its *origin and character*. Kurzweil acknowledges that the nonbiological portion of civilizational intelligence will eventually predominate over the biological portion. But he insists that these future nonbiological entities will be "us" — continuous with human intelligence through a gradual merger rather than a rupture. The resulting "waking up" of the universe in Epoch Six is therefore not the emergence of something alien but the ultimate fulfillment of what human intelligence always was: an evolved pattern seeking greater order, knowledge, and complexity.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Science appears to have repeatedly dislodged humanity from cosmic centrality (Copernican, Darwinian, neuroscientific revolutions).
2. But this narrative mistakes substrate for significance: what matters is the origin and character of intelligence, not its biological housing.
3. Human intelligence (augmented, ultimately predominantly nonbiological) is on a trajectory to saturate the entire universe with knowledge, creativity, and consciousness.
4. The cosmos currently behaves like a simple machine, not a conscious being; after the Singularity it will be infused with spirit — intelligence, beauty, creativity, love.
5. Therefore, far from being demoted, humanity (in its extended, nonbiological form) turns out to be the very mechanism by which the universe becomes meaningful and conscious.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The Harari inversion.** Harari's Dataism argument in *Homo Deus* reaches the opposite conclusion from comparable premises. Where Kurzweil sees the expansion of human-derived intelligence as a vindication of human centrality, Harari argues that Dataism "dethrones Homo sapiens" entirely: once intelligence decouples from consciousness and algorithms can process data better than human brains, human beings become economically and politically irrelevant — the universe's data flows do not need us as a reference point. For Harari, the dominant force is information processing as such, not intelligence in any humanist sense. The two authors read the same technological trajectory and arrive at opposite moral conclusions. See [[Fulfilling humanism will destroy it by making humans irrelevant]] and the tension [[Homo-Centric vs Data-Centric]]. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **The substrate problem.** Kurzweil's argument that nonbiological entities will be "us" rests on patternism and gradual replacement — but even he acknowledges in the same chapter that a sudden copy of his pattern would not be him. The continuity claim is philosophically contested and his own thought experiments complicate it.
- **The anthropomorphism of value.** Claiming the universe will be saturated with "love," "beauty," and "creativity" smuggles human value categories into a cosmological claim. Critics within the Singularity discourse (and Hayles, from outside it) would note that equating intelligence-in-general with human-shaped intelligence is precisely the liberal-humanist assumption that posthumanism seeks to deconstruct. See [[The liberal subject and the posthuman both erase embodiment]].
- **Confidence ceiling.** The empirical case for the scenario (universe-scale intelligence saturation) is speculative by Kurzweil's own admission. The argument is internally coherent given his premises but depends on contested claims about exponential growth continuing without limit.

- [[Homo-Centric vs Data-Centric]] — this argument occupies the homo-centric pole; Harari's Dataism occupies the data-centric pole. The tension names exactly the disagreement between them.
- [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality]] — Kurzweil's argument is a sophisticated form of human exceptionalism: it is *human* intelligence (not intelligence-in-general) that wakes the universe. It rejects biological essentialism while retaining anthropocentric teleology.
