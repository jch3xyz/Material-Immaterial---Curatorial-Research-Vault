# To perceive an extension of ourselves is necessarily to embrace it

## Claim

To behold, use, or perceive any extension of ourselves is necessarily to embrace it: there is no opting out of the new sense ratio a medium evokes, because to take in the extension is to accept it into our personal system and undergo the closure of perception that follows automatically.

## Expanded Explanation

In Chapter 4 McLuhan fuses two old sources — the 113th Psalm and Blake's *Jerusalem* — into a single law of perception. The Psalmist describes idols of silver and gold made by men's hands, and concludes that "They that make them shall be like unto them; Yea, every one that trusteth in them." McLuhan reads this as "a simple fact of sense 'closure'": the beholding of idols, or the use of technology, conforms men to them. Blake develops the same intuition into "an entire theory of communication and social change," announcing in *Jerusalem* that "If Perceptive Organs vary, Objects of Perception seem to vary: / If Perceptive Organs close, their Objects seem to close also." The idol and the Narcissus image are, for McLuhan, the same figure: an extension of ourselves that we mistake for an other and so embrace without recognizing it as our own.

The decisive move is that perception is not optional or detached. To take in an extension is already to take it on. "There is, for example, no way of refusing to comply with the new sense ratios or sense 'closure' evoked by the TV image." The very act of beholding, listening, or reading admits the medium into the body's sensory economy and triggers a redistribution of the senses that "follows automatically." One cannot sample a medium from the outside; using it at all means serving it.

This embrace is what installs us in the role of Narcissus — not self-love, but [[Narcosis]], a "subliminal awareness and numbness in relation to these images of ourselves." Because [[Autoamputation|self-amputation forbids self-recognition]], the continuous, daily use of our own technology numbs us to the fact that what we behold is ourselves extended. The argument thus grounds [[Media as Extensions of Man]] in a physiology of perception: every extension reshapes the [[Sense Ratios|sense ratios]], and there is no neutral vantage outside that reshaping from which merely to look.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Any invention or technology is an extension (a self-amputation) of our physical bodies, and such extension demands new ratios or new equilibriums among the other organs and extensions of the body.
2. As an extension and expediter of the sense life, any medium at once affects the entire field of the senses — so a medium cannot be perceived without already altering the perceiver's sense ratio.
3. The Psalmist's idol and the Greek Narcissus name the same fact of "closure": beholding or using a thing conforms the user to it ("They that make them shall be like unto them").
4. Therefore to behold, use, or perceive any extension of ourselves is necessarily to embrace it — to accept it into our personal system and undergo the closure or displacement of perception that follows automatically.
5. This continuous embrace is what puts us in the Narcissus role of subliminal awareness and numbness; to use the extensions at all, we serve them "as gods or minor religions."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim collapses *perceiving* into *endorsing*: it leaves little room for critical, ironic, or resistant uses of a medium, treating compliance with the sense ratio as total and automatic. (McLuhan would answer that the resistance he denies is sensory-perceptual closure, not conscious judgment — but the argument as stated forecloses the "detached observer" stance, which is the matter of [[Detachment vs Participation]].)
- It rests on the contested physiological premise (Hans Selye, Adolphe Jonas) that all extensions are "autoamputations" maintaining equilibrium; the law of perception inherits whatever is doubtful in that theory of disease.
- The reading of Narcissus as numbness rather than self-love, and of the Psalmist's idols as a "fact of sense 'closure,'" is McLuhan's interpretive recasting of those sources, not their plain sense.
