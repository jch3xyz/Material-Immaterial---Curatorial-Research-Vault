# Naomi Klein's shock model misreads neoliberal power

## Claim

Naomi Klein's shock model describes a real technology of power, but because shock is essentially negative — disciplinary, biopolitical, coercive, immunological — it cannot be the engine of neoliberalism, which operates instead through a positive, seductive [[Psychopolitics]].

## Expanded Explanation

The seventh chapter of *Psychopolitics* is a polemic against Klein's *The Shock Doctrine*. Han accepts the central parallel Klein draws between her two "Doctor Shocks" — the CIA-funded Montreal psychiatrist Ewen Cameron, who used electroshock to "de-pattern" the psyche back to a "clean slate," and Milton Friedman, "the theologian of the neoliberal market," who exploits post-catastrophe trauma to "depattern" whole societies "back to a state of pure capitalism." Han grants that this names a genuine technology of power. What he denies is that it names *neoliberal* power. The chapter is the book's hinge: it clears away the wrong (negative, shock-based) model so Han can install the right one (positive, seductive, smart).

Han's argument turns on the nature of shock itself. Shock therapy "owes its efficacy to paralyzing and annihilating the contents of the psyche"; its "essential trait is negativity." Negativity belongs to the [[Digital Panopticon|disciplinary]] order — shock is "a genuinely disciplinary technique," one of the "biopolitical measures of coercion," "orthopaedic in nature." Cameron's hospital is literally "a panopticon far crueller than Bentham's." Cameron belongs to the [[Immunological Age]]: his practice is defensive negativity directed "against the Other, or the Enemy," structured by a Manichaean opposition of Good and Evil. None of this describes neoliberalism, which has no enemy to repel and nothing to paralyze.

Against this negative, immunological, disciplinary apparatus Han poses his thesis about actual neoliberal power. It "is dominated by positivity." It does not threaten but stimulates; it "enlists Liking" rather than administering "bitter medicine"; it "flatters the psyche instead of shaking it"; it "seduces the soul." It is pre-emptive rather than reactive: it "protocols desires, needs and wishes instead of 'depatterning' them" and, "by means of calculated prognoses," "anticipates actions … instead of cancelling them out." This is [[Smart Power|SmartPolitics]]: it "seeks to please and fulfil, not to repress." Klein's conspiratorial, shock-centered frame "blinds her to the actual workings of neoliberal psychopolitics" — she has mistaken a disciplinary technique for the logic of neoliberalism and so cannot see the positive, voluntary, seductive structure that is its real engine.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Klein's *Shock Doctrine* parallels Cameron (electroshock "de-patterning" of the mind) and Friedman ("depatterning" of societies into "pure capitalism"); Han accepts this parallel as describing a real technology of power. *(Concede)*
2. But shock works by [[Negativity]]: it paralyzes, annihilates, empties, "wipes the psyche clean" — its "essential trait is negativity."
3. Negativity is the signature of the disciplinary, biopolitical, immunological order: shock is "a genuinely disciplinary technique," "orthopaedic," a "panopticon far crueller than Bentham's," aimed "against the Other, or the Enemy." *(Reclassify)*
4. Neoliberal power, by contrast, is dominated by [[Positivity]]: it works with "positive stimuli," "enlists Liking," "flatters," "seduces," pre-empts and protocols desire rather than repressing it.
5. Therefore a model built on shock/negativity cannot be the model of neoliberalism; Klein's frame misidentifies a disciplinary technique as neoliberalism's logic and thereby "blinds her to the actual workings of neoliberal psychopolitics." *(Conclude)*

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Cameron–Friedman parallel quoted from Klein: both dream of restoring a "natural"/"pristine"/"prelapsarian" state by deliberately inflicting "painful shocks" — only "bitter medicine" could "clear those distortions and bad patterns out of the way" (*The Shock Doctrine*, p. 60), via `[[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/07 Shock]]`.
- Cameron's panopticon: isolation chambers, sensory deprivation, drug-induced sleep, cardboard tubes on the arms to prevent self-touch — "a panopticon far crueller than Bentham's," via `[[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/07 Shock]]`.
- The functional contrast: shock "owes its efficacy to paralyzing and annihilating the contents of the psyche" (negativity) versus neoliberal power that "enlists Liking," "seduces the soul," and "seeks to please and fulfil, not to repress" (positivity), via `[[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/07 Shock]]`.
- The immunological framing: Cameron "represents a phenomenon of the [[Immunological Age]]"; shock is "the negativity of immunological defence against the Other, or the Enemy," via `[[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/07 Shock]]`.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Han concedes Klein is *partly* right — shock is "a real technology of power" — so the disagreement is over scope and classification, not over whether shock exists or works; a defender of Klein could argue neoliberal regimes empirically *do* deploy disciplinary shock (austerity, debt, crisis) alongside seductive smart power, making the two technologies complementary rather than mutually exclusive. `Interpretive synthesis`
- Han's dismissal that Klein "smacks of conspiracy" is rhetorical rather than argued; the charge of melodrama does not by itself establish that shock is non-neoliberal. `Strongly implied`
- The argument depends on Han's prior periodization (immunological/disciplinary negativity giving way to positive psychopolitics); a reader who rejects that epochal scheme need not accept that shock "belongs to disciplinary society alone." `Interpretive synthesis`
