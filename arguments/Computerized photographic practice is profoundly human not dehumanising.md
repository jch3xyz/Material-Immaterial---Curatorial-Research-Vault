# Computerized photographic practice is profoundly human not dehumanising

## Claim

The artists of digital photography use high technology to make human themes *more* vivid rather than diminished, confounding the critics who see in the electronic age only a technoculture of progressive dehumanisation, and confirming that the computerization of photographic practice is profoundly human at base.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott closes "Photography at the Interface" — his catalogue essay for the artists bringing American digital photography to France for the first time — with a direct humanist counter-claim against technophobic critique. The catalogue's artists have "closed the shutters on the picture-window view of reality" and "opened the door into data space," yet Ascott insists they remain "very grounded in the present." Their subjects are the most human possible — "family, ecology, political oppression, and cultural discontinuity" — and crucially these themes are "made more vivid rather than diminished by the high technology they have employed." The technology is not a solvent of the human but an intensifier of it.

This is the structural inversion the whole essay performs: where the technophobe assumes that computation, virtuality, and the immaterial image at the interface must drain the human warmth from a practice, Ascott argues the opposite — that the new "complexity of technological systems and diversity of media" is precisely what lets these artists deliver "images and issues of a subtle sensibility." The dehumanisation thesis is not rebutted by retreating from technology but by going further into it. The computer does not stand between the human and the image; it is the new site of co-production, collaboration, and participation that *deepens* the human stake in the work (Directly stated).

The argument is the photographic, interface-scale instance of Ascott's larger telematic humanism. It runs parallel to ch.20's account of "love in the telematic embrace" — the claim that networked, computer-mediated relation is a form of human connection and affection, not its negation — and it resonates with O'Gieblyn's `Re-enchantment`: against the disenchantment narrative that reads technology as the engine of a cold, meaning-evacuated world, Ascott reads the same technology as a vehicle for renewed human vividness. It is also a key internal counterweight to the "uncritically euphoric" charge often levelled at Ascott: here the optimism is at least argued, grounded in the specific human content of specific artists' work, not merely asserted (Interpretive synthesis).

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise: These artists have fully entered "data space" — they work with digital, computerized, immaterial images at the computer interface, having abandoned the "picture-window view of reality."
2. Premise: Their themes are emphatically human and concrete — family, ecology, political oppression, cultural discontinuity — and they remain "grounded in the present."
3. Premise: These themes are "made more vivid rather than diminished by the high technology they have employed."
4. Inference: If high technology intensifies rather than evacuates human content, then the critics who see "only a technoculture of progressive dehumanisation" in the electronic age are wrong — these artists "confound" them.
5. Conclusion: The computerization of photographic practice is "profoundly human at base."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The catalogue artists themselves: their work demonstrates that family, ecology, political oppression, and cultural discontinuity are intensified, not diminished, by digital means — see the raw passage below.
- The reconception of digital photography as co-production and participation: the viewer is "repositioned," "empowered as a manipulator of that image," admitted "into the process of construction" — the technology adds a human, collaborative layer rather than removing the human from the loop (Strongly implied as the mechanism by which the human is intensified).
- The "both/and as well as either/or" stance: Ascott refuses to set digital and analogue "in complete and total opposition," so the digital is not figured as the dehumanising replacement of an authentic analogue practice but as an extension of it.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim is asserted at the level of a curatorial endorsement, not demonstrated by independent analysis; "more vivid rather than diminished" is Ascott's verdict on artists he is championing, which is the very "uncritically euphoric" register critics fault in him. The counter-evidence (whether the same technologies can also flatten or commodify the human) is named only as the critics' position to be "confounded," never engaged on its merits (Interpretive synthesis).
- The dehumanisation thesis Ascott rebuts has its own vault interlocutors who would not be so easily confounded: Han's `Digital Panopticon` and the broader Dataism critique read the computerization of human practice as a vector of control and self-exposure, not of profound humanity. The argument's optimism is a stance within a live `Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment` dispute, not its settlement.
- "Profoundly human at base" is doing essentialist work — it presumes a stable human "base" that the technology serves, which sits uneasily beside the posthumanist and cyborg positions (Hayles, Haraway) elsewhere in the vault that would question whether there is a pre-technological human base to be vivified at all.
