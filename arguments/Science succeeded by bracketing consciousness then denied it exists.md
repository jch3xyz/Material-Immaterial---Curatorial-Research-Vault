# Science succeeded by bracketing consciousness then denied it exists

## Claim

Modern science would not have been possible without [[René Descartes|Descartes]]'s seventeenth-century division between mind and matter, which let us "speak exhaustively about nature without reference to God or ourselves"; but the move that made science succeed—setting consciousness aside because it was too difficult to study objectively—eventually hardened from a method into a metaphysics, "the conclusion that because consciousness cannot not be studied scientifically, it does not exist." The same Cartesian split underwrites both the "hard problem" of consciousness and the quantum measurement problem: two impasses where the perceiving subject, supposedly excluded at the start, refuses to be eliminated.

## Expanded Explanation

[[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] grants [[René Descartes|Descartes]] his due before turning the knife. "Despite all the flak Descartes gets for disenchanting the world, modern science would not have been possible without the division he made between mind and matter." His insistence that we could exclude our subjective minds from the physical world introduced a radical idea—"that we could speak exhaustively about nature without reference to God or ourselves." [[Thomas Nagel]] names this third-person standpoint "the view from nowhere": to describe the world empirically, we set aside *res cogitans*, the immediate way we experience the world in our minds, and limit ourselves to *res extensa*, "the objective, mathematical language of physical facts." Without these distinctions, O'Gieblyn writes, "it's difficult to imagine the hallmarks of modernity: Newtonian physics, secularism, empiricism, and the industrial revolution." This is the [[Disenchantment]] narrative told as triumph rather than loss.

The cost is the argument's hinge. Success "required sidelining the world of the mind, obscuring precisely the phenomenon by which we have traditionally defined our worth as humans." The crucial sentence states the slide from method to metaphysics in one breath: "Science put a bracket around consciousness because it was too difficult to study objectively, but this methodological avoidance eventually led to metaphysical denial, to the conclusion that because consciousness cannot not be studied scientifically, it does not exist." A bracket is a procedural convenience—*set this aside for now*. The denial is a claim about being—*there is nothing there to set aside*. O'Gieblyn's point is that the second was smuggled in under cover of the first. Subjective experience came to "seem entirely unreal," a private drama that "cannot be quantified or verified," which is exactly the condition the [[Eliminativism|eliminativists]] then read off as proof of non-existence (see [[Eliminativism]]). And the deniers remain "captive, in their own way, to these seventeenth-century assumptions": to call consciousness an illusion "is to place it outside the material world, deeming it something—much like Descartes's soul—that does not exist within time or space." The denial of the soul keeps the soul's metaphysical shape.

Chapter 7 ("Paradox") supplies the structural payoff. The cogito made first-person experience the foundation of reality, "but this foundation was from the very beginning shaky"—placing consciousness outside the physical world "made the mind seem increasingly unreal." O'Gieblyn then ties two seemingly distant problems to one root: "Both the 'hard problem' of consciousness and the inability to build machines with interiority are bound up, in complex ways, with this division that was made in the seventeenth century" (see [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]]). The disenchantment Descartes inaugurated "cast doubt not only on the mind but on the external world as well," and the most acute return of the repressed subject is the quantum [[The Measurement Problem|measurement problem]], which "suggests that the physicist changes, or perhaps conjures, the quantum world simply by observing it." The hard problem and the measurement problem are mirror images: in the first, the subject is the thing physics cannot reduce; in the second, the subject is the thing physics cannot delete. As O'Gieblyn puts it, both impasses unsettle "our most basic assumptions about the relationship between subject and object, mind and world, implying that there might be some things in the world that can be perceived only from a first-person point of view."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Descartes divided reality into *res cogitans* (subjective mind) and *res extensa* (objective, measurable matter), making it possible "to speak exhaustively about nature without reference to God or ourselves"—[[Thomas Nagel]]'s "view from nowhere."
2. This division is the precondition of modernity: "modern science would not have been possible without" it; its hallmarks—Newtonian physics, secularism, empiricism, the industrial revolution—are unimaginable without the bracketing of the subject.
3. The bracketing was *methodological*: consciousness was set aside "because it was too difficult to study objectively," not because it was judged unreal.
4. But the method silently became a metaphysics: "this methodological avoidance eventually led to metaphysical denial, to the conclusion that because consciousness cannot not be studied scientifically, it does not exist."
5. Within science, subjective experience therefore came to "seem entirely unreal," which the eliminativists read as proof of non-existence—yet to call it an illusion is to keep treating it, like Descartes's soul, as something "outside the material world," so even the denial stays Cartesian.
6. The same seventeenth-century split generates the hard problem of consciousness *and* the inability to build machines with interiority—both "bound up … with this division that was made in the seventeenth century."
7. It also generates the measurement problem, where the subject excluded at the outset reappears as an observer who "conjures" the world by looking.
8. Conclusion: science succeeded by bracketing consciousness and then denied it exists; but the perceiving subject cannot be eliminated, and its return—as hard problem and as measurement problem—marks the limit of the very method that excluded it.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The bracket-to-denial slide is an interpretive claim, not a logical entailment.** That science set consciousness aside does not strictly entail that anyone concluded it does not exist; O'Gieblyn establishes the move by pointing to the [[Eliminativism|eliminativists]] (Dennett's "Cartesian theater," [[Galen Strawson]]'s "Great Denial"), but a defender of [[Eliminativism]] would say they deny consciousness on independent grounds—introspection's unreliability, the homunculus regress—not merely because it resists measurement.
- **The deniers can return the charge.** O'Gieblyn argues the deniers "remain captive … to these seventeenth-century assumptions," but Dennett's whole point is that the "Cartesian theater" is the *error* he is dissolving; he would reject the claim that calling consciousness an illusion keeps it Cartesian, insisting instead that it is "unconscious all the way down."
- **"Bound up … in complex ways" is deliberately loose.** The Chapter 7 sentence tying the hard problem and machine interiority to the seventeenth-century division concedes its own imprecision; the genealogical link is asserted as a family resemblance, not demonstrated as a derivation.
- **The measurement-problem parallel may overreach.** Most physicists treat measurement as an interpretive puzzle, not evidence that consciousness is irreducible; O'Gieblyn herself flags that consciousness-causes-collapse "is, however, a minority view," and that many physicists hold the wave function "does not collapse at all." The "perceiving subject that cannot be eliminated" is her reading, not the consensus of physics.
- **A success this complete may not be a flaw.** One could argue that excluding what it cannot explain is simply what makes science *science*, and that the persistence of consciousness as a residue is no indictment—exactly the "philosophical leap" O'Gieblyn warns against runs in both directions.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — this argument *is* the genealogy of the tension: Descartes's *res cogitans* / *res extensa* split is the origin of the modern opposition between immaterial mind and material world, and the bracket-to-denial slide is one attempt to resolve it by abolishing the immaterial pole.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the same exclusion of the subject that denies consciousness also underwrites the fantasy that mind is disembodiable information; what gets bracketed is the embodied, first-person vantage on both sides.
