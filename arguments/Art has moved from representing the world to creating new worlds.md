# Art has moved from representing the world to creating new worlds

## Claim

For Ascott the era of mimetic, representational art is ending: art's role is shifting from *providing representations of the world* to *creating worlds and inventing alternative realities* — a process from which art itself emerges, and in which the observer becomes an active participant rather than a spectator.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott twice declares "the end of the era of mimetic art" (and, in the *Telenoia* formulation, "the end of an art in which the mimetic and symbolic were central"). The traditional function of art — to mirror, depict, or symbolise an external, already-given world — is being superseded. In its place art becomes generative: it brings worlds into being and invents alternative realities rather than picturing the existing one. This is a move from imitation to emulation, which Ascott marks in the same passage: "The attempt is no longer to imitate life but to emulate it, as part of the co-evolution with nature that is our contemporary goal."

The shift is inseparable from a shift in the observer. Where mimetic art positions the viewer outside the frame, contemplating a representation, the new art is "a process in which the observer of art becomes an active participant, a process from which art emerges." Art is no longer a finished object to be looked at but an unfolding process the participant helps constitute — it is [[Emergence]], not representation. The work has no fixed prior content to be decoded; meaning and form arise out of the encounter itself, an instance of what Ascott elsewhere calls [[Apparition]].

Ascott grounds this not in older machine metaphors but in "the new biology, artificial life, neural networks, cognitive science, and radical constructivism," whose convergence he locates precisely in "their replacement of the representational function of art." The end of mimesis is thus the entry into a "post-biological era": art as a world-making, world-emulating technology — a fulfilment of the lineage the vault tracks through [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], and Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]], where the work is an interactive system from which experience emerges rather than a depicting object. `Directly stated`.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise: The historical function of art has been mimetic — to provide representations of (and symbols for) an external, pre-existing world.
2. Premise: The metaphors now governing art come from the new biology, artificial life, neural networks, cognitive science, and radical constructivism, which converge in replacing the representational function of art.
3. Premise: Under these conditions the observer is no longer a detached spectator of a representation but an active participant from whose engagement the work emerges.
4. Conclusion: Art's role shifts from *representing* the world to *creating* worlds and inventing alternative realities — from imitation to emulation, from representation to emergence.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The twin programmatic declarations of "the end of the era of mimetic art" / "the end of an art in which the mimetic and symbolic were central," in the ARS Electronica Center Datapool text and in *Telenoia* (see Sources).
- The named intellectual sources of the new art — the new biology, artificial life, neural networks, cognitive science, radical constructivism — whose shared trait is "the replacement of the representational function of art."
- The contrast of *imitate* vs *emulate*: "The attempt is no longer to imitate life but to emulate it, as part of the co-evolution with nature that is our contemporary goal."
- The figure of the participant-from-whom-art-emerges, recasting the observer as constitutive of the work — cross-linked to [[Reciprocal Communication]] as the interactive mode the new art requires.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The thesis is asserted programmatically rather than argued case by case; Ascott proclaims the end of mimesis as a manifesto rather than demonstrating it across a body of works.
- Ascott himself concedes a countercurrent: "the recent popularity of computer simulations of the external surfaces of the world" — i.e. digital technology is in fact widely used to intensify representation, which complicates the claim that the representational era is ending.
- "Creating worlds" and "inventing alternative realities" risk being read as a more total, simulated representation rather than a genuine break with mimesis; the distinction between *emulating* life and *imitating* it bears more weight than the passage spells out.
- The claim that "art emerges" from the participant is a strong ontological wager about authorship and the locus of the artwork that not all interactive or new-media practice would accept.
