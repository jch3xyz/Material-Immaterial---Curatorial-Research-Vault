# The true goal of the kinetic revival was immateriality not machine motion

## Claim

The mid-century kinetic revival of the New Tendency was misnamed: although it carried the word "kinetic," its artists — for the most part painters — pursued not machine motion but immateriality, the "physically sensed but insubstantial," sought through fields of energy, light, and fluid traces rather than through the clattering machine.

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham distinguishes the New Tendency of 1956–1965 sharply from the mechanical kineticism it superseded. The Constructivists of the 1920s and Boccioni's Futurists had fastened motion to the visible apparatus of the first industrial revolution — the tick-tock of the clock, the meshing of gears, the watch escapement. The New Tendency abandoned that machinery. Its artists, "for the most part painters," cared "only for visual effect"; their "disregard for the objective status of works of art was complemented by a will to realize motion in new forms — not in the clattering machines and watch escapements of Boccioni, but in the fluid traces of completely ethereal phenomena." The machine, where present, was hidden, a controlling force kept out of view.

What the movement actually wanted was a cluster of insubstantial qualities. Burnham names them directly: the climate that produced the New Tendency was driven by "a common quest for the physically sensed but insubstantial," a quest that "embraced infinite spatial extension, immateriality, control through invisible forces, and the use in art of the temporal dimension." This places Burnham's 1968 account as a precursor framing of what Lucy Lippard would, in the same years, call [[Dematerialization]] — the move of the art object away from material thingness toward field, idea, and event. The kinetic revival is, on Burnham's reading, one branch of the same dematerializing pressure that ran through formalist and Constructivist sculpture and would issue into conceptual art.

The evidence Burnham assembles is consistent. "Immateriality" was Yves Klein's "rallying cry," and "immateriality, manifested in barely revealed signs, became the standard of the New Tendency." Lucio Fontana's Spazialismo ("White Manifesto," 1946) called for "luminous and malleable substances" and a "tetradimensional art." Jesús-Rafael Soto's interference patterns "attest to the constant recurrence of immateriality in modern art." And the repetitive raster or field structure — the movement's unifying compositional device — "became a symbol of infinity" and "the symbol of an artistic yearning for immateriality." Motion, the ostensible subject, turns out to be a vehicle: the field is what mattered, because "in theory the field structure could be extended infinitely."

## Reasoning Structure

1. The New Tendency artists were mostly painters whose concern was "only for visual effect," not the construction of working machines.
2. They explicitly rejected the visible machine — "the clattering machines and watch escapements of Boccioni" — in favor of "the fluid traces of completely ethereal phenomena."
3. The climate that produced the movement was a "common quest for the physically sensed but insubstantial," embracing infinite extension, immateriality, invisible forces, and the temporal dimension.
4. The movement's central figures and its central device all instantiate this quest: Klein's immateriality as "rallying cry," Fontana's Spazialismo, Soto's recurrence of immateriality, the boundless raster as "symbol of infinity" and "symbol of an artistic yearning for immateriality."
5. Therefore the true goal of the kinetic revival was immateriality, not machine motion; "kinetic" names its means, immateriality its end — a precursor of Lippard's dematerialization.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham concedes the immateriality was never fully achieved: Klein's "immaterial sensitivity" was "not fully realized in his art," and the raster realized immateriality "only on the most provisional and iconic terms." The goal is asserted as the movement's aim, not its accomplishment.
- The machine is not wholly absent — Burnham notes that "if not obviously, machines were to remain the controlling forces"; immateriality is the aim, but mechanical means often persisted underneath, hidden from view.
- A faction of the New Tendency (the Groupe de Recherche d'Art Visuel, Group N) stressed "experimental objectivity, anonymity, perceptual psychology" rather than the "poetry, idealism, immateriality, luminosity" of Group Zero and NUL — so "immateriality" characterizes one wing more than the whole movement, even if Burnham treats it as the underlying quest of all.
- The reading that this is a "precursor framing of Lippard's dematerialization" is an interpretive synthesis across the two authors; Burnham does not name Lippard's term.
