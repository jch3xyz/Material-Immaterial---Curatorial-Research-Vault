# Spengler mistook a media effect for a metaphysical fate

## Claim

Spengler read the rise of non-Euclidean geometry and the dissolution of number into "functions" as the end of Western man and grounded that fate in a metaphysical "soul stuff," when it was in fact a media effect — the phonetic alphabet's invention of Euclidean visual space giving way, in the electric age, to the extension of the central nervous system.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan reads *The Decline of the West* as a case study in misdiagnosis. Spengler's apocalypse "originated in large part from his concern with the new mathematics": "Non-Euclidean geometries, on one hand, and the rise of Functions in number theory, on the other, seemed to Spengler to spell the end of Western man." Where Spengler saw a metaphysical destiny — a "special tremor in the soul stuff that embosomed the Greek world" — McLuhan locates a cause in the form of media. Euclidean space is not a feature of the Western soul but "a direct result of the action of the phonetic alphabet on the human senses," just as number "is an extension of the physical body of man, an extension of our sense of touch." The "infinity of functional processes" that Spengler watched traditional number and geometry dissolving into is simply "the extension of our central nervous system in electrical technologies."

Because Spengler "had not grasped the fact" of the media-cause, he mistook a perceptual reorganization for a cosmic verdict. McLuhan reframes this as a recurring pattern, not a singular tragedy: "This is a striking instance of how easily men of any one particular culture will panic when some familiar pattern or landmark gets smudged or shifted because of the indirect pressure of new media." Spengler is the type-case of the would-be irrationalist who, having "derived from radio a subconscious mandate to announce the end of all 'rational' or visual values," is "quite innocent of any understanding of the medium that prompts the song they sing."

The deepest point is McLuhan's redefinition of rationality itself. Spengler treated the loss of visual, numerical magnitude as the loss of reason. But "it never occurred to him that the ratio among corporeal things could never be less than rational. That is to say, rationality or consciousness is itself a ratio or proportion among the sensuous components of experience, and is not something added to such sense experience." If reason is a *ratio* among the senses, then a new medium that re-weights the senses does not abolish reason; it reorganizes it. "The intensification of one sense by a new medium can hypnotize an entire community" — which is exactly what happened to Spengler, hypnotized by the electric dethroning of the visual into pronouncing "the demise of the West."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Spengler observed a real change: number was dissolving into functions and Euclidean geometry into non-Euclidean relations, and he experienced this as the end of Western man.
2. He attributed the change to a metaphysical source — a "tremor in the soul stuff" of the Greek/Apollonian world — rather than to a technological one.
3. But Euclidean space is itself "a direct result of the action of the phonetic alphabet on the human senses," and number is "an extension of our sense of touch": both are media effects, not soul-deep destinies.
4. The "infinity of functional processes" he feared is the electric extension of the central nervous system — a further media effect, the implosive successor to the alphabet's explosion.
5. Rationality is "a ratio or proportion among the sensuous components of experience," so a medium that alters sense ratios reorganizes reason rather than ending it; a single sense intensified by a new medium "can hypnotize an entire community."
6. Therefore Spengler panicked at a smudged perceptual pattern and named it metaphysical fate — a media effect mistaken for a cosmic verdict. "Had Spengler taken the time to discover the origins of both number and Euclidean space in the psychological effects of the phonetic alphabet, The Decline of the West might never have been written."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The argument is itself a media-determinist reading.** McLuhan's deflation of Spengler depends on his prior thesis that the alphabet "invented" Euclidean space and that electric media extend the nervous system; a reader who rejects that causal frame can read McLuhan as substituting one totalizing explanation (media-cause) for another (soul-stuff), not as disproving Spengler. The claim is internal to McLuhan's own system.
- **It does not refute Spengler's observation, only his explanation.** McLuhan concedes the perceptual shift Spengler saw is real ("the electric age brings number back into unity with visual and auditory experience, for good or ill"); the disagreement is over *cause*, not over whether something is happening.
- **Polemical framing.** McLuhan yokes Spengler to Hitler ("Spengler, as much as Hitler, had derived from radio a subconscious mandate") and calls the irrationalists "singing-telegram delivery boys." The rhetoric strengthens the "panic" diagnosis but is interpretive coloring, not demonstration.
- **"Soul stuff" is McLuhan's gloss.** The phrase compresses Spengler's culture-morphology into a single dismissible term; whether it is a fair summary of Spengler's metaphysics is left unargued.

## Related Arguments

- [[The medium not its content is the message]] — the parent thesis: if it is the *form* of a medium that reorganizes "human association and action," then a cultural crisis is to be read off the medium, not off the soul. Spengler's error is precisely the failure to look at the medium.
- [[Media are make-happen agents not make-aware agents]] — Spengler felt the effect of new media without becoming aware of its cause ("quite innocent of any understanding of the medium that prompts the song they sing"); he is the type-case of media working as unrecognized make-happen agents.

## Tensions

- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the periodizing axis the argument runs on: the alphabet/print "explosion" that invented visual-Euclidean space is giving way to the electric "implosion" that extends the nervous system; Spengler stood at the hinge and read the transition as terminal decline rather than as a shift of media regimes.
