# Surveillance reproduces Protestant salvation anxiety

## Claim

The disciplinary power of digital surveillance is not principally Orwellian — the fear of being watched, caught, and punished — but Weberian: because it is structurally impossible to know which behaviors are monitored or what those in power know about you, people self-censor and over-comply, reproducing exactly the compulsive industriousness that Calvinist uncertainty about salvation once generated.

## Expanded Explanation

O'Gieblyn routes a contemporary problem in predictive analytics back through Max Weber's *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*. The Calvinist could never know the status of their own salvation — the divine will was "a black box," and "only God knew the names written in the book of life." Weber's insight was that this *epistemic* condition, not any explicit command, drove behavior: "Those who cannot know whether or not they are chosen will do everything in their power to act as though they are." The uncertainty itself, not a visible threat, produced "a spirit of industriousness and self-regulation."

O'Gieblyn argues that the modern surveillance subject inhabits the same structure. Following Daniel J. Solove, she insists the dominant frame for understanding data collection has been wrong: the menace is not the Orwellian watcher "eagerly searching for signs of political dissent" but the Kafkaesque opacity of a faceless bureaucracy whose "inscrutable purposes" use your information without letting you "participate in how their information is used." The decisive feature is illegibility, not visibility — and "we are not permitted to know why we're not permitted to know."

The empirical instance is the post-Snowden self-censorship study. After Edward Snowden's disclosures, searches for terms like "Al Qaeda" and "jihad" declined and *kept* declining over a year, "despite the fact that there was very little evidence of people being prosecuted or punished." Crucially, "people were not acting out of fear: they had simply absorbed the logic of the surveillance state into their behavior, such that it seemed like a choice." This is the Weberian mechanism precisely: anxiety about an unknowable judgment converted into pre-emptive, voluntary over-compliance. The power "stems from the impossibility of deciphering what those in power know about you and which behaviors are being monitored and predicted."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Calvinist predestination made the status of one's salvation structurally unknowable — the divine will was "a black box."
2. Weber observed that this uncertainty, not any command, produced compulsive industriousness: those who cannot know whether they are chosen act "above and beyond what is required."
3. Modern surveillance reproduces the same epistemic condition: black-box predictive systems, legal/proprietary opacity, and "behavioral futures" make it impossible to know what is monitored or known about you.
4. Solove reframes the threat from Orwellian (visible watcher → fear) to Kafkaesque (inscrutable bureaucracy → uncertainty).
5. Conclusion: the disciplinary force is the *unknowability*. The post-Snowden self-censorship study is the contemporary instance — people internalize the surveillance logic "such that it seemed like a choice," over-complying to "demonstrate their innocence."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Weber's *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*: salvation anxiety as the engine of the Protestant work ethic (see [[Transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology]] for the parallel religion-to-technology routing in O'Gieblyn).
- Calvin's *Institutes*: the divine will as a black box, salvation status unknowable — O'Gieblyn's own crisis of faith as first-person evidence. Relates to [[The Black Box]] and [[Disenchantment]].
- Daniel J. Solove on *The Trial*: the real harms are "bureaucratic ones — indifference, error, abuse, frustration, and lack of transparency and accountability," not Orwellian surveillance.
- Shoshana Zuboff on "behavioral futures" and surveillance capitalism's indifference to interiority: "It does not care what you believe. It does not care how you feel." Connects to the reduction of persons to [[Pattern and Randomness]].
- The Berkeley Technology Law Review post-Snowden study: sustained decline in searches for terrorist terminology with no corresponding prosecutions — self-censorship absorbed "such that it seemed like a choice." This is the engine of the [[Digital Panopticon]] and of [[Algorithmic Determinism]].

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- O'Gieblyn notes the asymmetry is not perfect: salvation anxiety can also produce *resignation* rather than over-compliance. "Once you have reason to believe that your worst suspicions are true, it becomes pointless to fight them." Her own loss of faith is the counter-case — when the verdict feels already settled, the Weberian spurt to over-perform collapses.
- The analogy is interpretive: Weber describes a theological-economic formation, and the mapping onto data surveillance is O'Gieblyn's synthesis, even though she attributes the core observation to Weber directly.
- A purely Orwellian reading is not wholly displaced — the [[Society of Control]] and predictive policing involve real coercive consequences (e.g., COMPAS sentencing), not only diffuse anxiety; the Kafkaesque frame supplements rather than refutes the Orwellian one.
