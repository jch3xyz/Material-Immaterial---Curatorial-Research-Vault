# The artist must turn to cybernetics as the integrative science for art

## Claim

Because science as a whole is too fragmented to consult wholesale, the artist who would orient himself in the modern world must turn to cybernetics — the one science that is integrative, a coordinator of science just as art is the coordinator of experience — making it the proper scientific reference for art.

## Expanded Explanation

In "The Construction of Change" (1964), Roy Ascott argues that the artist can no longer rely on visual observation, intuitive judgement, and day-to-day experience alone to understand the world he responds to. He needs a scientific tool and reference. But science is a problem: it works "on many fronts—too many highly specialised fields" for any artist to consult them all except casually. The fragmentation of the sciences makes a wholesale turn to "science" impossible.

Ascott's solution is to identify the one branch of science that is itself a synthesis of the others. Cybernetics — Wiener's "control and communication in animal and machine" — is "essentially integrative, drawing many disparate sciences together." It ranges across territories of enquiry rather than carving out one specialism. This integrative character is what makes it the right reference for art, and Ascott states the parallel as an equation of roles: cybernetics "is a coordinator of science, as art is the coordinator of experience." Cybernetics does for the fragmented sciences what art does for fragmented experience; their methods rhyme.

The recommendation is programmatic, not merely descriptive. Cybernetics takes "a dynamic view of life, not unlike that of the artist": it studies phenomena "in so far as they do something or are part of something that is being done," seeing "the system or process before evaluating the 'thing.'" Its concepts — information, perception, translation, logic, and chance — are "singularly relevant" to art, and, "linked to technology," it is already remaking the human condition. This 1964 recommendation is the art-historical seed of [[Cybernetic Art|cybernetic art]] and, later, Ascott's [[Telematic Art|telematic art]]: the moment a science of [[Reciprocal Communication|control and communication]] is named as the integrative reference discipline for art.

## Reasoning Structure

1. To orient himself in the modern world, the artist needs more than visual observation and intuition; he needs science as a tool and reference.
2. But science as a whole is too fragmented — too many highly specialised fields — to be consulted wholesale.
3. Cybernetics is essentially integrative: it draws disparate sciences together and ranges across many fields rather than occupying one specialism.
4. Cybernetics is therefore "a coordinator of science, as art is the coordinator of experience" — its integrative function mirrors art's own.
5. Cybernetics also takes a dynamic, process-and-system view of life close to the artist's, and its concepts (information, perception, logic, chance) are directly relevant to art.
6. Therefore the artist should turn specifically to cybernetics as his scientific reference — the seed of a cybernetic (and ultimately telematic) art.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The fragmentation premise: "Science as a whole works on many fronts—too many highly specialised fields, in fact, for the artist to consult them all, except casually." (ch. 05)
- The integrative claim and the role-equation: "Cybernetics, however, is essentially integrative, drawing many disparate sciences together... It is a coordinator of science, as art is the coordinator of experience." (ch. 05)
- The method-affinity: cybernetics "takes a dynamic view of life, not unlike that of the artist," studying "the system or process before evaluating the 'thing.'" (ch. 05) — the [[Object and System|object→system]] inversion shared with [[Jack Burnham]]'s [[Systems Esthetics]].
- Relevance of cybernetic concepts: "cybernetics deals with concepts of information, perception, translation, logic, and chance that are singularly relevant to his art." (ch. 05)
- Editorial corroboration: Shanken records that Ascott "began studying the science of cybernetics and immediately recognized its congruence with his concepts of interactive art," and that "The Construction of Change" "proposed radical theories of art and education based on cybernetics." (ch. 04)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The recommendation is of its moment (1964), when cybernetics carried the prestige of a would-be master-science; the claim that one discipline can "coordinate" all of science is itself a strong, contestable systems-theoretic assumption rather than a demonstrated fact.
- Ascott concedes that "the behavioural sciences alone can hardly supply the total backbone to one's art" — the turn to cybernetics is a supplement to, not a replacement for, the artist's traditional reliance on observation, intuitive judgement, and experience.
- The art-and-technology alliance Ascott recommends was contested in his own period: Shanken notes that Art & Language "resist[ed]... the purposeful conjunction of art and technology," reading media-based cybernetic art as complicit with a "technocratic ideology of progress" (ch. 04).
- The equation "coordinator of science : coordinator of experience" is an analogy, not an argument; it asserts a structural likeness between cybernetics and art without establishing that art's coordinating function is of the same kind.

## Tensions

- [[Art vs Technics]] — the argument deliberately yokes art to a technological science; Ascott resolves the tension by making cybernetics art's reference rather than its master.
- [[Object vs System]] — turning to cybernetics means "first seeing the system or process before evaluating the 'thing,'" the shift from object to system that this argument underwrites.
