# We are not the product but the raw material of surveillance capitalism

## Claim

Under surveillance capitalism we are neither the customer nor the product but the raw material of an extraction operation: the free services are "hooks," not value exchanges, and the actual customers are the firms that buy prediction products in [[Behavioral Futures Markets|markets for future behavior]].

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff corrects two competing folk theories of the digital economy at once. The first — "you are the customer" — assumes that the search, mail, and map services we use are objects of a value exchange, a reciprocity between producer and consumer. They are not. They are, in her word, "hooks": free affordances whose function is to lure us into an extractive operation in which our personal experience is scraped and packaged. The second folk theory — the cynical correction "if it's free, then you are the product" — sounds knowing but is, Zuboff insists, "also incorrect." It still locates us inside the transaction as a saleable thing. We are not the thing sold. (`Directly stated`)

What is sold are predictions about our behavior — [[Prediction Products|prediction products]] manufactured from [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral surplus]] — and they are sold to actual customers who are not us: "the enterprises that trade in its markets for future behavior." We are, rather, the *source* of the surplus and the *object* from which raw materials are extracted and expropriated for what Zuboff calls Google's "prediction factories." The grammar of the relation is therefore not consumer-to-firm but raw-material-to-extractor. (`Directly stated`)

This reframing converts the user from a subject of value realization into the object of a [[Surveillance|surveillance]]-based extraction operation — "the means to others' ends." It is the argument that grounds the whole apparatus of [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance capitalism]]: behavioral data is the unilaterally claimed raw material, prediction is the manufactured commodity, and the market is one in which we are traded but never participate. (`Strongly implied`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. A value exchange establishes constructive producer-consumer reciprocities; surveillance capitalism's products and services do not — they are "hooks" into an extractive operation.
2. Therefore we are not its "customers." (Folk theory one refuted.)
3. Nor are we the "product": the product sold is *predictions about our behavior*, not us.
4. We are instead the sources of behavioral surplus — the objects from which raw materials are extracted and expropriated.
5. The actual customers are the enterprises that trade in markets for future behavior.
6. Conclusion: our role in the operation is that of raw material, not party to a transaction — "the means to others' ends."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The "product" framing it refutes is not wholly wrong in spirit: behavioral data *about* us is the input to the saleable commodity, so the popular slogan gestures at a real asymmetry even as it misnames our position. Zuboff's correction is precise rather than total.
- The argument is anchored in the Google-advertising case of Chapters 1 and 3; its generalization to the whole economy is a thesis Zuboff develops across the book rather than a claim settled in these chapters. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- It does not by itself establish that extraction is *involuntary* or *coercive* — that depends on her later accounts of dispossession and the asymmetries of knowledge; here the stress is on the grammar of the economic relation, not its legality.
