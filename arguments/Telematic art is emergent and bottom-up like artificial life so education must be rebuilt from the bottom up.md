# Telematic art is emergent and bottom-up like artificial life so education must be rebuilt from the bottom up

## Claim

Telematic art is emergent and bottom-up in exactly the sense Christopher Langton defines artificial life, so the classical top-down apparatus of art education is obsolete and must be reconstructed from the bottom up.

## Expanded Explanation

Ascott performs a wholesale analogy transfer. He quotes Langton's definition of artificial life — "Natural life merges out of the organized interactions of a great number of non living molecules, with no global controller responsible for the behavior of every part," and "It is this bottom-up, local determination of behavior that AL employs in its primary methodological approach" — and then asks whether this very formula does not "precisely describe the art we practice." The networked artwork has no global controller and no authoring master; "the artist or author is a complex and often widely distributed system," and meaning is "the perpetual coming into being of meaning, matter, and mind." Art on the net is therefore not made top-down by an authorial subject but emerges, like a living system, from local interactions among distributed agents (both human and artificial). This is `[[New Media Art]]` understood not as a new style but as a new ontology of creation — `[[Emergence]]` applied to authorship.

The pedagogical conclusion follows directly. If the work is bottom-up and emergent, then a hierarchical, prescriptive institution cannot host it. Ascott declares that "Traditional art education, at this point of its terminal decline, has shown itself to have nothing to offer the demands of the emerging culture," and that "Art education in the era of digital systems, if it is to exist at all, has to be reconstructed from the bottom up." His proposed institution, the Planetary Collegium, is itself described in the vocabulary of artificial life: it is an "emergent organism" that "cannot be planned and implemented top-down" but is "already emerging, bottom-up from the infinity of interactions within the Internet." The structure of the school must mirror the structure of the art.

The analogy also carries Ascott's commitment to `[[Embodiment]]` and an enacted, non-representational mind: he invokes Varela's "view that cognition has no ground beyond its history of embodiment, seeing the mind as an emergent and autonomous network, rather than an information-processing input-output device." This locates the argument in `[[Art as Experiential Technology]]` — art and learning as processes one inhabits and enacts, not contents one transmits or receives. It is also the immaterial, process-side pole of the `[[Object vs System]]` and `[[Material vs Immaterial]]` tensions: the "bottom-up process of creation" supersedes "finality of form."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Langton defines artificial life as bottom-up, distributed, local determination of behavior, with no global controller — life as the organization of matter rather than the matter organized.
2. Telematic / networked art has the same structure: a distributed author-system, connectivity and interactivity, meaning that emerges from a multiplicity of local interactions in electronic space.
3. Therefore the bottom-up process of creation replaces "the classical top-down imposition of ideology or aesthetic" — authorship is emergent, not sovereign.
4. An institution that teaches an emergent, bottom-up practice cannot itself be top-down and prescriptive without contradiction.
5. Traditional, hierarchical art education is therefore obsolete ("terminal decline"); art education "has to be reconstructed from the bottom up," as a non-hierarchical, emergent organism (the Planetary Collegium).

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Langton's published definition of artificial life, quoted verbatim and cited `[Langton 1989, 2]` — the load-bearing premise of the analogy. See `[[Christopher Langton]]`.
- The five "defining features of our art" intrinsic to the bottom-up process: connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, emergence — `raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/26 The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-`.
- Varela, Thompson, and Rosch's *The Embodied Mind* — the enactive, decentralised, embodied account of cognition that grounds the pedagogy in emergence rather than information transfer.
- The Planetary Collegium itself, offered as an "emergent organism" that "cannot be planned and implemented top-down" — the institutional proof of concept.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument is an **analogy transfer**, not a derivation: Langton's "bottom-up, distributed, local determination of behavior" describes molecules with no intentions, whereas networked art involves intentional, acculturated human agents. Ascott applies the AL formula "wholesale" without arguing that the disanalogy (intention, value, judgment) is harmless.
- The pedagogical conclusion ("must urgently be revised," "reconstructed from the bottom up") is normative and rhetorically declared ("terminal decline"), resting on the structural-mirroring premise that an institution must share the form of its object — a premise Ascott asserts rather than defends.
- Ascott concedes the technology is "a two-way process" (Kelly) and warns elsewhere in the chapter that the computer has been mis-treated "as a kind of magic wand," which qualifies but does not weaken the core claim.
