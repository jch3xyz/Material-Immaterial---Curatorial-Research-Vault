# Telematic art moves beyond both object art and time-based art

## Claim

In the networked condition, art is no longer a discrete object identifiable at a fixed point in space and time, nor a linear sequence of moving images unfolding in serial progression; telematic art therefore moves beyond both [[Object Art]] and time-based art. `Directly stated`

## Expanded Explanation

This is the title thesis of Ascott's 1990 essay "Beyond Time-Based Art: ESP, PDP, and PU." Ascott enumerates what networking is *no longer*: not "an art object discretely to be identified in time and space," not "a sequence of (moving) images to be experienced in linear progression," not even "a melange of space-time juxtapositions to be received as a complete, pre-ordered, and autonomously coherent entity." Each of these older forms — the object, the time-based work, the cohered space-time montage — presupposes a unified spectator standing at a determinate location and moment. Ascott's point is that the network dissolves exactly that position.

The argument turns on what happens to time when the perceiving subject is no longer a single fixed point. "Time used in such production implies a fixed point in a perceiving system, a self-standing intelligence." But in the [[Telematic Art|telematic]] condition intelligence is dispersed across the network, the perceiving system is "global in its gaze," and the parts interact "asynchronic rather than serial or simultaneous." Asynchronic, distributed, global perception cannot be organized around the one-place-one-time logic that object art and time-based art both require. Time itself "becomes something other" — what Ascott elsewhere in the chapter names schizo-time and hyper time (see [[Telematic Time]]). The work is no longer an object to behold nor a film to watch through, but a continuum of data the user moves through and transforms, "never to interact with the same set of data ... twice."

The positive content of "beyond" is [[Simulation (Ascott)|simulation]]: telematic art "uses simulation to render what is invisible visible, to bring the virtual, the potential, the unseen, and unrealised into view." This is the network/participatory continuation of the object→system thesis that [[Jack Burnham|Burnham]] built for sculpture two decades earlier (see [[Object and System]], [[Systems Esthetics]]) — and a foundational claim for [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] as fields defined by process, connectivity, and information rather than by the contemplated material object.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Object art and time-based art both presuppose a unified spectator at a fixed point in space and time (a "fixed point in a perceiving system, a self-standing intelligence").
2. In the telematic network, intelligence is dispersed, the perceiving system is global, and interactions are asynchronic rather than serial or simultaneous — there is no fixed point.
3. Networking therefore presents neither a discrete object in fixed space-time nor a linear sequence of moving images nor a pre-ordered coherent space-time entity.
4. Conclusion: telematic art moves beyond both object art and time-based art, substituting simulation that renders the invisible, virtual, and unrealised visible.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The enumeration of what networking is "no longer": object / linear sequence of moving images / pre-ordered autonomously coherent entity (raw ch. 19) — establishes the supersession of *both* the object and the time-based work.
- The dispersed-intelligence / global-gaze / asynchronic-interaction claim — supplies the *why*: the dissolution of the fixed perceiving point.
- The Heraclitus and Schrödinger's Cat figures — the user "neither precisely here nor there, actual or virtual," "never ... the same river twice" — dramatize the lost fixed point at the level of the participant.
- The closing simulation thesis — "moving beyond object art and time-based art, uses simulation to render what is invisible visible" — states the conclusion verbatim.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Ascott describes a programmatic aspiration for art "fully PDP and networked," not an empirical survey; much networked work of 1990 still circulated as documented objects and recorded sequences, so the "beyond" is normative more than descriptive. `Interpretive synthesis`
- "Beyond" need not mean abolition: the discrete object and time-based image persist as components inside networked works (the user still encounters "images, text, sound"), so the supersession is of the *organizing frame*, not of objecthood or duration as such. `Strongly implied`
- The claim leans on Ascott's reading of the new physics (parallel universes, the observer effect) as a model for art; readers who reject that mapping may grant the network premise while doubting the schizo-time conclusion. `Interpretive synthesis`
