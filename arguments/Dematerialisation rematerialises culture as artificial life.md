# Dematerialisation rematerialises culture as artificial life

## Claim

In a dialectical reversal, the dry, immaterial trajectory of digital culture does not terminate in pure information: the dematerialisation of art, telemedia, and virtual reality "leads inexorably to the re-materialisation of culture in the form of artificial life" — the future, for Ascott, "will be moist."

## Expanded Explanation

This is Ascott's most compressed statement of the move that turns the vault's [[Dematerialization]] arc back on itself. Under the entry "Immateriality" in his glossary of technoetic terms, Ascott does not define the immaterial as an endpoint but as a *passage*: the dematerialisation of the art object, of telemedia, and of [[Virtuality|virtual reality]] does not dissolve into a final disembodied informatics but "leads inexorably to the re-materialisation of culture in the form of [[Artificial Life|artificial life]]." The screen-based, immaterial world is the dry pole; its consequence, pushed far enough, is a return to "molecules and atoms, nanotechnology and neurons" — a wet, living substrate. The two passages name the same loop from opposite ends: chapter 32 names the destination (artificial life), chapter 31 names the engine ([[Visionary Pragmatism|visionary pragmatism]]) and the materials (molecules, atoms, nanotechnology, neurons).

This is the **direct extension of Lippard's dematerialization**. Where [[Dematerialization (Lippard)|Lippard]]'s conceptual artists drove the art object toward the immaterial — the idea, the document, the system — Ascott takes the immaterial *as accomplished* and asks what lies on its far side. His answer is not the persistence of pure pattern (the Kurzweil/Moravec uploading horizon) but a re-entry into matter: the dry digital, having exhausted the object, reconverges with the wet biological in a third, [[Moistmedia|moist]] domain whose proper art-form is artificial life. The dematerialisation that Lippard launched is thus not reversed but *completed* by being turned inside out — immateriality is the hinge on which culture swings back into the living.

The reversal is dialectical, not merely additive. The immaterial is a necessary stage: only by passing *through* dematerialisation (the screen, the network, the database, telepresence) does culture acquire the computational and connective apparatus that makes a designed, bottom-up, evolutive life — [[Technoetics|technoetic]], moist, fluid — conceivable at all. "The future will be moist" is therefore not a rejection of the digital but its consequence: the dry world's own logic, carried to term, re-materialises. This is why the argument belongs on the *re-materialisation* side of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension rather than the immaterialist side, and why it pairs with Ascott's companion claim that [[Networking re-materialises the body it does not dematerialise it|networking re-materialises the body]] — the body's loop and culture's loop are the same gesture at two scales.

## Reasoning Structure

1. **Premise:** Twentieth- and early-twenty-first-century culture has dematerialised — art (after Lippard), telemedia, and virtual reality have moved from the object to the immaterial (the screen, the network, information).
2. **Premise:** The immaterial is not a stable terminus; "visionary pragmatism" pushes the consequences of a screen-based, immaterial world further.
3. **Inference:** Carried to term, that immaterial trajectory reconverges with matter — "molecules and atoms, nanotechnology and neurons" — i.e. it re-materialises.
4. **Conclusion:** Dematerialisation therefore "leads inexorably to the re-materialisation of culture in the form of artificial life" — the dry loops back into the wet via biotechnology; "the future will be moist."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Asserted "inexorability."** Ascott states the loop as inevitable ("leads inexorably"), but it is a programmatic, visionary forecast in a manifesto/glossary, not a demonstrated causal mechanism. It is labelled `Directly stated` because Ascott explicitly asserts it, not because artificial-life culture is established.
- **"Re-materialisation" is doing a lot of work.** The same phenomena Lippard's lineage would still call *immaterial* (networks, information, virtuality) are here re-described as the gateway to a new materiality. Whether this is a genuine ontological reversal or a rhetorical re-signing of the immaterial under the opposite sign is open — the same ambivalence noted in [[Networking re-materialises the body it does not dematerialise it]].
- **Speculative substrate.** "Molecules and atoms, nanotechnology and neurons" and a culture re-materialised *as* [[Artificial Life|artificial life]] presume maturities of nanotechnology and synthetic biology that, at the time of writing, were largely projective.
- **Tension with the uploading horizon.** Insofar as a strand of the same milieu (Moravec, Kurzweil) reads the immaterial trajectory as ending in substrate-independent *pattern* (see [[We are not our bodies but a pattern of information that persists through material turnover]] and [[Mind Uploading]]), Ascott's re-materialisation thesis is a counter-claim, not a consensus — the immaterial does not, for them, loop back into the wet.
