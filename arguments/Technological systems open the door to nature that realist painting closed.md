# Technological systems open the door to nature that realist painting closed

## Claim

Computerised and instrumental systems are the threshold that opens onto nature as living process, whereas the faithfully realistic and naturalistic painting of Western culture was a cultural membrane that staged nature in timeless immobility and kept us separated from its processes.

## Expanded Explanation

Roy Ascott inverts the popular view of art's relationship to nature. The received wisdom holds that naturalistic painting brings us close to nature and that technology alienates us from it. Ascott argues the reverse. The "faithfully realistic and naturalistic painting of Western culture" produced what he calls a "cultural membrane" — the window, the proscenium arch within which the artist "staged" nature and "framed it in a timeless immobility." Because "nature is all process," a representational art that fixes nature into a series of theatrical sets necessarily holds us apart from what nature actually is.

The corrective is instrumentation. "Scientific inquiry, aided by advanced computing, imaging, and telemetry systems, is yielding up to us an entirely new kind of nature" — energy transformations, emergent behaviours, order in chaos, molecular life, quantum events, even the movement of consciousness across the brain — "none of which is observable directly by the human eye." The microscope (Ascott dates it to Janssen, 1590) revealed dynamic processes that artists, fixated on staging nature as theatrical sets, "failed for so long to respond to." Technological and computerised systems thus furnish "a threshold, an open doorway into the natural world."

This argument is the local hinge of Ascott's larger "Nature II" thesis: that "the logical outcome of the immateriality of the information society is the reinstatement in the twenty-first century of a natural materiality," not by returning to old metaphors of nature but by participating, through second-order cybernetics and the connectivist paradigm, in the "creative processes of nature." The reversal of painting's relation to nature is the premise that makes the seeming paradox — that the most artificial technologies bring us "back to nature" — coherent.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Nature is not a static scene but pure process ("nature is all process").
2. Naturalistic painting froze nature into a window / proscenium arch — "a timeless immobility" — and so acted as a "cultural membrane" that distanced us from those processes.
3. Instruments and computerised systems (microscope, imaging, telemetry) disclose precisely the processual nature painting cannot — emergent behaviours, molecular and quantum events, the brain's activity — much of it invisible to the unaided eye.
4. Therefore technological and computerised systems, not realist painting, are "the threshold, an open doorway into the natural world."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The five-hundred-year staging of nature by Western artists and writers — "a picture of nature quite unlike that 'staged' for our viewing" — invoked as the cultural membrane to be overturned (chapter 28).
- The instruments that disclose process: "advanced computing, imaging, and telemetry systems" yielding "evolutive, emergent behaviours, its order in chaos … molecular life, and quantum events," none "observable directly by the human eye" (chapter 28).
- The historical scandal of the microscope: Janssen's compound microscope (1590), yet artists for centuries "blindly pursued the representation of nature as a series of theatrical sets" rather than the "invisible which moves and lives beneath the gross forms" (Boccioni, quoted by Ascott, chapter 28).
- The Heisenberg / Cézanne pairing — that the apparatus (including the observer's consciousness) determines nature's "natural" behaviour — used to dissolve the opposition between artifice and nature (chapter 28).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The popular view Ascott explicitly writes against — that naturalistic painting brings us closest to nature and that technology is "the very epitome of the artificial" — is itself the strongest objection; Ascott concedes the paradox ("How is it that the most advanced technologies … could bring us back to nature?") and answers it only by redefining nature as "Nature II," a "second-order nature," rather than the "old metaphors of nature."
- The argument leans on a contestable premise — that nature is exhaustively "all process" — and treats representational painting's stillness as a defect rather than a different mode of attention.
- It is a manifesto-grade claim ("by the twenty-first century, [classical nature] will be dead"), forecasting rather than demonstrating that telematic and molecular technologies will "reinstate" a natural materiality; the instruments give access to *images* and *data* of process, which is not self-evidently the same as access to nature itself.

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott routes the argument through this tension's deep structure: the "immateriality of the information society" is the very thing that, for him, "reinstates" a "natural materiality" (Nature II), so the access-to-nature claim is a turn within the material/immaterial dialectic rather than a flight from it.
- [[Object vs System]] — the realist painting is the framed object/window that staged nature; the computerised, telemetric, connectivist apparatus is the system that opens onto nature-as-process. The argument is a direct extension of the object-to-system spine into the domain of nature.
