# Twenty-first-century architecture will change at the level of behaviour not form

## Claim

Where twentieth-century architecture served the body and its appearance, twenty-first-century "moist architecture" will embody mind: engineering will embrace ontology, system will supersede structure, seeding will replace designing, and the goal will become the building of sentience — self-organising buildings with a life of their own. (`Directly stated`)

## Expanded Explanation

In the closing sections of his 2000 "Moist Manifesto" essay, Roy Ascott extends the moistmedia thesis — the convergence of the dry digital, the wet biological, and the spiritually numinous — into architecture, which he treats as "a useful focus of visionary pragmatism." His historical diagnosis is that "in twentieth-century architecture, the body ruled": the body "loves surfaces, solidity, resistance," it "wants its world to be limitless but safely ordered," and so the century's architecture was preoccupied with appearance and shelter. The coming century reverses this: "architecture progressively will embody mind; technoetics will be at the foundation of practice." Because mind "seeks connectivity and complexity, uncertainty and chaos" and "knows reality to be layered and ambiguous, constantly collapsing and reforming," the architecture that reflects it "will be like nothing the world has ever known."

The decisive shift is from *what a building is* to *how a building behaves*. "The paradigmatic change in architecture will be registered at the level of behaviour rather than form." The twentieth century's "exaggerated interest in what a building looks like, its mere appearance, will give way to a concern with the quality of its gaze, how it sees us, how it perceives our needs." Questions of physical structure are "overshadowed by ambitions for their dynamism and intelligence, their ability to interact with one another and with us, to communicate, learn and evolve within the larger ecology." Ascott condenses this into a chain of substitutions: "Engineering will embrace ontology. Time will become more dominant than space, system more significant than structure." And the method changes with the object: "Seeding... will become at least as important as designing, and design will be a bottom-up process, always seeking short-term evolutionary gains."

The end point is "the building of sentience, a moist architecture that has a life of its own, that thinks for itself, feeds itself, takes care of itself, repairs itself, plans its future, copes with adversity." Such an architecture is "as much emotional as instrumental, as intuitive as ordered," and it is reciprocal — "we shall want to get inside the mind of such architecture and shall demand an architecture that can get into our own minds." This is Jack Burnham's [[Object and System|object→system]] transition restated in an architectural key: structure (the object) yields to system, appearance to behaviour, designed form to seeded process — the same trajectory that, in Burnham, carries sculpture out of objecthood and into the system. (`Interpretive synthesis` on the Burnham parallel; the architectural claim itself is `Directly stated`.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. The body and the mind want different things from space: the body "loves surfaces, solidity, resistance" and wants order and shelter; the mind "seeks connectivity and complexity, uncertainty and chaos."
2. Twentieth-century architecture answered to the body, hence its preoccupation with appearance, structure, and the look of the building.
3. In the moist (technoetic, post-biological) culture, architecture instead "will embody mind" — so the relevant variable becomes behaviour, not form.
4. Therefore the paradigmatic change registers at the level of behaviour: a building's gaze, responsiveness, intelligence, and capacity to "communicate, learn and evolve" displace its mere appearance.
5. Consequently the governing terms invert — engineering becomes ontology, time supersedes space, system supersedes structure, and seeding (bottom-up emergence) supersedes top-down designing.
6. Conclusion: the goal of architecture becomes the building of sentience — buildings that have "a life of their own," that self-maintain, self-repair, and self-plan within a larger self-organising planetary ecology.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim is **predictive and programmatic**, not descriptive: Ascott explicitly frames the whole essay as "a textual portal to the future... inclusive rather than conclusive," so the argument is a manifesto-projection rather than a report on built work. (`Directly stated`)
- "Behaviour not form" is a deliberate overstatement: form is not abolished but subordinated ("system more significant than structure," structure "overshadowed" — not eliminated). The opposition is rhetorical emphasis, and a critic could note that any behaving building still has a form.
- The vision presupposes technologies Ascott concedes are unrealised — "intelligent artificial life remains the great challenge" — so the building of sentience is contingent on advances (nano-engineering, artificial neural networks, genetic intervention) that the essay forecasts rather than demonstrates.
- A surveillance critique sits latent in the imagery: a building whose value is "the quality of its gaze, how it sees us" reframes the dwelling as an apparatus that watches its occupants. Ascott reads this affirmatively (responsiveness, care), but read against the vault's [[Digital Panopticon]] this "gaze" is double-edged — an instructive divergence rather than a settled good. (`Speculative connection`)

## Tensions

- [[Object vs System]] — this argument is the architectural instance of Burnham's keystone opposition: the building as object (form, appearance, structure) yields to the building as system (behaviour, dynamism, self-organisation).
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott's "re-materialisation of culture involving molecules and atoms, nanotechnology and neurons" routes the immaterial digital back into matter; the moist building is neither purely material structure nor immaterial information but a behaving synthesis of both.
