# Raw capitalism is antisocial and must be cooked by democratic institutions

## Claim

Left to itself the self-regulating market contains powerful forces of divergence that are antisocial, so capitalism must be "cooked" — tethered to society by a democratic double movement of countervailing institutions — and the neoliberal demolition of that double movement released the raw capitalism in which surveillance capitalism took root.

## Expanded Explanation

In setting the historical stage for surveillance capitalism, Shoshana Zuboff borrows two economists' diagnoses and fuses them into a single thesis about why raw markets must be domesticated. From Karl Polanyi she takes the [[The Double Movement|double movement]]: the conclusion that "the operations of a self-regulating market are profoundly destructive when allowed to run free of countervailing laws and policies," and that society spontaneously throws up "a network of measures and policies… integrated into powerful institutions designed to check the action of the market relative to labor, land, and money." The market expands; in a second, answering movement, society builds institutions that tether it. From Thomas Piketty she takes the law of accumulation, summarized as r > g — the rate of return on capital tends to exceed the rate of economic growth — "a dynamic that produces ever-more-extreme income divergence and with it a range of antidemocratic social consequences." `Directly stated`

The two converge in the chapter's signature metaphor. "A précis of Piketty's extensive research may be stated simply: capitalism should not be eaten raw. Capitalism, like sausage, is meant to be cooked by a democratic society and its institutions because raw capitalism is antisocial." Piketty's own warning supplies the literal claim under the figure: "A market economy… if left to itself… contains powerful forces of divergence, which are potentially threatening to democratic societies and to the values of social justice on which they are based." The "cooking" is exactly Polanyi's double movement — the democratic institutional work, achieved in the US through the Progressive Era, the New Deal, and the Great Society, that renders capitalism social. `Directly stated`

The argument's force is historical and political rather than merely descriptive. Zuboff's central historical claim is that this tethering was deliberately dismantled: "The double movement was scheduled for demolition under the neoliberal flag, and implementation began immediately," beginning with Carter-era deregulation and defining "the Reagan and Thatcher eras, virtually every subsequent US presidency, and much of the rest of the world." The demolition of the double movement *is* the un-cooking of capitalism — the release of raw, untethered markets — and on Zuboff's reading [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance capitalism]] is the mutant form that grew in that institutional vacuum, since "neoliberal ideology and policy also provided the habitat in which surveillance capitalism could flourish." The argument closes forward into the present: a twenty-first-century double movement adequate to *behavioral* (rather than industrial) capitalism has not yet been invented. `Strongly implied`

## Reasoning Structure

1. A self-regulating market left to itself "contains powerful forces of divergence" (Piketty, r > g) and is "profoundly destructive when allowed to run free of countervailing laws and policies" (Polanyi). Raw capitalism is therefore antisocial.
2. Historically, society answers with a *double movement* — institutions that "check the action of the market relative to labor, land, and money" and "support the market form while tethering it to society." This is the "cooking" that renders capitalism social.
3. Under neoliberalism the double movement "was scheduled for demolition," beginning in 1976; the countervailing institutions that tethered the market were dismantled.
4. Demolishing the double movement leaves capitalism *raw* — untethered, divergent, antisocial.
5. Therefore surveillance capitalism, "inconceivable outside the digital milieu," found in this de-cooked neoliberal habitat the institutional shelter it needed to root and flourish; and re-domesticating it requires a new, not-yet-invented double movement.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Polanyi's *double movement* — the spontaneous countermovement of laws, policies, and institutions ("workers' compensation, factory inspection, municipal trading, public utilities, food safety, child labor") that emerged "in every European society during the second half of the nineteenth century" ([[The Double Movement]]).
- The US history of the double movement: Progressive-Era trust busting, the New Deal and post-war Keynesian institutionalization, the Great Society's civil-rights and environmental legislation — credited by "many scholars" with the success of market democracy.
- Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* and the law r > g, plus his evidence of "political capture" — the 2015 finding that 158 US families funded almost half of presidential-campaign money in 2016.
- The neoliberal "demolition": Carter-era deregulation of airlines, transportation, and finance — a "ripple" that became "a tidal wave that washed away controls" — extended through the Reagan and Thatcher eras ([[Neoliberalism]]).
- A 2016 IMF report warning that neoliberal trends "have not delivered as expected," with inequality diminishing "the level and the durability of growth."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument concedes that capitalism *as such* is acceptable once "cooked" — it indicts raw markets, not markets per se. A stronger critique (e.g. Han's [[Dictatorship of Capital]] or [[Capital as Religion]]) would hold that no double movement can domesticate capital that has become a cult "without atonement."
- Surveillance capitalism's novelty — claiming human experience itself as free raw material — may exceed anything Polanyi's nineteenth-century double movement (built for labor, land, and money) could contain; the "cooking" metaphor risks under-describing a genuinely new logic. See [[Surveillance capitalism is a logic not a technology]].
- The "raw"/"cooked" framing treats the double movement as politically contingent and recoverable; [[Inevitabilism]] and the [[Society of Control]] suggest re-tethering may be categorically harder now than in 1900.
- The case is built largely on cited authorities (Polanyi, Piketty, the IMF) and US/UK institutional history; whether r > g and the demolition thesis generalize is asserted more than demonstrated in this chapter.
