# Abstract time was created by literacy reinforced by the clock not by the clock alone

## Claim

Abstract, uniform, divisible time is a product not of the mechanical clock by itself but of phonetic literacy reinforced by the clock — because the clock can impose its fragmenting "assembly-line" succession only where the visual stress inseparable from the alphabet has already restructured the sensorium.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan grants the clock real power. As "a machine that produces uniform seconds, minutes, and hours on an assembly-line pattern," the clock separates time "from the rhythms of human experience" and "helps to create the image of a numerically quantified and mechanically powered universe." It is one of the great agents of uniform fragmentation of the [[Sense Ratios]] in Western culture. But for McLuhan the clock is a *reinforcer* and *amplifier*, not the *cause*, of abstract time. The cause is alphabetic literacy. He states it flatly in the chapter's keystone sentence: "It was not the clock, but literacy reinforced by the clock, that created abstract time and led men to eat, not when they were hungry, but when it was 'time to eat.'" `Directly stated`

The dependency runs deeper than sequence; it is a condition of possibility. "For the clock to dominate, there has to be the prior acceptance of the visual stress that is inseparable from phonetic literacy." The clock's social triumph began "in the world of the medieval monasteries," but McLuhan insists that the monastic submission of the human organism to mechanical time was itself parasitic on literacy: "The acceptance of such fragmenting of life into minutes and hours was unthinkable, save in highly literate communities. Readiness to submit the human organism to the alien mode of mechanical time was as dependent upon literacy in the first Christian centuries as it is today." Literacy is the prior visual discipline — "an abstract asceticism that prepares the way for endless patterns of privation" — that lets time become "an enclosed or pictorial space that can be divided and subdivided," filled or kept free. `Directly stated`

This is, explicitly, a correction of Lewis Mumford. Mumford gave the clock priority — "Lewis Mumford has suggested that the clock preceded the printing press in order of influence on the mechanization of society" — but, McLuhan charges, "Mumford takes no account of the phonetic alphabet as the technology that had made possible the visual and uniform fragmentation of time. Mumford, in fact, is unaware of the alphabet as the source of Western mechanism." The dispute is not over whether the clock fragments — both agree it does — but over the *prior cause* of mechanization. The same medium-priority logic governs McLuhan's account of [[The Phonetic Alphabet]] elsewhere in the book: the alphabet, not the clock, is the root visual technology, and [[Typographic Man]] is the figure in whom that visual stress reaches mass scale, so that "the clock combined with the uniform respectability of the new typography to extend the power of social organization almost to a national scale." `Directly stated`

## Reasoning Structure

1. The clock is a genuine engine of fragmentation: it produces uniform units "on an assembly-line pattern" and separates time "from the rhythms of human experience."
2. But fragmenting time requires a prior reorganization of the senses toward the visual — the abstract, uniform, sequential bias that McLuhan attributes to the phonetic alphabet (a reordering of [[Sense Ratios]]).
3. Therefore the clock can *dominate* only given "the prior acceptance of the visual stress that is inseparable from phonetic literacy" ([[The Phonetic Alphabet]]); monastic clock-discipline was "unthinkable, save in highly literate communities."
4. Conclusion: abstract time is created by "literacy reinforced by the clock," not by the clock alone — and Mumford, who credits the clock with priority, errs because he "takes no account of the phonetic alphabet."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Mumford's position** is the standing counterargument: the clock, not print or the alphabet, is the key machine of mechanization (a thesis McLuhan reports — "the clock preceded the printing press in order of influence on the mechanization of society"). McLuhan does not refute the clock's fragmenting power; he subordinates it, which leaves Mumford's narrower claim about the clock's industrial-social effects partly intact. `Directly stated` (that McLuhan reports the view); the subordination is McLuhan's move.
- The argument rests on McLuhan's larger and contestable thesis that the phonetic alphabet uniquely produced the visual, uniform, sequential bias of the West; it inherits that thesis's burden of proof.
- McLuhan offers the monastic and "first Christian centuries" cases as illustration rather than systematic historical demonstration; the dependence of clock-discipline on literacy is asserted more than evidenced.
