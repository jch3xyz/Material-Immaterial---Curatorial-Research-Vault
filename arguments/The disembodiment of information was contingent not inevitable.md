# The disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable

## Claim

Information came to be conceptualized as a disembodied pattern—an entity separable from any material substrate—not because technology made this inevitable, but because situated scientific and cultural debates, above all at the Macy Conferences on [[Cybernetics]], made it seem the better choice; a simplification needed for engineering hardened into an ideology, and that reification was a contestable decision rather than a necessity.

## Expanded Explanation

This is the load-bearing historical thesis of *How We Became Posthuman*. Hayles is not contesting [[Information]] theory itself, nor the cellular-automata model, nor any particular technical result; she is contesting the leap by which abstract, bodiless information came to be treated as more essential than the material forms in which it is instantiated. Her method is to return to "moments before it became a black box"—the live discussions at the Macy Conferences (1943–1954)—where the reification of information was still being argued over, "alternate models were proposed," and "objections were raised." Recovering those debates "demystif[ies] the assumption that information is more essential than matter or energy."

The pivotal contest is between [[Claude Shannon]] and [[Donald MacKay]]. Shannon (with [[Norbert Wiener]]) defined information as a probability function "with no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning," so that a message would carry the same value regardless of the context in which it was embedded. MacKay, a British researcher, tried to build an information theory in which meaning—the change a message produces in the receiver's mind—was constitutive. As the information theorist Nicolas Tzannes later put it, Shannon and Wiener define information "in terms of what it is," MacKay "in terms of what it does." Defining information as what it *is* makes it a thing that can be transported across any medium unchanged; defining it as what it *does* makes it "an action," one that "necessarily implies context and embodiment." The Shannon-Wiener definition became the U.S. industry standard.

Crucially, Hayles insists this outcome was *won*, not given: "Reification was triumphant not because it had no opposition but because scientifically and culturally situated debates made it seem a better choice than the alternatives." Homeostasis won over reflexivity, and disembodied information won over embodied information, "largely because it was more manageable quantitatively"—MacKay's model foundered on the staggering problem of mathematically modeling changes in a receiver's mind. The decisive move is rhetorical and ideological, not technical: in Shannon's own engineering context, divorcing information from meaning was "an appropriate and sensible decision," but taken out of that context it licensed the fantasy of downloading a mind into a computer. "Thus, a simplification necessitated by engineering considerations becomes an ideology in which a reified concept of information is treated as if it were fully commensurate with the complexities of human thought."

## Reasoning Structure

1. To say humans are essentially informational patterns presupposes that information is a disembodied entity, separable from its material substrate (premise inherited from Moravec/Minsky-style extrapolation; see [[The Body as Data]]).
2. That presupposition was not delivered by technology; it was constructed at identifiable historical sites—pre-eminently the Macy Conferences—where information was first theorized as a quantity.
3. At those sites the definition was genuinely contested: Shannon-Wiener defined information as a context-free probability function (what information *is*); MacKay defined it through its effect on the receiver's mind (what information *does*), which keeps meaning, context, and embodiment in the picture.
4. The Shannon-Wiener definition prevailed not because it was true or forced, but because it was quantitatively tractable and because situated debates "made it seem a better choice than the alternatives"—the choice was overdetermined but not necessitated.
5. The engineering simplification (bracketing meaning for stable transmission) was then decontextualized and elevated into an ideology that treats reified information as equivalent to human thought (Moravec "decontextualizes the theory").
6. Therefore the disembodiment of information is a historically contingent result of negotiations among particular people, definitions, models, and techniques—"the result of historically specific negotiations rather than of the irresistible force of technological determinism."
7. Conclusion: because the reification was a contestable choice, it remains open to contest; "neither was it inevitable" that information lost its body, nor is it inevitable that we accept ourselves as essentially informational patterns.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Overdetermination cuts against pure contingency.** Hayles is careful: the outcome was "overdetermined" and "not an arbitrary decision." Many forces pushed the same way—the needs of emerging technologies for reliable quantification, the postwar/McCarthy-era ethos privileging objectivity, the personalities of dominant figures like McCulloch. The argument is therefore "not inevitable, but heavily weighted," which is a narrower claim than "freely chosen." An opponent could say the alternatives were never live in any practical sense.
- **The losing alternative may have lost for good reasons.** MacKay's meaning-based theory "was not widely accepted" largely because no one could mathematically model the changes a message triggers in a receiver's mind—"difficulty of quantification and loss of universality." If MacKay's program was simply unworkable with available techniques, a critic could argue the result was closer to necessity than Hayles allows. Hayles's reply is that "workability" is itself constituted by what counts as legitimate science in a given context, but this risks circularity.
- **Scope of the claim.** Hayles explicitly is *not* attacking information theory's validity within engineering; Shannon's choice was "appropriate and sensible" in context. The contingency she asserts attaches to the *ideological extrapolation* of the theory beyond its warrant, not to the theory. Readers who conflate the two will misread the argument as anti-science.
- **Evidentiary base is largely one community.** The Macy Conferences were "not the only forum," and the argument rests heavily on this single crossroads plus U.S. techno-scientific culture; the British school where MacKay's theory remained "foundational" complicates any clean victory narrative.
