# In telematic art meaning is produced through interaction not transmitted

## Claim

In a telematic art, meaning is not created by the artist, dispatched down the channel, and received intact by a viewer; it is the product of the interaction between observer and system, and its content is in a state of endless flux. *(Directly stated.)*

## Expanded Explanation

This is the keystone argument of the title essay of *Telematic Embrace* (ch. 20). Ascott names and rejects the inherited picture of art as communication: the classical Shannon–Weaver model in which a message travels one way from a sender to a receiver, with only contingent "noise" in the channel able to modify what was launched at the source. Mapped onto art, that model casts the artist as sender and therefore as the *originator, creator, owner, and controller* of meaning, and reduces the viewer to "at best, a skilled decoder or interpreter of the artist's 'meaning,' or, at worst, simply a passive receptacle of such meaning." It is the model, Ascott notes, that underwrites "the industry of criticism and exegesis."

Against this he sets a relational account. Because the observer in an interactive telematic system *is by definition a participator*, meaning is not transmitted but co-produced: it is "the product of interaction between the observer and the system, the content of which is in a state of flux, of endless change and transformation." The content is unstable not only because many users criss-cross the network but because it "is embodied in data that is itself immaterial" — "pure electronic différance" until it is reconstituted at the interface as image, text, or sound. (See [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]], which Ascott extends from Burnham's object→system lineage.)

Crucially, Ascott generalizes the claim beyond the technology. Following Barthes, he holds that telematic networking only "makes explicit in its technology and protocols what is implicit in all aesthetic experience, where that experience is seen as being as much creative in the act of the viewer's perception as it is in the artist's production." The network is thus less an exception than a literalization: it builds into its protocols a [[Negotiated Meaning|negotiation of meaning]] that was always at work, but which the object-as-fixed-semantic-configuration concealed. This is the conceptual engine of [[Reciprocal Communication]] and of [[The Participator]].

## Reasoning Structure

1. Classical communications theory treats communication as a one-way dispatch from sender to receiver, in which only channel "noise" can alter a message fixed at the source.
2. Applied to art, this makes the artist the sole originator/owner of meaning and the viewer a decoder or passive receptacle.
3. But in an interactive telematic system the observer is *by definition* a participator, and the content is immaterial data in perpetual flux ("pure electronic différance") until reconstituted at the interface.
4. Therefore meaning cannot be a fixed payload that is transmitted; it must be generated in the interaction between observer and system.
5. Generalizing (after Barthes), the viewer's perception is as creative as the artist's production in *all* aesthetic experience — telematic art merely makes this explicit in its protocols.
6. Conclusion: in telematic art, meaning is produced through interaction, not transmitted.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Shannon–Weaver model named as the rejected baseline ("Classical communications theory holds... that communication is a one-way dispatch, from sender to receiver"), explicitly cited to *Shannon and Weaver 1949*. See [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]].
- Barthes invoked for the generalization that aesthetic experience is "as much creative in the act of the viewer's perception as it is in the artist's production" (cited to *Barthes 1977*) — i.e. the death-of-the-author premise underwriting [[Negotiated Meaning]].
- Maturana and Varela cited for art "residing in a cultural communications system... a system in which the viewer actively negotiates for meaning" (cited to *Maturana and Varela (1987) 1992*) — the autopoietic/observer-relative grounding (cf. [[Autopoiesis]], [[The Observer]]).
- The *Gesamtdatenwerk* (the integrated data work) as the artifact whose "capacity to engage the intellect, emotions, and sensibility of the observer" must be assessed not as fixed content but as interactive potential.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The argument concerns telematic/interactive systems specifically; Ascott's stronger move — that *all* aesthetic experience is co-productive — is a Barthesian generalization, not a claim the network alone proves. The confidence label there is best read as `Strongly implied` by Ascott's own framing ("makes explicit... what is implicit in all aesthetic experience").
- A defender of authorship could object that infrastructure, framing, and protocol design are themselves authorial acts, so the artist does not vanish but relocates to the level of the system — Ascott concedes the artist designs the *Gesamtdatenwerk* even as he denies the artist owns its meaning.
- "Flux" and "endless change" risk dissolving the work into pure indeterminacy; the counter-pressure in Ascott is that meaning is still *negotiated* (constrained, reciprocal), not arbitrary — see [[Negotiated Meaning]].
