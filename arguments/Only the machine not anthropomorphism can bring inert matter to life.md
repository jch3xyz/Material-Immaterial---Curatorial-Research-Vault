# Only the machine not anthropomorphism can bring inert matter to life

## Claim

For Burnham, the sculptor forsook the human figure not out of a desire to destroy the biological replica but out of the realization that if he was serious in the quest to bring inert matter to life, anthropomorphism would not accomplish it — only the machine could eventually reconstruct intelligent life, or what Burnham calls "life-bearing artifacts."

## Expanded Explanation

Burnham re-motivates the central event of modern sculpture — the abandonment of naturalism and the free-standing human form. The standard art-historical story treats this as a stylistic revolt (abstraction as antithesis to naturalism, the "scribble in the air" Herbert Read laments). Burnham rejects that account. The deeper drive, he argues, was teleological: sculpture had always been "an archaic mode of form realization" predicated on "the conviction that artistic vitality could be substituted for biological vitality." The sculptor's true, "obsession"-level project across "nearly twenty-five centuries" was to bring inert matter to life — to give the shaped object "indwelling vitality." Once that goal is foregrounded, the figure had to go: a more faithful imitation of the human body brings one no closer to animating it, because anthropomorphism is the wrong instrument for the task. `Directly stated`.

The positive claim is the radical one. If anthropomorphism cannot accomplish the animation of matter, what can? Burnham's answer is the machine — even "as unsubtle and inefficient as it was" — because it "remained the only means by which man would eventually reconstruct intelligent life, or what might be called life-bearing artifacts." This is the hinge that turns the whole book toward its conclusion: modern sculpture is "a preparatory stage representing steps toward the simulation of biological life," the moment "when the sculptor begins to imitate the machine maker and the creator of scientific models." Much modern sculpture, accordingly, "has been concerned with the creation of pseudo-machines which haphazardly approximate the life impulse." `Directly stated`.

This argument grounds the teleology that drives Burnham's later chapters toward `Cybernetic Art` and `Cyborg Art`, and it anchors the vault's `Artificial Life` thread in a 1968 art-historical source: the wager that life is reconstructible by non-biological, machinic means — a wager that converges with the cybernetic and computational accounts of life elsewhere in the corpus. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Reasoning Structure

1. The enduring purpose of sculpture across millennia was to bring inert matter to life — to substitute artistic vitality for biological vitality.
2. A faithful replica of the human figure (anthropomorphism) does not advance that purpose: a better likeness is not a closer approach to actual animation.
3. Therefore the sculptor's turn away from naturalism was not iconoclasm against the biological replica but the recognition that anthropomorphism is the wrong means.
4. The machine — however crude — is the only means by which intelligent life, "life-bearing artifacts," could eventually be reconstructed.
5. Conclusion: modern sculpture is a preparatory stage toward the simulation of biological life, realized in "pseudo-machines" and, ultimately, in cybernetic and cyborg art.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The framing premise that sculpture sought to substitute artistic for biological vitality: "Sculpture has for much of its duration been predicated on the conviction that artistic vitality could be substituted for biological vitality." (Introduction)
- The teleological reading of modern sculpture as "a preparatory stage representing steps toward the simulation of biological life, a point in human evolution when the sculptor begins to imitate the machine maker and the creator of scientific models." (Introduction)
- The "pseudo-machines which haphazardly approximate the life impulse" as the actual products of this drive. (Introduction)
- The closing displacement of the "élan vital" by the Greek "kybernetes" as "the expression of a new and even more effective prime mover" — the cybernetic reframing of vitality. (Introduction) — see `Cybernetics`.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The claim attributes a single, latent intention ("the realization that...") to the historical sculptor; this is Burnham's interpretive reconstruction of motive, not an artist's stated program, and competing accounts (Worringer's abstraction-as-relief-from-cosmic-anguish, Read's stylistic exhaustion) read the same turn differently. Burnham explicitly departs from Worringer.
- It is teleological: it reads the early-century abandonment of the figure as aimed at an end (machinic life) only fully visible from the 1960s vantage of `Cybernetics` and cyborg art — a hindsight that risks the very canonization-of-the-"new" Burnham elsewhere warns against.
- "Bring inert matter to life" and "life-bearing artifacts" are asserted as a real prospect ("only a step from here to suppose..."); whether machines actually constitute life rather than simulate its appearance is left as a wager, paralleling the constructed character of the strong claim of artificial life noted elsewhere in the vault.
