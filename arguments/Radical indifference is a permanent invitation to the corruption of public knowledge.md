# Radical indifference is a permanent invitation to the corruption of public knowledge

## Claim

Because [[Radical Indifference]] evaluates content only by its capacity to generate engagement and behavioral surplus — never by its truth — lies and truth are processed identically as long as the pipelines stay full, so the corruption of public knowledge is a permanent structural feature of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] rather than an accidental failure of moderation.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff argues that surveillance capitalism does not care *what* circulates through its systems, only *that* it circulates and feeds the flow of surplus. This stance — radical indifference — is the operationalization of [[Equivalence Without Equality]]: the platform measures all content on a single axis of engagement, treating a Washington Post investigation, a New York Post gossip item, and a flat-out lie from a fabricated newspaper as equivalent inputs. The distinct moral meanings and human consequences of these texts are invisible to the accumulation logic that ranks them. Disinformation is therefore not a bug the system is straining to fix; it is exactly what a system blind to truth will amplify whenever falsehood happens to be more engaging.

This corruption falls on what Zuboff calls the public-facing "first text" within [[The Two Texts]] — the visible feed we read and write — while the proprietary "shadow text" of behavioral surplus, extracted from our engagement, remains the actual prize. Content moderation exists, but Zuboff reframes it: corruption is flagged as a problem only when it threatens to interrupt the flow of surplus into the shadow text, either by driving users away or by attracting regulators. Moderation is thus *defensive*, a protection of supply operations, "not as acts of public responsibility." The hidden, low-wage "content moderation" workforce is calibrated to reject as little as possible while staying below the threshold of user revulsion.

The stakes are epistemic. By forfeiting the integrity of public knowledge "for the sake of the volume and scope of the shadow text," radical indifference sustains what Zuboff calls the pathological division of learning in society — surveillance capitalists know everything about us, while the public sphere on which democratic self-knowledge depends is hollowed out and left vulnerable to anyone, including organized disinformation campaigns, who learns to exploit "the blind eye of radical indifference."

## Reasoning Structure

1. Radical indifference means the system judges content by engagement-equivalence, not by truth ("it doesn't matter what is in the pipelines as long as they are full and flowing").
2. Under equivalence without equality, truthful and false content are processed identically because both can generate surplus; falsehood is often *more* engaging.
3. The system intervenes against corruption only when it threatens the flow of surplus into the shadow text — so moderation is defensive, not responsible.
4. Therefore the first text (public knowledge) is permanently open to corruption, and disinformation thrives by structural design, not accident.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- **The Bosworth memo (2016, leaked 2018).** Andrew Bosworth's internal Facebook memo states "anything that allows us to connect more people more often is de facto good" and "The best products don't win. The ones everyone uses win" — Zuboff reads this as radical indifference articulated as a managerial discipline in which positives and negatives are treated as equivalent.
- **The Denver Guardian / News Feed standardization.** Facebook standardized its News Feed so that investigations, gossip, and "flat-out lies in the Denver Guardian, an entirely bogus newspaper" all "looked roughly the same as each other" — equivalence without equality rendering the first text exceptionally vulnerable to "fake news."
- **760 million fake-news reads.** Economists Hunt Allcott and Matthew Gentzkow found 760 million instances of users reading intentionally orchestrated lies before the 2016 US election — about three such stories per adult American.
- **The hidden moderation workforce.** Estimates of at least 100,000 secret "content moderators" ("janitors") apply a rulebook designed "to reject as little content as possible," seeking the equilibrium between pulling in surplus and repelling users — a calculation "that has nothing to do with assessing the truthfulness of content."
- **Subprime/mortgage fraud advertising.** Google continued serving fraudulent mortgage advertisers until 2011, when the US Treasury required it to suspend relationships with 500+ advertisers tied to 85 alleged mortgage-fraud schemes — disinformation that "survived and even thrived because it fulfilled economic imperatives."

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Platforms claim — and Zuboff acknowledges they "absolutely have the tools to shut down fake news" — that they do moderate; the argument's force depends on reading moderation as defensive rather than absent, which is an interpretive judgment about motive.
- The 760-million figure measures *reads*, not persuasion or behavior change; Allcott and Gentzkow themselves are cautious about disinformation's electoral *effect*, even as Zuboff uses it to indicate scope.
- Zuboff treats radical indifference as flowing necessarily from "economic imperatives" and "the logic of accumulation"; a critic might argue some of the corruption is contingent policy choice or competitive pressure rather than an inescapable structural law.
