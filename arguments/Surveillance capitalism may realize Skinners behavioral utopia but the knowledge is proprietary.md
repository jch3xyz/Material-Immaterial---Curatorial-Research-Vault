# Surveillance capitalism may realize Skinner's behavioral utopia but the knowledge is proprietary

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism finally builds the "instruments and methods" that B. F. Skinner lacked and so realizes his scorned behavioral utopia, but with a fatal twist that Planck, Meyer, and Skinner never anticipated — the knowledge that displaces our freedom is proprietary, theirs, while the lost freedom is solely ours.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff traces [[Instrumentarian Power]] back through [[Radical Behaviorism]] to a lineage of thinkers — the physicist Max Planck, his student Max Meyer, and Meyer's intellectual heir B. F. Skinner — who held that "freedom is ignorance," that the felt experience of free will is merely a gap in our knowledge of the causes of behavior, destined to close as measurement and computation advance. For this lineage, freedom was a price worth paying: the forfeit of freedom to knowledge would deliver a rational, harmonious modernity, and in Meyer's and Skinner's framing the resulting knowledge would belong to, and benefit, the collective — a "scientifically induced global harmony of organisms." Skinner imagined a "technology of behavior" pervasive enough to observe, compute, and reinforce behavior unobtrusively, but he died without the instruments to build it; the prize "would elude Skinner in his lifetime."

Zuboff's argument is that surveillance capitalism, through [[Big Other]] — "the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior" — supplies exactly the apparatus Skinner lacked. What was once "relegated to a gauzy world of dream and delusion" is, "in a dramatic reversal," now "a flourishing fact." The behavioral utopia of *Walden Two*, dismissed for a decade as a dystopian nightmare, is being built — not as fiction but as a market regime.

The decisive move is the twist. The behaviorist tradition imagined freedom traded *for knowledge that would serve the collective* — Zuboff's animal-telemetry precedent traded "animals' freedom for the sake of scientific knowledge intended to benefit the animals themselves." Surveillance capitalism inverts the terms: "The knowledge that now displaces our freedom is proprietary. The knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us." The dreamers never anticipated that the displacing knowledge would be privately owned — bent to "others' wealth and power" rather than to any shared illumination. This is the asymmetry that distinguishes the realized utopia from the imagined one.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Premise (lineage): Planck → Meyer → Skinner held that knowledge and freedom are adversaries, that the liberal idea of freedom recedes as scientific knowledge of behavior advances, and that forfeiting freedom to knowledge is the road to a rational modernity. *(Directly stated, Ch. 12)*
2. Premise (the missing instruments): Skinner foresaw the form of [[Instrumentarian Power]] but lacked the apparatus to realize it — "a fully operational technology of behavior was the prize that would elude Skinner in his lifetime." *(Directly stated, Ch. 13)*
3. Premise (the apparatus arrives): [[Big Other]] supplies the continuous, omnipresent, sensate, computational apparatus that fulfills "Skinner's call for the 'instruments and methods' of 'a behavioral technology.'" *(Directly stated, Ch. 12–13)*
4. Sub-conclusion: The scorned dream is therefore now "a flourishing fact"; "it is we who may see the *Walden Two* vision realized." *(Directly stated, Ch. 12–13)*
5. Premise (the unanticipated term): Planck, Meyer, and Skinner imagined freedom forfeited *for knowledge held in common and for collective benefit*; they "failed to anticipate the actual terms of this surrender." *(Directly stated, Ch. 12)*
6. Conclusion: The fatal twist is that the displacing knowledge is proprietary — "The knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us" — so the realized utopia inscribes "a sharp new asymmetry," knowledge for them and lost freedom for us. *(Directly stated, Ch. 12–13)*

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The Planck → Meyer → Skinner lineage and the doctrine that "freedom is ignorance," elaborated at length in Ch. 12 (Meyer's *Psychology of the Other-One*, 1921; Skinner's *The Behavior of Organisms*, 1938, and *Beyond Freedom & Dignity*, 1971). Surveillance capitalism "follows the logic of Planck, Meyer, and Skinner in the forfeit of freedom for knowledge." *(Directly stated, Ch. 12)*
- Skinner's explicit call (in *Beyond Freedom & Dignity*) for "a technology of behavior… comparable in power and precision to physical and biological technology," and his lament that the necessary "instruments and methods" were "not available in the field of human behavior." *(Directly stated, Ch. 12)*
- The contrast of two utopias — Skinner's *Walden Two* (1948), dismissed as dystopian, versus Orwell's *1984* (1949) — establishing that Skinner's vision was utopian *from the standpoint of the power it describes*, and that surveillance capital realizes Skinner, not Orwell. *(Directly stated, Ch. 12)*
- The animal-telemetry precedent (Stuart MacKay), which "traded animals' freedom for the sake of scientific knowledge intended to benefit the animals themselves" — the benign baseline against which the proprietary twist is measured. *(Directly stated, Ch. 12–13)*
- Skinner's penultimate (1990) essay and his late "weariness and despair" that human "friction" — the stubborn attachment to free will — kept the technology of behavior out of reach; under [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]] that resistance "appears to be waning." *(Directly stated, Ch. 13)*

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The realization is conditional, not accomplished: Zuboff writes that *if* surveillance capitalism "continue[s] to flourish," *then* "it is we who may see the *Walden Two* vision realized." The argument is a trajectory and a warning, not a claim that the utopia is complete. *(Strongly implied)*
- The "Skinner realized" framing is an analogy, not an identity. Zuboff stresses that [[Instrumentarian Power]] is "the precise antithesis of Orwell's Big Brother" and indifferent to souls and meanings; whether surveillance capital is best read as "fulfilling Skinner" or as a genuinely new species that merely *resembles* his vision is itself contestable — and Zuboff insists on its unprecedentedness. *(Interpretive synthesis)*
- Skinner himself imagined behavioral engineering serving "the good life" and social equality, not private profit; a defender of the behaviorist tradition could argue surveillance capital is a *betrayal* rather than a *fulfillment* of Skinner — which is, in fact, exactly the "fatal twist" Zuboff isolates as the discontinuity. *(Strongly implied)*
