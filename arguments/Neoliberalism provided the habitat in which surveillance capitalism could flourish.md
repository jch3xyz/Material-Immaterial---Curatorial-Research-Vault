# Neoliberalism provided the habitat in which surveillance capitalism could flourish

## Claim

Surveillance capitalism is inconceivable outside the digital milieu, but the digital milieu alone does not explain it; neoliberal ideology and policy supplied the *habitat* — the policy vacuum, the legitimating worldview, and the redesigned corporate form — in which surveillance capitalism could take root and flourish.

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff's central historical-causal argument in Chapter 2 is that surveillance capitalism is the product of a "destructive collision of two opposing historical forces." One vector is the centuries-long history of modernization that produced the individual as the locus of moral agency — the [[Second Modernity]] and its "claims to self-determination." The other is the decades-long elaboration of the neoliberal economic paradigm, which she frames not as a neutral backdrop but as "economic violence" aimed at reversing, subduing, and even destroying the individual urge toward psychological self-determination. Surveillance capitalism grows in the wound where these forces meet.

The neoliberal contribution is threefold, and each strand is a named precondition Zuboff develops into its own note. First, [[Neoliberalism]] as ideology: Hayek's defense of the self-regulating market as an unknowable "extended order" that supersedes legitimate political authority, and Friedman's radical free-market agenda, flowed into a "policy vacuum" and furnished the intellectual superstructure that legitimated a new theory of the firm. Second, [[The Shareholder Value Movement]]: Jensen and Meckling, leaning on Hayek, "took an ax to the pro-social principles of the twentieth-century corporation," reinterpreting the manager as a parasite on ownership and enshrining share price as the firm's "objective function" — producing the lean, externally disciplined corporate form that the surveillance capitalist firm would inherit. Third, the demolition of [[The Double Movement]]: Polanyi's "network of measures and policies" that had tethered the market to society — the Progressive Era, the New Deal, the Great Society — was "scheduled for demolition under the neoliberal flag" beginning in 1976, stripping away the countervailing institutions that might otherwise have checked surveillance capitalism's excesses.

The decisive move is that this institutional landscape does not merely permit surveillance capitalism but *bends* people to it. Neoliberalism's "atomized, isolated individual, doomed to a life of perpetual competition," preoccupied with survival and stripped of the social supports the double movement once provided, is precisely the second-modernity individual who will accept "the draconian quid pro quo" in which "information and connection are ransomed for the lucrative behavioral data" that fund the regime. The habitat both shelters the predator and prepares the prey.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Surveillance capitalism arises from a collision between modernization (the self-determining individual of the [[Second Modernity]]) and the neoliberal paradigm.
2. Neoliberal ideology (Hayek's "extended order," Friedman's free-market creed) filled a "policy vacuum" and legitimated a new theory of the firm.
3. That theory was operationalized as [[The Shareholder Value Movement]], which dismantled the pro-social twentieth-century corporation and produced the lean, share-price-disciplined corporate form.
4. Simultaneously, [[The Double Movement]] — the institutions that tethered markets to society — was demolished, removing the countermeasures that might have checked surveillance capitalism.
5. The resulting institutional landscape both shelters surveillance capitalism and bends atomized, insecure individuals into accepting its quid pro quo of connection-for-behavioral-data.
6. Conclusion: neoliberalism is the *habitat* — not the cause in the technological sense, but the enabling ideological and institutional ecology — in which surveillance capitalism could flourish.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- Hayek's "extended order" argument — the market as an "information-gathering process… that no central planning agency, let alone any individual, could know as a whole, possess, or control" — as the intellectual superstructure legitimating a new theory of the firm (Ch. 2, "The Neoliberal Habitat"). See [[Neoliberalism]].
- Jensen and Meckling's 1976 article reinterpreting the manager as "a sort of parasite," launching the "shareholder value movement" and enshrining share price as the basis of incentive (Ch. 2). See [[The Shareholder Value Movement]].
- Polanyi's "double movement" — the "network of measures and policies… designed to check the action of the market relative to labor, land, and money" — and its scheduled "demolition under the neoliberal flag" beginning with Carter's 1976 deregulation through the Reagan and Thatcher eras (Ch. 2). See [[The Double Movement]].
- The August 9, 2011 framing event: a decade of digital growth that "had failed to mitigate the punishing austerity of neoliberal economics and the extreme inequality that it produced," anchoring the London riots (Ch. 2).
- The 2016 IMF report warning that global trends toward neoliberalism "have not delivered as expected," diminishing growth and creating "permanent vulnerability to economic crisis" (Ch. 2).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **Digital-determinist objection.** One might argue surveillance capitalism is simply the inevitable product of the digital milieu and that neoliberalism is incidental. Zuboff preempts this: she insists surveillance capitalism is a logic, not a technology (see [[Surveillance capitalism is a logic not a technology]]), and that the same digital affordances "summoned the emancipatory promise of the internet" — the technology cuts both ways; the habitat decides which way it grows.
- **Habitat is not strict causation.** Zuboff's own metaphor ("habitat," "shelter," "root and flourish") frames neoliberalism as an *enabling ecology* rather than a deterministic cause. The argument is historical-causal and institutional, not a claim that neoliberalism mechanically produced surveillance capitalism. `Strongly implied`.
- **Naomi Klein contrast.** Zuboff elsewhere distinguishes her account of neoliberal power from Klein's shock-doctrine model (see [[Naomi Klein's shock model misreads neoliberal power]] — a Han argument); Zuboff's emphasis is on a chronic institutional habitat and a "bending" of subjects rather than acute disaster-exploitation. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **Scope.** The chapter's evidence is heavily US/UK-centric (the double movement's demolition is "most comprehensively executed in the UK and the US"), which Zuboff acknowledges; the habitat thesis travels less cleanly to economies with different neoliberal trajectories.
