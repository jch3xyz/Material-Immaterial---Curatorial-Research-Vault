# Big Other outruns society and law in the destruction of sanctuary

## Claim

As the digital era intensifies, the centuries-old solution of "justice as sanctuary" no longer holds: [[Big Other]] self-authorizes the destruction of the right to sanctuary, outrunning society and law because no existing legal regime has caught up to surveillance capitalism's dominance of the division of learning, the momentum of its dispossession cycle, and its trade in behavioral futures.

## Expanded Explanation

The principle of sanctuary is, in Zuboff's account, the most ancient antidote to power — an exit from totalizing power that has persisted "since the beginning of the human story," from the Greek *asylon* ("unplunderable") through the medieval privilege of asylum attached to churches and monasteries, to its modern reappearance in constitutional protections and the home as an inviolable fortress. One historian named the evolution from the sacred privilege to its absorption into the rule of law "justice as sanctuary." The right to sanctuary is therefore not merely a legal nicety but the precondition for the inward, disconnected time and space in which the self ripens: "If you've got nothing to hide, you are nothing." (`Directly stated`)

Surveillance capitalism dissolves this. According to [[Big Other]]'s architects, "these walls must come down. There can be no refuge." The smart home instruments the walls that once stood as "the sentinels of sanctuary," so that "the boundaries that define the very experience of home are to be erased." The first citadel to fall is the most ancient — the principle of sanctuary itself. (`Directly stated`)

The decisive move in the argument is that this destruction is *self-authorized* and outpaces the institutions that might restrain it. Big Other "overwhelms considerations of justice with its tactical mastery of shock and awe." The named de facto evidence of "a new condition that has not been tamed by law" is fivefold: surveillance capitalism's dominance of the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning]]; the "unrepentant momentum" of its [[The Dispossession Cycle|dispossession cycle]]; the institutionalization of its means of behavior modification; the convergence of these with the requirements of social participation; and the manufacture of prediction products for trade in [[Behavioral Futures Markets|behavioral futures markets]]. Existing privacy, data-protection, antitrust, and Fourth Amendment law have failed to keep pace; even expanded protections from the *state* do not shield us from the assault on sanctuary wrought by instrumentarian power. (`Directly stated`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Sanctuary — the "unplunderable" inviolable space — has been the fail-safe exit from totalizing power across the whole human story, evolving from sacred privilege into the rule of law ("justice as sanctuary").
2. Surveillance capitalism's [[Big Other]] requires that "these walls must come down": it instruments the home, erases its boundaries, and "swallows refuge whole."
3. This destruction is not constrained by existing institutions because it is *self-authorized* and moves by "shock and awe" faster than law can respond.
4. The condition "has not been tamed by law" is evidenced by five interlocking facts: dominance of the division of learning, the dispossession cycle's momentum, institutionalized behavior modification, its convergence with social participation, and behavioral-futures trade.
5. Therefore the centuries-old solution of "justice as sanctuary" no longer holds — sanctuary is being deleted, leaving "glass life" with "no exit," "where the only walls are made of glass."

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The genealogy of sanctuary: the Greek *asylon* ("unplunderable"), the European right of asylum surviving into the eighteenth century, and its modern reappearance in constitutional protections — the home as inviolable "castle," the "privacies of life" (legal scholar Linda McClain, 1995). (Ch. 17)
- The psychological case: the self-other balance "cannot be negotiated adequately without the sanctity of 'disconnected' time and space" — "If you've got nothing to hide, you are nothing." (Ch. 17)
- The failure of law: US privacy laws "have failed to keep pace with the march of instrumentarianism"; the Fourth Amendment "as currently construed does not help us here" because it circumscribes only the individual-state relationship; even the GDPR's promise is contingent on "popular movements on the ground." (Ch. 17)
- The terminal image: "glass life" and "no exit," in which "the natural human yearning for refuge must be extinguished and the ancient institution of sanctuary deleted." (Ch. 17)

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Zuboff allows that law *could* catch up: the GDPR "in time" might "move out in front of Big Other," reasserting a division of learning aligned with democratic values — so the destruction is a present failure, not a metaphysical inevitability. The argument is conditional ("What will taming require? What kind of life is left to us if taming fails?").
- The remedy she identifies is not better defensive privacy law but "synthetic declarations" institutionalized in new centers of democratic power and collective action — implying the destruction of sanctuary is a *political* defeat that political mobilization could reverse, not a settled fact.
- "Hiding" (privacy protests, counter-declarations) is named as a tempting but inadequate response: it satisfies the need for sanctuary only by accepting glass life as the baseline, and so "robs us of the life-sustaining inwardness, born in sanctuary."
