# Object art shifts the obligation to vitalize sculpture onto the viewers perception

## Claim

With the exhaustion of [[Vitalism]] and reductive [[Formalism (Burnham)|formalism]], [[Object Art]] stops trying to make the sculpture come alive from within and instead transfers the obligation to "vitalize" it onto the viewer's own perceptual activity, so that the de-idealized object becomes a vehicle through which we are forced to "see ourselves seeing objects."

## Expanded Explanation

For Burnham this is the culminating move of [[Form Exhaustion]] and the rise of [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]]. Classical and vitalist sculpture located life *inside* the object — in an "inner life" or "essential spirit" the maker breathed into the material. Object or Minimal sculpture (Judd, Morris, Flavin, Tony Smith) renounces that fiction: it "admits to being itself-nothing else." Stripped of imagery, associated meaning, and "ideal accretions," the object can no longer claim to be vital on its own account. What remains of the demand for vitality has to go somewhere, and Burnham's thesis is that it migrates to the perceiver. The work's animation is now an event in the viewer's seeing rather than a property of the bronze or plexiglas.

The philosophical warrant is drawn from Maurice Merleau-Ponty, whom Burnham reads as the phenomenologist of "the act of perception and bodily gesture while standing among objects-and in turn being perceived by other beings." Merleau-Ponty's example of the cube — that one can never *see* it with all six sides and twelve edges intact, but "can only think it" — exposes the gap between the object's geometric ideal and the partial, situated perception we actually have. Judd's minimal box does not fill that gap with imagery; it dramatizes it, forcing us to "see ourselves seeing objects unencumbered by the analytical games and value judgments promoted through art education." The sculpture's reason for being is to make the viewer's own perceptual ordering visible to itself.

This is the phenomenalist core of Object art and, more broadly, of the experiential turn that links it to [[Art as Experiential Technology]]: the form is "not an end in itself, but only the means-the vehicle-by which perceptual experiences are made possible." Burnham frames it as a transfer of *responsibility* — and notes, with characteristic skepticism, that it is an open question "if viewers will accept such a responsibility." The argument bears directly on [[Embodiment]], because the locus of the work is no longer the object but the perceiving, moving, intersubjectively situated body of the spectator.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Vitalist sculpture claimed an "inner life" dwelling within the object; reductive formalism claimed irreducible Euclidean form. Both have approached exhaustion (the vitalist/formal-reductivist crisis after 1960).
2. Object sculpture renounces internal vitality and imagery: it "admits to being itself-nothing else" and offers "meaning as a series of perceptual experiences."
3. The demand to "vitalize" the work — to make it come to life before the viewer — does not vanish; it is *shifted* to the viewer's capacity to analyze his own ways of seeing.
4. Merleau-Ponty supplies the ground: perception is a situated bodily act, and the cube can only be *thought*, never seen in its geometric completeness — so the gap between ideal form and actual perception becomes the work's subject.
5. Conclusion: Object art makes us "see ourselves seeing objects"; vitality becomes an event in perception, the object a vehicle, and the viewer the bearer of the obligation that sculpture used to discharge internally.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Burnham himself flags the pitfall: "Placing such an immense burden on the observer eventually allows the sculptor to present an empty gallery space for inspection... Or he can simply ask gallery goers to observe each other." The phenomenalist logic, pushed to its limit, can void the object entirely. (`Directly stated`)
- He concedes the approach cannot manufacture content: "phenomenalism cannot imbue an object with attributes which are not there. It can only force us to be aware of the chaos and multi-layered nature of our visual habits." (`Directly stated`)
- He doubts whether the transfer will hold: "It remains to be known if viewers will accept such a responsibility," and suggests durable Object art "combines the phenomenalist approach with more traditional criteria." (`Directly stated`)
- A skeptic could read Object art's coldness as mere "aesthetics of sterility" (Kozloff's phrase, reported by Burnham) — a retreat into hyperformalism rather than an experiential advance; Burnham argues this misses its reason for being. (`Strongly implied`)

## Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the locus of the artwork shifts from inert materiality (weight, mass, form) toward an immaterial perceptual event in the embodied viewer; the object remains present yet ceases to be where the "life" of the work resides.
