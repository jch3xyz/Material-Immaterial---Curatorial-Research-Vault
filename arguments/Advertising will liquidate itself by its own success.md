# Advertising will liquidate itself by its own success

## Claim

Advertising's real aim is not to sell any particular product but to achieve a "programmed harmony among all human impulses and aspirations and endeavors" — a collective consciousness in which production, consumption, desire, and effort are all pre-harmonized; the paradox is that at the moment this aim is fully realized, advertising will have "liquidated itself by its own success," having made itself unnecessary.

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan reframes advertising away from the familiar charge that it lies about products and toward a structural thesis about what the whole enterprise is *for*. "To put the matter abruptly," he writes, "the advertising industry is a crude attempt to extend the principles of automation to every aspect of society." Advertising is therefore continuous with automation and with the electric age's drive to interrelate every function: it is not a parasite on the economy but the leading edge of a tendency to organize the social field into a single integrated process. The corset that "protests that 'it is not the corset that you ieel'" and the corporate ad that dramatizes "the container function, by means of great art" are not selling objects; they are dramatizing the producer–consumer process as one "unified and compressed image."

The terminus of that tendency is stated as an ideal: "Ideally, advertising aims at the goal of a programmed harmony among all human impulses and aspirations and endeavors. Using handicraft methods, it stretches out toward the ultimate electronic goal of a collective consciousness." Two things are doing the work here. First, the goal is *programmed harmony* — desire and effort, production and consumption, brought into a "pre-established harmony," a phrase that frames advertising as the engineering of a collective mind rather than the manipulation of isolated buyers. Second, advertising is explicitly transitional: it pursues an "electronic goal" with "handicraft methods." The means (the laboriously hand-built ad, the "toil, attention, testing, wit, art, and skill of many people") are artisanal and pre-electric; the end is the electric-age condition of a shared, programmed consciousness. Advertising is thus a craft straining toward something that, once attained, would no longer need the craft.

Hence the paradoxical self-liquidation. "When all production and all consumption are brought into a pre-established harmony with all desire and all effort, then advertising will have liquidated itself by its own success." If desire and supply are perfectly pre-harmonized, there is nothing left to persuade: the gap between what is made and what is wanted — the gap advertising exists to close — has vanished. McLuhan reinforces this with a second, present-tense formulation: "Ads have proved to be a self-liquidating form of community entertainment." Advertising is not a stable institution but a vanishing mediator, a transitional form whose perfection is its own abolition.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Advertising is "a crude attempt to extend the principles of automation to every aspect of society" — its logic is the integration of every social function into one process, not the sale of discrete products.
2. Its *ideal* terminus is a "programmed harmony among all human impulses and aspirations and endeavors" — the "electronic goal of a collective consciousness."
3. It pursues that electronic goal by pre-electric, artisanal means ("Using handicraft methods"), so it is by nature a transitional form, not a permanent one.
4. Total success means total harmony: all production, consumption, desire, and effort brought into a "pre-established harmony."
5. But persuasion presupposes a gap between desire and supply; in total harmony that gap is gone, leaving nothing to advertise.
6. Therefore at the point of complete success advertising "will have liquidated itself" — its perfection abolishes its function ("a self-liquidating form").

- The craft evidence underwriting "handicraft methods": "Any expensive ad represents the toil, attention, testing, wit, art, and skill of many people," built "on the tested foundations of public stereotypes" (Ch. 23).

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The terminus is an explicit *ideal* ("Ideally, advertising aims..."), a limiting case rather than an observed event; McLuhan offers no mechanism by which perfect "pre-established harmony" would actually be reached, so the self-liquidation is asserted as a logical endpoint, not demonstrated.
- The argument trades on equating advertising with automation and with the electric "collective consciousness"; a critic could hold that advertising manufactures and renews dissatisfaction faster than it satisfies it (the chapter's own title — "Keeping Upset with the Joneses" — suggests perpetual unrest), which would make harmony a receding horizon and the institution self-perpetuating rather than self-liquidating.
- "Pre-established harmony" borrows a metaphysical phrase (Leibniz) whose transfer to a social-economic claim is analogical; the unity of the thesis rests on McLuhan's pattern-reading, not on empirical argument.
- McLuhan immediately notes that TV "favors much more consciousness concerning the unconscious," so that "any ad consciously attended to is comical" — which complicates the harmony thesis, since the very medium of the electric age also exposes and defangs the subliminal technique advertising relied on.
