# Instrumentarianism is a new species of power not a form of totalitarianism

## Claim

Instrumentarianism is an unprecedented species of power that has been disastrously misread "through the old lenses of totalitarianism": where totalitarianism transformed the **state** into a project of *total possession* that demanded the soul, [[Instrumentarian Power]] transforms the **market** into a project of *total certainty* that is profoundly indifferent to the soul and instead observes, tunes, and herds behavior toward others' ends.

## Expanded Explanation

For Zuboff the reflex to name surveillance capitalism "digital totalitarianism" — to reach for Orwell's Big Brother whenever this new power is confronted — is not merely imprecise; it actively disarms resistance. She calls this an instance of the "horseless-carriage" syndrome: we attach our new sense of peril to old, familiar facts, "unaware that the conclusions to which they lead us are necessarily incorrect." The twentieth century's vivid memory of totalitarian terror becomes the wrong compass for a twenty-first-century conjuring of power that operates by an entirely different inner logic. (`Directly stated`)

The decisive contrast is one of *aim* and *means*. Totalitarian power was "bent on the reconstruction of the human species through the dual mechanisms of genocide and the 'engineering of the soul'": it demanded possession of each person "from the inside out," through violence, terror, conversion. Instrumentarian power "has no interest in our souls or any principle to instruct"; it is "profoundly and infinitely indifferent to our meanings and motives." It operates not through the means of violence but through the [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|means of behavioral modification]] — the actuation that Zuboff catalogues as "tuning, herding, conditioning." Where totalitarianism wanted total possession of the soul, instrumentarianism wants "guaranteed outcomes," a condition of "certainty without terror." (`Directly stated`)

This indifference is precisely what makes instrumentarianism easy to undervalue. "Because it does not claim our bodies for some grotesque regime of pain and murder, we are prone to undervalue its effects and lower our guard." Instead of death, torture, reeducation, or conversion, "instrumentarianism effectively exiles us from our own behavior" — it severs our insides from our outsides, our interiority from our observable actions, leaving behavior to take on a life of its own that is delivered to surveillance capitalism's aims. The danger is not that instrumentarianism is *like* totalitarianism, but that it is a genuinely new, genuinely incomprehensible species of power that the totalitarian template renders invisible. (`Directly stated`)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Totalitarianism and instrumentarianism are *both* unprecedented species of power that "defied human comprehension" when they appeared — so the impulse to interpret the new one through the categories of the older one is understandable but mistaken (the horseless-carriage syndrome).
2. Their **aims** are opposite: totalitarianism sought *total possession* — the reconstruction of the human species, the engineering of the soul, from the inside out, through genocide and terror. Instrumentarianism seeks *total certainty* — guaranteed behavioral outcomes — and is "infinitely indifferent" to the soul, meanings, and motives.
3. Their **means** are opposite: totalitarianism "operated through the means of violence"; instrumentarianism "operates through the means of behavioral modification" — observation, rendition, tuning, herding, conditioning.
4. Their **locus** is opposite: totalitarianism was "a transformation of the state into a project of total possession"; instrumentarianism is "the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty," unimaginable outside the digital milieu and the logic of [[Surveillance Capitalism]].
5. Therefore equating the two "impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist, neutralize, and ultimately vanquish its potency": effective resistance requires naming instrumentarianism on its own terms.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The conceptual contrast statement: totalitarianism = transformation of the state into total possession; instrumentarianism = transformation of the market into total certainty (Ch. 1 and Ch. 12).
- The **horseless-carriage syndrome** as the diagnostic figure for the misreading — Zuboff's recurring metaphor for attaching new peril to old facts.
- The **intellectual origin story**, which severs instrumentarianism from totalitarianism's lineage: instrumentarianism's "viewpoint of observation" was forged not in genocidal politics but "in the controversial intellectual domain known as 'radical behaviorism' and its antecedents in turn-of-the-century theoretical physics" — Max Planck → Max Meyer → [[B. F. Skinner]], with Meyer's *Psychology of the Other-One* (1921) and Skinner's "technology of behavior" supplying the gaze that reduces persons to "an organism among organisms."
- The **Walden Two vs. 1984** contrast: Skinner's utopia and Orwell's dystopia, often confused, in fact "reflect two distinct species of power" — "In most respects, they are precise opposites." Orwell "laid bare the disease" of the soul's domination; Skinner "asserted the antidote" of behavioral engineering that "transcends the use of force and rejects the need to dominate human souls."
- The **China social-credit** case as proof of the analytic stakes: invariably called "digital totalitarianism" and compared to *1984*, "it is better understood as the apotheosis of instrumentarian power fed by public and private data sources and controlled by an authoritarian state" — i.e., instrumentarian means harnessed to state rather than market ends ("certainty without terror"), not a revival of totalitarianism.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The convergence objection.** Zuboff herself notes that instrumentarian power "found necessary support from public institutions" and that state and market demonstrate "a shared commitment to a relentless drive toward guaranteed outcomes" — and the China case fuses instrumentarian and state power. A critic could argue this convergence blurs the very distinction the argument insists upon; Zuboff's reply is that the *means* (behavioral modification, radical indifference) remain instrumentarian even when annexed to state ends.
- **A distinction without a difference?** Because instrumentarianism "exiles us from our own behavior" and erodes democracy "from within," one might ask whether the loss of freedom is not as grave as totalitarian domination, making the taxonomic distinction merely academic. Zuboff's counter is pragmatic: the distinction is not about relative gravity but about *recognizability and resistance* — the old lens renders the new power invisible.
- **Scope.** The claim is a conceptual/historical taxonomy, not an empirical prediction; Zuboff concedes China's total-certainty system "may be impossibly ambitious" and that experts doubt a single system of that scale can be achieved.
