# There is no erotics of transparency

## Claim

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], eros is structurally dependent on the [[Negativity]] of the secret, the veil, and intermittence; because [[Transparency]] eliminates precisely these negativities, a society organized around transparency necessarily collapses eros into pornography, obscenity, and spectacle.

## Expanded Explanation

Han's argument is libidinal-economic in form: desire is not aroused by the bare object but by what is withdrawn, withheld, or staged as appearance-and-disappearance. The seducer, the veil, the mask, and the figural garb of scripture all operate by the same logic — they install distance, ambivalence, and deferral, and it is across this distance that eros travels. To make the object transparent is therefore not to liberate it for love but to flatten it into pornographic exposure. "Transparency or straightforwardness would be the end of eros — that is, pornography" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/06 Ch3 - Evidence|Ch3 — Evidence]]).

From this libidinal premise Han derives a sociological thesis. If contemporary society makes transparency its highest value — through postprivacy, [[Exhibition Value]], hypervisible media, and the imperative to show — then it cannot simultaneously be a society of eros. It is, by structural necessity, a "porno-society" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/06 Ch3 - Evidence|Ch3 — Evidence]]). The chain transparency = pornography = obscenity = spectacle is not a rhetorical flourish but a strict consequence: when the secret vanishes in favor of total exhibition and bareness, what remains is the [[Positivity]] of the smooth, the deictic "there is nothing other than this," the unary image without [[Punctum|punctum]], and finally the spectacle whose mode of operation is infection and abreaction rather than reading.

The argument thereby joins Han's broader critique: transparency is not the medium of the beautiful (Benjamin), not the space of desire (Lacan), not the room of play (Foucault), and not the dwelling of grace. It is the operational logic of a [[Capitalism]] that maximizes [[Exhibition Value]] by handing everything over to hypervisibility.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Desire and pleasure are governed by a libidinal economy in which the object becomes desirable through withdrawal, concealment, and detour. ("Precisely the negativity of the secret, the veil, and concealment incite desire and make pleasure more intense.")
2. Transparency is the operation that eliminates secret, veil, and concealment by demanding total exposure and symmetry. ("Transparency represents a condition of symmetry.")
3. Therefore transparency is structurally incompatible with eros: it is "foreign to libidinal economy" and "hostile to pleasure."
4. What remains when eros is subtracted from exposure is pornography: the direct, unmediated, intermittence-less presentation of nudity ("The positivity of exhibiting nudity without a veil is pornographic").
5. Therefore a society whose dominant imperative is transparency is necessarily a porno-society — not contingently, but by the same logic that makes the unveiled object pornographic.
6. Pornographic images are unary (Barthes): they lack [[Punctum|punctum]], offer no rift or ambivalence, "empty out into spectacle." So the porno-society is also a society of the spectacle.
7. The full chain: transparency → exhibition → obscenity → pornography → spectacle. Each step is governed by the elimination of the same negativity.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- Han mounts the argument against [[Giorgio Agamben]], who proposes that exposed, profanated nudity opens a "new form of erotic communication" by emptying the face into pure mediality. Han rejects this: "The face that has become complicit with nudity is already pornographic." A reader sympathetic to Agamben could argue that Han forecloses too quickly the possibility of an eros after the secret, conflating exhibition with pornography by definitional fiat.
- The argument leans heavily on a romantic-symbolist phenomenology of veiling (Benjamin, Barthes, Baudrillard, Lacan). It does not seriously engage feminist, queer, or sex-positive critiques of the "veil-as-erotic" tradition, for which the veil has also been an instrument of constraint, not only of desire.
- "Postprivacy" practices are dismissed as detrimental to pleasure, but the empirical question — whether participants in such practices in fact report attenuated eros — is not addressed. The argument is conceptual, not sociological.
- The libidinal-economic premise is asserted via citation (Simmel, Lacan, Foucault) rather than independently demonstrated. If one rejects the premise that desire requires negativity, the whole chain (transparency → pornography → spectacle) collapses.
- Han's chain is a strong identity claim, not a tendency claim. He does not write that transparency tends toward pornography but that direct exhibition is already pornography. This leaves little room for gradations or counter-examples.
