# Games are extensions of the social man and are mass media

## Claim

Games, like institutions, are extensions of social man and the body politic in the same way technologies are extensions of the animal organism; as collective dramatizations of a culture's inner life they are faithful models of that culture and media of interpersonal communication — so the chapter's closing question, "Are games mass media?", is answered "Yes."

## Expanded Explanation

McLuhan builds the argument by analogy. Just as a wheel, a road, or a spoken word extends some faculty of the individual organism, a game extends the group: "Games, like institutions, are extensions of social man and of the body politic, as technologies are extensions of the animal organism." Both serve the same regulatory function — they are "counter-irritants or ways of adjusting to the stress of the specialized actions that occur in any social group." A game is thus not idle pastime but a corporate response to the dominant action of a culture, "popular art, collective, social reactions to the main drive or action of any culture."

Because a game absorbs both the action and the reaction of a whole population, it crystallizes a culture into a single perceptible form: as extensions of the popular response to workaday stress, "games become faithful models of a culture. They incorporate both the action and the reaction of whole populations in a single dynamic image." This places games on the same footing as language: "all games are media of interpersonal communication, and they could have neither existence nor meaning except as extensions of our immediate inner lives." Like any medium, a game's relevance lies in its form, not its content — "it is the pattern of a game that gives it relevance to our inner lives, and not who is playing nor the outcome of the game" — which is why the closing answer follows directly: games are "situations contrived to permit simultaneous participation of many people in some significant pattern of their own corporate lives."

The argument generalizes one rung further: "Any game, like any medium of information, is an extension of the individual or the group." A game restructures whatever it does not extend — "Its effect on the group or individual is a reconfiguring of the parts of the group or individual that are not so extended" — which is precisely the medium-as-message logic applied to play.

## Reasoning Structure

1. Technologies extend the animal organism; games and institutions, by the same logic, extend social man and the body politic.
2. As collective extensions of a population's response to its workaday stresses, games incorporate both action and reaction "in a single dynamic image" and so become faithful models of a culture.
3. Like vernacular tongues, games exist only as extensions of inner life and are therefore media of interpersonal communication.
4. The relevance of a game (like any medium) lies in its pattern, not its content or outcome — so a game is "any medium of information."
5. A medium that contrives "simultaneous participation of many people in some significant pattern of their own corporate lives" is by definition a mass medium.
6. Conclusion: games are mass media.

## Evidence Used by the Author

- The structural analogy itself: games/institutions extend social man as technologies extend the animal organism — [[Media as Extensions of Man]], [[Specialism as Counter-Irritant]] (games as "counter-irritants").
- Cross-cultural readings of games as cultural models: the New Guinea Willigiman-wallalua war games as "a kind of model of the universe"; the Olympic games as enactments of "the agon, or struggle of the Sun god"; poker "as the expression of all the complex attitudes and unspoken values of a competitive society."
- Games as register of cultural change: the displacement of baseball ("a game of one-thing-at-a-time, fixed positions") by football ("nonpositional") under the TV image — [[Detribalization and Retribalization]], [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]].
- The form-over-content principle drawn from game theory and information theory, which "have dealt with the information content of systems" and so "miss the central structural core of the experience" — [[The Medium Is the Message]], [[Media as Translators]].

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- McLuhan concedes games' moral thinness: "Seen as live models of complex social situations, games may lack moral earnestness, it has to be admitted" — they are reactions to a culture's typical action, not the "profound reappraisal" of high art ("[[The Artist as Integral Awareness]]").
- Not every contest qualifies as a true game/medium: war, the stock market, and business are disqualified because "the rules are not fully known nor accepted by all the players" and "the audience is too fully participant," so the model-like distance from the situation collapses.
- The "mass media" claim rests on McLuhan's own redefinition of medium as any extension of the social organism; on a narrower (broadcast-only) definition of mass media, treating tennis or thirteen playing cards as a mass medium would not follow.
