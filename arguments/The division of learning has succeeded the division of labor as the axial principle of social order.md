# The division of learning has succeeded the division of labor as the axial principle of social order

## Claim

The division of learning has succeeded the division of labor as the axial principle of social order; it makes the same economy-to-society migration Durkheim charted a century ago, but surveillance capital is dragging it into Durkheim's "abnormal" (pathological) form, where asymmetries of knowledge and power grow so extreme that they make "conflict itself impossible."

## Expanded Explanation

Zuboff builds the argument on an explicit analogy to Émile Durkheim's *The Division of Labor in Society*. Durkheim's controversial move was to take a principle that economists (from Adam Smith's pin factory onward) understood purely as a means to labor productivity and show that it had "burst through those factory walls to becoming the critical organizing principle of industrial society." Its real significance was not output but solidarity: the division of labor "renders societies possible which, without it, would not exist," establishing "a social and moral order *sui generis*." Zuboff names this as a general law — "the principles of capitalism initially aimed at production eventually shape the wider social and moral milieu" — so that an economic ordering principle becomes the *moral* basis of the society around it. (Directly stated.)

Her thesis is that the division of learning now occupies exactly that position. As "people, processes, and things are reinvented as information," the workplace's earlier shift "from a division of labor to a division of learning" repeats at the scale of society: "the division of learning in society becomes the ascendant principle of social ordering in our time." It "follows the same migratory path from the economic to the social domain once traveled by the division of labor," and like its predecessor it now "passes far beyond purely economic interests," establishing "the basis for our social order and its moral content." The division of learning resolves itself into three questions — *Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?* — i.e. the distribution of knowledge, the authority over it, and the power undergirding that authority. (Directly stated.)

But the analogy carries Durkheim's warning as well as his diagnosis. Durkheim recognized that the principle could "take a dark turn," producing an "abnormal" (pathological) division of labor — and singled out "extreme asymmetries of power that make 'conflict itself impossible' by 'refusing to admit the right of combat.'" This is precisely where surveillance capitalism drives the division of learning. Its "unprecedented concentration of knowledge produces an equally unprecedented concentration of power," such that "powerful private interests are in control of the definitive principle of social ordering in our time, just as Durkheim warned of the subversion of the division of labor by the powerful forces of industrial capital a century ago." Surveillance capital "knows, decides, and decides who decides," operating "outside of our awareness and without means of combat" — Durkheim's pathology realized: a social-ordering principle privatized so asymmetrically that contestation itself is foreclosed. (Directly stated.)

## Reasoning Structure

1. Durkheim showed the division of labor migrated from the factory to society, becoming not merely an economic mechanism but the *moral* basis of industrial order ("a social and moral order *sui generis*").
2. As people, processes, and things are reinvented as information, the division of learning makes the *same* economy-to-society migration: it "becomes the ascendant principle of social ordering in our time" and "passes far beyond purely economic interests."
3. Therefore a new axial principle — the division of learning, resolved into *Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?* — has succeeded the division of labor as the basis of social order in an information civilization.
4. Durkheim also warned of an "abnormal"/pathological form, in which "extreme asymmetries of power" make "conflict itself impossible."
5. Surveillance capitalism's unprecedented concentrations of knowledge-and-power put "powerful private interests in control of the definitive principle of social ordering," operating "without means of combat."
6. Conclusion: the division of learning has indeed succeeded the division of labor as the axial principle — but surveillance capital is driving it into Durkheim's pathological form, foreclosing the very contestation by which past pathologies (via collective bargaining, public education) were cured.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- The Durkheim analogy is structural, not strict: Durkheim's *normal* division of labor was integrative (solidarity through reciprocity), whereas Zuboff treats the division of learning as integrative only in principle and already pathological in fact — so the parallel does real argumentative work mainly through its dark, "abnormal" pole. (Interpretive synthesis.)
- Calling the division of learning *the* axial principle competes with rival framings (e.g. the persistence of the division of labor under job polarization and automation); Zuboff grants the economic division of learning is "only the beginning," which concedes the two principles coexist rather than one cleanly replacing the other. (Strongly implied.)
- The claim that asymmetries make "conflict itself impossible" is Durkheim's strongest term; Zuboff's own remedy — reasserting "the people's right to contest, confront, and prevail" — implies contestation remains possible in principle, so the foreclosure is political and contingent, not absolute.
