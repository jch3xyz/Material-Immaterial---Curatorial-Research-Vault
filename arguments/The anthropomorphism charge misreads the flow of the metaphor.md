# The anthropomorphism charge misreads the flow of the metaphor

## Claim

The standard objection that artificial-intelligence and plant-sentience research anthropomorphize their objects "misunderstands the flow of the metaphor": the real operation is the *reverse* — a reduction of the human to the machine or the plant — and the inclusive redefinition of "intelligence" and "consciousness" required to make it work strikes a "risky ontological bargain" that shrinks humanity to "the exchange of information," a caricature so rudimentary "that it can be applied to virtually anything."

## Expanded Explanation

This is the counter-thesis at the hinge of [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]'s "Network" chapter, and it is the inverse twin of the book's framing argument, [[Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image]]. There the reversibility of metaphor let *mind-as-machine* flip into machine-as-mind; here O'Gieblyn turns that reversibility against the most common critique of the new sciences of nonhuman intelligence. Critics accuse roboticists and plant ecologists of *anthropomorphism* — of projecting human qualities (thought, behavior, awareness) onto machines and plants. O'Gieblyn's claim is that this gets the direction exactly wrong. "Proponents of decentralized intelligence are less interested in projecting human qualities onto nonhuman objects than they are in reconfiguring human intelligence through the lens of these inanimate systems." The metaphor flows from the machine/plant *to* the human, not the other way around. The operation is not anthropomorphism but its mirror — call it mechanomorphism (or, in [[Monica Gagliano|Gagliano]]'s coinage, "phytomorphising the human").

Her two witnesses make the reversal explicit. Rodney Brooks claimed that we "overanthropomorphize humans…who are after all mere machines" — so the corrective to anthropomorphism is to recognize that the human was always already a machine. The ecologist Monica Gagliano, routinely accused of anthropomorphizing plants, says her intention "is precisely the opposite": "I'm interested in phytomorphising the human… I want humans to become more like plants." In both cases the lowering term is the human; the nonhuman system is the standard to which we are assimilated. This is why the charge of anthropomorphism is a category error about which way the figure points — and it is the concrete, chapter-6 instance of the abstract reversibility established in Chapter 1.

The bargain comes in the price. To make "intelligence" and "consciousness" stretch across plants, insects, beehives, and machines, the terms must be redefined downward — "intelligence" from abstract thought to mere problem-solving or environmental adaptation, "consciousness" from self-awareness to bare awareness of one's environment or behavior that "appear[s] deliberate and intentional." These "redefinitions are meant to be more expansive and inclusive," O'Gieblyn grants, but "they radically change how we understand these qualities when they are applied to ourselves." The cost of seeing ourselves "as one with the natural world" is to "reduce our understanding of our humanity to such a rudimentary caricature — the exchange of information — that it can be applied to virtually anything." The inclusive move is purchased by an ontological shrinkage of the human to a node in [[Information]] flow — the same content-emptied informational subject that [[Mind as Machine]], [[The Body as Data]], and [[Posthumanism]] each install from a different direction. What looks like generosity toward nonhuman life is, on O'Gieblyn's reading, an [[Eliminativism|eliminativist]] downgrade of the human self.

## Reasoning Structure

1. AI and plant-sentience research are routinely criticized for anthropomorphism — for projecting human qualities onto machines and plants.
2. But "this objection misunderstands the flow of the metaphor": its proponents are "less interested in projecting human qualities onto nonhuman objects than they are in reconfiguring human intelligence through the lens of these inanimate systems."
3. The figure therefore flows from machine/plant *to* human, not human to machine/plant — the reverse of anthropomorphism. (Brooks: we "overanthropomorphize humans…who are after all mere machines"; Gagliano: "phytomorphising the human… I want humans to become more like plants.")
4. To extend "intelligence" and "consciousness" to nonhuman systems, those terms must be redefined far more broadly (problem-solving, adaptation, environmental awareness, intentional-seeming behavior).
5. These redefinitions "radically change how we understand these qualities when they are applied to ourselves."
6. Conclusion: this is "a risky ontological bargain" — union with nature is bought by shrinking humanity to "the exchange of information," a caricature "that it can be applied to virtually anything." The charge of anthropomorphism conceals a reduction of the human.

## Counterarguments and Limitations

- **The reversal is not exhaustive.** That *some* proponents (Brooks, Gagliano) explicitly aim to reconfigure the human does not show that all anthropomorphism-charges are misdirected; a researcher may genuinely over-read intention into a robot or plant. O'Gieblyn's earlier discussion of Brooks granting "agency" to Herbert and Cog "only because a human observer was willing to see it as such" concedes that projection is also real — so the two directions can coexist rather than one simply displacing the other.
- **The bargain may be one O'Gieblyn dramatizes rather than demonstrates.** Calling the informational definition of humanity a "rudimentary caricature" presupposes the very thing the redefiners deny — that there is an irreducible interior surplus ([[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|the hard problem]], "what it is like") beyond the exchange of information. An [[Eliminativism|eliminativist]] would answer that the caricature is simply the truth, not a bargain.
- **Inclusivity has independent ethical force.** Daniel Dennett's worry, later in the same chapter, runs the other way: that resisting the attribution of personhood is "an ugly talent, reeking of racism or species-ism." The pressure to extend intelligence outward is not only an "ontological bargain" but a moral-political demand against anthropocentrism — which complicates the claim that the redefinition is a net loss.
- **Scope and register.** The argument is asserted and illustrated within a literary-philosophical chapter; "the flow of the metaphor" is a figure of speech doing analytic work, not a worked-out theory of reference. It is best read as the chapter's interpretive thesis, leaning on the structural premise already established in Chapter 1 ([[Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image]]).
