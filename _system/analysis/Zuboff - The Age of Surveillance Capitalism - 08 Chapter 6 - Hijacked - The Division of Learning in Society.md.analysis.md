## Executive Summary

Chapter 6 is the **capstone of Part I** ("The Foundations of Surveillance Capitalism"). Its work is to name the deepest stakes of everything Part I has documented: surveillance capitalism's seizure of the **division of learning in society**, which Zuboff names "the axial principle of social order in an information civilization." The chapter proceeds in six moves. (I) It frames the founding of surveillance capitalism as a **conquest by declaration**, analogized to Columbus's "conquest pattern" and the Spanish *Requirimiento*, and lays out the **six Google declarations** — a cascading set of claimed rights (to take, to own, to know, to decide, and to defend those rights) that, like the *Requirimiento*, manufacture legitimacy from thin air. (II) It introduces the **division of learning** as the successor to the twentieth-century division of labor, organized around three essential questions — **"Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?"** — questions of knowledge, authority, and power. (III) It distinguishes the **two texts**: the public-facing "first text" that we author and read, and the hidden **"shadow text"** (read-only for surveillance capitalists) into which our behavioral surplus is dragooned — "about us, but… not for us." (IV) It describes the **New Priesthood**: the hyperscale material infrastructure (data centers, 2.5 million servers, the TPU chip) and the scarce caste of ~10,000 machine-intelligence specialists whose genius is "meant to storm the gates of human experience," reverting the diffusion of knowledge to a pre-Gutenberg order. (V) It declares the division of learning **hijacked** — its unauthorized **privatization** — drawing on Durkheim's warning about the "abnormal/pathological" division of labor and on early privacy scholars Simitis and Schwartz. (VI) A closing review: the unprecedented power of surveillance capital is antidemocratic, originates not in the state, and cannot be met by privacy or antimonopoly frameworks; the chapter pivots Part I → Part II, where extraction expands from the online world into the "real" world via the prediction imperative and economies of scope and action, threatening the **right to the future tense**.

This chapter is foundational for the vault's **Surveillance** research focus and is densely resonant with Han's `Digital Panopticon` / `Psychopolitics`, Harari's `Dataism`, and the broader `Big Data` / `The Body as Data` cluster. It also extends the vault's `Information` note (the digitization of "language, cultural assets, traditions, institutions, rules, and laws"), and supplies the keystone Durkheim and Searle interlocutors.

## Arguments

- **Surveillance capitalism founds itself by declaration — a conquest pattern that fabricates legitimacy from thin air**
  - Reasoning structure: Searle's theory of declarations (facts created by representing them as created) → the historical conquest pattern (Columbus, the Requirimiento) imposes new facts and then engineers acceptance → Google's six declarations follow exactly this template (terms-of-service replace monarchical edicts) → declarations "rise or fall on the strength of others' acceptance," and these have been allowed to stand.
  - Evidence: the Requirimiento read into beards in the dark; Verizon's copy-cat dispossession proving the declarations stand; the six enumerated declarations.
  - Source passage: > "These twenty-first-century invaders do not ask permission; they forge ahead, papering the scorched earth with faux-legitimation practices. Instead of cynically conveyed monarchical edicts, they offer cynically conveyed terms-of-service agreements whose stipulations are just as obscured and incomprehensible."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Surveillance capitalism is founded by declaration on the model of conquest.md`

- **The division of learning has succeeded the division of labor as the axial principle of social order — and surveillance capital has hijacked it**
  - Reasoning structure: Durkheim showed the division of labor migrated from the factory to become the moral basis of industrial society → as people, processes, and things are "reinvented as information," the division of learning makes the same migration from economy to society → but surveillance capital commands the answers to Who knows / decides / decides who decides → so the division of learning falls into Durkheim's "pathological"/"abnormal" form, with asymmetries so extreme they make "conflict itself impossible."
  - Evidence: Durkheim's *Division of Labor in Society*; the pulp-mill ethnography; the digitization figures (98% of world information digital by 2013); Hilbert; job polarization data.
  - Source passage: > "today our societies are threatened as the division of learning drifts into pathology and injustice at the hands of the unprecedented asymmetries of knowledge and power that surveillance capitalism has achieved."
  - Source passage: > "This means that powerful private interests are in control of the definitive principle of social ordering in our time, just as Durkheim warned of the subversion of the division of labor by the powerful forces of industrial capital a century ago."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The division of learning has succeeded the division of labor and been hijacked.md`

- **Surveillance capitalism produces two texts; the shadow text is about us but not for us**
  - Reasoning structure: the public first text (search, News Feed, our posts) functions as the supply operation → its behavioral surplus fills the hidden shadow text → surveillance capitalists then apply shadow-text learning back to shape the public text to their interests (algorithmic ranking, News Feed ordering) → so we author the first text but are excluded from the second, of which we are the objects.
  - Evidence: Frank Pasquale on the Googleplex's value-laden ranking decisions; Google/Facebook algorithmic manipulations "derived from surplus."
  - Source passage: > "As the source from which all the treasure flows, this second text is about us, but it is not for us. Instead, it is created, maintained, and exploited outside our awareness for others' benefit."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The shadow text is about us but not for us.md`

- **Surveillance capitalism reverts the diffusion of knowledge to a pre-Gutenberg order via a new priesthood**
  - Reasoning structure: Gutenberg democratized knowledge, bypassing the priesthood → the internet was believed to extend that democratization → but a hidden countermovement captures learning behind a "narrow priesthood" of computational specialists, privately owned machines, and concentrated capital → the world's AI talent (~10,000 people) and infrastructure pool inside a few firms → so knowledge re-concentrates, reversing Gutenberg.
  - Evidence: hyperscale (2.5M servers, 15 data-center locations, the TPU); the "missing generation" of UK data scientists; Google as top contributor to scientific journals; Domingos ("whoever learns fastest wins").
  - Source passage: > "In this hidden movement the competitive struggle over surveillance revenues reverts to the pre-Gutenberg order as the division of learning in society shades toward the pathological, captured by a narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists, their privately owned machines, and the economic interests for whose sake they learn."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Surveillance capitalism reverts knowledge to a pre-Gutenberg priesthood.md`

- **Surveillance capitalism is antidemocratic and cannot be met by privacy or antimonopoly frameworks — the fight must be over capitalism itself**
  - Reasoning structure: its power does not originate in the state and is not reducible to technology or bad people; it is a logic of accumulation → privacy-rights and monopoly frameworks have not interrupted the accumulation mechanisms (asking for privacy "is like asking Henry Ford to make each Model T by hand or asking a giraffe to shorten its neck") → therefore resistance must contest the economic logic, collectively, not merely seek encryption / anonymity / data ownership (which "only acknowledge the inevitability of commercial surveillance").
  - Evidence: Simitis (1980s) and Schwartz (1989) anticipating the autonomy/democracy threat; the limits of privacy law; the closing call.
  - Source passage: > "If there is to be a fight, let it be a fight over capitalism. Let it be an insistence that raw surveillance capitalism is as much a threat to society as it is to capitalism itself. This is not a technical undertaking…"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The fight is over capitalism not privacy or monopoly.md`

- **The danger is the unprecedented: we assess new threats through the lens of old experience and so are caught off guard**
  - Reasoning structure: like the Taíno cacique who could not foresee the conquest in his gesture of hospitality, we could not imagine the acts of invasion and dispossession → the unprecedented "cannot be reduced to known harms" and "does not easily yield to known forms of combat" → assessing the new through old categories risks catastrophe.
  - Evidence: the Taíno analogy; the "original sin of simple robbery"; the limits of the "privacy/monopoly" frameworks.
  - Source passage: > "Like the Tainos, we faced something altogether new to our story: the unprecedented. And, like them, we risk catastrophe when we assess new threats through the lens of old experience."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/We risk catastrophe assessing the unprecedented through old experience.md` (this is "The Power of the Unprecedented" — a running motif of the book; strong durable candidate, may already be planned in earlier chapters — see Human Review / matches_existing).
