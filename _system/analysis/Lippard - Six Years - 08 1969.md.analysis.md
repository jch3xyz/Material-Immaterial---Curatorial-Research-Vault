## Executive Summary

This is the 1969 year-chronicle (source pages 68–133): the single densest and most pivotal year in Lippard's documentary anthology. It is structured as a near-month-by-month log of conceptual-art books, exhibitions, artist statements, and reviews, with Lippard's own editorial voice mostly compressed into bracketed notes and the occasional retrospective aside (e.g. her own commentary on *557,087*). 1969 is the year conceptual art "crystallized" institutionally and rhetorically. The chronicle is overwhelmingly a vehicle for ENTITIES (artists, dealers, exhibitions) and EMBLEMATIC artist statements that crystallize the book's core concept — the *dematerialization of the art object* — and its satellites: art-as-idea (LeWitt, Kosuth), the work as information/document (Huebler, Siegelaub, N.E. Thing Co.), the catalogue/exhibition as the primary medium (Siegelaub), the immaterial/invisible work (Barry's gases, radio carrier waves, telepathy), systems aesthetics (Haacke, LeWitt, Burnham), and the early stirrings of art-vs-market and art-and-politics (Siegelaub on "power," Lozano's *General Strike Piece*, the anti-war "Time" panel). Concept yield is intentionally THIN; the value here is entities + verbatim candidate passages.

## Key Concepts

- **Dematerialization of the art object** — the book's title concept; 1969 is its high-water year. Smithson uses the literal word; Siegelaub, Kosuth, Barry, Huebler enact it. The art's primary existence is no longer the physical object.
  - Source passage: > "My interest in the site was really [back] to the origins of material, sort of a dematerialization of refined matter." (Smithson, Norvell interview)
  - Source passage: > "when art does not any longer depend upon its physical presence, when it has become an abstraction, it is not distorted and altered by its representation in books and catalogues. It becomes primary information" (Siegelaub, Meyer interview)

- **Conceptual Art / art as idea** — the work IS the idea; the physical residue is secondary or absent.
  - Source passage: > "Ideas alone can be works of art; they are in a chain of development that may eventually find some form. All ideas need not be made physical." (LeWitt, "Sentences on Conceptual Art," no. 10)
  - Source passage: > "I felt this made it clear that the art is conceptual and not experiential" (Kosuth, Prospect '69 interview)

## Definitions

- **Weiner's "Statement of Intent"** (the canonical three-clause declaration, repeated thereafter for all his work): > "1. The artist may construct the piece. 2. The piece may be fabricated. 3. The piece need not be built. Each being equal and consistent with the intent of the artist, the decision as to condition rests with the receiver upon the occasion of receivership." (January 1969 catalogue) — defines the work as idea whose material realization is optional.
- **Primary vs secondary information** (Siegelaub): primary information is the dematerialized work itself, which a catalogue can carry undistorted; secondary information is a reproduction of a physical work, which "bastardizes" it.

## Arguments

(Lippard's own argumentative voice is sparse in a chronicle; the "arguments" here are emblematic artist/dealer positions, plus Lippard's one sustained retrospective aside on *557,087*.)

- **Claim: The catalogue/information can BE the exhibition when art is dematerialized.**
  - Reasoning structure: if the work's primary existence is an idea (information), not a physical object, then a printed container carries it without loss; the object becomes "supplementary."
  - Evidence: Siegelaub's January show ("exhibition consists of the catalog"), *One Month* ("first exhibition to exist in catalogue form alone"), and the Meyer interview on primary vs secondary information.
  - Source passage: > "in the January, 1969, show the catalogue was primary and the physical exhibition was auxiliary to it. You know, it's turning the whole thing around." (Siegelaub, Meyer interview)
  - Confidence: high (directly stated by Siegelaub)
  - Recommended wiki note: `(none new)` — attach to `[[Seth Siegelaub]]` and `[[Information]]`

- **Claim: Dematerialized art reconfigures power and resists the collector/market — "no one has anything."**
  - Reasoning structure: swift global communication of idea-art means there is no coveted object to own; "power" is redefined as the ability to make things happen / reach multitudes, not dollars.
  - Evidence: Siegelaub's Meyer interview.
  - Source passage: > "The idea of swift communication implies that no one has anything. ... Physically. There is nothing a person could covet and hold on to." (Siegelaub, Meyer interview)
  - Confidence: medium (Siegelaub's claim; Lippard documents rather than fully endorses; the recuperation irony is implicit)
  - Recommended wiki note: `(none new)` — candidate passage for the market/commodity-resistance thread of `Material vs Immaterial`

- **Claim (Lippard, retrospective aside): the year taught that "concept art" was an unwanted category lumping disparate practices.**
  - Reasoning structure: Lippard resists the consolidation of a "concept art" label; *557,087* was conceived as "anti-taste," not a concept-art show; the book itself continues "to emphasize fragmentation, variety, and disparity of intention."
  - Evidence: her own commentary appended to *557,087*.
  - Source passage: > "When it originated, in the fall of 1968, 'concept art' had not yet crystallized ... I was particularly concerned not to be providing a new category in which disparate artists would be lumped." (Lippard on *557,087*)
  - Confidence: high (Lippard's own words)
  - Recommended wiki note: `(none new)` — human-review item; a rare instance of the author's voice in a chronicle

## Metaphors

- **"My work is impure; it is clogged with matter" / "an artistic disaster ... a quiet catastrophe of mind and matter"** (Smithson) — Smithson's counter-image to pure dematerialization; he insists art "has to deal with the material too," resisting fully disembodied conceptualism.
- **Invisibility / the imperceptible** (Barry) — the wire that becomes "virtually invisible," then "material which is invisible, or at least not perceivable in a traditional way"; energy "outside the narrow arbitrary limits of our own senses." A figure for the immaterial work.
- **Picture-frame convention vs language-without-edge** (Weiner, in the WBAI panel) — the rectangular painting has an "edge," but "When you are dealing with language, there is no edge."

## Symbols

- **The empty/closed gallery** — Barry's "During the exhibition the gallery will be closed" (Art & Project Bulletin 17); Lippard's *Number 7* room "apparently 'empty'"; Siegelaub's "Gallery is only a Phone number." The vacated exhibition space as emblem of dematerialization.
- **Inert gases released into the atmosphere** (Barry, *Inert Gas Series*) — "From a measured volume to indefinite expansion"; the artwork dispersing into invisibility.
- **The mailed bulletin / postcard / catalogue** (On Kawara "I got up" postcards; Dibbets, Brouwn, Kosuth Art & Project Bulletins; Siegelaub catalogues) — the document/mailer as the work's body.
- **The index card / record / telephone** (Lippard's *557,087* "95 4"×6" index cards in random order"; *Art by Telephone*'s LP catalogue) — non-object media as exhibition.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial / dematerialization vs "clogged with matter."** Siegelaub, Kosuth, Barry, Weiner push toward the immaterial idea; Smithson explicitly resists ("There is no escape from matter... My work is impure; it is clogged with matter"). The chief tension of the book, and the art-historical instance of the existing `Material vs Immaterial` note.
  - Source passage: > "There is no escape from matter. There is no escape from the physical nor is there any escape from the mind." (Smithson)

- **Conceptual / language vs object — and even "conceptual" as a contested word.** In the WBAI "Art Without Space" panel, Weiner refuses Kosuth's "conceptual" framing: > "You like the word conceptual. ... I don't really see it fitting me." Kosuth insists language use is conceptual "whether you like it or not, baby." Internal disagreement among the four core conceptualists.

- **Systems vs anti-systems.** Haacke and Burnham embrace systems thinking ("think in terms of systems"; "Real Time Systems"); Smithson rejects it: > "System is a convenient word, like object. It is another abstract entity that doesn't exist. ... I see no point in pinning any hopes on systems."

- **Dematerialization as market-resistance vs eventual recuperation.** Siegelaub frames idea-art as escaping the collector ("no one has anything"); yet the same year sees the practice institutionalized in major museum shows (Whitney *Anti-Illusion*, MoMA *Spaces*, MCA *Art by Telephone*) and Constable's review title "The New Art: Big Ideas for Sale." Lippard documents the tension without resolving it.
