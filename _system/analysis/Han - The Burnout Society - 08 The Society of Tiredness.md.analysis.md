## Executive Summary

"The Society of Tiredness" (printed pp. 30–34, trans. Erik Butler) is the closing chapter and constructive culmination of *The Burnout Society*. Having diagnosed the achievement society as a self-exploiting, hyperactive doping society poor in negativity and dominated by excess positivity, Han pivots to a *therapeutic* and *redemptive* gesture: not all tiredness is the pathological exhaustion of burnout. Reading Peter Handke's *Essay on Tiredness*, Han distinguishes a destructive, isolating, speechless **"I-tiredness" / "solitary tiredness"** (the exhausted ego of positive potency, incapable of doing) from a healing, world-trusting, eloquent **"we-tiredness" / "fundamental tiredness"** — a *tiredness of negative potency*, of the not-to. This deep tiredness loosens the strictures of the ego, opens a "between" of friendliness-as-indifference, restores the duality of seeing/being-seen and touching/being-touched, and founds a community of singularities that requires neither kinship nor function. Han threads this through the Sabbath as the day of not-to (the "interval," *Zwischenzeit*), Heidegger's *Gelassenheit* and the suspension of care, and Handke's "Pentecostal company" — proposing that the society of the future might be reimagined not as the activeness society but as a "society of tiredness." The chapter is the affirmative counter-image to the whole book: the redemptive use of negativity against the tyranny of the positive.

The chapter opens by extending the doping-society / performance-machine argument before turning to its Handkean redemption. This makes it a dual-purpose source: it both consolidates the book's negative diagnosis (doping society, performance-machine, excess positivity, psychic infarctions) and supplies its single most original positive concept (fundamental / we-tiredness as negative potency).

- **We-Tiredness / Fundamental Tiredness / Tiredness That Trusts in the World** — the healing counter-tiredness: "I am not tired 'of you' . . . but rather I am tired 'with you.'" It is "tiredness that trusts in the world" [weltvertrauende Müdigkeit], "fundamental tiredness," "candid tiredness." As "more of less of me," it loosens the ego's strictures, opens a "between," shifts "the gravity of being . . . from the ego to the world," reestablishes the duality of seeing/being-seen and touching/being-touched, and "makes lingering, abidance, possible." It is "a singular capacity," not exhaustion; it "inspires" and "allows spirit/intellect [Geist] to emerge."

## Definitions

- **We-tiredness vs I-tiredness** — explicitly defined contrastively: "I am not tired 'of you' . . . but rather I am tired 'with you.'" I-tiredness = solitary, worldless, world-destroying tiredness of the exhausted ego; we-tiredness = world-trusting, ego-loosening, community-founding tiredness.
- **Fundamental tiredness** — defined as "not . . . a state of exhaustion in which one proves unable to do anything," but "a singular capacity" that "inspires" and "allows spirit/intellect [Geist] to emerge."
- **Sabbath** — defined etymologically: "a word that originally meant stopping [aufhören]"; "a day of not-to," "a day free of all in-order-to, of all care," "a day on which the use of the useless proves possible."
- **Positive potency vs negative potency (of tiredness)** — "The tiredness of exhaustion is the tiredness of positive potency. It makes one incapable of doing something. Tiredness that inspires is tiredness of negative potency, namely of not-to."

## Arguments

- **Claim: There is a redemptive, healing tiredness — "fundamental" / "we-tiredness" — that is the affirmative counter-image to burnout.**
  - Reasoning structure: contrastive (two species of tiredness); the achievement society produces only the pathological kind, but Handke names a second kind that the book proposes as the form of a future society.
  - Evidence: Handke's *Essay on Tiredness* read closely; the Pentecostal company; the Dutch still life.
  - Source passage: > If "Pentecostal company" offered a synonym for the society of the future, the society to come might also be called a society of tiredness.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/There is a healing fundamental tiredness that inspires rather than exhausts.md`

- **Claim: Inspiring tiredness is a power of *negative potency* (the not-to), whereas exhausting tiredness is the failure of *positive potency* (the can).**
  - Reasoning structure: maps the book's positivity/negativity diagnosis onto the faculty of potency; healing lies in the capacity *not-to*, not in more capacity-to.
  - Evidence: the "positive potency vs negative potency" formulation; the Sabbath of not-to; *Gelassenheit*.
  - Source passage: > The tiredness of exhaustion is the tiredness of positive potency. It makes one incapable of doing something. Tiredness that inspires is tiredness of negative potency, namely of not-to.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Healing lies in negative potency the power not-to.md`

- **Claim: Exhaustion and tiredness in achievement society are caused by excess positivity, not by an immunological encounter with the negative Other.**
  - Reasoning structure: causal re-attribution; rejects the immunological/viral paradigm of the previous century in favor of a "neuronal" / positivity paradigm.
  - Evidence: psychic infarctions; the absence of the immunologically Other.
  - Source passage: > They are not immunological reactions presupposing the negativity of the immunologically Other. Rather, they are caused by a too-much of positivity.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: update existing `arguments/Transparency is a neoliberal dispositive that dismantles negativity` is adjacent but NOT identical; recommend new `arguments/The pathologies of achievement society are caused by excess positivity not the negative Other.md` (this is the book's signature thesis and merits its own argument note). Human Review: confirm not duplicative of existing Han positivity arguments.

- **Claim: Solitary I-tiredness is itself a form of violence — it destroys the common, proximity, and language.**
  - Reasoning structure: tiredness-as-violence; the isolated ego "fills the field of vision entirely," rendering one mute, blind, and speechless toward the Other.
  - Evidence: Handke's "divisive tiredness" passages; "drove us to violence . . . in our manner of seeing, which distorted the other."
  - Source passage: > Tiredness of this kind proves violent because it destroys all that is common or shared, all proximity, and even language itself
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Solitary I-tiredness is a violence that destroys the common and language.md`

- **Claim: The society of the future should be reimagined as a "society of tiredness" — a community of singularities founded on the not-to, not on activity, kinship, or function.**
  - Reasoning structure: programmatic / utopian closing move; the "Pentecostal company" as social model; community through a "friendly *and*."
  - Evidence: the community "that requires neither belonging nor relation"; the Dutch still life as "all in one"; the new Orpheus giving "dispersed individuals the keynote."
  - Source passage: > The "Pentecostal company" that inspires not-doing stands opposed to the society of activity. Handke pictures it as "tired to a man" (41). It is a society of those who are tired in a special way.
  - Confidence: medium (programmatic/aspirational rather than analytic)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The society to come is a society of tiredness a community of singularities.md`
