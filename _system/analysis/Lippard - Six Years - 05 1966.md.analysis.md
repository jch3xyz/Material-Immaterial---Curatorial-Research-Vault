## Executive Summary

This is the opening year-chronicle of *Six Years* — a dense documentary anthology, not an argumentative essay. Lippard's own voice is almost entirely absent (no first-person framing in this section); the "text" is an assembled bibliography and chronology of books, manifestos, exhibitions, and verbatim artist statements from 1966. The section functions as the **seed bed** for the book's thesis: it gathers the earliest emblems of what Lippard will call the *dematerialization of the art object* — Brecht's and Ono's scored "Events," Ruscha's "antiphotography" books and "readymade facts," Baldessari quitting handmade work for *information*, Bernar Venet's linguistic/logical presentations "devoid of any formalistic/aesthetic content," the first earthworks (Long, Morris, Smithson, Weiner, de Maria), Bochner's xerox-notebook show *Working Drawings...Not Necessarily Meant to Be Viewed as Art*, and Latham's *Art and Culture* (a book literally chewed, dissolved, and bottled). Recurring threads: art as *idea/information*; the *document* and the *score/instruction* replacing the object; *language* as medium; the printed publication / exhibition-as-anthology as the artwork's vehicle; ephemerality and process; and the critique of photography-as-art in favor of "neutral," "commercial," "technical" data. The year furnishes the raw EMBLEMATIC quotes; the durable vocabulary (dematerialization, conceptual art, the document, systems, the exhibition-as-medium) is *instantiated* here rather than theorized.

## Key Entities

People / artists:
- **George Brecht** — *Chance-Imagery* (1966); Fluxus-adjacent "Events" since 1960 (e.g. *Three Aqueous Events*: ice–water–steam; *Time-Table Event*; *Two Exercises*). Score/instruction as work.
- **Allan Kaprow** — *Assemblage, Environments and Happenings* (Abrams, 1966).
- **Bruce Nauman** — first show (Nicholas Wilder, LA, 1966); body works, punning photographs, films ("Films are about seeing").
- **Edward Ruscha** — *Every Building on the Sunset Strip* (1966); "antiphotography" artist books; "photography is dead as a fine art"; pictures as "facts" / "readymades."
- **Yoko Ono** — Wesleyan lecture (1966); instruction/Event works; "painting to construct in your head."
- **Bernar Venet** — "presentation and utilization of linguistic and logical" material "devoid of any formalistic/aesthetic content."
- **Dan Graham** — cosmological-to-bodily mileage list (March 1966); *Detumescence* classified ad (Nov. 1966); *Homes for America* (Arts, 1966–67).
- **John Baldessari** — March 1966, San Diego: "stops making works by his own hand and signature"; suspects "information could be interesting in its own right and need not be visual."
- **Joseph Kosuth & Christine Kozlov** — Ad Reinhardt typescript (SVA, 1966).
- **Sol LeWitt** — "Ziggurats" (Arts, Nov. 1966).
- **Robert Smithson** — *Tar Pool and Gravel Pit* model (Dwan, 1966); "Quasi-infinities and the Waning of Space"; "The Domain of the Great Bear" (with Bochner).
- **Mel Bochner** — organizer of *Working Drawings...* (Dec. 1966); "Primary Structures" review (Arts, June 1966).
- **Robert Morris** — model for *Project in Earth and Sod*; organized *Eccentric Abstraction* (with Lippard, Fischbach, Sept. 1966).
- **Richard Long** — early earthworks in England (1966).
- **Lawrence Weiner** — 1960 "exhibition" in Mill Valley: a crater made by explosives.
- **Walter De Maria**, **Daniel Buren**, **John Latham** (*Art and Culture* / "STILL & CHEW"), **Hanne Darboven**, **N.E. Thing Co. (Iain & Ingrid Baxter)**, **Joseph Beuys** (*Eurasia* / Siberian Symphony action), **Christine Kozlov**.
- **Lucy R. Lippard** — compiler; co-organizer (with Morris) of *Eccentric Abstraction*.

Curators / dealers / venues: Seth Siegelaub (not yet present in 1966 but the relevant book-thread starts here), Nicholas Wilder, Dwan Gallery, Fischbach Gallery, Galerie Fournier, Visual Arts Gallery (NY), Molly Barnes Gallery.

Exhibitions: *Eccentric Abstraction* (Fischbach, 1966); *Bagged Place* (N.E. Thing Co., UBC, 1966); Daniel Buren (Galerie Fournier, 1966); *Working Drawings and Other Visible Things on Paper Not Necessarily Meant to Be Viewed as Art* (Visual Arts Gallery, Dec. 1966).

Movements / formations: Fluxus; "IT" (anonymous Vancouver group, source of N.E. Thing Co.); Buren–Toroni–Parmentier–Mosset (BMPT, formed Dec. 1966).

- **Conceptual art / art as idea / art as information** — art whose substance is a proposition, language, or data rather than a crafted object.
  - Source passage: > “based on school mathematics, physics, chemistry and industrial drawing, and a manifesto against sensibility, against the expression of the individual's personality.”

## Arguments

Lippard advances no explicit thesis-argument *in her own voice* in this section (it is documentary). The arguments below are the emblematic positions the chronicle *stages through* its quoted artists — recorded as low/medium-confidence because they are the artists' claims, assembled by Lippard, not her stated argument.

- **Claim (Ruscha): photography is exhausted as fine art; its value is as neutral commercial "fact" / readymade.**
  - Reasoning structure: aesthetic ("arty") photography is rejected; the photograph is repositioned as technical/industrial data, a mass-produced object, a "collection of facts."
  - Evidence: the *Various Small Fires* / *Twenty-six Gasoline Stations* book program.
  - Source passage: > “I think photography is dead as a fine art; its only place is in the commercial world, for technical or information purposes.”
  - Confidence: medium (artist's stated position, quoted by Lippard)
  - Recommended wiki note: none in this pass; supports the `Information` / document threads.

- **Claim (Baldessari): information can be interesting in its own right and need not be visual — so the artist may stop making by hand.**
  - Reasoning structure: if the interest lies in information, the handmade visual object becomes optional; the signature/hand is abandoned.
  - Evidence: March 1966, San Diego — stops making works by his own hand and signature.
  - Source passage: > “I was beginning to suspect that information could be interesting in its own right and need not be visual as in Cubist, etc. art,”
  - Confidence: medium
  - Recommended wiki note: none in this pass; key support for `Information` (art) and `Material vs Immaterial` (art instance).

- **Claim (Beuys, contra a naive dematerialization): invisibility does not by itself make a work conceptual — to assume so is "a very materialistic way of thinking."**
  - Reasoning structure: a counter-claim within the dematerialization discourse — merely reversing "visible → invisible" changes nothing; the conceptual is not secured by absence.
  - Evidence: Beuys in conversation with Ursula Meyer.
  - Source passage: > “People assume immediately, if a work is not visible, it is particularly conceptual, That is a very materialistic way of thinking if one takes any thought and reverses it in itself,”
  - Confidence: medium
  - Recommended wiki note: none; valuable as the internal dissent on the `Material vs Immaterial` axis (an artist resisting the immaterial = conceptual equation).

## Metaphors

- **Exchanging menus in the air** (Ono) — wartime hunger reframed as the origin of "painting to construct in your head"; the immaterial/imagined work as nourishment-without-substance. > “when we had no food to eat, and my brother and I exchanged menus in the air.”
- **The iron plate / frozen ground** (Beuys) — "it is hard to walk and the ground is frozen"; an explicit metaphor named as such within the *Eurasia* action.

## Symbols

- **The chewed/dissolved book** (Latham, *Art and Culture* / "STILL & CHEW") — Greenberg's canonical text literally chewed, acid-dissolved into "a form of sugar," fermented, and bottled: the art-object as ingested, transformed, returned as document. A near-literal emblem of *dematerialization* (and of art-vs-criticism / institutional revolt — Latham loses his teaching post).
- **The xerox notebook on a pedestal** (Bochner) — copies, not originals, displayed as the exhibition; the document occupying the object's plinth.
- **The dead rabbit / "Eurasia"** (Beuys) — translatable symbol-system (East/West, the cross) in a process-action.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial (art-historical instance)** — the whole year stages the object's recession into idea, score, document, and process; yet Beuys's caution ("a very materialistic way of thinking") marks an *internal* dissent: invisibility ≠ conceptuality. Maps onto existing `tensions/Material vs Immaterial`.
- **Art vs the commodity/market** — Ruscha's "mass-produced object" with a "very commercial, professional feel" embraces the commodity form even as the dematerialized work is often read as resistance to the saleable object; an early ambivalence (dematerialization as resistance *and* as new commodity) that the book will pursue.
- **Object vs document** — the original drawing vs the xerox copy "not necessarily meant to be viewed as art" (Bochner).
- **Concession to art vs neutrality** — Nauman tilting the film "as a concession to art—so it looked like I did something to it" vs Ruscha's demand for "absolutely neutral material."

- **Lippard ↔ Hayles** (interpretive parallel — flag): Lippard's *dematerialization of the art object* (1966–72) ↔ Hayles's *disembodiment of information*. Both diagnose an era-defining move toward the immaterial — art-as-idea / information-as-pattern — and both can be read against the `Material vs Immaterial` hierarchy ("information... more essential... than materiality").
