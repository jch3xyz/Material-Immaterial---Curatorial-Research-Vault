## Executive Summary

"The Psibernetic Arch" is a short, programmatic Ascott essay first published (untitled) in *Studio International*, April 1970, here dated 1970 and spanning source pages 164–167. It is a piece of *futurology* written by an artist: Ascott rejects the "surprise-free" predictions of economists, sociologists, and astrologers in favour of the artist's freer "generating of futuribles" as "a creative act." The essay's governing figure is an **arch — a rainbow arch — bridging two apparently opposed spheres: cybernetics and parapsychology** ("technology and telepathy; provision and prevision; cyb and psi"). Ascott proposes a coming "**psibernetic** era / culture": a general-systems fusion of cybernetic prediction-control-communication with extrasensory perception (telepathy, clairvoyance, psychokinesis), which he calls "the most perfect form of communication and social feedback imaginable, although its manifestation is erratic."

The essay is historically important within Ascott's arc because it sits at the late-1960s **cybernetic-art** pole — extending the feedback/behavioural concerns of his earlier "construction of change" and the **Cybernetic Art Matrix (CAM)** (explicitly cross-referenced: "the blueprint for a Cybernetic Art Matrix [CAM; see chapter 3 above]") — while *prefiguring* the network/consciousness preoccupations of his later **telematic** and **technoetic** work (ESP as "instant global sympathy, a galactic sympathy perhaps"; psi+computer linkage; trance/automatism/altered states). It is the seed of Ascott's lifelong "technology + consciousness" program: here cybernetics is wedded not yet to telecommunications networks but to *psychic* communication, with art as the practice that "has anticipated anticipation."

Ascott grounds the essay in 1960s science-and-state context: the Soviet redefinition of cybernetics as the science of government (the planned "Unified Information System," 1970–75), the Leningrad Laboratory of Biological Cybernetics and L. L. Vasiliev's psi research, J. B. Rhine at Duke, Helmut Schmidt's random-event-generator ESP work at Boeing, and Herman Kahn's scenario-sketching at the Hudson Institute. The art-historical core is Ascott's claim to have "contributed the idea of a general behavioural quality in twentieth-century art that admits of cybernetic analysis," his swipe at "cybernetic camp" (fashionable hardware exhibitions), and his vision of a new *anticipatory / predictive / visionary* art — "the art of futuribles," "an art that reads like the palm of your hand," with "the critic as seer." The closing pages turn personal and practical: Ascott describes working "at what amounts to the level of trance" with random behaviour against a deliberate grid, ouija boards and a wired planchette feeding a domed light-and-sound system ("a kind of system with a psychic brain"), and speculates on "linking a sensitive clairvoyant to a computer."

## Key Entities

People
- **Roy Ascott** — author; describes his own painting practice (trance-level working, the grid, "pictomancy") and his CAM blueprint.

- **Helmut Schmidt** — did random-event-generator ESP work at Boeing Scientific Research laboratories, Seattle; later joined Rhine's lab.
- **Herman Kahn** — Hudson Institute futurologist; predictive systems via "raw Cartesian procedures of assembling hard facts and extrapolating," sketching "alternative futures" (cited as Kahn and Wiener 1967).
- **Montaigne** — quoted in closing on dreams ("It is pricing life exactly at its worth, to abandon it for a dream").
- **Stalin** — named for the suppression of psi (and cybernetics) research.

## Arguments

- **Claim: The generating of alternative futures (futuribles) is, for the artist, a creative act — not surprise-free prediction.**
  - Reasoning structure: contrast (economists/sociologists/astrologers serve clients and seek surprise-free predictions; the artist has "no client" and so is "far less constrained").
  - Evidence: the futurology-conference framing; Kahn's scenario method redescribed as "not to predict the future but to sketch alternative futures."
  - Source passage: > "For the artist, there is no client— at any rate, in advance of the assembly of his ideas— and so, for us, the generating of futuribles is essentially a far less constrained affair and may become a creative act."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Generating alternative futures is a creative act for the artist`

- **Claim: A coming "psibernetic" culture will arch cybernetics and parapsychology, and art has already anticipated it.**
  - Reasoning structure: general-systems unification of "prediction, control, and communication" across the cyb and psi poles; art as the anticipatory practice.
  - Evidence: the arch model; ESP as "the most perfect form of communication and social feedback"; the claim that art "has anticipated the fact" and "anticipated anticipation."
  - Source passage: > "there is no doubt that the cybernetic era—perhaps we should call it the psibernetic era—is upon us, and that art, however obscurely, has anticipated the fact."
  - Confidence: medium (Strongly implied / programmatic prediction — Ascott frames it as "a prediction")
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/A psibernetic culture will arch cybernetics and parapsychology`

- **Claim: The new art need not employ either advanced hardware or ectoplasm — competing with technology on its own level is futile; art expresses the process and system underlying technological culture.**
  - Reasoning structure: distinction between the *media* of cyb/psi and the *expression* of their underlying process/system; "the creativity in technology is to be found in its own advance."
  - Evidence: the "cybernetic camp" critique; "we see much evidence in art now of the futility of trying to compete with technology at its own level."
  - Source passage: > "this does not necessarily mean that it needs to employ the media of either. Neither advanced hardware nor elaborate ectoplasm need be involved!"
  - Source passage: > "Art is concerned to give expression to process and system underlying technological culture, and it will be increasingly concerned to ally this with the expression of behaviour at the psychic level."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Art need not use the media of technology but expresses its underlying process and system`

- **Claim: A cybernated economy of abundance frees humans to choose and invent — art has anticipated this leisure-to-anticipate.**
  - Reasoning structure: post-industrial / abundance premise → freedom of "personal choice and invention" → art as the rehearsal of that freedom.
  - Evidence: Soviet "economy of abundance"/"automation is a universal law of development"; "In art, the action is where the power is."
  - Source passage: > "the real value for human beings of a cybernated culture: the leisure and abundance to choose and anticipate."
  - Source passage: > "Given raw materials and automatic processing, we are free to elaborate and realise our dreams."
  - Confidence: medium (Directly stated as prediction, but utopian/speculative; note the 1970 optimism)
  - Recommended wiki note: optional — fold into `Psibernetics` / `Futuribles`, or `arguments/A cybernated culture of abundance frees humans to anticipate and invent`. Reviewable.

- **Claim: ESP, being medialess, is the limit-case of perfect feedback and even a route to galactic communication/travel.**
  - Reasoning structure: ESP as feedback without media → "more instant than instant electronic communications" → "instant global sympathy, a galactic sympathy" → out-of-time behaviour as "the only real hope of travel around the galaxy."
  - Source passage: > "Out-of-time behaviour, clairvoyance, and psychokinesis are, for example, the only real hope of travel around the galaxy and beyond."
  - Confidence: low (Speculative — Ascott's own visionary register)
