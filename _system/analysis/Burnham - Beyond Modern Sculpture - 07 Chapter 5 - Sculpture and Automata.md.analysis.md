## Executive Summary

This is the **first chapter of Part Two ("Sculpture as System")** and the hinge of the entire book: Burnham crosses from sculpture-as-OBJECT (Part I) to sculpture-as-SYSTEM by tracing the ten-thousand-year history of *automata* — "all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men." His master claim is that automata constitute a branch of **"subsculpture"** (images ranked below fine-art sculpture) whose history "has always run close to that of technology," and that this history reveals "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge": "Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically." The chapter is a long genealogy — Egyptian servant-figures and oracle-statues (proto-automata) → Greek ritual proto-automata and Hero of Alexandria's mechanical theatres → medieval clockwork "jacks" and the Strasbourg cathedral clock → the eighteenth-century golden age (Vaucanson's flute-player and digesting Duck, Descartes's bodily mechanism, Newton's clockwork cosmos, Pascal's and Leibniz's NON-anthropomorphic calculators) → the nineteenth-century debasement of the android and the rise of the **robot** (Čapek's *R.U.R.*, the word "robot" = "forced labor") → cybernetic pseudo-organisms (Grey Walter's tortoises, Ashby's homeostat, Shannon's mice) → the manikin/robot theme in modern art (Archipenko, Duchamp-Villon, Léger, Picabia, Duchamp's *Large Glass*, de Chirico, Schlemmer's Bauhaus stage, Bellmer's dolls). Two through-lines matter for the vault: (1) the **bifurcation of the automaton into two branches** — the amusing humanoid facsimile vs. the logic/programming machine (Pascal→Leibniz→"cybernetics") — which Burnham flags will culminate in his final chapter (Cybernetic/Cyborg Art); (2) the automaton as a **sociological symptom**: as a "technological population's collective obsession" it dreams of its own replacement, breeding alienation, "the cheerful robot," and Bettelheim's "Joey, the Mechanical Boy." This chapter substantively grounds the committed research-foci stubs **New Media Art** and **Art as Experiential Technology** (machine/kinetic/programmed art with reciprocal response) and anticipates Haraway's cyborg and Hayles's posthuman body.

## Definitions

- **Subsculpture** — "types of images aesthetically ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts." (Burnham coinage.)
- **Automata** — "all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men." (Burnham's working definition.)
- **Robot** — from Čapek; "comes from the Czech word meaning 'forced labor,' and implies a lack of autonomy and free will." In *R.U.R.* the robots "were not metallic imitations of men but androids, machines constructed to appear human."
- **Proto-automata** — Egyptian servant-figures and the wooden Artemis: "While not self-propelled, they do possess the option of alternate positions" / "almost liturgical instruments, working hand-in-hand with the keepers of the cult."
- **Ka** — the Egyptian "animus or soul" with which priests could "invest images," enabling oracle-statues.
- **Dromenon** — "meaning rite done or pre-drama," the earliest Dionysian ritual (Jane Harrison).
- **Mimesis (Harrison's sense)** — "the action or doings of a mime; that is, the acting out or re-creation... of an event through feelings or gestures," generating "a certain emotional state surrounding it" rather than the thing itself.
- **"Frankenstein complex" (Asimov)** — the fatalism that "tampering with organic creation could have nothing but a tragic end," which Asimov "totally rejects."

## Arguments

- **Claim: The history of automata is the aesthetic core of a Faustian, distinctly Western drive to re-create life by nonbiological means.**
  - Reasoning structure: defines automata as a branch of subsculpture whose history tracks technology; identifies "the progressive mechanization of automata" as what "characterizes Western culture beyond these other forms of subsculpture"; reads this as art converging with the desire to make life without biology.
  - Evidence: the ten-millennia genealogy; Pygmalion; the Faustian/Apollonian contrast; Čapek's "Faustian fatalism."
  - Source passage: > "What must be scrutinized... is the progressive mechanization of automata. Here the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge shows itself; Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The drive to make automata is a Faustian urge to re-create life nonbiologically`

- **Claim: The maker of anthropomorphic images ultimately works to "bring to life" the figure — but automata, alone among the methods, achieve this by literal movement at the cost of psychical depth.**
  - Reasoning structure: enumerates methods of "bringing to life" (metaphor of art, mimetic representation, magical properties, literal animation); ranks literal animation lowest in "psychical reverberations" yet "the most expedient."
  - Source passage: > "as in the case of automata, by literal movement and animation. Generally, this last embodies the fewest psychical reverberations. While it denies aesthetic-religious sensitivity, it has become the most expedient."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Literal animation brings the image to life with the fewest psychical reverberations`

- **Claim: Once sculpture is willed to move it forfeits the contemplative aloofness proper to art and becomes a symbol of spiritual fatigue.**
  - Reasoning structure: builds on Harrison's ritual→drama→art sequence; the machine, like the actor, "engages the mind on immediate terms," lacking "that aloofness and serenity which surround the static object"; thus moving statuary signals decay.
  - Evidence: Alexandrian mechanical Dionysus as "an omen of the exhaustion of spiritual and physical vitality."
  - Source passage: > "Ironically though, sculpture, once willed to move, becomes the symbol of ennui and spiritual fatigue."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Sculpture willed to move forfeits contemplative distance and signals spiritual fatigue`

- **Claim: With Pascal and Leibniz the automaton bifurcates into the humanoid facsimile and the logic/programming machine — and the second branch, the automation of thought, is the future of automata.**
  - Reasoning structure: presents Pascal's calculator as "the first nonanthropomorphic automata" and "the beginning of the automation of thought processes," Leibniz as "the spiritual father of modern cybernetics"; explicitly defers the consequences to the final chapter.
  - Source passage: > "the problem of designing automata... has been divided into two branches... the designing of logic systems and programming techniques for the solution of problems put into mathematical form."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The automaton split into the humanoid facsimile and the logic machine`

- **Claim: After 1945 cybernetics replaces the imitation of outward form with the modeling of organic behavior, producing pseudo-organisms tenuously linked with life itself.**
  - Reasoning structure: contrasts the conscious-seeming robot ("a Luddite fear of the conscious machine without ever really being conscious") with Walter/Ashby/Shannon constructs that are "working models... analogues of various aspects of the nervous system."
  - Source passage: > "After 1945 experimental research in man-machine relationships was coordinated under the title of cybernetics, resulting in electronic pseudo-organisms quite unlike robots in appearance."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Cybernetics shifts the automaton from copying form to modeling organic behavior`

- **Claim: The robot is a sociological symptom — as a population reconciles itself to "robot life," it acquiesces to government by technological expediency.**
  - Reasoning structure: links the nineteenth-century debasement of automata to "rising signs of human alienation"; the public's appetite for an "automaton existence" becomes political danger; Joey, the cheerful robot, and the totalitarian "robot" sense of the word are evidence.
  - Source passage: > "The danger is that as the public becomes bored and reconciled to the possibility of an automaton existence it acquiesces to government by technological expediency."
  - Confidence: medium
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The robot is a sociological symptom of alienation and technological government`

- **Claim: A machine can be more responsive to human needs than another human, so machines may displace people even in relational roles (teaching).**
  - Reasoning structure: economic + affective argument; teaching machines cheaper and more programmable than training good teachers; machines become "the accepted surrogate for human attention and affection."
  - Source passage: > "In the long run machines may win out because it is cheaper and more efficient to mass produce teaching machines with effective programs than to train sufficient quantities of good teachers."

## Metaphors

- **Pygmalion and Galatea** — Ovid's sculptor whose ivory figure comes alive; "the mystical equation of all art: that with hard work and faith in divine results a creation may magically become more than the sum of the artist's energies." The founding myth of the living art-object — but Burnham notes the exchange is "not an altogether happy" one (flesh decays). Cross-links to `The Cyborg`, `Artificial Life`.
- **The clockwork universe / the world as a great clock wound by God** — the Newtonian-Cartesian cosmos "operating cycle after cycle... probably never running down." The classical machine as inadequate archetype of life.
- **The "jacks" / "keeper of the clock"** — the mid-fourteenth-century jointed figures that replaced the human "keeper of the clock," ringing the hours: the moment the human timekeeper is mechanically superseded inside the cathedral clock.
- **Vaucanson's Duck (Le Canard)** — the digesting, defecating mechanical duck, "more than the outward features of an organism," "a step in the direction of modern physiological analysis": the high-water mark of the life-simulating automaton.
- **Vaucanson threatens to replace the silk weavers with donkeys** — the inventor's threat that becomes "a sustained nightmare for many craftsmen of the last century": automation as displacement of labor.
- **Joey, the Mechanical Boy (Bettelheim)** — the nine-year-old who "believed himself a machine, rid of the humanity which he finds unbearable," who must be "plugged in" to eat and have "machinery" reassembled to "live him" during sleep: "the human who finds security in actually becoming a robot." The chapter's most haunting image of internalized mechanization.
- **The toy robot crashing into the wall (Antonioni's *Red Desert*)** — the battery toy that "repeatedly drives into a wall, stops, then backs up and turns, only to crash into the wall again": modern purposelessness and ennui figured as the failed automaton.
- **The "cheerful robot"** — the derisive name for "someone with a strong sense of loyalty to his organization": the robot as figure of conformist alienation.
- **The Apollonian statue vs. the Faustian machine** — sculpture "coolly, with a sense of well-defined limit"; the machine "radiates an impertinence for everything that stands still about it. It revels in drastic change." (Also a candidate tension.)
- **The "Nine Malic Molds" / red-lead robot uniform (Duchamp)** — the hollow uniformed male types ("gendarme, cuirassier, delivery boy... priest, policeman...") who are "actually hollow containers for the Bride's lighting gas," "provisionally painted with red lead... like croquet mallets": the man reduced to a servile, interchangeable mechanical function — joined to Baudelaire's "immense cortège of undertakers' mutes."
- **The mechanized stage as organism with man in its bowels (Schlemmer)** — "a sort of giant organism, with man, the spectator nested in its bowels, getting his sensory fill while standing outside directing the feast."

## Symbols

- **The robot (clanking stainless-steel man)** — "an often threatening creature clad in polished stainless steel, possessing abrupt motorized motions, metallic voice, and flashing lights for eyes"; "Televox," "Electro, the Man of Steel," etc.; "pathetic titans, more helpless and powerful than any human being." The popular-folklore icon of the automatic age.
- **The manikin / the doll** — de Chirico's "stuffed and sewn men," Kokoschka's fetish-doll escorted "to the opera in Dresden," Bellmer's dismembered *The Doll* ("the physical unconscious," "the plaything of a 'Peeping Tom'"): the eroticized, uncanny human surrogate.
- **The oracle-statue (speaking image)** — the Egyptian stone image "transformed into speaking oracles," voiced by a hidden priest through a tube: the image animated for political-religious power; "the dividing line between magician and priest was nonexistent."
- **The Strasbourg cathedral clock** — "the most elaborate masterpiece of the art form," "a mechanical, miniature cosmos," "a monumental concession to technology": the Renaissance universe compressed into one machine, with Death and the ages of man as its motifs.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Apollonian (static sculpture) vs. Faustian (moving automata)** — quietude/limit/probable success vs. the drive to transcend natural limits, "madness... in the indefinite possibilities of technology." Maps onto the book's object↔system axis. Source: > "sculpture, in its quietude... and static order, remains Apollonian while automata possess a Faustian drive to transcend natural limitations."
- **Object vs. System / Stasis vs. Motion** — the chapter's structural role: the move from the inert, place-bound statue (Part I) to the self-animated, life-simulating assembly (Part II). The static object holds contemplative distance; the moving machine "engages the mind on immediate terms."
- **Anthropomorphic facsimile vs. non-anthropomorphic logic machine** — the two branches of automata; copying outward human form vs. modeling thought/behavior. Resolves historically toward the non-human (cybernetic pseudo-organism, power amplifier, the logic machine).
- **Biological vs. nonbiological creation (life vs. its simulation)** — the Faustian urge to "re-create life nonbiologically"; Descartes's claim that a machine could duplicate animals but not man (speech + reason) vs. the Enlightenment's subsequent pursuit of exactly speech and intelligence. Maps to `Material vs Immaterial` / `Embodiment vs Information`.
- **Human vs. machine / autonomy vs. servility** — Čapek's "forced labor," Duchamp's hollow "Malic Molds," Baudelaire's uniformed mutes, Joey the mechanical boy: the human emptied into mechanical function vs. the machine acquiring human responsiveness.
- **Ritual/participation vs. spectatorship/contemplation (Harrison)** — the separation of actor and spectator as "the birth of art and the death of ritual"; mass participation hardens into "organized observation."
- **Precision/gladness vs. distress over mechanization (Schlemmer)** — "Not distress over mechanization, but gladness for precision" — yet shadowed by Kleist's Faustian "dangers for man of such power uncontrolled."
