## Executive Summary

This chapter develops Han's diagnosis of contemporary society as a "society of exhibition" by extending Walter Benjamin's distinction between *cult value* and *exhibition value*. Han argues that capitalism's compulsion to display has dissolved cult value entirely: things only exist insofar as they are seen, and value accrues only through attention. He maps this onto a series of related collapses — from countenance to face, from analog negativity to digital positivity, from aesthetic distance to tactile contact, from eros to pornography, from dwelling to advertising. The chapter is structured around accumulating equivalences: exhibition = pornography = obscenity = hypervisibility = transparency. Throughout, Han mobilizes Benjamin, Barthes, Baudrillard, Heidegger, and Levinas, weaving them into a unified critique of the visual-economic regime of late capitalism. The central wager is that transparency is not a neutral or democratic ideal but a violent imperative that abolishes negativity, distance, secrecy, and the possibility of dwelling.

## Arguments

- **Claim**: Exhibition value, as the form value takes under late capitalism, cannot be reduced to either Marxian use value or exchange value because it is generated by attention alone.
  - Reasoning: Han accepts Benjamin's distinction but argues exhibition value belongs to a third order outside utility and labor.
  - Source passage: > "Exhibition value, which signals the fulfillment of capitalism, cannot be derived from the Marxian opposition between use value and exchange value. … It exists thanks only to the attention it produces."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Exhibition value is irreducible to use or exchange value.md`

- **Claim**: In the society of positivity, anything that "rests in itself" — that has no exhibition value — has no value at all.
  - Reasoning: Display is constitutive of being for commodities; existence without visibility is meaningless.
  - Source passage: > "Whatever rests in itself—that is, remains what it is [bei sich verweilt]—possesses no value. Value accrues only insofar as objects are seen."
  - Confidence: high

- **Claim**: Digital photography is the technological emblem of pure positivity because it eliminates the negative (literally the photographic negative, and metaphorically birth, aging, death, destiny).
  - Reasoning: Where Barthes located photography's essence in mourning and "this-is-how-it-was," digital photography expresses an entirely different temporality without negativity.
  - Source passage: > "Digital photography wipes out all negativity. It requires neither a darkroom nor developing. No negative precedes it. It is purely positive. Becoming, aging, and dying have all been erased."
  - Confidence: high

- **Claim**: The countenance has been replaced by the face — a commodity surface stripped of its aura and of the transcendence Levinas located in it.
  - Reasoning: Facebook/Photoshop and exhibition compulsion convert the face into pure visibility, immanent and same.
  - Source passage: > "The face is a visage on display, 'strip[ped] of its aura.' … Transparency stands opposed to transcendence. The face inhabits the immanency of the Same."
  - Confidence: high

- **Claim**: The society of exhibition is structurally a society of pornography, where unrelenting display destroys both eros and sex.
  - Reasoning: Pornographic exhibition strips secrecy and otherness, alienating desire from itself and the body from dwelling.
  - Source passage: > "The society of exhibition is a society of pornography. Everything has been turned outward, stripped, exposed, undressed, and put on show."
  - Confidence: high

- **Claim**: The imperative of transparency is violent: it suspects whatever refuses visibility.
  - Reasoning: Transparency is not neutral information policy but an iconic compulsion that absolutizes the visible and external.
  - Source passage: > "The imperative of transparency suspects everything that does not submit to visibility. Therein lies its violence."
  - Confidence: high

- **Claim**: Hypercommunication accelerates by reducing complexity, and is anesthetic rather than aesthetic; "like"-judgments require no contemplation.
  - Reasoning: Sensory perception is slow; transparency must strip negativity to speed circulation.
  - Source passage: > "Anesthetic hypercommunication reduces complexity in order to accelerate itself. It is significantly faster than sensory communication. The senses are slow."
  - Confidence: high

- **Claim**: Transparency replaces aesthetic distance with tactile contiguity, abolishing proximity (which requires distance) in favor of distancelessness.
  - Reasoning: Proximity contains distance; distancelessness destroys both nearness and the gaze.
  - Source passage: > "Lack of distance is not proximity. If anything, it destroys it. … Heidegger speaks of a 'pure nearness which sustains the distance.'"
  - Confidence: high

- **Claim**: Dwelling is impossible in a display-room world; dwelling is replaced by solicitation in service of attention capital.
  - Reasoning: Compulsory exhibition exploits the body and the thing, both of which require non-display.
  - Source passage: > "When the world becomes a display room, dwelling proves impossible. Dwelling yields to solicitation [Werben], which serves to heighten the capital of attention [Aufmerksamkeitskapital]."
  - Confidence: high

## Metaphors

- **The display room (Welt als Ausstellungsraum)** — world as exhibition space
  - Key passage: > "When the world becomes a display room, dwelling proves impossible."
  - Source domain: gallery / showroom
  - Target: the contemporary lifeworld under transparency

- **Pornography as social diagnosis** — society as pornographic exhibition
  - Key passage: > "The society of exhibition is a society of pornography."
  - Source domain: pornography
  - Target: the social form of total exposure

- **Obscenity as overexposure** — disappearance via too-much-visibility rather than via darkness
  - Key passage: > "things visible do not come to an end in obscurity and silence—instead they fade into the more visible than visible: obscenity."
  - Source domain: visual extremes / pornographic excess
  - Target: how things "disappear" under transparency (via Baudrillard)

- **Smooth / gleaming surface** — the transparent face
  - Key passage: > "The gleaming surface is transparent in its own way. … It possesses no deep hermeneutic structure."
  - Source domain: polished material
  - Target: the post-hermeneutic image

- **"Operation Schönheit"** — beauty/fitness regime as a quasi-surgical operation maximizing exhibition value
  - Key passage: > "Operation Schönheit pursues the goal of maximizing exhibition value."
  - Source domain: surgical / military operation
  - Target: the disciplining of the body for display

- **Prostitution / promiscuity of the look** — Baudrillardian image Han adopts
  - Key passage: > "Transparency ultimately proves to be 'the total promiscuity of the look with what it sees,' namely 'prostitution.'"
  - Source domain: sexual commerce
  - Target: the regime of total visibility

- **Epidermal contiguity** — eye and image as skin-on-skin
  - Key passage: > "'epidermal contiguity of eye and image,' just a breath away."
  - Source domain: skin contact
  - Target: the post-gaze tactile relation to images

- **Horror vacui** — fear of the empty as engine of hypercommunication
  - Key passage: > "The mass of information and communication derives from a horror vacui."
  - Source domain: classical aesthetics / psychology
  - Target: drive behind information saturation

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Cult value vs. Exhibition value**
  - Poles: existence / display; secret / visibility
  - Key passage: > "In the society of positivity, things become commodities; they must be displayed in order to be; cult value disappears in favor of exhibition value."

- **Positivity vs. Negativity**
  - Poles: smoothness, additivity, sameness / secrecy, otherness, mortality, distance
  - Key passage: > "Digital photography is the corollary of an entirely different way of living, one that dispenses with negativity more and more."

- **Transparency vs. Transcendence**
  - Poles: immanent same / the Other (Levinas)
  - Key passage: > "Transparency stands opposed to transcendence. The face inhabits the immanency of the Same."

- **Countenance vs. Face**
  - Poles: aura / commodity surface; Antlitz / Gesicht
  - Key passage: > "The face is a visage on display, 'strip[ped] of its aura.' … It is the commodity form of the 'human countenance.'"

- **Aesthetic distance vs. Tactile contiguity**
  - Poles: gaze, contemplation, lingering / touch, epidermal contact, acceleration
  - Key passage: > "Because distance is lacking, no aesthetic contemplation, no lingering, proves possible. Tactile perception is the end of the aesthetic distance of the gaze."

- **Proximity vs. Distancelessness**
  - Poles: spatial nearness that preserves distance / uniform de-distantiation
  - Key passage: > "Lack of distance is not proximity. If anything, it destroys it. Proximity is rich in space, whereas distancelessness annihilates space."

- **Eros vs. Pornography**
  - Poles: desire with secret/other / total exposure that destroys desire
  - Key passage: > "Pornography destroys not just eros, but also sex. … Pleasure on display, in an exhibition, is not pleasure at all."

- **Dwelling vs. Solicitation (Wohnen / Werben)**
  - Poles: peaceful self-possession / advertising compulsion
  - Key passage: > "Dwelling yields to solicitation [Werben], which serves to heighten the capital of attention."

- **Aesthetic vs. Anesthetic**
  - Poles: sensory reflection / numbed reflex
  - Key passage: > "Its aestheticization is ultimately anesthetic."

- **Narrative vs. Information / Acceleration**
  - Poles: novelistic temporal density, destiny / present-tense flatness
  - Key passage: > "Today's photography, fulfilled entirely by exhibition value, displays a different temporality. It is determined by the present which lacks negativity and therefore destiny—it admits no narrative tension."
