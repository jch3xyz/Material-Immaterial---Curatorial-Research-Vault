## Executive Summary

This is the foundational essay of Ascott's *telematic* turn — the 1984 text in which he first systematically theorizes the convergence of computers and telecommunications as a new artistic medium, an aesthetic, and a transformation of human consciousness. Written after his 1978 conversion to "networking" (the famous "I got high on networking"), the essay narrates Ascott's own early projects (the 1980 NEA/Infomedia Notepad project; Saturn Encounter; the 1982 planetary I Ching throw in Bob Adrian's "World in 24 Hours"; the 1983 distributed-authorship work "La Plissure du Texte" at the Musée d'Art moderne de la Ville de Paris) and then builds the conceptual apparatus.

The argument has three moves. (1) **A new medium**: borrowing the term "telematics" from Nora and Minc's *L'Informatisation de la société*, Ascott names the marriage of computers and communications networks as a paradigm change in culture and "a quantum leap in human consciousness." (2) **A new aesthetic of art**: telematic networking dissolves the one-way sender/receiver, artist/viewer dichotomy of post-Renaissance perspectival art into a single "user" who, in accessing the system, transforms it — distributed authorship, negotiated meaning, the artwork as "a web of relationships between ideas and images in constant flux." He grounds this art-historically in the tilt of the picture plane from vertical to horizontal (Duchamp, Steinberg's "flatbed picture plane," Jackson Pollock's "alloverness"/"all-at-onceness" as "the very epitome of telematic networking"). (3) **A new consciousness**: networking is "out of body," timeless, oceanic-but-precise; it points toward a planetary / network consciousness (Teilhard's noosphere, Peter Russell's "global brain"), figured through the metaphor of weaving/web (Capra, Bohm, Barthes's "text means tissue," tantra, Indra's net).

The essay is a primary anchor for `Telematic Art`, the new concept `Network Consciousness`, `Distributed Authorship`, and `Telematic Discourse`, and it extends the just-realized Burnham stubs `New Media Art` and `Art as Experiential Technology`, while powerfully cross-linking to McLuhan (global village, electric implosion, "no detachment of frame"), Hayles/Haraway (the out-of-body, networked, distributed body/mind), and O'Gieblyn/Harari (planetary brain, re-enchantment, consciousness).

## Key Concepts

- **Telematics / Telematic Art** — the convergence of computers and telecommunications as a unified medium; Ascott adopts "telematics" (from Nora and Minc) for "the medium as a whole" and "networking" for "the activity."
  - Source passage: > "the convergence of these two, in themselves vastly powerful media, constitutes a paradigm change in our culture and, without be... ing foolishly optimistic, I hope, what may amount to a quantum leap in human consciousness."
  - Source passage: > "telematics is the 'springing to life born of the marriage between computers and communications networks, which will culminate in the arrival of universal satellites, transmitting images, data and sounds'"
  - Source passage: > "I prefer to use the terms 'networking,' for the activity, and 'telematics,' for the medium as a whole."

- **Network Consciousness** — the planetary/shared consciousness Ascott sees emerging from global telematic networking; the title's destination. Distinct from McLuhan's global village (which it extends) and Han's `Hypercommunication`.
  - Source passage: > "the engagement of creative minds in telematic systems will affect human consciousness and transform our culture."
  - Source passage: > "In a sense, there is one wholeness, the flow of the network in which every idea is a part of every other idea, in which every participant reflects every other participant in the whole."
  - Existing wiki note: `(none)`

- **Networking (as artistic medium / behaviour)** — the activity of interacting through terminals; convivial, addictive, "out of body," timeless; "a shared activity of mind and a form of behaviour that is both a dance and an embrace."
  - Source passage: > "Networking is a shared activity of mind and a form of behaviour that is both a dance and an embrace."
  - Source passage: > "Logging on to the network, sharing the exchange of ideas, propositions, visions, and sheer gossip is exhilarating; in fact, it becomes totally compelling and addictive."

## Arguments

- **Claim: Telematic networking is a quantum leap in human consciousness, not merely a new technology.**
  - Reasoning structure: the convergence of two powerful media (computers + telecommunications) is a paradigm change; many writers across disciplines independently see a planetary consciousness emerging.
  - Evidence: Nora/Minc, Bell, Teilhard, Russell, Henderson, Bateson cited as a chorus.
  - Source passage: > "the convergence of these two... constitutes a paradigm change in our culture and... what may amount to a quantum leap in human consciousness."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematic networking is a quantum leap in human consciousness`

- **Claim: Telematic art dissolves the sender/receiver, artist/viewer dichotomy into a single participating "user."**
  - Reasoning structure: post-Renaissance perspectival art is a one-way, isolating, alienating discourse; the horizontal/flatbed turn (Duchamp→Pollock) and now the network resolve the dichotomy into reciprocity; the observer transforms the system by entering it.
  - Evidence: McLuhan ("no detachment of frame"), Steinberg's flatbed plane, Pollock's arena, Rogers and Kincaid's convergence model.
  - Source passage: > "the dichotomy of artist/viewer or sender/receiver of the earlier era is resolved into a unitary 'user' of the creative system."
  - Source passage: > "This grand reciprocity, this symmetry of sender and receiver, is such that a mirror image is exchanged, in which sender is receiver and receiver sender."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematic art dissolves the artist viewer dichotomy into a single participating user`

- **Claim: In the telematic mode the artwork is a process / web of relationships in perpetual flux, never complete, with no single authorship.**
  - Reasoning structure: distributed authorship + negotiated meaning + the participator-who-transforms means there can be no finished object; "creative data" is in continual transformation, like the dance of subatomic particles.
  - Evidence: Zukav's quantum dance; Indra's net; "La Plissure du Texte."
  - Source passage: > "art itself becomes, not a discrete set of entities, but rather a web of relationships between ideas and images in constant flux"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The telematic artwork is a web of relationships in perpetual flux not a discrete object`

- **Claim: Telematic discourse is decentralising and subversive — it evades the institutional control of discourse.**
  - Reasoning structure: read through Foucault's *L'Ordre du discours*: all discourse is controlled/selected/organised/redistributed; but telematic discourse is dispersed, asynchronous, invisible, planetary, so it can constitute an inclusive "fellowship of discourse" outside the closed systems of book/broadcast/entertainment.
  - Evidence: Foucault (will to truth, fellowships of discourse); the "invisible college," "floating museum."
  - Source passage: > "telematic discourse can exist outside such closed systems, or that a much more inclusive, indeed, planetary, 'fellowship of discourse' can be created"
  - Source passage: > "To engage in telematic communication is to be at once everywhere and nowhere. In this, it is subversive."
  - Confidence: high (Ascott flags it himself as "perhaps naively")
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematic discourse evades the institutional control of discourse`

- **Claim: Telematics revives ancient holistic values — the shift to the horizontal is a renewal, not just the new.**
  - Reasoning structure: the vertical/perspectival "one-way view of the world" is isolating and tied to a society bent on mastering the external; the re-emergence of holistic, co-operative, planetary interest is "all of a piece" with flatbed inclusiveness; networking is "a return to values expressed in the culture of the very distant past."
  - Evidence: Lévi-Strauss on table manners; McLuhan on electric implosion; Capra/Bohm/tantra.
  - Source passage: > "I see this shift to the horizontal as a renewal as much as 'the new,' a return to very ancient values belonging to a more holistic worldview."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The shift to the horizontal in art is a renewal of ancient holistic values`

## Metaphors

- **Weaving / the seamless web / tissue** — the master metaphor: "an interweaving of imaginations"; Capra & Bohm's "seamless web," Barthes's "Text means tissue," tantra ("meaning weaving"). Candidate metaphor note `The Seamless Web` or fold into `Network Consciousness`. (Note McLuhan already has a `The Seamless Web` metaphor — see Review item.)
- **Indra's net** — the Buddhist net of pearls in which each reflects all others; the figure of total reciprocal reflection in the network. Strong candidate metaphor note `Indra's Net`.
- **Pollock's arena / "alloverness" and "all-at-onceness"** — Jackson Pollock's drip-field as "the very epitome of telematic networking," an inclusive arena that embraces the viewer in the making of meaning.
  - Source passage: > "Pollock creates 'fields' of intertwining, interweaving, branching, joining, crossing, linking lines of energy, creating an 'alloverness' and 'all-at-onceness' that is the very epitome of telematic networking."
- **The flatbed picture plane (Steinberg)** — the tilt of the picture plane from vertical to horizontal; the surface as "any receptor surface on which objects are scattered, on which data is entered" — the analogue of "operational processes," not of nature. (Quoted from Steinberg 1972.)
- **The non-localised table-top / electronic round table** — the network as a compact, horizontal, ethical meeting place (Lévi-Strauss).
- **Out-of-body / the timeless sea** — networking "puts you, in a sense, out of body, linking your mind into a kind of timeless sea"; "more precise" than Jung's oceanic feeling.
  - Source passage: > "networking puts you, in a sense, out of body, linking your mind into a kind of timeless sea."
- **Jouissance / "coming together"** — Barthes's solitary textual jouissance recast as the distributed, collective "bliss" of telematic text; the title pun "La Plissure du Texte" (pleating) on *Le Plaisir du texte*.
  - Source passage: > "Telematic text by contrast, rather than affording a jouissance solitaire, offers the means of a 'coming together.' It is a distributed but not dissipated jouissance"
- **The invisible college / the floating museum** — telematics replaces "the bricks and mortar of institutions of culture and learning with an invisible college and a floating museum."
- **The high / addiction** — "I got high on networking" / "I haven't come down from that high yet"; Don Burgy "hooked." The phenomenology of conviviality. (Note: an affirmative addiction figure — distinct from Han's pathological registers.)
- **The dance of subatomic particles (Zukav)** — "creative data" in continual transformation, "which never ends and is never the same."

## Symbols

- **The terminal / console / VDU (visual display unit)** — the sensuous interface: "the rhythm of the printer, the unrolling of the paper, the glow of the CRT... the whirring and bleeping of control signals." The electronic space of the text/image as "a new kind of space... ethereal and metaphysical... though no less palpable." (A candidate symbol of the telematic medium's materiality/immateriality.)
- **"La Plissure du Texte" (1983)** — the distributed-authorship project at the Musée d'Art moderne, Paris; the essay's exemplary telematic artwork (the "pleating of the text"). A landmark work; record as an artwork entity, not a wiki concept.
- **The planetary I Ching throw (1982)** — the global coin-toss for the third hexagram *chun* ("difficulty at the beginning"), Ascott's emblem of networking's troubled but promising birth.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **One-way linearity vs negotiated reciprocity** — the perspectival/components model of communication (source→receiver, persuasion) vs the convergence/cybernetic model (mutual exchange, sharing meanings). Maps onto Burnham's `One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication` and the existing `Reciprocal Communication`.
  - Source passage: > "meanings are not asserted and consumed in one-way linearity, but negotiated, distributed, transformed, and layered in multiple exchanges, where the authorial role is decentralised and scattered in space and time."
- **Vertical vs horizontal (perspective vs flatbed)** — the upright, isolating Renaissance view vs the inclusive, holistic "bird's-eye"/flatbed orientation. Candidate tension `Vertical vs Horizontal` (or fold into the argument).
- **Object vs process / completion vs perpetual flux** — the discrete artwork vs the never-complete networked organism. Strongly aligned with Burnham's `Object vs System` and `Material vs Immaterial`.
- **Conviviality vs control (freedom vs institutional capture)** — telematic discourse as evading control vs the "controlling institutions" (multinationals, the military, regulatory bodies) and the "telematic imperative." Ascott's hope is hedged ("perhaps naively"). Cross-links to Han's `Digital Panopticon` / `Freedom vs Control` and Haraway's `The Informatics of Domination` (the same networks read as domination).
- **Materiality vs immateriality of the text/electronic space** — the text as "a concrete entity with its own space" yet inhabiting "non-localised, timeless space"; "more ethereal and metaphysical than paint, though no less palpable." Feeds `Material vs Immaterial`.
- **Determinism/certainty vs uncertainty/ambiguity** — some seek the precise, deterministic message; telematic creation "search[es] for and... play[s] with uncertainty and ambiguity."
