## Executive Summary

This is the second section of Haraway's 1985 *A Cyborg Manifesto* (cited from the 2016 *Manifestly Haraway*, University of Minnesota Press). Where section 01 establishes the cyborg as an ironic ontology and political myth, this section "Fractured Identities" performs the manifesto's central **critique of essentialist feminist identity** and proposes its constructive alternative: a politics of **affinity, not identity**. Haraway argues that gender, race, and class are historically constituted, not natural, and so cannot ground an "essential unity" of women. There is, she insists, "nothing about being 'female' that naturally binds women" — and "not even such a state as 'being' female." The "concept of woman" has become elusive; "woman disintegrates into women in our time."

In place of natural unity, Haraway endorses **coalition through affinity**, theorized through Chela Sandoval's model of **"oppositional consciousness"** and the constructed political identity of **"women of color"** — "a kind of postmodernist identity out of otherness, difference, and specificity," built "only on the basis of conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship." She draws on Katie King's critique of feminist **taxonomies** that police deviation from "official women's experience" and remake feminist history as a teleology favoring one's own tendency.

The section then mounts a paired **critique of the two dominant totalizing feminisms**: (1) **Marxist/socialist-feminism**, which extends the Marxian ontology of *labor* (via analogy to women's household work and reproduction) and thereby essentializes through the structure of labor while inheriting a "preeminently Western self"; and (2) **Catharine MacKinnon's radical feminism**, whose ontology of *sexual appropriation* constructs woman as "a nonsubject, a nonbeing" — "the self-knowledge of a self-who-is-not" — totalizing "in the extreme" so as to "obliterate the authority of any other women's political speech and action." Both, Haraway charges, demand the status of *totality* rather than *partial explanation*; both leave "no structural room for race." Her caricature-diagram maps each feminism's core structure with "race, by addition" appended last — a structural figure for the "embarrassed silence about race among white radical and socialist-feminists."

Crucially, this section grounds the manifesto's **refusal of innocence and organic wholeness**: "with the loss of innocence in our origin, there is no expulsion from the Garden either"; "Innocence, and the corollary insistence on victimhood as the only ground for insight, has done enough damage." "Cyborg feminists have to argue that 'we' do not want any more natural matrix of unity and that no construction is whole." The cyborg here is the figure of a *fractured, partial, contradictory, permanently unclosed* self that can still build effective, faithful, ironically socialist-feminist political unity. The closing line names the stakes of distinguishing playful from world-historical difference: "'Epistemology' is about knowing the difference."

## Key Entities

- **Donna J. Haraway** — author; writing from a self-named "white, professional middle-class, female, radical, North American, mid-adult" location.
- **Chela Sandoval** — feminist theorist (cited n.d.; 1984); originator of "oppositional consciousness" and the analysis of "women of color" as a constructed, coalitional political identity. A substantively engaged source.
- **Katie King** — feminist theorist; critic of feminist taxonomies and of the "political/poetic mechanics of identification" in cultural feminism ("the poem").
- **Catharine MacKinnon** — radical feminist legal theorist (cited 1982, 1987); Haraway's primary foil — her "version of radical feminism" is treated as a caricature of totalizing Western identity-theory.
- **Julia Kristeva** — French theorist; cited for the (date-doubtful) claim that women appeared as a historical group after WWII, alongside groups like youth.
- **"women of color"** — a contested, self-consciously constructed coalitional identity (movement/category), central to the section.
- **Marxism / Marxist humanism / socialist-feminism / radical feminism** — theoretical movements analyzed and critiqued.
- **Patriarchy, colonialism, capitalism ("advanced capitalism"), anticolonialist/anti-orientalist discourse, decolonization** — the historical systems whose "contradictory social realities" force gender/race/class consciousness.

## Arguments

- **There is no natural basis for the unity of women; "woman" is not an essential category.**
  - Reasoning structure: gender/race/class are products of historical experience (patriarchy, colonialism, capitalism) → "being female" is itself a contested constructed category → therefore no natural unity is possible → consciousness/unity is "an achievement," not a "natural fact."
  - Evidence: the constructedness of "female" in "sexual scientific discourses"; the contested origin of "women of color"; "woman disintegrates into women."
  - Source passage: > "There is nothing about being “female” that naturally binds women. … Gender, race, or class consciousness is an achievement forced on us by the terrible historical experience of the contradictory social realities of patriarchy, colonialism, and capitalism."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/There is nothing natural that binds women into a unity.md`

- **Effective political unity must be built through affinity and coalition, not natural identification.**
  - Reasoning structure: since natural unity is foreclosed and "endless splitting" / "searches for a new essential unity" fail → the alternative is "conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship," exemplified by "women of color."
  - Evidence: Sandoval's oppositional consciousness; "women of color" as effective unity "that does not replicate the imperializing, totalizing revolutionary subjects of previous Marxisms and feminisms."
  - Source passage: > "But there has also been a growing recognition of another response through coalition—affinity, not identity."
  - Source passage: > "Sandoval argues that “women of color” have a chance to build an effective unity that does not replicate the imperializing, totalizing revolutionary subjects of previous Marxisms and feminisms"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Political unity must be built through affinity not natural identity.md`

- **Both Marxist/socialist-feminism and MacKinnon's radical feminism are totalizing and leave no room for race.**
  - Reasoning structure: each feminism essentializes via a single ontological ground — labor (socialist) or sexual appropriation (radical) — and demands the status of a *totality* rather than a *partial explanation*; each annexes other dominations "by analogy, simple listing, or addition," appending "race, by addition." → both reproduce the incorporating "Western" self and silence race.
  - Evidence: the caricature-diagram ("labor, by analogy reproduction, by extension sex, by addition race"; "sex, by analogy labor, by extension reproduction, by addition race"); "embarrassed silence about race."
  - Source passage: > "neither Marxist nor radical feminist points of view have tended to embrace the status of a partial explanation; both were regularly constituted as totalities."
  - Source passage: > "There was no structural room for race (or for much else) in theory claiming to reveal the construction of the category woman and social group women as a unified or totalizable whole."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Totalizing feminisms leave no structural room for race.md`

- **MacKinnon's radical feminism constructs woman as a nonbeing and obliterates other women's political speech.**
  - Reasoning structure: MacKinnon grounds "woman" in another's desire (sexual appropriation) rather than the self's labor → woman "does not exist as a subject"; feminist consciousness becomes "the self-knowledge of a self-who-is-not" → the theory totalizes "in the extreme," achieving "what Western patriarchy itself never succeeded in doing—feminists' consciousness of the nonexistence of women."
  - Evidence: MacKinnon's ontology of objectification vs. alienation; the "intentional erasure of all difference."
  - Source passage: > "Ironically, MacKinnon’s “ontology” constructs a nonsubject, a nonbeing. Another’s desire, not the self’s labor, is the origin of “woman.”"
  - Source passage: > "It is a totalization producing what Western patriarchy itself never succeeded in doing—feminists’ consciousness of the nonexistence of women, except as products of men’s desire."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/MacKinnon's radical feminism constructs woman as a nonbeing.md`

- **Cyborg feminists must refuse innocence, organic wholeness, and victimhood as grounds for insight.**
  - Reasoning structure: there is no innocent origin (no Garden) → therefore no expulsion, no guilt-or-innocence indulgence → "no construction is whole" → insisting on victimhood "as the only ground for insight" has "done enough damage."
  - Evidence: the denaturing of "woman" ("denatures them as heat denatures a fragile protein"); the white feminist "discovery" of the "noninnocence of the category 'woman'."
  - Source passage: > "Cyborg feminists have to argue that “we” do not want any more natural matrix of unity and that no construction is whole. Innocence, and the corollary insistence on victimhood as the only ground for insight, has done enough damage."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Cyborg politics refuses innocence and organic wholeness.md`

- **Difference must be navigated, not abandoned: knowing which differences are playful and which are world-historical domination is the work of epistemology.**
  - Reasoning structure: rejecting essential unity risks "lapsing into boundless difference and giving up on the confusing task of making partial, real connection" → so one must distinguish playful difference from "poles of world historical systems of domination" → "'Epistemology' is about knowing the difference."
  - Evidence: the closing paragraph's warning against boundless difference.
  - Source passage: > "But in the consciousness of our failures, we risk lapsing into boundless difference and giving up on the confusing task of making partial, real connection. Some differences are playful; some are poles of world historical systems of domination. “Epistemology” is about knowing the difference."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Epistemology is knowing which differences are domination.md` (or fold into the affinity argument). Human-review flag on whether to mint separately.
