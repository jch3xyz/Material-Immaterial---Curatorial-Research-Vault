## Executive Summary

Shanken's ~95-page introduction does three things. (1) It **periodizes Ascott's career** into two great phases — 1960s **cybernetic / behaviourist art** (the artwork as a feedback system the spectator completes; the Change Paintings; the cybernetic art matrix [CAM]; cybernetic art pedagogy at Ealing/Ipswich) and 1980s–2000s **telematic art** (computer-mediated networked art; distributed authorship; the telematic embrace; networked/global consciousness) — and gestures at the late **technoetic / "moist" / shamanic** phase (Telenoia, moistmedia, Weaving the Shamantic Web, the Planetary Collegium). (2) It **builds the genealogy and context**: Ascott's training under Victor Pasmore and Richard Hamilton; his 1961 "Eureka" discovery of Wiener / Ashby / F. H. George; the parallel histories of kinetic art, conceptual art, mail art, video art, satellite/computer-conferencing art into which telematic art is inserted; and the lineage of telecommunications-as-medium from Moholy-Nagy's Telephone Pictures and Brecht's radio theory forward. (3) It **defends and critiques** Ascott's utopianism — the theory of love/"passionate attraction" in the telematic embrace, the noosphere/global-brain prophecies (Teilhard, Russell, Bateson, Lovelock), second-order cybernetics (von Foerster: observer→participator), and the form/content/process debate — staging Ascott against Baudrillard's media pessimism and Youngblood's/Adrian's skepticism.

For the vault, this chapter is the **bridge document** between the just-ingested **Burnham** (1968 systems-esthetics, the OBJECT→SYSTEM pole) and the rest of the Ascott corpus. Shanken explicitly names Burnham repeatedly (Burnham's "psychic dress-rehearsal," his *Software* exhibition, his account of US cybernetic art lagging Europe, his vitalist history in *Beyond Modern Sculpture*), and explicitly names McLuhan's *Understanding Media* / global village as Ascott's prophetic frame. It also names Lippard (her *Six Years* dedication page quotes Ascott's "The Construction of Change"), Hayles (quoted on Wiener and on cybernetics-as-poststructuralism), Baudrillard, Barthes, and Foucault. This is the densest single cross-link node in the Ascott book.

## Definitions

- **Telematics (Ascott's, via Shanken)** — "computer-mediated communications networking between geographically dispersed individuals and institutions . . . and between the human mind and artificial systems of intelligence and perception." (Shanken quoting Ascott, p. 232) Also Shanken's plain gloss: "Telematics integrates computers and telecommunications." Origin: "The term télématique, later anglicized as 'telematics,' was coined by Inspector General Simon Nora and Finance Inspector Alain Minc" (1978).
- **Cybernetics (as Shanken frames it for Ascott)** — Wiener's "control and communication in the animal and the machine"; from Greek *kubernetes*, "steersman." Shanken: "The scientific discipline of cybernetics emerged out of attempts to regulate the flow of information in feedback loops in order to predict, control, and automate the behavior of mechanical and biological systems." (Existing `Cybernetics` note — UPDATE, do not redefine.)
- **Cyberspace** — "the state of mind an individual experiences in telematic networks"; coined by William Gibson in *Neuromancer* (1984). (Shanken)
- **Homeostasis (as restated)** — "a state of operational equilibrium, or homeostasis" maintained by self-regulating feedback. (Existing `Homeostasis` note — no new author section needed unless planner wants Shanken's restatement.)
- **Telepresence (Kac's, via Shanken)** — "the experience of being physically present and having one's own point of view at a remote location." (Shanken quoting Kac.) Likely plain text unless it recurs.

## Arguments

- **Claim: Art is behaviour, not object — the locus of art shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour.**
  - Reasoning structure: contrasts "deterministic" one-way transmission art (passive spectator) with two-way, feedback-governed behavioural art (active participant).
  - Evidence: Shanken quotes Ascott's "Behaviourist Art" at length on the artist/artifact/spectator behavioural triad and the feedback loop.
  - Source passage (Shanken quoting Ascott): > "Modern art, by contrast, is concerned to initiate events, and with the forming of concepts of existence. The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated, Ascott's own words quoted)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour` (or similar) — the clean Ascott counterpart to Burnham's "modern sculpture... achieving... the simulation of life" and to the `Object vs System` tension. Anchor ch. 3.

- **Claim: Software predominates over hardware — process replaces product, system supersedes structure.**
  - Reasoning structure: once art is behaviour, the informational/processual layer is primary; the material artifact is secondary.
  - Evidence: the 1967 "Behaviourables and Futuribles" manifesto line.
  - Source passage (Shanken quoting Ascott): > "When art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware in the creative sphere. Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Software predominates over hardware when art becomes behaviour` (or fold into `Behaviourist Art`). Strong `Material vs Immaterial` / `Object vs System` cross-link.

- **Claim: Telematic art creates a (networked) consciousness greater than the sum of its parts.**
  - Reasoning structure: distributed exchange among all nodes yields emergent collective mind; the conceptual idea IS part of the artwork.
  - Evidence: Shanken's reading of CAM→world brain, La Plissure du Texte, Aspects of Gaia; Teilhard/Russell scaffolding.
  - Source passage: > "the conceptual idea of Ascott's telematic art—that electronic telecommunications technologies may contribute to the creation of a networked consciousness that is greater than the sum of its parts—is an integral part of his work and the history of the genre." (Shanken)
  - Confidence: high (Shanken's central thesis-claim about Ascott)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematic networks can create a consciousness greater than the sum of its parts`. Cross-link `Network Consciousness`, `Emergence`, `The Global Village`.

- **Claim: Telematic art's decentralized, non-hierarchical, authorless structure is subversive.**
  - Reasoning structure: no centre/top/bottom; "everywhere and nowhere"; it subverts solitary authorship, individual ownership, and the institutions of culture.
  - Evidence: Shanken quotes Ascott's "Art and Telematics" (1984) at length.
  - Source passage (Shanken quoting Ascott): > "To engage in telematic communication is to be at once everywhere and nowhere. In this, it is subversive. It subverts the idea of authorship bound up within the solitary individual."
  - Confidence: high (Ascott's words; Shanken positions it as Ascott's "distinctly political statement")
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The decentralized structure of telematic networks is subversive` — note Shanken's own caveat that "hierarchies are not necessarily evil." Cross-link `Decentralization`.

- **Claim: There is love in the telematic embrace — passionate attraction is activated in networked systems.**
  - Reasoning structure: Fourier's passionate attraction + Duchamp's *Large Glass* (which "always includes... the reflection of the observer") as model; the participator becomes "a progenitor of the love that is 'contained in this total embrace.'"
  - Evidence: Shanken's exegesis of "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" (ch. 16); also stages the skeptical counter (narcissism, autoerotic soliloquy, the screen as prison).
  - Source passage: > "If there is love in the telematic embrace, that love emerges as a dialogical process of interaction in which exchanges of information create bonds through shared systems of meaning and value." (Shanken's synthesizing answer)
  - Confidence: high (this is the book's titular argument; note it is CONTESTED — Shanken stages Baudrillard/Manovich against it)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/There is love in the telematic embrace` (or via `Telematic Love`). Anchor ch. 16.

- **Claim (Shanken's meta-claim, NOT Ascott's): Ideas concretize through interdisciplinary exchange, so it is spurious to credit any one field with originating a general concept — artists' writings are an under-recognized site of this.**
  - Reasoning structure: argues against the "scientific models precede art" hierarchy; defends artists' writing as theory itself.
  - Evidence: Flynt/Kosuth/Art & Language; the marginalization of both media art and artists' writings.
  - Source passage: > "the process of cultural formation depends on an interrelated exchange of ideas across disciplines such that in many cases it may be spurious to credit one field or another with originating any general concept." (Shanken)
  - Confidence: high (this is Shanken's, the editor's, framing argument — attribute to Shanken)
  - Recommended wiki note: optional `arguments/...` — likely better captured in the book/author notes' framing than as a standalone Ascott concept. Human-review flag below.

## Metaphors

- **The Telematic Embrace** — the central image (also a concept): minds clasped in a planetary embrace of love and connectivity. "one must remain vigilant that its hugs do not squeeze too tightly." (Shanken)
- **The Information Bar / "metaphorical cocktail lounge"** (Aspects of Gaia) — a bar where "the consumption of data was intended to result in greater clarity of mind, rather than an alcohol-induced stupor." (Shanken) Candidate symbol/metaphor for later chapters.
- **The screen as prison vs window** — the skeptical counter-image: the computer/cinema screen "can equally be seen as a prison, because it demands that the viewer be physically present at a precise location" (Shanken via Manovich); Ascott rejects the "window on the world" for art-as-"map of actual and potential relationships," and the monitor as a "screen of operation" not "of representation."
- **The spider dissolving in its web** (Barthes, quoted by Ascott via Shanken) — "the subject unmakes himself, like a spider dissolving in the constructive secretions of its web" — the dissolution of the author into distributed authorship.
- **Pleating / weaving the text** ("La Plissure du Texte" puns on Barthes's *Le Plaisir du texte*) — the networked text "pleated together through the process of distributed authorship."
- **The black box** (cybernetics/behaviorism) — Ascott's behaviourist artwork "functioning, in part, like a cybernetic black box," but (unlike Skinner's) meant to be transformed by interaction. (Shanken)
- **The computer as 'intelligence amplifier' / 'tool for the mind'** (Shanken quoting Ascott via Ashby): > "[The computer] is a tool for the mind, an instrument for the magnification of thought, potentially an 'intelligence amplifier.' . . . The computer may be linked to an artwork, and the artwork may in some sense be a computer."
- **The global village** (McLuhan, quoted by Shanken as Ascott's frame) — "electric extensions of the central nervous system . . . produc[ing] a 'global village' of social interdependence" in art. Direct McLuhan cross-link.

## Symbols

- **Duchamp's *Large Glass* / *The Bride Stripped Bare...*** — Ascott's model for telematic love: a transparent work that "always includes both its environment and the reflection of the observer," generating love from the "tension and interaction of male and female, natural and artificial, human and machine." Also a "proto-cyborgian confluence." (Shanken)
- **Duchamp's *Fountain*** — Shanken's historical analogue for telematic art's context-shift: moving a common object/technology into the domain of art "endow[s] the common object with a 'new idea.'"
- **The Change Painting** — the founding object/anti-object: interchangeable plexiglas panels rearranged by the spectator; "The act of changing becomes a vital part of the total aesthetic experience of the participant." (Shanken quoting Ascott's 1960 brochure) — the literal seed of behaviourist art.
- **The I Ching** — recurring symbol of the East/West, science/mysticism convergence; "the first planetary throwing of the I Ching using computer conferencing" ("Ten Wings," 1982).
- **"Good Morning Mr. Orwell" (Paik)** — the anti-Orwell, two-way, liberatory satellite telecast: "I see video not as a dictatorial medium but as a liberating one." (Shanken quoting Paik) — a `Digital Panopticon`/surveillance counter-symbol.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Object vs Behaviour / Object vs System** — Ascott's "field of objects" → "field of behaviour"; the cleanest art-side restatement of the vault's `Object vs System` (Burnham) and `Material vs Immaterial`. KEYSTONE cross-link.
- **Material vs Immaterial** — telematic art "emphasized the immateriality of process rather than the production of objects"; "the transmission of immaterial bits of digital information." Adds an Ascott pole to the EIGHT-author `Material vs Immaterial` tension.
- **Process vs Product** — "Process replaces product in importance"; recurs throughout (Bear: "the process was paramount"; Youngblood: "the connecting armature was important, not the resulting display").

New tension candidate: `Liberation vs Control` (telematics) — distinct from but resonant with Han's `Freedom vs Control`.

- **East vs West / Science vs Mysticism** — Ascott "attempted to merge categories that are commonly considered incommensurable (East and West, science and mysticism, technology and art)"; sustains paradox like "an appropriate response to a koan."
