## Executive Summary

Chapter 10, "Make Them Dance," is the climactic chapter of Part II ("The Advance of Surveillance Capitalism"). Its argument is that the prediction imperative completes itself in a new phase Zuboff calls **economies of action**: the move from merely *knowing* and *predicting* behavior to *actuating* it — intervening in the real world to "make them dance," shifting the locus of control over the future tense from "I will" to "You will." She names three concrete techniques of automated behavior modification — **tuning** (nudges, subliminal cues, choice architecture), **herding** (controlling a person's immediate context to foreclose alternatives, e.g. the uncontract shutting down a car), and **conditioning** (B. F. Skinner's operant reinforcement at scale) — and argues these complete the surveillance-capitalist "means of production," producing a fully operational **means of behavioral modification**.

The chapter then offers two extended case studies as "experiments." First, **Facebook**: the 2010 "61-million-person" voter-mobilization study and the 2014 "emotional contagion" study (N=689,003), which proved Facebook can tune real-world behavior and emotion at scale through subliminal cues, exploiting users' natural **empathy** and operating outside their awareness; plus a leaked 2017 Australian document showing Facebook pinpointing the moments when 6.4 million young people feel "stressed," "defeated," "worthless," to time advertising for maximum vulnerability. Second, **Pokémon Go** (John Hanke / Niantic, incubated at Google): an augmented-reality game that herds players through the physical world to "sponsored locations" — a real-world **behavioral futures market** ("a game about a game"), extending surveillance capitalism's logic from the screen into reality.

The chapter's final section, "What Were the Means of Behavioral Modification?", is a historical pivot: in the 1960s–70s the United States, through the Senate Subcommittee on Constitutional Rights (Sam Ervin) and revelations about the CIA's MKUltra and Skinner's *Beyond Freedom & Dignity*, mobilized to **reject behavior modification as a mode of state power**, producing the Common Rule, institutional review boards, and the Belmont Report. Zuboff's devastating point: those same means did not die but "resurfaced... as a creature of the market," now aimed not at "them" (prisoners, enemies, captives) but at "us" — everyone — under surveillance capitalism, free of the constitutional constraints that once contained the state. The chapter closes by recapitulating the **division of learning** (Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?) and the figure of users "exiled from our own behavior," reduced to "human natural resources."

## Definitions

- **Tuning** — "It may involve subliminal cues designed to subtly shape the flow of behavior at the precise time and place for maximally efficient influence." Includes the nudge: "any aspect of a choice architecture that alters people's behavior in a predictable way" (Thaler & Sunstein, quoted).
- **Choice architecture** — "the ways in which situations are already structured to channel attention and shape action." "social situations are always already thick with tuning interventions, most of which operate outside our awareness."
- **Herding** — "a second approach that relies on controlling key elements in a person's immediate context.... Herding enables remote orchestration of the human situation, foreclosing action alternatives and thus moving behavior along a path of heightened probability that approximates certainty."
- **Operant conditioning** — Skinner's "application of reinforcements to shape specific behaviors"; "behavior modification" / "behavioral engineering," "in which behavior is continuously shaped to amplify some actions at the expense of others."
- **Economic orientation** — Max Weber's term, here decisive: "In the end, it's not the devices; it's Max Weber's 'economic orientation,' now determined by surveillance capitalism."
- **Autonomy / heteronomy** — "The word autonomy derives from the Greek and literally means 'regulation by the self.' It stands in contrast to heteronomy, which means 'regulation by others.'"
- **The Common Rule** — "These standards must be adhered to by all federally funded research. The Common Rule enforces procedures for informed consent, avoidance of harm, debriefing, and transparency, and it is administered by panels of scientists, known as 'internal review boards.'"
- **Electrophysiology** (subcommittee usage) — "the use of mechanical devices to control various aspects of human behavior"; the report's horror at "devices" worn by a subject "constantly to monitor and control his behavior through a computer."

## Arguments

- **Claim: Surveillance capitalism completes itself by adding actuation — it no longer only knows and predicts behavior, it acts on it to manufacture guaranteed outcomes.**
  - Reasoning: Predictions are only extrapolations without causal knowledge (Varian: data measures correlation not causality); to close the gap toward certainty you must run experiments and intervene; intervention = actuation = economies of action; the most predictive surplus of all is behavior that has already been modified.
  - Evidence: engineer interviews ("ubiquitous intervention"); the tuning/herding/conditioning triad; Varian on continuous automated experimentation.
  - Source passage: > Now the real aim is ubiquitous intervention, action, and control. The real power is that now you can modify real-time actions in the real world.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Surveillance capitalism completes itself by moving from prediction to actuation`

- **Claim: Individual awareness is the enemy of behavior modification; therefore human consciousness itself is a threat to surveillance revenues.**
  - Reasoning: There is no autonomous judgment, consent, resistance, or self-regulation without awareness; tuning works precisely by evading awareness (it exploits empathy and operates "without their awareness"); so surveillance capitalists structurally require evasion of awareness.
  - Evidence: the emotional-contagion study's opening sentence ("without their awareness"); the self-regulation/self-awareness research literature (Wagner & Heatherton; the Cambridge "susceptibility to persuasion" scale — premeditation and commitment as resistances).
  - Source passage: > Facebook's practices suggest an equally disturbing conclusion: human consciousness itself is a threat to surveillance revenues, as awareness endangers the larger project of behavior modification.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Awareness is the enemy of behavior modification so consciousness threatens surveillance revenues`

- **Claim: Economies of action shift the locus of control over the future tense from "I will" to "You will," exiling us from our own behavior — replacing autonomy with heteronomy.**
  - Reasoning: Modifying behavior secretly and for profit supplants self-determination with externally engineered determination; this is not "more of the same" capitalism but a new engine of growth backed by a global computational architecture.
  - Evidence: Facebook's declaration of experimental authority; the autonomy/heteronomy etymology; the class-vs-engineered-behavior contrast.
  - Source passage: > The struggle for power and control in society is no longer associated with the hidden facts of class and its relationship to production but rather by the hidden facts of automated engineered behavior modification.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Economies of action shift control of the future tense from I will to You will`

- **Claim: Pokémon Go is "a game about a game" — the visible game herds players through the real world to constitute a second, hidden game board: a real-world behavioral futures market.**
  - Reasoning: Players think they hunt Pokémon; the real game's players are the paying merchants ("sponsored locations") to whom Niantic guarantees foot traffic; the game extends surveillance capitalism's online logic into physical reality (scale + scope + action).
  - Evidence: Hanke's "cost per visit" admission; the McDonald's/Starbucks/Sprint deals; Niantic's data-greedy "surveillance policy"; the Franken letter's evasive response.
  - Source passage: > The genius of Pokémon Go was to transform the game you see into a higher-order game of surveillance capitalism, a game about a game.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Pokemon Go is a game about a game that herds players into a real-world behavioral futures market`

- **Claim: The means of behavioral modification, once rejected by US democracy as illegitimate state power in the 1970s, have resurfaced as a creature of the market — now aimed at "us," not "them," and free of constitutional constraints.**
  - Reasoning: The Ervin subcommittee, MKUltra revelations, and the backlash to Skinner produced the Common Rule, IRBs, and the Belmont Report, rejecting behavior modification as governmental power; but the project did not die — it migrated to the corporation, which enjoys personhood without democratic obligation; a state dependent on corporate wealth declines to contest it.
  - Evidence: Ervin's 1974 report preface (Enlightenment/sanctity-of-the-individual); the 1966 Harvard Law Review assuming only governmental misuse; the contrast of mid-century "them" (prisoners, enemies) vs today's "us."
  - Source passage: > The project would resurface in a wholly unexpected incarnation as a creature of the market, its unprecedented digital capabilities, scale, and scope now flourishing under the flag of surveillance capitalism.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Behavior modification rejected as state power resurfaced as a creature of the market`

- **Claim: Tuning exploits natural human empathy — a "risky strength" — turning a social and moral capacity into a vector of manipulation.**
  - Reasoning: Susceptibility to subliminal cues and contagion depends on empathy (perspective-taking, affective resonance); Facebook's tuning succeeds by exploiting the empathy present in its user population; empathy is essential to social bonding yet predisposes us to influence.
  - Evidence: the empathy psychology literature; the contagion study's success; the Australian document's exploitation of teenage emotional vulnerability.
  - Source passage: > The successful tuning evident in both Facebook experiments is the result of the effective exploitation of the natural empathy present in its population of users.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Behavior modification exploits empathy as a risky strength`

- **Claim: Self-regulation depends on self-awareness; the bulwarks against persuasion are premeditation and commitment.**
  - Reasoning: Self-awareness enables a self that sets goals and can change its behavior; the single most important determinant of resisting persuasion is "the ability to premeditate," with commitment second.
  - Evidence: Wagner & Heatherton; the Cambridge susceptibility-to-persuasion scale.
  - Source passage: > the single most important determinant of one's ability to resist persuasion is what they call "the ability to premeditate."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: capture inside the consciousness/awareness argument, or `arguments/Premeditation and commitment are the bulwarks against persuasion`

## Metaphors

- **"Make Them Dance" / Writing the Music** — the chapter's title figure for herding: "We are learning how to write the music, and then we let the music make them dance." The architect composes the context; the subject merely dances to it, believing the movement is their own.
- **The Trojan Horse** — surveillance capitalism's economic orientation "secreted deep in the belly of the Trojan horse," the "Greeks" hidden behind the veil of the digital; the gift (the free service / the game) conceals the extractive aim.
- **The Music / Choreography of the Herd** — the herd "dancing" to externally composed music; behavior modification as choreography rather than command.
- **The Niantic (the doomed whaling ship)** — Hanke named his company after a 19th-century whaler "undone by greed," abandoned by a crew infected with gold fever and left "wedged deep in the sandy shallows" of San Francisco; Zuboff turns it into an omen of a new "gold rush" at the frontier of economies of action.
- **The Probe / Tiny Probe Rising from Google** — "Niantic itself is like a tiny probe rising from the immensity of Google's mapping capabilities... as it constructs and tests the prototype of a global means of behavior modification."
- **A Game About a Game** — Pokémon Go as a higher-order game in which the visible players are themselves "a wholly different kind of human game board" for the real, hidden game.
- **Eminent Domain / "blank spaces to be sweated"** — the AR game as "a for-profit declaration of eminent domain in which reality is recast as an unbounded expanse of blank spaces to be sweated for others' enrichment."
- **The Brain as Phonograph** (Dulles, quoted) — the brainwashed brain "becomes a phonograph playing a disc put on its spindle by an outside genius over which it has no control" — the mid-century image of mind control that prefigures the chapter's argument.
- **The Wake of Cash Following the Herd** — "the players in the real game vie for proximity to the wake of cash that follows each smiling member of the herd."
- **Deciphering Ancient Hieroglyphs** — the closing image of a future self "bent over the old texts of self-determination in the dim light, the shawl around our shoulders, magnifying glass in hand, as if deciphering ancient hieroglyphs" — autonomy reduced to an unreadable relic.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Autonomy vs Heteronomy** ("I will" vs "You will") — self-regulation versus regulation by others; the chapter's central normative opposition. Maps to the focus brief's `The Right to the Future Tense`. Cross-link to existing `Free Will vs Determinism`.
- **Awareness vs Evasion** — consciousness as the precondition of freedom versus surveillance capitalism's structural need to operate beneath awareness. Strong candidate tension.
- **Behavior modification as state power vs as market power** — the historical hinge: democracy rejected the former in the 1970s but has not contested the latter. "them" vs "us."
- **The simple feedback loop vs the captured loop** — the imagined fitness tracker / fridge serving only its owner versus the device whose loop is "obliterated" by surveillance capitalism's economic orientation.
- **Self-improvement vs exploitation (gamification)** — games as benign motivation versus "exploitationware"; the same components serve the user or the architect.
- **Empathy as social good vs empathy as vulnerability** — a "risky strength."
- **Public outcry / self-regulation vs structural persistence** — every scandal (the contagion study, the Australian leak) yields apologies and "new frameworks," yet the practice continues because revenues are bound to economies of action ("It did not, because it cannot").
