## Executive Summary

This is the literary-criticism counterpart to Hayles's theoretical chapters: she reads Philip K. Dick's mid-1960s novels (roughly 1962–1966) as fictional "boundary work" that dramatizes the central anxiety of cybernetics — that when "system boundaries are defined by information flows and feedback loops rather than epidermal surfaces, the subject becomes a system to be assembled and disassembled rather than an entity whose organic wholeness can be assumed." Dick is positioned as a parallel (not a borrower) to second-wave cybernetics and especially to Maturana and Varela's autopoietic theory: both Dick and the autopoietic theorists were grappling with the problem of incorporating the observer into the system. The chapter's organizing figure is the "schizoid android," a multivalent pun fusing (a) a person who acts like a machine, (b) a literal machine, and (c) the gendered "dark-haired girl / schizoid woman" drawn from Dick's biography (his mother Dorothy; his twin Jane, who died at six weeks). Hayles tracks how androidism, schizophrenia, and a collapse of "inside / outside" boundaries co-occur across *The Simulacra*, *The Three Stigmata of Palmer Eldritch*, *We Can Build You*, *Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?*, *Dr. Bloodmoney*, *Martian Time-Slip*, and *Ubik*. She binds these collapses to a Marxist critique of capitalist reification (commodity fetishism, incorporation, the "tomb world" of entropy) and to a theory of the observer that distinguishes Dick from Maturana: where the autopoietic observer is assumed rational and restrained, Dick's observers are psychotic, megalomaniac, world-creating subjects whose "outside" position is achieved only by forcing others "inside" their fantasy. Crucially for the book's larger thesis, Hayles notes that Dick — unlike Varela — "leaps over the importance of embodiment," dismissing the construction of intelligent machines with hand-waving about "homeostatic mechanisms," so that without embodiment to stabilize his skepticism, epistemological cracks "widen into rifts in the fabric of the world." The chapter is thus a demonstration that the erasure of embodiment has narrative and ontological consequences, and that the cybernetic redefinition of the subject as an information-system is registered, literarily, as a horror of reversible inside/outside boundaries.

## Definitions

- **Schizoid android** — "the coming together of a person who acts like a machine with a literal interpretation of that person as a machine."
  - > "the schizoid android represents the coming together of a person who acts like a machine with a literal interpretation of that person as a machine."
- **The human (Dick's definition)** — that which can create its own goal; unique, unpredictable, emotional, feeling alive — "a compendium of qualities that the liberal humanist subject is supposed to have," all of which his fiction undermines.
  - > "he suggests that the human is that which can create its own goal."
  - > "The list reads like a compendium of qualities that the liberal humanist subject is supposed to have. Yet every item on the list is brought into question by the humans and androids of Dick's fiction."
- **Autopoietic vs allopoietic** — political reframing: power struggles take the form of an autopoietic system forcing another to become allopoietic (made to serve the stronger's goals).
  - > "power struggles often take the form of an autopoietic system forcing another system to become allopoietic, so that the weaker system is made to serve the goals of the stronger rather than pursuing its own systemic unity."
- **Tropism** — programmed behavior; "a word he picked up from Norbert Wiener's account of cybernetic creations such as the Moth, which had build into it a 'tropism' for light." Dick's own "tropism" for dark-haired girls makes him paradoxically machine-like.
- **Kipple** — "a neologism that Dick uses for the entropic decay that has been nibbling away at the apartment building."
- **The android's defining trait** — predictability: "the android is characterized by predictability 'most of all,' Dick wrote in 'The Android and the Human.'"

## Arguments

- **Claim: When boundaries are cybernetic (information flows / feedback) rather than epidermal, the subject becomes a disassemblable system.**
  - Reasoning structure: Wiener's 1948 *Cybernetics* already harbored the anxiety in its subtext (ch. 4); Dick's androids literalize unstable self/world boundaries.
  - Evidence: textual readings across the mid-sixties novels; the techno-animated landscape (homeostatic doors, coin-demanding coffeepots).
  - Source passage: > "Subterranean fears about the integrity of the subject under the cybernetic paradigm were already present in the subtext of Norbert Wiener's 1948 Cybernetics, as we saw in chapter 4."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Cybernetic boundaries make the subject a disassemblable system`

- **Claim: Dick and autopoietic theory are parallel, not causal — both confront incorporating the observer.**
  - Reasoning: "almost certainly not a case of direct influence"; "no evidence that he knew of it"; both experimented with radical epistemologies.
  - Source passage: > "Dick's relation to the work of Maturana and Francisco Varela is almost certainly not a case of direct influence. Rather, both Dick and the creators of autopoiesis were responding to the problem of incorporating the observer into the system."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dick parallels autopoietic theory without direct influence`

- **Claim: Dick erases embodiment, and this erasure is what lets his skepticism unravel reality.**
  - Reasoning: Unlike Maturana and especially Varela, Dick "leaps over the importance of embodiment"; descriptions of bodies rarely appear; the emphasis is on perception and thinking; without embodiment as stabilizer, skepticism becomes ontological collapse.
  - Source passage: > "Unlike Maturana and especially Varela, however, he leaps over the importance of embodiment. His fiction displays the same orientation, for it shows almost no interest in how intelligent machines are constructed."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Erasing embodiment lets epistemological skepticism become ontological collapse`

- **Claim: The android stages reification in both directions, fusing capitalist critique with the question of authentic humanity.**
  - Reasoning: the android is a commodity (bought/sold/reified) and also a social being wrongly treated as object; commodity fetishism (Marx via Freedman) projects social relations onto object relations (Kongrosian "turning inside out").
  - Source passage: > "the android performs an extraordinarily complex staging of reification in Dick's fiction."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The android stages reification in both directions`

- **Claim: The qualities Dick assigns to "the human" are the liberal humanist subject's attributes — and his fiction undermines every one.**
  - Reasoning: Dick's checklist (self-determined goals, uniqueness, unpredictability, emotion, vitality) is the liberal-humanist inventory; humans in his fiction feel dead and unempathic, androids feel and rebel.
  - Source passage: > "The list reads like a compendium of qualities that the liberal humanist subject is supposed to have. Yet every item on the list is brought into question."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dick undermines the liberal humanist criteria of the human`

- **Claim: Dick uses the included observer to estrange consensus reality, where Maturana uses it to recuperate the everyday.**
  - Reasoning: Maturana/Varela posit rational, restrained laboratory observers; Dick's observers are psychotic and megalomaniac, so granting them constructive power yields darker conclusions.
  - Source passage: > "Whereas Maturana and Varela use the 'domain of the observer' to recuperate everyday notions like cause and effect, Dick uses it to estrange further consensus reality."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dick's observer estranges reality while Maturana's recuperates it`

- **Claim: Dick resolves reflexive infinite regress by naming it God; Maturana resolves it by bounding the observer.**
  - Reasoning: Maturana: "We do not see what we do not see"; Dick (after 2-3-74 and the *Exegesis*): wherever regress appears, the voice says he has encountered the infinite, therefore God — an "outside" safe from co-optation.
  - Source passage: > "Both end at the same point, in infinite regresses that, for mystical reasons, he chose to call God rather than a Maturanian reality that remains outside the compass of human knowing."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dick names the reflexive regress God to secure an uncoopted outside`

- **Claim: The mid-sixties fiction's modest, ironic accommodations are more valuable than the grand system-building of the Exegesis.**
  - Reasoning: Hayles prefers the ending of *Do Androids Dream* (Deckard's tolerance for biological and mechanical creatures; "The electric things have their lives, too") as a fitting response to the open ethical problems second-wave cybernetics raised but didn't solve.
  - Source passage: > "One could do worse than to accept this as a fitting conclusion to the deep epistemological and ethical problems that second-wave cybemetics raised but did not conclusively solve."
  - Confidence: high (this is Hayles's own evaluative stance)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Modest ironic accommodation answers what cybernetics left unsolved`

## Metaphors

- **Turning Inside Out / Right Side Out** — the governing spatial metaphor: Maturana "turned first-wave cybernetics inside out"; Kongrosian literally turns inside out; *Dr. Bloodmoney* turns enfolded twins "right-side out."
  - > "In effect, he turned first-wave cybernetics inside out."
  - > "'I'm turning inside out!' Kongrosian wailed."
- **The Tomb World** — entropic, time-stopped landscape of death and decay; the externalized form of the distended schizophrenic ego.
  - > "He plunges all the way down into the tomb world, a world where nothing but him lives, a world populated by the decaying skeletons and rotting carcasses of animals."
- **The Android as Tropism / Moth** — Wiener's light-seeking Moth; Dick's compulsive "tropism" for dark-haired girls marks him as machine-like.
  - > "his attraction for the dark-haired girl as a programmed 'tropism,' a word he picked up from Norbert Wiener's account of cybernetic creations such as the Moth."
- **Kipple (entropic avalanche)** — decay as a force that becomes "an avalanche of destruction" when boundaries destabilize.
  - > "an avalanche of destruction. Chairs crumble; the table melts askew; gaping holes appear in the walls."
- **The Spider** — the schizoid woman as alien predator "who goes about her business oblivious of her effects on others"; Pris/Irmgard cutting the spider's legs; the woman dissolved into a spider-web corpse in *The Three Stigmata*.
  - > "Louis likens Pris to a spider, seeing her as an alien being who goes about her business oblivious of her effects on others."
- **Megalomaniac expansion vs ego shrinkage** — the self either "expand[s] to megalomaniac proportions" enveloping the universe, or shrinks to "merely a dot on the horizon, an atom in a cold, pitiless, inanimate landscape."
  - > "the self is unable to expand to megalomaniac proportions, it is likely to shrink so that it becomes merely a dot on the horizon."
- **VALIS / God as the named infinite** — the regress christened as the Vast Active Living Intelligent System / God.

## Symbols

- **The Android** — "not so much a fixed symbol... as a signifier that enacts as well as connotes the schizoid"; the privileged late-20c object for cultural appropriation.
  - > "The android is not so much a fixed symbol, then, as a signifier that enacts as well as connotes the schizoid, splitting into the two opposed and mutually exclusive subject positions of the human and the not-human."
- **The Empathy Box / Mercer** — quasi-religious fusion device; touchstone of empathy that androids fail; "both fake and genuine"; tool of state quiescence.
  - > "a quasi-religious figure who appears when a human grips the handles of the empathy box."
- **The Electric Toad / Electric Sheep** — fake animal whose discovered "trap door" reveals the miracle as a delusion, yet Deckard affirms "The electric things have their lives, too."
  - > "The sign is a delusion; the miracle is a fake."
- **Der Alte / The Simulacrum-President** — the fake figurehead as "a metaphor for the entire political process"; government as "a giant android."
  - > "the entire government is a fake, its real machinery hidden behind Nicole's beautiful face."
- **The Twin (Jane / Bill & Edie)** — the subordinate "inside" twin vs the surviving "outside" twin; *Dr. Bloodmoney*'s Edie and Bill are "Dick and Jane turned inside out."
  - > "Edie and Bill are Dick and Jane turned inside out."
- **Ubik** — the shifting signifier that mutates from capitalist commodity to "ubiquitous God"; "Outside the text... Ubik must be none other than Philip K. Dick."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Inside vs Outside** — the chapter's master tension: occupying the "outside" position of an entity that defines its own goals vs being forced "inside" as an allopoietic component.
  - > "one tries to claim the privileged 'outside' position of an entity that defines its own goals while forcing one's opponent to take the 'inside' position of an allopoietic component incorporated into a larger system."
- **Human vs Android (and its reversal)** — some humans are unfeeling as androids; some androids out-feel humans (Roy Baty weeps; Rachael loves/manipulates).
  - > "If some humans can be as unfeeling as androids, some androids turn out to be more feeling than humans."
- **Embodiment vs Information / disembodied skepticism** — Dick's leap over embodiment vs Varela's insistence on it; the body absent except as female sexual marker.
  - > "he leaps over the importance of embodiment."
- **Dark-Haired Girl vs Schizoid Woman** — the same female figure oscillates between empathic anchor and cold android, more wildly the closer the male draws to her.
  - > "the oscillation between the dark-haired girl and the schizoid woman becomes more pronounced the closer the male character draws to her."
- **Megalomania vs Victimization (ego inflation vs shrinkage)** — the male subject's "agonized dance"; capitalism inflates desire then deflates the subject.
  - > "the [male] subject expands and contracts in an agonized dance between megalomania and victimization."
- **Words vs Things** — performative efficacy of language vs the resistant materiality of the real; only words cross the inside/outside membrane.
  - > "nothings can pass between 'inside' and 'outside,' only words."
- **Fake vs Authentic (reality and the human co-construct)** — "Fake realities will create fake humans"; Mercer is at once fake and genuine.
  - > "Fake realities will create fake humans. Or, fake humans will generate fake realities and then sell them to other humans, turning them, eventually, into forgeries of themselves."
- **Rational observer vs Psychotic observer** — Maturana's restrained laboratory observer vs Dick's insatiable, world-creating, power-hungry observer.
  - > "the rational observer can be assumed to exercise restraint. Dick makes no such assumption."
