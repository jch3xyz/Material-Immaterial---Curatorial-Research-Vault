## Executive Summary

This very short chapter (two pages) is Han's most direct and explicit reading of Gilles Deleuze's 1990 essay "Postscript on the Societies of Control." Han uses Deleuze's two zoomorphic figures — the **mole** of disciplinary society and the **snake** of neoliberal control society — to stage the central historical transition his whole book turns on: the passage from a power that **confines bodies** (Foucauldian biopolitics, the disciplinary milieus of enclosure) to a power that **modulates souls** (psychopolitics, the neoliberal regime). The mole burrows through closed spaces of confinement (family, school, prison, barracks, hospital, factory); it is a "subjugated subject," a labourer whose restricted movement caps its productivity. The snake does not move through pre-given space but "makes space by means of its own movement"; it is an entrepreneur, a "project," not a subject. Crucially, Han insists this passage from mole to snake — from *subject* to *project* — is **not** a liberation or an exit from capitalism but "a mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same." The whole point of the snake-model is to generate more productivity by removing the spatial limits the mole could never exceed.

Han then maps Deleuze's contrast onto his own biopolitics/psychopolitics distinction: the disciplinary regime organizes itself as a "body" (it is biopolitical); the neoliberal regime "seems like a 'soul'," and **psychopolitics is its form of government**. He glosses the neoliberal soul-government through a chain of seemingly positive, motivational terms — motivation, projects, competition, optimization, initiative, "healthy competition," "wonderful motivation" — all of which he names as "features of the psychopolitical technology of domination." The chapter closes on the snake's signature instrument: **guilt and debt** (the German pun *die Schuld / die Schulden*), which the neoliberal regime "employs as instruments of domination." This is the chapter that supplies Han's book-level architecture (biopolitics → psychopolitics; subject → project; confinement → modulation; discipline → motivation/debt) in compressed, almost diagrammatic form, with Deleuze as the named source.

## Arguments

- **Claim: The neoliberal control society is not an escape from capitalism but its intensification (mole→snake = subject→project = mutation, not liberation).**
  - Reasoning structure: The apparent liberation (openness, breaking-down of borders, the entrepreneurial self that "makes space" rather than being confined) is reframed as a productivity strategy. The system switches models precisely because the disciplined mole "cannot exceed a certain level of productivity"; the snake removes that ceiling. Therefore the openness serves capital, not freedom.
  - Evidence: The mole's "restricted movements impose limits on productivity"; "the capitalist system is switching from the mole-model to the snake-model in order to generate more productivity"; capitalism "remains one and the same."
  - Source passage: > "Accordingly, the capitalist system is switching from the mole-model to the snake-model in order to generate more productivity."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The shift to control society is an intensification of capitalism not a liberation.md`

- **Claim: Power has shifted from the disciplinary body (biopolitics) to the neoliberal soul (psychopolitics).**
  - Reasoning structure: Han adopts Deleuze's body/soul distinction to periodize power. The disciplinary regime governs as a body (biopolitics) by confining; the neoliberal regime governs as a soul (psychopolitics) by motivating. The technology of domination therefore migrates from spatial confinement to affective/motivational modulation.
  - Evidence: "the disciplinary regime organizes itself as a 'body'. It is a biopolitical regime"; "The neoliberal regime, in contrast, seems like a 'soul'. As such, psychopolitics is its form of government."
  - Source passage: > "Motivation, projects, competition, optimization and initiative represent features of the psychopolitical technology of domination that constitutes the neoliberal regime."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Power has shifted from the disciplinary body to the neoliberal soul.md` (book-level thesis; this chapter is its clearest compressed statement).

- **Claim: Neoliberal domination operates through positive, motivational terms (motivation, competition, optimization) that disguise it as freedom and health.**
  - Reasoning structure: Han re-reads the vocabulary of self-help/management/HR ("healthy competition," "wonderful motivation," initiative, optimization) as a "technology of domination." What presents itself as the subject's own drive is the regime's instrument.
  - Evidence: Deleuze's quoted phrases "healthy competition" and "wonderful motivation"; Han's list of motivational features named as "the psychopolitical technology of domination."
  - Source passage: > "it is 'constantly introducing an inexorable rivalry presented as healthy competition . . . wonderful motivation'."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: fold into the `Psychopolitics` concept and the "smart/friendly power" arguments developed in adjacent chapters; possibly `arguments/Neoliberal domination disguises itself as motivation and freedom.md`.

- **Claim: Debt/guilt (Schuld/Schulden) is the defining instrument of neoliberal control.**
  - Reasoning structure: The snake "above all" embodies guilt and debts; the regime "employs" them "as instruments of domination." Debt binds the indebted entrepreneur-of-the-self to perpetual future productivity; the German homophony fuses economic debt with moral guilt.
  - Evidence: "the snake embodies the guilt and debts ... that the neoliberal regime employs as instruments of domination."
  - Source passage: > "Above all, the snake embodies the guilt and debts (die Schuld, die Schulden) that the neoliberal regime employs as instruments of domination."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Debt and guilt are the instruments of neoliberal control.md` (or fold into a `Debt and Guilt (Schuld)` concept note).

## Metaphors

- **The Mole and the Snake** — The chapter's title figure and its organizing metaphor. Two animals personify two regimes of power: the burrowing mole (disciplinary confinement, labourer, subject, closed space) versus the space-making snake (neoliberal control, entrepreneur, project, open space, debt). The metaphor is borrowed from Deleuze but elaborated by Han (the snake "makes space by means of its own movement"; the snake "embodies the guilt and debts"). This is the single most important figurative payload of the chapter and a strong candidate for a dedicated metaphor note.
  - Source passage: > "The animal of disciplinary society is the mole." / "The snake is the animal of the neoliberal regime."
- **The body and the soul (of the regime)** — The disciplinary regime "organizes itself as a 'body'"; the neoliberal regime "seems like a 'soul'." A figure that maps the biopolitics→psychopolitics shift onto the body/soul couple. (Directly stated, attributed to Deleuze.)
- **Burrowing vs steering a course** — The mole "moves through predetermined spaces" (it is given its space); the snake "creates space through the course it steers" (it generates its own space). The spatial logic of subjugation vs. self-projection.

## Symbols

- **The mole** — symbol of the disciplined, subjugated, confined labourer; the animal of enclosure.
- **The snake** — symbol of the neoliberal entrepreneurial self: serpentine, open-spaced, project-driven, debt-bearing. (The snake also carries an unspoken biblical/temptation valence — seduction, the Fall, debt-as-guilt — but the text does not state this; speculative.)

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Discipline vs Control (Confinement vs Modulation)** — The structuring opposition: closed milieus of confinement vs. open, borderless control; the mole vs. the snake. This is Deleuze's own opposition, adopted wholesale.
  - Source passage: > "Disciplinary society consists of settings and institutions of confinement." vs. "the snake does not move in closed spaces. Rather, it makes space by means of its own movement."
  - Maps to existing tension `Freedom vs Control` (partially) but is more precisely a *discipline vs control / enclosure vs modulation* opposition. Candidate: `tensions/Discipline vs Control.md` (or fold into the `Society of Control` update).
- **Body vs Soul (Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics)** — The regime's self-organization as body vs. soul; the deepest periodizing opposition of the book. Candidate tension: `Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics`.
- **Subject vs Project** — The mole is a "subjugated subject"; the snake is a "project." The neoliberal "project" supersedes the disciplinary "subject" — but as intensification, not liberation. Candidate tension: `Subject vs Project`.
- **Freedom-as-openness vs Control** — The openness/borderlessness reads as freedom but functions as a more efficient control (higher productivity, debt-binding). Overlaps the existing `Freedom vs Control` tension.
- **Apparent rupture vs underlying continuity** — Han's deliberate paradox: a dramatic mutation of forms (mole→snake) that is nonetheless "capitalism, which remains one and the same." The figures change; the system does not.
