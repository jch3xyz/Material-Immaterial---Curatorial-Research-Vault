## Executive Summary

This is Lippard's own framing text for the book — the single richest source in *Six Years* for her durable concepts. It does three things. First, it names and qualifies the book's title concept, **"dematerialization"** of the art object, while conceding the term is "inaccurate" and redefining it operationally as a *de-emphasis on material aspects* (uniqueness, permanence, decorative attractiveness). Second, it states the book's anti-individualistic, anti-narrative *method*: the form deliberately "reflects chaos rather than imposing order," foregrounding "timing, variety, fragmentation, and interrelationships." Third — via lengthy excerpts from a December 1969 interview by Ursula Meyer — it stages Lippard's political turn and her sharpest claims about **art vs. the market**: that dematerialized/non-object art is "a drastic solution to the problem of artists being bought and sold," but that the art world "is probably going to be able to absorb conceptual art as another 'movement'" because "the art establishment depends so greatly on objects which can be bought and sold." It also articulates conceptual art as the proposition that **"ideas can be art,"** the **decentralization** of the New York power structure, the artist as **"interruptive device,"** and Lippard's discovery (via Argentina, fall 1968) that "everything, even art, exists in a political situation." This Preface is where the book's framing concepts and arguments live; downstream chapters are mostly documents.

- **Ursula Meyer** — interviewer (December 1969 interview excerpted here); also thanked in acknowledgments.

- **Doug Huebler** — sees the connection between his work and Reinhardt's "in the way he imposes an art framework on life."
- **Robert Barry, Lawrence Weiner, Joseph Kosuth, Iain Baxter, Bruce Nauman, Robert Morris** — artists discussed in the closed-/open-system, acceptive/rejective typology.

## Definitions

- **Dematerialization** (Lippard's operational redefinition): "a process of dematerialization, or a deemphasis on material aspects (uniqueness, permanence, decorative" [attractiveness — text truncated by image break]. Lippard concedes it is "an inaccurate term." This is the canonical author-definition for the book and should anchor `concepts/Dematerialization.md` and the Lippard section of `Material vs Immaterial`.
- **Ephemeralization** (Fuller, quoted by Lippard): "the design science strategy of doing even more with even less per unit of energy, space, and time."
- **The book's stated scope-limit**: "This is not a book about a// dematerialized art and the point | want to make is phenomenological rather than historical." (i.e., dematerialization is a *phenomenological* lens, not a movement-historical claim).

## Arguments

- **Claim: Dematerialized/non-object art is a deliberate resistance to the commodity form — an attempt to escape being "bought and sold."**
  - Reasoning structure: object-based art "lends itself more easily to capitalist marketing devices"; therefore producing non-objects removes the saleable thing and makes the buyer "a patron rather than a collector." Museums, being "basically acquisitive," are bypassed.
  - Evidence: Lippard's own typology of collectors vs. patrons; the structural dependence of the art establishment on saleable objects.
  - Source passage: > "The artists who are trying to do non-object art are introducing a drastic solution to the problem of artists being bought and sold so easily, along with their art."
  - Source passage: > "The people who buy a work of art they can't hang up or have in their garden are less interested in possession. They are patrons rather than collectors. That's why all this seems so inapplicable to museums, because museums are basically acquisitive."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dematerialization is a resistance to the art commodity.md`

- **Claim: That resistance is likely to fail — the market recuperates conceptual art as "another movement."** (The book's title-page promise, here stated plainly; "The Postface offers some contradictions.")
  - Reasoning structure: the art establishment's economic dependence on saleable objects means it will simply absorb and neutralize an art "opposed to the prevailing systems."
  - Evidence: structural-economic claim about the establishment; prediction of no immediate change.
  - Source passage: > "I think the art world is probably going to be able to absorb conceptual art as another “movement” and not pay too much attention to it. The art establishment depends so greatly on objects which can be bought and sold that | don't expect it to do much about an art that is opposed to the prevailing systems."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The market recuperates dematerialized art.md`

- **Claim: Ideas can be art — conceptual art returns art to "content" and undermines (Greenbergian) formalism.**
  - Reasoning structure: LeWitt's premise (concept > visual result) "undermined formalism by insisting on a return to content"; the new art escapes "the Greenbergian standard of 'advance'."
  - Evidence: LeWitt's serial/systematic work; the journals' belated admission that "ideas can be art."
  - Source passage: > "Sol LeWitt's premise that the concept or idea was more important than the visual results of the system that generated the object undermined formalism by insisting on a return to content."
  - Source passage: > "There's more chance of people doing what they want and not having it measured against the Greenbergian standard of ‘advance’, or anybody else’s standards."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Ideas can be art.md` (or fold into `concepts/Conceptual Art.md`)

- **Claim: "Everything, even art, exists in a political situation" — Lippard's political turn.**
  - Reasoning structure: not that art must *look* political, but that the conditions of making, siting, and circulating art are inescapably political; biographically triggered by Argentina, fall 1968.
  - Evidence: the Argentine artists who "felt that it was immoral to make their art in the society that existed there."
  - Source passage: > "| was politicized by a trip to Argentina in the fall of 1968... It becomes Clear that today everything, even art, exists in a political situation. | don't mean that art itself has to be seen in political terms or /ook political, but the way artists handle their art, where they make it, the chances they get to make it, how they are going to let it out, and to whom—it's all part of a life style and a political situation."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Everything even art exists in a political situation.md`

- **Claim (methodological): The book's form deliberately enacts chaos, fragmentation, and anti-individualism rather than imposing narrative order.**
  - Reasoning structure: "Fragmentation is more like direct communication than the traditionally unified approach"; criticism "clog[s] up these direct reactive processes." The form is meant to force the reader to make up their own mind.
  - Evidence: no single artist's development traceable "without the help of the index"; emphasis on "timing, variety, fragmentation, and interrelationships above all."
  - Source passage: > "the form of the book intentionally reflects chaos rather than imposing order."
  - Source passage: > "The anti individualistic bias of its form... will hopefully emphasize timing, variety, fragmentation, and interrelationships above all."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: optional `arguments/Fragmentation is more like direct communication.md` — flag for human review (method, not thesis)

## Metaphors

- **"Ephemeralization"** (Fuller, adopted by Lippard) — "doing even more with even less per unit of energy, space, and time"; the design-science figure that frames dematerialization as efficient subtraction rather than mere loss. Kind: metaphor.
  - Key passage: > "(“Ephemeralization” is the term Buckminster Fuller uses for “the design science strategy of doing even more with even less per unit of energy, space, and time.”)"
- **The artist as "interruptive device, a jolt"** — art figured as an electrical/mechanical interruption within "present societal systems." Kind: metaphor.
  - Key passage: > "the artist working as an interruptive device, a jolt, in present societal systems."
- **"Imposition of a foreign pattern or substance"** — the art framework as something overlaid/grafted onto existing information; "anyone taking a photograph is geometricizing life." Kind: metaphor.
  - Key passage: > "the imposition of a foreign pattern or substance on existing situations or information"
- **"The devouring speed syndrome"** — the accelerated condition art is "made in," which must be "not only... taken into consideration, but... utilized." Kind: metaphor. (Cross-link candidate to `[[Acceleration]]` — Han.)
  - Key passage: > "They're thinking out ways to make art what they'd like it to be in spite of the devouring speed syndrome it’s made in."

## Symbols

- **The object (the saleable thing)** — the recurring antagonist of the Preface: the hangable/garden-able commodity, the thing museums acquire. "It's still an object" / "he's finally got past the object" used as value judgments Lippard wants to dissolve.
  - > "It isn't really a matter of how much materiality a work has, but what the artist is doing with it."
- **The index** — emblem of the book's anti-individualist form: the only place a single artist's "sequential development" can be reconstructed.
- **The "other culture" / "alternative information network"** — recurring figure for a decentralized art world outside the New York gallery-money-power structure.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial (art-historical instance).** The central axis: dematerialization vs. the persistent objecthood of even "a piece of paper or a photograph." Lippard concedes the immaterial pole is never literally immaterial — exactly the structure of `tensions/Material vs Immaterial.md`. This is the strongest cross-book link.
  - > "It isn't really a matter of how much materiality a work has, but what the artist is doing with it."
- **Resistance vs Recuperation (commodity).** Non-object art as "a drastic solution" to being "bought and sold" vs. the prediction that the establishment will "absorb conceptual art as another 'movement'." The unresolved engine of the whole book ("The Postface offers some contradictions").
- **Open vs Closed systems / acceptive vs rejective.** Lippard's typology: Weiner/Kosuth (and less so Barry/Huebler) retain "Art," work "in a straight, definite line and exclude," a "formal or structural point of view" (closed); Morris/Baxter/Nauman take "an acceptive instead of a rejective approach" (Dada-Surrealist, open). "Fundamentally it's a matter of degree of acceptance."
  - > "in the case of Barry or Huebler or Weiner, who use non-art, immaterial situations, it’s the imposition of a closed instead of an open system."
- **Centralization vs Decentralization.** New York as indispensable "center because of the stimulus here" vs. dematerialized art as the means to "get the power structure out of New York."
- **Order vs Chaos (form).** The book's deliberate refusal to "impos[e] order"; fragmentation valued over "the traditionally unified approach."

## References Between Authors

- **Lippard ↔ Hayles (striking interpretive parallel).** Lippard's *dematerialization of the art object* (1967–68) directly parallels Hayles's *disembodiment of information* / "the body's dematerialization." Both insist the "immaterial" is never literally immaterial: Lippard concedes paper and photographs are "as much an object... as a ton of lead"; Hayles insists information "must always be instantiated in a medium." Both treat dematerialization as an *ideology* with material conditions it obscures (Lippard: the market; Hayles: "the embodied circumstances that an ideology of dematerialization would obscure"). FLAG for human review as a headline cross-book link.
- **Lippard ↔ Han.** Lippard's "devouring speed syndrome" ↔ Han's `[[Acceleration]]`; her "alternative information network" / "other culture" and the prediction of market *absorption* ↔ Han's account of systems that "absorb" and level resistance. Implicit, not cited.
- **Lippard → Buckminster Fuller** — explicit borrowing of "Ephemeralization."
- **Lippard → Sol LeWitt** — explicit attribution of the concept-over-object premise.
- **Lippard → Ad Reinhardt / Clement Greenberg** — Reinhardt as "ending point"; Greenberg as the "standard of 'advance'" the new art escapes.
- **Lippard → John Latham / APG** — the "interruptive device" idea.
