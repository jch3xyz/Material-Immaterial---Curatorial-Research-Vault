## Executive Summary

Chapter 1 opens Part One ("Sculpture as Object") by reading the entire transformation of modern sculpture through a single, seemingly minor element: the sculpture base (plinth, pedestal, base). Burnham argues that the steady decline, withering, and final disappearance of the base is "symptomatic" of a much larger transformation — the movement of sculpture away from the inert, gravity-bound, place-rooted, idealized object toward something mobile, environmental, and finally toward "a physical system including invisible forces." The base is the sculptor's convention for rooting art to reality while keeping it apart; its function is biological (it reaffirms that mammals have a top/bottom, front/back, and gain mobility by contact with the earth) and psychic (it confers an aura of distance, dignity, presence, transcendence). As modern abstraction proceeds, the base "withers away" in a "secularization" of sculpture: the transcendent "art object" is replaced by objects accepted "for what they are, not for what they represent."

Burnham traces this through a long evolutionary sequence: Degas's tableau bases (the tub as base), Rosso's fusion of base and figure, Rodin's craggy unified plinth for the Burghers, Boccioni's Futurist base-as-launching-pad, Duchamp's Readymade (a "Goedel's Theorem of aesthetics"), Brancusi's animist bases that ARE sculpture, the Constructivists' (Gabo, Pevsner) engineering-derived dematerialization of support, the postwar "withering" (Calder, Lippold, Bertoia), floor-bound and Minimal/"Object" sculpture (Judd, Morris) that is "solid, palpable, and real," ceiling-bound sculpture (Grosvenor), and finally "air-borne" sculpture — tensegrity (Snelson/Fuller), Takis's telemagnetic/levitating works, and Alberto Collie's electromagnetically levitated disk held by a feedback signal, where "the base has vanished altogether!" The chapter's punchline is the chapter-and-book thesis in miniature: a sculpture freed from its base "becomes a different animal," whose raison d'être "is no longer that it embodies formal qualities, but that it exists as a physical system including invisible forces. The duality between matter and energy enters a new phase." This is the OBJECT → SYSTEM transition (the vault's titular Material/Immaterial axis) stated at the level of the base, and it bridges directly forward to Lippard's dematerialization (Herbert Read's "tortuous dematerialization" idiom lives in this milieu) and to the later cybernetic/kinetic/light chapters.

This is a dense, example-rich chapter. The durable conceptual yield: the base as biological/psychic orienting device; the secularization/de-idealization of the sculptural object; the object→system transition framed as the matter/energy duality entering a new phase; the Readymade as an idealism-of-idea and a Gödelian proof of art's provisionality; the base-as-anti-base dialectic; Brancusi's animism (the base as living habitat / the flowering-plant analogy); the Constructivist program of releasing mass from gravity via invisible engineering; the feedback-stabilized levitating sculpture (an early systems/cybernetic artwork in the book's opening pages); and the gravity/mobility, matter/energy, object/system tensions.

## Arguments

- **Claim: The disappearance of the base is symptomatic of sculpture's fundamental transformation from idealized object to mobile, non-idealistic art.**
  - Reasoning structure: the base encodes fixity, gravity-dependence, biological immobility, and idealizing transcendence; as avant-garde sculptors pursue spatial mobility and a "non-idealistic art," the base loses its reason for being. Evolutionary sequence of examples substantiates.
  - Evidence: Degas → Rosso → Rodin → Boccioni → Brancusi → Constructivists → postwar/floor-bound → air-borne.
  - Source passage: > "This chapter will show through successive examples that the sculptural will toward spatial mobility and a non-idealistic art are intimately tied to innovations in the function of the base."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The vanishing base is symptomatic of sculpture's transformation`

- **Claim: A sculpture freed from its base ceases to be a formal object and becomes a physical system of forces — the matter/energy duality enters a new phase.**
  - Reasoning structure: once levitation/feedback (Collie) removes the last physical support, the artwork's reason for being shifts from embodying formal qualities to existing as a system including invisible forces. This is the OBJECT→SYSTEM thesis stated at the level of the base.
  - Evidence: Collie's feedback-stabilized levitating disk; Takis's telemagnetic works; the forward reference to "sculpture as system."
  - Source passage: > "Its raison d'etre is no longer that it embodies formal qualities, but that it exists as a physical system including invisible forces. The duality between matter and energy enters a new phase."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/A base-less sculpture becomes a system of forces not a formal object`

- **Claim: The base is a biological/orientational device, so its removal is an attempt to defeat sculpture's "biological immobility" and gravitational dependence.**
  - Reasoning structure: traditional sculpture is "virtually life that cannot move"; the base ordains fixity rooted in mammalian anatomy; avant-garde sculptors collectively work to "disturb this traditional biological immobility."
  - Evidence: the bird-leaving-the-nest analogy; the gravity-defying trend.
  - Source passage: > "Traditional sculpture is virtually life that cannot move. Consequently it is given nowhere to go."
  - Source passage: > "there seemed to be a collective consciousness at work among avant-garde sculptors working both to disturb this traditional biological immobility, this fixity which the base ordains, and the sense of gravitational dependence so inherent in the anatomy of man."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The base encodes biological immobility and gravity that modern sculpture works to defeat`

- **Claim: The withering of the base is the "secularization" of sculpture — the loss of the transcendent art-object and its replacement by things accepted for what they are.**
  - Reasoning structure: the modern sensibility breaks the "psychic barrier"/object-viewer relationship that produced sculpture's transcendence, substituting an environment of "like status" between observer and object.
  - Evidence: base-less sculpture "functions with all the casualness of an umbrella stand or the early-morning fog over a field."
  - Source passage: > "the destruction or withering away of the base is very much a part of the 'secularization' of sculpture."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The withering of the base secularizes and de-idealizes sculpture`

- **Claim: Once any object on a base becomes sculpture, the base destroys itself as a means of identification — and the base can itself become sculpture.**
  - Reasoning structure: a dialectical dilemma — if setting any object on a base makes it sculpture, all objects are sculpture, and the base's identifying function collapses; conversely the base, the last substantial element, can be promoted to sculpture (Brancusi/Duchamp).
  - Source passage: > "That sculpture could be created by setting any object upon a base became a chief dialectical dilemma of modern sculpture. All objects, thereby, could be considered sculpture."
  - Source passage: > "If Brancusi can incorporate the base into his sculptures, what is to prevent the base, in later hands, from becoming sculpture?"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/If any object on a base is sculpture the base destroys its own meaning`

- **Claim: The Readymade is a Gödelian proof of the provisional, undecidable quality of art.**
  - Reasoning structure: the Readymade's irony is its idealism (all idea, no technique); rather than expiring, it became pervasive and "proved the provisional quality of art" — analogized to Gödel's incompleteness.
  - Source passage: > "a sort of Goedel's Theorem of aesthetics, which has proved the provisional quality of art."
  - Confidence: medium (it is Burnham's stated analogy; the "provisional quality of art" is the durable claim)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The Readymade is a Goedel's Theorem of aesthetics`

- **Claim: Constructivist sculpture dematerialized its support by importing the conceptual models of science and engineering, not merely by imitating flight.**
  - Reasoning structure: Burnham explicitly weighs "heavier-than-air flight" against "the conceptual models of science" and privileges the latter — a clean instance of his book thesis (science's form-world reshaping sculptural sensibility).
  - Source passage: > "more important were the conceptual models of science."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Constructivism dematerialized support by importing the conceptual models of science`

## Metaphors

- **The base as a "twilight zone" (physical and psychic)** — > "the base creates a twilight zone both physically and psychically." A threshold/liminal image for the base's double function.
- **The bird leaving the nest / coming down from the trees** — the evolutionary-biological analogy for the base's obsolescence. > "Imagine the growth of a young bird in the nest; finally it climbs out of the nest and begins to use its feet and wings... another species of birds may have decided to come out of the trees and walk along the ground." Recommend as metaphor: `The Bird Leaving the Nest`.
- **The flowering plant (subject and base)** — Brancusi's base/subject relation as stem-flower-root; severing roots is "an offense to both biology and art." (See Brancusi animism concept.)
- **"A different animal"** — the base-less sculpture as a new species of art-being. > "with sculpture completely liberated from its base it becomes a different animal!"
- **The egg with the hidden base-point** — Collie's image (quoted by Burnham) for why levitation lets the whole form be seen: > "It is like describing an egg. No matter how much of an egg a person can see, there is still a tiny place at the base where it cannot be seen." (Burnham-quoted, not Burnham's own.)
- **The base as launching pad / stage set** — Boccioni's blocks: > "The base no longer circumscribes activity but serves as a launching pad for all movement." And earlier: the base "functions as a stage set, a baroque platform impelling flux and activity."
- **Bachelard's "immensity is the movement of a motionless man"** — quoted from The Poetics of Space on intimate immensity; used to argue tiny Giacometti figures can outweigh room-size environments. > "Indeed, immensity is the movement of a motionless man." (Bachelard-quoted.)
- **Gabo's substructure as "roots" / the construction as a tree** — Bijenkorf Monument: Gabo's steel-concrete substructure called the "roots," the twisting structure "as a tree which would be the symbol of growth and regeneration." Burnham's gloss is a key seed of the book's organic/technological convergence: > "It is perhaps providential that one of the first artists to fully employ technological means should seize upon an organic metaphor as a symbol of regeneration."
- **"Pockets of interior space" / "craggy slab... Cubist relief"** — the Burghers' plinth imagery (texture/space metaphors; lesser durability).
