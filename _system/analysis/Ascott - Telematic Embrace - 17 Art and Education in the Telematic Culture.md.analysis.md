## Executive Summary

This 1988 essay is one of Ascott's foundational statements of **telematic art** and the theory of the **telematic culture**. It opens with the etymology and history of "telematics" (Simon Nora's coinage; the Nora–Minc report to the French president; the rise of Minitel as the first mass videotex system), then mounts its central thesis: **interactivity is the essence of all telematic systems**, and therefore art is not an object but a **process, system, behaviour, and interaction** in which meaning is co-created by participants — what Ascott, drawing on Maturana/Varela and Rogers/Kincaid, calls **dispersed authorship**.

The essay's polemical spine is a historical contrast between the **Renaissance paradigm** (one-way, perspectival, vanishing-point space that places the viewer outside the work and the artist outside the world, mirroring a one-way social pyramid of information flow) and the emergent **telematic / network paradigm** (mutual, convergent, planetary, participatory). Ascott marshals a "science of wholeness" — Bateson's ecology of mind ("human plus computer system plus environment constitutes a thinking system"), Bertalanffy's general system theory, quantum physics' "undivided wholeness," and Peter Russell's global brain — to argue that the convergence of computers and telecommunications makes the "thinking system" **planetary**, approaching **global consciousness**. He frames the planet at Lacan's *stade du miroir* (mirror stage), seeing its own whole-earth image beamed back from space.

The second half turns to **art education and institutions**. Ascott diagnoses the problem as **isolation** (the crude separation of atelier, museum, library, concert hall, academy) and proposes **convergence** as the remedy. He attacks the **life drawing class** as the central anomaly of the art academy — "the body immobilised, without mind . . . the ideal of all repressive cultures" — calling instead for hand-to-mind over hand-to-eye coordination and for **technological extensions of the senses**. He casts the computer as a **matrix** (a universal machine, the "lingua franca" of digitisation) and anticipates the **dematerialisation of academies, galleries, and museums** into pervasive networks, electronic "traces," worldwide electronic seminars, and learning networks. He warns against the **homogenisation of culture** (telematics must instead generate difference and diversity) and against using telematic systems "merely as tools of production" (Socrates over Cato: critical reflection over productivity). He closes calling for artists, designers, scientists, and politicians to work together to "create cybernetic systems" supporting new art practice — and foresees the "symbiosis of computers and human beings and the integration of natural and artificial intelligence."

This is a primary-text anchor for **Telematic Art**, **Network Consciousness**, **Dispersed Authorship**, and **The Telematic Embrace** cluster, and it is a strong EXTENSION source for the Burnham-realized committed stubs `New Media Art` and `Art as Experiential Technology`, for `Distributed Cognition`, `The Global Village`, `Material vs Immaterial` / `Object vs System`, `Systems Theory`, `Cybernetics`, `Dematerialization`, and `Reciprocal Communication`.

- **Everett M. Rogers** and **D. Lawrence Kincaid** (text says "Kinkaid"/"Kincaid") — network/convergence model of communication (*Communication Networks*, 1981).

- **Gregory Bateson** — "ecology of mind"; "human plus computer system plus environment constitutes a thinking system."
- **Ludwig von Bertalanffy** — general system theory.
- **Peter Russell** — the global brain / global interaction approaching a level of complexity comparable to the human nervous system (*The Global Brain*, 1983).
- **Jacques Lacan** — the *stade du miroir* (mirror stage), applied to the planet gaining self-awareness.
- **Marcel Duchamp** — named as having brought "the power of context and new position of the observer as participant to our attention" (but "it was not Duchamp alone"). Existing forward-stub.
- **Socrates** vs **Cato** — the closing antithesis: critical reflection/personal development/sustained enquiry vs estimating everything by what it produces.

Institutions / technologies / places:

- The Paris **Métro** (Minitel advertising).

## Arguments

- **Claim: Art is not an object but a process and system; meaning is created in interaction, not transmitted.**
  - Reasoning structure: imports the communication-theory critique of the linear "components"/"tube" model (Rogers–Kincaid's convergence model; Maturana–Varela's structural determinism — "communication depends on not what is transmitted, but what happens to the person who receives it") and applies it to art.
  - Evidence: > "Art does not reside in the object alone, nor is meaning fixed or stable within the physical limits of the artist's work. Art is all process, all system."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Art is all process all system not an object` (and extend `Object vs System`, `Systems Esthetics`).

- **Claim: The Renaissance perspectival paradigm encodes a one-way social pyramid of control, which the telematic network paradigm overturns.**
  - Reasoning structure: reads the vanishing point and rules of representation as a "perfect metaphor of the ordering of parts" in pyramidal societies where information flows one way from apex to base, fashioning consciousness and enforcing a dominant scientific paradigm; the telematic network restores mutuality and dissolves the producer/consumer (artist/viewer) and observer/world distinctions.
  - Evidence: > "Renaissance space is authorised as 'real' space by many of those societies in which information flows one way, from the apex of the social pyramid to the base"
  - Evidence: > "The primary effect of creative interaction within such networks is to render obsolete the distinction in absolute terms between the artist and viewer as producer and consumer, respectively."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The Renaissance perspectival paradigm encodes one-way social control` (or fold into `Dispersed Authorship` + the Renaissance metaphor).

- **Claim: The convergence of computers and telecommunications makes the thinking system planetary, approaching global consciousness.**
  - Reasoning structure: from Bateson's ecology of mind + Bertalanffy's general system theory + quantum "undivided wholeness" + Russell's global brain — the individual is not an isolated entity; telematic interconnection reaches a level of complexity where "we can no longer perceive ourselves as isolated individuals or cultures."
  - Evidence: > "With the convergence of computers and telecommunications, the 'thinking system' becomes planetary."
  - Evidence: > "our global interaction through telecommunication networks . . . seemingly is reaching a level of complexity and interconnectedness in which we can no longer perceive ourselves as isolated individuals or cultures."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematic convergence makes the thinking system planetary` → `Network Consciousness`.

- **Claim: A telematic culture must generate difference and diversity, not homogenise.**
  - Reasoning structure: the massive memory of computers can preserve "all the differences of individual experience, local culture, and regional attributes"; the aim is "to generate difference in the multiplicity of viewpoints," not to unify ideas or conventionalise images.
  - Evidence: > "The aim of a telematic culture cannot be to homogenise experience and unify ideas or conventionalise images but to generate difference in the multiplicity of viewpoints, preferences, dreams, and concerns"
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematic culture must generate difference not homogenise it`.

- **Claim: The life drawing class is the central anomaly of the art academy — it immobilises the body without mind and rehearses archaic codes of representation.**
  - Reasoning structure: life drawing ("objective drawing") is defended as perfecting hand-to-eye coordination, but Ascott argues for hand-to-mind coordination; the eye alone cannot reveal the body as "systems within systems"; this requires "technological extensions of the senses."
  - Evidence: > "There we find the body immobilised, without mind; is that not the ideal of all repressive cultures?"
  - Evidence: > "But is it not hand-to-mind coordination we should seek?"
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Life drawing immobilises the body without mind` (or fold into a `Telematic Education` / `Hand-to-Mind vs Hand-to-Eye` note). Human review item.

- **Claim: The project of twentieth-century art is to make the invisible visible.**
  - Reasoning structure: art "has progressively sought to be in touch with unseen forces and fields, systems, relationships, connections, and transformations and to make them visible" — requiring technological extension of perception.
  - Evidence: > "the project of the art of our century has been essentially to make the invisible visible."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The project of twentieth-century art is to make the invisible visible` (resonates with O'Gieblyn's re-enchantment and the later Ascott cyberception/technoetics arc).

- **Claim: Used merely as tools of production, telematic networks promote sterility and alienation (Socrates over Cato).**
  - Reasoning structure: the universal machine "can contain as much creative thought and express as much emotion as we put into it" ("garbage in, garbage out"), but a productivist deployment will "very effectively promote sterility and alienation"; education should follow Socratic principles (critical reflection, personal development, sustained enquiry), not Catonic ones (estimating everything by what it produces).
  - Evidence: > "There is no doubt, though, that if used merely as tools of production, telematic networks and computer systems will certainly and very effectively promote sterility and alienation in the culture."
  - Evidence: > "The principles of Socrates—critical reflection, personal development, and sustained enquiry—must not be undermined in this new technological environment by the principles of Cato, which estimate everything by what it produces."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematic networks as mere tools of production promote alienation` (cross-link Han's productivist `Achievement Society` / `Auto-Exploitation` as a sympathetic critique).

## Metaphors

- **Network as the metaphor for the emerging culture** — Ascott's master image; explicitly named.
  - Source passage: > "Our assertion of network as the metaphor for the emerging culture appears to find support in fields beyond art."

- **The metaphor of the tube / conduit (the FALSE model, rejected)** — Maturana–Varela's image of communication as something generated at one point, carried by a conduit, delivered to a receiver. Ascott quotes it to reject it.
  - Source passage: > "According to the metaphor of the tube, communication is something generated at a certain point. It is carried by a conduit (or tube) and is delivered to the receiver at the other end."

- **Renaissance space / the vanishing point as the metaphor of social ordering** — perspectival space ("everything in its place and a place for everything") as a metaphor of the hierarchical, one-way pyramid society.
  - Source passage: > "The ordering of space in Renaissance painting . . . a space subject to the authority of the vanishing point . . . can be seen as the perfect metaphor of the ordering of parts in the societies to which it gave expression."

- **The planet at the *stade du miroir* (Lacan's mirror stage)** — the planet, seeing the whole-earth image beamed back by astronauts and remote sensors, gains a sense of self, like Lacan's infant.
  - Source passage: > "It is as if the planet is at the stade du miroir, the point in its development where Jacques Lacan theorizes the infant sees in its newly reflected image its own unique identity and gains a sense of self"

- **The computer as a matrix** — not a "stand-alone generator of images" but the matrix through which all data passes and across diverse media. (Also a key concept.)
  - Source passage: > "As a matrix, it is much more than a stand-alone generator of images, for it extends enormously the capability of the artist to integrate and work between diverse media"

- **"Garbage in, garbage out"** — the universal machine reflects what is put into it; creative thought in, creative thought out.
  - Source passage: > "'Garbage in, garbage out' is, I suppose, the phrase to invoke here. That is to say, the universal machine—which the computer is—can contain as much creative thought and express as much emotion as we put into it."

- **The "horseless carriage" stage of the artist-friendly interface** — the figure for how primitive current (1988) interfaces are (keyboards, light pens, mice) relative to what is coming.
  - Source passage: > "We are at the 'horseless carriage' stage in the development of the artist-friendly interface."

- **The body immobilised, without mind** — the life-drawing model as the figure of the repressive culture's ideal subject.
  - Source passage: > "There we find the body immobilised, without mind; is that not the ideal of all repressive cultures?"

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Object vs System / Material vs Immaterial** — "Art does not reside in the object alone . . . Art is all process, all system." Ascott extends Burnham's object→system spine into the networked, telematic register.
  - Source passage: > "Art is all process, all system."

- **Isolation vs Convergence** — Ascott's own named pair: the problem (institutional isolation) and the remedy (convergence into networks).
  - Source passage: > "'Isolation' and 'convergence' are terms that encapsulate what could be seen as the problem and the remedy"

- **One-Way Linearity vs Interactivity / Mutual Convergence** — the "tube" / "components" model vs the convergence/network model; the Renaissance one-way despatch vs telematic mutuality. (Cross-link Burnham's `One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication`.)
  - Source passage: > "The concept of interactivity also has an important place in recent theories of communication, in contrast to the one-way linearity of older models."

- **Producer vs Participant (Artist/Viewer collapse)** — telematic interaction renders the producer/consumer (artist/viewer) distinction obsolete; the "new composite role" is "simply of participant in a system creating meaning seen as art."
  - Source passage: > "The new composite role becomes that simply of participant in a system creating meaning seen as art."

- **Homogenisation vs Difference** — the planetary scope risks homogenising culture; Ascott insists telematics must instead generate difference.
  - Source passage: > "we shall want to do everything to avoid a homogenisation of culture."

- **Creativity vs Production (Socrates vs Cato)** — telematic systems as creative/convivial media vs as "tools of production" that promote sterility and alienation.
  - Source passage: > "if used merely as tools of production, telematic networks and computer systems will certainly and very effectively promote sterility and alienation in the culture."

- **Hand-to-Eye vs Hand-to-Mind** — the life-drawing critique: perfecting hand-to-eye coordination vs seeking hand-to-mind coordination.
  - Source passage: > "But is it not hand-to-mind coordination we should seek?"

- **Latent tension: sterile/inhuman high tech vs conviviality** — Ascott notes the "popular conception of high technology . . . as that of a sterile, inhuman and emotionless environment" against "the sheer conviviality of communication in electronic networks." (This is the seed of the later "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" thesis.)
  - Source passage: > "The popular conception of high technology, we are told, is that of a sterile, inhuman and emotionless environment."
