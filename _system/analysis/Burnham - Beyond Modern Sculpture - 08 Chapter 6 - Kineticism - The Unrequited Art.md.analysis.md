## Executive Summary

This is the first chapter of Part II ("Sculpture as System"), and it carries Burnham's master thesis — the migration of sculpture from the OBJECT to the SYSTEM — into its first concrete movement: Kinetic Art. Burnham's framing claim is that the drive to make art kinetic is "one of the prime artistic urges of the present century," yet Kineticism is "The Unrequited Art": an art whose aspiration (motion, immateriality, life-like temporal process) chronically outruns its means (the inadequate, deterministic "closed kinematic chain" of the first age of machines). The chapter is structured as a long historical morphology: (1) the prehistory of Kineticism in machine theory (Reuleaux, Ampère, kinematics/kinetics) and in the scientific recording of organic motion (Marey, Muybridge); (2) the "early failures" of the four pivotal pioneers — Duchamp, Gabo, Calder, Moholy-Nagy — each of whom recognized the potential of motion, made a few experiments, and withdrew; (3) the New Tendency / field kinetics revival of c. 1955–1965 (Fontana, Klein, Vasarely, Soto, Tinguely, Group Zero, NUL, G.R.A.V., Group N/T) with its turn from machine-kinematics toward immateriality, field theory, and the depersonalized "systeme d'art"; and (4) a closing meditation on the unresolved problem of TIME and PROGRAMMING (determinate vs. aleatoric), on the artist–engineer symbiosis (Billy Klüver, E.A.T.), and on the inescapability of technics (Ellul, Mumford).

The chapter is load-bearing for the vault's titular axis in several ways. It introduces the explicit formula "the Kinetic construction is not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art" — the object-to-system move named in a single phrase. It threads "immateriality" as the explicit "rallying cry" of the whole New Tendency (Klein), pushing sculpture toward the dematerialized — and it links Kinetic feedback, equilibrium-seeking, and the birth-life-death cycle to the simulation of biological life. It anticipates participatory / experiential / response-based art (the active spectator who "lives the work," art as a "situation" rather than an object) and grounds the committed stubs **New Media Art** and **Art as Experiential Technology**. Crucially, it culminates in Ellul's "la technique" / self-augmentation and Mumford's "technics," and the artist–engineer symbiosis — the systems-esthetics foreground.

## Definitions

- **Kinematics vs. Kinetics** — Burnham explicitly distinguishes the two:
  - > "Kinematics (or cinematique as Ampere called his new science) consequently became the study of motion unattached to forces or objects... Essentially kinematics has remained the geometry of touching surfaces."
  - > "Kinetics, on the other hand, is concerned with motions resulting from forces directly connected to physical systems."
  - Recommended: definition note `Kinematics vs Kinetics (Burnham)` OR include the distinction inside the `Kinetic Art` concept.

- **"Closed kinematic chain" (Reuleaux)** — the machine as "the ability of man to control motion and power in a determinate fashion"; a "faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies." (See concept above.)

- **"Pair closure" (Reuleaux)** — > "He developed the schematic picture of precisely how individual elements of a machine engage or come in contact with each other... This was reduced to the principle of what Reuleaux called 'pair closure.'"

- **Micro-time vs. Macro-time (Schöffer/Stockhausen, serial composition)** — > "micro-time is a sequence of sound vibrations which occur so fast that we perceive them as a single tone; macro-time refers to the perceivable duration of tones and of the intervals between tones." (Likely review-only / one-off; useful citation, possibly not a durable standalone note.)

## Arguments

- **Kinetic Art is "The Unrequited Art": its aspiration outruns the deterministic means of the first age of machines, making it a heroic, productive failure.**
  - Reasoning: motion is a prime artistic urge of the century, but the machine is a restrictive closed kinematic chain; therefore artists must either trivialize/defy mechanics or fail; the failure is itself significant.
  - Evidence: the four pioneers' withdrawal; the technical breakdown problem; Leider's critique.
  - Source passage: > "Successful Kinetic Art until now has either defied or trivialized the principles of mechanical invention."
  - Source passage: > "Stated categorically and given the standards set by past art, the present Kinetic revival is with very few exceptions a failure."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Kinetic art is unrequited its aspiration outruns the means of the first age of machines`

- **The kinetic construction is not an object but a system (objet d'art → systeme d'art).** [KEYSTONE — the object-to-system spine]
  - Reasoning: a kinetic work is a series of interacting components requiring maintenance/repair; unlike the inert painting/sculpture it behaves like a living, serviceable system.
  - Evidence: the breakdown/resupply problem; purchasers returning broken machines expecting a "lifetime guarantee."
  - Source passage: > "the Kinetic construction is not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The kinetic construction is a system not an object`

- **The machine is fundamentally at odds with art, so artists must "humanize" it — inject imprecision, fallibility, even self-destruction — to make it "live."**
  - Reasoning: art demands psychic involvement and a not-always-rational order; the determinate machine forbids this; therefore artists use the machine as a power source / randomizing device, parody it, or build in defects.
  - Evidence: Tinguely's "joyous"/free machine, his cripples and self-destroying machines; Duchamp calling direct machine-art "unartistic"; Man Ray's *Destructible Object*.
  - Source passage: > "to be 'human' is to expose oneself through animal vulnerability and fallibility."
  - Source passage: > "Yet to function in actuality-and artistically-it had to be injected with elements of imprecision and irrationality. Then, perhaps, it could begin to live, in doubt and indecision, as human beings do."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The machine must be humanized with imprecision and fallibility to make art live`

- **Immateriality, not machine-motion, became the true goal of the Kinetic revival; the New Tendency sought the "physically sensed but insubstantial."**
  - Reasoning: New Tendency artists (mostly painters) cared only for visual effect, not the clattering machine; their field/light/fluid means pursued infinite extension and the dematerialized.
  - Evidence: Klein's immateriality rallying cry, *Le Vide*; Fontana's *Spazialismo*; Soto's "art detached from any language of expression"; the raster as symbol of infinity.
  - Source passage: > "Among artists providing the climate which brought the New Tendency to life was a common quest for the physically sensed but insubstantial. This quest embraced infinite spatial extension, immateriality, control through invisible forces, and the use in art of the temporal dimension."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The true goal of the kinetic revival was immateriality not machine motion`

- **Art's correspondence with science (field theory, relativity, quantum mechanics) is a visual analogy pulled "from the air," not a borrowed methodology.** [Burnham's reification / re-synchronization thesis, stated for kinetics]
  - Reasoning: innovators of a style are extraordinarily perceptive; they do not rationally borrow from scientific sources; the correspondence rests on visual analogy and stops short of physical reality.
  - Evidence: Vasarely "devouring works on relativity, wave mechanics, cybernetics" yet finding the analogy only after the plastic accomplishment; Soto's "World of Relations"; the raster ↔ Einsteinian field analogy.
  - Source passage: > "the innovators of an art style-usually extraordinarily perceptive beings -pull them from the air, but in any case do not borrow them from scientific sources as a matter of rational decision."
  - Source passage: > "As intoxicating as it is to tie New Tendency field configurations to Einsteinian mechanics, the analogy stops short of physical reality and rests upon visual correspondences."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Art's correspondence with science is visual analogy not borrowed method`

- **Technics is self-augmenting and inescapable; the artist's only "hiding place" from technological proliferation is madness/irrationality (Dada).**
  - Reasoning: Ellul/Mumford — technology breeds technology; technics replaces symbolism and reduces every art form to technique; only madness is inaccessible to the machine; hence the internal contradiction of an art of machines.
  - Evidence: Ellul's *The Technological Society*; the doubling of scientists; Dada as "a breath of sanity."
  - Source passage: > "Beyond a certain plateau, technology breeds technology."
  - Source passage: > "Only madness is inaccessible to the machine. Every other 'art' form can be reduced to technique"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Technics is self-augmenting and only madness escapes the machine`

- **The real way to kill an art movement is for it to realize its goals; Kineticism survives precisely because it remains unrequited.** [the chapter's closing inversion]
  - Reasoning: unrealized art has heroic, open-ended promise; the self-destroying machine (Leider's suggestion) is "only a Luddite solution"; achievement, not destruction, ends a movement.
  - Source passage: > "The real way to kill an art movement is for it to realize its goals-an objective which Kineticism has yet to achieve."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/An art movement dies by realizing its goals not by self-destruction`

- **As art accepts the technological scale of values, it requires ever more outside (engineering) help — a new artist-engineer symbiosis.**
  - Reasoning: the puniness of artist-summoned technology vs. the millions needed to perfect film/TV; Klüver's College Art Association observation; E.A.T.'s formation.
  - Source passage: > "as art accepts the technological scale of values, as it looks toward electronic know-how to solve its present inadequacy, the more outside help it will require."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/As art accepts technology it depends increasingly on the engineer`

- **Machines unknowingly obey evolution and man may be preparing his own successor (Butler), now theoretically plausible (Turing/von Neumann self-reproduction).** [the cyborg/automaton-successor thread, carried from Ch.5]
  - Reasoning: Butler's satirical extension of Darwin — machines as superior organisms in infancy, man as nursemaid — became "theoretically possible by the 1930's" via Turing's computability theorem and von Neumann on self-reproduction.
  - Source passage: > "Butler envisioned machines as superior organisms-though still in a stage of infancy-and man as a kind of nursemaid whose purpose in life would disappear once the machine gained the ability to take care of itself and reproduce its own kind."
  - Confidence: medium (Burnham reports Butler/Turing/von Neumann; the self-reproduction link is "strongly implied")
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Man may be unknowingly preparing his own successor in the machine` (overlaps Ch.5; flag for cross-chapter merge — relates to `Artificial Life` self-reproduction in Hayles).

## Metaphors

- **The Unrequited Art** — the chapter's governing figure: an art whose love (for motion, life, immateriality) is never returned by its means. Coexist as concept AND metaphor (same pattern as `Hell of the Same`).
  - > "Kineticism: The Unrequited Art"

- **The machine rendered harmless, like Milton's Samson Agonistes** — the motor hidden, "forced to labor far in the recesses of the work of art."
  - > "The machine, like Milton's Samson Agonistes, was thought to have been rendered harmless, forced to labor far in the recesses of the work of art."

- **The "joyous"/free machine (Tinguely)** — the machine entered into as a game, made poetic and free, diverting its restrictive nature.
  - > "If you respect the machine, if you enter into a game with the machine, then perhaps you can make a truly joyous machine-by joyous, I mean free"

- **The Bicycle Wheel as a turtle on its back** — the utilitarian wheel rendered functionally immobile; "motion that goes nowhere and a machine that does not 'work.'"
  - > "the utilitarian wheel has been rendered functionally immobile-like a turtle on its back. It is motion that goes nowhere and a machine that does not 'work' in the accepted sense."

- **The artist as "unfrocked artist" on the brink of raw technology (Duchamp)** — drawing back from "a rational world of impersonally controlled effects."
  - > "Duchamp realized that he had placed himself on the brink of raw technology. Such a situation demanded that one either draw back or plunge into a rational world of impersonally controlled effects."

- **The sky as an extensionless pristine canvas (Klein)** — immateriality projected onto the void; "an immense painting lacking any sense of dimension."
  - > "The sky itself became an extensionless pristine canvas waiting for some enterprising draftsman."

- **Tinguely's machines as a colony / collective psychology** — machines "creating" their like, "devouring" each other, "going on strike," "committing" suicide; a peculiar feedback and group psychology.
  - > "machines 'creating' their like, 'devouring' each other, machines 'working' or 'going on strike,' machines 'creating' works of art, 'committing' suicide"

- **Tinguely's metamechanical work as the archaic kouros / a creature on Dante's Inferno** — the machine "nakedly subject to the whims of the gods"; the radio drawings as biological specimens / sad creatures who "at least had each other."
  - > "Standing alone in a room, one of Tinguely's metamechanical works appears nakedly subject to the whims of the gods-like the standing male nudes of archaic Greece, the kouroi."
  - > "like the sad creatures on the upper circles of Dante's Inferno, they at least had each other."

- **Self-annihilation as an inglorious aesthetic act / the mock world-suicide spectacle** — *Study No. 2 for an End of the World*; Huxley's Gammas assisting with their own nullification.
  - > "this mock world-suicide spectacle objectifies the despair and inevitability of the present situation in a way left untouched by painting."

- **The raster / boundless field as a symbol of infinity** — the discrete-element grid extendable to infinity, like Klein's art of the sky.
  - > "the boundless raster became a symbol of infinity."

- **The room of Bury sculptures that "rocks with subliminal activity"; wood straining like the hull of a sailing ship** — microscopic, peripheral, multisensory motion felt as aftereffect.
  - > "Without the interference of other human visitors, a room of Bury sculptures rocks with subliminal activity."

- **The "great un-spanned void"** between what artists could do and the importance society designates for them. — the discrepancy between desire and realization.
  - > "a great un-spanned void between what artists feel they could do given the material means, and the actual importance which society designates for its artists"

## Symbols

- **The Bicycle Wheel (Duchamp, 1913)** — the first modern Kinetic work; the readymade as optical/kinetic ambiguity; the "wheel-of-life fatalism."
  - > "The Bicycle Wheel by Duchamp cannot be regarded primarily as a mobile object although it is the first modern Kinetic work."

- **The mobile (Calder)** — equilibrium-seeking system; static when at rest, alive under wind/interference; the "quasi-random motion" archetype.

- **The crank-shaft / the machine as "the image" (Rickey)** — the machine not as projection but as the thing itself; "The concreteness of machines is heartening."
  - > "The crank-shaft exists in its own right; it is the image."

- **The self-destroying machine** — Tinguely's *Study No. 2 for an End of the World*; the cripple-machine with built-in defects; Leider's suggested (and rejected) "Luddite solution."

- **The water box / Weather Cube (Haacke)** — nonmechanical, natural-time motion (gravity, condensation, evaporation); the system simulating natural process. [Realizes Haacke from a Lippard forward-stub in his Kinetic/systems origin.]
  - > "With the water boxes of Hans Haacke, gravity becomes a means of simulating control and a sense of purposefulness."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Object vs. System (objet d'art vs. systeme d'art)** — the vault's keystone axis, named in the chapter; the kinetic work as interacting, serviceable system vs. the inert traditional object.
  - > "the Kinetic construction is not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art."

- **Material vs. Immaterial** — solid bronze/steel/stone vs. Klein's gaseous/liquid/luminous "immaterial sensitivity"; the field/raster's "yearning for immateriality." [UPDATE the existing 7-author tension.]

- **Determinism vs. Randomness (Aleatoric)** — strictly programmed kinetics vs. random/pseudo-random/statistical field motion; the engineering cost of determinacy vs. the ease of letting "randomness run its course."
  - > "any kind of detailed and precise programming, demands more engineering ability from artists than a decision to let randomness run its course."

- **Art vs. Technics (the irrational vs. the ultra-rational)** — the machine's "compelling logic and almost authoritarian ethos" vs. the irrational/madness that is art's last refuge; Schöffer (no incompatibility) vs. Tinguely (regret/haunting) vs. Ellul (technics → oblivion).
  - > "art has always been a subtle balance between irrational and rational human characteristics."

- **Motion vs. the Motion-Producing Mechanism (Gabo's dilemma)** — the motor "interfered" with the motion it produced and had to be made small/inobvious; "the motion is of importance and not the mechanism which produces it."
  - > "Somehow the motor 'interfered' with the motion it produced and had to be made small and inobvious enough not to cause competition with the sculpture itself."

- **Individual signature/recognition vs. Collective Anonymity** — the New Tendency's communalized research and rejection of the "cult of personality" vs. the artists' "frantic" working-for-recognition that fractured every group (Zagreb schisms; G.R.A.V., Group N disbanding).
  - > "they fought the cults of egoism and commercialism while ardently working for recognition-first as groups, then as individuals."

- **Permanence vs. Ephemerality (the breakdown problem)** — the conservative desire for relative permanence vs. the work whose "physical life may not extend beyond the duration of a single exhibition."

- **Realized vs. Unrequited (Heroic Failure)** — the closing inversion: a movement dies by realizing its goals; Kineticism's unrealized state is "a sign of encouragement."

- **Real motion vs. Virtual/Illusory motion** — Gabo's insistence that the illusion of motion (Victory of Samothrace; the eye apprehending linear rhythms in succession) need not be real motion vs. the New Tendency's "actual movement... replacing or superseding the previous virtual movement."
