## Executive Summary

"Behaviourables and Futuribles" is one of Ascott's earliest and most concentrated manifestos, originally produced as a poster-sized broadside in 1967 and reprinted in *Control 5* (1970). It is a primary-text statement of his **1960s cybernetic / behaviourist art** position — the pole of 1960s cybernetic art opposite Jack Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* systems esthetics. Where Burnham theorizes the art object's migration from object to system through sculpture's internal history, Ascott theorizes the artwork as a unit of **behaviour**: a feedback-driven system completed by the participating spectator.

The manifesto's compressed thesis — "**Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure**" — programmatically inverts the modernist art object. Ascott names two new categories of art-thing: the **behaviourable** (the artwork read as behaviour, as a process in its history) and the **futurible** (the artwork as a speculative, anticipatory projection of possible futures — "the delineation of futuribles"). The artwork is also a **trigger**: "a behaviourable in its history, a futurible in its structure, a trigger in its effect."

The text then performs a striking adaptation of McLuhan: Ascott imports the **hot/cool distinction** and reframes it as the **"thermodynamics of an artwork."** A "hot" artwork is "densely stacked with information bits, highly organised, and rigidly determined," admitting little feedback (a one-way channel from artist to spectator); a "cool" artwork is "loosely stacked, of uncertain order... ambiguous, entropic," and so invites the observer to "participate, projecting his own sense of order." This is Ascott's information-theoretic restatement of his participatory, feedback-based esthetic — and a direct cross-link to McLuhan's *Understanding Media* (hot/cool media) and to the vault's `One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication` tension.

The manifesto also advances the **Cybernetic Art Matrix (CAM)** — "essentially a futurible — anticipatory and speculative" — Ascott's projected institution fusing art, learning, and behaviour ("Artist on the campus. We can create new rituals in the centres of learning"), and calls for art schools restructured "as homeostatic organisms, living, adaptive instruments." Finally, it gestures toward what will become his 1990s **technoetics**: the claim that in the post-industrial society "it is not technology that will carry us through so much as **psychotechnology**," taking us "beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering" into ESP, parapsychology, out-of-body states, and "total telepathy" — "Instant communications with no media." This is the documentable seed of Ascott's later psi / Shamantic Web / technoetic arc, embedded in a 1967 cybernetic-art text.

The text is aphoristic, exclamatory, manifesto-cadenced ("Object hustlers! Reduce your anxiety!"; "Message ends."). It closes by restating its keynote: "**Art is now a form of behaviour.**"

## Arguments

- **Claim: Art is now a form of behaviour; process and system displace product and structure.**
  - Reasoning structure: the world is now understood as all process and constant change; process at the human level is behaviour; therefore art (mirroring the world) is essentially behaviourist, and software/process/system supersedes hardware/product/structure.
  - Evidence: the cybernetic/post-industrial diagnosis of the world as process; the analogy world::process = human::behaviour = art::behaviourist.
  - Source passage: > "Now that we see that the world is all process, constant change, we are less surprised to discover that our art is all about process too. We recognise process at the human level as behaviour, and we are beginning to understand art now as being essentially behaviourist."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Art is now a form of behaviour so process supersedes product` (or similar). Cross-link to Burnham's `For Object Art presence is everything for Systems Art information is the key` and to `Object vs System` — Ascott is the behaviourist counterpart to Burnham's object-side argument.

- **Claim: A cool, low-information, ambiguous artwork generates participation and feedback; a hot, over-determined one does not.**
  - Reasoning structure: an information-theoretic / cybernetic restatement of participation — feedback in the artifact/observer system is inversely related to the work's informational density and determinacy; over-determined ("hot") works are one-way channels, while ambiguous/entropic ("cool") works open the system to the observer's projection and unpredicted interaction.
  - Evidence: the explicit thermodynamic/feedback model; the "cut-out mechanism" by which an overheated work flips to a very cool state allowing high-order feedback.
  - Source passage: > "Hot artwork admits of very little feedback in the system artifact/observer, it's really a one-way channel... Call it cool when the information bits are loosely stacked, of uncertain order, not clearly connected, ambiguous, entropic. Then the system allows the observer to participate."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/A cool ambiguous artwork generates feedback and participation a hot one does not` (or fold into `One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication`).

- **Claim: The aim of behaviourist art is to generate change, and generating alternative futures (futuribles) makes the present more changeable.**
  - Reasoning structure: dreaming up alternative futures increases the present's changeability; change is the essence and aim of behaviourist art; art "creates mythic futures."
  - Evidence: the futurible program; the social-modelling aspiration; the closing on telepathic "waves of alternative behaviours."
  - Source passage: > "We are very much concerned with generating futuribles—maybe that's because the more we can dream up alternative futures, the more changeable the present can become. And change is what we are all about—change for its own sake."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: capture inside `Behaviourist Art` / `Futuribles`, or `arguments/Generating alternative futures makes the present more changeable`.

- **Claim: Behaviourist / process culture need not end the art object — it gives the object new values.**
  - Reasoning structure: a reassurance to "object hustlers" — process and behaviour reframe rather than abolish the object; the behaviourist object becomes legible as behaviour ("read like the palm of your hand").
  - Evidence: direct address.
  - Source passage: > "Process culture and behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object, as long as it means the beginning of new values for art. Maybe the behaviourist art object will come to be read like the palm of your hand."
  - Confidence: medium (Directly stated, but rhetorically hedged; a nuance worth preserving against the easy reading that behaviourist art simply dematerializes the object — cross-links to the `Material vs Immaterial` and `Object vs System` tensions).
  - Recommended wiki note: fold into `Behaviourist Art`; flag as the Ascott data-point that he does not simply abolish the object (contrast Lippard's dematerialization and Burnham's object→system).

- **Claim: The post-industrial future will be carried by psychotechnology, beyond behavioural engineering, into the psychic/parapsychological.**
  - Reasoning structure: technology alone will not "carry us through"; psychotechnology exceeds Skinner's behavioural engineering into ESP/astral/out-of-body "future behaviours"; the parapsychological frontier promises "instant communications with no media... total telepathy."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Process vs Product / System vs Structure** — the manifesto's founding inversion ("Process replaces product... system supersedes structure"). Maps onto `Object vs System` (Burnham) and `Material vs Immaterial`.
- **Hot vs Cool (one-way vs participatory)** — the determined, one-way "hot" artwork vs the ambiguous, feedback-rich "cool" artwork. Maps directly onto `One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication` (Burnham) and `Hot vs Cool Media` (McLuhan).
- **Discrete vs Continuous art systems / Classical vs Behaviourist art** — Ascott's own paired opposition: "two classes of art system—the discrete and the continuous. That's like classical and behaviourist art."
  - Source passage: > "We could talk about the levels of resolution for examining two classes of art system—the discrete and the continuous. That's like classical and behaviourist art."
- **Technology vs Psychotechnology** — the post-industrial pivot from technology to psychotechnology / parapsychology.
- **Object retained vs object dissolved** — the productive tension between "process culture... need not mean the end of the object" and "software predominates over hardware." (Ascott keeps the object but revalues it — a nuance against pure dematerialization.)
- **Order vs Uncertainty / Definition vs Ambiguity** — via the Lévi-Strauss quotation on myth: "the necessary paradox of definition and ambiguity, of order and uncertainty, of the tangible and the infinite." Also stated as "The mythology of change and uncertainty and the ritualisation of the will to form."
