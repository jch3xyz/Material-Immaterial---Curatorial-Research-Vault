## Executive Summary

This 1995 essay is the bridge text between Ascott's mature **telematic** phase and his late **moistmedia / technoetic** turn. Its thesis is counterintuitive and programmatic: the most artificial, immaterial technologies — telematic networks and, coming next, nanotechnology and artificial life — will return us "back to nature," but to a *new* nature, "second-order nature, emergent nature, Nature II." Ascott argues that the old metaphor of "nature" (the pure, the pastoral, the god-given) is exhausted and "past its 'sell by' date"; the binary oppositions that sustained it (town/country, natural/artificial, material/immaterial) are collapsing under ubiquitous telematic networks and molecular biology. In their place he proposes the **connectivist paradigm** — "everything is connected, everything interacts with and affects everything else" — which he grounds in **second-order cybernetics** (the observer included in the system), connectionism / neural nets in science, quantum non-locality (Bohm, Bell, Aspect), and intuitive holistic sciences (Sheldrake's morphic resonance, Lovelock's Gaia). He renames telematic art practice "connectivism" so as to flee the romantic/modernist baggage of the word "art." The essay also forecasts the **rematerialization of art**: the convergence of the electronic/virtual trajectory with the biological/molecular trajectory — "a marriage of the immaterial and the material" — which is the seed of the later "the future will be moist" thesis. It develops Ascott's signature couplings: *electronic space* ↔ *molecular time*; *virtualisation* of presence and the *distributed self*; the parallel **artificial life (AL) ↔ interactive art (IA)** ("For 'life,' read 'art'!"); and the move from "What is reality?" to "How do we interact with a proliferation of separate realities?". It closes on the explicitly post-human and re-enchanting register: art as "a form of mediation between human and post-human consciousness, just as, in past cultures, it has been used to mediate between mankind and the gods."

This chapter is a dense node for cross-linking: it directly invokes McLuhan (the rear-view-mirror "rear-view mirror effect," sense ratios, the penalties of literacy), the posthuman/bionic body (Moravec, Drexler — links to Hayles/Haraway), Gibson's *Neuromancer* cyberspace, and the re-enchantment/transcendence cluster (O'Gieblyn). It is the strongest single-chapter statement so far of Ascott's distinctive *participatory/connectivist* pole against Burnham's *object→system* systems esthetics.

## Definitions

- **Connectivist paradigm** — "which holds that everything is connected, everything interacts with and affects everything else." (Directly stated)
- **Second-order cybernetics** — cybernetics that "included the observer of a system as a part of the system itself," with boundaries "intrinsically fuzzy, permeable, uncertain, and transformable, and utterly dependent on the position, attitude, and intention of the observer." (Directly stated)
- **Connectionism** — "These explorations into the microstructure of cognition, known as connectionism, parallel distributed processing, or neural networks." (Directly stated; kept DISTINCT from connectivism in art)
- **Connectivism (in art)** — Ascott's proposed replacement for the word "art" when referring to telematic art practice; "it may be useful... to avoid using the term 'art'... and to use the term 'connectivism' when we are referring to telematic art practice." (Directly stated, Note 1)
- **Molecular time** — "that bundle of meanings and interpretations of time provided in different but connected ways by cognitive science, neurobiology, computer science, and quantum physics, each of which recognises the variable rates, jumps, delays, phases, and reversals of time." (Directly stated)
- **Cybernetics** — "the study of control and communication in living and artificial systems." (Directly stated — Wiener-derived definition; matches existing `Cybernetics` / `Cybernetics (Burnham)` framing)
- **Implicate meaning** — Ascott's adaptation of Bohm's implicate order: "each quantum of meaning is many-layered, carrying within it a multiplicity of possible semantic trajectories, depending on the participating viewer, reader or listener." (Directly stated)
- **The "many meanings" hypothesis** — proposed "in complementarity to Hugh Everett's 'many worlds' interpretation of quantum mechanics — the observer as participant generating a rich profusion of parallel worlds/parallel meanings." (Directly stated, Note 2)

## Arguments

- **The most artificial, immaterial technologies will bring us "back to nature" — to a new, second-order Nature II.**
  - Reasoning structure: paradox/inversion — working in electronic (immaterial) space redefines our living in natural (material) space; the logical outcome of the information society's immateriality is "the reinstatement... of a natural materiality."
  - Evidence: nanotechnology (Drexler), artificial life, molecular engineering; the McLuhan rear-view-mirror argument that a new medium's content is the prior medium.
  - Source passage: > "The logical outcome of the immateriality of the information society is the reinstatement in the twenty-first century of a natural materiality."
  - Source passage: > "How is it that the most advanced technologies, electronic and molecular, the very epitome of the artificial, could bring us back to nature? For that is what I propose."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The most artificial technologies will return us to a new second-order nature`

- **The metaphor of "nature" is exhausted; the binary oppositions that sustained it are collapsing.**
  - Reasoning structure: historical/semantic — nature was always a metaphor placed "in opposition — to culture, the city, technology"; with ubiquitous telematic networks and biological synthesis, town/country and natural/artificial no longer hold.
  - Evidence: physicists and molecular biologists have "irreversibly altered the common view of nature"; new instrument-revealed nature (quantum, molecular, cosmological) is invisible to the eye.
  - Source passage: > "It is the first virtual reality—in which the pure data of an undifferentiated wholeness are programmed, shaped, and categorised according to our language, fears, and desires."
  - Source passage: > "nature seems to be past its 'sell by' date."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Nature is a metaphor now exhausted by science and telematics`

- **Technological/computerised systems open the door to nature; realist painting closed it.**
  - Reasoning structure: reversal of the popular view — naturalistic landscape/figure painting was "a cultural membrane" / "the proscenium arch" that staged nature in "timeless immobility" and kept us separated from its processes; instrumentation (microscope, imaging, telemetry) gives access to nature as process.
  - Evidence: artists "failed for so long to respond... to the dynamic processes of life revealed by the microscope"; Cézanne's mobile viewpoint; Heisenberg's observer-dependent particle.
  - Source passage: > "it is these technological and computerised systems that are providing us with a threshold, an open doorway into the natural world; whereas landscape and figure painting... have kept us separated from it."
  - Source passage: > "That membrane was the window, the proscenium arch within which the artist 'staged' nature, framed it in a timeless immobility."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Technological systems open the door to nature that realist painting closed`

- **Interactive/connectivist art parallels artificial life (AL ↔ IA): bottom-up, distributed, no global controller.**
  - Reasoning structure: analogy by substitution — Langton's AL definition becomes a definition of telematic art if "life" is read as "art."
  - Evidence: Langton 1989 quotation on emergent behavior; the absence of a "global controller."
  - Source passage: > "It is this bottom-up, distributed, local determination of behavior that AL employs in its primary methodological approach to the generation of lifelike behaviors." [Langton 1989]
  - Source passage: > "For 'life,' read 'art'!"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Interactive art parallels artificial life as bottom-up distributed behavior`

- **Much "computer art" is not yet connectivist or telematic — it is digital conversion of old forms; the computer must be "let loose" into living systems.**
  - Reasoning structure: critique/diagnosis — the computer is "still too much contained... isolated as a black box"; until released "into nature, into essentially living systems," the new art form won't emerge.
  - Evidence: postmodernism's "reactionary and arid banality"; "a kind of digital conversion of video, film, painting, sculpture, even the book."
  - Source passage: > "until it is released, let loose in the world, into nature, into essentially living systems, the new art form's future will not emerge."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The computer must be released into living systems for the new art to emerge`

- **The self and human presence are ongoing dynamic processes, not material finalities.**
  - Reasoning structure: ontological reframing — telematics virtualises presence and distributes the self; art has shifted from representation/magic to virtualisation.
  - Evidence: the historical survey of what art celebrates (gods → heroes → artist → "presence of presence" → virtualisation); poststructuralist attacks on authorship.
  - Source passage: > "All of which recognises the self and human presence as ongoing dynamic processes rather than as a material finality."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial (RECONCILED, not opposed, in Ascott)** — the vault's keystone tension: Ascott uniquely proposes their *marriage*. "A marriage of the immaterial and the material." Where Lippard/Burnham track dematerialization (object→system, → "scribble in the air") and Han/Hayles theorize immateriality, Ascott foresees a *rematerialization*: virtualisation then materialisation (nano/AL). This makes him the NINTH author on the tension and a turning point in its arc.
- **Natural vs Artificial (collapsing)** — "renders the line between natural and artificial systems meaningless"; "We are all becoming... bionic." The binary that defined nature dissolves.
- **Town vs Country (collapsing)** — "the binary opposition of town and country... is disappearing" under telematic ubiquity.
- **First-order vs Second-order (cybernetics / nature)** — observer outside vs observer inside the system; antagonist-of-nature vs participant-in-nature.
- **Representation vs Virtualisation** — "We are less interested in representations of the world than in its virtualisation"; "What is reality?" → "How do we interact with a proliferation of separate realities?"
- **Reductionism vs Holism / connectivism** — science "tearing it up into little pieces" vs "attempting to unify it"; Ascott sides with the holistic, anti-reductionist, intuitive (Sheldrake, Bohm, Gaia).
- **Connectivist art vs "computer art"** — telematic/living-systems art vs the "black box" digital conversion of old forms.
- **Linear/causal vs non-linear** — "Linear systems, rigid causality, and analytical certitude have given way to a more playful interaction with non-linear systems."
- **The schizophrenia of presence ("Here and not here")** — "Therein lies the schizophrenia of our condition. Telematic systems... allow us to be both here and elsewhere at the same time." (Note: distinct register from Hayles's `The Schizoid Android`.)
