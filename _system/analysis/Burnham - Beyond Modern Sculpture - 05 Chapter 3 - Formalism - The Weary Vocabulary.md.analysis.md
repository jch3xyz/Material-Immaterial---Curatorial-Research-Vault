## Executive Summary

Chapter 3 (Part One, "Sculpture as Object," pp. 110–166) is Burnham's diagnosis of *formalism* as "the weary vocabulary" of modern sculpture: the dwindling, prescientific reservoir of "material-optical-tactile features" (shape, proportion, scale, structure, texture, color, context) on which sculpture has subsisted since classical antiquity. His master claim is that formalism is exhausting itself because, to stay vital, its vocabulary must "constantly change and grow," yet it cannot escape the brute fact that the sculptor "cannot rid themselves of the presence of objects." This is the central object-bound predicament of the book's Part I.

The chapter's engine is the disjunction between the *form-world of science* and the *form-world of sculpture*. Burnham argues that the 1890–1925 revolution in physics (relativity, quantum theory, the dissolution of solid matter into "events," fields, and probability clouds) and the rise of technics ("la technique," after Ellul) progressively de-legitimized the inert, unique, place-bound art object. He traces this through six dense movements: (1) formalism as a prescientific, exhausting methodology; (2) the *scientific demiurge*—the psychic drive shared by scientist and sculptor to reveal invisible underlying order, plus the artist's love-hate envy of science's resources, anonymity, and teamwork; (3) *invention in sculpture* as an industrial-style proliferation of hybrids (the welded box from David Smith back to neon pylon signs); (4) the *scientific model* (substantive, mathematical, computer-simulation) as the sculptor's exotic, "unworldly" reservoir of pure form, and its obsolescence as physics abandoned visual models—accounting "in part for the steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's"; (5) *mathematical influences* (Vantongerloo, Pevsner, Gabo, Bill) culminating in topology, the Möbius band, and Spengler's "liberation of geometry from the visual," with Minimal/Object sculpture as the "thingified" Platonic-essence endgame; and (6) *space, scale, structure, and optical properties*, ending on the chapter's pivotal thesis that "light—not form—determines our perception of sculpture," that sculpture is becoming a modulator of light, and that the desire to "embody space without matter—like music—fulfills a profound striving of Western technology" to "produce effect without the hindrance or fallibility of objects."

This chapter is foundational for the vault's titular Material vs Immaterial axis and the dematerialization lineage: Burnham (1968) names the "steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's" and Herbert Read's "glitter principle," predating and feeding Lippard's *dematerialization of the art object*. It also seeds the book's object-to-system migration (the move toward light, optical effect, and reflection as a *system* of relationships rather than a discrete mass) and substantively grounds the committed research-foci stub Art as Experiential Technology (sculpture as light-modulating, optical, kinetic, reflective system) — and, more weakly, New Media Art (computer-simulation models, light/optical media). The OCR carries heavy artifacts (broken words, stray characters, dropped figure references); only clean verbatim substrings are quoted below.

## Definitions

- **Formalism (Burnham)** — "those material-optical-tactile features of sculpture which can be identified and logically examined as separate physical entities ... shape, proportion, scale, structure, texture, color, context." (`definitions/Formalism (Burnham).md`)
- **The Scientific Model** — "The model serves as an analogy of a proposed system or set of relationships, though not necessarily as a physical object." Divided into the *formal* (mathematical relationships) and the *substantive* (physical-system analogies and spatial-theory models). (`definitions/The Scientific Model (Burnham).md`)
- **Topology** — "the study of the innate properties of objects in space. These properties remain unaffected by changes in magnitude, position, or proportion and are only altered by the tearing or joining of an object's parts." (candidate definition note)
- **Enjambment (in sculpture)** — by analogy to the poetic device, "by which the eye picks up the disjointed path of a line"; the continual break and juxtaposition of linear elements (Gonzalez, Noguchi, von Schlegell). (minor; capture as candidate definition or inside a structure note)
- **The "Glitter Principle" (Herbert Read)** — fragmented reflectivity from "semi-random, reflective surfaces positioned in spatial configurations" rather than a continuous coherent mass. (capture as candidate definition / inside the light-modulator note)

## Arguments

- **Claim: Formalism is exhausting itself because its vocabulary cannot grow fast enough to stay vital, leaving sculpture in stasis.**
  - Reasoning structure: classical formal qualities have decreased; the only escape valve (constant change/growth) has "happened only marginally"; therefore each generation faces the charge of imitation and a "world of fifty-year-old manifestoes."
  - Evidence: the historical decline of figurative composition/proportion/anatomy; the "subtraction of traditional formal properties."
  - Source passage: > "Saddled more or less with the formalist vocabulary, each succeeding generation of sculptors has had to answer the charge of imitation-without too much success."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Formalism is exhausting itself but sculpture cannot escape the object.md`

- **Claim: Sculpture cannot follow science into nonvisual analysis because it is bound to the presence of the object.**
  - Reasoning structure: the scientific demiurge drives both fields toward invisible underlying order; science succeeds (it has nonvisual/mathematical means), sculpture stalls because it remains "phenomenological," tied to a 25-century-old atomist theory of matter.
  - Evidence: the dilemma of representing sub-atomic "events," fields, and probability clouds in bronze and marble.
  - Source passage: > "A present dilemma of sculpture is its inability to move beyond phenomenological means and into those very areas of nonvisual analysis established by science."
  - Source passage: > "If matter were actually a series of extremely brief, highly connected 'events,' then why was the sculptor creating a private and hermetic 'reality' in bronze and marble?"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Sculpture cannot follow science into nonvisual analysis because it is bound to the object.md`

- **Claim: The obsolescence of the visual scientific model is a primary cause of sculpture's dematerialization (1910–1950s).**
  - Reasoning structure: sculptors adopted ball-and-stick/atomic/topological models as a reservoir of "pure form"; but science abandoned visual models for mathematics just as sculptors took them up; sculptors' works were attempts at "visible representation of the invisible," so as the referents dematerialized, so did the sculpture.
  - Evidence: the ~half-century lag between ball-and-stick models and "open sculpture"; Russell/Heisenberg's "end of sight-physics"; Snelson's *Atom*.
  - Source passage: > "It is ironic that some sculptors of the 1920's felt influenced by scientific models precisely at the time when models were beginning to lose their importance for the mathematician and physicist."
  - Source passage: > "This accounts in part for the steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's. The model has been simply one way for the sculptor to cling to a tenuous reality."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The obsolescence of the visual scientific model dematerialized sculpture.md`

- **Claim: Technology's logic of duplication and interchangeability has dealt a "death blow" to the unique art object, shifting value from material output to the generative power of ideas.**
  - Reasoning structure: the technological strategy treats unique entities as a "cardinal sin"; mass-produced protosculpture and "multiples" erode uniqueness; therefore artistic uniqueness migrates to ideas.
  - Evidence: cement garden dwarfs, bronzed *Thinker* bookends, gallery "multiples," Arthur Clarke on the erosion of ownership.
  - Source passage: > "In terms of uniqueness, sculpture has been handed a death blow because, as Arthur Clarke, the science popularizer, has observed, the idea of ownership gradually erodes as all articles are made commonly accessible."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Technology shifts artistic uniqueness from material output to generative ideas.md` (cross-link to `Conceptual Art`)

- **Claim: The old genteel boundary between art and science/technology has collapsed; the present avant-garde operates on the amoral law of technics ("Since it was possible, it was necessary").**
  - Reasoning structure: the 1920s pioneers drew firm art/science lines; by the late 1950s a new sensibility (Pop/Op/Kinetic/Robot Art) fused art with technics; the stance became "an-emotional, an-idealistic, an-ethical."
