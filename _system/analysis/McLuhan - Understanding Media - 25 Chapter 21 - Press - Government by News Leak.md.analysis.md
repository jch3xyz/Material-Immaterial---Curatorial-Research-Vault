## Executive Summary

This is a **Part II case-study chapter**. McLuhan treats the press (the newspaper) as a concrete application of the Part I apparatus — above all the doctrine that *the medium is the message* (here in its strongest political form: "the owners of media always endeavor to give the public what it wants, because they sense that their power is in the medium and not in the message or the program"), the *Hot and Cold* participation axis ("the newspaper is a hot medium"), the *Content of Any Medium Is Always Another Medium* (the press is a mosaic "successor to the book-form," and "its technology is also a mosaic of all the technologies of the community"), *Hybrid Energy* (the telegraph crossing print/rotary presses, the typewriter solving the typesetting problem), and the *Global Village* retribalization theme (the press as a tribal/communal image; radio "restores tribal sensitivity" in Egypt while the press effects visual nationalism). The chapter's central analytic claim — durably distinctive and worth flagging as a candidate concept — is **the press as a mosaic / corporate (communal) image** opposed to the book as a private "point of view": "The book is a private confessional form that provides a 'point of view.' The press is a group confessional form that provides communal participation." Built on telegraph speed, this mosaic form "means, not a detached 'point of view,' but participation in process," which makes the press "inseparable from the democratic process." The title figure — **government by news leak** — is McLuhan's name for how electric speed forces decisions to be "announced before they are made" and translates "top secrecy" into "public participation and responsibility by the magic flexibility of the controlled news leak," accommodating Western man to "the electric world of total interdependence." Secondary durable strands: "human interest" defined as a technical/mosaic term ("that which happens when multiple book pages or multiple information items are arranged in a mosaic on one sheet"); the press as **paid learning** (ad-supported media as "a form of 'paid learning'") in a world where "information is the crucial commodity, and ... solid products are merely incidental to information movement"; **real news is bad news** (the hot medium needs "bad news for the sake of intensity and reader participation," and "ads are news ... what is wrong with them is that they are always good news"); the press as **public confessional** revealing the "inside story" / "the seamy side"; and the cross-cultural contrast — the press has "totally diverse effects in different cultures" (oral Russia/Europe vs. visual America), so to suppose it "has the same function in America and Russia ... is not really in touch with the medium." The chapter restages the failure-of-perception theme: critics (Newton Minow, Daniel Boorstin) "give attention to the program 'content' of our media while ignoring the form." Mostly this reinforces the Part I master concepts; the mosaic/corporate-image account of the press and the "government by news leak" figure are the candidate distinctive ideas.

## Definitions

No new master canonical term is *defined* here (the master definitions — The Medium Is the Message; Hot and Cold Media; Extensions of Man; Autoamputation — are minted in Part I). The chapter offers several working glosses worth recording:

- **"Human interest" (technical/mosaic definition)** — McLuhan explicitly redefines a journalistic cliché as a formal/technical term of the mosaic medium.
  - Source passage: > "it is necessary to state at once that 'human interest' is a technical term meaning that which happens when multiple book pages or multiple information items are arranged in a mosaic on one sheet."

- **The mosaic / corporate (communal) image** — the press's defining form: communal, inclusive, participational, opposed to the book's private point of view.
  - Source passage: > "The mosaic is the mode of the corporate or collective image and commands deep participation. This participation is communal rather than private, inclusive rather than exclusive."

- **Equitone** — Addison and Steele's prose technique matching the printed word: "It consisted in maintaining a single level of tone and attitude to the reader throughout the entire composition." (Contrast: the telegraph's "headlines, journalese, and telegraphese.")
  - Source passage: > "It was the technique of equitone. It consisted in maintaining a single level of tone and attitude to the reader throughout the entire composition."

- **The dateline** — "the only organizing principle of the newspaper image of the community"; remove it and "one day's paper is the same as the next." A compact gloss on the press's temporal-mosaic form.
  - Source passage: > "Apart from the vernacular used, the dateline is the only organizing principle of the newspaper image of the community. Take off the dateline, and one day's paper is the same as the next."

## Arguments

- **The press is a mosaic, participational ("group confessional") form, opposed in its very format to the book's private "point of view" — and is therefore inseparable from the democratic process.**
  - Reasoning structure: the book is "a private voice"; the press is "a communal mosaic or corporate image." The mosaic of juxtaposed discontinuous items "gives the press its complex dimension of human interest" and "commands deep participation," which "means ... participation in process." Hence "the press is inseparable from the democratic process, but quite expendable from a literary or book point of view." The "book-oriented man" who demands the press present "a fixed point of view on a single plane of perspective" "represent[s] a failure to see the form of the press at all" — "as if the public were suddenly to demand that department stores have only one department."
  - Evidence: the doubling of *Time*/*Newsweek* circulation after TV (mosaic news magazines vs. passive picture magazines); the classified ads and stock quotations as "the bedrock of the press"; the editorial as "the one book-feature" long ignored; Douglas Cater on the press unifying the fragmented branches of government.
  - Source passage: > "the press is inseparable from the democratic process, but quite expendable from a literary or book point of view."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: primary support for the candidate concept **The Press as Mosaic / Corporate Image**; capture in source summary; link to **Media Hot and Cold** and **Acoustic vs Visual Space** (Part I forward-references).

- **Electric speed forces "government by news leak": politics moves from representation/delegation toward immediate total involvement, and secrecy is converted into participation via the controlled leak.**
  - Reasoning structure: "As the speed of information increases, the tendency is for politics to move away from representation and delegation ... toward immediate involvement of the entire community in the central acts of decision." Slow information makes delegation "mandatory"; electric speed makes a delegated/representational order function "only by a series of subterfuges and makeshifts" that look like "base betrayals" to "old press men." The publicity/secrecy paradox (Cater) is resolved: "Top secrecy is translated into public participation and responsibility by the magic flexibility of the controlled news leak."
  - Evidence: the Krock/AP "manages news" headline (Kennedy); diplomacy announcing decisions "before they are made"; Cater's *The Fourth Branch of Government*; the press as "an individualistic technology dedicated to shaping and revealing group attitudes."
  - Source passage: > "As the speed of information increases, the tendency is for politics to move away from representation and delegation of constituents toward immediate involvement of the entire community in the central acts of decision."
  - Source passage: > "It is by this kind of ingenious adaptation from day to day that Western man is beginning to accommodate himself to the electric world of total interdependence."

The owners "give the public what it wants, because ... their power is in the medium and not in the message."
  - Evidence: Newton Minow / "the Wasteland of the Media"; the Artemus Ward joke about Shakespeare as a daily-newspaper correspondent; the English-conversation reductio; the closing thesis line.
  - Source passage: > "Failure in perception occurs precisely in giving attention to the program 'content' of our media while ignoring the form, whether it be radio or print or the English language itself."

- **All media (not just modern ones) make and stage a fictive image of the community; Boorstin's "pseudo-event" charge betrays ignorance of pre-electric media.**
  - Reasoning structure: McLuhan rebuts Boorstin: "the pseudo or fictitious character has always permeated the media." Long before corporate image-making, "the press had created the image of the community as a series of on-going actions unified by datelines." "He made the news" is "strangely ambiguous" — to be in the paper is "both to be news and to make news"; "the press is a daily action and fiction or thing made."   - Evidence: Benjamin Harris's 1690 paper waiting for news; the press as ventriloquist/painter's palette; Boorstin's *The Image*; the dateline as sole organizing principle.

## Metaphors

- **Government by news leak / "the magic flexibility of the controlled news leak"** — the title figure: top secrecy converted into public participation by the day-to-day device of the leak; "Western man ... accommodat[ing] himself to the electric world of total interdependence."
  - Source passage: > "Top secrecy is translated into public participation and responsibility by the magic flexibility of the controlled news leak."
- **The press agent as ventriloquist / the newspaper as his dummy (and as a painter's palette)** — the managed-news image: "Today's press agent regards the newspaper as a ventriloquist does his dummy. He can make it say what he wants. He looks on it as a painter does his palette and tubes of pigment."
  - Source passage: > "Today's press agent regards the newspaper as a ventriloquist does his dummy. He can make it say what he wants."
- **The mosaic / the corporate image** — the controlling figure for the newspaper page: multiple discontinuous items in juxtaposition, a "communal image or cross-section," "a do-it-yourself kind of world."
  - Source passage: > "If we pay careful attention to the fact that the press is a mosaic, participant kind or organization and a do-it-yourself kind of world, we can see why it is so necessary to democratic government." *(NB OCR "kind or organization" = "kind of organization".)*
- **The book/press as public confessional and "barbaric yawps over the roofs of the world"** — the "inside story" revealed by mere form: "Montaigne giving to the private reader the delicate contours of his mind, or Hearst and Whitman resonating their barbaric yawps over the roofs of the world."
  - Source passage: > "It is the printed form of public address and high intensity with its precise uniformity of repetition that gives to book and press alike the special character of public confessional."
- **"Buying a piece of the reader" / the free show** — the advertising relation: advertisers "buy a piece of the reader, listener, or viewer as definitely as if they hired our homes for a public meeting"; the "free show" is the only devised way to pay for attention.
  - Source passage: > "they buy a piece of the reader, listener, or viewer as definitely as if they hired our homes for a public meeting."
- **"The book arrives too late" (Lamartine)** — the press's instantaneity against the book's belatedness; book and newspaper as "quite different forms."
  - Source passage: > "the French poet Lamartine had said, 'The book arrives too late,' drawing attention to the fact that the book and the newspaper are quite different forms."
- **The battery radio on the camel (the rejuvenated tribe)** — radio restoring tribal "fury of oral agitation" among the Bedouin, against the press's visual nationalism; "to use the world [word] 'nationalism' ... is to conceal the situation from ourselves."
  - Source passage: > "The battery radio carried on the camel gives to the Bedouin tribes a power and vitality unknown before."
- **"Re-cognizing experience ... an unbought grace of life" / "a delightful playback of earlier awareness"** — why we turn first to news of what we already witnessed; media use as the constant exercise of the senses.
  - Source passage: > "Experience translated into a new medium literally bestows a delightful playback of earlier awareness."
- **The wallpaper newspaper / the Eskimo's magazine-papered igloo / the kitchen-floor paper** — the press's persistence as environment and mosaic even when not read for content; even floor-laid, "an ordinary newspaper ... will reveal news items that one had missed when the paper was in hand."
  - Source passage: > "Wallpaper manufacturers have recently begun to issue wallpaper that presents the appearance of a French newspaper. The Eskimo sticks magazine pages on the ceiling of his igloo to deter drip."
- **"Liaisons dangereuses among the media" / the book-page's "scandalous affairs ... with electronic creatures from the other side of the linotype"** — the cross-media hybridizing the "austere continuity of book culture ... scorns to notice"; echoes the Ch5 *Hybrid Energy* figure.
  - Source passage: > "it scorns to notice these liaisons dangereuses among the media, especially the scandalous affairs of the book-page with electronic creatures from the other side of the linotype."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Fragmentation vs Wholeness (Book / Private Point of View vs Press / Communal Mosaic)** — the chapter's master opposition: the book as "a private voice" providing "a 'point of view'" vs. the press as "a communal mosaic or corporate image" of "participation in process." McLuhan calls them "as incompatible as any two media could be." This is a particular instance of the Part I **Fragmentation vs Wholeness (Visual vs Acoustic / Print vs Electric)** tension and the print/**Typographic Man** apparatus.
  - Source passage: > "As forms, as media, the book and the newspaper would seem to be as incompatible as any two media could be."
- **Hot vs Cold (intensity / bad news vs. participation)** — the newspaper as "a hot medium" requiring "bad news for the sake of intensity and reader participation"; ads "always good news" must "match the penetrating power of bad news." Maps to the Part I tension **Hot vs Cold**. NB the chapter also stresses the press's *high participation* (a cold-medium trait in the Part I scheme) via its mosaic form — an internal complexity to flag.
  - Source passage: > "the newspaper is a hot medium. It has to have bad news for the sake of intensity and reader participation."
- **Numbness vs Awareness (the form-blind critic vs. the student of media)** — critics (Newton Minow, Boorstin) and the "book-oriented man" attend to content and "ignore the form"; the secondary-school students "could not for a moment accept the suggestion that the press ... could be used with base intent." "Unawareness of the nature of the press in its subliminal or latent action is as common among politicians as among political scientists." Maps to the Part I tension **Numbness vs Awareness**.
  - Source passage: > "Unawareness of the nature of the press in its subliminal or latent action is as common among politicians as among political scientists."
- **Mechanical vs Electric (the printed-uniform book/equitone vs. the telegraph-driven mosaic)** — Addison/Steele's "equitone" brought language "into line with print"; "The telegraph broke language away again from the printed word" into "headlines, journalese, and telegraphese." The mechanical, uniform, lineal print order vs. the electric, discontinuous, mosaic order. Maps to the Part I tension **Mechanical vs Electric**.
  - Source passage: > "The telegraph broke language away again from the printed word, and began to make erratic noises called headlines, journalese, and telegraphese." *(NB OCR stray underscore "_telegraphese" = "telegraphese".)*
- **Acoustic vs Visual (Oral Russia/Europe vs. Visual America; press as visual unifier vs. radio as tribal/oral)** — the press's diverse cross-cultural effects: literate-but-oral America vs. oral-but-literary Russia; the press creates "a visual, not-too-involved kind of unity" hospitable to many tribes, while "Radio restores tribal sensitivity and exclusive involvement in the web of kinship." Maps to the Part I tension **Acoustic vs Visual Space** and the **Global Village** retribalization theme.
  - Source passage: > "The press, on the other hand, creates a visual, not-too-involved kind of unity that is hospitable to the inclusion of many tribes, and to diversity of private outlook."
- **Representation/Delegation vs Immediate Total Involvement (slow vs. electric information speed)** — slow information makes "delegation and representation mandatory"; electric speed pushes "toward immediate involvement of the entire community in the central acts of decision," reducing the old representational order to "subterfuges and makeshifts." A within-chapter governance tension; relates to **The Global Village** and **Government by News Leak**.
  - Source passage: > "Slower speeds of information make delegation and representation mandatory."
- **Good News vs Bad News (ads vs. news; the Presidency vs. the Senate)** — ads are "always good news"; "Real news is bad news"; the Presidency's disadvantage is that "it tries to be a source of good news," while Senators have the run of "the seamy side." A content-level corollary of the Hot/Cold intensity logic; candidate sub-tension, likely folded into **Hot vs Cold**.
  - Source passage: > "Ads, in contrast, have to shrill their happy message loud and dear in order to match the penetrating power of bad news." *(NB OCR "loud and dear" = "loud and clear".)*
