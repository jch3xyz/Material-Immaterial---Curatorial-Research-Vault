## Executive Summary

A very short (two-page) programmatic statement Ascott prepared for a conference he organized at the **Center for Critical Inquiry, San Francisco Art Institute, in 1977** (previously unpublished). It is a manifesto-in-miniature for a new kind of art criticism that Ascott calls **connective criticism** — an interdisciplinary "metacritique" that would interconnect the methodologies of science, psychology, linguistics, social and economic theory, metaphysics, and philosophy into a "unified field of inquiry." The piece argues for a shift of critical attention "from form to function," from the *what* and *how* of art to the **why**.

The chapter is significant for the Ascott arc because it transposes his 1960s **cybernetic / behaviourist art** vocabulary directly onto criticism itself: art is declared "a system" that "finds its form in the interaction of many parts," and criticism is positioned as a *formative* element of that system, structurally equivalent to the participating spectator who completes the work. "Critical inquiry is a part of the making of art." Art is "an open system" and so criticism must be open and speculative, never "definitive"; speculative criticism "acts as a springboard to further dimensions of creative work, triggering new behaviors and attitudes of mind" — a direct echo of his behaviourist-art language. The chapter therefore extends the systems/cybernetic-art lineage shared with Jack Burnham (whose 1968 systems esthetics likewise reframes the object as an open, interactive system) and supplies the participatory-feedback rationale (viewer as completing agent) in the register of criticism. It is brief, so its principal yield for the vault is one or two durable concepts (Connective Criticism; the open-system / participatory account of criticism) plus extensions to existing systems-art notes, rather than a large note set.

## Key Concepts

- **Connective Criticism** — Ascott's proposed interdisciplinary mode of art criticism: the intersection and combination of specialized inquiries into a "unified field" / "metacritique" that holds together the contradictions of separate approaches while identifying correspondences and new relationships. The chapter's titular and central coinage.
  - Source passage: > "Such intersection would constitute a field of connective criticism, which would not only contain the ambiguities, polarities, and contradictions of these separate approaches, but would identify correspondences, similarities, and new relationships."
  - Source passage: > "Other critical disciplines will be encouraged to lend their conceptual frameworks and methodologies to the search for such a metacritique."

- **Art as an Open System** — the cybernetic/systems claim that art is a system whose form emerges from the interaction of many parts, is open-ended, and is completed by the participant; criticism is one of those formative parts. This is the conceptual bridge from Ascott's behaviourist/cybernetic art to his theory of criticism.
  - Source passage: > "Art is a system and finds its form in the interaction of many parts. The role of criticism in this system, like the role of the viewer or participant, is formative."
  - Source passage: > "Art is an open system."
  - Source passage: > "Critical inquiry is a part of the making of art."

- **Criticism as Formative Participation (the critic as completing agent)** — criticism does not merely elucidate; it "augments the many contexts within which [art] can be experienced and understood." The critic occupies the same structural role Ascott gives the spectator in behaviourist art: a feedback element that helps complete and evolve the work.
  - Source passage: > "The critical analysis of art not only clarifies but augments the many contexts within which it can be experienced and understood. Critical inquiry is a part of the making of art."
  - Existing wiki note: closely related to `Reciprocal Communication` (Burnham-anchored, to be Ascott-extended) and the participatory account in `New Media Art` / `Art as Experiential Technology`.

- **Speculative Criticism / Criticism as Agent of Change** — the claim that criticism, properly used, is "the agent of speculation and change," a "springboard" that triggers "new behaviors and attitudes of mind." The behaviourist-art vocabulary ("triggering new behaviors") applied to the critical act.
  - Source passage: > "It has a vital cultural function as the agent of speculation and change, pointing to future possibilities for the artist, perceiving new social needs, new sources of ideas, paths to awareness, and areas of interaction."
  - Source passage: > "Speculative criticism acts as a springboard to further dimensions of creative work, triggering new behaviors and attitudes of mind."
  - Existing wiki note: `(none)`
  - Recommended action: fold into `Connective Criticism` (the speculative/change-agent function is one facet of the connective program), or note as a sub-claim; not a separate note on its own.

- **Shift from Form to Function (the turn to "why")** — Ascott's restatement of the systems-esthetics move away from formal/stylistic analysis toward function, purpose, and meaning; criticism should "pursue the why," not just the what or how.
  - Source passage: > "But for those of us seeking a more comprehensive insight there is a shift of interest from form to function."
  - Source passage: > "Rather than limiting our inquiry to the what or how or art, we wish to pursue the why."
  - Existing wiki note: parallels Burnham's `Object and System` / `Systems Esthetics` (object→system, form→behaviour) and `Form Exhaustion`.

## Definitions

- **Connective criticism** — explicitly defined in the text as the field constituted by the intersection of specialized critical inquiries: it "would not only contain the ambiguities, polarities, and contradictions of these separate approaches, but would identify correspondences, similarities, and new relationships."
- **Metacritique** — used (not formally defined) as the goal-object of connective criticism: a higher-order critical framework drawing on the conceptual frameworks and methodologies of multiple disciplines. Strongly clarified rather than dictionary-defined.
- **Definitive critique** — defined negatively as a self-contradiction, given art's open-endedness: "A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms."

## Arguments

- **Claim: Criticism is a formative part of the art system, not external commentary on it.**
  - Reasoning structure: premise — art is an open system whose form arises from the interaction of many parts; premise — the viewer/participant is one formative part that completes the work; therefore — criticism, occupying the analogous structural role, is likewise formative and "a part of the making of art."
  - Evidence: the systems analogy; the assertion that criticism "augments the many contexts" of experience.
  - Source passage: > "Art is a system and finds its form in the interaction of many parts. The role of criticism in this system, like the role of the viewer or participant, is formative."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Criticism is a formative part of the art system` (or fold into `Connective Criticism`).

- **Claim: Because art is an open system, criticism must be open-ended and speculative; a definitive critique is impossible.**
  - Reasoning structure: art is open-ended by nature → there are no limits to the experience/ideas a participatory artwork generates → therefore criticism must mirror that openness, and any "definitive" critique contradicts itself.
  - Evidence: the explicit pairing of art's open-endedness with the participant's role in its evolution.
  - Source passage: > "Art is a starting point; by its very nature, it is open-ended. Criticism must be as well. A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms, just as there are no limits to the experience and ideas an artwork can generate for the observer when he can participate in its evolution."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: fold into `Connective Criticism`.

- **Claim: A unified, interdisciplinary "connective" criticism is needed because criticism and the understanding of art are in crisis.**
  - Reasoning structure: diagnosis — critical analysis receives "scant attention," there is "a crisis in criticism" and "a crisis in the understanding of art"; the purposes/meanings/functions/sources of art are "obscure"; remedy — combine the resources of specialized inquiries into a unified field that draws on all the resources of the human mind.
  - Evidence: the rhetorical inventory of disciplines (science, psychology, language, social/economic theory, metaphysics, philosophy); the observation that valuable specialized inquiries exist but are siloed within "narrow terms of reference."
  - Source passage: > "If there is a crisis in criticism, there is certainly a crisis in the understanding of art."
  - Source passage: > "How much more valuable would these studies be if they could combine their resources into a unified field of inquiry."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: this is the core argument of `Connective Criticism`; capture there.

- **Claim: A fully creative culture requires informed participants with access to ideas across many domains of knowledge.**
  - Reasoning structure: meanings/values/realities can only "freely unfold and blossom" if the participants are informed; therefore an institution (a Center for Critical Inquiry) that connects disciplines serves the culture's creative capacity.
  - Evidence: the closing institutional proposal.
  - Source passage: > "But for a fully creative culture, in which meanings, values, and realities can freely unfold and blossom, the participants must be informed, with access to ideas and attitudes from many dimensions of knowledge and experience."
  - Confidence: medium (Directly stated as a normative claim; institutional in scope rather than a strong theoretical thesis)
  - Recommended wiki note: cite inside `Connective Criticism`; not a standalone argument.

## Metaphors

- **"Springboard"** — speculative criticism "acts as a springboard to further dimensions of creative work." Criticism as a launching device that propels new art, not a verdict on finished art.
  - Source passage: > "Speculative criticism acts as a springboard to further dimensions of creative work, triggering new behaviors and attitudes of mind."
- **"Reforge the broken links" / "dark corners"** — the imagery of broken links between creation and knowledge (the Frye quotation), and of art's obscure purposes as "dark corners" one must "probe." The "broken links → reforged links" image is notably resonant with Ascott's later *network*/connectivity vocabulary, even though here it is borrowed from Frye.
  - Source passage: > "to seek to 'reforge the broken links between creation and knowledge, art and science, myth and concept,' as Northrop Frye has exhorted us to do, is a necessary function of criticism at this point."
- **"Unfold and blossom"** — a quasi-organic figure for how meanings, values, and realities mature in a fully creative culture.
  - Source passage: > "in which meanings, values, and realities can freely unfold and blossom"

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Form vs Function** — the explicit pivot of the chapter: limiting inquiry to "style and form" vs. the "shift of interest from form to function" and the pursuit of "why." This is Ascott's restatement, in the register of criticism, of the same object→system / form→behaviour move that drives Burnham's systems esthetics.
  - Source passage: > "there is a shift of interest from form to function."
- **Definitive vs Open-Ended (Closed vs Open Criticism)** — a definitive critique is "a contradiction in terms" because art is "an open system"; criticism must remain open-ended and speculative.
  - Source passage: > "A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms."
- **Specialized/Siloed inquiry vs Unified/Connective field** — valuable but isolated "pockets of highly specialized inquiry" within "narrow terms of reference" vs. their combination into "a unified field of inquiry."
  - Source passage: > "there are pockets of highly specialized inquiry into art currently being pursued which, within their narrow terms of reference, are extremely valuable."
