## Executive Summary

This is a Part II case-study chapter on a single medium — **the print**, in the specific sense McLuhan intends: not typography (that is the next chapter, "The Printed Word"), but the *pictorial* print — the woodcut, the engraving, the map, the blueprint, the comic strip — "the art of making pictorial statements in a precise and repeatable form." The chapter's headline thesis is that the supreme and most easily overlooked quality of the print is **repeatability**: "It is simply that it is a pictorial statement that can be repeated precisely and indefinitely... Repeatability is the core of the mechanical principle that has dominated our world, especially since the Gutenberg technology." This restates the Part I apparatus: the print is the visual extension of man that fragments an integral action into uniform, repeatable units; its *message* is repeatability itself, independent of any particular picture's content.

The chapter is theoretically load-bearing for three reasons beyond simple reinforcement. First, it is one of the clearest places where McLuhan applies the **hot/cold distinction to a visual print medium**, and does so counter-intuitively: the *old woodcut* and the modern comic strip/comic book are COOL — "very little data about any particular moment in time, or aspect in space," compelling "a high degree of participation by the viewer in order to complete what is only hinted at" — and McLuhan explicitly says, against popular intuition, that "typography as a hot medium involves the reader much less than did manuscript," and that "the comic book and TV as cool media involve the user, as maker and participant, a great deal." The woodcut/cartoon is read as the structural ancestor of the TV image's "mosaic mesh of dots." Second, the chapter contains McLuhan's most developed statement of the **visualization of space**: medieval and "native" man inhabited a world in which "each thing made its own space" — discontinuous, heterogeneous, non-objective — whereas intensified visual/retinal information "transformed the print into a three-dimensional world of perspective and fixed point of view," producing "uniform, continuous and 'rational' space." Einstein and relativity are then read as announcing "the dissolution of uniform Newtonian space as an illusion." Third, the chapter offers a compact statement of the **storage/expedite function of media** — maps, blueprints, the alphabet as a visual store of information — with the aphorism "to store is to expedite."

Everything maps cleanly onto the Part I concepts. The one genuinely distinctive contribution worth flagging is **The Print as Repeatable Pictorial Statement** (repeatability as the mechanizing principle, with its surprising consequence that the *pictorial* print and its woodcut/comic descendants are cool/participational while typography is hot). A second candidate — **Each Thing Makes Its Own Space** (discontinuous/acoustic-tactile space vs uniform perspectival visual space) — is a strong, memorable McLuhan formulation but is better realized as a section/citation anchor inside the Part I `Acoustic vs Visual Space` concept than as a competing node. The chapter also supplies a rich vein of cross-author hooks: maps-as-media and the "store is to expedite" line connect to Hayles (informatics/storage) and to the Information cluster; the cartographic/native anecdotes (Prince Modupe, the Melanesian carver) connect to the visual-space-vs-tactile-space and detribalization themes.

## Key Entities

- **Ferdinand and Isabella** — "maritime monarchs" in whose era maps were "top-secret, like new electronic discoveries today"; the black-market map trade.
- **Prince Modupe** — author of the autobiography *I Was a Savage* (OCR renders the title as "| Was a Savage"); his map anecdote ("Maps are liars... The things that hurt one do not show on a map. The truth of a place is in the joy and the hurt that come from it") frames the chapter's account of non-perspectival space. (Same figure used in the Ch9 frame anecdote.)
- **Pliny the Elder** — quoted (via the *Natural History*) on the inability of Greek and Latin botanists to transmit visual information about plants in words ("a bare recital of their names").
- **Gutenberg** — the typographic watershed; the chapter situates the print in relation to "Gutenberg's development of printing from movable types" and to woodcut printing that preceded it.
- **Andrea Mantegna** — illuminator of manuscripts whose letters "rise like monuments, stony, stable and finely cut" (the iconic/sculptural letter).
- **Millard Meiss** — art historian quoted on Mantegna's lettering.
- **Arthur Rimbaud** — "Rimbaud's sonnet on the vowels" (Voyelles) cited as a foretaste of the return of the iconic letter.
- **Georges Braque** — "some of Braque's paintings" cited likewise.
- **Roberto Valturio (rendered "Volturius")** — the *Art of War* (De re militari), printed at Verona in 1472 "with many woodcuts to explain the machinery of war."
- **René Descartes** — surveyed the seventeenth-century "confusion of tongues" and strove "toward a reduction of philosophy to precise mathematical form," fragmenting philosophy into specialist sciences.
- **Hieronymus Bosch** — painter who "interfused medieval forms in Renaissance space," giving the older plastic/tactile image inside the new visual perspective; "looked ahead to the modern world with horror."
- **Lewis Carroll** — *Alice in Wonderland*; "the mathematical contemporary of Clerk Maxwell," who "greeted the electronic age of space-time with a cheer" and gave "a playful foretaste of Einsteinian time-and-space."
- **James Clerk Maxwell** — named as Carroll's mathematical contemporary.
- **Albert Einstein / relativity theory** — "Relativity theory in 1905 announced the dissolution of uniform Newtonian space as an illusion."
- **William Shakespeare (*King Lear*) and Alexander Pope (*The Dunciad*)** — named, with Bosch, as artists who looked ahead to the modern world "with horror."
- **Kafka, Joyce, Eliot** — anticipated by Carroll's "fantasia of discontinuous space-and-time."
- **Picasso and the Marx brothers and MAD** — the close: after Einstein "the way was made clear for Picasso and the Marx brothers and MAD."
- **Cultural/technical forms**: maps, blueprints, geometry; the *Biblia Pauperum* ("Bibles of the Poor"); Books of Hours, Emblems, Shepherds' Calendars; the woodcut; the comic strip and comic book; the photograph; the TV image; the postage stamp; early radio receiving sets; the assembly line / labor-saving devices / automation.
- **The Melanesian carver** — anthropological film: a tribe that could carve a decorated drum "with such skill, coordination, and ease" that audiences applauded, but could not build a crate (two planks at a 90-degree angle) — "They couldn't crate what they had created."
- **Nigerian students** — unable to indicate the direction of shadows (three-dimensional perspective casting); illustration that "sun, objects, and observer are experienced separately."

## Arguments

- **Claim: Repeatability is the core of the mechanical principle; the print's (and typography's) message is repeatability itself.**
  - Reasoning structure: The print is defined by its precise, indefinite repeatability. Movable type generalized this into a method for "mechanizing any handicraft" by "segmenting and fragmenting an integral action." Therefore the durable effect (message) of the print is not any picture's content but the principle of repeatable, fragmentable, uniform production that "has dominated our world."
  - Evidence: the woodcut → typography sequence; the alphabet's prior separation of sights/sounds; the print's two-hundred-year career in devotional and military illustration (Valturio's *Art of War*, 1472; Books of Hours).
  - Source passage: > "The message of the print and of typography is primarily that of repeatability. With typography, the principle of movable type introduced the means of mechanizing any handicraft by the process of segmenting and fragmenting an integral action."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: tie to `Typographic Man` / **The Print as Repeatable Pictorial Statement**; coordinate with the Ch18 argument `Repeatability is the key to Western power` (do not duplicate — Ch18 frames repeatability as homogenization/Western power; Ch16 frames it as the mechanizing of pictorial statement).

- **Claim: A cool medium and a hot medium involve the viewer in inverse proportions — and the *pictorial* print's low-definition forms (woodcut, comic, TV mosaic) are cool, while typography is hot.**
  - Reasoning structure: A medium offering little data forces high participation to complete it; one offering much data permits little. The old woodcut and comic strip give little data and demand completion; the TV image's "mosaic mesh of dots" does likewise. Therefore the comic book and TV are cool/participational, and — counter to popular intuition — typography (high-definition) involves the reader *less* than handwritten manuscript did.
  - Reasoning structure (cont.): McLuhan offers the cultural symptom — "Since the advent of TV, the comic book has gone into a decline" — as evidence that two cool media (comic, TV) compete for the same participational function.
  - Evidence: the woodcut/comic strip's "few hints provided by the bounding lines"; the TV "mosaic mesh of dots"; the comic book's post-TV decline.
  - Source passage: > "It is, perhaps, obvious enough that if a cool medium involves the viewer a great deal, a hot medium will not. It may contradict popular ideas to say that typography as a hot medium involves the reader much less than did manuscript, or to point out that the comic book and TV as cool media involve the user, as maker and participant, a great deal."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: corroborating source for `Hot and Cold Media` and the def `Hot and Cold Media (McLuhan)`; cross-link Ch17 (Comics) and Ch31 (Television). Do not mint a separate argument unless the coordinator wants `arguments/A cool medium involves the viewer a hot medium does not`.

- **Claim: Increasing visual precision dissolved the older heterogeneous space (where each thing made its own space) into uniform, continuous, perspectival space — which relativity then revealed to be itself an illusion.**
  - Reasoning structure: Medieval/"native" perception had no homogeneous container-space; each object generated its own space (still true for "the native" and "the modern physicist"). As retinal/visual information intensified, objects ceased to cohere in self-made space and became contained in uniform "rational" space (perspective, fixed point of view). Einstein/relativity (1905) then announced the dissolution of that uniform Newtonian space "as an illusion or fiction, however useful," reopening discontinuous space-time — clearing the way "for Picasso and the Marx brothers and MAD."
  - Evidence: the Nigerian-students shadow-direction anecdote; the Melanesian carver who could not crate his carving ("They couldn't crate what they had created"); Bosch (medieval forms in Renaissance space, "with earnest nightmare intensity"); Lewis Carroll's "fantasia of discontinuous space-and-time" anticipating Einstein.
  - Source passage: > "For medieval man, as for the native, space was not homogeneous and did not contain objects. Each thing made its own space, as it still does for the native (and equally for the modern physicist)."
  - Confidence: high (with OCR-quote caution — see Human Review)
  - Recommended wiki note: anchor inside `Acoustic vs Visual Space`; optionally `arguments/Visual precision dissolved heterogeneous space into uniform perspectival space`. Cross-link `Sense Ratios`, `Typographic Man`, and the Mechanical-vs-Electric tension (relativity = the electric-age reopening of discontinuous space-time).

- **Claim: The visual bias of Western science is an effect of the print/alphabet — because visual information cannot be stored verbally.**
  - Reasoning structure: Words can tell how to make a bucket but cannot describe one; Pliny records that Greek/Latin botanists could not transmit plant information verbally. This "inadequacy of words to convey visual information" blocked ancient science; Western science overcame it only via the visual store of the alphabet/print. Hence "science in the Western world has long been dependent on the visual factor," and electricity's "multiple non-visual means of storing and retrieving information" now shift science's base.
  - Evidence: the bucket example; Pliny the Elder on botanists; the alphabet "reduces even spoken language to a visual mode."
  - Source passage: > "The fact that visual information about flowers and plants cannot be stored verbally also points to the fact that science in the Western world has long been dependent on the visual factor."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: tie to **Extensions of Man** (visual) and `Sense Ratios`; cross-link the `Information` / Hayles `Informatics` storage cluster. Optionally `arguments/Western science is dependent on the visual factor of the alphabet and print`.

- **Claim: Maps are media that abstract and store space — and that abstraction effaces the lived, felt truth of place.**
  - Reasoning structure: Maps were once "top-secret, like new electronic discoveries today" because, as media that store and expedite spatial information, they are instruments of power. But the uniform/continuous space of the modern map "was unknown to the medieval cartographer"; Prince Modupe's father rejects the map ("Maps are liars"), insisting "The truth of a place is in the joy and the hurt that come from it." McLuhan reads this as the clash between abstract visual space and lived tactile/experiential space.
  - Evidence: the Ferdinand-and-Isabella black-market map trade; the medieval cartographer whose maps "resembled modern nonobjective art"; the Modupe anecdote.
  - Source passage: > "Maps are liars, he told me briefly... The things that hurt one do not show on a map. The truth of a place is in the joy and the hurt that come from it."
  - Confidence: high (the value-judgment is Modupe's father's; McLuhan reports it)
  - Recommended wiki note: capture in source summary; link `Acoustic vs Visual Space` (lived tactile vs abstract visual space) and the store/expedite media-function. Possible memorable image — flag for human review.

## Metaphors

- **"Maps are liars" / "The truth of a place is in the joy and the hurt"** (Prince Modupe's father) — the lived, tactile, experiential truth of a place set against the abstract uniform visual space of the cartographic medium. Strong candidate image for the visual-vs-tactile-space theme. Source: > "Maps are liars, he told me briefly... The truth of a place is in the joy and the hurt that come from it."
- **"They couldn't crate what they had created"** (the Melanesian carver) — the tribal carver's effortless mastery of traditional/non-perspectival form, set against the inability to build a crate (two planks at a 90-degree angle); the comedy of the missing Euclidean/perspectival habit. Memorable McLuhan epigram. Source: > "they struggled unsuccessfully for three days to make two planks intersect at a 90-degree angle, then gave up in frustration. They couldn't crate what they had created."
- **"Each thing made its own space"** — the figure for heterogeneous, non-objective, discontinuous medieval/tribal space (vs the uniform container of perspective). Recurs as the chapter's master image. Source: > "Each thing made its own space, as it still does for the native (and equally for the modern physicist)."
- **The iconic letter ("rise like monuments, stony, stable and finely cut")** — Mantegna's illuminated letters given "plastic stress to an almost sculptural degree"; the letter as engraved icon, "very near to auditory resonance, as it is also to tactile and sculptural quality." The return of the iconic letter in modern graphic arts, advertising, and newspaper headlines. Source: > "Above all, the world of the illuminated manuscript was one in which lettering itself was given plastic stress to an almost sculptural degree."
- **"The bounding lines" / "the mosaic mesh of dots"** — the woodcut/cartoon as a few hints to be completed by the viewer; the TV image as a mosaic of dots requiring the same completion. The bridge image linking old print, comic, and TV as cool media. Source: > "to complete what is only hinted at in the mosaic mesh of dots."
- **"The more there were, the fewer there are" (the great law of bibliography)** — cheap popular prints (and postage stamps, early radio sets) survive poorly precisely because they were abundant and despised. Source: > "The great law of bibliography comes into play in this matter of the printing that precedes Gutenberg: 'The more there were, the fewer there are.'"
- **Bosch / Carroll as straddling two space-worlds** — Bosch tells "what it felt like to live straddled between the two worlds of the old and the new," giving the medieval tactile image inside the new visual perspective "with earnest nightmare intensity"; Carroll inverts this, driving "a fantasia of discontinuous space-and-time" through the uniform Euclidean Victorian world. Twin figures for the space-revolution. Source: > "Hieronymus Bosch, by means of paintings that interfused medieval forms in Renaissance space, told what it felt like to live straddled between the two worlds of the old and the new during this revolution."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Hot vs Cold** — the chapter's most distinctive tension-instance: the cool woodcut/comic/TV (low data, high participation) vs the hot typography/print (high data, low participation), with the counter-intuitive corollary that typography involves the reader *less* than manuscript. Maps to Part I tension **Hot vs Cold**.
  - Source passage: > "if a cool medium involves the viewer a great deal, a hot medium will not."
- **Fragmentation vs Wholeness (Visual vs Acoustic / Print vs Electric)** — the master opposition: the integral spoken word / tactile self-made space / the whole man's "involvement" vs the print's "segmenting and fragmenting [of] an integral action," visual "blueprinting and precision" as "an explosive force that fragments the world of power and knowledge." Maps to Part I **Fragmentation vs Wholeness** and the Visual-vs-Acoustic axis.
  - Source passage: > "Intensity of stress on visual blueprinting and precision is an explosive force that fragments the world of power and knowledge alike."
- **Uniform/continuous space vs heterogeneous/discontinuous space** — "uniform, continuous and 'rational' space" of perspective and fixed point of view vs the world where "each thing made its own space." This is the spatial form of Visual vs Acoustic, and relativity's reopening of discontinuous space-time folds it into **Mechanical vs Electric**.
  - Source passage: > "in the low definition world of the medieval woodcut, each object created its own space."
- **Mechanical vs Electric** — the print's repeatable, fragmenting mechanical principle ("the mechanical principle that has dominated our world... since the Gutenberg technology") vs electricity's "multiple non-visual means of storing and retrieving information" and the relativity-age dissolution of uniform space. Maps to Part I **Mechanical vs Electric**.
  - Source passage: > "Relativity theory in 1905 announced the dissolution of uniform Newtonian space as an illusion or fiction, however useful."
- **Abstract visual truth vs lived tactile/experiential truth** — the map's measured miles vs the father's "cargo-laden, heat-weighted treks"; "The things that hurt one do not show on a map." A value-laden contrast McLuhan reports rather than asserts; resonates with **Numbness vs Awareness** (the abstracted, detached visual sense numb to lived involvement).
  - Source passage: > "The things that hurt one do not show on a map. The truth of a place is in the joy and the hurt that come from it."
- **Specialist servitude/leisure vs whole-man involvement** — the historical bracket on automation: "work meant specialist servitude, and leisure alone meant a life of human dignity and involvement of the whole man." A fragmentation-vs-wholeness reading of labor; cross-links the Automation reversal.
  - Source passage: > "leisure alone meant a life of human dignity and involvement of the whole man."
