## Executive Summary

This is not a discursive essay but a **telematic art project proposal / instruction-score**, written by Ascott in 1993 for the *Teleskulptur III* exhibition in Graz, Austria (ed. Richard Kriesche, Graz: Kulturdata, 1993). It is one of the rare *practice* documents in the collection — a working blueprint that shows what Ascott's theory of telematic art looks like when it is actually built, scheduled, and wired. The work, "Heavenly Bodies," proposes the collaborative, remote construction of "twelve new signs of the zodiac" by twelve groups of geographically dispersed participants, coordinated through a central node ("the Graz Interface Zone") via videophone and fax over strictly timed transmission periods. Each group contributes fragments — a video image of an "actual, mythical, or artificial" animal/human part, a three-second synthesized sound bite, and a "divinatory, prophetic, or anticipatory" text — which are spatially configured on a wall grid, joined into a linear figure, and absorbed into "the evolving circle of zodiac signs."

For the vault, the chapter's value is as the concrete, machinic embodiment of concepts that Ascott's essays argue abstractly: the **distributed, networked artwork** assembled from many remote authors and completed only in aggregate; the spectator/participant as co-constructor (continuous with his 1960s behaviourist/cybernetic art); the **harmony of the spheres** ("music of the spheres") synthesized from the twelve sound bites; and a deliberate fusion of high telecommunications technology with archaic divinatory/cosmological systems (the zodiac, prophecy, "the music of the spheres"). That last move — re-enchanting a global telematic network with myth, divination, and the cosmos — directly prefigures Ascott's later **technoetics / Shamantic Web / re-enchantment** vocabulary and links strongly to O'Gieblyn's re-enchantment thesis. The piece is also a precise demonstration of McLuhan's **global village** and network consciousness made literal: six transmission points (Santa Monica, Toronto, Paris, Den Bosch and Utrecht in Holland, and Gwent, Wales) feeding one interface to build a single shared image.

No new theoretical *terms* are coined here; rather, the document supplies a primary-source EXEMPLAR to cite inside `Telematic Art / Telematics`, `Network Consciousness`, and the realized art-cluster concepts (`New Media Art`, `Art as Experiential Technology`). Recommend NO new concept notes from this chapter alone — instead use it as a source citation / case study attached to those existing/forthcoming notes.

## Metaphors

- **The celestial map / illuminated zodiac on the gallery wall** — the grid of twelve videophones, each holding a fragment, read together as "an illuminated celestial map." The networked, distributed image figured as the night sky / a cosmos collaboratively charted.
  - Source passage: > "images will appear in each of the twelve videophones hung on the North Wall grid, as an illuminated celestial map, configured overall according to the grid references sent with each image."

- **The harmony / music of the spheres** — the synthesized aggregate of twelve sound bites figured as the Pythagorean *musica universalis*, the cosmic harmony of the planets. Ancient cosmology recruited to name a real-time networked sound assembly.
  - Source passage: > "The twelve received sound bites will be synthesized to produce a sound structure (harmony of the spheres) to accompany the new zodiacal sign."

- **The zodiacal creature assembled from remote parts** — a single mythic animal-figure composed from twelve fragments sent by twelve dispersed groups; an image of the collective body / networked organism stitched from distributed contributions.
  - Source passage: > "The transmitted parts will also be graphically joined in such a way that the image of a zodiacal creature will be created."

## Symbols

- **The Zodiac (teleconstructed)** — the central symbol: the twelve signs, the wheel of the heavens, here rebuilt for "the Twenty-First Century" out of networked fragments. Ancient divinatory cosmology as the scaffold for a telematic artwork — the re-enchantment move in symbolic form.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Archaic divination/cosmology vs cutting-edge telecommunications** — the project's productive paradox: the most ancient symbolic systems (zodiac, prophecy, music of the spheres) realized through the most advanced 1993 networking apparatus (videophone, fax, LED, computer). This is the generative tension that anticipates Ascott's later technoetics and bears on `Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment`.
  - Source passage: > "The text will be divinatory, prophetic, or anticipatory in register."

- **Distributed/remote authorship vs centralized assembly** — meaning is contributed from six dispersed points worldwide, yet the work only coheres at one central interface (Graz) that joins, reduces, and configures the parts. The networked-and-decentralized vs the single-node-that-totalizes; relates to the network/distributed-cognition cluster.
  - Source passage: > "Each in turn receives data from the interface."

- **Material gallery installation vs immaterial transmitted data** — the work exists simultaneously as physical apparatus on the North Wall (videophones, LED signs, monitors, digital clocks) and as immaterial, time-scheduled streams of image/sound/text data; the configuration is even "reduced to a linear figure" and absorbed into the computer. Bears on `Material vs Immaterial` (the eight-author tension) and `Object vs System`.
  - Source passage: > "At the end of the hour, the whole configuration will be loaded into the main computer, reduced to a linear figure, and placed in the evolving circle of zodiac signs."
