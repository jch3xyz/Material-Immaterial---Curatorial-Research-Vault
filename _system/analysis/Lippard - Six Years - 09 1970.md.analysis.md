## Executive Summary

This is the "1970" section (source pages 134–207): a dense documentary anthology, not an essay. Lippard compiles books, exhibition records, magazine articles, and verbatim artist statements for the calendar year, interleaving brief italicized notes of her own. The year is the high-water mark of "dematerialized" conceptual art going institutional: the chronicle records the major museum and catalogue events of 1970 — the Museum of Modern Art's **Information** (organized by Kynaston McShine), the Jewish Museum's **Software** (organized by Jack Burnham), the New York Cultural Center's **Conceptual Art and Conceptual Aspects** (Donald Karshan), Oberlin's **Art in the Mind**, the **Tokyo Biennale '70: Between Man and Matter**, La Jolla's **Projections: Anti-materialism**, and Seth Siegelaub's magazine-as-exhibition **July/August 1970** issue of *Studio International* and **18 Paris IV. 70**.

The year's argumentative center of gravity is a turn toward self-examination of the medium itself: the **catalogue/exhibition as medium** (Siegelaub), the **document/documentation** as the artwork's mode of existence, **language as art form** (Art & Language, Kosuth, Ian Wilson's "oral communication," Weiner), and **systems** (LeWitt's wall-drawing-as-instruction, Haacke). Crucially for this book's argument, 1970 contains explicit internal dissent about the very word "dematerialization": **Douglas Huebler** ("There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality") and **Burn & Ramsden** ("one can't loose material conditions by simplistic 'dematerialization'") reject the term even as Lippard's title canonizes it. Lippard's own longest statement here is her contribution to David Lamelas's *Publication* (Sept. 25, 1970), an extended meditation on whether art and literature, art and the market, art and the critic-as-artist are distinguishable — and on art's resistance to, yet entanglement with, the commodity ("It's all getting distasteful... is it still worth two hundred bucks or a grand or whatever these things are 'worth' on the market?"). The political turn is present but muted: the chronicle records Adrian Piper's and the Art Workers' protest withdrawals (work withdrawn "AS A PROTECTIVE MEASURE AGAINST THE INCREASINGLY PERVASIVE CONDITIONS OF F[EAR]"), Luis Camnitzer's "Contemporary Colonial Art," and Don Celender's social-survey "Movements."

## Key Concepts

## Definitions

- **N.E. Thing Co. nomenclature** — "VSI—Visual Sensitivity Information (term NETCo. uses instead of 'art')"; reframes all art as "information handled sensitively." Explicit redefinition substituting "information" for "art."
- **Art (Kosuth)** — three areas of current activity: "aesthetic, 'reactive,' and conceptual"; conceptual art defined as "an inquiry into the nature of art."
- **LeWitt's wall drawing** — "The plan exists as an idea but needs to be put into its optimum form"; the instruction is the art, the wall its residue.

## Arguments

- **Claim: "Dematerialization" is the wrong word — there is no art without physicality.**
  - Reasoning structure: internal dissent from participating artists; objects "disappear" not by losing matter but by a shift in the semiotic/conceptual structure.
  - Evidence: Huebler and Burn & Ramsden statements (quoted above).
  - Source passage: > "There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality." (Huebler)
  - Confidence: high (verbatim)
  - Recommended wiki note: feeds `Material vs Immaterial` (art section) — argument that the immaterial pole is itself contested by practitioners.

- **Claim: With conceptual art the critic/intermediary becomes unnecessary; the audience does its own work.**
  - Reasoning structure: documents transmit faster, the primary experience is the audience's own.
  - Evidence: Lippard's WBAI remarks.
  - Source passage: > "Then critics [become] unnecessary because the primary experience is their audience's own. The [responsibility] lies with the audience instead of an intermediary." (Lippard, WBAI symposium)
  - Confidence: high (verbatim, lightly OCR-gapped)
  - Recommended wiki note: supports a `Conceptual Art` / `The Document` note in synthesis.

- **Claim: What a thing is called (art vs literature vs criticism) matters more than what it is, because naming determines context, market value, and identity.**
  - Reasoning structure: Lippard's recursive series of questions about media, plagiarism, exchange, and the curator/critic-as-artist.
  - Evidence: the Lamelas *Publication* statement.
  - Source passage: > "It's not the medium that counts, and it's not the message that counts, it's how either or both are presented, in what context, that counts."
  - Confidence: high (verbatim)
  - Recommended wiki note: candidate `arguments/` note in synthesis (context determines art-status).

## Metaphors

- **"Art the container"** (Lippard) — language "has to be trapped firmly in the bounds of Art the container to be an Art Form." Art as a container/frame that confers status. Strong figure for the exhibition-as-medium theme.
- **The mask / camouflage of language-as-art** (Michel Claura) — "this mask is its form. Its form is [its] language"; art as "a language of dissimulation." A counter-figure to transparency.
- **"Withdrawal as an Art Form"** (Nauman, also echoed by Piper's protest withdrawal) — absence/withholding as a positive medium.

## Symbols

- **The blank / the empty** — Ian Wilson, *Art and Project Bulletin 30* (Nov 30, 1970): "(Completely blank.)"; Arnatt's "I have done nothing"; Baldessari's cremation of his earlier work. The blank page/empty gallery as conceptual statement.
- **The telegram / postcard / mailing** — On Kawara's "I am not going to commit suicide" telegrams and daily postcards; NETCo. telex hookups; the document-in-the-mail as artwork.
- **The countdown clock** — Arnatt's *1220400-0000000* "exhibition of the duration of the exhibition"; time itself as the displayed object.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial (art instance)** — the book's title asserts "dematerialization," yet 1970's most articulate participants (Huebler, Burn & Ramsden) deny that matter can be lost. Poles: dematerialization / irreducible physicality.
  - Key passage: > "There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality." (Huebler)
- **Idea vs Desire / Idea vs Object** — Andre vs the conceptualists at WBAI: "I have very few ideas but I have some strong desires"; "the object is just a locus."
- **Art vs Literature / Art vs Criticism** — Lippard's central worry in the Lamelas piece: are they interchangeable, and does it matter? "Artists want to be called artists. Writers want to be called writers. Even if it doesn't matter."
- **Resistance vs Recuperation (market)** — dematerialized art positioned against the commodity, yet "Cash support demands 'goods'" and the immaterial re-enters exchange ("worth two hundred bucks or a grand").
- **Autonomy vs Politics** — the muted political turn: protest withdrawals (Piper), Camnitzer's "Contemporary Colonial Art," vs the largely formalist-linguistic bulk of the year's output.
