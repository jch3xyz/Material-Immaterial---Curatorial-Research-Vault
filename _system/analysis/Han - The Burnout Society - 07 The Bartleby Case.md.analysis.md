## Executive Summary

This short chapter (printed pp. 25–29) is the literary-critical climax of *The Burnout Society*'s diagnostic argument. Han submits Melville's "Bartleby, the Scrivener" to a **pathological reading**, against the dominant tradition of "metaphysical and theological interpretations." His central polemical target is **Giorgio Agamben**, whose *ontotheological* reading elevates Bartleby's "I would prefer not to" into the highest **potentiality** — the angelic figure of pure potency, the "writing tablet" voided of predicate, the herald of a second Creation (*de-creation*, *Ent-Schöpfung*).

Han argues this reading "already founders on the facts of the narrative." His decisive move is *periodizing*: Bartleby belongs to a **disciplinary society**, not the **achievement society** Han is diagnosing. The story is "A Story of Wall-Street" — saturated with the disciplinary architecture of walls, partitions, screens, the "dead wall," and the prison called the "Tombs." Its inhabitants have "all degraded to the state of *animal laborans*," suffering the *neurotic* disorders (hyperactivity, psychosomatic complaints) of an obedience-subject world. Bartleby's symptoms are those of **neurasthenia** — "a lack of drive and apathy" — NOT the **depression**, self-reproach, fear of failure, and "wearing-out of the ego" that constitute the *late-modern* pathology of the achievement-subject. Crucially, Bartleby "does not face the imperative to be himself," is not "burdened by the late-modern imperative of letting his self flourish [*das Ich selbst beginnen zu lassen*]," and his monotonous copying "leaves no free space in which private initiative would prove necessary." He is sick from neither **excess positivity** nor possibility.

Against Agamben's reading of "I would prefer not to" as **negative potency** (the "power not-to") or *potentia absoluta*, Han insists the phrase here "expresses neither the negative potency of not-to nor the instinct for delay and deferral that is essential for 'spirituality.'" Bartleby's "dim eyes," his refusal of every "errand," his prior employment at the **Dead Letter Office**, and the omnipresence of death convict the story of an irreducible **negativity** that "contradicts Agamben's ontotheological interpretation." The tale is therefore "not a tale of de-creation [*Ent-Schöpfung*], but rather a story of exhaustion [*Erschöpfung*]." Han clinches the reading with the German pun *Ent-Schöpfung* (de-creation) / *Erschöpfung* (exhaustion): "On errands of life, these letters speed to death" — "All efforts to live lead to death." The chapter thus polices the boundary of Han's own conceptual periodization: *Bartleby is not the achievement-subject*; he is the last man of the disciplinary epoch the achievement society has superseded.

## Definitions

- **Obedience-subject** (Gehorsamssubjekt) — the subject-form of disciplinary society; Bartleby "still represents an obedience-subject." Defined by contrast with the achievement-subject: no imperative to be oneself, no depression, no self-reproach, no "project of being an 'I.'"
  - Source passage: > He still represents an obedience-subject. He does not develop symptoms of depression, which is a hallmark of late-modern achievement society.

- **Neurasthenia** (vs. depression) — Bartleby's clinical condition: "a lack of drive and for apathy." Han explicitly distinguishes it from the *depression* of the achievement-subject ("Feelings of inadequacy, inferiority, or fear of failure do not belong to Bartleby’s emotional household").
  - Source passage: > Bartleby develops the symptoms characteristic of neurasthenia.

- **De-creation (Ent-Schöpfung)** — Agamben's category (here attributed to him): the undoing of the border between Being and Nothingness, a "second Creation."
  - Source passage: > of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung] that undoes the border between what has been and what has not, between Being and Nothingness.

- **Exhaustion (Erschöpfung)** — Han's counter-category: the story's true register; what *Müdigkeitsgesellschaft* names. Paronomastically opposed to *Ent-Schöpfung*.
  - Source passage: > This “Story of Wall-Street” is not a tale of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung], but rather a story of exhaustion [Erschöpfung].

## Arguments

- **Bartleby admits a pathological reading, not only a metaphysical/theological one.**
  - Reasoning structure: Han concedes the dominant reception (metaphysical/theological) but asserts an alternative grounded in the story's *facts* — its sinister office atmosphere, its neurotic figures, Bartleby's clinical symptoms.
  - Evidence: the office "deficient in what landscape painters call 'life'"; "Turkey" in "a strange, inflamed, flurried, flighty recklessness of activity"; "Nippers" with "psychosomatic digestive troubles"; Bartleby's "silent immobility" and neurasthenia.
  - Source passage: > Melville’s “Bartleby,” which has often been the object of metaphysical and theological interpretations, also admits a pathological reading.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Bartleby is a case of disciplinary neurasthenia not achievement-society depression` (anchor argument)

- **Bartleby belongs to disciplinary society — he is an obedience-subject, not an achievement-subject.** (the chapter's periodizing thesis)
  - Reasoning structure: From the disciplinary *architecture* (walls/partitions/screens/dead wall/Tombs) and Bartleby's *symptoms* (neurasthenic apathy, no depression, no imperative to self), Han concludes the story diagnoses the *prior* social formation, not the late-modern one. The copying work "leaves no free space in which private initiative would prove necessary, or even possible" — therefore no excess possibility, no auto-exploitation, no "project of being an 'I.'"
  - Evidence: "Wall" as "one of the most frequently used words"; the "dead wall revery"; the "Tombs"; "He still represents an obedience-subject"; "Monotonous copying... leaves no free space in which private initiative would prove necessary."
  - Source passage: > The society that Melville describes is still a disciplinary society. Walls and partitions, the elements of disciplinary architecture, traverse the entire narrative.
  - Source passage: > Bartleby, who still lives in a society of conventions and institutions, does not know the wearing-out of the ego that leads to depressive I-tiredness.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Bartleby belongs to disciplinary society not achievement society` (or fold into the above)

- **Agamben's ontotheological reading founders on the facts of the narrative and ignores the change of mental structure.**
  - Reasoning structure: Han charges Agamben with (a) ignoring the pathological elements and (b) ignoring the *psychischer Strukturwandel* — the historical shift in mental structure. Then he marshals counter-evidence against each of Agamben's claims (potentiality / empty tablet / Angel of Annunciation / kerygma / de-creation): Bartleby's "dim eyes" deny "the purity of divine potentiality"; he refuses every "errand"; the Dead Letter Office and pervasive death prove *negativity*, not pure potency.
  - Evidence: the Agamben quotation ("the extreme figure of the Nothing... pure, absolute potentiality... he is now nothing other than his white sheet"); Han's rebuttals — "dim eyes (45) already speak against the purity of divine potentiality"; "he disregards the fact that Bartleby refuses every 'errand'"; the Dead Letter Office passage; "On errands of life, these letters speed to death."
  - Source passage: > Agamben’s ontotheological interpretation of “Bartleby,” which pays no attention to pathological elements, already founders on the facts of the narrative. It also fails to take note of the change of mental structure [psychischer Strukturwandel] in the present day.
  - Source passage: > Bartleby’s “dim eyes” (45) already speak against the purity of divine potentiality he is supposed to embody.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Agamben's ontotheological reading of Bartleby founders on the negativity of the text` (and capture in the reference note `Byung-Chul Han and Giorgio Agamben`)

- **"I would prefer not to" expresses apathy and lack of drive — not the negative potency of not-to.**
  - Reasoning structure: Direct denial of Agamben's reading of the formula as *potentia* / "power not-to" / the deferral essential to "spirituality." Han instead reads it as a symptom that "seal[s] Bartleby's doom."
  - Evidence: "Rather, it stands for a lack of drive and for apathy, which seal Bartleby’s doom."
  - Source passage: > his signature phrase, “I would prefer not to,” expresses neither the negative potency of not-to nor the instinct for delay and deferral that is essential for “spirituality.”
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: capture under the `Negative Potency` concept note and the Agamben reference note

- **The tale is a story of exhaustion (Erschöpfung), not de-creation (Ent-Schöpfung).** (the clinching thesis; title-deep)
  - Reasoning structure: From the demonstrated *negativity* (being-unto-death, the realm of the dead, "all efforts to live lead to death"), Han reframes the entire story under his book's category of exhaustion — punning *Ent-Schöpfung* / *Erschöpfung*. The ending ("Ah Bartleby! Ah humanity!") is "both a lament and an indictment," not messianic hope.
  - Evidence: "On errands of life, these letters speed to death"; "All efforts to live [Bemühungen ums Leben] lead to death"; "The narrative does not open onto messianic hope"; the falling "last column of some ruined temple"; the "wreck in the mid Atlantic."
  - Source passage: > “On errands of life, these letters speed to death”; this is the central message of the tale. All efforts to live [Bemühungen ums Leben] lead to death.
  - Source passage: > This “Story of Wall-Street” is not a tale of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung], but rather a story of exhaustion [Erschöpfung].
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Bartleby is a story of exhaustion not de-creation` (anchor argument; title-deep to the book)

- **Kafka's "Hunger Artist" harbors fewer illusions than Bartleby; the panther embodies a desire-free joy of living, against the merely apparent freedom of refusal.**
  - Reasoning structure: Comparative literary case. The hunger artist's death "provides a great relief" and makes room for the panther's "joy of living free of desire." Han contrasts the panther's vital freedom with the hunger artist's freedom "only from the negativity of refusal," which is "just as insubstantial [scheinhaft]" as the freedom the panther "guards 'in his jaws.'" Bartleby is aligned with the hunger artist via "Mr. Cutlets."
  - Evidence: the Hunger Artist quotation (the panther "furnished almost to bursting... the joy of living streamed with such ardent passion"); "the hunger artist derives a feeling of freedom only from the negativity of refusal; this feeling is just as insubstantial [scheinhaft]."
  - Source passage: > In contrast, the hunger artist derives a feeling of freedom only from the negativity of refusal; this feeling is just as insubstantial [scheinhaft] as the freedom that the panther guards “in his jaws.”
  - Confidence: medium (a supporting comparative move; the Kafka/panther material is developed more fully elsewhere in Han)
  - Recommended wiki note: capture as a metaphor/symbol; possibly a small argument note if it recurs across the book

## Metaphors

- **The "dead wall" / the wall associated with death** — disciplinary architecture as mortification. "The wall is always associated with death." Bartleby's "dead wall revery"; he stares at the "dead brick wall."
  - Source passage: > Reference often occurs to the “dead wall”: “The next day I noticed that Bartleby did nothing but stand at his window in his dead wall revery” (28).

- **The office as "a huge square cistern" / "deficient in... 'life'"** — the lifeless workspace; the disciplinary world hostile to life.
  - Source passage: > The workspace, which seems like “a huge square cistern,” proves “deficient in what landscape painters call ‘life’” (5).

- **The panther (Kafka) vs. the freedom "in his jaws"** — the joy of living free of desire, against the merely apparent (*scheinhaft*) freedom of refusal.
  - Source passage: > the joy of living streamed with such ardent passion from his throat that for the onlookers it was not easy to stand the shock of it.

- **The empty writing tablet / "white sheet" (Agamben's image, rejected)** — Bartleby "has become the writing tablet; he is now nothing other than his white sheet." Han turns it: the "white sheet" only because "voided of any and all relation to the world or meaning."
  - Source passage: > The scrivener has become the writing tablet; he is now nothing other than his white sheet.

- **The Angel of Annunciation / kerygmatic messenger (Agamben's image, rejected)** — "Agamben makes Bartleby into an angelic messenger, the Angel of Annunciation, who... 'predicates nothing of nothing.'"
  - Source passage: > He embodies “pure being without any predicate.” Agamben makes Bartleby into an angelic messenger, the Angel of Annunciation.

- **The falling column / the mid-Atlantic wreck (Bartleby's death)** — "the 'last column of some ruined temple' falls. He goes under like a 'wreck in the mid Atlantic.'"
  - Source passage: > When Bartleby dies, the “last column of some ruined temple” falls. He goes under like a “wreck in the mid Atlantic.”

- **Ent-Schöpfung / Erschöpfung (de-creation / exhaustion)** — the controlling paronomasia of the chapter (and of the book's title-thesis).
  - Source passage: > This “Story of Wall-Street” is not a tale of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung], but rather a story of exhaustion [Erschöpfung].

  - Source passage: > disciplinary society is signified by the recurrent motif of the thick-walled prison called the “Tombs.” There, all life is extinguished.

- **The Dead Letter Office / "dead letters"** — the closing motif binding Bartleby to death. "Dead letters! does it not sound like dead men?" "On errands of life, these letters speed to death."

## Candidate Source Citations

> Melville’s “Bartleby,” which has often been the object of metaphysical and theological interpretations, also admits a pathological reading.

> This “Story of Wall-Street” describes an inhumane working world whose inhabitants have all degraded to the state of animal laborans.

> his signature phrase, “I would prefer not to,” expresses neither the negative potency of not-to nor the instinct for delay and deferral that is essential for “spirituality.” Rather, it stands for a lack of drive and for apathy, which seal Bartleby’s doom.

> The society that Melville describes is still a disciplinary society. Walls and partitions, the elements of disciplinary architecture, traverse the entire narrative.

> He still represents an obedience-subject. He does not develop symptoms of depression, which is a hallmark of late-modern achievement society.

> He does not face the imperative to be himself that characterizes late-modern achievement society. Bartleby does not fail in the project of being an “I.”

> What makes Bartleby sick is not excessive positivity or possibility. He is not burdened by the late-modern imperative of letting his self flourish [das Ich selbst beginnen zu lassen].

> Bartleby, who still lives in a society of conventions and institutions, does not know the wearing-out of the ego that leads to depressive I-tiredness.

> Agamben’s ontotheological interpretation of “Bartleby,” which pays no attention to pathological elements, already founders on the facts of the narrative. It also fails to take note of the change of mental structure [psychischer Strukturwandel] in the present day.

> The scrivener has become the writing tablet; he is now nothing other than his white sheet.

> Bartleby’s “dim eyes” (45) already speak against the purity of divine potentiality he is supposed to embody.

> He embodies “pure being without any predicate.” Agamben makes Bartleby into an angelic messenger, the Angel of Annunciation, who, for all that, “predicates nothing of nothing.”

> Dead letters! does it not sound like dead men?

> Bartleby’s Dasein is a negative being-unto-death. This negativity contradicts Agamben’s ontotheological interpretation, which makes Bartleby the herald of a second Creation—of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung] that undoes the border between what has been and what has not, between Being and Nothingness.

> “On errands of life, these letters speed to death”; this is the central message of the tale. All efforts to live [Bemühungen ums Leben] lead to death.

> In contrast, the hunger artist derives a feeling of freedom only from the negativity of refusal; this feeling is just as insubstantial [scheinhaft] as the freedom that the panther guards “in his jaws.”

> This “Story of Wall-Street” is not a tale of de-creation [Ent-Schöpfung], but rather a story of exhaustion [Erschöpfung]. The exclamation that ends the tale is both a lament and an indictment: “Ah Bartleby! Ah humanity!”
