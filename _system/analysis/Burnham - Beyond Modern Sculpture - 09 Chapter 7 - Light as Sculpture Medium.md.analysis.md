## Executive Summary

Chapter 7 is the third chapter of Part Two ("Sculpture as System") and tracks the entrance of *emitted light* — light directly discharged from the art object or system — into twentieth-century sculpture. Burnham's master systems thesis is stated at the outset: light is "the most flexible visual art form yet devised," and the trend of Light Art is to *dissolve the discrete art object into a responsive environmental system*. He frames Light Art as moving toward the elimination of the specific object and the transformation of the environment into "a light-modulating system sensitive to responses from organisms which invade its presence" — i.e., the object-to-system migration that organizes the whole second half of the book, now realized as responsive, life-like, participatory space.

The chapter is structured as a long historical survey (color organ → Bauhaus → postwar Europe → American light/neon art → the future). It is dense with figures (Wilfred's Lumia, Moholy-Nagy's Light-Space Modulator, Kepes, Fontana, Klein/Yves Klein, Piene's Light Ballet, Schöffer's luminodynamics, the GRAV/Le Parc labyrinths, Dan Flavin, Chryssa, Raysse) but carries several durable conceptual through-lines: (1) **luminosity** as the perceptual basis of light art and an *emotional/primeval stimulant* tied to life; (2) the recurring drive toward **total immateriality** — the dematerialization of the art object into pure light/energy — which Burnham names as "always... an aspect of the Light artist's program"; (3) the **commerce-drives-technology** thesis (light art matures only where it has a "remunerative commercial outlet," and American light art succeeds precisely through its "uninhibited adoption of commercial and advertising technique"); (4) Ceram's distinction between **mechanical and technical thinking** as the real "mutation" enabling a medium, and Burnham's claim that what changed was not invention but *public sensibility*; (5) the **art object as a system of variable life expectancy** — light is fragile, mortal, dependent on its electrical "mother," anticipating Ch. 8's cybernetic/life themes; (6) **"ephemeralization"** of the art object (light as a further step beyond linear/transparent/groundless sculpture); (7) the future horizon — laser/hologram, plasma as "the fourth state of matter," surveillance and computer-display systems as artistic frontiers, and the closing **"cultural trend toward intangibility"** in which "pure energy and information seem to be the essences of art." The chapter ends on McLuhan ("a glowing light bulb... radiated pure information") and Piene ("Light... is the incarnation of visible energy"), making this chapter the book's clearest bridge to the vault's material/immaterial and information axes.

This chapter substantively grounds the long-deferred research-foci stubs **New Media Art** and **Art as Experiential Technology**: Light Art is an electromechanical/programmed system, environments are participatory and responsive ("provoke spectators into acts of participation"; viewer-controlled rheostats; sensing photo-cells), and the medium is explicitly technological. It also strongly anchors **Dematerialization** at its pre-Lippard root (Burnham's "total immateriality" / "ephemeralization" / "intangibility").

## Definitions

- **Emitted light** — "light directly discharged from the art object or system," defined by the quality of luminosity. (Contrasted with reflected and radiant light.)
- **Luminosity** — "the visual impression received when an area of light exceeds a threshold of brilliance relative to its surroundings," appearing as internal energy in the source.
- **Lumia (Wilfred)** — Wilfred's name for his independent "Art of Light," to develop "free of all the usual comparisons with music"; an art that has "destroyed the experience of temporal duration" (time "rendered completely homogeneous, becoming formless and extinct").
- **Continuous discontinuity (Schöffer)** — indeterminate light programs "never quite the same but always appearing the same."
- **Plasma** — "the fourth state of matter: being neither solid, liquid, or gas, but the state that certain gases reach when they are 'ionized.'" Fluorescent, neon, and arc welding are "forms of low-temperature plasma."
- **Hologram (Gabor)** — "glass-plate positive for viewing pictures in three-dimensional space" (Denis Gabor, 1947).
- **Mechanical→technical mutation (Ceram)** — the change "from mechanical to technical thinking" that makes an invention "take effect" rather than merely occur.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial** — the chapter's central axis: emitted light dematerializes the object ("total immateriality," "ephemeralization," "intangibility"), yet light is paradoxically "wedded to glass tubing and cumbersome electrical equipment," "fragile," low-durability — immaterial aspiration shackled to gross material apparatus.
  - Poles: total immateriality / pure energy-information vs. fragile material electrical apparatus.
  - Source: > "Equally true, light as it exists in today's art is a relatively inflexible and fragile medium. It is still wedded to glass tubing and cumbersome electrical equipment."
  - Existing wiki note: `tensions/Material vs Immaterial.md` (7-author) — update with Burnham section.
- **Object vs System / Environment** — the discrete art object vs. its dissolution into a responsive environmental system (the "painters" of the light-screen vs. the "sculptors"/environmentalists of dispersed light).
  - Source: > "a dichotomy has evolved in Light Art between those favoring patterns of light confined to a screen and those artists interested in light's prime property of spatial dispersion."
- **Art vs Commerce / Advertising** — light matures via commercial outlets and advertising technique, yet artists insist on art-status against it (Billy Apple: "I am not making electric toasters, lamps or signs. I am an artist dealing in my own medium").
  - Source: > "I am not making electric toasters, lamps or signs. I am an artist dealing in my own medium."
- **Determinacy vs Indeterminacy (programmed light)** — Schöffer's preference for indeterminate, statistically-leveled "continuous discontinuity" over "a repetitive determinate program."
  - Source: > "the indeterminacy of unstructured light sequences merged with revolving sculptures is regarded by the artist as being superior to a repetitive determinate program."
- **Spectacle vs Participation** — GRAV's stated aim to "provoke spectators into acts of participation," not "create a super spectacle."
  - Source: > "to provoke spectators into acts of participation."
- **Time vs Timelessness (duration destroyed)** — Wilfred's Lumia abolishes temporal duration ("Time is rendered completely homogeneous, becoming formless and extinct") vs. Hirschfeld-Mack/Piene who sought acoustical-visual rhythm and durational change.
  - Source: > "Time is rendered completely homogeneous, becoming formless and extinct."
