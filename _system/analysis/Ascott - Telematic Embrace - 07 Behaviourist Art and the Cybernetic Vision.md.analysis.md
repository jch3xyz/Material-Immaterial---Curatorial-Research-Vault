## Executive Summary

This is the foundational manifesto of Ascott's 1960s "cybernetic vision" in art — a two-part essay first published in *Cybernetica* 9 (1966) and 10 (1967). It is also, chronologically, the **proto-text of the entire vault art/systems cluster**: it predates Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) by one to two years and stakes out the **participatory / behavioural / feedback pole** of 1960s cybernetic art, against which Burnham's *object→system* systems esthetics is the complementary pole. Where Burnham reads the history of sculpture as a drift from the object toward the responsive system, Ascott reads the whole of modern art as a drift from a "deterministic vision" toward a "behavioural tendency" in which the artwork is no longer a finished message but a **feedback loop completed by the spectator**.

The essay does two things, which Ascott names explicitly: (a) it diagnoses the "emergence of a cybernetic vision in art," characterised by an "evolving behavioural tendency," and (b) it proposes a speculative institution — the **Cybernetic Art Matrix (CAM)** — a networked, self-organising, world-spanning framework for creative behaviour, education, and information flow in a coming "cybernated society."

Part one builds the theory of *behaviourist art*: art as an open-ended, retroactive (feedback) process in which "the spectator now engages in decision-making play," the artist plays a game that "is never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play," and the artifact functions "as both matrix and catalyst." Ascott surveys the modern tradition under six headings — art as a behavioural analogue, trigger, environment, structure, ritual, and synthesis — citing cubism, futurism, Duchamp, kinetic art, Cage, McLuhan's electric media and "global village," and his own change-paintings. Part two defines the "cybernetic vision" not as the art *of* cybernetics or robot art but as *l'esprit cybernétique* — "the spirit of cybernetics, which may inform art and in turn be enriched by it" — grounded in "the idea of the perfectibility of systems" and "the perfectibility of man." It then elaborates CAM across four subsystems (CAM/LIGA: Learning, Information, Generative, Amenities), envisioning a network of CAMs that "would constitute the creative element in a 'world brain'."

For vault purposes the chapter is overwhelmingly a **create-or-extend** event for the Burnham/art cluster: it richly EXTENDS the just-realized committed stubs **New Media Art** and **Art as Experiential Technology** (which the HANDOFF explicitly flags Ascott to extend), and provides Ascott primary-text author-sections for **Reciprocal Communication**, **Cybernetics**, **Feedback Loops**, **Systems Theory**, **Systems Consciousness**, **Object and System / Object vs System / Material vs Immaterial**, **Cyborg Art / The Cyborg**, **Emergence**, **The Global Village**, and others. It also yields several NEW Ascott-coined concepts (Behaviourist Art, The Cybernetic Vision, The Construction of Change, The Cybernetic Art Matrix, The Artwork as Matrix and Catalyst, Art as System and Process) and a key vault-internal reference, **Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham** (the two poles of 1960s cybernetic art) plus **Roy Ascott and Marshall McLuhan** (electric media / global village → network).

## Key Entities

- **Roy Ascott** — author; a working "Behaviourist" artist (Appendix A), founder/director of studies of the Groundcourse at Ealing School of Art (1961–64) and Ipswich School of Art (1964–66); exhibited at the Centre d'Art Cybernétique, Paris (1964).
- **Edward A. Shanken** — editor of the volume; supplies the explanatory endnotes (cited here as "—Ed.").
- **Marshall McLuhan** — cited directly via *The Gutenberg Galaxy* (1962) and *Understanding Media* (1964); the "global village," "electric media," "extensions of the central nervous system," and the claim that media "alter sense ratios or patterns of perception steadily and without resistance."
- **Norbert Wiener** — named (in Appendix A, alongside C. E. Shannon and A. G. Pask) as a system whose work Ascott made analogues of; foundational to the cybernetics Ascott invokes throughout.
- **C. E. (Claude) Shannon** — *Homage to Shannon* (1963), Ascott's analogue of "the simple information flow: input (mode A) → noise → output (mode B)."
- **A. G. (Gordon) Pask** — cybernetician; named with the "Fun Palace Project" team (with architect Cedric Price) and as a system Ascott modelled.
- **W. Ross Ashby** — source of "intelligence amplifier" (the computer as a tool for the mind); also "self-organising system" framing.
- **Stafford Beer** — "fungoid systems" as a cybernetic model for biological/growth art.
- **F. H. George**, **John Diebold** — cited on the coming "cybernated society."
- **Pierre de Latil** — "Cybernetics... is the science of the organisation of effects, and of the automatic control of effects."
- **Manfred Clynes and Nathan S. Kline** — named for the "cyborg concept" (CAM/A item 12: "cybernetic machine extension of skills").
- **Artists cited** (one-off, plain text): Cézanne (the pivotal expression of change), Poussin (the deterministic foil), Picasso, Braque, Balla, Duchamp (*Nude Descending a Staircase*, *Large Glass*, the readymade), Malevich, Klee, Brancusi, Vasarely, Bridget Riley, Agam, Munari, Gerstner, Soto, Pasmore, Hill, Rothko, Newman, Still, Noland, Matisse, Schöffer (CYSP I, cybernetically controlled), Bury, Takis, Malina, Moholy-Nagy (Light-Space Modulator), Calder, Man Ray, Tinguely, Metzger, Pollock (action painting), Arp, Cage, Xenakis, Joyce, Burroughs, Godard, et al.
- **Movements / groups**: cubism, futurism, dada, surrealism, constructivism, Nouvelle Tendance, Bauhaus, the Groupe de Recherche d'Art Visuel, Gutai.
- **Technologies / institutions**: the computer ("intelligence amplifier"/"thought amplifier"), communication satellites, TV link, holography, "visual telex," the IBM 7090, the Centre d'Art Cybernétique, the journal *Cybernetica*.

The participatory pole of 1960s cybernetic art (complement to Burnham's object→system systems esthetics).
  - Source passage: > "The participational, inclusive form of art has as its basic principle 'feedback,' and it is this loop that makes an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork / observer."
  - Source passage: > "to draw the spectator into active participation in the act of creation; to extend him, via the artifact, the opportunity to become involved in creative behaviour on all levels of experience—physical, emotional, and conceptual. A feedback loop is established"

## Definitions

Terms explicitly defined or strongly clarified:

- **The Cybernetic Vision in art** — defined negatively then positively: NOT "the art of cybernetics," illustration of cybernetics, or robot art, but "the spirit of cybernetics, which may inform art and in turn be enriched by it"; a "stance" before a technique. (Candidate: `The Cybernetic Vision (Ascott)`.)
- **Behavioural (as a replacement for "visual") art** — > "a more inclusive term than 'visual' art must be found, and the one I propose is 'behavioural.'" Encompasses tactile, postural, aural modalities and the artist/artifact/spectator triad.
- **Behavioural structure** — > "By this term, I literally mean 'structures that behave,' that is, art forms that articulate their parts in response to the promptings of their internal or external environments." (= the kinetic-art region.)
- **Behaviourist art (functional)** — > "Behaviourist art constitutes... a retroactive process of human involvement, in which the artifact functions as both matrix and catalyst."
- **Cybernetics (Ascott's gloss)** — > "before it is a method or an applied science, it is a field of knowledge that shapes our philosophy, influences our behaviour, and extends our thought"; and (via de Latil) "the science of the organisation of effects, and of the automatic control of effects." (Candidate: `Cybernetics (Ascott)` definition OR author-section in `Cybernetics`.)
- **l'esprit cybernétique** — the cybernetic spirit; "It is more precise to speak about forming a cybernetic spirit than about teaching cybernetics" (Couffignal, via Ascott/Ed.).
- **CAM/LIGA** — > "CAM/LIGA serves as a sort of genetic code for the nascent organism CAM" — the four constants Learning (Education), Information (Communications), Generative (Development), Amenities (Hardware).

## Arguments

- **Modern art is unified by a single behavioural tendency — the shift from object to behaviour, from message to event.**
  - Reasoning: against the popular view of modern art as anarchic/chaotic, Ascott claims "unity in its diversity"; the unifying quality is "behavioural" and marks the transition "from the old deterministic culture to a future shaped by a cybernetic vision."
  - Evidence: the six-aspect survey (cubism → Cage); the contrast of Poussin's one-way channel with the modern artist's dialogue.
  - Source passage: > "The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour, and its function has become less descriptive and more purposive."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Modern art is unified by a behavioural tendency from object to event`.

- **The spectator completes the work; art has become a game that is never won.**
  - Reasoning: feedback makes the artist/artwork/observer triad an integral whole; the work's "final resolution must come from the observer"; the artist sets the context but not the outcome.
  - Source passage: > "Nowadays, art is moving towards a situation in which the game is never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play. While the general context of the art experience is set by the artist, its evolution in any specific sense is unpredictable and dependent on the total involvement of the spectator."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The spectator completes the work and art becomes a game never won`.

- **The cybernetic vision is a stance, not a technique — and it can unify art, science, and human values.**
  - Reasoning: distinguishes *l'esprit cybernétique* from cybernetics-as-method; argues "a unity of art, science and human values is possible" and that "an essentially cybernetic vision could unify and feed such culture," because art has already anticipated it via the behavioural tendency.
  - Source passage: > "Cybernetics is consistent with behaviourist art; it can assist in its evolution, just as, in turn, a behavioural synthesis can embody a cybernetic vision."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The cybernetic vision is a stance not a technique that can unify art science and human values`.

- **Aesthetic value is migrating from the object to the system and process.**
  - Reasoning: most material handling can be computed; the designer's creative work is "at the level of systems"; beauty "resides in our perception and awareness of a system rather than an object."
  - Source passage: > "Beauty thus defined, in which space is shaped by time and form relates to function, is the beauty of a cybernetic vision."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Aesthetic value is migrating from the object to the system and process`. (Cross-link to Burnham's object→system arguments — the complementary pole.)

- **The computer is a tool for the mind — an intelligence amplifier — not merely an extension of physical power.**
  - Reasoning: distinguishes the computer from welding gear; it is "an instrument for the magnification of thought," enabling man/computer creative interaction and even artificial creative intelligence.
  - Source passage: > "It is a tool for the mind, an instrument for the magnification of thought, potentially an 'intelligence amplifier,' to use H. Ross Ashby's term."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The computer is a tool for the mind an intelligence amplifier not an extension of physical power`.

- **Cybernation is a difference in kind, not degree — and it will be participatory and producer-oriented, not a mass-production dystopia.**
  - Reasoning: refutes "the common fallacy to equate an automated world with the old industrial era"; automation can make the unique/custom-built cheap, dissolving rigid hierarchies into a "cybernetic-politic" of participation.
  - Source passage: > "It is a common fallacy to equate an automated world with the old industrial era, holding that cybernation is merely mechanisation intensified. However, there is a difference not merely in degree but in kind"
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Cybernation is a difference in kind not degree and will be participatory not a mass-production dystopia`.

- **Art education must be re-founded on system and process, formed in the cybernetic spirit, to equip artists for twenty years hence.**
  - Reasoning: conventional art education over-specialises into obsolete "styling"; CAM/L proposes a non-hierarchical "switch-back," operant/play/creativity learning to produce broad system-designers; "the early formative years... should be imbued with a cybernetic spirit."
  - Source passage: > "What is required, it seems, is that the early formative years of an artist's development and education should be imbued with a cybernetic spirit"
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Art education must be refounded on system and process in the cybernetic spirit`. (May be folded into `Cybernetic Art Matrix` if a standalone is too granular — flag for human review.)

## Metaphors

- **The behavioural Tarot pack** — each artwork as endlessly reshuffleable coordinates that always produce meaning. > "Each artwork becomes a sort of behavioural Tarot pack, presenting coordinates that can be endlessly reshuffled by the spectator, always to produce meaning."
- **The artist as the free man controlling his world by taking endless risks** — the artist's behaviour as ritual paradigm of freedom-with-responsibility. > "the artist's behaviour is a ritual in which he acts out the role of the free man controlling his world by taking endless risks as he plunges into the unknown territories of form and idea."
- **The deterministic "deep-freeze vision"** — Renaissance perspectival art as a frozen, time-banished, mechanistic universe; the world viewed "through a window let into the wall." > "This deep-freeze vision, with its resultant sets of static relationships, required little more of the spectator than that he should step into the artist's shoes"
- **The educational ladder → the educational switch-back** — CAM/L's reversible mobility between input-types replacing the one-way hierarchy of schooling. > "The 'educational ladder' becomes the 'educational switch-back' within the CAM learning net."
- **CAM as organism / genetic code (CAM/LIGA)** — CAM as a living, growing, self-organising organism whose LIGA programme is its genetic code. > "CAM/LIGA serves as a sort of genetic code for the nascent organism CAM"
- **The world brain** — the international CAM network as the embryonic creative element of a planetary mind (see concepts). (Cross-link to McLuhan's `The Global Village`.)
- **The Pillar of Information as the memory of the organism** — CAM's data-store "acting in effect as the memory of the whole CAM organism."
- **The artist's "arena"** — the canvas/studio as the field within which the artist creates and which feeds back to him (also Pollock's action-painting arena). > "the canvas being the arena within which the artist creates."

## Symbols

- **The change-painting / hinged relief** — Ascott's own interchangeable transparent-panel works; the material emblem of behaviourist participation (Appendix A).
- **The Tarot pack** — the artwork as a reshuffleable, meaning-generating deck (also metaphor).
- **Homage to Shannon (1963)** — Ascott's analogue of information flow (input → noise → output); an artwork that is literally a diagram of cybernetic communication.
- **The vanishing point / perspective screen** — symbol of the deterministic, "rigidly mechanistic belief in the essential predictability of a causal universe."
- **The Pillar of Information** — the CAM's memory-store and access point; a symbol of pooled, uncensored, networked knowledge.
