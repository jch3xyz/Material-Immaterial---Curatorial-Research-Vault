## Executive Summary

This is the pivotal conceptual chapter of *The Agony of Eros* and the fullest primary-text statement in the vault of Han's **negative** theory of eros. Its title names the book's core positive proposal: eros is *Nicht-Können-Können* — "being able not to be able," the negative counterpart of the achievement society's boundless *You can*. Han's argument runs: (1) achievement society replaces disciplinary *should* with *can*, turning the subject into a self-exploiting "entrepreneur of the self" (a direct extension of *The Burnout Society* / *Psychopolitics*); (2) this "You can" is the very structure that annihilates the Other, because ability, mastery, grasping, and possessing all convert alterity into an object to be consumed; (3) eros, by contrast, is "a relationship to the Other situated beyond achievement, performance, and ability," and its condition is *the atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability*. Han grounds this almost entirely in **Emmanuel Levinas** (*Time and the Other* — the caress, pleasure, the "countenance," absence, futurity) and **Martin Buber** (primal distance, *Urdistanz*, the Thou vs the It). The chapter also delivers Han's sharpest reading of **Foucault** (the neoliberal *Homo oeconomicus* only *thinks* himself free), his "capitalism is not a religion" refutation of **Benjamin** (capitalism has debt but no atonement, hence depression as "psychic insolvency"), a **Hegel**-anchored insistence on the negativity of pain, and a bravura polemical reading of *Fifty Shades of Grey* as the perfect specimen of the positivized, health-obedient, negativity-free "formula for enjoyment." It closes on the economic register: eros is an "asymmetrical relationship to the Other" that "interrupts the exchange rate"; otherness "admits no bookkeeping."

## Definitions

- **Being Able Not to Be Able (*Nicht-Können-Können*) (Han)** — the negative potency proper to eros; the capacity, against the compulsive *You can*, to *not* be able — to relinquish mastery so that the Other may appear as Other. Key passage: > "Being able not to be able (Nicht-Können-Können) represents its negative counterpart. The negativity of otherness—that is, the atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability—is constitutive of erotic experience"
- **Atopia (Han via Levinas)** — the Other's placelessness; alterity that "cannot be assimilated into the language of the Same" and "eludes all ability." Key passage: > "the atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability—is constitutive of erotic experience"
- **Primal Distance / *Urdistanz* (Buber via Han)** — the distance that is "the very 'principle of being-human'" and "the transcendental condition for any alterity existing at all"; it "prevents the Other from being reified into an object, an 'it.'" Key passage: > "'Primal distantiation' prevents the Other from being reified into an object, an 'it.' The Other as sexual object is no longer a 'Thou.'"
- **Auto-Exploitation vs Allo-Exploitation (Han)** — self-exploitation "proves much more efficient than allo-exploitation because it is accompanied by a feeling of liberty." Key passage: > "Auto-exploitation proves much more efficient than allo-exploitation because it is accompanied by a feeling of liberty. This makes possible exploitation without domination." ## Arguments

- **Absolutizing ability annihilates the Other; only "being able not to be able" lets the Other appear.**
  - Reasoning structure: eros = relation to the Other beyond ability → but possessing/grasping/knowing are "synonyms of power" → therefore any object one *can* master ceases to be Other → the erotic relation must register as a "failure" in the vocabulary of power.
  - Evidence: Levinas, *Time and the Other* (the "absolutely original relationship of eros… impossible to translate into powers"; "If one could possess, grasp, and know the other, it would not be other").
  - Source passage: > "Absolutizing ability is precisely what annihilates the Other. A successful relationship with the Other finds expression as a kind of failure. Only by way of being able not to be able does the Other appear"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Absolutizing ability annihilates the Other.md` (or similar canonical phrasing)

- **The neoliberal "You can" is more coercive than the disciplinary "You should," because there is no way to resist oneself.**
  - Reasoning structure: *should* hits a productivity ceiling → replaced by *can* → *can* appears as freedom, so its compulsion is unrecognized → auto-compulsion is inescapable ("there is no way to resist oneself") → whoever fails is solely at fault, with no pardon.
  - Evidence: Foucault's *Homo oeconomicus* / "management of freedom" read against the grain; the *Be free* imperative producing depression and exhaustion.
  - Source passage: > "You can exercises even greater constraint than You should. Auto-compulsion proves more fatal than allo-compulsion, because there is no way to resist oneself."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: extends the existing Han argument cluster (`Freedom itself is producing coercion` / `The freedom of Can is more coercive than the disciplinary Should` — the latter is a committed stub in the Psychopolitics notes). Recommend REALIZING/linking `The freedom of Can is more coercive than the disciplinary Should` here, or extending `Freedom itself is producing coercion`. Human-review item on which canonical argument name to use.

- **Foucault mistook neoliberal freedom for civil liberty and so missed the violence of auto-exploitation.**
  - Reasoning structure: Foucault treats the "least state" / "management of freedom" as enabling *bürgerliche Freiheit* → but *Homo oeconomicus* "only thinks himself free when in fact he is exploiting himself" → Foucault's "ethics of the self" is blind to "the violence of the freedom that underlies auto-exploitation."
  - Evidence: *The Birth of Biopolitics*, 47, 63, 215; the "I am going to see to it that you are free to be free" citation.
  - Source passage: > "Foucault fails to notice the structure of violence and coercion underwriting the neoliberal dictum of freedom. Consequently, he interprets it as the freedom to be free"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: update `references/Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault.md` (this is the sharpest Agony-of-Eros instance of Han's standing "Foucault failed to make the turn" critique)

- **Capitalism is not a religion: it has debt without atonement, and this unredeemable debt is the ground of depression.**
  - Reasoning structure: relief/gratification presume the Other → the achievement-subject lacks a binding connection to the Other → so both a debt-crisis and a gratification-crisis occur → religion pairs guilt with pardon, but capitalism pairs debt only with default → no atonement → failure is unredeemable → hence "psychic insolvency" (depression, burnout).
  - Evidence: explicit contra Benjamin ("Capitalism as Religion"); the Latin *solvere* etymology.
  - Source passage: > "capitalism—counter to widespread belief (e.g., Benjamin)—is not a religion. … But capitalism only works with debt and default. It offers no possibility for atonement"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Capitalism is not a religion it has debt without atonement.md` (new) + update `Byung-Chul Han and Walter Benjamin`

- **Total proximity (social media, sexualization) does not bring the Other near — it abolishes the Other by abolishing distance.**
  - Reasoning structure: nearness is "negative" because remoteness is "inscribed within it" → abolishing remoteness abolishes nearness itself → what results is "crowding," positivity without the animating power of the opposite → the Other, brought maximally near, "disappears."
  - Evidence: Buber's primal distance; the sexual "it" with no "countenance"; "The power of negativity lies in the fact that things are enlivened precisely by their opposite."
  - Source passage: > "By means of social media, we seek to bring the Other as near as possible… But this does not mean that we have more of the Other; instead, we are making the Other disappear. … Instead of closeness, it entails crowding."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: interpretive synthesis inside `The Other` / `Hell of the Same`; cross-link `Pathos of Distance`.

- **Erotic desire requires the negativity of the Other's absence and futurity; the total present of consumption abolishes it.**
  - Reasoning structure: erotic desire is tied to "absence in a horizon of the future" → the future is "the time of the Other" → totalizing the present as the time of the Same eliminates the absence that makes the Other unattainable → society "as a search engine, a machine for consumption" abolishes the desire for what is absent.
  - Evidence: Levinas on the caress ("a game with something slipping away") and pleasure; the "click" temporality (additive, without moment/decision); the musealized past and the optimized-present future.
  - Source passage: > "Erotic desire is tied to a particular absence of the Other—not the absence of nothingness, but rather 'absence in a horizon of the future.' The future is the time of the Other."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated); anticipates ch. 6 (Fantasy)
  - Recommended wiki note: interpretive synthesis inside `Eros` / `The Other`; strong `Narration vs Addition` and `Fragrance of Time` cross-links.

- **Love and sexuality are subsumed under the performance principle; every negativity (injury, transgression, dirt) is eliminated.**
  - Reasoning structure: love is "positivized into sexuality" → sex = achievement, sexiness = capital, the body = display-value commodity → the Other is fragmented into "sexual part-objects" → *Fifty Shades of Grey* literalizes this: the sadomasochism is a "job offer" with health rules, hard limits, and safewords, lacking Bataille's negativity of transgression.
  - Evidence: E. L. James, *Fifty Shades of Grey* (233, 104); Hegel on pain-as-negativity; Bataille's erotics of transgression.
  - Source passage: > "The sadomasochistic practices the novel describes amount to nothing more than sexual diversions. They lack the negativity of overstepping—such as occurs in Georges Bataille's erotics of transgression."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: interpretive synthesis inside `Positivity` / `Pornography (Han)` / `Achievement Society`; the *Fifty Shades* reading is a strong candidate quotation.

## Metaphors

- **The Exploiter Is the Exploited / Perpetrator and Victim in One** — the achievement-subject collapses the master/slave positions into a single self-exploiting figure. > "The exploiter is the exploited. The achievement-subject is perpetrator and victim in one."
- **Psychic Insolvency** — depression figured as bankruptcy; the impossibility of "paying off a debt" (solvere) that can never be atoned. > "it amounts to psychic insolvency."
- **The Caress as a Game with Something Slipping Away** — Levinas's figure of erotic desire reaching "for what is vanishing into the future without end." > "The caress is a 'game with something slipping away.'"
- **The Countenance that Gives and Conceals vs the Pornographic Face** — the veiled, secret-bearing countenance of eros against the fully-exhibited, secretless face of pornographic consumption. (Bridges to `The Veil` and ch. 5.)
- **Society as a Search Engine / a Machine for Consumption** — the world reconfigured to abolish "the desire for what is absent—what cannot be found, seized, and consumed." > "Society, as a search engine, a machine for consumption, is abolishing the desire for what is absent"
- **The Blink of an Eye Yielding to Pointing-and-Clicking** — the erosion of the decisive moment (Augenblick) into additive, click-time. > "As the temporality of the click, it lacks decision and resolve. The blink of an eye is yielding to pointing-and-clicking."
- **The Musealized Past / the Museum of the Repeatable Present** — memory as living narrative destroyed by "musealization," data-storage "without time." (Cross-links `Narrativity`, `Narration vs Addition`, `Fragrance of Time`.)

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Can vs Should** (existing tension family / concept `Can vs Should`) — "achievement society is wholly dominated by the modal verb *can*—in contrast to disciplinary society, which… deploys *should*." Central and directly stated.
- **Ability vs Being-Able-Not-To (Positive Potency vs Negative Potency)** — the erotic application of the existing `Positive Potency vs Negative Potency` tension: mastery/grasping vs the power not-to. Directly stated.
- **Positivity vs Negativity** (existing tension) — "The power of negativity lies in the fact that things are enlivened precisely by their opposite. Mere positivity lacks any such power to animate." A canonical formulation.
- **Nearness vs Crowding (Distance vs Proximity)** — negative nearness (remoteness inscribed within it) vs positive "crowding." A new opposition worth recording; cross-links `Pathos of Distance` / `Primal Distance`.
- **The Same vs the Other (Present vs Future)** — "The present that stands at one's disposal is the temporality of the Same. In contrast, the future lies open to the event." Bridges `Hell of the Same` ↔ `The Other` ↔ `The Event`.
- **Consumable Difference vs Otherness** — "Otherness is not consumable difference"; capitalism eliminates otherness to subordinate everything to consumption. A sharp paradox: difference is marketed while alterity is destroyed.
- **Love as Wound vs Love as Formula for Enjoyment** — love "invades and wounds us" (Levinas) vs love "positivized into a formula for enjoyment," free from "the negativity of injury, assault, or crashing."
