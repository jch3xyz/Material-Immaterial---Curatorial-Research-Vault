## Executive Summary

This chapter is the pivot of *How We Became Posthuman*: it is where Hayles builds the positive theoretical machinery she will use to **resist the erasure of embodiment** that the rest of the book diagnoses. She argues that the postmodern/cybernetic orthodoxy — "that the body is primarily, if not entirely, a linguistic and discursive construction" — is not evidence that the body has vanished but evidence that "a certain kind of subjectivity has emerged," one "constituted by the crossing of the materiality of informatics with the immateriality of information." Crucially, she insists the dream of dematerialization is itself materially produced: "The body's dematerialization depends in complex and highly specific ways on the embodied circumstances that an ideology of dematerialization would obscure."

To talk about embodiment without re-absorbing it into discourse, she erects a four-term framework of **two interacting polarities**: (1) **the body vs. embodiment** — the normalized cultural/Platonic construct versus the contextual, instantiated, performative experience ("there is no body as such; there are only bodies"); and (2) **inscription vs. incorporation** — abstract transportable sign-systems versus embodied practices "encoded into bodily memory by repeated performances until [they] become habitual." The homology that organizes the chapter: "as the body is to embodiment, so inscription is to incorporation."

The first half is a critical survey: Foucault's Panopticon as the paradigm case of **disembodiment as a source of analytic power** ("the abstraction of the Panopticon … gives Foucault's analysis its power and universality" but "diverts attention away from how actual bodies … impose, incorporate, and resist"); correctives from Elaine Scarry, Elizabeth Grosz (the Möbius-strip model and "there is no body as such"), Hubert Dreyfus (embodied, non-algorithmic learning), Pierre Bourdieu (the *habitus*), Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Paul Connerton (how societies remember through bodily ritual), and Mark Johnson (the body writes discourse through metaphoric networks).

The second half is a literary demonstration: a reading of William Burroughs's *The Ticket That Exploded* situated in the **media history of audiotape** (Poulsen's Telegraphone, steel tape, film tape, the BBC, the late-1950s convergence of inscription + erasability). Burroughs "turns the tables" on the dematerializers: "Instead of discourse dematerializing the body, in *Ticket* the body materializes discourse." The body becomes a tape-recorder; "the word is now a virus"; randomness is the only escape from the "prerecorded universe." Hayles closes by tying Burroughs to Maturana (reflexivity in biology) and Philip K. Dick (reflexivity in psychology), all three agreeing "that the observer cannot stand apart from the systems being observed," and reading these mutating bodies as "a harbinger of the posthuman body."

This chapter most directly realizes the committed stubs **Embodiment**, **Embodiment vs Information**, and (by anticipation) **The Body as Data**; it provides primary material for **Feedback Loops**, **The Extrapolated Body**, and the **pattern/randomness** machinery, and it touches **Cybernetics**/**Posthumanism** as horizon rather than focus.

## Definitions

- **Embodiment** (explicitly defined against "the body"):
  > "Embodiment differs from the concept of the body in that the body is always normative relative to some set of criteria. … In contrast to the body, embodiment is contextual, enmeshed within the specifics of place, time, physiology, and culture, which together compose enactment."
  - Key passage: > "Whereas the body is an idealized form that gestures toward a Platonic reality, embodiment is the specific instantiation generated from the noise of difference."

- **Incorporating practice** (defined, following Connerton/Merleau-Ponty):
  > "I mean by an incorporating practice an action that is encoded into bodily memory by repeated performances until it becomes habitual."

- **Inscription** (defined as conceptual abstraction):
  > "they constitute inscription as a conceptual abstraction rather than as an instantiated materiality." And: inscription "is usually considered as a system of signs operating independently of any particular manifestation."

- **The *habitus*** (Bourdieu, quoted):
  > "the 'durably installed generative principle of regulated improvisations.'"

- **Medial ecology** (Kittler, named):
  > "a process Friedrich Kittler has aptly called 'medial ecology.'"

- **The four (then five) characteristics of incorporated knowledge** — a definitional list: improvisational/contextual; "deeply sedimented into the body and … highly resistant to change"; "partly screened from conscious view because it is habitual"; it "define[s] the boundaries within which conscious thought takes place"; and (fifth) it is "often linked with new technologies."

## Arguments

- **Claim: The "disappearance of the body" is not a fact but the symptom of a new, materially-produced subjectivity.**
  - Reasoning: ecstatic dematerialization rhetoric (Baudrillard, the Krokers, Hardison, Moravec) is re-read as ideology; the very conditions enabling the claim are embodied/material.
  - Evidence: the cluster of quoted dematerialization claims, then the reversal.
  - Source passage: > "I believe they should be taken as evidence not that the body has disappeared but that a certain kind of subjectivity has emerged."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The disappearance of the body marks a new subjectivity, not an actual disappearance`

- **Claim: Abstraction buys analytic power by erasing embodiment — and Foucault's Panopticon is the exemplary case.**
  - Reasoning: the Panopticon's universality depends on detaching power "from any specific use," which simultaneously hides the limits real bodies impose; Foucault thus "reinscribes as well as challenges" the disembodiment he exposes.
  - Evidence: Foucault's own statement that the Panopticon "must be detached from any specific use"; Scarry as counter-model.
  - Source passage: > "On the one hand, the abstraction of the Panopticon … gives Foucault's analysis its power and universality. On the other hand, it diverts attention away from how actual bodies … impose, incorporate, and resist incorporation of the material practices he describes."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Abstraction gains analytic power by erasing embodiment`

- **Claim: Embodiment is neither essentialist nor algorithmic.**
  - Reasoning: embodied experience is dispersed along a spectrum (no privileged position = no essence); embodied learning has an open "inner horizon" that cannot be fully formalized (Dreyfus); formalizing embodied knowledge changes what it is (Bourdieu).
  - Evidence: Varela/Thompson/Rosch; Dreyfus's cup-grasping child; Bourdieu's Kabyle calendar.
  - Source passage: > "If embodiment is not essentialist, it is also not algorithmic."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Embodiment is neither essentialist nor algorithmic`

- **Claim: The body writes discourse (incorporation produces inscription), not only the reverse.**
  - Reasoning: bodily orientation in space generates metaphoric networks (Johnson); Bourdieu/Merleau-Ponty show conscious thought as an epiphenomenon of the body's phenomenal base; "To look at thought in this way is to turn Descartes upside down."
  - Evidence: verticality metaphors ("upright," "at the top," "a downer"); Burroughs literalizing this.
  - Source passage: > "It is a truism in contemporary theory that discourse writes the body; Johnson illustrates how the body writes discourse."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The body writes discourse, not only the reverse`

- **Claim: Incorporation is the crucial link in the feedback loop between technology and discourse.**
  - Reasoning: embodiment is "formed by technology at the same time that it creates technology"; new technologies reshape bodily use, which generates new experiential frameworks, which seed new discursive systems.
  - Evidence: the fifth characteristic of incorporated knowledge; the audiotape case.
  - Source passage: > "In the feedback loop between technological innovations and discursive practices, incorporation is a crucial link."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated) — primary realization of `Feedback Loops` for this chapter.
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Incorporation is the crucial link in the feedback loop between technology and discourse`

- **Claim: In *The Ticket That Exploded*, the body materializes discourse rather than discourse dematerializing the body.**
  - Reasoning: Burroughs's media-historical moment (audiotape's inscription + erasability) lets him equate body and tape-recorder; "the word" becomes a literal viral parasite with physical effects; randomness is the only exit from the "prerecorded universe."
  - Evidence: word-virus passages; the Exhibition / "writing machine" / "reality studio"; the cut-up; Lilly's tanks.
  - Source passage: > "Instead of discourse dematerializing the body, in Ticket the body materializes discourse."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: capture as a reading within the chapter's source note; optionally `arguments/In Burroughs's Ticket the body materializes discourse`.

- **Claim: Burroughs, Maturana, and Dick converge on reflexivity, dissolving the observer/system boundary and undoing liberal humanism.**
  - Reasoning: each locates reflexivity differently (cybernetic fusion of language/technology; biology; psychology) but all deny a detached observer; this anticipates the third wave (autopoiesis) and the posthuman.
  - Evidence: the closing paragraph; the forward gesture to Wiener and to third-wave informatics.
  - Source passage: > "they shared a realization that the observer cannot stand apart from the systems being observed. In exploring how to integrate observer and world into a unified field of interaction, they also realized that liberal humanism could not continue to hold sway."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated) — connects to `Posthumanism`, `Cybernetics`, the three-waves schema.

## Metaphors

- **Two sine waves in syncopation** — the relation between the body/embodiment polarity and the inscription/incorporation polarity. > "I envision these two bimodalities acting in complex syncopation with each other, like two sine waves moving at different frequencies and with different periods of repetition."
- **The Möbius strip** (Grosz) — outside becomes inside becomes outside; Hayles adopts it for "volatile bodies" / mutating surfaces but critiques its inability to chart gradations.
- **Funes the Memorious** (Borges) — pure particularity without abstraction; embodiment articulated apart from the body would be "a froth of discrete utterances."
- **The body as tape-recorder** — the central literary-technical metaphor; "the body became a tape-recorder," regulated by the "principles that govern magnetic tape in its reproduction, erasure, and reconfiguration."
- **The word as virus / the "Other Half" / the word as the body's parasite** — discourse as bodily infestation; "crab parasites," "green fish parasites."
- **Caterpillar → iridescent winged creature** (Hardison/Moravec) — carbon man "preparing to become" silicon; the transcendence-of-the-body fantasy Hayles critiques.
- **"Word dust" / "picture dust" falling from magnetized walls** — inscription literalized as falling particulate; smog over Los Angeles.
- **The "reality studio" / "Grey Room"** — culture's central control room producing prerecorded sound and image tracks; the film bank that is "empty."
- **"Turn Descartes upside down"** — the inversion making conscious thought an epiphenomenon of the body's phenomenal base.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **The body vs. embodiment** — normalized Platonic abstraction vs. instantiated contextual particular; the chapter's first axis. > "there is no body as such; there are only bodies."
- **Inscription vs. incorporation** — transportable abstract sign vs. context-bound embodied practice.
- **Materiality vs. immateriality (of information)** — the title tension; immaterial information is produced by material conditions. Maps to `Material vs Immaterial`.
- **Abstraction/analytic power vs. attention to bodies** — the productive double-bind in Foucault: power gained = bodies lost.
- **Presence vs. absence / repetition vs. mutation** — the paradoxical "technoconceptual actors" of magnetic tape; voice severed from presence.
- **Pattern vs. randomness** — randomness as the only non-prerecorded element; "the recognition that pattern is always already penetrated by randomness." Maps to the book's `pattern/randomness` machinery.
- **Liberation vs. threat in posthuman mutation** — the fish boy's fluidity is hope, but "for human subjects … this destabilization is bound to be threatening rather than simply liberating."
- **Theory's generality vs. the particular** — theory must abstract, yet this chapter "abstractly and generally insist[s] on the importance of the particular."
- **Liberal humanist subject vs. reflexive/posthuman subject** — the observer who cannot stand apart; liberal humanism "could not continue to hold sway."
