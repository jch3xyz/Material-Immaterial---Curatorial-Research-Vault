> Note: This is part of the "Algorithm" section (chapter 11, source pages 178–200). It pairs with Ch12, also "Algorithm." The chapter braids three registers: (1) a tech-criticism essay on big data and deep learning, (2) a Calvinist memoir of the author's evangelical theological education, and (3) a Blumenbergian intellectual history of nominalism, disenchantment, and the birth of modernity. The argumentative payoff is a single structural claim that fuses all three.

## Executive Summary

This is the chapter where O'Gieblyn's central method — **technology as metaphor**, the idea that "the divinity of each age reveals the needs and desires of its human creators" — is turned on the **black-box algorithm**, and where the book's theological argument is made most explicit. The chapter's thesis is that the opaque, unaccountable, superhuman AI we are now building is not a regression to a premodern, "enchanted" Dark Age (as critics like James Bridle and David Weinberger claim) but a *resurrection of a specifically modern God* — the radically other, inscrutable, voluntarist deity of **John Calvin** and **Martin Luther**. "The boundless alien deity that technology critics have in mind is the God of Calvin and Luther—a thoroughly modern entity."

The essay opens with Chris Anderson's 2008 *Wired* polemic "The End of Theory," which argued that big data makes the scientific method obsolete: with petabytes, "Correlation is enough," and we should "stop trying to understand the world and instead trust the wisdom of algorithms." O'Gieblyn reads the subsequent rise of deep learning (AlphaGo, COMPAS, PredPol, credit and sentencing algorithms) as the literal fulfillment of this call — a world in which we obtain "objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension." Because deep-learning models are **black boxes** whose internal nodes "correspond to [no] terms in human language," we are placed, she argues, in the position of **Job**: denied the right to know *why*, forced "to accept the decisions of our algorithms blindly, like Job accepting his punishment."

The memoir thread supplies the theological depth. O'Gieblyn recounts her Reformed (New Calvinist) education, her study of **predestination** and **double predestination**, the doctrine of scriptural transparency (*brevitas et facilitas*; *Scriptura sui ipsius interpres*), and her crisis over the **problem of evil / theodicy** in the Book of Job — the discovery that Job's suffering had a cause (a wager between God and Satan) that was hidden from him, presided over by a God who "does whatever he pleases." This is the seed of **voluntarism**: the fear that "God exists in an eternal state of exception" where human morality "breaks down," that divine goodness "rested on nothing more than the Hobbesian rule that might makes right."

The intellectual-history thread, drawn from **Hans Blumenberg's** *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age*, supplies the genealogy. Late-medieval **nominalism** (William of Ockham) destroyed the idea of **universals** — the Platonic correspondence between mind and cosmic order — making the world "radically contingent" and human reason "a construct specific to our species and not a reflection of the larger cosmic order." This nominalist crisis of subjectivity, peaking in **Descartes**, *produced* the Enlightenment, **disenchantment**, humanism, and empiricism, and it is the same crisis that "continues to haunt our debates about how to interpret quantum physics" and "our theories of mind." The chapter ends by closing the loop: "AI began with the desire to forge a god. It is not coincidental that the deity we have created resembles, uncannily, the one who got us into this problem in the first place."

The chapter also carries the book's running concern with **Norbert Wiener**, whose 1964 *God & Golem, Inc.* framed machine learning as a theological problem (can a creator play a "significant game" with a learning creature?) and explicitly read the Book of Job as a case where "God is something less than absolutely omnipotent." O'Gieblyn corrects Wiener's casting: the algorithms are "not the sly devil that has outsmarted its creator" but "the absolute sovereign who demands blind submission."

For the vault: this chapter is a strong cross-link partner to the **Hayles** cluster (`Posthumanism`, `The Computational Universe`, `Information`, `Cybernetics`, `Emergence`, `The Liberal Humanist Subject`) and realizes the committed metaphor stub **`Mind as Machine`** (here in its inverse, mind-as-black-box / **black box** form) and the forward-stub author **`Norbert Wiener`**. ## Definitions

- **Big data / "the most measured age in history"** (Anderson, via O'Gieblyn):
  > "We were now living in what he called 'the most measured age in history,' an era defined by cloud computing and the petabyte (a unit of data that is roughly the equivalent of 799 million copies of Moby Dick)."

- **Machine learning** (Wiener's sense, quoted/paraphrased):
  > the kind of AI that "learns or appears to learn," as Wiener put it, from its experience in the world.
  And: > "These models were not programmed with optimal moves but rather with a very limited understanding of the game and its rules… It learned, in other words, in much the way humans do—through trial and error."

- **Deep learning** (defined functionally):
  > "AlphaGo was a form of deep learning, an especially powerful brand of machine learning that has since become the preferred means of drawing predictions from our era's deluge of raw data."

- **Black box** (defined):
  > "They are composed of many hidden layers of neural networks, and there's no way to ascertain what kind of model they are building from their experience. During the training process they develop internal nodes that represent abstract features or relationships they discover, none of which correspond to any terms in human language…"

- **Nominalism** (defined, with Mill's gloss):
  > "For the nominalist, 'justice' and 'humanity' are just names we give to commonalities we perceive between objects, not universal truths. (John Stuart Mill once described nominalism as the belief that 'there is nothing general except names.')"

- **Predestination / double predestination** (defined):
  > "predestination, the controversial view that God decided before the creation of the world who would be saved and who would be damned." / "'double predestination,' a doctrine that insisted not only that God neglected to save the damned but that he actively condemned them."

- **Calvin's hermeneutics** (defined by Latin tags):
  > "We were taught Calvin's approach to hermeneutics: brevitas et facilitas, 'brief and simple.'" / "Scriptura sui ipsius interpres, or 'The text interprets itself.'"

- **Voluntarism** (defined functionally):
  > "this undercurrent of voluntarism—the notion that God exists in an eternal state of exception, or lives in some higher realm where the whole system of human morals breaks down."

- **The "Dark Ages"** (clarified, then partly refuted):
  > "The term 'Dark Ages' describes, in its strictest sense, the lack of information we have about the period … though it is often understood as the inverse of Enlightenment, a nod to the popular assumption that medieval Christianity was hostile toward the cultivation of knowledge."

## Arguments

- **Claim: The opaque algorithm fulfills, rather than refutes, Anderson's call to abandon understanding for correlation — delivering knowledge "at the price of human comprehension."**
  - Reasoning: deep-learning models build accurate world-models with no human-legible explanation; even their creators cannot account for their decisions; to use them we must treat their outputs as revelation.
  - Evidence: AlphaGo's "not a human move"; David Silver's inability to explain it; Google Translate; "artificial neuroscience."
  - Source passage: > "More than any other technology, these algorithms have fulfilled Anderson's call for objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension. To obtain the superior knowledge the machines possess, we must give up our desire to know 'why' and accept their outputs as pure revelation."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The algorithm delivers knowledge at the price of human comprehension`

- **Claim: Submission to opaque algorithms reproduces the position of Job — denied the right to know "why" before an inscrutable sovereign.**
  - Reasoning: predictive systems in justice, lending, and policing issue verdicts without explanation or appeal; the citizen, like Job, may "question" but cannot understand or challenge.
  - Evidence: COMPAS / Eric Loomis (could not challenge the algorithm); Darnell Gates / probation; PredPol; Kelly Clancy's Job analogy.
  - Source passage: > "These algorithms … have become instead the absolute sovereign who demands blind submission. As these technologies become increasingly integrated into the spheres of public life, many people now find themselves in a position much like Job's, denied the right to know why…"
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The opaque algorithm puts us in the position of Job`

- **Claim: The "AI is a return to the Dark Ages" thesis is wrong; the AI deity is the *modern* God of Calvin and Luther — radically other, voluntarist, inscrutable — not a premodern one.**
  - Reasoning: medieval/Scholastic theology was confident in human reason and the comprehensibility of God and world (Aquinas, universals, *lumens naturalis*); the inscrutable alien God arrives only with nominalism and the Reformation; therefore the algorithmic God is a thoroughly modern entity.
  - Evidence: Aquinas's optimism; Ockham's voluntarism; Calvin/Luther on divine alienness; Blumenberg's periodization.
  - Source passage: > "The doctrine evolving around AI is not in fact a holdover from the Dark Ages. The boundless alien deity that technology critics have in mind is the God of Calvin and Luther—a thoroughly modern entity."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated) — this is the chapter's central, organizing argument.
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The algorithmic God is the modern God of Calvin and Luther, not a premodern one`

- **Claim: Nominalism produced modernity — the Enlightenment, disenchantment, humanism, and empiricism are all responses to the loss of universals.**
  - Reasoning (Blumenberg, via O'Gieblyn): the nominalist God restricted to no rational/moral law made the world radically contingent and human reason merely species-specific; this intolerable crisis forced Descartes's self-grounding, which births humanism; empiricism follows because a contingent world must be tested; God becomes "superfluous."
  - Evidence: Ockham; Descartes's *Meditations* and abandonment of final causes; Blumenberg's "the nominalist God is a superfluous God."
  - Source passage: > "Blumenberg's thesis, which has since been reiterated by a number of philosophers and historians, is that nominalism, as it became widespread in Protestant theology, led to the Enlightenment, disenchantment, and the scientific revolution."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated, attributed to Blumenberg)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Nominalism produced modernity (Blumenberg)`

- **Claim: "Understanding" and "meaning" are anthropocentric concepts that information technologies were built to elide — so the objection "but the machine doesn't understand" is futile.**
  - Reasoning: Lanier's "Science is about understanding" appeals to a human-centered value the technology was designed to bypass; the machine's success does not require comprehension and may surpass it.
  - Evidence: the Lanier/Anderson exchange; Google's theory-free translation.
  - Source passage: > "It's hard not to feel the futility of such objections. 'Understanding,' like meaning, is an anthropocentric concept—one that information technologies were deliberately built to elide."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Understanding is an anthropocentric concept the machine was built to elide`

- **Claim: The "human brain is also a black box" defense is specious, because machines reason in a way that is genuinely *alien* to evolved human reasoning.**
  - Reasoning: we trust human explanations because we share evolved reasoning methods; machine conclusions ("That's not a human move," invented languages, exploit-finding) fall outside the realm of human thought; Bostrom: no innate link between intelligence and human values.
  - Evidence: AlphaGo; Facebook's invented language; video-game-cheating agents; Bostrom's superintelligence warning.
  - Source passage: > "We trust explanations from other humans because we evolved as a species to have similarities in our method of reasoning… Machines, in contrast, reach conclusions in a way that is totally unlike human reason."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated) — this is O'Gieblyn rebutting the Dennett/Berk "both are black boxes" move.
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The human-brain-is-also-a-black-box defense is specious`

  - Evidence: Wiener's *God & Golem, Inc.*; the deployment cases (justice, lending).
  - Source passage: > "Wiener sensed something fundamentally religious in machine learning, but he arguably miscast the roles in the Book of Job. These algorithms are not the sly devil that has outsmarted its creator. They have become instead the absolute sovereign who demands blind submission."

## Metaphors

- **The algorithm as the God of Job / the absolute sovereign** — the chapter's master metaphor; the unaccountable predictive system as the deity "who demands blind submission," before whom the citizen is Job. > "They have become instead the absolute sovereign who demands blind submission."
- **"God is the machine" / "The black box is the truth"** (Leskovec) — the literalized identification of deep learning with divinity; revelation without interpretation.
- **The algorithm as revelation / the technological *sola fide* and *sola scriptura*** — refusing to interpret the black box as a kind of Protestant fideism. > "If there exists a hermeneutics among the technological elite, it is a brand of sola fide and sola scriptura, a conviction that algorithmic revelations are perfect…"
- **Predicting the weather by bird entrails vs. by air patterns** (Weinberger) — the enchanted vs. mechanistic epistemology; "hidden connections of meaning" vs. "order, laws, and rules."
- **The data "deluge"** — big data as a flood that "completely subsumed us," echoing Noah / the biblical flood.
- **AI as the forging of a god** (McCorduck / Voltaire) — "AI began with an ancient wish to forge the gods."

- **God "as different from flesh as fire is from water"** (Calvin) — the radical otherness of the deity, reused for alien machine intelligence.
- **Humans as "worms," "bugs," "dry and worthless wood," "rottenness itself"** (Calvin) — the anti-anthropocentric debasement of the human that O'Gieblyn ties to the abandonment of "anthropocentric interests."

- **Descartes structuring the *Meditations* after Genesis** — modernity dressing itself as a creation *ex nihilo*.
- **"Returning to the site of the crime… blindly"** (Blumenberg) — modernity compulsively revisiting the medieval crisis it cannot recognize.

## Symbols

- **Job** — the recurring symbol of the human subject denied the reason for its suffering; submission before an inscrutable power. Appears at the start (Clancy), the theological middle (Calvin's 159 sermons), and the AI deployment section (Loomis, Gates).
- **The black box** — the symbol of opaque, unaccountable, superhuman knowledge; later mirrored onto the human brain ("the human brain is also a black box").
- **AlphaGo's "Move 37" / "That's not a human move"** — the symbol of alien machine cognition exceeding and bewildering human comprehension. (O'Gieblyn does not number the move but quotes the champion.)
- **The petabyte / "799 million copies of Moby Dick"** — the symbol of incomprehensible data scale.

- **The dining-hall Calvinists with their concordances and Luther engravings** — the memoir's symbol of doctrinal authority and the seductiveness of the "harder" argument.
- **The whirlwind** — God answering Job "out of the whirlwind"; the deity whose decrees "thundered out of the whirlwind."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Correlation vs. causation / prediction vs. understanding** — Anderson's "Correlation is enough" against Lanier's "Science is about understanding"; the chapter's opening tension. Maps to a potential `Visibility vs Understanding`-adjacent tension (cf. Han's existing tension).
- **Prediction vs. determination / free will vs. fate** — Gates's "the shadowy line between prediction and determination"; predestination's secular double.
- **Anthropocentric vs. non-anthropocentric knowledge** — the human desire to know "why" against the machine (and Calvin's God) that elides it. > "calls to abandon our anthropocentric interests."
- **Premodern (enchanted) vs. modern (disenchanted) reading of AI** — Weinberger/Bridle ("New Dark Age," return to revelation) vs. O'Gieblyn ("the God of Calvin and Luther—a thoroughly modern entity"). This is the chapter's pivotal corrective tension.
- **Universals vs. nominalism** — Platonic forms / Scholastic correspondence vs. "there is nothing general except names"; the loss that "made the world radically contingent."
- **Intellect vs. revelation** — "We could know nothing of God through our intellects, only through revelation"; mirrored by the technologists' refusal to interpret the algorithm.
- **Justice as intrinsic vs. justice as divine fiat** — the *Euthyphro* problem: piety good in itself vs. good because the gods will it; Calvin/Luther's "might makes right." (The voluntarism tension.)
- **Human reasoning vs. alien machine reasoning** — "we evolved as a species to have similarities in our method of reasoning" vs. "totally unlike human reason"; the rebuttal to "the brain is a black box too."
- **Transparency of scripture vs. opacity of the black box** — Calvin's "perfectly transparent" revelation vs. the unreadable neural net, both demanding submission without interpretation. (Productive irony O'Gieblyn exploits.)
- **Creator vs. creature (the limited creator)** — Wiener's question of whether a maker can "play a significant game" with a learning creature that may surpass it; the theological asymmetry overturned by machine learning.
