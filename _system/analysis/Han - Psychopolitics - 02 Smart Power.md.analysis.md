## Executive Summary

This short chapter is Han's compressed phenomenology of power under neoliberalism. It opens with a counter-intuitive thesis about power as such: the highest form of power is not violence or coercion but the kind that works invisibly, "quietly," without drawing attention to itself — power that need not say "no." Han then maps two regimes onto this distinction. *Disciplinary power* is "negative": inhibitive, prohibitive, repressive, commanded by negativity, and therefore inefficient (it spends energy forcing people into "the straightjacket of commandments and prohibitions"). The *neoliberal regime*, by contrast, "beams forth in positivity": its technology of power is "subtle, supple and smart," escaping all visibility so that the subjugated subject is not even aware of its own subjugation and "thinks itself free."

The chapter's core construct is **smart, friendly, permissive power**. Rather than repressing, this power "activate[s], motivate[s] and optimize[s]"; it pleases and fulfills rather than forbidding and depriving; it makes people *dependent* rather than *compliant*; it says "yes" more often than "no"; it "operates seductively, not repressively"; it "meets the subject halfway." Crucially, smart power "cosies up to the psyche rather than disciplining it" — this is the chapter's proto-statement of psychopolitics: power that targets the psyche and works through it. Han ties this to the digital surface of contemporary life: smart power "is constantly calling on us to confide, share and participate," it reads "our conscious and unconscious thoughts," it "places its stock in voluntary self-organization and self-optimization." Its emblem is the **Like button**: "Neoliberalism is the capitalism of 'Like.'" The chapter culminates in Han's diagnosis of the crisis of freedom — power no longer negates or represses freedom "so much as exploit[s] it" — and in the lexical hinge of free choice (*Wahl*) collapsing into mere free selection (*Auswahl*) from items on offer. It closes with a borrowed warning label for the capitalism of Like: "Protect me from what I want."

## Key Entities

- **Byung-Chul Han** — author.
- **Michel Foucault** — unnamed but structurally present: "disciplinary power," "disciplinary regime," and the whole architecture of negative/repressive power vs. a productive, capillary power are Foucauldian; the chapter is Han's pivot away from a purely disciplinary/biopolitical account toward psychopolitics.
- **Gilles Deleuze** — unnamed but implicit: the periodization from disciplinary "negativity" to a smart, modulating positivity prefigures Deleuze's society-of-control argument (developed explicitly in the next chapter, "The Mole and the Snake").
- **The Like button** — the named emblem ("signal and seal") of smart power and of "the capitalism of 'Like.'"
- **"Protect me from what I want"** — a quoted phrase Han adopts as a "warning label" for the capitalism of Like (uncredited in the excerpt; the line is famously Jenny Holzer's *Truisms* / *Survival* series, later a Placebo song title — attribution needs verification at synthesis).
- **Nineteenth-century capitalism** — the disciplinary, prohibition-based foil against which neoliberalism's "capitalism of Like" is defined.
- *Herrschaftszusammenhang* — German term Han uses for "the whole context of domination" that "remains entirely hidden."
- *Wahl* / *Auswahl* — the German pair (free choice vs. free selection) carrying the chapter's argument about the hollowing of freedom.

## Key Concepts

- **Smart Power / Friendly Power** — Han's name for the dominant technology of power under neoliberalism: permissive, seductive, pleasing, psyche-targeting, invisible. It "guides their will to its own benefit," meets the subject halfway, and "cosies up to the psyche." This is the chapter's central new concept and a load-bearing term for the whole book.
  - Source passage: > "Power that is smart and friendly does not operate frontally - i.e., against the will of those who are subject to it. Instead, it guides their will to its own benefit. It says 'yes' more often than 'no'; it operates seductively, not repressively."

  - Recommended action: create new note (`Smart Power`).

- **Permissive Power / Permissivity** — The shift Han diagnoses: power "assuming increasingly permissive forms," shedding negativity, "presenting itself as freedom." Permissivity is the modality by which power disappears.
  - Source passage: > "Today, power is assuming increasingly permissive forms. In its permissivity - indeed, in its friendliness - power is shedding its negativity and presenting itself as freedom."
  - Existing wiki note: (none).

- **Power that works invisibly (power of the highest order)** — Han's general thesis that maximal power is quiet, unseen, non-coercive: "The greater power is, the more quietly it works." Violence betrays weakness, not strength.
  - Source passage: > "Power that relies on violence does not represent power of the highest order... Wherever power does not come into view at all, it exists without question. The greater power is, the more quietly it works. It just happens: it has no need to draw attention to itself."

## Definitions

- **Smart / friendly power** — "Smart power with a liberal, friendly appearance - power that stimulates and seduces - is more compelling than power that imposes, threatens and decrees." (operational definition by contrast).
- **Permissive power** — "Today, power is assuming increasingly permissive forms. In its permissivity - indeed, in its friendliness - power is shedding its negativity and presenting itself as freedom."
- **Free choice (Wahl) vs. free selection (Auswahl)** — "Free choice {Wahl) is eliminated to make way for a free selection (Auswahl) from among the items on offer." Freedom is redefined downward from authoring options to picking among supplied ones.
- **Power of the highest order** — defined negatively: NOT violence. "Power that relies on violence does not represent power of the highest order... The greater power is, the more quietly it works."
- **Neoliberalism (here)** — "the capitalism of 'Like'": power exercised through consumption and communication, distinguished from "nineteenth-century capitalism, which operated by means of disciplinary constraints and prohibitions."

## Arguments

- **Claim:** The highest form of power is invisible and non-coercive; violence is a sign of weakness, not strength.
  - Reasoning structure: power that must break a will reveals that an opposing will has formed (weakness); power that "just happens," unseen, "exists without question."
  - Evidence: the asymmetry between power that announces itself (coercion) and power that works "quietly."
  - Source passage: > "The mere fact that another will manages to form and turn against the power-holder attests to the latter's weakness... The greater power is, the more quietly it works."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The highest form of power is invisible not coercive`

- **Claim:** Disciplinary (negative) power is inefficient; permissive (positive) power is more efficient because subjects subordinate themselves voluntarily.
  - Reasoning structure: forcing compliance through prohibition costs energy and provokes resistance; activating, motivating, and optimizing makes people do the work themselves.
  - Evidence: "expends a great deal of energy"; "makes sure that people subordinate themselves to power relations on their own."
  - Source passage: > "A significantly more efficient technology of power makes sure that people subordinate themselves to power relations on their own. Such a dynamic seeks to activate, motivate and optimize - not to inhibit or repress."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Permissive power is more efficient than disciplinary power`

- **Claim:** Neoliberal power does not stand opposed to freedom but exploits it — this is the crisis of freedom.
  - Reasoning structure: if power negated freedom (disciplinary model), freedom could resist it; by exploiting freedom, power makes the subject's own freedom the vehicle of domination, so the subject "thinks itself free."
  - Evidence: "the subjugated subject is not even aware of its own subjugation"; freedom is exploited, not repressed; choice collapses into selection.
  - Source passage: > "Today's crisis of freedom stems from the fact that the operative technology of power does not negate or repress freedom so much as exploit it."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated) — this is the through-line to `Exploitation of Freedom` and the book's title thesis.
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The crisis of freedom is that power exploits freedom rather than repressing it`

- **Claim:** Smart power targets and inhabits the psyche; it works by seduction and the solicitation of self-disclosure rather than by silencing.
  - Reasoning structure: instead of imposing silence (the disciplinary mode), smart power "constantly call[s] on us to confide, share and participate"; it reads conscious and unconscious thought and guides the will from within.
  - Evidence: "cosies up to the psyche"; "reads and appraises our conscious and unconscious thoughts."
  - Source passage: > "Smart power cosies up to the psyche rather than disciplining it through coercion or prohibitions... it is constantly calling on us to confide, share and participate."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated) — foundational for `Psychopolitics`.
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Smart power colonizes the psyche through seduction and self-disclosure`

- **Claim:** Neoliberalism is "the capitalism of Like" — subjugation now occurs through consumption and communication, emblematized by the Like button, and is qualitatively distinct from nineteenth-century disciplinary capitalism.
  - Reasoning structure: where old capitalism used constraints and prohibitions, the new one uses pleasing, consuming, communicating; the affirmative click ("Like") is the very form domination takes.
  - Evidence: "Its signal and seal is the Like button"; "Neoliberalism is the capitalism of 'Like.'"
  - Source passage: > "Now, people subjugate themselves to domination by consuming and communicating - and they click Like all the while. Neoliberalism is the capitalism of 'Like\."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Neoliberalism is the capitalism of Like`

- **Claim:** Friendly power is more powerful than repressive power precisely because it is invisible and felt as freedom.
  - Reasoning structure: invisibility removes the object of resistance; the sensation of freedom forecloses opposition; "the subject thinks itself free."
  - Evidence: "It manages not to be seen at all"; "Consequently, the subject thinks itself free."
  - Source passage: > "Friendly power proves more powerful, as it were, than purely repressive power. It manages not to be seen at all."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: fold into `arguments/The highest form of power is invisible not coercive` or a dedicated `arguments/Friendly power is more powerful than repressive power`.

## Metaphors

- **"Beams forth in positivity"** — the neoliberal regime figured as radiance/light (positivity as luminous emission), against disciplinary negativity. Connects to the *Lightless Radiation* / illumination imagery of *The Transparency Society*.
  - Source passage: > "it does not describe the neoliberal regime - which beams forth in positivity."
- **"Cosies up to the psyche"** — power figured as an intimate, ingratiating bodily nearness (snuggling/cuddling) rather than a striking force; affective closeness as a technique of control.
  - Source passage: > "Smart power cosies up to the psyche rather than disciplining it through coercion or prohibitions."
- **"The straightjacket of commandments and prohibitions"** — disciplinary power figured as a physical restraint/medical-carceral garment; the inefficient, coercive negative.
  - Source passage: > "to force people into the straightjacket of commandments and prohibitions."
- **"Meets the subject halfway" / "guides their will" / "leads astray instead of erecting obstacles"** — power figured as a companion or guide moving alongside the subject, redirecting rather than blocking; the spatial logic of seduction vs. the wall/obstacle logic of repression.
  - Source passage: > "It leads astray instead of erecting obstacles.. Instead of standing opposed to the subject, smart and friendly power meets the subject halfway."
- **"Signal and seal"** of the Like button — the Like button as heraldic emblem/sigil of an entire regime; the brand-mark of the capitalism of Like.
  - Source passage: > "Its signal and seal is the Like button."
- **"Warning label" / "Protect me from what I want"** — the capitalism of Like figured as a consumer product requiring a health/hazard label; the slogan as the self-aware caption of desire under smart power.
  - Source passage: > "The capitalism of Like should come with a warning label: Protect me from what I want."

## Symbols

- **The Like button** — the recurring emblem of smart power / the capitalism of Like; affirmation ("yes") as the form of subjugation. (Already a canonical symbol from *The Transparency Society*; this chapter deepens it.)
  - Source passage: > "Its signal and seal is the Like button."
- **"Yes" vs. "No"** — the affirmative/negative as symbolic poles of the two power regimes: smart power "says 'yes' more often than 'no'"; disciplinary power is the "no" that "shatter[s] the will."
  - Source passage: > "Only in its negative form does power manifest itself as a violence that says 'no' by shattering the will and annulling freedom."
- **The straightjacket** — symbol of disciplinary coercion (carceral/psychiatric restraint).
  - Source passage: > "the straightjacket of commandments and prohibitions."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Positivity vs. Negativity** — the master opposition: disciplinary power "commanded by negativity" vs. the neoliberal regime that "beams forth in positivity." (Canonical tension `Positivity vs Negativity` already exists.)
  - Source: > "Because it is negative, it does not describe the neoliberal regime - which beams forth in positivity."
- **Freedom vs. Control** — freedom is not the opposite of power but its medium; power "presents itself as freedom" and "exploit[s] it." (Canonical tension `Freedom vs Control` already exists.)
  - Source: > "power is shedding its negativity and presenting itself as freedom."
- **Repression vs. Seduction** — the core methodological contrast: "it operates seductively, not repressively"; "stimulates and seduces" vs. "imposes, threatens and decrees."
  - Source: > "Smart power with a liberal, friendly appearance - power that stimulates and seduces - is more compelling than power that imposes, threatens and decrees."
- **Compliance vs. Dependence** — disciplinary power seeks compliance; smart power seeks dependence.
  - Source: > "Instead of making people compliant, it seeks to make them dependent."
- **Prohibition vs. Permission ("no" vs. "yes")** — inhibitive vs. permissive articulation; the negative "no" vs. the affirmative "yes."
  - Source: > "Its mode of articulation is inhibitive, not permissive."
- **Free choice (Wahl) vs. free selection (Auswahl)** — authentic freedom of choice vs. consumer selection among given options; the hollowing of freedom.
  - Source: > "Free choice {Wahl) is eliminated to make way for a free selection (Auswahl) from among the items on offer."
- **Visibility vs. Invisibility of power** — the more powerful, the more invisible; disciplinary power is visible/coercive, smart power "manages not to be seen at all."
  - Source: > "Wherever power does not come into view at all, it exists without question."
- **Disciplinary regime vs. Neoliberal regime** — the historical/structural periodization underlying the chapter (nineteenth-century disciplinary capitalism vs. the capitalism of Like).
  - Source: > "It is fundamentally different from nineteenth-century capitalism, which operated by means of disciplinary constraints and prohibitions."
