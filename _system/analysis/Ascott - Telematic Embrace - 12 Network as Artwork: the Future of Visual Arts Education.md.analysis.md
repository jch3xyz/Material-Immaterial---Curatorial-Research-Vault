## Executive Summary

This is a very short Ascott primary text — an **abstract** written for the *World Future Society Education Section Meeting* at the University of Houston, Clear Lake City, October 1978, and noted as "Previously unpublished." Despite its brevity (two pages, ~330 words of body), it is a keystone seed-text in Ascott's arc: it is the first place in the collection where his title-phrase **"Network as Artwork"** is articulated as a paradigm, and it does so through the apparently institutional lens of *art-education futures*.

Ascott's claim is that post-secondary visual-arts education must be re-imagined in a "post-institutional context" governed by **mobility and networking**. He predicts the dissolution of the discipline-based curriculum (departments of painting, sculpture, etc.) in favour of a global, exploratory **"learning net"** organized around polarities of information / structure / concept and probes into **cultural / technological / noetic** areas. The physical apparatus of art — colleges with 9-to-5 schedules, monolithic buildings, permanent institutions, museums, and the gallery-marketplace "art centres" (New York, London) — will "physically disintegrate," replaced by **exhibition systems** and **moving centres**. Crucially, these tendencies are not merely administrative: they "breed new hybrids" (quantum artists, bioaestheticians, video-planners), produce "more holistic thought and images," and culminate in a **"connective, network paradigm for art and art theory, calling for a connective criticism."**

This 1978 abstract is therefore the institutional/pedagogical face of the same idea that becomes the telematic embrace in the 1980s: the artwork is not an object but a *network* — a distributed, participatory, globally indexed system — and education, criticism, art, and the museum must all be reconceived as **systems** rather than objects. It sits squarely on the vault's **Object vs System** / **Material vs Immaterial** spine (the museum dematerializing into an "exhibition system"; the art centre dematerializing into "moving centres") and prefigures McLuhan's global village translated into a pedagogical/curatorial program. The word **"noetic"** here (the "cultural/technological/noetic" triad) is an early, pre-coinage anticipation of Ascott's later **technoetics**.

## Arguments

- **Claim: The future of art is a connective, network paradigm — "Network as Artwork."**
  - Reasoning structure: increased connectivity (driven by mobility, travel, teleconferencing, networking) → new hybrid practitioners + more holistic thought → deepened participation → a connective/network paradigm for art, theory, and criticism.
  - Evidence: the chained predictions about hybrids, holism, participation.
  - Source passage: > "the currently emerging mode of participation in art will develop further, leading to a connective, network paradigm for art and art theory, calling for a connective criticism."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The future of art is a connective network paradigm` (or fold into `Network as Artwork` as its core thesis)

- **Claim: Art education must be reconceived in a post-institutional context governed by mobility and networking, dissolving the discipline-based curriculum.**
  - Reasoning structure: post-secondary art education examined "post-institutionally" → mobility/networking become the binding constraints → curriculum shifts from rigid departmental compartmentalization to a global, exploratory learning net → the fine-art/applied-design dichotomy dissolves.
  - Evidence: the explicit rejection of "departments of painting, sculpture, etc."; the "learning landscape … global in scope"; "Such implied access and interaction will dissolve the fine art /applied design dichotomies."
  - Source passage: > "Curriculum design in visual arts learning will not be based on rigid compartmentalization and specialization (departments of painting, sculpture, etc.), although skill banks allowing for media-specific instruction will remain. Instead, a learning landscape will be available to the student that will be global in scope"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: fold into `Network as Artwork` / `The Learning Net`

- **Claim: The physical institutions of art (colleges, buildings, museums, gallery art-centres) will dematerialize into systems and moving centres.**
  - Reasoning structure: networked access via travel + teleconferencing makes fixed attendance redundant → fewer permanent buildings/institutions → museums become "exhibition systems," art centres become "moving centres."
  - Evidence: "fewer permanent, monolithic buildings"; "Museums, too, will tend to physically disintegrate, becoming instead exhibition systems."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Object/institution vs System/network** — the abstract's structuring opposition: the permanent, monolithic, departmentalized, place-bound institution (college, museum, art centre) vs the mobile, distributed, networked system (learning net, exhibition systems, moving centres). This maps directly onto the vault's `Object vs System` (Burnham) and `Material vs Immaterial` tensions.
  - Source passage: > "This will lead to fewer permanent, monolithic buildings for art education, fewer permanent institutions."
- **Fine art vs applied design** — Ascott explicitly predicts the network paradigm will dissolve this professional dichotomy.
  - Source passage: > "Such implied access and interaction will dissolve the fine art /applied design dichotomies and antipathies that currently exist at the professional level."
- **Specialization/compartmentalization vs holistic/connective thought** — departmental specialism vs the "more holistic thought and images" and "connective" paradigm the network breeds. (Resonates with McLuhan's fragmentation-vs-wholeness and detribalization/retribalization.)
  - Source passage: > "More holistic thought and images will occur"
- **Residual tension (not a contradiction, but a nuance):** Ascott preserves "skill banks allowing for media-specific instruction" even while dissolving departments — i.e. media-specific *skill* survives, discipline-specific *institution* does not. Worth a Human Review note.
