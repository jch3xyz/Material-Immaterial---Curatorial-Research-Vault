## Executive Summary

This is the chapter where Hayles introduces her signature concept of the **flickering signifier**. Building on Chapter 1's account of how information was constructed as a disembodied, probabilistic pattern (Shannon, Wiener, Weaver), she argues that the contemporary epistemic shift she names — away from the dialectic of **presence/absence** and toward the dialectic of **pattern/randomness** — is reshaping both human bodies and textual bodies. The chapter's master move is to read the materiality of information technologies (typewriter vs. computer, print vs. digital text, virtual reality) alongside contemporary "information narratives" (Gibson, DeLillo, Calvino, Burroughs, Leyner, Robbe-Grillet) to show that signification itself has mutated.

The conceptual armature: (1) information is pattern, not presence; noninformation should be randomness, but information theory paradoxically equates information with randomness *as well as* pattern, binding them in a dialectic. (2) Lacanian **floating signifiers** (print-based, organized around absence/castration) give way to **flickering signifiers** (electronic, organized around pattern/randomness/mutation), which exist as flexible chains of markers through layered codes ("a signifier on one level becomes a signified on the next-higher level"). (3) **Mutation** is to the pattern/randomness dialectic what **castration** is to the presence/absence dialectic — its constitutive catastrophe. (4) The shift coincides with the move from the **liberal humanist subject** ("possessive individualism") to the **posthuman**, in which "computation rather than possessive individualism is taken as the ground of being." (5) Crucially, Hayles insists the pattern/randomness dialectic does *not* erase the material world — "information in fact derives its efficacy from the material infrastructures it appears to obscure" — and she names the entire network of relations among technologies, bodies, and texts **informatics** (following Haraway).

The chapter also stages Hayles's central ethical-critical wager: against "the dream or nightmare of the body as information," she argues for a "double vision" attending to the material interfaces that make disembodiment "such a powerful illusion," and insists that "embodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated."

- **Surprisal** — the information of an individual message: "For an individual message, the information increases as the probability that the event will occur diminishes; the more unlikely the event, the more information it conveys. Appropriately, this quantity is usually called the 'surprisal.'"

- **Functionality** — "a term used by virtual reality technologists to describe the communication modes that are active in a computer-human interface."

## Arguments

- **Claim: Information has been constructed as pattern, never present in itself — and is paradoxically equated with both pattern and randomness.**
  - Reasoning structure: Information theory defines information as a probability distribution over coding elements (pattern). Noninformation should then be randomness. But because average information is maximized when symbols are equiprobable (maximum randomness), information gets equated with randomness too — a "powerful paradox" yielding the insight that noise can drive reorganization to higher complexity.
  - Evidence: Weaver's interpretation of Shannon; the "surprisal"; the reading-list / *Gravity's Rainbow* / *Vineland* examples.
  - Source passage: > "Identifying information with both pattern and randomness proved to be a powerful paradox, leading to the realization that in some instances, an infusion of noise into a system can cause it to reorganize at a higher level of complexity."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Information is pattern not presence`

- **Claim: An epistemic shift is displacing presence/absence with pattern/randomness across a wide range of cultural sites.**
  - Reasoning structure: Examples drawn from money, surrogacy/IVF, automated factories, DNA forensics, computer law, online sex show "discursive formations based on pattern and randomness" competing with and displacing formations based on presence/absence.
  - Evidence: the catalogue of informatic cultural sites; the claim about the "long tradition of dominance" of presence/absence in the West.
  - Source passage: > "the surprise is not that formations based on them continue to exist but that these formations are being displaced so rapidly across a wide range of cultural sites."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Pattern and randomness are displacing presence and absence`

- **Claim: Floating signifiers (Lacan) give way to flickering signifiers (informatics).**
  - Reasoning structure: Lacan's print-based dialectic centers absence (signifieds as ungraspable flow; castration). Informatics opens the signifier to internal play of difference through layered codes; a global command transforms the whole chain. Lacanian psycholinguistics (language + sexuality) is replaced by flickering signification (language + machine).
  - Evidence: the machine-code/compiler/processing-program chain; "changing the font is as easy as giving the system a single command."
  - Source passage: > "In contrast to Lacanian psycholinguistics, derived from the generative coupling of linguistics and sexuality, flickering signification is the progeny of the fascinating and troubling coupling of language and machine."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Flickering signifiers replace floating signifiers`

- **Claim: The pattern/randomness shift does NOT erase materiality; the erasure is an illusion to be investigated.**
  - Reasoning structure: It is *because* material interfaces have changed that pattern/randomness can seem dominant; information's efficacy depends on the very material infrastructures it appears to obscure. The illusion of erasure should be the object, not the premise, of inquiry.
  - Evidence: book/body analogy; print's newly visible features; the closing insistence that "embodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated."
  - Source passage: > "In fact, it is precisely because material interfaces have changed that pattern and randomness can be perceived as dominant over presence and absence. The pattern/randomness dialectic does not erase the material world."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Information does not erase materiality`

- **Claim: Human and posthuman are historically specific constructions; the posthuman supersedes the liberal humanist subject when computation replaces possessive individualism as the ground of being.**
  - Reasoning structure: Traces a trajectory — man the tool-user → tool-maker (Oakley, tool as detachable extension) → McLuhan (media as prostheses, autoamputation) → the Macy Conferences' homeostasis/reflexivity debate → 1960s reflexivity → 1980s self-organization → Moravec/Rheingold's posthuman coupling so intense that biological organism and informational circuit can no longer be distinguished.
  - Evidence: Oakley 1949; McLuhan; the three-phase cybernetics chronology; Moravec; Rheingold.
  - Source passage: > "they concur that the posthuman implies not only a coupling with intelligent machines but a coupling so intense and multifaceted that it is no longer possible to distinguish meaningfully between the biological organism and the informational circuits in which the organism is enmeshed."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The posthuman replaces the liberal humanist subject`

- **The Tool / the Prosthesis (Oakley's "detachable extension of the forelimb")** — symbol marking the threshold between the human (tool apart from body) and the posthuman (tool as prosthesis on the head).
  - > "considered functionally they are detachable extensions of the forelimb."
- **The Blinking Cursor** — symbol of the computer "demanding a response," contrasted with print's patient durability.
  - > "there isn't a cursor blinking at me, as if demanding a response."
- **Word Dust / the demagnetized circuit (Calvino's Lotaria scene)** — symbol of the textual body crumbled to frequency-counted fragments ("the the, of of of of").
  - > "The multicolored wires now grind out the dust of dissolved words."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Pattern/Randomness vs. Presence/Absence** — the book's organizing tension. Pole A: pattern and randomness (informatic, electronic). Pole B: presence and absence (print-based, Western metaphysical tradition).
  - > "discursive formations based on pattern and randomness jostle and compete with formations based on presence and absence."
- **Pattern vs. Randomness (internal dialectic)** — not opposites but "complements or supplements"; randomness is the contrasting term that lets pattern be understood as such; noise can drive reorganization.
  - > "pattern and randomness are bound together in a complex dialectic that makes them not so much opposites as complements or supplements to one another."
- **Materiality vs. Immateriality** — "materiality and immateriality are joined in a complex tension that is a source of exultation and strong anxiety." The illusion of erasure vs. the persistence of the material substrate.
  - > "In general, materiality and immateriality are joined in a complex tension that is a source of exultation and strong anxiety."
- **Embodiment vs. Information** — the "dream or nightmare of the body as information" vs. embodiment as instantiated, local, specific, non-replicable. The book's ethical crux.
  - > "Against this dream or nightmare of the body as information, what alternatives exist?"
- **The Human vs. the Posthuman** — liberal humanist subject (possessive individualism) vs. posthuman (computation as ground of being, seamless coupling with machines).
  - > "Flickering signification brings together language with a psychodynamics based on the symbolic moment when the human confronts the posthuman."
- **Homeostasis vs. Reflexivity (vs. self-organization)** — the Macy Conferences' wavering between man as a bounded "homeostatic self-regulating mechanism" and a "reflexive" man "spliced into an informational circuit"; later giving way to self-organization. (The "three waves" in compressed form.)
  - > "Participants wavered between a vision of man as a homeostatic self regulating mechanism whose boundaries were clearly delineated from the environment and a more threatening, reflexive vision of a man spliced into an informational circuit that could change him in unpredictable ways."
- **Access vs. Possession (Ownership)** — possession presupposes presence; access tracks patterns. Reconfigures the public/private distinction central to the novel.
  - > "access differs from possession because the former tracks patterns rather than presences."
- **Floating vs. Flickering Signifiers** — Lacanian absence/castration (print) vs. informatic pattern/randomness/mutation (electronic).
  - > "This textual fluidity, which users learn in their bodies as they interact with the system, implies that signifiers flicker rather than float."
- **Castration vs. Mutation** — the catastrophe of presence/absence vs. the catastrophe of pattern/randomness.
  - > "Mutation is the catastrophe in the pattern/randomness dialectic analogous to castration in the presence/absence dialectic."
- **Body vs. Message / Signal vs. Materiality** — both books and bodies are simultaneously physical structure and informational expression; their "parallel doubleness."
  - > "the literary corpus is at once a physical object and a space of representation, a body and a message."
