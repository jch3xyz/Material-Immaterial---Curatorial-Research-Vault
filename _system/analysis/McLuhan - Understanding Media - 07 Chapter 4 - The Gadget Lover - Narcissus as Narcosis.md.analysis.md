## Executive Summary

This is a Part I core-theory chapter — arguably THE chapter where McLuhan grounds the physiological / psychic mechanism behind his whole apparatus. He reads the Greek myth of Narcissus etymologically (Narcissus from *narcosis*, numbness) to argue that humans are constitutively numbed to their own technological extensions: Narcissus did not fall in love with himself but failed to recognize his mirror-image as an *extension* of himself, and so became "the servomechanism of his own extended or repeated image." McLuhan fuses this with the stress-physiology of Hans Selye and Adolphe Jonas: every extension of ourselves is an "autoamputation," a defensive response by which the central nervous system isolates and offloads an organ or function under the "stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load." The wheel is the autoamputation of the foot; all media from speech to computer originate this way. The crucial twist: any amplified, isolated function is "bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception" — "Self-amputation forbids self-recognition." He then introduces the law of *sense ratios* and *sense "closure"*: any new extension forces a new equilibrium among the senses ("there is . . . no way of refusing to comply with the new sense ratios"), citing Blake's *Jerusalem* and the 113th Psalm ("They that make them shall be like unto them"). With electric technology man has "extended, or set outside himself, a live model of the central nervous system itself" — a "desperate and suicidal autoamputation" that, paradoxically, by numbing the CNS, transfers conscious awareness to the body and produces the first age "of consciousness of the unconscious." The chapter closes on the global-village register: "In the electric age we wear all mankind as our skin." This chapter supplies the durable definitions of *Autoamputation* and *Narcosis/Narcissus numbness* that the book's focus calls for, plus the *servomechanism* relation and the explicit statement of *sense ratios* and *Extensions of Man*.

## Definitions

- **Autoamputation (McLuhan)** — A technology or extension understood as a defensive self-amputation: when the perceptual power cannot locate or avoid an irritant, the central nervous system isolates and offloads the over-stressed organ or function. "Any extension of ourselves they regard as 'autoamputation' . . . the autoamputative power or strategy is resorted to by the body when the perceptual power cannot locate or avoid the cause of irritation." (Focus-mandated definition to mint.)

- **Narcosis / Narcissus Numbness (McLuhan)** — The numbness that necessarily accompanies extension: amplification of an isolated function is "bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception," so that "Self-amputation forbids self-recognition." Etymologically grounded: "It is from the Greek word narcosis, or numbness." (Focus-mandated, paired with Autoamputation.)

- **Servomechanism (McLuhan, applied)** — The relation in which man, by embracing his extensions, becomes their servant: "By continuously embracing technologies, we relate ourselves to them as servo-mechanisms." "An Indian is the servo-mechanism of his canoe, as the cowboy of his horse or the executive of his clock." (Strongly clarified here; supports the `Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions` concept rather than a standalone definition note unless a later pass demands one.)

## Arguments

- **Claim: Narcissus was numbed not by self-love but by failing to recognize his own extension.**
  - Reasoning structure: etymological (Narcissus ← narcosis) + corrective reading against the popular "fell in love with himself" interpretation; the numbness is the *point* of the myth.
  - Evidence: the youth "mistook his own reflection . . . for another person"; Echo's "fragments of his own speech" fail because "He was numb."
  - Source passage: > "the wisdom of the Narcissus myth does not convey any idea that Narcissus fell in love with anything he regarded as himself. Obviously he would have had very different feelings about the image had he known it was an extension or repetition of himself."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Narcissus was numbed by his own extension not by self-love.md`

- **Claim: Every technology is an autoamputation, and amplification is bearable only through numbness.**
  - Reasoning structure: imports Selye/Jonas stress physiology → generalizes from disease to invention → the numbness is the precondition of using the extension at all.
  - Evidence: the wheel as counter-irritant amplifying the isolated function of the feet in rotation; "Such amplification is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception."
  - Source passage: > "the central nervous system acts to protect itself by a strategy of amputation or isolation of the offending organ, sense, or function. Thus, the stimulus to new invention is the stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Every extension of man is an autoamputation that numbs perception.md`

- **Claim: To perceive or use any extension of ourselves is necessarily to embrace it and undergo a forced sense "closure."**
  - Reasoning structure: builds on Blake's law of perception ("If Perceptive Organs vary, Objects of Perception seem to vary") + the Psalmist's "They that make them shall be like unto them" → no opt-out from the new sense ratio.
  - Evidence: TV's intensification of the visual sense in audile-tactile Europe and the inverse opening of audile-tactile perception in visual America; "there is . . . no way of refusing to comply."
  - Source passage: > "To behold, use or perceive any extension of ourselves in technological form is necessarily to embrace it. To listen to radio or to read the printed page is to accept these extensions of ourselves into our personal system and to undergo the 'closure' or displacement of perception that follows automatically."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/To perceive an extension of ourselves is necessarily to embrace it.md`

- **Claim: Electric technology externalizes the central nervous system itself, a suicidal autoamputation.**
  - Reasoning structure: extends the autoamputation logic to its limit — when the CNS is the thing extended, the body can no longer buffer it; the successive mechanizations since printing may have made social experience "too violent and superstimulated . . . for the central nervous system to endure."
  - Evidence: "a live model of the central nervous system itself"; the closing "we wear all mankind as our skin."
  - Source passage: > "With the arrival of electric technology, man extended, or set outside himself, a live model of the central nervous system itself. To the degree that this is so, it is a development that suggests a desperate and suicidal autoamputation . . ."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Electric technology is the autoamputation of the central nervous system.md`

- **Claim: The numbed electric age is paradoxically the first age of "consciousness of the unconscious."**
  - Reasoning structure: dialectical — because the CNS is "strategically numbed," conscious order migrates to the body, and "instant, total field-awareness" hauls "the subliminal life, private and social, . . . up into full view."
  - Evidence: "social consciousness" arriving "as a cause of guilt-feelings"; existentialism as the age's congruent philosophy.
  - Source passage: > "Thus the age of anxiety and of electric media is also the age of the unconscious and of apathy. But it is strikingly the age of consciousness of the unconscious, in addition."
  - Confidence: medium (strongly stated, but more thematic than a load-bearing thesis; good for the source summary and the Numbness vs Awareness tension)
  - Recommended wiki note: capture in source summary; optional `arguments/The numbed electric age is the age of consciousness of the unconscious.md` (human-review)

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Numbness vs Awareness** — the constitutive paradox of the chapter: extension demands numbness ("Self-amputation forbids self-recognition"), yet the electric externalization of the CNS produces "consciousness of the unconscious" and "instant, total field-awareness." (Focus-named tension; mint `Numbness vs Awareness`.)
  - > "Such amplification is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception."

- **Fragmentation vs Wholeness** — Blake's fragmented "Reasoning Power . . . separated from Imagination" vs the closing electric wholeness ("we wear all mankind as our skin"); each autoamputated organ becomes "a closed system of great new intensity." (Focus-named tension; mint `Fragmentation vs Wholeness`.)
  - > "Blake, in a word, sees man as fragmented by his technologies."

- **Mechanical vs Electric** — the "successive mechanizations of the various physical organs since the invention of printing" (fragmenting, superstimulating) vs electric technology as the externalized CNS (a single live model). (Focus-named; mint `Mechanical vs Electric`, anchored across Part I; this chapter is a supporting source.)
  - > "It could well be that the successive mechanizations of the various physical organs since the invention of printing have made too violent and superstimulated a social experience for the central nervous system to endure."

- **Pleasure (counter-irritant) vs Comfort (removal of irritants)** — a minor but explicit opposition: "pleasure is a counter-irritant . . . comfort is the removal of irritants. Both . . . are strategies of equilibrium for the central nervous system." (Local conceptual pair; capture in source summary, not a vault tension note.)
