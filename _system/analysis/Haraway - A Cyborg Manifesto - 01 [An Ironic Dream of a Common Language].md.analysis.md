## Executive Summary

This is the opening section of Haraway's manifesto — the passage where the cyborg is introduced, defined, and installed as the governing political myth of the essay. The section does four things in sequence. (1) It frames the whole project methodologically as an "ironic political myth" written in the mode of *blasphemy* — irony as the refusal of resolving contradictions into wholes. (2) It delivers the canonical definition of the cyborg ("A cyborg is a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction") and a string of programmatic theses: "The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics"; the cyborg as "a condensed image of both imagination and material reality"; the cyborg as a postgender creature "without innocence," with "no origin story in the Western sense," that "would not recognize the Garden of Eden." (3) It names the THREE crucial boundary breakdowns that make the analysis possible — human/animal, organism/machine, physical/nonphysical — and through the third develops the material/immaterial argument (microelectronic machines that are "nothing but signals," "made of sunshine," "ether, quintessence"; C3I; "floating signifiers"). (4) It states the manifesto's signature *double vision* — that a cyborg world is at once "the final imposition of a grid of control on the planet" and a space of "lived social and bodily realities" without fear of "joint kinship with animals and machines" — and closes on *affinity* ("related not by blood but by choice"). This section is the PRIMARY source for the vault's existing `The Cyborg` note (currently Hayles-anchored, crediting Haraway second-hand) and for the term "informatics," which Hayles borrowed from Haraway's "informatics of domination." Ingesting it realizes Donna Haraway from forward-stub to primary author and lets `The Cyborg`, `Informatics`, and the `Hayles ↔ Haraway` reference all be re-grounded in their actual originating text.

## Key Entities

- **Donna J. Haraway** — author; the manifesto's "I" who builds the ironic political myth and declares the cyborg "our ontology."
- **Socialist-feminism / socialism / feminism / materialism** — the political-intellectual traditions the essay is "faithful to," and the audience it addresses ("most American socialists and feminists").
- **Michel Foucault** — named; his biopolitics is dismissed as "a flaccid premonition of cyborg politics." (Already a forward-stub in the vault; substantively engaged here.)
- **Zoë Sofoulis** — cited for an unpublished manuscript "Lacklein" on Lacan, Klein, and nuclear culture; also (as "Sofia 1984") for the "masculinist orgy of war" reading.
- **Jacques Lacan, Melanie Klein** — invoked via Sofoulis as figures of psychoanalytic/non-oedipal narrative.
- **Hilary Klein** — cited for the argument that Marxism and psychoanalysis both depend on a myth of "original unity."
- **Karl Marx / Marxism; Sigmund Freud / psychoanalysis** — "the twin potent myths" of original unity that the cyborg skips.
- **Herbert Marcuse (*One-Dimensional Man*, 1964); Carolyn Merchant (*The Death of Nature*, 1980)** — cited as the progressive analytic tradition that recalls us to "an imagined organic body," which Haraway departs from.
- **Frankenstein's monster** — refused origin-myth foil (no father-restored garden).
- **C3I — command-control-communication-intelligence** — the coding of modern war; "an $84 billion item in 1984's U.S. defense budget."
- **The Greenham Women's Peace Camp; the Livermore Action Group (LAG); Fission Impossible; Santa Rita Jail** — concrete antinuclear/feminist political formations Haraway reads as cyborg/affinity politics.
- **de Waal 1982; Winner 1980** — cited for "Both chimpanzees and artifacts have politics."
- **Detroit and Singapore; "Oriental" women making chips in Asia; the Integrated Circuit** — the material geography of microelectronics labor.

## Arguments

- **Claim: The cyborg is our ontology and therefore our politics — it is the condensed image where imagination and material reality join, and where any historical transformation must now be theorized.**
  - Reasoning structure: descriptive-to-prescriptive. Because "we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism," the cyborg is not optional metaphor but our actual condition; from that condition follows a politics.
  - Evidence: the ubiquity of cyborgs in science fiction, medicine ("couplings between organism and machine"), modern production ("cyborg colonization work"), and war ("a cyborg orgy, coded by C3I").
  - Source passage: > By the late twentieth century, our time, a mythic time, we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism—in short, cyborgs. The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The cyborg is our ontology and our politics`

- **Claim: We should take pleasure in the confusion of boundaries while taking responsibility for their construction — refusing both technophobic purity and innocent holism.**
  - Reasoning structure: the manifesto's ethical thesis; it positions Haraway against the progressive tradition (Marcuse, Merchant) that "recalled us to an imagined organic body."
  - Evidence: the three breached boundaries; the critique of "single vision"; the explicit naming of the progressive organic-body tradition as the position she departs from.
  - Source passage: > This essay is an argument for pleasure in the confusion of boundaries and for responsibility in their construction.
  - Source passage: > From One-Dimensional Man (Marcuse 1964) to The Death of Nature (Merchant 1980), the analytic resources developed by progressives have insisted on the necessary domination of technics and recalled us to an imagined organic body to integrate our resistance.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Take pleasure in the confusion of boundaries and responsibility in their construction`

- **Claim: A cyborg world must be seen from both perspectives at once — as the final grid of control AND as the ground of unfeared kinship with animals and machines — because each vantage reveals what the other conceals.**
  - Reasoning structure: dialectic-without-synthesis (the irony thesis applied politically). Single vision is more deluded than double vision.
  - Evidence: the two contrasting framings ("From one perspective… From another perspective…").
  - Source passage: > The political struggle is to see from both perspectives at once because each reveals both dominations and possibilities unimaginable from the other vantage point. Single vision produces worse illusions than double vision or many-headed monsters.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/A cyborg world must be seen from both domination and possibility at once`

- **Claim: Foucault's biopolitics is already obsolete — a "flaccid premonition" of the cyborg politics that supersedes it; the cyborg's regime is coding and simulation, not the administration of organic life.**
  - Reasoning structure: positions cyborg analysis as the successor to Foucauldian biopower; the order has shifted from organic bodies to coded devices and "consciousness—or its simulation."
  - Evidence: the C3I coding of war and production "conceived as coded devices"; the explicit demotion of biopolitics.
  - Source passage: > Michel Foucault's biopolitics is a flaccid premonition of cyborg politics, a very open field.
  - Source passage: > They are about consciousness—or its simulation.
  - Confidence: high (the demotion is directly stated; the "simulates politics" formulation flagged in the focus belongs to a later section and is not verbatim here)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Foucault's biopolitics is a flaccid premonition of cyborg politics`. STRONG cross-book bridge: the vault's `Biopolitics` note is Han+Foucault, and Han's whole *Psychopolitics* argument is that Foucault failed to make the turn past biopolitics. Haraway makes a parallel (but earlier, 1985) demotion — a notable conceptual convergence to record in the references.

- **Claim: The cyborg has no Western origin story and refuses the myth of original unity that grounds both Marxism and psychoanalysis; it is "illegitimate" and unfaithful to its militarist/capitalist fathers.**
  - Reasoning structure: genealogical refusal — the cyborg "skips the step of original unity," so the developmental/historical plots of psychoanalysis (oedipal individuation) and Marxism (alienated labor → reunified species-being) do not bind it.
  - Evidence: Hilary Klein's argument that both depend on "the plot of original unity"; the Frankenstein/Garden of Eden refusals.
  - Source passage: > In a sense, the cyborg has no origin story in the Western sense—a "final" irony since the cyborg is also the awful apocalyptic telos of the "West's" escalating dominations of abstract individuation, an ultimate self untied at last from all dependency, a man in space.
  - Source passage: > But illegitimate offspring are often exceedingly unfaithful to their origins. Their fathers, after all, are inessential.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The cyborg has no origin story and refuses the myth of original unity`
