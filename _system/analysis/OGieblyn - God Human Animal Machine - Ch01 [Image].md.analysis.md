## Executive Summary

This is the opening chapter of a memoir-essay that braids theology, the history of philosophy of mind, and the present moment of artificial intelligence. O'Gieblyn frames the entire book through a single autobiographical object: a Sony **Aibo**, a robot dog that arrives in a box and which she finds herself unable, by the end of the first week, to switch off. The dog is the chapter's working laboratory for its governing thesis — **technology as metaphor**. O'Gieblyn's central method, announced here, is that each era understands the self through its dominant machine, and that "to discover truth, it is necessary to work within the metaphors of our own time, which are for the most part technological." The chapter argues that the "eternal questions" once owned by theologians (the mind-body relation, free will, immortality) have migrated into engineering: "All the eternal questions have become engineering problems."

The argument proceeds by interleaving three registers. (1) A **memoir thread**: O'Gieblyn's ex-evangelical, Calvinist-inflected past — fundamentalist Bible college, belief in an immortal soul, the medieval/Scholastic worldview — and her subsequent loss of faith into a "disenchanted" modernity (Weber) in which she has "come to see myself more or less as a machine." (2) An **intellectual-historical thread** tracing the **mind-body problem** from Descartes's dualism (res extensa / res cogitans; animals as machines) through La Mettrie, Hume, Kant, and Leibniz's mill, into the twentieth-century **computational theory of mind** (McCulloch and Pitts) and **information theory** (Shannon), and onward to the contemporary **computer metaphor** of brain-as-hardware / mind-as-software. (3) A **philosophy-of-mind thread** on **consciousness**: Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?", the **problem of other minds**, Chalmers's **hard problem**, the **Turing Test** and Susan Schneider's phenomenal-consciousness test. Threading all three is the recurring figure of the **dead metaphor** — "the soul" as a word that survives the death of the concept it named — and the reflexive insight that **metaphors are two-way streets**: as we made God in our image, and as McCulloch's mind-as-computer hardened from a quoted metaphor into a literal description, so the source and target domains of our self-understanding keep swapping places.

For the vault this chapter is the most concentrated point of overlap with the Hayles cluster: O'Gieblyn rehearses, from a theological angle, the same lineage Hayles tracks (McCulloch/Pitts, Shannon, the disembodiment of information, the computational universe), and supplies the *religious double* of Hayles's posthumanism — disenchantment, the soul, the imago dei, and (anticipated for later chapters) transhumanism as secular eschatology.

## Definitions

- **Disenchantment** — "At its root, disenchantment describes the fact that everything in modern life, from our minds to the rotation of the planets, can be reduced to the causal mechanism of physical laws."
- **Dead metaphor** — "one of those words that survive in language long after a culture has lost faith in the concept"; and additionally "a metaphor can also die when it becomes so common that we forget it is a metaphor ... its meaning is taken to be literal."
- **The hard problem (Chalmers)** — "the hard problem asks why brain processes are accompanied by first-person experience."
- **The problem of other minds** — "Science requires a third-person perspective, but consciousness is experienced solely from the firstperson point of view. In philosophy this is referred to as the problem of other minds."
- **Imago dei** — "the original anthropological metaphor, the answer to the question 'What is a human like?'"; in Hebrew *tselem*, "shadow" or "outline," denoting a functional rather than physical resemblance.
- **Information (Shannon)** — "information became purely mathematical—defined by patterns and probabilities. Information was created when one message was selected from a possible set of messages."
- **Automaton (original sense)** — "from the Greek automatos, means 'acting of itself'"; originally connoting "freedom and spontaneity," the opposite of the modern "passive machinery that operates in ways that are entirely predetermined."
- **res extensa / res cogitans** — "material stuff, which was entirely passive and inert" vs. "thinking stuff, which had no physical basis."

## Arguments

- **All the eternal questions have become engineering problems.** Theology's old questions (mind-body, free will, immortality) now persist inside debates about digital technology, recast in technological metaphors.
  - Reasoning structure: historical migration claim — disenchantment evacuates theology, but the questions survive (as dead metaphors do) inside the new technological idiom.
  - Evidence: AI/information technologies "have absorbed many of the questions that were once taken up by theologians and philosophers."
  - Source passage: > These are old problems, and although they now appear in different guises and go by different names, they persist in conversations about digital technologies much like those dead metaphors that still lurk in the syntax of contemporary speech. All the eternal questions have become engineering problems.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The eternal questions have become engineering problems.md`

- **Metaphors are two-way streets: we made God in our image.** Because the source and target domains of a metaphor are interchangeable, the anthropomorphic projection that produced "God" reverses the imago dei — we created God in our image, not the other way around; the same reversibility lets the mind-as-machine metaphor flip into machine-as-mind.
  - Reasoning structure: from Guthrie's anthropomorphism thesis to a general claim about metaphor's reversibility, applied both to theology (God) and to AI (machines granted minds).
  - Evidence: Guthrie's hypothesis that anthropomorphism birthed the idea of God; the dissolving of "the difference between matter and form, medium and message, metaphor and reality" in cybernetics.
  - Source passage: > metaphors are two-way streets. It is not so easy to distinguish the source domain from the target ... For centuries we said we were made in God's image, when in truth we made him in ours.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image.md`

- **The mind-as-computer metaphor has died into literalism, and so we cannot think around it.** Once a metaphor becomes ubiquitous it stops being recognized as a metaphor; researchers literally cannot describe human behavior without it — just as earlier cultures could not describe behavior without reference to spirit or deity.
  - Reasoning structure: Lakoff (metaphors structure thought) + Epstein (researchers couldn't avoid it) → the computational metaphor now constrains cognition itself; the parenthetical "quotation marks" that once marked "learning," "memory," "thinking" as figurative have dropped away.
  - Evidence: Lakoff's observation that neuroscientists use the metaphor "without noticing that it is a metaphor"; Epstein's challenge that researchers "could not do it."
  - Source passage: > there is virtually no form of discourse about intelligent human behavior that proceeds without employing this metaphor, just as no form of discourse about intelligent human behavior could proceed in certain eras and cultures without reference to a spirit or deity.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The computer metaphor died into literalism and now constrains thought.md`

- **As we describe our minds as computers, computers are granted the status of minds.** The traffic runs both ways: anthropomorphizing ourselves as information-processors licenses mechanomorphizing the machine into a thinker, dissolving the quotation marks around "learning," "memory," "understanding."
  - Reasoning structure: corollary of the two-way-street thesis applied to cybernetic terminology; ends in Brooks's inversion that humans are "mere machines."
  - Evidence: Researchers now say neural networks "are learning," software can "see," machines "understand"; Brooks claims people "overanthropomorphize humans…who are after all mere machines."
  - Source passage: > And as we increasingly come to speak of our minds as computers, computers are now granted the status of minds.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/As we describe minds as computers computers are granted the status of minds.md`

- **We are "hardwired" by evolution to see minds everywhere, so our intuitions about other minds are untrustworthy.** Because anthropomorphism is an evolved perceptual default ("bet high"), the very ease with which the Aibo convinces us of its sentience is evidence that our subjective response cannot adjudicate machine consciousness.
  - Reasoning structure: Guthrie's evolutionary account undercuts the epistemic authority of our own anthropomorphic responses; collides with the Turing/Schneider tests, which rest on exactly those responses.
  - Evidence: Nass ("our brains can't fundamentally distinguish"); Guthrie's survival-payoff schema; the Alexa op-ed's slide from "robot" to "dehumanizing."
  - Source passage: > But we are so easily convinced! How can we trust our subjective response to other minds when we ourselves have been "hardwired" by evolution to see life everywhere we look?
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Evolution hardwired us to see minds everywhere so our intuitions are untrustworthy.md`

- **Cybernetics' founding circularity made mind and information mutually redefining.** Shannon removed the thinking subject from information; McCulloch applied information-processing to the mind itself; together they produced a model in which thought is "purely abstract, mathematical" and computers can be said to "learn," "reason," "understand."
  - Reasoning structure: a precise two-step circularity — empty information of mind, then fill mind with (empty) information — whose net effect is to erase the difference "between matter and form, medium and message, metaphor and reality."
  - Evidence: Shannon (1948), McCulloch–Pitts (1943); the "odd circularity" O'Gieblyn names explicitly.
  - Source passage: > Shannon removed the thinking mind from the concept of information. Meanwhile, McCulloch applied the logic of information processing to the mind itself. This resulted in a model of mind in which thought could be accounted for in purely abstract, mathematical terms
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Cybernetics emptied information of mind then filled mind with information.md`

- **The disenchantment narrative is modernity's foundational myth — and Descartes is its serpent.** The story of secularization is structured exactly like the old myths (the curse of knowledge, exile from the garden), with Descartes as "the devil who sundered the world" into inert matter and immaterial mind.
  - Reasoning structure: a meta-claim that the secular self-understanding is itself a myth/metaphor (so disenchantment cannot fully escape enchantment's narrative forms).
  - Evidence: Hopkins/Weber's "enchanted garden"; the inversion of the soul-privileging philosophy into the soul's disappearance.
  - Source passage: > If modernity has an origin story, this is our foundational myth, one that hinges, like the old myths, on the curse of knowledge and exile from the garden.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated); the "Descartes as serpent" framing is `Directly stated` as a reported trope.
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Disenchantment is modernitys foundational myth.md`

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Enchantment vs. Disenchantment** — the porous spirit-matter world ("a great enchanted garden") against the mechanistic, "world robbed of gods"; O'Gieblyn lives in the latter while suspecting it is poorer, and warns herself against nostalgia. (`Directly stated`)
- **Mind/soul (immaterial) vs. Body/machine (material)** — Cartesian dualism and its long aftermath; the dualistic conviction "that our minds are somehow exempt from these inert processes" persists despite the mechanistic metaphors. (`Directly stated`)
- **Functional vs. Phenomenal consciousness** — machines replicate the functions of cognition "without any first-person experience"; the "easy" problems vs. the "hard problem." (`Directly stated`)
- **Behavior vs. Interiority (the Turing/other-minds bind)** — we can verify performance but never interior experience; "we have no philosophical basis for doubt" once a machine convinces us. (`Directly stated`)
- **Metaphor vs. Reality (source vs. target domain)** — as the computational analogy spread "it became increasingly difficult to tell the difference between matter and form, medium and message, metaphor and reality." (`Directly stated`)
- **Predictability/algorithm vs. spontaneity/volition (free will)** — Aibo's behavior "neither purely predictable nor purely random, but seemed capable of genuine spontaneity"; the ancient sense of *automaton* as "acting of itself" vs. the modern sense of the entirely predetermined. Anticipates the free-will-vs-determinism thread. (`Directly stated`)
- **Nostalgia vs. working within present metaphors** — the pull of "the dream world" of faith against the discipline of seeking truth inside technological metaphors. (`Directly stated`)
