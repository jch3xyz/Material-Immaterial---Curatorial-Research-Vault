## Executive Summary

This is one of Ascott's central, fully realized **telematic-art** manifestos. Written in 1989 (peak of his networked-art period, the same era as "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?"), it argues that **computer networking is the constitutive medium and metaphor of late-twentieth-century culture** and that its true vocation is artistic-philosophical rather than informational: "to make the invisible visible." The essay moves through three named stages signaled in its subtitle — **Connectivity, Transformation, Transcendence** — building from the technical fact of networked interconnection, through the network's power to *transform* information, identity, and reality, to a frankly spiritual claim that networking answers a "deep psychological desire for transcendence... a kind of biotechnological theology."

The chapter's keystone is the coinage **Gesamtdatenwerk** ("integrated data work" / "total data work"), Ascott's deliberate echo of Wagner's **Gesamtkunstwerk** (total artwork). Where Wagner's total artwork was staged in an opera house, the Gesamtdatenwerk is staged on "the planet as a whole, its data space, its electronic noosphere" — a permanently open, indeterminate, reciprocal work that collapses the distinction between artist and viewer, producer and consumer. The essay also contains Ascott's most quotable redefinition of the computer ("The computer is not primarily a thing, but a set of behaviours") and his interface philosophy (the interface as "a doorway into data space, a synaptic interval in a human-computer symbiosis" rather than a "membrane" separating a discrete machine-object from the self).

A second major argument-thread is **the physics of information**: Ascott maps quantum physics onto computer science (quanta:data :: transformation of energy:transformation of information), invokes indeterminacy/uncertainty (Heisenberg, Schrödinger, John Bell, Alain Aspect; Bergson, Nietzsche in philosophy), and concludes that reality is *constructed*, plural, "virtual," and participation-dependent — "reality is the production of observation." This grounds his constructivist epistemology and his explicit, polemical rejection of "the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought."

For the vault, this chapter is the **direct extension point** for the two committed art/new-media stubs Burnham realized (`New Media Art`, `Art as Experiential Technology`), the strongest single anchor for new Ascott concepts (`Gesamtdatenwerk`, `Network Consciousness`, `Data Space`, `The Telematic Embrace`/telematics, `Telenoia`-adjacent connectivity, the **Connectivity/Transformation/Transcendence** triad), and a major McLuhan bridge (the global village → planetary network/noosphere; the computer as extension/symbiosis of human senses). It is also the cleanest Ascott↔Burnham contrast: Burnham's *object→system* systems esthetics (the artwork becomes a system) versus Ascott's *system→network* telematics (the system becomes a planet-wide participatory data field).

## Key Entities

People, authors, institutions, technologies named in the chapter:
- **Roy Ascott** — author; founding theorist of telematic art, pioneer (with Burnham) of 1960s cybernetic art.

- **Henri Bergson** and **Friedrich Nietzsche** — named as philosophers who "in their different ways have pointed to this way of thinking about reality and cognition" (constructed, processual, perspectival reality).

- **John Bell** and **Alain Aspect** — named as having "theorized and demonstrated" the physical manifestations of quantum nonlocality/indeterminacy ("more dramatically").

- **Edward A. Shanken** — editor of the 2003 collection (front matter / editorial omission note; not Ascott's argument).

- Concepts invoked from physics/science: quantum physics, the "stochastic leap," phase space, the principle of indeterminacy and uncertainty, strict causality, "undifferentiated wholeness."

## Arguments

- **The overarching project of late-twentieth-century art is to make the invisible visible.**
  - Reasoning structure: Networking, mediated/accelerated by the computer, amplifies thought, memory, and the human senses, and so is uniquely suited to bring within human reach what lies "outside of our perceptual range, the far side of the mind."
  - Evidence: enumeration of what networking extends (mind to mind, memory to memory, global reach, diverse input modes in "universal data space").
  - Source passage: > "networking is particularly suited to take on the great challenge of late twentieth-century art, which can be seen as the overarching project of our time: to make the invisible visible. That is, to bring to our senses, to make available to our minds, within the human constraints of space and time, what is otherwise beyond our reach, outside of our perceptual range, the far side of the mind."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated, repeated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The project of our time is to make the invisible visible`.

- **Computer networking answers a desire for transcendence — a "biotechnological theology."**
  - Reasoning structure: Networking provides a field of human/artificial-intelligence symbiosis that yields cultural transformation and "enriched definitions of the human condition," and ultimately satisfies the wish "to reach the immaterial, the spiritual," to be "out of body, out of mind."
  - Evidence: the explicit equation of networking with transcendence and theology.
  - Source passage: > "Computer networking, in short, responds to our deep psychological desire for transcendence—to reach the immaterial, the spiritual—the wish to be out of body, out of mind, to exceed the limitations of time and space, a kind of biotechnological theology."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated). Strong O'Gieblyn `Re-enchantment` / `Disembodied Information as the Soul` bridge, and the keystone Ascott-side anchor for `Material vs Immaterial` ("to reach the immaterial, the spiritual").
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Computer networking is a desire for transcendence a biotechnological theology`.

- **Reality is constructed, plural, and "virtual," produced by participatory observation — a repudiation of postmodern pessimism.**
  - Reasoning structure: From quantum physics ("reality is the production of observation") Ascott derives a constructivist, perspectival, indeterminate epistemology; networking lets us construct and enter multiple coexisting realities; this is a *constructive* alternative to postmodern relativism's pessimism.
  - Evidence: Bergson/Nietzsche (philosophy); Heisenberg/Schrödinger/Bell/Aspect (physics); the indeterminacy/uncertainty principle.
  - Source passage: > "It is through computer networking that we can deal creatively with relativism and with pluralism, providing a radically constructive repudiation of the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Reality is the production of observation and networking lets us construct it`.

- **The computer must be made invisible; it is not a thing but a set of behaviours.**
  - Reasoning structure: If art's project is to render the invisible visible, the "very visible computer" must itself become invisible/transparent — understood as pure system and behaviour, not as screen/keyboard apparatus. To use it as "no more than the apparatus of the accountant" is "to be condemned to produce art of the 'bottom line'... an art of finalities, of completion," denying the medium's "open-endedness, nonlinearity, and fecund incompleteness."
  - Evidence: the clerk/accountant figure; the membrane-vs-doorway interface contrast; the demand for body-/gaze-responsive interfaces.
  - Source passage: > "The computer is not primarily a thing, but a set of behaviours."; and > "To do so is to deny, in other words, the essential qualities of open-endedness, nonlinearity, and fecund incompleteness that are the great distinguishing potential of computer-mediated art practice."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated).
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The computer is not a thing but a set of behaviours and must be made invisible`.

- **The Gesamtdatenwerk abolishes the distinction between artist and viewer (producer and consumer).**
  - Reasoning structure: Because reciprocity and interaction are of its essence, the planetary networked work cannot differentiate maker from spectator; "to participate in such a network is always to be involved in the creation of meaning and experience." This is the telematic culmination of Ascott's 1960s "the spectator completes the work."
  - Evidence: the window-vs-doorway figure ("One can no longer be at the window, looking in on a scene composed by another, one is instead invited to enter the doorway into a world where interaction is all").
  - Source passage: > "Since reciprocity and interaction are of its essence, such work cannot differentiate between "artist" and "viewer," producer and consumer. To participate in such a network is always to be involved in the creation of meaning and experience."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated). Direct `Reciprocal Communication` extension; cross-links Lippard `Conceptual Art`/`Dematerialization` (distribution/participation) and Burnham's spectator-completes-the-system.
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The networked artwork abolishes the distinction between artist and viewer`.

## Metaphors

- **Gesamtdatenwerk echoing the Gesamtkunstwerk** — the central analogy: the total *data* work as the planet-scale successor to Wagner's total artwork; the opera house replaced by "the planet as a whole, its data space, its electronic noosphere." (Metaphor + concept; primary source for the `Gesamtdatenwerk` note.)
  - > "echoing the Gesamtkunstwerk, or "total artwork," conceived by Richard Wagner."

- **Data as quanta / the physics of information** — the governing analogy structuring the whole middle of the essay: data:computer science :: quanta:quantum physics; data "jump" between semantic states as quanta jump levels; the "stochastic leap" from chaos to new order.
  - > "And, as with quantum behaviour, data are discontinuous; they "jump" between semantic states."

- **The interface as a synaptic interval (vs. a membrane) / the doorway vs. the window** — the interface as a synapse in a human-computer symbiosis, a doorway into data space; opposed to the membrane that walls off the computer-object, and to the "window" of perspectival representation one looks *in* through.
  - > "a doorway into data space, a synaptic interval in a human-computer symbiosis."

- **The clerk / the accountant / "art of the bottom line"** — the demeaning figure of the constrained user: reduced by keyboard-and-mouse to "the posture of a clerk," producing "an art of finalities, of completion."
  - > "the user is forced into the posture of a clerk."

- **The computer interface as a holographic unity** — each interface is "an aspect of a holographic unity"; "to be in or at any one interface is to be with all the interfaces throughout the network of which it is a part."
  - > "The computer interface is, in each individual case, an aspect of a holographic unity."

- **Data harvest** — gathering images/music/texts "from the endlessly flowing data stream" understood as "a kind of data harvest," a mode of accessing/selecting/displaying distinct from painting/book/film aesthetics.
  - > "should be understood as a kind of data harvest."

- **The electronic noosphere** — the planetary networked thinking-layer as the site of the Gesamtdatenwerk (Teilhardian image).
  - > "its data space, its electronic noosphere."

## Symbols

- **The opera house vs. the planet** — Wagner's bounded performance site versus the unbounded planetary data space; the move from the nineteenth-century total artwork to the late-twentieth-century total data work.
- **The window vs. the doorway** — the perspectival window (representation, the viewer outside looking in on "a scene composed by another") versus the doorway (immersion, participation, "a world where interaction is all"). Closely tied to the "membrane vs. synaptic interval" interface figure.
- **The keyboard and the "mouse"** — the impoverished, "culture-bound" gestural apparatus that imprisons the user as a clerk; emblem of the not-yet-invisible computer.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Object/thing vs. system/behaviour** — the computer as "thing, as object, as apparatus, as machine" vs. as "pure system, universal transformative matrix," "a set of behaviours." (Maps to `Object vs System` (Burnham) and `Material vs Immaterial`; this is Ascott's behaviourist-telematic version of Burnham's object→system.)
- **Material vs immaterial / spiritual** — networking as the route "to reach the immaterial, the spiritual"; data as "everywhere and nowhere," "time-free and time-resistant." (Direct `Material vs Immaterial` extension.)
- **Connectivity/planetary culture vs. homogenization** — superconnectivity explicitly *not* the neutralization of cultural/regional/individual difference, but "the interpenetration of parallel universes of discourse." (A built-in answer to the global-village-as-flattening worry.)
- **Completion/finality vs. open-endedness / fecund incompleteness** — "art of the 'bottom line'" (finalities, completion) vs. the network medium's "open-endedness, nonlinearity, and fecund incompleteness." (Recommended tension: `Completion vs Incompleteness` or `Finality vs Open-Endedness`.)
- **Artist vs. viewer (collapsed)** — the producer/consumer distinction that the Gesamtdatenwerk dissolves. (Maps to `Reciprocal Communication`; recommend folding into that argument rather than a new tension.)
- **Linear/causal vs. nonlinear/indeterminate** — "Strict causality operates only within a limited stratum of events"; data and creative behaviour are "always nonlinear, indeterminate, and uncertain"; networking is "a non-linear creative medium."
- **Constructive optimism vs. postmodern pessimism** — Ascott's "radically constructive repudiation of the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought." (A genuine intellectual positioning, candidate for a reference/argument rather than a tension note.)
