## Executive Summary

This is the keystone "power" chapter of Part III. It performs Zuboff's central theoretical move: naming the NEW species of power that surveillance capitalism wields. She calls it **instrumentarianism** — "the instrumentation and instrumentalization of behavior for the purposes of modification, prediction, monetization, and control." The chapter's whole rhetorical architecture is a contrast: instrumentarian power is defined by what it is NOT, namely **totalitarianism**. Zuboff devotes Section II to a substantial intellectual history of totalitarianism (Gentile, Mussolini, Stalin, Hitler; the failure of contemporaries — Duranty, *Look*, *Time* — to comprehend it; and the postwar naming work of Friedrich, Arendt, Conquest, Lefort) precisely in order to model a method: an unprecedented power "literally defied human comprehension," and our task now is the same work of "naming a strange form of power unprecedented in the human experience" so that resistance becomes possible. The chief polemical target is the reflexive use of **Orwell's Big Brother** and "digital totalitarianism" as the lens for today's threats — Zuboff argues this is "the syndrome of the horseless carriage," attaching new peril to old facts, and that the equation "impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist."

The "opposite horizon" (Section III) is the crux: totalitarianism worked through violence and the engineering of the soul "from the inside out"; instrumentarian power works through the **means of behavioral modification** and is "profoundly and infinitely indifferent to our meanings and motives" — it has no interest in our souls. This is Zuboff's signature concept of **radical indifference**, here developed at length though not named with that phrase in this chapter. Sections IV–VI then supply instrumentarianism's intellectual genesis in **B. F. Skinner's radical behaviorism** and its antecedents (Max Planck → Max Meyer's 1921 *Psychology of the Other-One* → Skinner). The "Other-One" viewpoint recasts the human as "an organism among organisms," makes **freedom a synonym for ignorance** ("freedom is ignorance"), and calls for a "technology of behavior." Section VII stages **two utopias** — Skinner's *Walden Two* (1948) and Orwell's *1984* (1949) — as the figureheads of the two species of power, and closes with the chapter's haunting thesis: surveillance capitalism may finally realize Skinner's dream, but with a fatal twist Skinner never foresaw — "The knowledge that now displaces our freedom is proprietary."

For the vault, this chapter is the primary anchor for the new concepts **Instrumentarian Power**, **Two Species of Power**, **Radical Indifference**, and **The Means of Behavioral Modification**, and it richly EXTENDS the existing surveillance/data/determinism cluster (Digital Panopticon, Society of Control, Free Will vs Determinism, Algorithmic Determinism, Friendly Big Brother). It realizes the committed forward-stub **Surveillance**. It is deeply resonant with Han's digital panopticon/psychopolitics, Harari's Dataism (organisms-are-algorithms), and Kurzweil's embraced ubiquitous computing.

## Key Entities

- **Shoshana Zuboff** — author; recounts personal graduate-school debates with Skinner and Richard Herrnstein at Harvard.
- **B. F. (Burrhus Frederic) Skinner** — radical behaviorist psychologist (Harvard); author of *The Behavior of Organisms* (1938), *Walden Two* (1948), *Science and Human Behavior* (1951), *Beyond Freedom & Dignity* (1971), *About Behaviorism* (1974); central interlocutor — the intellectual father of instrumentarianism's "technology of behavior."
- **Hannah Arendt** — author of *The Origins of Totalitarianism* (1951); the chapter's model of comprehension as the necessary response to radical evil.
- **George Orwell** — *1984* (1949); Big Brother as the figurehead of totalitarian power and Zuboff's foil for instrumentarianism.
- **Max Meyer** — obscure German-trained psychologist (University of Missouri); author of *Psychology of the Other-One* (1921); the bridge from Planck's physics to Skinner's behaviorism.
- **Max Planck** — physicist; Meyer's dissertation advisor; source of the doctrine of an absolute, law-governed external world discoverable by mathematics, including "the laws of human behavior."
- **John B. Watson** — founder of behaviorism (1913 manifesto); Skinner blames him for behaviorism's bad reputation ("showman as scientist").
- **Richard Herrnstein** — Skinner's student/colleague at Harvard; gave Zuboff the phrase "the vortex of stimuli."
- **Émile Durkheim, Max Weber** — named as the great interpreters of industrial society who failed to anticipate totalitarianism.
- **Giovanni Gentile, Benito Mussolini** — coined/popularized "totalitarianism"; *The Doctrine of Fascism* (1932).
- **Joseph Stalin** — "engineers of the soul" toast (production of souls > production of tanks).
- **Carl Friedrich, Robert Conquest, Claude Lefort, Richard Shorten** — scholars of totalitarianism cited on its "improbability," "egocrat," and "the experiment in reshaping humanity."
- **Walter Duranty / *Look* magazine / *Time* magazine** — the cultural icons of comprehension-failure (Stalin lionized as participatory manager / "Man of the Year").
- **Lewis Mumford** — historian/critic who called *Walden Two* a "totalitarian utopia"; Zuboff uses him as an example of the dangerous confusion of the two species.
- **Stuart MacKay** — telemetry of elk herds; the technical origin of the apparatus (traded animals' freedom for knowledge that benefits the animals — contrasted with surveillance capitalism, where the lost freedom is ours and the knowledge is theirs).
- Institutions: Harvard Psychology Department, William James Hall, Memorial Hall.

## Definitions

- **Instrumentarianism (Zuboff)** — "the instrumentation and instrumentalization of behavior for the purposes of modification, prediction, monetization, and control." With the two-part gloss: "instrumentation" = the puppet (the connected material architecture of sensate computation that renders, interprets, and actuates human experience); "instrumentalization" = the social relations orienting the puppet masters to human experience.
  - Recommended: `definitions/Instrumentarian Power (Zuboff)`

- **Totalitarianism (per Zuboff's synthesis)** — a political project bent on "the reconstruction of the human species through the dual mechanisms of genocide and the 'engineering of the soul'"; it demands possession of each person "from the inside out"; "Total loyalty—the psychological basis for domination—can be expected only from the completely isolated human being." Defined as the FOIL, not a vault concept of its own.
  - Recommended: render inside `Two Species of Power` / the tension; possibly a definition `Totalitarianism (Zuboff via Arendt)` if needed for the contrast.

- **The Other-One (Meyer/Skinner)** — the "viewpoint of observation" that treats the human as an external other, an "organism among organisms," excluding inner experience from science: "consider only those facts which can be objectively observed in the behavior of one person in its relation to his prior environmental history."
  - Recommended: `definitions/The Other-One (Meyer via Zuboff)` or fold into the concept.

- **Operant behavior (Skinner)** — "active, observable 'doing'"; "Behavior is what an organism is doing—or more accurately what it is observed by another organism to be doing." (Supports `Radical Behaviorism`.)

## Arguments

- **Claim: Surveillance capitalism's power is a NEW species — instrumentarianism — not totalitarianism, and conflating the two cripples resistance.**
  - Reasoning structure: define-by-contrast; historical analogy (totalitarianism was also unprecedented and eluded comprehension); the "horseless carriage" error model.
  - Evidence: the intellectual history of totalitarianism's naming (Arendt, Friedrich, Conquest, Lefort); the documented failure of contemporaries (Duranty, *Look*, *Time*) to comprehend it.
  - Source passage: > the equation of instrumentarian power with totalitarianism impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist, neutralize, and ultimately vanquish its potency.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Instrumentarian power is a new species of power not totalitarianism`

- **Claim: Instrumentarian power is radically indifferent to the soul; it operates through behavioral modification, not violence or soul-engineering.**
  - Reasoning structure: the "opposite horizon" — totalitarianism penetrates inward "from the inside out"; instrumentarianism is "trained on measurable action" and indifferent to meaning.
  - Evidence: the visceral catalogue of what instrumentarianism does NOT want (souls, grief, terror, the inside) vs what it does (behavioral surplus, measurable action).
  - Source passage: > Surveillance capitalists have no interest in murder or the reformation of our souls.
  - Source passage: > It does not demand possession of each person from the inside out.… It is profoundly and infinitely indifferent to our meanings and motives.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)

  - Evidence: Orwell's O'Brien passage ("we capture his inner mind, we reshape him"); Skinner's Frazier ("All we need is adequate behavioral engineering").

## Metaphors

- **The Puppet and the Puppet Masters** — instrumentation = the puppet (the connected sensate-computation architecture); instrumentalization = the puppet masters (surveillance capital wielding the machines). The defining image of instrumentarianism.
  - > “instrumentation” refers to the puppet: the ubiquitous connected material architecture of sensate computation that renders, interprets, and actuates human experience.
- **The Syndrome of the Horseless Carriage** — attaching new peril to old familiar facts; reaching for "familiar vernaculars of twentieth-century power like lifesaving driftwood." (Also: "lifesaving driftwood.")
- **The Vortex of Stimuli / "God is a VI"** — Herrnstein's phrase + the lab sign making determinism the behaviorist's God.
- **Freedom as an undigested bit of beef (Dickens/Scrooge/Marley)** — Zuboff's Dickens allusion: freedom is "an undigested bit of fear, a crumb of denial that, once metabolized, will dispel the apparition and deliver us to reality."
- **The melting iceberg of ignorance on a warming planet** — Skinner's view that ignorance about behavior is "destined to yield to the mounting heat" of measurement and computation.
- **Winston's soul "white as snow" / "He loved Big Brother"** — the totalitarian endgame (soul successfully engineered) — Orwell's image as the contrast pole.
- **MacKay's herds of elk / telemetry** — the technical origin of the apparatus; the contrast where animals' freedom is traded for knowledge that benefits the animals.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Instrumentarian vs Totalitarian Power** — the chapter's master opposition: behavioral modification vs violence; indifference to the soul vs possession of the soul; market project vs political project; remote/panvasive vs handmade/inside-out. (The canonical tension this chapter realizes.)
  - > Totalitarian power cannot succeed by remote control. Mere conformity is insufficient. Each individual inner life must be claimed and transformed by the perpetual threat of punishment without crime.
- **Freedom vs Knowledge (Freedom vs Determinism)** — behaviorism's adversary thesis; freedom shrinks as measurement grows. Connects to existing `Free Will vs Determinism`.
- **Soul vs Behavior / Inside vs Outside / Lived Experience vs Observable Behavior** — Meyer's "shift from soul to other, inside to outside, lived experience to observable behavior."
  - > Meyer argued that the future of the social sciences and of civilization itself rested on this shift from soul to other, inside to outside, lived experience to observable behavior.
- **Public vs Private (the eroding boundary)** — Skinner: "The line between public and private is not fixed.… The problem of privacy may, therefore, eventually be solved by technical advances." The anticipatory frame for surveillance capitalism's rendition frontier.
- **Certainty vs Trust** — "Machine processes replace human relationships so that certainty can replace trust."
- **Unresolved problem / Zuboff's twist** — Planck, Meyer, and Skinner "each failed to anticipate the actual terms of this surrender": the knowledge is proprietary. The behaviorist dream of collective benefit collides with surveillance capitalism's private appropriation.
