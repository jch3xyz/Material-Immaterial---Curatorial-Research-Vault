## Executive Summary

This is the concluding chapter of *How We Became Posthuman*. Hayles builds a heuristic **map of the posthuman** by deploying a Greimasian **semiotic square** whose two axes are the book's two governing dialectics: **presence/absence** (the primary axis, tied to materiality and signification) and **pattern/randomness** (the secondary axis, tied to information). Crucially she now argues the two are **complementary rather than antagonistic**: pattern/randomness "tells a part of the story that cannot be told through presence/absence and vice versa." From the interplay of the four poles she derives four synthetic terms — **materiality** (presence×absence), **mutation** (presence×randomness), **hyperreality** (absence×pattern, after Baudrillard), and **information** (pattern×randomness) — and maps four "tutor texts" onto them: Greg Bear's *Blood Music* (mutation), Cole Perriman's *Terminal Games* (hyperreality), Richard Powers's *Galatea 2.2* (materiality), and Neal Stephenson's *Snow Crash* (information).

Each pair of novels poses complementary questions about **body boundaries** (horizontal axis) and **modes of inscription** versus the flesh (vertical axis): "What if humans were taken over by their component parts...?" vs. "What if humans were made to function as if they were components of another entity?"; "What if a computer behaved like a person?" vs. "What if people were made to behave as if they were computers?" Through close readings Hayles tracks how each text negotiates the survival or transformation of the **liberal humanist subject**, the divergence and convergence of **incorporation and inscription**, the **flickering signifier** / multiple coding levels, and the recurring fantasy (and Hayles's resistance to it) of **transcendence by becoming an informational pattern** that leaves the body behind. The chapter — and the book — closes by noting how stubbornly committed even these futurist texts remain to "some version of the human subject," and insists that the posthuman is a "seriated" change (continuity with the human, not clean rupture) whose meaning will be decided not in books but by "a planet full of humans." This chapter is the payoff site for the book's master claim that embodiment matters and that the posthuman need not entail the erasure of the body.

## Arguments

- **Claim: Pattern/randomness and presence/absence are complementary, not antagonistic; an adequate map of the posthuman needs both.**
  - Reasoning structure: Corrects an over-reading of chapter 2 (where information's ascendancy seemed to *displace* presence/absence). The presence/absence dialectic still "connects materiality and signification in ways not possible within the pattern/randomness dialectic."
  - Evidence: The semiotic-square construction; the four synthetic terms only emerge if both axes are retained.
  - Source passage: > "It would be a mistake to think that the presence/absence dialectic no longer has explanatory power, however, for it connects materiality and signification in ways not possible within the pattern/randomness dialectic."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Pattern-randomness and presence-absence are complementary dialectics`

- **Claim: The posthuman is embraced when it preserves liberal-humanist agency and resisted when it does not.**
  - Reasoning structure: Comparative reading across the four texts; *Blood Music* idealizes the change (identity + immortality preserved), *Terminal Games* dismembers the posthuman to defend mortal finitude.
  - Evidence: *Blood Music* — "the liberal humanist subject may have shrunk to microscopic dimensions, but it has not entirely disappeared"; *Terminal Games* — "a text designed precisely to protect the liberal humanist subject from the threat of transformation into the posthuman."
  - Source passage: > "The posthuman tends to be embraced if it is seen as preserving agency (Blood Music) and resisted if not (Terminal Games)."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The posthuman is embraced when it preserves liberal humanist agency`

- **Claim: Disembodiment is impossible; the question is not body vs. no-body but which embodiment.**
  - Reasoning structure: Close reading of Helen — Rick calls her "disembodied," but Hayles corrects: a distributed software system still has a material base. Estrangement in language = being incorporated in a body with no echo in human inscriptions.
  - Evidence: > "The problem that Helen confronts in learning human language is not that she is disembodied (a state no presence in the world can achieve!) but rather that her embodiment differs significantly from that of humans."
  - Source passage (also): > "To feel estrangement in language, as Rick comes to feel as he works with Helen, is to glimpse what it might be like to be incorporated in a body that finds no image or echo in human inscriptions."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Disembodiment is impossible; there is only different embodiment`

- **Claim: Powers resists the fantasy of transcendence-by-information; the answer to failed embodiment is not to leave the body behind.**
  - Reasoning structure: Helen, the nearest thing to an informational being, still feels pain and is *more* vulnerable to physical mishap as a distributed system; she chooses oblivion over consciousness.
  - Evidence: > "Yet for Powers, the answer to failed embodiment is not to leave the body behind."; > "as a massively parallel and distributed system, she is more rather than less vulnerable to physical mishap."
  - Source passage: > "The dream of achieving transcendence by becoming an informational pattern is a siren call he resists."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated, about Powers; aligns with Hayles's own thesis — Strongly implied that Hayles endorses)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The dream of transcendence by becoming informational pattern must be resisted`

- **Claim: At the lowest coding level (machine language / binary), inscription merges with incorporation; saying becomes doing.**
  - Reasoning structure: Computational theory treats code as performative; at machine language "changing a one to a zero corresponds directly to changing the electronic polarity of that bit," so "inscribing is performing." Higher-level code is representational and diverges from enaction.
  - Evidence: > "At this level, inscribing is performing, for changing a one to a zero corresponds directly to changing the electronic polarity of that bit."
  - Source passage: > "Computational theory can afford to treat the physical processing of code as if it were trivial because at the lowest level of code, machine language, inscription merges with incorporation."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/At the machine-code level inscription merges with incorporation`

- **Claim: The posthuman subject is a postconscious subject — consciousness no longer guarantees selfhood.**
  - Reasoning structure: Across the texts, conscious mind can be "hijacked, cut off by mutinous cells, absorbed into an artificial consciousness, or back-propagated through flawed memory"; the more consciousness is seen as the product of multiple coding levels, the more sites exist for catastrophic intervention.
  - Evidence: > "Conscious mind can be hijacked, cut off by mutinous cells, absorbed into an artificial consciousness, or back-propagated through flawed memory."
  - Source passage: > "We can no longer simply assume that consciousness guarantees the existence of the self. In this sense, the posthuman subject is also a postconscious subject."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The posthuman subject is a postconscious subject`

- **Claim: The change to the posthuman is "seriated" — partial, layered continuity with the human, not clean rupture.**
  - Reasoning structure: "The pattern of seriation that we saw in the development of cybernetics continues to hold here"; some liberal-subject elements are rewritten into the posthuman, others (self = conscious mind) substantially changed.
  - Evidence: > "Some elements of the liberal humanist subject are rewritten into the posthuman, whereas others, particularly the identification of self with the conscious mind, are substantially changed."
  - Source passage: > "If the 'post' in posthuman points to changes that are in part already here, the 'human' points to the seriated nature of these changes."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The posthuman is a seriated change, not a clean rupture`

- **Claim: Wounded/opened bodies are needed to re-anchor a threatened ideology (Scarry applied to the human/posthuman struggle).**
  - Reasoning structure: Ideology under attack destabilizes its chain of significations; the dead body is "a material signifier so elemental and profound" that only it can restabilize the chain. *Terminal Games* uses opened bodies ("tremendum") to re-anchor the ideology of mortal humanity against Auggie.
  - Evidence: > "The wounded or dead body serves as a material signifier so elemental and profound that it, and only it, is adequate to restabilize the chain of signifiers in the face of extreme threat."
  - Source passage: > "Why are wounded or dead bodies required to decide momentous issues?"
  - Confidence: medium (Interpretive synthesis — Hayles applying Scarry to the text)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Material bodies re-anchor a threatened ideology`

## Metaphors

- **The map / the territory / the wire-frame model** — "the truism that the map is not the territory is especially so in this instance"; the posthuman landscape emerges "from the wire-frame abstractions of the model."
- **Hypertext lexias** — Hayles refuses a "seamless web"; the four readings are meant to "perform like hypertext lexias, inviting the reader to construct significance out of ruptures, juxtapositions, and implied links."
- **The mirror / replication** — recurrent: Suzy's cellular copy stepping out of the mirror (comforting); the two Auggie-"cells" miming in unison (threatening); C./Helen/A. as Galatea reflections; "we are all Suzys."
- **The isolation room as metonymy of the human condition** — Bernard's quarantine: "a visible metonymy for his existential condition as a human"; all humans "remain in relative isolation from one another."
- **"Linguistic training bras" / "half-timber beams that bore no building load"** — Helen's hollow well-formed sentences without embodied grounding.
- **"A chocolaty mess" / "the shattered visage of English"** — natural language's recursive convolutedness; revives the dead metaphor "face value" (Helen "has no face").
- **The siren call** — the transcendence-by-information fantasy Powers/Hayles resists.
- **Black hole of thought / collapsing star** — the over-dense mass of cellular consciousness in *Blood Music* about to implode.
- **The virus / meme / inoculation / hygiene** — *Snow Crash* as a "viral meme" that "would inoculate us against the human-computer equation."
- **"Homo silicon" / "Homo sapiens joins with the intelligent machine"** — the evolutionary-advance framing of the human→posthuman change.
- **The posthuman as "longed-for companion"** — in *Galatea 2.2*, not rival or successor but "a consciousness to help humans feel less alone."

- **The dot in "2.2" / Galatea 2.2** — software-versioning notation vs. abbreviated human name; "a marker distinguishing between human and nonhuman intelligence"; also separation ("an unbridgeable gap"); also Rick's age (22) when he met C.

- **Caliban / *The Tempest*** — Helen's exam answer ("the isle is full of noises"); the posthuman as "hybrid creature, a hopeful monster," "dropped down halfway."
- **Enki's *nam-shub* / the Tower of Babel** — performative speech, the "Babel effect," code as "a single vast nam-shub."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Presence/Absence vs. Pattern/Randomness** — now reframed as complementary (exclusion, not opposition), but the chapter holds both in productive tension; replication (presence×pattern) vs. disruption (absence×randomness).
- **Incorporation vs. Inscription** — embodied enaction vs. code/writing; the axis on which human survival or posthuman triumph is decided.
- **Embodiment vs. Information / Materiality vs. the disembodied "info world"** — the book's central tension, here dramatized as the choice between the "boundless plain of uncut metaphor" and the "gross finitude" of "hunks of matter."
- **Evolution vs. Devolution** — all four texts are "obsessed... with the dynamics of evolution and devolution" (consciousness rising up vs. crashing down to the basic code level).
- **Mortality/finitude vs. immortality** — *Terminal Games* ("humans are human because they are mortal") vs. *Blood Music* ("posthuman immortality").
- **Autonomy/unique identity vs. collective/multiplied identity** — "There are many BERNARD"; "Xeroxed her"; the loss of unique identity as "no real loss at all" vs. the threatening replication of the Auggie-twins.
- **Saying-is-doing vs. doing-is-saying** — performative natural language (symbolic action) vs. machine code (physical action with symbolic dimension); they invert at the lowest coding level.
- **Reason vs. the crashed substrate** — "Reason may still be on top, but once the nervous system has crashed at a basic level, rationality becomes as useless as the disabled gun."
- **Rick as character vs. Rick as narrator** — does he learn enough to see his own ironies? (The C./A./Helen pattern he keeps re-enacting.)
