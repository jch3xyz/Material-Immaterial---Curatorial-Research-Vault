## Executive Summary

This chapter is Han's analysis of *emotion as a productive, exploitable force* — the affective engine of neoliberal psychopolitics. Its argumentative backbone is a triadic semantic distinction that the chapter builds with unusual care: **feeling (Gefühl)** vs. **emotion (Emotion)** vs. **affect (Affekt)** (with a fourth term, **atmosphere/mood (Stimmung)**, added for contrast). Feeling is objective, narratable, durational, constative, non-intentional; emotion is subjective, fleeting, dynamic, performative, intentional, goal-oriented; affect is eruptive, momentary, non-performative, discharge-oriented. The whole conceptual apparatus exists to ground one claim: **emotional capitalism exploits the performativity of emotion** — its capacity to "call forth certain actions" — which feelings, lacking performativity, do not afford.

Han uses the distinction polemically against **Eva Illouz** (*Cold Intimacies: The Making of Emotional Capitalism*), whom he credits with naming the phenomenon but faults for (a) conflating feeling, emotion, and affect, (b) wrongly locating emotional capitalism in Weber's Protestant ethic / ascetic accumulation, and (c) failing to see that emotion acquires value only with the **switch to immaterial production**. The historical claim: ascetic, rational, accumulative capitalism (disciplinary society) treats emotion as a *disturbance* to be weeded out — "Machines function best when all emotions and feelings have been switched off" — whereas neoliberal consumer/immaterial capitalism *deploys emotion as a resource* for productivity, motivation, and limitless consumption ("today we do not consume things so much as emotions").

The chapter ties emotion to the larger *Psychopolitics* thesis at its close: because emotion is steered by the **limbic system** — the "pre-reflexive, half-conscious, physico-instinctual level of action that escapes full awareness" — **neoliberal psychopolitics seizes on emotion to influence action below the threshold of reflexive freedom**, cutting "deep inside" and steering "the integral person, the person as a whole." Emotion is thus the chapter's instance of *smart, friendly power*: it does not repress; it liberates, motivates, and feels like freedom ("being free means giving free rein to emotions"), while exploiting that very subjectivity "mercilessly." Key supporting moves: the shift from **rationality (the medium of disciplinary society) to emotionality** under acceleration and instability ("a dictatorship of emotion"); **Emotional Design** as a consumption technology; and the corporate installation of the **integral person** — "social and emotional skills" (Daimler-Chrysler, Hewlett-Packard via Gorz/Illouz) — into the production process.

## Definitions

Explicit/strongly-clarified definitions in the chapter (all verbatim):

- **Feeling (Gefühl)** — objective, recountable, durational, constative, non-intentional. > "feeling refers to something objective." / "Feeling can be recounted. It has narrative length, or breadth." / "Feelings are constative."
- **Emotion (Emotion)** — subjective, fleeting, performative, intentional, goal-oriented, dynamic, situative. > "Emotions are not constative, but performative. They refer to actions and deeds. Furthermore, they are intentional and goal-oriented." / "Emotions are dynamic, situative and performative."
- **Affect (Affekt)** — eruptive, momentary, non-performative, discharge-directed. > "affects are not performative so much as eruptive; they lack performative directionality." / "affect is often limited to a single moment."
- **Atmosphere / Mood (Stimmung)** — even more objective than feeling; a "way-it-is"; static, constellative, non-intentional, non-performative; a *Befindlichkeit* (state of mind / state of being). > "Atmosphere/mood is neither intentional nor performative. … As such, atmosphere is static and constellative - whereas emotion is dynamic and performative."
- **The where / whither / wherefore schema** — Han's compressed grammar of the three: atmosphere = *where* (a state/disposition); emotion = *whither* (a direction); feeling = *wherefore* / "why". > "Where distinguishes a state, a disposition; in contrast whither - a direction - defines emotion. Feeling, in turn, is a matter of wherefore, 'why'."
- **Anxiety vs. Fear** — anxiety is a *feeling* (non-intentional, no concrete object); fear is intentional. Used to refute Illouz/Weber. > "Often, the feeling of anxiety has no concrete object. That is what makes anxiety different from fear, which has an intentional structure."

Recommended definition note: `definitions/Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect (Han).md` (parallel to `Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)`; possibly include Stimmung as the fourth term).

## Arguments

- **Claim**: Emotional capitalism exploits emotion because emotion (unlike feeling or affect) is *performative* — it is dynamic, situative, and calls forth action; feelings cannot be exploited because they have no performativity.
  - Reasoning structure: Build a semantic taxonomy (feeling/emotion/affect) → isolate the property of performativity → identify that property as the exploitable unit.
  - Evidence: The taxonomy itself; the closing limbic-system claim.
  - Source passage: > "Emotions are dynamic, situative and performative. Emotional capitalism exploits precisely these qualities. Feelings, in contrast, cannot be readily exploited inasmuch as they have no performativity."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Emotional capitalism exploits the performativity of emotion.md`

- **Claim**: The boom in emotion is not a free-standing cultural or psychological fact but derives from neoliberalism / an economic process, which deploys emotion as a resource to raise productivity and achievement.
  - Reasoning structure: Diagnostic genealogy — emotion-researchers mistake a symptom for a cause; the real cause is economic.
  - Evidence: The opening critique of emotion-research; the neoliberal-regime claim.
  - Source passage: > "Clearly, Illouz has failed to notice that the boom of emotion in our times ultimately derives from neoliberalism. The neoliberal regime deploys emotions as resources in order to bring about heightened productivity and achievement."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The emotion boom derives from neoliberalism.md`

- **Claim**: Emotion becomes a means of production / acquires economic value only with the switch to immaterial production — not in Weber's ascetic, rational, accumulative capitalism.
  - Reasoning structure: Historical periodization against Illouz; ascetic capitalism = rational logic, emotion as disturbance; immaterial/consumer capitalism = emotive logic.
  - Evidence: The Weber refutation; the immaterial-production thesis; corporate examples.
  - Source passage: > "Illouz fails to account for the fact that emotion comes to possess value for capitalism only when a switch to immaterial production occurs. Emotions have become a means of production only in our own times."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Emotion becomes a means of production only under immaterial capitalism.md`

- **Claim**: Disciplinary society treats emotion as a disturbance to be eliminated (the body trained into an unfeeling machine); neoliberal/emotional capitalism does the opposite — it activates and exploits emotion. (The disciplinary→psychopolitical pivot, instanced in the register of affect.)
  - Reasoning structure: Binary contrast between two economic-political regimes; "concerted orthopaedy" (Foucauldian) vs. emotional activation.
  - Evidence: The disciplinary-society paragraph.
  - Source passage: > "In disciplinary society, where one's task is to function, emotions represent disturbances. Accordingly, every effort is made to weed them out. … Machines function best when all emotions and feelings have been switched off."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Disciplinary society suppresses emotion neoliberalism exploits it.md`

- **Claim**: Consumer capitalism turns consumption of emotion into a *limitless* field, because emotions — unlike things, bounded by use value — can be consumed without end.
  - Reasoning structure: Use value (finite) vs. emotive/cultic value (infinite); Emotional Design as the technology.
  - Evidence: The consumption paragraph.
  - Source passage: > "All in all, today we do not consume things so much as emotions. The former cannot be consumed without end - but the latter can. … they open up a field of consumption that is new and knows no limit."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Emotions open a limitless field of consumption.md`

- **Claim**: Emotionalization feels like freedom and the unfolding of personality, which is precisely how neoliberal power exploits subjectivity — freedom and exploitation coincide.
  - Reasoning structure: The *Psychopolitics* master-move (freedom-as-crisis) localized in affect: emotion is hailed as unbridled subjectivity, then that subjectivity is exploited "mercilessly."
  - Evidence: The "feeling of liberty" / "banks on freedom" passages.
  - Source passage: > "emotionality takes its place, which is attended by the feeling of liberty - the free unfolding of personality. After all, being free means giving free rein to emotions."
  - Source passage: > "Emotional capitalism banks on freedom. … Neoliberal technologies of power exploit this same subjectivity mercilessly."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Emotional capitalism banks on freedom.md` — strong candidate to MERGE with / link to existing `Exploitation of Freedom`.

- **Claim**: Because emotion is seated in the limbic system (pre-reflexive, half-conscious, the level of the drives), neoliberal psychopolitics steers action *below* the threshold of reflexive awareness, operating "deep inside" the integral person.
  - Reasoning structure: Neuro-anatomical grounding → psychopolitics targets the pre-reflexive → emotion is the "highly efficient medium" of whole-person steering.
  - Evidence: The closing limbic-system paragraph.
  - Source passage: > "Neoliberal psychopolitics seizes on emotion in order to influence actions on this pre-reflexive level. By way of emotion, it manages to cut and operate deep inside. As such, emotion affords a highly efficient medium for psychopolitically steering the integral person, the person as a whole."

  - Reasoning structure: Methodological critique grounded in Han's taxonomy; anxiety/guilt reclassified as feelings; ascetic capitalism reclassified as rational not emotional.
  - Evidence: The two Illouz paragraphs.
  - Source passage: > "It is mistaken to understand anxiety in terms of emotion. Anxiety is a feeling. … But what Weber analyses is the ascetic capitalism of accumulation, which obeys rational logic more than it follows emotional logic."

## Metaphors

- **Machine with emotions switched off** — the disciplinary body as an unfeeling machine; "concerted orthopaedy" kneading "a shapeless mass of dough" into a functioning machine. > "Disciplinary society's 'concerted orthopaedy' seeks to make a shapeless mass of dough into an unfeeling machine. Machines function best when all emotions and feelings have been switched off."
- **Dough / shapeless mass** — the raw human material to be disciplined into a machine (Foucauldian docility figured as kneading dough). > "a shapeless mass of dough."
- **Stream / flow of affect (shitstorm)** — affect as a discharging current; the shitstorm as a "stream of affect." > "Shitstorms are streams of affect."
- **Cutting / operating deep inside** — psychopolitics figured as surgery: emotion lets power "cut and operate deep inside" the person (a quasi-surgical incision into the pre-reflexive). > "By way of emotion, it manages to cut and operate deep inside."
- **Dictatorship of emotion** — political metaphor: acceleration installs emotion as a coercive sovereign over the productive process. > "the pressure of acceleration now is leading to a dictatorship of emotion."
- **Ferment / fermentation** — positive emotions as the "ferment" that makes motivation grow (biological/brewing metaphor for motivational management). > "Positive emotions provide the ferment that makes motivation grow."
- **Raw material** — emotions as exploitable "raw material" for optimizing corporate communication. > "Emotions provide 'raw material' with which to optimize corporate communication."
- **Oceanic feeling / cosmic oneness** — figure for non-intentional feeling ("ein ozeanisches Welt-Gefühl"); used to mark the limit-case of objectless feeling. (Freudian resonance.) > "A feeling of cosmic oneness - an oceanic sense of the world."
- **Manager as motivation coach** — the new managerial type: rational action replaced by affect-management. > "Increasingly, they resemble motivation coaches."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Rationality vs. Emotionality** — the chapter's structuring opposition: rationality (objective, general, steady, slow, the medium of disciplinary society) vs. emotionality (subjective, situative, volatile, fast). > "Rationality is defined by objectivity, generality and steadiness. As such, it stands opposed to emotionality, which is subjective, situative and volatile."
- **Feeling vs. Emotion** — durational/objective/constative/narratable vs. fleeting/subjective/performative; the exploitability gap lives here.
- **Emotion vs. Affect** — performative/directional vs. eruptive/discharging; both subjective but distinct in directionality.
- **Stability vs. Instability** — the neoliberal economy dismantles continuity and integrates instability to raise productivity (instability *as* an emotionalizing technology). > "The neoliberal economy, increasingly dismantling continuity and progressively integrating instability in order to enhance productivity, is pushing the emotionalization of the productive process forward."
- **Disciplinary Society vs. Neoliberal/Emotional Capitalism** — suppression of emotion (body-as-machine) vs. activation/exploitation of emotion (whole-person).
- **Use Value vs. Emotive/Cultic Value** — finite consumption of things vs. limitless consumption of emotions. > "It is not use value but emotive or cultic value that plays a constitutive role in the economy of consumption."
- **Material vs. Immaterial Production** — accumulative material capitalism vs. immaterial/communicative capitalism where emotion becomes a means of production. (Direct link to vault tension `Material vs Immaterial`.)
- **Freedom vs. Exploitation (Freedom vs Control)** — emotion felt as liberty / "free unfolding of personality" is the very surface neoliberal power exploits "mercilessly." (Direct link to vault tension `Freedom vs Control`.)
- **Reflexive Freedom vs. Pre-reflexive Steering** — conscious agency vs. limbic, sub-awareness manipulation; the psychopolitical hook.

Recommend a definition note `Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect (Han)` plus a concept/argument note for "performativity of emotion." Decide whether Stimmung (atmosphere/mood) gets its own note or rides in the definition.
- **`Emotional Capitalism` vs. `Exploitation of Freedom` / `Auto-Exploitation`** — substantial overlap (all are neoliberal self-exploitation). Recommend `create_distinct` for Emotional Capitalism (it is specifically the affective register) but link tightly. See matches_existing.
- **Illouz attribution** — "emotional capitalism" is *Illouz's* coinage (her book's subtitle). Any `Emotional Capitalism` note must credit Illouz for the term and mark Han's reframing (neoliberal/immaterial, not Weberian). Do not present the term as Han's invention.
- **"Concerted orthopaedy" / disciplinary society** — Foucauldian, not original to Han; verify whether other Psychopolitics chapters (esp. 04 Biopolitics, 05 Foucault's Dilemma, 03 The Mole and the Snake) treat disciplinary→control more fully, and route this chapter's disciplinary contrast into that spine rather than duplicating.
- **`Psychopolitics` as canonical note** — this chapter references the book's master-concept but does not define it; ensure the ch. 1–2 ingest creates the canonical `Psychopolitics` note and that this chapter links it rather than stubbing a rival.
- **"Oceanic feeling" attribution** — Freud's term (*Civilization and Its Discontents*, via Romain Rolland). Han uses it without attribution. Flag as `possible_resonance` only; do not assert direct citation.
- **Immaterial-labour lineage** — Gorz is cited; Negri/Lazzarato/Marx stand behind it. Decide how many forward-stubs to commit (recommend `André Gorz` realized by citation; `Karl Marx`, `Antonio Negri` as forward-stubs).

## Candidate Source Citations

> "Affect and emotion refer to strictly subjective matters, whereas feeling refers to something objective."

> "Feeling can be recounted. It has narrative length, or breadth. Neither affect nor emotion admits an account."

> "Digital communication fosters the immediate release of affect: catharsis (Affektabfuhr). Simply on the basis of its temporality, digital communication conveys affect more than it transmits feeling. Shitstorms are streams of affect. They represent exemplary phenomena of digital communication."

> "Emotions are not constative, but performative. They refer to actions and deeds. Furthermore, they are intentional and goal-oriented. Feelings, on the other hand, do not necessarily display an intentional structure."

> "Emotions are dynamic, situative and performative. Emotional capitalism exploits precisely these qualities. Feelings, in contrast, cannot be readily exploited inasmuch as they have no performativity."

> "Clearly, Illouz has failed to notice that the boom of emotion in our times ultimately derives from neoliberalism. The neoliberal regime deploys emotions as resources in order to bring about heightened productivity and achievement."

> "emotionality takes its place, which is attended by the feeling of liberty - the free unfolding of personality. After all, being free means giving free rein to emotions."

> "Emotional capitalism banks on freedom. It hails emotion as the expression of unbridled subjectivity. Neoliberal technologies of power exploit this same subjectivity mercilessly."

> "Thus, the pressure of acceleration now is leading to a dictatorship of emotion."

> "All in all, today we do not consume things so much as emotions. The former cannot be consumed without end - but the latter can. Emotions assume dimensions beyond the scope of use value."

> "Illouz fails to account for the fact that emotion comes to possess value for capitalism only when a switch to immaterial production occurs. Emotions have become a means of production only in our own times."

> "In disciplinary society, where one's task is to function, emotions represent disturbances. Accordingly, every effort is made to weed them out. … Machines function best when all emotions and feelings have been switched off."

> "The boom in emotion today stems, not least of all, from a new, immaterial mode of production in which communicative interaction plays an ever-greater role. It calls not just for cognitive competence, but also for emotional competence. In this context, the integral person is installed in the very process of production."

> "Now, sociality, communication and even individual conduct are being exploited. Emotions provide 'raw material' with which to optimize corporate communication."

> "Emotions are performative in so far as they call forth certain actions. As inclinations, they represent the energetic - the sensory, or even sensuous - basis for action."

> "Emotions are steered by the limbic system, which is also where the drives are seated. They form the pre-reflexive, half-conscious, physico-instinctual level of action that escapes full awareness."

> "Neoliberal psychopolitics seizes on emotion in order to influence actions on this pre-reflexive level. By way of emotion, it manages to cut and operate deep inside. As such, emotion affords a highly efficient medium for psychopolitically steering the integral person, the person as a whole."
