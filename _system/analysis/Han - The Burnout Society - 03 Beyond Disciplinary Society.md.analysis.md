## Executive Summary

This short, dense chapter is the foundational diagnostic move of *The Burnout Society* and the **primary source** for much of the vault's existing Han vocabulary (Achievement Society, Auto-Exploitation, Can vs Should, Positivity/Negativity, the Achievement-Subject). Han announces the historical-epochal claim that defines his project: Foucault's **disciplinary society** of hospitals, prisons, barracks, and factories has been superseded by the **achievement society** [Leistungsgesellschaft] of fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, and shopping malls; its "obedience-subjects" have become **achievement-subjects** who are "entrepreneurs of themselves."

The chapter makes four interlocking arguments. (1) The **modal shift from Should to Can**: disciplinary society is governed by the negativity of *May Not* and *Should* (prohibition, compulsion), while achievement society is governed by the positivity of *Unlimited Can* ("Yes, we can"), because the positivity of Can is *more efficient* at maximizing production than the negativity of Should — so the social unconscious switches from Should to Can without revoking it (continuity in the drive to maximize production). (2) An extended **polemic against Alain Ehrenberg**: Han accepts Ehrenberg's location of depression in the disciplinary→achievement transition but rejects his "economy of the self" diagnosis (depression as failure to become oneself), insisting instead that it is the *pressure/imperative to achieve* — plus impoverished attachment [Bindungsarmut] and the *systemic violence* of achievement society — that causes "exhaustive depression." (3) A correction of Ehrenberg's **misreading of Nietzsche**: the new human type is not Nietzsche's "sovereign man"/superman but **"the last man," who does nothing but work**; the achievement-subject "lacks all sovereignty." (4) The theory of **auto-exploitation**: the achievement-subject, "lord and master of itself," subject to no external instance of domination, gives itself over to *compulsive freedom* — "the free constraint of maximizing achievement" — and so auto-exploitation is "more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it." The exploiter is the exploited; perpetrator and victim collapse; the depressive is "predator and prey at once," an **animal laborans that exploits itself.** Depression is figured as **excessive positivity** — a "neuronal"/internalized war, a self at war with itself — which "eludes all immunological schemes."

This chapter overwhelmingly *extends and corroborates* existing canonical notes rather than minting wholly new vocabulary — Han's own *Psychopolitics* already imported much of this apparatus secondhand; here is the **primary text**.

## Definitions

- **Achievement society** [Leistungsgesellschaft] — defined ostensively and by contrast: a "society of fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories" whose inhabitants are "achievement-subjects … entrepreneurs of themselves," governed by Unlimited Can rather than prohibition.
  - Source passage: > It has long been replaced by another regime, namely a society of fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories.
- **Auto-exploitation** — self-exploitation that is voluntary and unconstrained, in which "the exploiter is simultaneously the exploited" and which is "more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it."
  - Source passage: > Excess work and performance escalate into auto-exploitation. This is more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it.
- **Depression (Han, contra Ehrenberg)** — not the failure to become oneself (Ehrenberg's economy of the self) but "exhaustive depression" caused by the imperative to achieve, by impoverished attachment, and by systemic violence; "creative fatigue and exhausted ability"; the sickness of excessive positivity.
  - Source passage: > It is not the imperative only to belong to oneself, but the pressure to achieve that causes exhaustive depression. Seen in this light, burnout syndrome does not express the exhausted self so much as the exhausted, burnt-out soul.
  - Recommended: a `definitions/Depression (Han)` note is a reasonable candidate (parallels the existing `(Han)` definition notes), distinguishing Han's diagnosis from Ehrenberg's. See Human Review.

## Arguments

- **Claim: The disciplinary society has been superseded by the achievement society.**
  - Reasoning structure: epochal/periodizing claim grounded in a shift of institutional topology (prisons/factories → fitness studios/office towers) and subject-type (obedience-subject → achievement-subject); Foucault's power analysis is declared inadequate to the new psychic/topological changes, and Deleuze's "control society" is rejected as "still … too much negativity."
  - Evidence: the institutional catalogues; the "entrepreneurs of themselves" formula.
  - Source passage: > Foucault's analysis of power cannot account for the psychic and topological changes that occurred as disciplinary society transformed into achievement society. Nor does the commonly employed concept of "control society" do justice to this change. It still contains too much negativity.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The achievement society supersedes the disciplinary society` (NEW — the foundational periodizing argument of Han's corpus; closely related to the existing `Foucault failed to make the turn to psychopolitics` but distinct — that one is about *method*, this is about *epoch*). See Human Review for naming.

- **Claim: The social unconscious switches from Should to Can because the positivity of Can is more efficient than the negativity of Should — but continuity in maximizing production prevails (Can does not revoke the Should).**
  - Reasoning structure: functionalist/productivist argument — disciplinary technology hits a productivity ceiling where "the negativity of prohibition impedes further expansion," so the positive scheme of Can supplants it to heighten output; the achievement-subject is "faster and more productive than the obedience-subject," yet remains disciplined ("completed the disciplinary stage").
  - Evidence: the modal-verb taxonomy (May Not/Should vs Can); the productivity-ceiling claim.
  - Source passage: > Beyond a certain point of productivity, disciplinary technology—or, alternately, the negative scheme of prohibition—hits a limit. To heighten productivity, the paradigm of disciplination is replaced by the paradigm of achievement, or, in other words, by the positive scheme of Can …
  - Source passage: > However, the Can does not revoke the Should. The obedience-subject remains disciplined. It has now completed the disciplinary stage.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: corroborates `Can vs Should` and the existing argument `The freedom of Can is more coercive than the disciplinary Should` (a deferred Psychopolitics stub) — strong candidate to **realize** that stub from this primary text. See Human Review.

- **Claim: Ehrenberg misdiagnoses depression by reading it only through the economy of the self; the real cause is the imperative to achieve (plus impoverished attachment and systemic violence).**
  - Reasoning structure: immanent critique — Han accepts Ehrenberg's *placement* of depression in the disciplinary→achievement transition but rejects his *explanation*; lists what Ehrenberg "overlooks" (Bindungsarmut, systemic violence/"psychic infarctions"); reframes burnout as the "exhausted, burnt-out soul," not the "exhausted self."
  - Evidence: two verbatim Ehrenberg citations and a point-by-point rebuttal.
  - Source passage: > Problematically, however, Ehrenberg considers depression only from the perspective of the economy of the self: the social imperative only to belong to oneself makes one depressive.
  - Source passage: > He also overlooks the systemic violence inhabiting achievement society, which provokes psychic infarctions. It is not the imperative only to belong to oneself, but the pressure to achieve that causes exhaustive depression.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Depression is caused by the imperative to achieve not the failure to become oneself` (NEW — the central polemic of the chapter; the Ehrenberg disagreement). See Human Review for naming.

- **Claim: The new human type is not Nietzsche's sovereign man but the last man who does nothing but work; the achievement-subject lacks all sovereignty.**
  - Reasoning structure: corrective re-reading of Nietzsche against Ehrenberg's appropriation; the achievement-subject is "exposed to excessive positivity without any defense" and so is the antithesis of sovereignty.
  - Evidence: Ehrenberg's "sovereign man" citation; Nietzsche's last-man figure.
  - Source passage: > In fact, Nietzsche would say that that human type in the process of becoming reality en masse is no sovereign superman but "the last man," who does nothing but work. The new human type, standing exposed to excessive positivity without any defense, lacks all sovereignty.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: captured inside the Ehrenberg-polemic argument and a `Byung-Chul Han and Friedrich Nietzsche` reference section; the sovereign-man/last-man contrast is the hinge. (Han↔Nietzsche reference note exists from Psychopolitics.)

- **Claim: Auto-exploitation is more efficient than allo-exploitation because a feeling of freedom attends it; perpetrator and victim collapse into one.**
  - Reasoning structure: the disappearance of an external "instance of domination" [Herrschaftsinstanz] does not free the subject but makes freedom and constraint coincide; "compulsive freedom" / "the free constraint of maximizing achievement" produces a self-referential violence; the depressive is "predator and prey at once," "wounded by internalized war."
  - Evidence: the lord-and-master/subject-to-itself formula; the predator-prey and internalized-war figures.
  - Source passage: > This is more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it. The exploiter is simultaneously the exploited. Perpetrator and victim can no longer be distinguished.
  - Source passage: > No-longer-being-able-to-be-able leads to destructive self-reproach and auto-aggression. The achievement-subject finds itself fighting with itself. The depressive has been wounded by internalized war.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: corroborates `Auto-Exploitation`, `Exploitation of Freedom`, and the argument `Freedom itself is producing coercion`; update all with this primary-source passage. The perpetrator=victim collapse is the canonical formulation and originates here.

- **Claim: Depression is the sickness of excessive positivity and eludes all immunological schemes.**
  - Reasoning structure: positions the achievement-society pathology against the immunological/viral paradigm of the prior century (the "self … still represents an immunological category" but depression escapes it); the violence is internalized rather than coming from an external negative Other.
  - Evidence: the immunological-category claim; "a humanity waging war on itself."
  - Source passage: > Depression is the sickness of a society that suffers from excessive positivity. It reflects a humanity waging war on itself.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Depression is the sickness of excessive positivity` (NEW) OR fold into the `Positivity` primary-source section. The immunological framing links to the existing `Immunological Age` concept (Psychopolitics). See Human Review.

## Candidate Source Citations

> Twenty-first-century society is no longer a disciplinary society, but rather an achievement society [Leistungsgesellschaft ]. Also, its inhabitants are no longer "obedience-subjects" but "achievement-subjects." They are entrepreneurs of themselves.

> Foucault's analysis of power cannot account for the psychic and topological changes that occurred as disciplinary society transformed into achievement society. Nor does the commonly employed concept of "control society" do justice to this change. It still contains too much negativity.

> Unlimited Can is the positive modal verb of achievement society. Its plural form—the affirmation, "Yes, we can"—epitomizes achievement society's positive orientation.

> Disciplinary society is still governed by no. Its negativity produces madmen and criminals. In contrast, achievement society creates depressives and losers.

> The positivity of Can is much more efficient than the negativity of Should. Therefore, the social unconscious switches from Should to Can. The achievement-subject is faster and more productive than the obedience-subject.

> However, the Can does not revoke the Should. The obedience-subject remains disciplined. It has now completed the disciplinary stage.

> Problematically, however, Ehrenberg considers depression only from the perspective of the economy of the self: the social imperative only to belong to oneself makes one depressive.

> It is not the imperative only to belong to oneself, but the pressure to achieve that causes exhaustive depression. Seen in this light, burnout syndrome does not express the exhausted self so much as the exhausted, burnt-out soul.

> In fact, Nietzsche would say that that human type in the process of becoming reality en masse is no sovereign superman but "the last man," who does nothing but work. The new human type, standing exposed to excessive positivity without any defense, lacks all sovereignty.

> The depressive human being is an animal laborans that exploits itself—and it does so voluntarily, without external constraints. It is predator and prey at once.

> It erupts at the moment when the achievement-subject is no longer able to be able [nicht mehr können kann]. First and foremost, depression is creative fatigue and exhausted ability [Schaffens- und Könnensmüdigkeit].

> The complaint of the depressive individual, "Nothing is possible," can only occur in a society that thinks, "Nothing is impossible."

> Depression is the sickness of a society that suffers from excessive positivity. It reflects a humanity waging war on itself.

> The self, in the strong sense of the word, still represents an immunological category. However, depression eludes all immunological schemes.

> However, the disappearance of domination does not entail freedom. Instead, it makes freedom and constraint coincide. Thus, the achievement-subject gives itself over to compulsive freedom—that is, to the free constraint of maximizing achievement.

> Excess work and performance escalate into auto-exploitation. This is more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it. The exploiter is simultaneously the exploited. Perpetrator and victim can no longer be distinguished.
