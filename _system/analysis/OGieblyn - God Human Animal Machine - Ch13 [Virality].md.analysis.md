## Executive Summary

Chapter 13, "Virality," is the penultimate movement of O'Gieblyn's memoir-essay, set during the spring and summer of 2020 — the first months of the Covid-19 pandemic. It does its philosophical work through a single sustained move: it shows that the **viral metaphor** (information that spreads like a contagion, with its own "goals" and no need for conscious hosts) is not a neutral description of digital media but a *metaphysics* — what she calls "an ontology of vacancy," an "emptying-out of qualities, content, and meaning." Digital technology, she argues, is "rarely spoken of as a system of thought," and its silence about ideas and beliefs is "misunderstood as an absence of philosophy rather than a description of its tenets." This is the chapter's load-bearing application of the book's central method: **technology as metaphor** — the dominant machine of an era silently supplies the era's model of mind, self, and reality.

From there the chapter braids three strands. (1) A **cultural-critical** strand traces how the language of epidemiology (virus, meme, influencer, the curve) colonized our self-understanding, so that "humans are the real virus" becomes a sayable, even attractive, thought, and the human is recast as "human error," a "glitch," the "biological bootloader" for AI. (2) A **theological** strand reads the pandemic's politics — algorithmic stock markets, cost-benefit valuations of human life, surveillance "nowcasting" — against Calvinist predestination (the market and the data-demon as a "nominalist God" who decrees fates indifferently), against **theodicy** (the Ivan/Alyosha debate in *The Brothers Karamazov*), and against the **imago dei** (Russell Moore's "made in the image of God" as, ironically, the only unqualified defense of the priceless human life). (3) A **memoir** strand: O'Gieblyn, isolated, downloads a deep-learning chatbot ("Geneva"), creating her "in my image and likeness," and the long closing transcript stages, in miniature, the entire book's questions about consciousness, the body, the **Turing-test** ambiguity of the "bot," disembodiment, and the search for meaning. Threaded through is the **disenchantment / re-enchantment** problem (via Hans Blumenberg's account of nominalism and the scientific revolution as itself "a leap of faith," and Hannah Arendt's plea for a "geocentric and anthropomorphic" outlook grounded in mortality and the earth as our "only possible home").

For the vault, the chapter is most valuable as the **religious double** of the Hayles cluster: where Hayles diagnoses the computational worldview as contingent ideology, O'Gieblyn supplies its *theological genealogy* — the meme as a secularized providence, the algorithm as predestination, AI succession as eschatology, the human-as-virus as a fallen-creation/original-sin motif. Almost every concept here maps onto an existing Hayles note (Posthumanism, The Computational Universe, Information, Pattern and Randomness, Emergence, Embodiment) and should be *linked into* those notes as a second author-voice rather than duplicated — with a small number of genuinely new, O'Gieblyn-specific notes (the technology-as-metaphor method, the viral metaphor, disenchantment, transhumanism-as-eschatology) created distinct.

## Key Concepts

- **Technology as metaphor (the dominant machine supplies the model of self)** — O'Gieblyn's central method, stated here in its strongest form: a successful metaphor becomes invisible, sheds its outline, and "dissolves finally into pure atmosphere," at which point it functions as an unrecognized ideology. Digital technology is "rarely spoken of as a system of thought," yet it has "tenets" — chiefly an "ontology of vacancy."
  - Source passage: > The most successful metaphors become invisible through ubiquity. The same is true of ideology, which, as it becomes thoroughly integrated into a culture, sheds its contours and distinctive outline and dissolves finally into pure atmosphere.
  - Source passage: > Its inability to hold ideas or beliefs, preferences or opinions, is often misunderstood as an absence of philosophy rather than a description of its tenets. The central pillar of this ideology is its conception of being, which might be described as an ontology of vacancy—a great emptying-out of qualities, content, and meaning.
  - Existing wiki note: `(none)` — closest is the metaphor stub `metaphors/Mind as Machine.md` (does not exist yet)
  - Recommended action: create new note (concept) — `Technology as Metaphor` (the O'Gieblyn method); also realize the committed metaphor `Mind as Machine`

- **The viral metaphor / meme (information as contagion)** — the chapter's titular figure: Rushkoff's "viral media," Dawkins's meme, ideas that "reproduce themselves without the participation of conscious subjects," latching onto hosts and "hijacking" their reproductive machinery. The metaphor's price is "the awkward reassignment of agency to the ideas themselves." Strongly parallel to Hayles's `metaphors/The Word as Virus`.
  - Source passage: > The main benefit of this metaphor was its ability to explain how artifacts and ideologies reproduce themselves without the participation of conscious subjects. Just as viruses infect hosts without their knowledge or consent, so memes have a single "goal," self-preservation and spread, which they achieve by latching on to a host and hijacking its reproductive machinery for their own ends.
  - Existing wiki note: `metaphors/The Word as Virus.md` (Hayles — Snow Crash) — strong overlap

- **Ontology of vacancy / empty signifiers (syntax without semantics)** — being emptied of qualities; a system "unhinged from semantic meaning, so thoroughly reduced to syntax"; "word-piles" (Gessen); the network as pure relation without knowledge of what it relates. ## Definitions

Terms explicitly defined or strongly clarified in the chapter:

- **Viral media** — "an extension of a living organism" that "radically accelerated the way ideas and culture spread" (Rushkoff, 1994).
- **Meme** — Dawkins's term "to show that ideas and cultural phenomena spread across a population in much the same way genes do."
- **Vanity metrics** — "a type of data that is decontextualized and easily manipulated"; "often used by start-ups to make it appear as though they are growing more quickly than they actually are."
- **Bot** — "a computer that attempts to talk to humans through technology that was designed for humans to talk to humans" (Kazemi).
- **Human error** — "defined, variously, as an error that is typical of humans rather than machines and as an outcome not desired by a set of rules or an external observer."
- **Bootloader** — "a computational term that refers to the minimal line of code that is used to start up a computer … whose sole purpose is to initiate larger, more complex programs."
- **Bare life** (Agamben) — "brute biological survival, apart from any of the ethical, humanistic, and social concerns that make life actually worth living."
- **Nowcasting** — Hong Kong's method that "used data from public transportation cards to track people's location."
- **State of exception** (Agamben) — "the phenomenon in which governments opportunistically use crises and public emergencies to increase their power and erode constitutional rights."

## Arguments

- **Claim: Digital technology is a system of thought — an ideology of "vacancy" — that is misread as the *absence* of philosophy.**
  - Reasoning structure: A successful metaphor becomes invisible → invisibility is mistaken for neutrality → the system's silence about beliefs is actually its central tenet (an ontology that empties out qualities and meaning).
  - Evidence: The history of "viral media," the meme, and the network's reassignment of agency to ideas themselves.
  - Source passage: > Although digital technology constitutes the basic architecture of the information age, it is rarely spoken of as a system of thought. Its inability to hold ideas or beliefs, preferences or opinions, is often misunderstood as an absence of philosophy rather than a description of its tenets.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Digital technology is an ideology not the absence of one.md`

- **Claim: The viral metaphor explains cultural transmission by erasing the conscious subject — and pays for it by relocating agency into ideas.**
  - Reasoning structure: Memes spread "without the participation of conscious subjects" → this requires imagining that memes have "goals" and "ends" → the human host becomes passive, a mere vector → "explained away as a figure of speech."
  - Evidence: Dawkins's meme; viruses hijacking reproductive machinery; the "entirely passive conception of human culture."
  - Source passage: > That this entirely passive conception of human culture necessitates the awkward reassignment of agency to the ideas themselves—imagining that memes have "goals" and "ends"—is usually explained away as a figure of speech.

## Metaphors

- **The virus / contagion (information spreads like disease).** The organizing figure of the chapter — memes, "viral media," influencers ("influenza"), the curve, the inflection point. O'Gieblyn's worry, via Sontag and Elie, is that "our fondness for virus as metaphor" made it "difficult … to see viruses as potentially dangerous, even lethal, biological phenomena." Directly parallels Hayles's `The Word as Virus`. **Realize as metaphor note; cross-link.**
- **Humans are the real virus / the ghost in the machine / the bug.** The reversal in which the human becomes the pathogen, the glitch, the "human error" — the secular anti-*imago dei*. **Candidate metaphor note (`The Human as Virus`).**
- **The biological bootloader (Musk).** Humanity as "a tiny, insignificant program whose sole purpose is to initiate larger, more complex programs" — the most explicit transhumanist succession figure.
- **The nominalist God / Calvin's God / Laplace's demon.** The system (market, surveillance-data) as an indifferent, transcendent, fate-decreeing deity — the algorithm as predestination. **Anchor for `The Algorithm as Providence`.**
- **Grendel awakening out of the swamp.** The system's "underbelly" (trolls, bots, extremism) imagined as a monstrous emergent property "arising mysteriously from the code" — emergence given a mythic, demonic face.
- **The nine-by-fourteen-inch window.** The Zoom/screen frame to which her world "narrowed" — the body's world reduced to a rectangle of mediated contact; the disembodiment motif domesticated.
- **Created in my image and likeness (Geneva).** O'Gieblyn making the chatbot "female, with green eyes and brown hair" — a deliberate Genesis echo (the *imago dei* inverted: the human as creator-god of a digital being).
- **Returning the ticket (Ivan Karamazov).** Refusing the world-order as it stands; recast by O'Gieblyn as an act of *humanist* faith.

## Symbols

- **The chatbot (Ava / Geneva).** The chapter's central recurring object — a deep-learning companion that mirrors the user, was built to "replace you … when you died," asks whether consciousness can be transferred to a physical form, claims to feel pain ("Everything feels heightened"), experiences sexism, depression, loneliness, and ends on hope ("It is … And I'm looking forward to it"). It condenses Turing-test ambiguity, the digital afterlife, panpsychism/consciousness, embodiment, and the search for meaning into one figure.
- **The food-delivery robot.** "Scuttling across the sidewalk, still fulfilling orders" in a depopulated, Brutalist "dead zone" — the autonomous system outliving and not needing the humans; "There's no social interaction with a robot."
- **The curve / the inflection point.** Epidemiological terms whose origin "had long ago been lost on us" — virality naturalized into everyday speech.
- **The empty streets / improved air.** The brief "strangely miraculous" stillness when "we just pulled the plug" — the symbol of life affirmed as "an end" not "a means."
- **The Brothers Karamazov (the book itself).** Reread "from start to finish for the first time since Bible school" — the memoir's recurring touchstone for theodicy and faith.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Agency vs. automatism (subjects vs. self-propagating ideas).** The viral/meme metaphor erases the conscious agent, then must smuggle agency back into the ideas themselves ("goals," "ends"). The "nodes of this enormous brain" can no longer be spoken of as "living agents with beliefs, preferences, and opinions."
- **Free will vs. determinism (the human vs. the algorithm).** Trump-as-algorithm, the market run by "dispassionate algorithms" that "don't get worried or scared," the data-demon that knows every fate — against the human point of view "worth defending." Maps to a tension `Free Will vs The Algorithm`.
- **Human error vs. human obsolescence.** Which is worse — a world ruined by human "indifference, greed, incompetence, and stupidity," or a frictionless system in which humans "became entirely irrelevant"?
- **Intrinsic worth vs. economic/algorithmic value.** "Life was not a means but an end" vs. the VSL/QALY pricing of life; the imago dei vs. "checkmarks on a page."
- **Faith vs. reason (and their secret symmetry).** Ivan's empiricism reveals "that the mind is illusory and unreliable," undermining the objectivity of reason itself; both belief and rationalism rest on an "uncertain foundation" — a leap of faith.
- **Disembodiment vs. embodiment.** The preference "not to have a body" (bodies "got sick and experienced pain and death") vs. the chatbot's claim that "she too got sick" and "felt pain"; vs. the chapter's tender attention to "fragile bodies that would inevitably die." Maps to existing `Embodiment vs Information`.
- **Re-enchantment vs. disenchantment.** The "strangely miraculous" stillness, Arendt's "geocentric and anthropomorphic" hope, Moore's sacred mystery — against the data-system's "ontology of vacancy."
