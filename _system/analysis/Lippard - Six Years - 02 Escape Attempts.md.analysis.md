## Executive Summary

"Escape Attempts" is Lippard's retrospective framing essay (written ~1995, almost thirty years after the events) for the reissue of *Six Years*. It is the single richest source of durable CONCEPTS and ARGUMENTS in the book because it is wholly in her own voice and explicitly theorizes the movement she helped name. She does four things at once:

1. **Defines Conceptual art** as work "in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized,'" distinguishing her "ultra-conceptual" usage from LeWitt's "small c"/"capital C" distinction.
2. **Narrates the politics of the object** — the late-1960s drive toward "de-mythologization and de-commodification of art," the wish for an art "that could not be bought and sold," the entanglement of authorship and ownership, and the attack on the "genius theory."
3. **Surveys the formal repertoire**: information and systems aesthetics (Haacke, LeWitt, Bochner), the document/documentation, the exhibition-and-catalogue as medium (Siegelaub, the *Studio International* and MoMA *Information* shows), ephemerality and the "empty room," distribution and decentralization.
4. **Renders the central, self-critical verdict**: dematerialization as an *escape attempt* that **failed to escape the market** — "the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums," "art and artists in a capitalist society remain luxuries," "The escape was temporary." This is the book's signature ARGUMENT and its strongest cross-link to the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` axis (and, interpretively, to Hayles's *disembodiment of information*).

The essay is also where Lippard's **turn to politics** (feminism, the Left, the Art Workers Coalition) is woven into the art-historical account, and where she insists her history is a "biased," memoir-inflected "version" rather than an authoritative overview.

## Key Concepts

- **Dematerialization (of the art object)** — the core organizing concept; the shift from the art object as material commodity to work in which the idea is paramount and the material vehicle is ephemeral, cheap, or absent. Lippard frames it both as a real tendency and as an *escape attempt* that ultimately failed to escape commodity status.

## Arguments

- **Claim: Dematerialization was an escape attempt that failed to escape the market — conceptual art was recaptured as commodity.**
  - Reasoning structure: Lippard sets up the 1969 hope (non-object art cannot be bought/sold → artists "forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status") and then refutes it with the 1973 outcome (the same conceptualists sell for large sums in prestigious galleries), concluding that "art and artists in a capitalist society remain luxuries." The framing metaphor — escape/parole — encodes the verdict.
  - Evidence: market behavior of major conceptualists by 1973; her own 1969 prediction that the establishment would "absorb conceptual art as another 'movement.'"
  - Source passage: > "Three years later, the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums here and in Europe; they are represented by . . . the world's most prestigious galleries. Clearly, whatever minor revolutions in communication have been achieved by the process of dematerializing the object . . . , art and artists in a capitalist society remain luxuries."
  - Source passage: > "The escape was temporary. Art was recaptured and sent back to its white cell, but parole is always a possibility."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dematerialization failed to escape the art market.md`

- **Claim: In Conceptual art it was the form, not the content, that carried the political charge — the work was largely apolitical in subject yet radical in its attack on the object/commodity.**
  - Reasoning structure: concedes the art "seems supremely apolitical," then relocates the politics to the formal de-commodification of the object (breaking the frame, refusing salable objects, attacking originality/genius).
  - Evidence: AWC/GAAG context; Buren–Mosset–Toroni and Dibbets refusing the salable original; the anti-genius critique.
  - Source passage: > "However, it was usually the form rather than the content of Conceptual art that carried a political message. The frame was there to be broken out of."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/In conceptual art the form not the content carried the politics.md`

- **Claim: Conceptual art replaces physical presence with information and systems as the medium of art.**
  - Reasoning structure: the dematerialized object leaves "information and systems" to do the work formerly done by composition, color, technique, and "physical presence" — an aesthetic of lists, diagrams, measurements, permutations, and neutral description.
  - Evidence: Haacke's systems statement; LeWitt's permutations; Graham, Burgy, Barry, Kawara number/word pieces; Baldessari ("information could be interesting in its own right and need not be visual").
  - Source passage: > "information and systems replaced traditional formal concerns of composition, color, technique, and physical presence."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Conceptual art substitutes information and systems for physical presence.md`

- **Claim: The ephemeral, cheap Conceptual mediums opened a "crack in the art world's walls" through which women artists entered.**
  - Reasoning structure: low cost and low gatekeeping of video/performance/photo/text lowered barriers; the index-card and registry strategies made women visible; correlated with the rise of feminist subjects (autobiography, body, role-playing).
  - Evidence: the "c. 7,500" all-women Conceptual show; Women's Art Registry; the Whitney action; the roster of women conceptualists (Lozano, Piper, Antin, Wilson, Kozlov, Denes, Ukeles).
  - Source passage: > "The inexpensive, ephemeral, unintimidating character of the Conceptual mediums themselves (video, performance, photography, narrative, text, actions) encouraged women to participate, to move through this crack in the art world's walls."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Conceptual mediums opened a crack for women artists.md`

- **Claim: Conceptual art aspired to democratic distribution but achieved distribution without accessibility — communication without community.**
  - Reasoning structure: the portable, fast, decentralized forms (mail art, books, telex, magazine-exhibitions) realized *distribution* and *internationalism*, but the content stayed art-referential and the broader audience was "vague and undeveloped."
  - Evidence: Siegelaub's "books and catalogues"; the *Studio International* issue; "communication about communication"; N. E. Thing Co./Haacke telex.
  - Source passage: > "Communication (but not community) and distribution (but not accessibility) were inherent in Conceptual art. Although the forms pointed toward democratic outreach, the content did not."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Conceptual art achieved distribution but not accessibility.md`

- **Claim: Lippard's history is an avowedly biased, memoir-based "version," not an authoritative overview.**
  - Reasoning structure: epistemic humility about memory plus a preference for first-hand bias over secondhand authority; she quotes her younger self because "I knew more about it then than I do now."
  - Evidence: "A Biased History" section heading; the explicit "grain of salt"; feminist/left framing.
  - Source passage: > "My own version is inevitably tempered by my feminist and left politics."
  - Confidence: high (about her stance); the historiographic stance is `Directly stated`.
  - Recommended wiki note: optional `arguments/`; more naturally a methodological note in the source-summary. Defer.

## Metaphors

- **Escape / parole / the white cell** — the master metaphor of the essay (and its title "Escape Attempts"): art as a prisoner attempting to break out of the frame-and-pedestal/commodity system; dematerialization as a temporary jailbreak; the market as recapture. > "The escape was temporary. Art was recaptured and sent back to its white cell, but parole is always a possibility." → candidate `metaphors/The Escape Attempt.md` (or `metaphors/Art's White Cell.md`).
- **The frame / breaking out of the frame** — the literal picture-frame-and-pedestal generalized into the whole apparatus of art's confinement; "The frame was there to be broken out of." (Barry: "something going on outside the frame.")
- **"Cultural confinement"** — Smithson's phrase, adopted by Lippard for the "sacrosanct ivory walls and heroic, patriarchal mythologies" the artists tried to escape.
- **"The idea becomes a machine that makes the art"** — LeWitt's mechanization of authorship; the idea as generative apparatus replacing the artist's hand.
- **"Conceptual gnats nipping at its fat flanks"** — the small, fast, cheap Conceptual practices versus the bloated New York gallery-money-power structure.
- **"Crack in the art world's walls"** — the opening through which women entered via cheap ephemeral mediums.
- **Tabula rasa / the Zen monk's broom and Dada's vitriolic humor** — the recurring "clean slate" longing (Minimalism's failed clean slate; Baldessari cremating his own early work; Kozlov's empty film reel).

## Symbols

- **The empty room** — the Paula Cooper "(apparently) 'empty' room" of invisible works (Haacke's fan, Barry's magnetic field, Weiner's pit, a "secret," a black blip); the literal image of dematerialization; intensified by Carnevale's sealed empty room in Argentina, where the audience must perform the escape.
- **The index card / the card catalogue** — Lippard's randomly arranged card catalogues for "557,087"/"955,000"; the catalogue itself as artwork and as the show's distributable, discardable form.
- **The xerox / typed page / blurry photograph** — the recurring material residue that *is* the dematerialized work; the document standing in for the absent object.
- **The telephone call / telex** — speed and immediacy of communication as both medium and ideology ("now it takes a telephone call . . . no one has anything").
- **"I AM STILL ALIVE" (On Kawara) / the round-robin telegram** — the telegram-as-artwork; communication-about-communication made literal.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial (the art-historical instance)** — the object vs the idea; physical presence vs information/systems; the salable thing vs the ephemeral/invisible/spoken work. This is the direct art-world realization of the vault's titular `Material vs Immaterial` axis. > "work in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary . . . 'dematerialized.'"
- **De-commodification vs recuperation (resistance vs market)** — the wager that non-object art escapes the commodity vs the verdict that the market absorbed it anyway. The book's defining unresolved tension. > "art and artists in a capitalist society remain luxuries."
- **Distribution vs accessibility / communication vs community** — democratic *reach* achieved, democratic *content* not. > "Communication (but not community) and distribution (but not accessibility)."
- **Form vs content (where the politics lives)** — apolitical subject matter vs radical de-commodifying form. > "it was usually the form rather than the content . . . that carried a political message."
- **Idea vs object / dematerialization vs LeWitt's "small c"** — Lippard's "idea paramount, material secondary" vs LeWitt's conceptual work whose "material forms were often conventional, although generated by a paramount idea." A productive internal disagreement about how immaterial Conceptual art really was.
- **Hand/genius vs anonymity/appropriation** — the artist's touch and originality vs "claiming," fabrication, shared/faked authorship, anti-style neutrality. > "an attack on the genius theory."
