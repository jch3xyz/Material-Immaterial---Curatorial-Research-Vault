# Analysis: Roy Ascott — "The Architecture of Cyberception" (1994)

## Source Metadata

Book: Telematic Embrace: Visionary Theories of Art, Technology, and Consciousness (ed. Edward A. Shanken, 2003)
Author: Roy Ascott
Raw file: raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/27 The Architecture of Cyberception.md

Date analyzed: 2026-06-08

This is Ascott's own primary text (chapter 27), first presented at ISEA94 in Helsinki and published in *Leonardo Electronic Almanac* 2 (1994). It is one of the founding statements of his 1990s third phase — the move from telematic art (1980s) into **cyberception, technoetics, and the post-biological body**.

## Executive Summary

The essay introduces and develops **cyberception** — Ascott's name for a new, "post-biological faculty" of perception-plus-conception that emerges when the human sensorium is fused with telematic networks (the "cybernet"). He argues we are no longer merely *using* technology but are *becoming* a new kind of being: "We are all interface," computer-mediated and computer-enhanced, distributed across real and virtual worlds simultaneously. Cyberception is a *qualitative*, not quantitative, change — an all-at-once, pattern-seeing, transpersonal awareness that replaces the linear, object-dividing perception of "the age of reason."

The essay's second half applies cyberception to **architecture and the city**. Western architecture is indicted for being "too much concerned with surfaces and structures and too little concerned with living systems" — it traffics in *appearance/representation/semiology* when art and life have moved to *apparition* ("the coming into being of what has never before been seen"). Ascott calls for cities reconceived as cybernetic, feedback-driven, interactive *zones of transformation*, designed in four zones (underground, street level, sky/sea, cyberspace), "grown" rather than designed (a "growbag culture," horticulture not warfare), to accommodate **telepresence** and the **distributed/disseminated self**. The artist, with their cyberception, is named as the one who can "seed" this new city.

The chapter is a keystone Ascott node: it is the fullest exposition of CYBERCEPTION in the corpus, ties cyberception to TELENOIA and NETWORK CONSCIOUSNESS, names the APPEARANCE→APPARITION shift, and threads the CONNECTIVITY / IMMERSION / INTERACTION / TRANSFORMATION vocabulary through an architecture/urbanism argument. It is also the chapter that most directly extends Burnham's OBJECT→SYSTEM thesis into the built environment ("the physics of space" vs "living systems"; "edificial" surfaces vs cybernetic systems).

## Arguments

- **Claim: We are not merely using technology but becoming a new (post-biological) kind of being; the individual is giving way to the interface.**
  - Reasoning structure: prosthetics/genetics are secondary; the real change is to consciousness and human presence (inhabiting real+virtual at once); this is qualitative, not quantitative.
  - Evidence: the new "sense of self"; "a set of selves"; "We are all interface."
  - Source passage: > "Actually, the sense of the individual is giving way to the sense of the interface. Our consciousness allows us the fuzzy edge on identity, hovering between inside and outside every kind of definition of what it is to be a human being"
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The individual is giving way to the interface.md`

- **Claim: Cyberception is a qualitatively new (or revived) faculty — pattern-seeing and all-at-once, the antithesis of linear, object-dividing perception.**
  - Reasoning structure: contrasts linear/categorical/"behind one another" perception (the age of reason / appearance) with whole-seeing, feedback-rich, multi-viewpoint awareness; frames cyberception as a *revived* faculty, "us finding ourselves again."
  - Evidence: "bird's-eye view," "the astronaut's view of the earth, the cybernaut's view of systems"; reading the Book of Changes, following the Tao.
  - Source passage: > "But cyberception means getting a sense of a whole, acquiring a bird's-eye view of events, the astronaut's view of the earth, the cybernaut's view of systems."
  - Source passage: > "In this, cyberception is not so much a new faculty as a revived faculty. It is us finding ourselves again, after the human waste and loss of the age of reason, the age of certainty, determinism, and absolute values. The age of appearance."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Cyberception is the antithesis of linear thought and a revival of a lost faculty.md`

- **Claim: The old artificial/natural debate is obsolete; we care about what can be made of ourselves, not what made us.**
  - Reasoning structure: dissolves the nature/artifice dichotomy along with other eroded dichotomies (urban/rural, work/play, day/night, local/global).
  - Evidence: the explicit dismissal; the list of "ideas whose inherent instability was always implicit in the dichotomies by which they were expressed."
  - Source passage: > "In fact, the old debate about artificial and natural is no longer relevant. We are only interested in what can be made of ourselves, not what made us."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The artificial-natural distinction is obsolete.md` (cross-link to Haraway's `The Breached Boundaries`).

- **Claim: Western architecture fails because it serves appearance/surface/representation rather than living cybernetic systems.**
  - Reasoning structure: indicts both classicism (inert stylistic past) and modernism (stylised present) for protecting "inertia"; buildings "speak" their function semiologically but "lie in their teeth" unless integrated into cybernetic systems.
  - Evidence: "the physics of space" without "a biology of building"; "edificial" surfaces; the city as battle zone.
  - Source passage: > "The problem with Western architecture is that it is too much concerned with surfaces and structures and too little concerned with living systems. There is no biology of building, simply the physics of space."
  - Source passage: > "Their buildings may speak 'hospital,' 'school,' 'library,' but unless they articulate these meanings within integrated, cybernetic systems, they lie in their teeth."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Architecture must turn from edificial surfaces to living cybernetic systems.md`

- **Claim: Architecture/the city must be grown, not designed — seeding replaces designing; horticulture, not warfare, is the guiding metaphor.**
  - Reasoning structure: buildings/cities are living systems; an evolutive, futures-oriented, feedback-rich, "intelligent" and "publicly intelligible" infrastructure is required; the artist is the one who can "seed" it.
  - Evidence: "growbag culture," "pollination and grafting," "classes in consciousness and gardening."
  - Source passage: > "It's a growbag culture that is needed, in which seeding replaces designing. Architectural practice should find its guiding metaphors in horticulture, rather than in warfare."
  - Source passage: > "It is artists who can become the sowers of these seeds, who can take the chances needed to allow new forms and features of the new city to grow."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The new city must be grown not designed.md`

- **Claim: The end of representation / the end of semiology — buildings will behave according to function rather than signify it; appearance gives way to apparition.**
  - Reasoning structure: semiology "is ceasing to underpin our structures"; behaviour replaces signification (a behaviourist echo of Ascott's 1960s cybernetic art).
  - Evidence: "Buildings will behave in ways consistent with their announced function, rather than speaking their role by semiological implication."
  - Source passage: > "The end of representation is nigh! Semiology is ceasing to underpin our structures. Buildings will behave in ways consistent with their announced function, rather than speaking their role by semiological implication."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The end of representation buildings behave rather than signify.md` (cross-link `Appearance vs Apparition`).

- **Claim: Telepresence / the distributed self is the most radical challenge to old architecture and the defining feature of the new human identity.**
  - Reasoning structure: collaborative/connected consciousness replaces the unitary/solitary mind; architecture has ignored the "teleself" and become indistinguishable from shopping/mall culture.
  - Evidence: "the disseminated self"; "Telepresence defines the new human identity perhaps more than any other aspect of the repertoire of cyberculture."
  - Source passage: > "Perhaps the most radical challenge to the old ideas of architecture comes from the consequences of telepresence, the disseminated self."
  - Source passage: > "Contemporary architecture and shopping have become more or less the same thing."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telepresence and the distributed self define the new human identity.md`

- **Claim: Cyberception lets us perceive the immaterial — process, emergence, connectivity, apparition — as palpably as we perceive material objects.**
  - Reasoning structure: the twentieth-century project of "making the invisible visible" is fulfilled by cyberception ("X-ray vision and the optics of outer space"); immaterial process is perceived "just as palpably and immediately as we commonly perceive material objects in material locations."
  - Evidence: Cassini at Titan ("it will be our eyes and minds that are there"); apprehending "the processes of emergence in nature."
  - Source passage: > "We cyberceive transformative relationships and connectivity as immaterial process just as palpably and immediately as we commonly perceive material objects in material locations."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Cyberception makes immaterial process perceptible as material objects.md` (strong `Material vs Immaterial` anchor for Ascott).

- **Idealism vs Pragmatism (architecture)** — Ascott rejects the either/or: > "Between idealism and pragmatism, between conception of the desired and perception of the possible, lie the evolutive initiatives of cyberception."
