## Executive Summary

This chapter is the second of the book's opening "Image" section, and it continues the memoir frame established in Chapter 1: O'Gieblyn has borrowed (on loan) a Sony Aibo robot dog, and her marriage becomes the domestic stage on which the chapter's larger philosophical questions are dramatized. Her husband's shifting reactions to the dog — first "creepy," then suspicious of its camera/surveillance, then unconsciously affectionate, then paranoid about Sony harvesting their data — braid a personal narrative through an essayistic argument about consciousness, animal minds, and machine minds.

The chapter's argumentative spine is **O'Gieblyn's signature method: technology as metaphor** — the recurring observation that "we say humans are like a god, like a clock, or like a computer," and that each dominant machine of an era becomes the lens through which the self is understood. Here that method is turned on the **computer-as-mind metaphor** (the "mind is software running on the brain's hardware"), which she argues has not escaped but merely re-inscribed **Cartesian dualism** — the soul "keeps slipping back in." She traces a cluster of consequences: the moving goalposts of machine intelligence (chess, Deep Blue/Kasparov, Searle, Hofstadter); the long Western denial of **animal minds** (Descartes's beast-machine, Darwin's "godlike intellect," Goodall and the chimps); the **hard problem of consciousness** and its eliminativist deniers (Dennett's "Cartesian theater," Galen Strawson's "the Great Denial"); the **disenchantment** of the West versus the un-disenchanted **animism** of Japan (Shinto/Buddhist object-funerals, the Aibo's Buddhist funeral, "all things have a bit of soul"); and the **simulation-versus-reality** problem (Frazer's imitative magic, "like produces like," Searle's rainstorm). It closes on **the view from nowhere** (Nagel) and the claim that science succeeded by bracketing consciousness out — so the computer metaphor has not solved the mind-body problem but only "transferred them to a new substrate" (Papert/Sikkema/Arendt).

The **ex-evangelical / Calvinist memoir thread** carries real argumentative weight here: O'Gieblyn contrasts her former Christian "naive, unquestioning faith in the faculty of higher thought" (Augustine, the mind connected to the Absolute, reason as the path to truth) with her present-day distrust of mind in a materialist university town where everyone talks about bodies, diets, and drugs and is told in yoga to "let go of the thinking mind." The theological register is not decoration: the chapter argues that the computational metaphor is a secularized continuation of the soul, and that eliminativism is itself "much like Descartes's soul" — placing consciousness outside time and space. This is the book's core thesis in miniature: modern technological metaphysics is a transposition of theology, and the existential problems were not solved, only re-housed.

For vault purposes the chapter is a major O'Gieblyn locus for: **technology as metaphor** (her central method), **the computer/mind metaphor and Cartesian dualism**, the **hard problem of consciousness** and **eliminativism**, **disenchantment / re-enchantment** (Weber's term, animism), **anthropomorphism** and the differentiation impulse, **simulation vs reality**, and **the view from nowhere / the exclusion of consciousness from science**. It overlaps heavily with the existing Hayles cluster (Posthumanism, The Computational Universe, Embodiment, The Body as Data, The Liberal Humanist Subject, Information) and is the natural place to realize the committed metaphor stub `Mind as Machine`.

## Definitions

Terms explicitly defined or strongly clarified in the chapter:

- **Imitative magic** — explicitly defined via Frazer: the magical principle "that like produces like," in which simulating a phenomenon is believed to cause the real effect.
  - > In his study of world mythologies, The Golden Bough, James George Frazer describes imitative magic as the principle "that like produces like." "The magician," he writes, "infers that he can produce any effect he desires merely by imitating it."

- **The "Cartesian theater" (Dennett)** — defined as the (purported) delusion that there is in the brain a perceiving homunculus watching the brain's representations on a screen.
  - > Dennett refers to the belief in interior experience derisively as the "Cartesian theater," invoking the popular delusion—again, Descartes's fault—that there exists in the brain some miniature perceiving entity, a homunculus...

- **"The Great Denial" (Strawson's name for eliminativism)** — the theory that consciousness does not exist.
  - > The philosopher Galen Strawson has dubbed this theory "the Great Denial"...

- **"Intuition of distinctness" (Papineau)** — the strong, perhaps innate sensation that the mind transcends the physical.
  - > The British philosopher David Papineau has argued that we all have an "intuition of distinctness," a strong, perhaps innate sensation that our minds transcend the physical.

- **The view from nowhere (Nagel)** — the third-person standpoint that excludes res cogitans and limits description to res extensa.
  - > Thomas Nagel refers to this thirdperson standpoint as "the view from nowhere."

- **res cogitans / res extensa (Descartes)** — the subjective mind vs. the objective, mathematical realm of physical facts.
  - > we must put aside res cogitans—the subjective, immediate way in which we experience the world in our minds—and limit ourselves to res extensa, the objective, mathematical language of physical facts.

- **kuyō** — the Japanese purification rite given at funerals, extended to long-used broken objects.
  - > it is often taken to a temple or a shrine to receive the kuyō, the purification rite given at funerals.

## Arguments

- **Claim: The computer-as-mind metaphor does not escape Cartesian dualism — it re-inscribes it.**
  - Reasoning structure: The metaphor was invented to dispense with the soul; but "mind = software, brain = hardware" makes the mind an immaterial pattern that supervenes on physical matter — structurally identical to Descartes's soul/body split. The appeal of the metaphor is precisely that it lets one keep an "autonomous mental life" (Putnam) while claiming physicalism. Physicalists' own language ("generate," "give rise to," "correlated with") betrays a covert dualism (Papineau). Nietzsche: we keep God because we keep grammar.
  - Evidence: Putnam's "Philosophy and Our Mental Life"; Papineau's "intuition of distinctness" and the generate/give-rise-to argument; the smoke/fire analogy.
  - Source passage: > But the metaphor is arguably appealing because it reiterates the Cartesian assumption that the mind is something above and beyond the physical.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The computer metaphor of mind reinstates Cartesian dualism`

- **Claim: As machines master formerly "human" tasks we keep moving the goalposts of human distinction — and our criteria are inconsistent.**
  - Reasoning structure: Early AI took intelligence to mean higher cognition (chess, theorems); when machines won at these (Deep Blue beats Kasparov), philosophers redefined real consciousness as emotion/perception/feeling — the traits we share with animals. Yet we spent centuries denying animals minds precisely because they lacked reason. The criterion flips to whatever currently preserves human exceptionalism.
  - Evidence: Searle on Deep Blue ("Chess is a trivial game"); Hofstadter's reversal; the difficulty AI has with perception/motor skills vs. abstract thought; Darwin's "godlike intellect"; the 1950s chimp consensus; Goodall.
  - Source passage: > But the more competent AI systems become at these cerebral tasks, the more stubbornly we resist granting them human intelligence.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/We move the goalposts of human distinction as machines master cognition`

- **Claim: Eliminativism (the denial that consciousness exists) is itself crypto-Cartesian and traps itself in the same seventeenth-century assumptions it claims to overcome.**
  - Reasoning structure: To say consciousness is an illusion is to place it outside the material world — "much like Descartes's soul" — something not in time or space. The deniers, like the dualists, remain captive to the mind/matter division. The "Cartesian theater" homunculus regress shows the incoherence of one model, but eliminativism overcorrects into denial.
  - Evidence: Dennett's "Cartesian theater," the zombie, the "illusion" of subjectivity; Strawson's "Great Denial"; the homunculus infinite regress.
  - Source passage: > To say that consciousness is an illusion is to place it outside the material world, deeming it something—much like Descartes's soul—that does not exist within time or space.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Denying consciousness is itself a Cartesian move`

- **Claim: Modern science succeeded precisely by bracketing consciousness out — and this methodological exclusion hardened into metaphysical denial.**
  - Reasoning structure: Descartes's mind/matter division let us "speak exhaustively about nature without reference to God or ourselves" (the view from nowhere, Nagel), enabling Newtonian physics, secularism, empiricism, industrialization. But bracketing consciousness because it was hard to study became the conclusion that, because it can't be studied scientifically, it doesn't exist. Believing science will one day explain all mental life requires "a philosophical leap" (Sikkema) that ignores that science was built by excluding what it couldn't explain.
  - Evidence: Nagel's view from nowhere; Arendt's "inner faculty without a world relationship"; Sikkema's "philosophical leap."
  - Source passage: > Science put a bracket around consciousness because it was too difficult to study objectively, but this methodological avoidance eventually led to metaphysical denial, to the conclusion that because consciousness cannot not be studied scientifically, it does not exist.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Science excluded consciousness and then denied it exists`

- **Claim: Building brain-simulations and hoping consciousness will emerge is a form of imitative magic — and a simulation of a thing is not the thing.**
  - Reasoning structure: Animist imitative magic holds "like produces like" (Frazer). Building simulations of brains in the hope consciousness will emerge is the same superstition: we conflate the imitation with the imitated. Searle: a simulated rainstorm doesn't make us wet; a computer simulation of consciousness isn't conscious. We can make only "graven images."
  - Evidence: Frazer's *The Golden Bough*; the effigy/rain-magic examples; Searle's rainstorm analogy.
  - Source passage: > But what kind of magical thinking makes us think that our paltry imitations are synonymous with the thing they are trying to imitate—that silicon and electricity can reproduce effects that arise from flesh and blood?
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Simulating a brain to produce consciousness is imitative magic`

- **Claim: The computational metaphor has not solved the mind-body problem — it has only transferred the old theological/philosophical problems to a new substrate.**
  - Reasoning structure: Metaphors usually die when shown insufficient; this one entrenches and redefines its objects instead. But all the metaphor demonstrates (Papert) is that the old problems "come up in equivalent form in the new context." The existential impasse of consciousness is not resolved by computation, only re-housed.
  - Evidence: Papert; the framing that "our metaphors, no matter how modern or inventive, will continue to reiterate this central impasse of science."
  - Source passage: > The metaphor has not solved our most pressing existential problems; it has merely transferred them to a new substrate.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The computer metaphor transfers the mind-body problem to a new substrate`

- **Claim: We are perhaps hardwired to experience our minds as separate from our bodies; the soul persists at the level of language and grammar.**
  - Reasoning structure: Papineau's "intuition of distinctness" suggests the mind/body split may be innate; even reductive physicalists speak as if mind "generates" or "accompanies" brain, as if it were a separate substance; "dead metaphors" and casual soul-talk pervade everyday speech. Nietzsche: we still believe in God because we still believe in grammar.
  - Evidence: Papineau; the smoke/fire analogy; Nietzsche.
  - Source passage: > Nietzsche said it best: we haven't gotten rid of God because we still believe in grammar.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated, though "hardwired" is hedged in the text as "it's possible")
  - Recommended wiki note: fold into `arguments/The computer metaphor of mind reinstates Cartesian dualism` or note as a sub-claim; flag for human.

- **Claim (memoir-borne): The author's loss of religious faith in reason has accompanied a broader cultural collapse of trust in the thinking mind, replaced by an obsession with the body.**
  - Reasoning structure: As a Christian she had "a naive, unquestioning faith in the faculty of higher thought" (Augustine; reason connected to the Absolute; logic as the path to eternal truth). Now, in a materialist university town, no one tries to persuade anyone of anything; convictions are seen as the product of hormones, culture, evolution, unconscious needs; conversation is about diets, drugs, exercise; yoga instructs her to "let go of the thinking mind." Materialism is "the only viable metaphysics in modernity."
  - Evidence: the Bible-school memories (predestination, covenant theology, eternal fate as a mental phenomenon); the university-town contrast; William James on consciousness; La Mettrie's *Man a Machine*.
  - Source passage: > Materialism is the only viable metaphysics in modernity, an era that was founded on the total irreconcilability of matter and mind.
  - Confidence: medium-high (the cultural generalization is interpretive; the personal/Christian contrast is directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Materialism is the only viable metaphysics in modernity` (or capture under a `Disenchantment` concept note). Flag for human.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Mind vs. matter / dualism vs. physicalism** — the governing tension: the computer metaphor promises physicalism but smuggles back the immaterial mind; eliminativism denies mind but thereby re-immaterializes it.
- **Higher cognition vs. embodied perception/feeling** — machines excel at abstract thought (chess, equations) but fail at the sensorimotor tasks we do unconsciously; so the locus of "real" consciousness keeps shifting from reason to feeling.
- **Anthropomorphism vs. the differentiation impulse** — the simultaneous urge to attribute life to machines/animals and to redraw the human boundary to exclude them (the husband chastises her for anthropomorphizing, then names and pets the dog).
- **Disenchantment vs. re-enchantment / animism** — the disenchanted West (Descartes, materialism) vs. an un-disenchanted Japan where objects have soul; the Aibo sits on this fault line.
- **Simulation vs. reality** — whether an imitation of a phenomenon (rainstorm, brain) reproduces the phenomenon; magical conflation vs. Searle's distinction.
- **Faith in reason vs. distrust of the thinking mind** — the Christian/Augustinian trust in higher thought vs. the materialist culture's reduction of conviction to hormones/evolution and its instruction to "let go of the thinking mind."
- **Privileged access vs. the unconscious** — if most thinking is unconscious and we have no privileged access to our motives, the self loses authority over itself; O'Gieblyn turns this on her own argument ("can't I do the same to myself?").
- **The view from nowhere vs. lived subjectivity** — objectivity purchased by excluding res cogitans vs. the irreducibility of first-person experience.
