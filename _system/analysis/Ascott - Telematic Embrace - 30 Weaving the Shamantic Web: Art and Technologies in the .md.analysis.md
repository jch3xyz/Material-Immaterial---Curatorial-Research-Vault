## Executive Summary

This is one of the keystone chapters of Ascott's late, **technoetic** phase (1990sã2000s). Where the 1980s chapters (15, 18, 20) built the *telematic-art* vocabulary (network consciousness, the telematic embrace, Gesamtdatenwerk), this 1998 essay completes the arc the book's subtitle promises ã *Art, Technology, and Consciousness* ã by placing **consciousness itself** at the literal center of art. Ascott proposes a new aesthetic he names the **technoetic aesthetic** (technology + *nous*/mind), grounded in the claim that "technology plus mind" can both explore consciousness and generate "distinctly new forms of art, new qualities of mind, new forms of cognition and perception." For the artist, crucially, "consciousness is more to be explored than to be explained, more to be transformed than understood, more to be reframed than reported" ã a programmatic anti-reductionism that distinguishes the artistic from the scientific approach to mind.

The chapter's signature move is a sustained **parallelism between two technologies of consciousness**: digital/telematic technology (virtual reality, hypermedia, the Net) and the ancient "plant technology" of shamanism (here specifically the Amazonian psychoactive brew *ayahuasca*, the "vine of the soul," and the Santo Daime ritual). Ascott reports first-person fieldwork at both poles ã VR labs (the Human Interface Technology Laboratory in Seattle; the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill VR Lab) and the Kuikuru shamans (*pagûˋs*) of the Xingu River region of the Mato Grosso in Brazil. From this parallel he derives his central new concepts: **double consciousness** (simultaneous access to "two distinctly different fields of experience"), the **double gaze** ("seeing at once both inward realities and the outward surfaces of the world"), **psi-perception** (the shamanic analogue of technologically aided **cyberception**), and the title figure, the **shamantic web** ã a deliberate fusion of *shamanic* + *semantic*, "the navigation of consciousness and the construction of meaning."

A third major thread is the proto-**moistmedia** ontology: Ascott argues that the artificial-life sciences, embracing "both dry (pixel) and moist (molecular) artificial organisms" and nanotechnology, are "eroding the boundaries between states of mind, between conception and construction." Using the scanning tunnelling microscope (STM) ã which lets us not only image single atoms but "manipulate them, one by one, atom by atom" ã he argues that "the prosthesis of vision can be at one and the same time instrumental in constructing what is envisioned. To see in the mind's eye is to realize in the material world." Here is his strongest single statement of the material/immaterial collapse: "The immaterial and material lose their categorical distinction." He coins **techno-qualia** for the resulting "whole new repertoire of senses."

The final thread is a polemic against the gallery/audience model of interactive art, escalating his long-running participation argument. The networked interactive work, installed in a museum, is "incarcerated within the very status it despises, that of pure object" because it still presupposes a "second observer" and a "phantom audience." Against this he invokes the *pagûˋ*'s ritual: "performative but ãÎ not intended as a public performance," an "enactment of psychic power, not a performance." His prescription ã "the removal of the second observer and the phantom audience from the canon of telematic art" ã yields "an art without audience in its inactive mode," "Eschewing the passive voyeur," speaking to "a kind of widespread intimacy, closeness on the planetary scale."

For the vault, this chapter is the **primary anchor** for the late-Ascott consciousness cluster: it should create `Technoetics` (the keystone, holding "technoetic aesthetic"), `Cyberception`, `The Double Gaze`, `Double Consciousness`, `The Shamantic Web`, and `Techno-Qualia`, while EXTENDING the existing `Re-enchantment` (Ascott as the art-side technoetic re-enchanter beside O'Gieblyn), `Material vs Immaterial` (the categorical-collapse pole), `New Media Art` / `Art as Experiential Technology` (the audience-less enactment refinement), `Reciprocal Communication` (intimacy/interaction without the second observer), `Distributed Cognition` (distributed mind), `Emergence` (artificial life self-assembly), `Embodiment` and `The Body as Data` (the "second body ãÎ of a million molecular points of light").
