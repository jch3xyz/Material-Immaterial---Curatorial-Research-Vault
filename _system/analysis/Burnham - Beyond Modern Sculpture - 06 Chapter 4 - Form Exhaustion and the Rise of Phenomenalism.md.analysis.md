## Executive Summary

This is the climactic chapter of Part I ("Sculpture as Object") and the hinge of the entire book: it diagnoses the **exhaustion of formalism and vitalism** as the two governing programs of twentieth-century sculpture and announces their succession by **phenomenalism** — a shift from making form to staging the *experience of perception*. The chapter opens with a "Resume" restating the book's master thesis: sculpture has moved from "material idealization of human form in Classical Greece" toward "seeming immateriality" and is becoming "a continuum of steadily changing ideas about the world" — a series of **technological reifications** that have rendered style criticism obsolete. Burnham then dispatches the prewar academy of "official taste" and the postwar academy of mass-produced biomorphic and reductivist sculpture (the "weary vocabulary" reaching its *reductio ad absurdum* in Sam Goodman's *No-Sculptures*). He argues that both vitalism and classical formalism collapse for the same reason: scientific conceptualization has moved into "a world of invisible values" (wave mechanics, electronic circuitry, statistical formulae) that a "strictly visual-geometrical" art can no longer mirror. The chapter's intellectual core imports phenomenology — **Husserl** (the "crisis in European science," phenomenological reduction, absolute consciousness) and especially **Merleau-Ponty** (perception as an existential bodily act, "carnal intersubjectivity," "I cannot see a cube as its geometrical definition presents it: I can only think it") — to theorize the new **Object / Minimal art** of Judd, Morris, Flavin, Tony Smith, et al. Object sculpture is "sculpture de-idealized": it abandons transcendent imagery, "admits to being itself — nothing else," and shifts the burden of "vitalizing" the work onto the viewer's own perceptual self-awareness, replacing "transcendent idealism" with "transcendent phenomenalism." Crucially for the vault, this is the chapter where Burnham (a) names the **object-to-experience** transition that Part II will reframe as object-to-system, (b) anchors the "immateriality/dematerialization" axis (sculpture's "steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality"), pre-dating Lippard, and (c) treats art as an instrument for triggering perceptual experience — grounding the committed stub *Art as Experiential Technology*.

## Definitions

- **Essential sculpture (Wilenski, 1932)** — quoted twice as the reductivist creed and then refuted by Goodman's *No-Sculptures*.
  - > "Essential sculpture is sculpture which has the same kind of meaning as the sphere, cube and cylinder."
- **Phenomenological reduction (Husserl)** — see concept above; "the process [by which] \"pure\" conscious experience could be distilled."
- **Carnal intersubjectivity (Merleau-Ponty)** — the relational perceptual field that "arises from the first glance" between viewer and object(s).
- **The classical attributes of sculpture** — the definition the chapter dismantles:
  - > "The characteristics of sculpture can be summarized in the fixed attributes of the Classical world: place, position, immobility, parts, proportion, and static homage to the human condition-in a word, the creation of immutable ideals through objects."

## Arguments

- **Both vitalism and classical formalism are exhausted because scientific reality has become invisible** — they were "visual-geometrical" programs and cannot mirror a world of wave mechanics, circuitry, and statistics.
  - Reasoning: parallel-decline argument (vitalism in science ↔ vitalism in art; scientific idealism ↔ formalism), evidenced by Husserl's "crisis in European science" and the transistor-radio/watch contrast.
  - Source passage: > "Sculpture that tried to hold on to artistic formalism had the same chance of survival as its scientific counterpart in a world ruled by invisible energy and statistical formulae."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Formalism and vitalism are exhausted because scientific reality has become invisible.md`

- **Modern sculpture's history is a sequence of technological reifications, not of styles** — Wölfflinian/cyclical stylistics cannot account for it; "style no longer relates to the epoch."
  - Reasoning: critique of art-historical method; sculpture "seems to move completely beyond the ken of style consciousness."
  - Source passage: > "Even fifty-five years later, sculpture outside the circle of twentieth-century representationalism still does not lend itself to the cyclical criteria of Wolfflinian stylistics; rather, it seems to move completely beyond the ken of style consciousness."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Modern sculpture's history is a sequence of technological reifications not of styles.md`

- **Twentieth-century sculpture gravitates toward seeming immateriality** — its "dialectical tension" is a "steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality (through forms of attenuated and unstable materiality), while at the same time resisting this trend." (Foundational for the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` axis; pre-dates Lippard's dematerialization.)
  - Reasoning: the loss of "weight, mass, and form" as the through-line of the century; the object-status of sculpture is "deceptive."
  - Source passage: > "the dialectical tension within twentieth-century sculpture remains its steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality (through forms of attenuated and unstable materiality), while at the same time resisting this trend."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Twentieth-century sculpture gravitates toward seeming immateriality.md`

- **Object/Minimal art shifts the burden of "vitalizing" the work onto the viewer's perception** — the form is de-idealized; the viewer must "reconstruct the sculpture for himself" and analyze "his private methods of seeing."
  - Reasoning: phenomenological turn; Merleau-Ponty's "I can only think it" (the cube); the work "forces us to see ourselves seeing objects."
  - Source passage: > "much of the obligation to \"vitalize\" sculpture-make it come to life before the viewer-has been shifted to the viewer's capacity for analyzing his priva te methods of seeing."
  - Source passage: > "Judd, perhaps, forces us to see ourselves seeing objects unencumbered by the analytical games and value judgments promoted through art education."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Object art shifts the burden of vitalizing the work onto the viewer's perception.md`

- **Phenomenalism cannot endow the object with what is not there; it can only reveal our perceptual habits** — Burnham's own critical caveat against over-claiming for Minimalism.
  - Reasoning: "beyond the domain of scientific analysis, phenomenalism cannot imbue an object with attributes which are not there."
  - Source passage: > "Yet in all candor, beyond the domain of scientific analysis, phenomenalism cannot imbue an object with attributes which are not there. It can only force us to be aware of the chaos and multi-layered nature of our visual habits."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Phenomenalism cannot endow the object with what is not there only reveal our perceptual habits.md`

- **The reductivist program is self-limiting because elementary geometric solutions are few** — followers vainly try to out-reduce the innovators after "optimal solutions are self-evident."
  - Source passage: > "it has been the reflex of all art movements that followers try to solve formal problems only after optimal solutions are self-evident in the work of innovators."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The reductivist program is self-limiting because elementary geometric solutions are few.md`

- **Official taste retroactively erases the recent past** — the prewar academic sculpture that filled museums has been "banished... to the cellars" by "the brainwashing effects of official taste change."
  - Source passage: > "Digging these volumes out of the library stacks composes a salutary exercise in discovering the brainwashing effects of official taste change."
  - Confidence: medium (vivid but localized to the art-historical argument)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Official taste retroactively erases the recent past.md` (optional — could remain as supporting evidence; see Human Review)
