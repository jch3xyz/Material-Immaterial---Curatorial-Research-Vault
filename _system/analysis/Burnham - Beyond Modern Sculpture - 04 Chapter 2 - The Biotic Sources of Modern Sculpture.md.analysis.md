## Executive Summary

Chapter 2 is the **biotic-sources** chapter of Part I ("Sculpture as Object"). Its task is to explain why, "if vitalism played such a reactionary role in the biological sciences, did it provide some of the most advanced justification for avant-garde abstract sculpture?" Burnham's answer is a long intellectual history that runs through five interlocking sections: (1) **organicism as an evolving creed** (Mumford's "organic ideology," the gravitation of technology back toward organic models); (2) the centuries-long **mechanistâ€“vitalist dispute** in biology (Descartes, La Mettrie, WĂ¶hler, Bernard, Haeckel, the steady erosion of the organic/inorganic boundary into "a single continuum"); (3) **Bergson's poetics of vitalism** (the *Ă©lan vital* as a beautiful, poetic, scientifically defeated idea that nonetheless became the reigning aesthetic of modern sculpture); (4) the **aesthetics of neo-vitalism** in Herbert Read and Henri Focillon (the organicâ€“geometric fusion, the morphogenetic theory of forms, Read's confession "I am an unregenerate Bergsonian"); and (5) close **case studies of five vitalist sculptors** â€” Gaudier-Brzeska, Brancusi, Arp, Hepworth, Moore â€” plus the postwar American welders (Roszak, Lassaw, Lipton).

The chapter's deepest argument, and the one that connects it to the book's master thesis, is that **vitalism is a transitional step in the migration "from inanimate object to system."** Burnham distinguishes two senses of "organic": (a) the appearance/metaphor of life in inert matter (the sculptor's vitalism â€” life is "plastically, present," not literally present), and (b) the functional organization of "interacting systems" (the engineer's/biologist's organicism). His historical claim is that the meaning of "organic" has "gradually moved from completely natural and primitive forms to a new set of meanings circumscribed by technological capability" â€” "from biotic appearances toward biotic functioning via the machine." Vitalism reinvested sculpture with conviction after the decline of realism, but it depends on a finite stock of "biotic forms"; "when these were exhausted, the idiom was finished." The chapter thus diagnoses **form exhaustion** at the level of biological metaphor and quietly anticipates the move to actual life-simulation (kinetic, cybernetic) developed in Part II. It is also a key source for the *vitalism = emergence* worry already logged in the vault's `Emergence` note (via O'Gieblyn) and for the "systems aesthetics" lineage in `Systems Theory` (via Lippard) â€” here grounded in its primary text.

## Definitions

- **Organicism (two definitions, Burnham)** â€” (1) "the awareness of the interrelation between systems and their components within larger systems so that behavior of the whole ensemble can be understood and manipulated"; (2) "any arrangement possessing the attributes of life." - **Vitalism vs Organicism (Burnham's distinction)** â€” "Vitalism is a preeminently poetic view of life, a celebration of the natural condition, while organicism is a beautiful view of the utilitarian evolution of systems, both biological and non biological."
- **Mechanism (mechanistic theory of organic matter, Burnham)** â€” "a belief in a physical basis, usually electrochemical, for all phenomena of life including human consciousness.
