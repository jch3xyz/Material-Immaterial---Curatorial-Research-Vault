## Executive Summary

This is Ascott's earliest essay in the volume (originally "Art And Didactics," *Cambridge Opinion*, January 1964) and the chronological ORIGIN of his cybernetic / behaviourist art theory. It does two things at once: (1) it sets out a theory of the artwork as a behavioural SYSTEM that the spectator completes — the "Change Painting" and kinetic construction whose parts are "not fixed and may be changed by the intervention of the spectator," so that "the participant becomes responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning"; and (2) it argues, explicitly and programmatically, that the artist must turn to **cybernetics** — "control and communication in animal and machine" — as the integrative science that can ground a discipline for art. The essay's title, "The Construction of Change," names Ascott's core 1960s practice: artworks built so that change is their substance, "analogues of ideas—structures that are subject to change and human intervention in the way that ideas themselves are." The second half describes the **Groundcourse** Ascott initiated and directed at Ealing School of Art (London) — a two-year pedagogy that "disorients" the student through behavioural exercises (acquiring a contrived new personality, forming six-member "sexagonal organisms," designing "calibrators" and "mind-maps" for one's behaviour), making art education itself a cybernetic feedback system in which "creative and pedagogic" activities "interact, each feeding back to the other."

Set against the just-ingested Burnham, this essay supplies the OTHER pole of 1960s cybernetic art: where Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) theorizes the OBJECT→SYSTEM shift in the autonomous art object, Ascott (1964, four years earlier) locates the system in the BEHAVIOURAL loop between artist, artwork, and participating spectator — the artwork "occupies a pivotal point between two sets of behaviour, the artist's and the spectator's. It is essentially a matrix, the substance between." This is the participatory/behavioural cybernetic art that the Burnham notes `New Media Art`, `Art as Experiential Technology`, `Cyborg Art`, and `Reciprocal Communication` were explicitly committed to be EXTENDED by when Ascott was ingested. It also reaches forward to McLuhan (the machine "extends his perception into furthest space" — extensions of man, same year, 1964) and to the network/posthuman cluster (man "becoming more and more predominantly a controller and less an effecter," the human–machine–environment feedback system).

## Key Concepts

- **The Construction of Change / the Change Painting** — the artwork built so that change is its substance; parts not fixed, alterable by the spectator; "analogues of ideas." This is the foundational form of Ascott's behaviourist/cybernetic art.
  - Source passage: > "I make structures in which the relationships of parts are not fixed and may be changed by the intervention of the spectator. As formal relationships are altered, so the ideas they stand for are extended."
  - Source passage: > "My Change Paintings and kinetic constructions are intended not only to discuss and project ideas but also to function as analogues of ideas—structures that are subject to change and human intervention in the way that ideas themselves are."
  - Existing wiki note: `(none)` — closest are Burnham's `New Media Art`, `Art as Experiential Technology`, `Object and System`.
  - Recommended action: create new note (`Behaviourist Art` is the broader durable name; the Change Painting is its keystone artwork — see Symbols). Cross-link to the EXTENDED Burnham notes.

- **Behaviourist Art / behaviour as the medium** — art understood as a behavioural problem; the spectator becomes a "decision maker," "responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning." The artist sets behavioural limits within which the participant behaves.
  - Source passage: > "To discuss what one is doing rather than the artwork that results, to attempt to unravel the loops of creative activity, is, in many ways, a behavioural problem."
  - Source passage: > "In response to behavioural clues in a construction (to push, pull, slide back, open, peg in, for example), the participant becomes responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning. He becomes a decision maker in the symbolic world that confronts him."

## Arguments

- **Claim: The spectator completes the artwork; meaning is extended through the participant's behaviour, not fixed by the artist.**
  - Reasoning structure: from a behavioural premise (art is "a behavioural problem," "behaviour relates to idea") to a structural design principle (parts not fixed, alterable by intervention) to a consequence (the participant "becomes responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning").
  - Evidence: the Change Paintings and kinetic constructions; the "push, pull, slide back, open, peg in" behavioural clues.
  - Source passage: > "Once positioned in relation to a work, he may become totally involved—physically as well as intellectually or emotionally. To project my ideas, I set limits within which he may behave."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The spectator completes the artwork by behaving within limits the artist sets` (or similar). Keystone of `Behaviourist Art`.

- **Claim: The artist must turn to cybernetics as the integrative science that can ground a discipline for art.**
  - Reasoning structure: science is too fragmented for the artist to consult wholesale; cybernetics is integrative, "a coordinator of science, as art is the coordinator of experience"; therefore cybernetics is the proper scientific reference for art; moreover it "deals with concepts of information, perception, translation, logic, and chance that are singularly relevant to his art."
  - Evidence: cybernetics' integrative method, its "dynamic view of life, not unlike that of the artist," its concern with process and behaviour.
  - Source passage: > "Cybernetics, however, is essentially integrative, drawing many disparate sciences together. ... It is a coordinator of science, as art is the coordinator of experience."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The artist must turn to cybernetics as the integrative science for art` — the keystone Ascott argument; anchors the `Cybernetics` EXTEND and the cybernetic-art lineage.

- **Claim: We should see the system or process before evaluating the "thing" — identity is relative; everything changes ceaselessly.**
  - Reasoning structure: a cybernetic ontology of process over object; identity is "always relative, yet it presupposes a whole"; method follows: investigate the system first.
  - Evidence: the cybernetic dynamic view of life; "Phenomena are studied in so far as they do something or are part of something that is being done."
  - Source passage: > "Everything changes ceaselessly; we investigate our world best by first seeing the system or process before evaluating the 'thing.'"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: this is Ascott's process-over-object thesis — DIRECTLY parallels Burnham's `Object and System` and grounds `Material vs Immaterial` (object→system). Cross-link both; possibly an argument note `See the system before the thing`.

- **Claim: Art is didactic and the artist has a moral responsibility to understand technological/scientific change.**
  - Reasoning structure: all art instructs (gives direction); society is in "enormous transition" driven by science and technology; therefore the artist must understand these changes — "Some real familiarity with scientific thought is indispensable to him."
  - Evidence: the Mann quotation ("murderous easy-goingness"), Goethe ("control circumstances as far as possible").
  - Source passage: > "The artist's moral responsibility demands that he attempt to understand these changes. Some real familiarity with scientific thought is indispensable to him."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The artist has a moral responsibility to understand technological change` (or fold into `Behaviourist Art` / a `Art as Didactics` note).

- **Claim: A true discipline of science for art is the opposite of scientism — engage the concepts, not the props.**
  - Reasoning structure: distinguishes substantive engagement (concepts, process, demonstration) from "a spurious scientism" (style, "the furniture of science, the props"); equally rejects anti-science.
  - Evidence: the critique of "a romanticism of machines and laboratories, micro-photographs and unidentified cross sections."
  - Source passage: > "To ignore the theory, the process, the demonstration must be a contradiction of science, and, indeed, of a forward reaching art. Anti-science in art is equally to be criticised."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: fold into the Ascott `Cybernetics` section; possibly an argument `Genuine scientific engagement in art is the opposite of scientism`.

## Metaphors

- **The artwork as "matrix" / "the substance between"** — the work as the connective medium between two behaviours, not an autonomous object. > "It is essentially a matrix, the substance between."
- **"Unravel the loops of creative activity"** — creativity figured as feedback loops to be unravelled; a cybernetic image of the creative process. > "to attempt to unravel the loops of creative activity."
- **The machine as that which "stands between man and his world"** — a mediating interface that "extends his perception"; proto-McLuhan extension/prosthesis image.
- **"Sexagonal organisms"** — the six-member student groups figured as living organisms, "interdependent and highly conscious of one another's capabilities and limitations." Biological metaphor for the collaborative cybernetic unit.
- **"A many-sided organism may evolve" / the Groundcourse as an organism "in the course of evolution"** — art education as a living, evolving system rather than a fixed curriculum.
- **Entropy as "a constant drift in the universe towards a state of total undifferentiation"** — quoted in a student exercise; "pockets of resistance are organising continuously" (life/order against entropy). Cross-links Burnham's `Entropy (Burnham)`.
- **The artist as "the free man par excellence"** — the artist acting out, "perhaps ritualistically," "man's 'capacity to create what is to be.'"

## Symbols

- **The Change Painting** — Ascott's signature 1960s artwork and the emblem of "the construction of change"; the participatory, alterable painting. (Figure 22, "Roy Ascott with Change Painting. Ca. 1959.") Strong candidate symbol note.
- **The Blackboard / "Blackboard Notes" (1967)** — per the editor's caption, "symbolic of a pedagogical context," "the ground for representing Ascott's fusion of studio and classroom practice." (Caption is Shanken's, not Ascott's prose.) Symbol of the didactics-art fusion.
- **The "calibrators" and "mind-maps"** — instruments students design "to read off their responses" and for "immediate reference to their behaviour pattern"; symbols of the cybernetic self-monitoring (feedback) at the heart of the Groundcourse.
- **The trolley / the converse personality** — the disorientation exercises (a student "limited to transporting himself about the school on a trolley"; "The shy girl must act out an easy sociability") as symbols of behaviour as plastic, controllable material.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Object vs System / process vs thing** — Ascott's "first seeing the system or process before evaluating the 'thing'" is the participatory/behavioural form of the same object→system shift Burnham theorizes for the autonomous object. Maps to `Object vs System` and `Material vs Immaterial`.
- **Fixed work vs changeable work (artist's control vs spectator's freedom)** — the artist "sets limits within which he may behave," yet the spectator "becomes a decision maker" who extends the meaning. Authorship is shared but bounded: a designed tension between control and openness. Relates to `Determinism vs Randomness` (Burnham) and to the cybernetic `One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication` (`Reciprocal Communication`).
- **Science vs art / scientism vs genuine engagement** — art "does not eschew science" yet must not become "a scientific work"; "only creative synthesis can produce the coordinating matrix for ideas that is art." A productive tension, not a contradiction. Maps to Burnham's `Art vs Technics`.
- **Control / prediction (science) vs play "in deep seriousness" (art)** — "Science seeks to reduce the unpredictable to measurable limits. ... By comparison, the artist plays, but it is play 'in deep seriousness' (Mann)." Both aim at "that optimum of control and creativity."
- **Order vs entropy** — invoked via the student exercise: drift toward undifferentiation vs "pockets of resistance ... organising continuously." A cybernetic/thermodynamic tension underlying the whole program.
