## Executive Summary

This is the 1967 entry of Lippard's documentary anthology — a dense, deliberately non-narrative chronicle of conceptual-, minimal-, earth-, and Arte Povera-adjacent activity for that year. Lippard's own argumentative voice is sparse; she stitches together exhibition records, bibliographic citations, artist statements, work descriptions, and verbatim quotes. The year is pivotal for the book's thesis: this is where Sol LeWitt's "Paragraphs on Conceptual Art" (Artforum, summer 1967) names "conceptual art" and declares that "the idea becomes a machine that makes the art"; where Joseph Kosuth asserts "the actual works of art are ideas" and exhibits "Art as Idea as Idea"; where Robert Ryman's stretcherless white painting is explicitly glossed by Lippard as "notable within the 'dematerialization' process"; and where the document, the catalogue, the xeroxed checklist, the artist's book (Ruscha), the magazine (0–9, Aspen 5–6), and the systems/serial statement become primary media. The entry also registers the parallel emergence of earthworks (Heizer, Oppenheim, Smithson, Long), Arte Povera (Celant's Genoa shows), and the anti-painting demonstrations of Buren, Mosset, Parmentier, and Toroni in Paris. For the vault, the year's chief yield is ENTITIES and emblematic VERBATIM QUOTES that crystallize the book's core concepts; durable concept/argument notes are owned by the essay-level sections, so they are kept short here.

## Definitions

- **Conceptual art (LeWitt, 1967)** — > "In concept art the idea or concept is the most important aspect of the work."; > "When an artist uses [a] conceptual form of art, it means that all of the planning and decisions are made beforehand and the execution is a perfunctory affair." (OCR-corrected from raw)
- **Art as idea (Kosuth)** — > "All I make are models. The actual works of art are ideas." (February, 1967)

## Arguments

(Lippard's argumentative voice is sparse this year. The one clearly authorial editorial claim:)

- **Claim:** Ryman's stretcherless, transport-light white paintings belong to the dematerialization process.
  - Reasoning structure: Lippard glosses a formal strategy (freeing painting from "a bulky, difficult-to-transport object") as an instance of her titular thesis.
  - Evidence: editorial gloss appended to the Ryman catalogue entry.
  - Source passage: > "these strategies enabled him to concentrate more ful[ly on] painting per se and freed him from a bulky, difficult-to transport object; as such, work is notable within the 'dematerialization' process."
  - Confidence: high (Lippard's own voice)
  - Recommended wiki note: feed `[[Material vs Immaterial]]` art section; no standalone argument note from a chronicle.

## Metaphors

- **"The idea becomes a machine that makes the art"** (LeWitt) — the most cited figure of the year; the artwork-as-program. (OCR garbled in raw as "mé that makes the art.")
- **NOIT / "chair of nonentity"** (John Latham) — matter as event-in-time rather than solid "particle."
- **Duchampian air** — Morris's *Twelve Bottles of Fresh Air* / *50cc of Paris Air* lineage; the immaterial readymade.

## Symbols

- **The xerox / photostat** — Kosuth's negative photostat; Kozlov's xeroxed canceled calendars; xeroxed checklists. The reproducible document as artwork-bearer.
- **The empty/invisible work** — Barry's near-invisible nylon sculptures and white-screen films; Ramsden's canvas turned face-to-wall, "UNTITLED (NONVISUAL ART)."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial** (the vault's titular axis) — directly instanced: object-bound painting → idea/model/document; the "art object" thinned toward invisibility, language, and information. This is the strongest cross-book link.
- **Art vs commodity/market** — the transport-light, model-based, documentary work implicitly resists the saleable object; this is the dematerialization-as-resistance thread the essay sections develop (and later complicate via recuperation). Sparse in 1967's raw voice; flag for synthesis, not assertion.
- **Idea vs execution** — LeWitt: planning is the art, "execution is a perfunctory affair."

## References Between Authors

- **Lippard ↔ Hayles** (interpretive parallel): Lippard's *dematerialization of the art object* runs parallel to Hayles's *disembodiment of information* — both narrate a cultural drift in which the material substrate is treated as inessential relative to idea/pattern/information, and both authors (Lippard later, Hayles throughout) work to recover or complicate the material pole. FLAG as speculative connection for human review; high interpretive value for the vault's titular axis.
- **Within-art lineage:** Duchamp (air, dust, readymade) → Morris, Ryman; Rauschenberg's "This is a portrait of Iris Clert if I say it is" telegram → Morris's "use this" telegram.
