## Executive Summary

This is the pivotal "hinge" chapter of *Psychopolitics*: Han turns his entire argument on a diagnosis of where Foucault stopped short. The thesis is that Foucault, having recognized in the late 1970s that disciplinary society no longer fit the times, turned to neoliberal forms of government but kept the wrong tools — the categories of **population** and **biopolitics**, which (Han insists) are genuine categories of *disciplinary* society and are "unsuited to describing the neoliberal regime." Foucault's failure was therefore a failure to "make the turn to psychopolitics." Han recasts Agamben's elegiac claim that Foucault's early death cut short the development of biopolitics: the early death did not deprive Foucault of the chance to *complete* biopolitics but to *abandon* it for neoliberal psychopolitics. The chapter then makes the book's central periodizing move: biopolitics concerns "the biological, the somatic, the corporal" — "a politics of the body in the fullest sense" — whereas neoliberalism, "a mutated form of capitalism," has "discovered the psyche as a productive force." This is tied to the shift to **immaterial/non-physical production** (information, programs): physical discipline gives way to mental optimization; Foucault's "docile body" has no place; cosmetic surgery and fitness studios replace disciplinary orthopaedics; "sexiness and fitness represent new economic resources." Han enlists Bernard Stiegler (biopower is "no longer the force behind our age"; replaced by "psychotechnological psychopower") but criticizes Stiegler for overemphasizing television and missing the panoptic structure of digital networks. The chapter closes on Foucault's late "**technologies of the self**": Han argues that the neoliberal regime *captures* the technology of the self — perpetual **self-optimization** becomes "a highly efficient mode of domination and exploitation." The neoliberal "entrepreneur of himself" practices **auto-exploitation** willingly and "even passionately." Power becomes subtle: it does not seize individuals directly but makes them act on themselves, so that "power relations are interiorized — and then interpreted as freedom." The closing formulation — "Self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, fall into one" — is the engine of the whole book and the precise point Han says "escaped Foucault."

## Definitions

- **Biopolitics (Han via Foucault)** — bounded to disciplinary society and capitalism's disciplinary form: "biopolitics fundamentally concerns the biological and the physical. All in all, it constitutes a politics of the body in the fullest sense." Foucault: it "socializes the body in its productive capacity."
- **Psychopolitics (Han)** — the neoliberal turn whereby capitalism, having "discovered the psyche as a productive force," targets and optimizes psychic/mental processes rather than disciplining the body; "the turn to psychopolitics… connects with the mode of operation of contemporary capitalism."
- **Technologies of the Self (Foucault, quoted)** — "intentional and voluntary actions by which men not only set themselves rules of conduct, but also seek to transform themselves, to change themselves in their singular being, and to make their life into an oeuvre that carries certain aesthetic values and meets certain stylistic criteria."
- **Self-optimization (Han)** — "the exemplary neoliberal technology of the self," which "represents nothing so much as a highly efficient mode of domination and exploitation."
- **Psychotechnological psychopower (Stiegler, via Han)** — what replaces biopower; the "telecratic" "programming industry" that reduces subjects to "impulsive creatures of consumption" and induces "mass regression."

## Arguments

- **Claim:** Foucault recognized the inadequacy of disciplinary society but failed to make the necessary turn to psychopolitics, because he retained the categories of population and biopolitics.
  - Reasoning: Biopolitics and population are categories *of* disciplinary society; applied to neoliberalism they cannot capture it. The 1978–9 lectures promised biopolitics but delivered only neoliberalism, without recasting the framework.
  - Evidence: Foucault's own self-critical admission that he "ended up talking at length… about neoliberalism" instead of biopolitics; and the disappearance of "population" and "biopolitics" from the rest of the lecture series.
  - Source passage: > "Consequently, he failed to do what the circumstances actually called for: to make the turn to psychopolitics."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Foucault failed to make the turn to psychopolitics`

- **Claim:** Agamben misreads what Foucault's early death cost us; it deprived him not of completing biopolitics but of *abandoning* it for neoliberal psychopolitics.
  - Reasoning: Han inverts Agamben's elegiac speculation; the real loss was the unrealized self-correction.
  - Source passage: > "if Foucault's early death kept him from anything, it deprived him of the chance to reevaluate his idea of biopolitics - and abandon it in order to investigate neoliberal psychopolitics."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Foucault's death deprived him of abandoning biopolitics not completing it`

- **Claim:** Today's *homines sacri* are not shut *out* of the system but shut *into* it.
  - Reasoning: A direct inversion of Agamben's exclusion-based account of bare life; under neoliberalism, capture is by inclusion, not exclusion.
  - Source passage: > "The homines sacri of today have not been shut out of the system - they have been shut into the system."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Today's homines sacri are shut into the system not out of it`

- **Claim:** Neoliberalism targets the psyche, not the body, because it is a mutated capitalism driven by immaterial production.
  - Reasoning: When information and programs (not material objects) determine capitalism, the body ceases to be the central force of production; productivity is enhanced by optimizing psychic processes, not overcoming physical resistance.
  - Source passage: > "But neoliberalism, a further development - indeed, a mutated form - of capitalism, is not primarily concerned with 'the biological, the somatic, the corporal'. It has discovered the psyche as a productive force."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Neoliberalism targets the psyche because production has become immaterial`

- **Claim:** Foucault's "docile body" is obsolete; the body is no longer disciplined but optimized (aesthetically/health-wise), becoming an economic resource.
  - Reasoning: Discipline released the body into production; now the body exits the immediate production process and becomes an object of aesthetic/health optimization. Cosmetic surgery and fitness studios replace disciplinary orthopaedics.
  - Source passage: > "Foucault's 'docile body' has no place in this production process. Cosmetic surgery and fitness studios are taking the place of disciplinary orthopaedics."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The docile body is replaced by the optimized body`

- **Claim:** Stiegler is right that biopower is no longer the dominant force, but wrong to make television the paradigmatic psychotechnical apparatus.
  - Reasoning: The literacy-vs-television opposition is a "superannuated scheme of cultural critique" that ignores genuinely digital technology (internet, social media) and the "panoptic structure of digital networks," and so misses neoliberal psychopolitics.
  - Source passage: > "the opposition between literacy and television affords a superannuated scheme of cultural critique, which fails to do justice to the digital revolution."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Stiegler's media critique misses digital psychopolitics`

- **Claim:** The neoliberal regime captures the "technology of the self"; self-optimization is a mode of domination and exploitation, not an ethics opposed to power.
  - Reasoning: Foucault's ethics of the self was developed "largely detached" from technologies of power, so it is "often assumed" to oppose domination; but neoliberalism makes the self-as-work-of-art a "beautiful but deceptive illusion" that exhausts the subject's resources.
  - Source passage: > "Foucault did not see that the neoliberal regime utterly claims the technology of the self for its own purposes"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Neoliberalism captures the technology of the self`

- **Claim:** Neoliberal power is subtle: it makes individuals act on themselves so that domination is interiorized as freedom — the fusion of freedom and exploitation that "escaped Foucault."
  - Reasoning: Rather than laying hold of individuals directly, power engineers freedom so that self-optimization and submission coincide; the subject auto-exploits while feeling free.
  - Source passage: > "Such engineering of freedom and exploitation, which occurs in order to effect self-exploitation, is what escaped Foucault."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Neoliberal power interiorizes domination as freedom`

  - Source passage: > "The self-as-a-work-of-art amounts to a beautiful but deceptive illusion that the neoliberal regime maintains in order to exhaust its resources entirely."
- **The entrepreneur of himself** — the neoliberal subject figured as self-CEO who exploits himself.

  - Source passage: > "Self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, fall into one."

  - Source passage: > "neoliberalism, a further development - indeed, a mutated form - of capitalism"

  - Source passage: > "by reducing (entmündigen) us to impulsive (triebgesteuert) creatures of consumption, it induces mass regression."

## Symbols

- **Cosmetic surgery / fitness studio** — the optimized, marketable body; replaces the prison/clinic/orthopaedic apparatus of disciplinary society.
  - Source passage: > "Cosmetic surgery and fitness studios are taking the place of disciplinary orthopaedics."
- **The docile body** — emblem of disciplinary society, now obsolete.
  - Source passage: > "Foucault's 'docile body' has no place in this production process."
- **Television (Stiegler's apparatus)** — symbol Han rejects as the paradigm of psychotechnical power, in favor of the internet / social media / panoptic digital networks.

- **Information and programs** — the immaterial products that displace material objects as what capitalism produces.
  - Source passage: > "What gets produced are not material objects, but immaterial ones - for instance, information and programs."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Biopolitics vs. Psychopolitics** — the chapter's master opposition: power over the body/population vs. power over the psyche.
  - Source: > "the turn to psychopolitics… connects with the mode of operation of contemporary capitalism."
- **Body vs. Psyche** — the biological/somatic/corporal vs. the psychic as productive force.
  - Source: > "It has discovered the psyche as a productive force."
- **Material vs. Immaterial** (production) — material objects vs. information/programs; physical force of production vs. immaterial production. (Maps to existing vault tension.)
  - Source: > "What gets produced are not material objects, but immaterial ones"
- **Discipline vs. Optimization** — physical discipline / orthopaedics vs. mental optimization / aesthetic intervention.
  - Source: > "Physical discipline has given way to mental optimization."
- **Freedom vs. Control / Exploitation** — the signature Han tension; freedom and exploitation "fall into one." (Maps to existing `Freedom vs Control`.)
  - Source: > "Self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, fall into one."
- **Allo-exploitation vs. Auto-exploitation** — external domination vs. self-exploitation done "willingly - and even passionately."
  - Source: > "the neoliberal achievement-subject engages in auto-exploitation willingly - and even passionately."
- **Shut out vs. Shut in** — Agamben's exclusionary bare life vs. Han's inclusionary capture.
  - Source: > "they have been shut into the system."
- **Technology of power vs. technology of the self** — Foucault's distinction, which Han collapses: the self's technology becomes power's technology.
  - Source: > "the neoliberal regime utterly claims the technology of the self for its own purposes"
- **Literacy vs. Television** (Stiegler) — Han names this opposition only to reject it as a "superannuated scheme of cultural critique."
  - Source: > "the opposition between literacy and television affords a superannuated scheme of cultural critique"
- **Ethics of the self vs. domination** — the *assumed* opposition (Foucault's self-care as resistance) vs. Han's claim that it is captured by power.
  - Source: > "it is often assumed that his ethics of the self stands in opposition to power and domination."
