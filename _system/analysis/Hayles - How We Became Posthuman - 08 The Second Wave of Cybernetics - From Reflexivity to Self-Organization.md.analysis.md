## Executive Summary

This is Hayles's account of the **second wave of cybernetics** (roughly 1960–1985), the period in which the discipline turned from the first wave's preoccupation with **homeostasis** to a preoccupation with **reflexivity** and **self-organization**. The chapter is organized around two Chilean biologists, Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela, and their theory of **autopoiesis** (self-making) — the claim that living systems are circularly organized networks that continuously produce the very components that produce them. Hayles uses autopoiesis to advance the three braided narratives that structure the whole book: (1) the **reification of information** and the question of whether information can exist without a body; (2) the cultural/technological construction of the **cyborg**; and (3) the transformation of the human into the **posthuman**.

The chapter's pivot is the 1959 paper "What the Frog's Eye Tells the Frog's Brain" (McCulloch, Pitts, Lettvin, Maturana), which showed that the frog's visual system does not *represent* a pre-given reality but *constructs* it. From this, Maturana drew the maxim "Everything said is said by an observer" and built an epistemology in which the **observer** is constitutive: time, causality, information, coding, and teleology are not intrinsic to living systems but are inferences drawn by an observer in his "domain of interactions." Hayles reads autopoiesis as both a radicalization and a reinscription of the **liberal humanist subject**: it relocates the ground of autonomy and individuality from C. B. Macpherson's *possessive individualism* (self-ownership) to **organizational closure** and reflexivity, yet it preserves autonomy and individuality as supreme values — even as it demotes consciousness to a mere epiphenomenon ("the liberal subject ... is literally losing its mind as the seat of identity").

Crucially for the book's central thesis, autopoietic theory *resists* the first wave's erasure of embodiment: it "draws attention to the fact that 'information' ... is an abstraction that has no basis in the physically embodied processes constituting all living entities." Hayles then traces the split between Maturana (who fixed the theory in relentless circularity and subordinated everything — evolution, reproduction, DNA — to autopoiesis) and Varela (who moved toward **embodiment**, **enaction**, and Buddhist not-self, ultimately rejecting closure). The chapter ends by pivoting toward the literary imagination of **Philip K. Dick**, whose "radical ontology" extends Maturana's "radical epistemology": where Maturana says the observer constructs the world, Dick imagines a world that exists only as long as an observer thinks it does — and his observers are unreliable, idiosyncratic, possibly psychotic.

This chapter is the realization, in Hayles's terms, of the second of her "three waves": **homeostasis → reflexivity → autopoiesis/self-organization** (with third-wave artificial life deferred to chapter 9). It is therefore the natural home for vault stubs on `[[Cybernetics]]`, `[[Systems Theory]]`, `[[Feedback Loops]]` (negated here — autopoiesis denies messages circulate in feedback loops), `[[Embodiment]]`, `[[Posthumanism]]`, and `[[Embodiment vs Information]]`.

## Key Entities

- **Humberto Maturana** — Chilean neurophysiologist; co-author of the frog's-eye paper; originator (by his own insistent claim) of autopoiesis; author of "Biology of Cognition" and (with Varela) *Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living*. Central figure of the chapter.
- **Francisco Varela** — Maturana's student and collaborator; later diverges toward enaction and embodiment; did the actual computer work on tessellation automata; co-author of *The Tree of Knowledge* and *The Embodied Mind*; Buddhist orientation.
- **Heinz von Foerster** — Austrian émigré, microwave/radar engineer in WWII Germany; principal editor of the published Macy Conference transcripts; "transitional figure linking first- and second-wave cybernetics"; author of *Observing Systems*; theorist of reflexivity and self-organizing systems.
- **Warren McCulloch** — Macy-group central player; co-author of frog's-eye paper; "remained wedded to realist epistemology"; helped von Foerster get to North America.
- **Walter Pitts**, **Jerry Lettvin** — co-authors of the frog's-eye paper.
- **Gregory Bateson** — his 1968 conference made clear that including the observer required reworking realist epistemology; held that the subjective inner world is a metaphor for the external world.
- **Lawrence Kubie** — introduced psychoanalytic reflexivity ("halitosis of the personality") at the Macy Conferences; contrast figure for Maturana's depersonalized observer.
- **Norbert Wiener** — father of cybernetics; liberal-humanist commitments; the "nightmare" of the human reduced to a cog; "Cybernetics is nothing if it is not mathematical."
- **Gordon Pask** — drew the cartoon of nested men in bowler hats for von Foerster.
- **G. Spencer-Brown** — *Laws of Form*; influence on the "operation of distinction."
- **John von Neumann** — self-reproducing machine; criticized by Maturana for modeling observers' descriptions rather than autopoietic processes.
- **Claude Shannon** (electronic rat), **Ross Ashby** (the homeostat) — first-wave artifact-builders, contrast cases.
- **Jean Piaget** — child neural development continues after birth in a social/linguistic environment (used against Maturana's inert "languaging").
- **Simon Baron-Cohen** — *Mindblindness: An Essay on Autism and Theory of Mind*; Hayles's devastating analogy that autopoietic theory describes the autistic person better than the normally responsive one.
- **Milan Zeleny** — editor of *Autopoiesis: A Theory of Living Organization*; wants to join autopoiesis with self-organizing dynamics.
- **Marvin Minsky** — *Society of Mind*; **R. Jackendoff** — *Consciousness and the Computational Mind*; used by Varela to show cognitive science deconstructing the unified self.
- **Richard Lewontin, Evelyn Fox Keller, Lily Kay, Richard Doyle** — critics of modern biology's overemphasis on DNA.
- **Philip K. Dick** — SF novelist; "radical ontology"; the chapter's exit ramp into literary analysis. (Treat as entity, not wiki note.)
- **Bernard Wolfe** — named as imagining a "mechanical human" cyborg (contrast with autopoiesis's cyborg = the state). (Entity.)
- **Albert Einstein** — the depersonalized "observer" of special relativity is Hayles's model for Maturana's observer.
- **Key texts/objects**: *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (cited "AC"), *The Tree of Knowledge*, *The Embodied Mind*, *Principles of Biological Autonomy*, *Observing Systems*; **tessellation automata** (Varela's autopoiesis simulations); **Robocop / Terminator** (named as the cyborgs autopoiesis is *not* about).

## Key Concepts

- **Autopoiesis (self-making / self-production)** — A living system is a circularly organized machine that continuously produces the components that produce it; life and autopoiesis are coextensive.
  - Source passage: > "The living organization is a circular organization which secures the production or maintenance of the components that specify it in such a manner that the product of their functioning is the very same organization that produces them"
  - Existing wiki note: (none) — relates to `[[Systems Theory]]`, `[[Cybernetics]]` stubs
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/Autopoiesis.md`

- **The Observer** — The constitutive subject of second-wave cybernetics; "Everything said is said by an observer." Information, coding, time, causality, and teleology are the observer's inferences, not intrinsic to systems. Maturana's observer is depersonalized (Einsteinian, positional) not psychoanalytic (Kubie).
  - Source passage: > "The observer is a living system and any understanding of cognition as a biological phenomenon must account for the observer and his role in it"
  - Source passage: > "In autopoietic theory, the opposite of objectivism is not subjectivism but relativism."
  - Existing wiki note: (none)
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/The Observer.md`

- **Reflexivity** — The "difficult and protean" central concept linking the two waves: the move of folding the observer back into the system observed. At the Macy Conferences it was trapped in psychoanalysis/solipsism; the second wave makes it operational via distinction and recursion.
  - Source passage: > "Central to the seriated changes connecting these second-wave developments to the first wave is the difficult and protean concept of reflexivity."
  - Source passage: > "the system's reflexive doubling back on its own representations generates the human subject as an observer."
  - Existing wiki note: (none)
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/Reflexivity.md`

- **The Three Waves of Cybernetics (homeostasis → reflexivity → self-organization)** — Hayles's periodization: first wave = homeostasis (Wiener, Ashby's homeostat); second wave = reflexivity/autopoiesis (Maturana, Varela, von Foerster); third wave = self-organization/artificial life (ch. 9).
  - Source passage: > "autopoietic theory begins to look less like the homeostasis of the first wave and more like the self-evolving programs that will be discussed in chapter 9 as exemplars of third-wave cybernetics."
  - Existing wiki note: (none) — strongly realizes `[[Cybernetics]]` stub
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/The Three Waves of Cybernetics.md`

- **Organizational Closure (operational closure)** — The foundational premise of autopoiesis: living systems are living because they close on themselves; closure (not self-possession) grounds autonomy and individuality.
  - Source passage: > "Closure and recursivity, then, play the foundational role in autopoietic theory that self-possession played in classic liberal theory."
  - Existing wiki note: (none)
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/Organizational Closure.md`

- **Structure vs. Organization** — A system's *organization* is the invariant web of relations that makes it a member of a class (conserved until death); its *structure* is the particular instantiation at a moment (changes constantly). The distinction does heavy lifting and (Hayles argues) breaks under evolution.
  - Source passage: > "Organization denotes those relations that must exist among the components of a system for it to be a member of a specific class. Structure denotes the components and relations that actually constitute a particular unity and make its organization real"
  - Existing wiki note: (none)
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/Structure vs Organization.md`

- **Structural Coupling** — How an autopoietic system is embedded in / interacts with its environment without that environment "instructing" it; the move that (allegedly) saves autopoiesis from solipsism.
  - Source passage: > "All living organisms must be structurally coupled to their environments to continue living; humans, for example, have to breathe air, drink water, eat food"
  - Existing wiki note: (none)
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/Structural Coupling.md`

- **Allopoiesis vs. Autopoiesis** — Autopoietic unities have only the goal of producing their own autopoiesis; allopoietic unities (e.g., a car) have a goal other than self-production. The distinction lets Maturana talk about power, subordination, and ethics (a person forced to function allopoietically = Wiener's "cog in a machine").
  - Source passage: > "When I drive my car, its functioning is subordinated to the goals I set for it. ... I function autopoietically, but the car functions allopoietically."
  - Existing wiki note: (none)
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/Allopoiesis vs Autopoiesis.md`

- **The Domain of Interactions / Descriptive Domain** — Each living system constructs its world through the domain of interactions made possible by its autopoietic organization; what lies outside does not exist for that system. Observers' causal, temporal, informational inferences belong to the *descriptive* domain, not to the processes.
  - Source passage: > "Each living system thus constructs its environment through the 'domain of interactions' made possible by its autopoietic organization. What lies outside that domain does not exist for that system."
  - Existing wiki note: (none)
  - Recommended action: create new note `concepts/Domain of Interactions.md`

- **Information as Observer's Inference (the body of information)** — Pivotal for the book: in autopoiesis, information, coding, and genetic codes are *not* intrinsic — there are no messages circulating in feedback loops. Autopoiesis "swerves" from the first-wave trajectory of disembodying information; information without a body does not exist except as an observer's inference.
  - Source passage: > "the notion of information refers to the observer's degree of uncertainty in his behavior within a domain of alternatives defined by him, hence the notion of information only applies within his cognitive domain"
  - Source passage: > "autopoietic theory draws attention to the fact that 'information,' so defined, is an abstraction that has no basis in the physically embodied processes constituting all living entities."

- **Enaction** — Varela's post-autopoiesis concept: cognition and perception are constituted through *perceptually guided action* and "recurrent sensory-motor patterns"; embodiment as a dynamic force, more open-ended than closure.
  - Source passage: > "Enaction sees the active engagement of an organism with the environment as the cornerstone of the organism's development."

  - Source passage: > "What is missing here ... is the detailed link between such agents and the incarnated coupling, by sensing and acting, which is essential to living"

  - Source passage: > "the foundational ground for establishing the subject's autonomy and individuality has shifted from self-possession ... Instead, these privileged attributes are based on organizational closure"

  - Source passage: > "The paradigmatic cyborg for autopoiesis is the state, not the kind of mechanical human imagined by Bernard Wolfe or Philip K. Dick."

## Definitions

- **Autopoiesis** — a circular organization that produces the components that produce it; coextensive with life.
  - > "The living organization is a circular organization which secures the production or maintenance of the components that specify it in such a manner that the product of their functioning is the very same organization that produces them"
- **Organization (Maturana's sense)** — "those relations that must exist among the components of a system for it to be a member of a specific class" (the conserved invariant).
  - > "Organization denotes those relations that must exist among the components of a system for it to be a member of a specific class."
- **Structure (Maturana's sense)** — "the components and relations that actually constitute a particular unity and make its organization real" (the changing instantiation).
  - > "Structure denotes the components and relations that actually constitute a particular unity and make its organization real"
- **Information (Maturana/Varela's sense)** — an observer-relative degree of uncertainty within a self-defined domain of alternatives; not intrinsic to systems; "Information, sensu strictu, does not exist."
  - > "the notion of information refers to the observer's degree of uncertainty in his behavior within a domain of alternatives defined by him, hence the notion of information only applies within his cognitive domain"
- **The observer** — for Maturana, the one who performs the "operation of distinction"; positional rather than personal.
  - > "the 'fundamental cognitive operation that an observer performs is the operation of distinction'"
- **Allopoietic** — a unity whose goal is something other than producing its own organization.
  - > "allopoietic unities have as their goal something other than producing their organization."
- **Enaction** — cognition/perception constituted through perceptually guided action and recurrent sensory-motor patterns.
  - > "Enaction sees the active engagement of an organism with the environment as the cornerstone of the organism's development."
- **Structural coupling** — an organism's recurrent interaction with (and dependence on) its environment that does not instruct its organization.
  - > "All living organisms must be structurally coupled to their environments to continue living"

## Arguments

- **Claim: The frog's eye constructs reality rather than representing it — blowing "a frog-sized hole in realist epistemology."**
  - Reasoning structure: Empirical → epistemological. Microelectrode data showed the frog's receptors respond selectively (small fast objects) and speak to the brain in a "highly organized and interpreted" language; therefore perception constructs, not copies, the world; therefore "Everything said is said by an observer."
  - Evidence: The 1959 frog's-eye experiment; the later color-vision work (no one-to-one map between perception and world).
  - Source passage: > "their work 'shows that the [frog's] eye speaks to the brain in a language already highly organized and interpreted instead of transmitting some more or less accurate copy of the distribution of light upon the receptors.'"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Perception Constructs Reality.md`

- **Claim: Information, coding, time, causality, and teleology are observer inferences, not intrinsic features of living systems.**
  - Reasoning: From organizational closure it follows that systems only realize their own processes in the present; messages, codes, goals, past/future are abstractions an observer adds in his descriptive domain.
  - Evidence: Maturana/Varela on genetic and nervous "coding," on information, on teleology; "Past, future and time exist only for the observer."
  - Source passage: > "In the autopoietic account, there are no messages circulating in feedback loops, nor are there even any genetic codes. These are abstractions invented by the observer to explain what is seen"
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Information and Causality Are Observer Inferences.md`

- **Claim: Information without a body does not exist — autopoiesis swerves from the first wave's disembodiment of information.**
  - Reasoning: If information is only an observer's inference and all living entities are physically embodied processes, then "information, so defined" has no basis in those processes; the body cannot be subtracted.
  - Evidence: Maturana's denial that information/coding are intrinsic; Varela's "Information, sensu strictu, does not exist."
  - Source passage: > "Autopoiesis thus swerves from the trajectory traced in chapters 3 and 4 with regard to information, insisting that information without a body does not exist other than as an inference drawn by an observer."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Information Without a Body Does Not Exist.md`
  - Note: this is the chapter's strongest contribution to the book's master thesis (`[[Embodiment vs Information]]`).

- **Claim: Autopoiesis reinscribes the liberal humanist subject even as it radicalizes it, relocating the ground of autonomy from self-possession to organizational closure.**
  - Reasoning: Macpherson's possessive individualism grounded the subject in self-ownership; autopoiesis grounds it in closure/recursivity; but both make autonomy and individuality the supreme value. Maturana's "anarchist society" of mutually respecting observers is "a reinscription of liberal humanist values."
  - Evidence: Comparison with Wiener; the closure-as-foundation argument; Maturana's social ethics.
  - Source passage: > "Closure and recursivity, then, play the foundational role in autopoietic theory that self-possession played in classic liberal theory."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Autopoiesis Reinscribes Liberal Humanism.md`

- **Claim: Autopoiesis points toward the posthuman by making consciousness a peripheral epiphenomenon.**
  - Reasoning: Self-consciousness is reduced to a linguistic effect; thinking is a fraction of autopoietic process; consciousness becomes epiphenomenal; therefore identity no longer resides in mind — the subject "is literally losing its mind as the seat of identity."
  - Evidence: Maturana's linguistic account of self-consciousness; the demotion of consciousness vs. autonomy/individualism.
  - Source passage: > "consciousness for him becomes a epiphenomenon rather than a defining characteristic of the human as an autopoietic entity."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Autopoiesis Points Toward the Posthuman.md`

- **Claim: Autopoiesis's great strength (closure/embodiment of the observer) is also its Achilles' heel — it cannot account for transformation (language, evolution).**
  - Reasoning: The same closure that gives epistemological muscle prevents the theory from handling non-circular, transformative dynamics; hence the "inert" view of language ("languaging") and the dilemma in articulating autopoiesis with evolution (either organization is conserved and evolution is effaced, or organization changes and autopoiesis is effaced).
  - Evidence: The Piaget/Baron-Cohen critique of "languaging"; the structure/organization dilemma over amoeba vs. human; "natural drift" sliding into "structural drift."
  - Source passage: > "The very closure that gives autopoietic theory its epistemological muscle also limits the theory, so that it has a difficult time accounting for dynamic interactions that are not circular in their effects."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated / Hayles's own critique)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Closure Cannot Account for Transformation.md`

- **Claim: Autopoietic theory describes the autistic person more accurately than the normally responsive one.**
  - Reasoning: By Baron-Cohen's account, the autistic child treats the environment as a mere trigger for self-closing processes; autopoiesis, in zealously building a self-enclosing entity, "ironically describes autistic individuals more accurately than it does normally responsive people."
  - Evidence: *Mindblindness*; the "trigger"/"orienting" language of autopoiesis vs. active linguistic shaping.
  - Source passage: > "Autopoietic theory, in its zeal to construct an autonomous sphere of action for self-enclosing entities, formulates a description that ironically describes autistic individuals more accurately than it does normally responsive people."
  - Confidence: medium (Hayles's pointed analogy; Interpretive but explicit)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Autopoiesis Describes the Autistic Subject.md` (review — provocative)

- **Claim: Maturana's theory cannot decide whether the autopoietic unity exists independently of the observer or is constituted by the observer's distinction (Scylla and Charybdis).**
  - Reasoning: The founding distinction between intrinsic processes and observer-attributions is unstable; "Biology of Cognition" leans relativist (the observer distinguishes the unity), "Autopoiesis: The Organization of the Living" leans absolutist (the unity constitutes itself). The theory is "vulnerable to deconstructive critique."
  - Evidence: Two essays in *AC* read against each other.
  - Source passage: > "Faced with this Scylla and Charybdis, Maturana at first steered toward relativism and then, as its dangers loomed closer, changed course and steered toward the absolutism of autopoietic processes existing in reality as such."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The Observer Paradox in Autopoiesis.md`

- **Claim: Varela diverges from Maturana toward embodiment, enaction, and Buddhist not-self, abandoning closure.**
  - Reasoning: Varela criticizes autopoiesis for going too far (applying to social systems) and not far enough (recovering a non-naive role for information); develops enaction; in *The Embodied Mind* treats the self as a story consciousness tells to ward off the fear of no-self; celebrates the dropping-away of the self rather than mourning it (contrast Wiener/Maturana's nostalgia).
  - Evidence: "Describing the Logic of the Living"; *The Embodied Mind*; Minsky's "society of mind"; Jackendoff.
  - Source passage: > "Varela, speaking in a voice now not conjoined with his teacher and mentor, celebrates the moment when the self drops away and awareness expands into a realization of its true nature."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Varela's Turn to Embodiment.md`

- **Claim: Dick's "radical ontology" extends Maturana's "radical epistemology."**
  - Reasoning: If "what we do not see does not exist" sinks from epistemology into ontology — if the observer is psychotic and idiosyncratic rather than generic — then reality itself becomes unstable. Dick imagines exactly this.
  - Evidence: Maturana's "We do not see what we do not see, and what we do not see does not exist"; the framing of Dick as obsessed with the same issues without knowing autopoiesis.
  - Source passage: > "In turning from Maturana's radical epistemology to Dick's radical ontology, we will follow our evolving stories ... into a phantasmagoric territory that continues to exist only as long as an observer thinks it does."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated, as a transition)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/From Radical Epistemology to Radical Ontology.md`

## Metaphors

- **The Frog (and the Frog's Eye/Brain)** — Master image of the chapter: the visual system that constructs rather than copies reality; "It all started with a frog"; "blew a frog-sized hole in realist epistemology."
  - > "It all started with a frog."
- **Men in Bowler Hats (infinite regress)** — Gordon Pask's cartoon of a man in whose head is another man, ad infinitum: the observer who observes himself observing; reflexivity made visible.
  - > "a man in a bowler hat, in whose head is pictured another man in a bowler hat, in whose head is yet another man in a bowler hat."
- **The Tar Baby of Self-Reflexive Language** — Maturana (like the Macy participants) keeps getting stuck in the trap of reflexive language he tries to escape.
  - > "Maturana, like the participants in the Macy Conferences, seems unable to escape from the tar baby of self-reflexive language."
- **Pulling Himself Up by His Own Bootstraps** — Trying to articulate a new epistemology using only the old, realist-laden language.
  - > "he was in the impossible position of pulling himself up by his own bootstraps, trying to articulate the new by using the only language available"
- **Scylla and Charybdis** — The two dangers (relativism vs. absolutism) between which Maturana must steer regarding the observer.
  - > "Faced with this Scylla and Charybdis, Maturana at first steered toward relativism"
- **The Brainstem of the Theory** — "This question lies coiled around the brainstem of autopoietic theory" — the observer/closure dilemma as embedded in the theory's anatomy.
  - > "This question lies coiled around the brainstem of autopoietic theory"
- **The Tree of Knowledge as an Engine that Vaporizes Contingency** — The circular book-form is read as a "textual technology" that circulates contingency away.
  - > "an engine of knowledge production that vaporizes contingency by continuously circulating it within the space of its interlocking assumptions."
- **Pulling a Rabbit from a Hat** — Von Foerster's solipsism "solution" assumes what it sets out to prove.
  - > "the argument was the philosophical equivalent to pulling a rabbit from a hat"
- **Consciousness as a Cognitive Balloon to Be Burst** — In enaction, consciousness/self is an illusion to be popped.
  - > "in enaction, consciousness is seen as a cognitive balloon that must be burst if humans are to recognize the true nature of their being."
- **Island of Life in a Sea of Entropy** — Wiener's image (invoked as the thing Varela's awareness is no longer): the bounded self against entropy.
  - > "No longer Wiener's island of life in a sea of entropy or Maturana's autonomous circularity"

## Symbols

- **The Frog / The Brain** — symbol of the constructivist nervous system; note the deliberate dropping of the possessive ("the brain") once the frog is wired into a cybernetic circuit — the body absorbed into apparatus.
  - > "Strictly speaking, the frog's brain had ceased to belong to the frog alone."
- **The Circle / Circularity** — the governing symbol of autopoiesis: circular organization, the book that ends where it begins, the recursive observer; symbol of closure and (Hayles's critique) of contingency-effacement.
  - > "the form of the book recapitulates the circularity of autopoietic theory."
- **The State (as paradigmatic cyborg)** — autopoiesis's symbol of the living social machine, displacing Robocop/Terminator.
  - > "In autopoietic theory, the machine of interest is much more likely to be the state than Robocop or Terminator."
- **The Boxes/Cartoon Figures in *The Tree of Knowledge*** — visual device marking the Maturana/Varela divergence (Maturana older, heavy glasses); symbol of the two diverging voices.
  - > "Each box has a cartoon figure representing the speaker. Maturana's figure wears heavy glasses and is noticeably older than Varela's"
- **The Toilet (structure/organization analogy)** — Maturana/Varela's "strangely inappropriate" mechanical analogy for a biological distinction; Hayles uses it as a symbol of the theory's strain.
  - > "A toilet's parts can be made of wood or plastic; these different materials correspond to differences in structure."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Homeostasis vs. Reflexivity vs. Self-Organization** — the three-wave periodization itself; first-wave behavior/homeostasis vs. second-wave reflexivity/process vs. third-wave self-organization.
  - > "autopoietic theory begins to look less like the homeostasis of the first wave and more like the self-evolving programs"
- **Observer-Constructed vs. Observer-Independent Reality** — does the observer's distinction create the autopoietic unity, or does the unity constitute itself? The unresolved core paradox.
  - > "If the theory says that the observer creates the system by drawing distinctions, it risks undercutting the ontological primacy of organizational closure."
- **Embodiment vs. Information** — the book's master tension, here taking the form: information as disembodied observer-inference vs. the physically embodied processes that constitute all living entities.
  - > "'information' ... is an abstraction that has no basis in the physically embodied processes constituting all living entities."
- **Closure vs. Transformation (circularity vs. evolution)** — the conserved circle of autopoiesis vs. the proliferating lines of evolutionary descent.
  - > "Either organization is conserved and evolutionary change is effaced, or organization changes and autopoiesis is effaced."
- **Autonomy vs. Allopoietic Subordination** — the autopoietic system's drive to self-production vs. being forced to function for another's goals (Wiener's "cog in a machine").
  - > "a system that is capable of autopoiesis being forced instead to function allopoietically, especially for humans."
- **Reinscription vs. Radicalization of Liberal Humanism** — autopoiesis simultaneously preserves and contests the Enlightenment subject.
  - > "the individualism and autonomy that Maturana champions challenge the premises embodied in liberal subjectivity at least as much as they reinscribe those premises."
- **Maturana vs. Varela (closure vs. enaction; nostalgia vs. celebration)** — the teacher clinging to circular closure vs. the student embracing embodiment, openness, and the joyful dissolution of the self.
  - > "While Varela moved on to other issues and ways of thinking about them, Maturana continued to occupy essentially the same position"
- **Objectivism vs. Relativism (not Subjectivism)** — Maturana's distinctive move: the opposite of objectivism is relativism, not subjectivism; the observer is positional, not personal.
  - > "In autopoietic theory, the opposite of objectivism is not subjectivism but relativism."
- **Radical Epistemology vs. Radical Ontology** — Maturana stops at "the observer constructs the world I know"; Dick pushes to "the world ceases to be when unobserved."
  - > "Will the observation that 'what we do not see does not exist' sink deep into the structure of reality, undermining not only our ability to know but the ability of the world to be?"
