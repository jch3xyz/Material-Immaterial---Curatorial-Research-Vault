## Executive Summary

A short, dense catalogue essay (1992, written for the exhibition "Digital Photography," first published in French) in which Ascott argues that photography has crossed a threshold from a "stable medium" into a "post-photographic practice." This is Ascott's telematic theory applied to a single medium: the digital photograph is no longer a fixed material image that the viewer *absorbs*, but a permeable, layered datum inside *hypermedia* that the viewer *immerses* in, *navigates*, and — crucially — *transforms*. The essay re-stages Ascott's career-long thesis (the artwork as an open, behavioural system completed by the participating spectator) for the digital image: authorship is distributed "across time" to a "participating viewer" who can "distort, collage, dismember, fractionise, and in every way transform the image at will." Ascott explicitly historicizes this against Walter Benjamin's "work of art in the age of mechanical reproduction," naming digital co-production the "second order of reproduction." He recasts the old photographic anxiety about truth, reality, and "faking" as obsolete: in a culture where "reality is built by us," the relevant question is not *what* photographs mean but *how* they mean, and "the lust for verisimilitude gives way to the love of modeling." The piece introduces (or instantiates) several of the book's keystone polarities — **absorption vs immersion**, **material image vs immaterial apparition**, **appearance vs the constructed image-world**, and the triad of **connectivity / interactivity / transformation** — and ends with a humanist reassurance: that "the computerization of photographic practice is profoundly human at base," confounding critics who see only "a technoculture of progressive dehumanisation."

This is primary Ascott (an essay chapter, NOT Shanken's ch.04 introduction); all quotes are attributable to Ascott. Note: the OCR splices the bibliographic colophon ("Originally published in French in Digital Photography…") mid-sentence into the body around lines 47-53, and breaks "Watzlawick" into "zlawick"/"Watzlawick" — quote only clean substrings.

## Definitions

- **Post-photographic practice** — practice in which the photograph ceases to be a stable, finished material object and becomes an unstable, layered, viewer-transformable datum within hypermedia.
  - > "It's that photography as a stable medium is giving way to a practice that celebrates instability, uncertainty, incompleteness, and transformation."

- **Post-photographic technology (the three image-operations)** — Ascott's tripartite definition of what the technology does to images: it *captures* ("seen" images from cameras), *constructs* ("unseen" data from remote sensors and data banks), and *generates* (from raw numbers).
  - > "This post-photographic technology captures images ('seen' images from still, video, and other cameras), constructs images ('unseen' data from remote sensors and data banks), and generates images (from raw numbers); it treats them, stores them, associates them, disburses them, and transmits them into a media flow that is—in every serious sense—unending and ubiquitous."

- **Hypermedia** (note 2) — "Hypermedia provides for a nonsequential inputting and navigation of multimedia data (photo, graphics, text, video, audio)."

- **Documentum / monumentum** — the doubled status of the classical photograph (etymological play: *documentum* from *docere*, to teach/give proof, touching *decere*/docile).
  - > "The photograph always lingered between documentum and monumentum, between the official piece of evidence and the legitimised record."

## Arguments

- **Claim: Digital co-production is a second order of reproduction, a stage beyond Benjamin's mechanical reproduction.**
  - Reasoning structure: periodization. Benjamin's age of mechanical reproduction reproduced the image; the digital age lets the viewer *co-produce and transform* the image itself, distributing authorship across time.
  - Evidence: the catalogue artists; CD-I interactivity; scanners.
  - Source passage: > "This is the second order of reproduction, a stage beyond Walter Benjamin's 'work of art in the age of mechanical reproduction' [Benjamin 1969]. This is the stage of co-production."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Digital co-production is a second order of reproduction beyond Benjamin`

- **Claim: The photographic truth/faking debate is obsolete because reality is constructed, not represented.**
  - Reasoning structure: the worry about "truth to appearance" and digital retouching presupposes a real that photography fails or fakes; Ascott rejects the premise — "reality is built by us" — so the interesting question shifts from *meaning* to *how meaning is announced* and how realities are *modeled*.
  - Evidence: the moved Giza pyramid; Blonsky's "semiotics of the third kind"; Watzlawick's radical constructivism.
  - Source passage: > "the lust for verisimilitude gives way to the love of modeling."
  - Source passage: > "But now, who cares?… because we have no trust whatsoever in either journalism or art as purveyors of reality".
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Post-photographic practice abandons verisimilitude for the modeling of reality` (or fold into the `Radical Constructivism` concept).

- **Claim: In post-photographic practice the power of transformation passes to the viewer.**
  - Reasoning structure: the technical openness of the digitized image (manipulable, recombinable) plus distribution (CD-I, scanners) repositions the viewer from absorber to manipulator/co-author.
  - Evidence: Berger ("Once digitized… can be endlessly manipulated, recombined… and transformed"); the public's ability to "distort, collage, dismember, fractionise."
  - Source passage: > "the repositioning of the viewer, to her empowerment as a manipulator of that image, as the one in whose hands the destiny of the image may lie."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: EXTEND `Reciprocal Communication` / `Art as Experiential Technology` with an Ascott section; or `arguments/The digital image distributes authorship to the participating viewer`.

- **Claim: The computerization of photographic practice is profoundly human, not dehumanising.**
  - Reasoning structure: rebuttal to technophobic critics; the catalogue artists use high technology to make human themes (family, ecology, oppression) MORE vivid, not less.
  - Evidence: the four artists' grounded, present-tense subject matter.
  - Source passage: > "They confound those critics of the electronic age who see only a technoculture of progressive dehumanisation."
  - Source passage: > "the assurance that the computerization of photographic practice is profoundly human at base."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Computerized photographic practice is profoundly human not dehumanising` (a clean Ascott humanist counter to the technoculture-critique; resonates with the book's later "love in the telematic embrace").

## Metaphors

- **The aquatic / fluid metaphor** — Ascott's recurring figure for the new condition: data, images, and meaning *float*, *flood*, and *flow*. "The metaphor for this practice is emphatically aquatic." Images exist "in a sea of interdata"; meanings, once "flowing in data space," dissolve into "fuzzy ambiguities, darting associations." (Anticipates Ascott's later "the future will be moist" / moistmedia.)
  - > "The metaphor for this practice is emphatically aquatic, notwithstanding the necessary computers, imaging peripherals, and optical and magnetic storage devices."
  - > "data images exist always and at all times in a sea of interdata, just as the hypertext exists within the boundless domain of the intertext".

- **The window vs the doorway** — the analogue photograph as a window onto actualities; the digital as a doorway into potentialities. A spatial metaphor for absorption→immersion.
  - > "The window onto a world of analogue actualities gives way to the doorway into a world of digital potentialities."

- **The permeable / woven data field** — the digital photographic image as "a lightly woven structure," "a permeable data field," Parada's "electronic loom." Permeability = openness to other image-sources.
  - > "now we are seeing the emergence in digital photography of a permeable data field… whose image is a lightly woven structure, open to other image sources".
  - > (Parada) "an electronic loom . . . into which I can weave other material".

- **Gold vs silver** — analogue ("silver" grains) vs digital; "artists to whom silver is no longer precious." "This is not a debate about the relative merits of gold and silver."

- **"CD killed the video star"** — a pop-cultural figure (riffing "Video Killed the Radio Star") for the medium succession into interactive disc culture.

- **The ink-jet printer as metaphor and instrument** — "the ink-jet printer is as much a metaphor as an instrument" — its crisp precision is what licenses semantic uncertainty and fuzzy shifts of meaning.

- **The artificial observer / "the eye of the neural net"** — the coming AI that "will surely become a part of the observing system." A forward-looking figure (Ascott's later "double gaze" / technoetic perceiving machine).
  - > "just around the corner… is the artificial observer, the eye of the neural net, the artificial intelligence that will surely become a part of the observing system."

- **The moved Giza pyramid** (National Geographic, Feb 1982) — the emblematic "digitally moved" image; the origin-anecdote of the truth-anxiety Ascott dismisses. ("Stewart Brand saw the Giza pyramid move.")

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Absorption vs Immersion** — classical absorption of a bounded image vs immersion-and-navigation in a hypermedia field. (Candidate new tension; see Key Concepts.)
- **Material vs Immaterial** — "the material image on paper" vs "the immaterial apparition at the computer interface"; "Photography both affirms gravity and gravitates around immateriality." (EXTEND existing.)
- **Both/and vs Either/or** — Ascott explicitly refuses a clean opposition: "It's simultaneously a case of both 'both/and' as well as 'either/or.'" He warns it would be "false to set up the different aspects of photography, digital or analogue, in complete and total opposition." (A productive self-qualification — the analogue is *absorbed within the digital discourse*, not abolished.)
- **Verisimilitude vs Modeling** — truth-to-appearance vs construction of realities ("the lust for verisimilitude gives way to the love of modeling").
- **Stability vs Instability / Substance vs Process** — the stable medium vs a practice celebrating instability; "between process and substance, activity and object."
- **Routine/docility vs the break of routine** — Feyerabend: clarity holds "as long as people remain docile"; post-photographic practice breaks the routine of seeing. (Resonates with McLuhan's narcosis/numbness and the "routines of seeing.")
