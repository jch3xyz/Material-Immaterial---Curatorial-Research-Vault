## Executive Summary

This chapter is Ascott's 1993 conceptual brief / building program for the proposed **Ars Electronica Center (AEC)** in Linz, Austria — a "museum of the twenty-first century" he describes not as a museum at all but as a "dynamic organism," a fluid "**datapool**." Originally published in German in the *Ars Electronica Center Projektstudie*, vol. 3 (Linz, 1993), it is at once a curatorial manifesto, an institutional design document, and a floor-by-floor architectural/programmatic plan with extensive name-checked artist/artwork references and technical infrastructure notes (ISDN, EARN, satellite, networking).

For the vault, the chapter's primary importance is that it is the **keystone primary-text anchor for Ascott's canonical tetrad/pentad of the electronic arts**: in its opening it states outright that "**Connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence are the key elements of the electronic arts in the 1990s**," and then defines each term in turn. Earlier Ascott chapters (12, 17, 18) flagged this triad/tetrad as "Ascott's later canonical triad" and explicitly recommended **deferring the canonical anchor to a denser later chapter** — this is that chapter. It is therefore the natural home for a tetrad/pentad concept note (or for a set of `Connectivity`, `Immersion`, `Interaction`, `Transformation` notes anchored here, with `Emergence` extended). The chapter is also the strongest primary-text anchor for **Interactivity / Interaction**, for the **art-as-process-not-object** thesis (extending `Object vs System` / `Systems Esthetics` / `Material vs Immaterial`), for **post-biological culture / the post-biological era** (a durable Ascott coinage that recurs across his late work), for the new biological/artificial-life metaphor-set displacing the 1980s quantum-physics/chaos/cosmology one, and for the **museum/institution as a living organism** rethinking of the cultural institution. It also strongly EXTENDS the Burnham-realized committed stubs `New Media Art` and `Art as Experiential Technology`, and cross-links to McLuhan (the building's "electronic central nervous system," telepresence/global community → `The Global Village`), to Hayles/Haraway (the post-biological / artificial-life networked body, animats and progranimals), and to O'Gieblyn (telemeditation, telematic healing, "the relationship between technology and consciousness is on the agenda" → re-enchantment / the later technoetics arc). The text is OCR-scanned and largely clean in its essay sections; the long back-matter is a list of artists, works, and infrastructure, useful mainly for entities/context, not for concept-bearing quotes.

## Arguments

- **Claim: Older cultural institutions cannot adapt to the electronic arts; a fundamentally new kind of institution — a fluid, organic system, not a museum — is required.**
  - Reasoning structure: the electronic arts embody open-endedness, immersion, and interactivity from which new art emerges; a fixed/static institution cannot host emergence; therefore a "dynamic organism" / matrix is needed.
  - Evidence: the datapool design; the contrast with "traditional museum, or school, workshop, studio, production facility, or art centre."
  - Source passage: > "Older cultural institutions cannot adapt to the needs of the new electronic arts—radical rethinking is needed to guarantee public involvement and cultural viability."
  - Source passage: > "Thus the institution that is required must constitute a fluid and flowing system, supporting these qualities of open-endedness and emergence, and encouraging connectivity at all levels."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The museum of the future must be a fluid organism not a static institution` (cross-link `The Datapool`, `Object vs System`, `Exhibition as Medium`).

- **Claim: Art is no longer a finite object but a process and a system — and this demands a new behaviour from the public, who must immerse themselves rather than observe and judge from a distance.**
  - Reasoning structure: if art is a fluid stream of data configurations, the contemplative-distance model of spectatorship fails; the public must "plunge into the datapool," becoming participants.
  - Evidence: the datapool behaviours; "no longer to observe, stand back, look from a distance, and judge, but to plunge into the datapool."
  - Source passage: > "This in turn calls for new behaviours on the part of the viewing public: no longer to observe, stand back, look from a distance, and judge, but to plunge into the datapool, immerse themselves in its fluid changeability, share in its swirling transformations, navigate its knowledge bases, dive to its depths of meaning."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: extend `Object vs System` / `Systems Esthetics` / `Material vs Immaterial`; possibly `arguments/Art is process and system not finite object` (cross-link the prior ch.17 argument `Art is all process all system not an object`).

- **Claim: The era of mimetic (representational) art has ended; art's role is now to create worlds and invent alternative realities, with the observer as active participant.**
  - Reasoning structure: simulations imitate surfaces, but the new goal is to emulate (not imitate) life and co-evolve with nature; representation gives way to world-making, from which art emerges.
  - Evidence: "the end of the era of mimetic art"; "no longer to imitate life but to emulate it."
  - Source passage: > "Despite the recent popularity of computer simulations of the external surfaces of the world, we are witnessing the end of the era of mimetic art."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Art has moved from representing the world to creating new worlds` (cross-link `Art as Experiential Technology`, the world-making / virtuality cluster).

- **Claim: The contradictions/tensions of such an institution are not weaknesses but complementary and energizing — its strength lies in holding opposites together.**
  - Reasoning structure: a list of oppositions (public vs advanced, local vs global, technological vs spiritual, digital vs human, immaterial output vs precise engineering) is reframed as complementarity; "the tensions are energising."
  - Evidence: the "Tensions and Strengths" section's enumerated oppositions.
  - Source passage: > "However, these oppositions are in fact complementary, and the tensions are energising. It is this complementarity and energy that will fuel the internal dynamic of the Center and give it cultural cohesion and artistic significance."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: record inside `The Datapool` / a tension note (see Tensions); possibly `arguments/The tensions of the electronic-arts institution are complementary and energising`.

- **Claim: A parallel exists between the emergence of proto-organisms from primeval rock pools and the emergence of a post-biological culture from the planet's pools of data.**
  - Reasoning structure: the datapool metaphor is grounded in an evolutionary analogy — data pools are the primeval soup of post-biological life.
  - Evidence: the explicit rock-pool / data-pool parallel.
  - Source passage: > "The datapool metaphor reminds us of the parallel between the emergence of proto-organisms from primeval rock pools and the emergence of the constituents of a post-biological culture from the pools of data forming on the planet today."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: fold into `The Datapool` and `Post-Biological Era`; cross-link `Emergence`, `Artificial Life`.

## Metaphors

- **The Datapool / the data sea / tributaries** — the master metaphor: data flows "endlessly transformed through human interaction," flowing "on into other domains, other pools, other tributaries of the data sea." (Candidate metaphor note `The Datapool`.)
- **Swimming / plunging / diving / "every toe dipped in to test the water"** — the bodily register of engagement in the datapool: "Splashing, paddling, plunging, wading, diving, fast crawl and dog paddle all have their place" as "descriptions of the variable degrees of physical, intellectual, and emotional engagement." Keeps "humour in the perspective."
  - Source passage: > "We hope that the design and programmes of AEC will be such that every toe dipped in to test the water will find the artistic temperature warm enough and fresh enough to invite further immersion in the datapool."
- **Primeval rock pool / primeval soup of data** — the evolutionary analogy: post-biological culture emerges from data pools as proto-organisms emerged from rock pools.
- **The osmotic membrane** — the entrance "separating the Center from the street" is "an osmotic membrane," a permeable biological boundary (the building-as-organism image).
- **The electronic central nervous system** — the building's cabling as a nervous system (McLuhan resonance).
- **Seedbed / test-bed / fertile ground** — the institution as "a seedbed for the future, a test-bed of present inquiry and experiment, and a fertile ground of historical reference."
- **The data drain ("waste data")** — at the bottom of the datapool, a feature carrying "computer/video images of 'waste data' leaving the system," activated by the visitor. (A wry, durable image of data ecology.)
- **The televator (Earth slipping away)** — the elevator with floor/ceiling screens giving "the impression of the museum, Linz, Austria, Europe, and the planet Earth slipping away as the elevator ascends" — a telepresence/cosmic-scale image (cross-link McLuhan's global village; the cosmic register of network consciousness).

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Object vs System / Material vs Immaterial** — explicit: "art not as finite object but as process and system"; and the listed opposition "its output is fluid and immaterial, but its engineering and construction are precise." Anchors an Ascott section in `Object vs System` and `Material vs Immaterial`.
  - Source passage: > "its output is fluid and immaterial, but its engineering and construction are precise"
- **Representation vs World-Making (mimesis vs emulation)** — "no longer to imitate life but to emulate it"; the end of mimetic art. (Candidate cross-link to `Metaphor vs Literalism`, or a new `Mimesis vs Emulation` / `Representation vs World-Making` tension.)
- **Technological/scientific vs poetic/spiritual** — listed opposition: "its references are technological and scientific, but its ethos is poetic and spiritual." (Seed of the later technoetics convergence; cross-link `Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment`.)
- **Local vs Global (community vs network)** — "it is rooted in the community, but its networks reach out to the world." (Cross-link `The Global Village`; resonates with prior ch.17 `Isolation vs Convergence`.)
- **Digital/electronic vs human/biological** — "its media orientation is electronic, but its perspective is biological"; "its processes are digital, but its attitudes are profoundly human."
- **Technology of power vs democratic/integrative effects** — "it uses the technology of power, but its effects are democratic and integrative." (A self-aware nod to the power of the medium — cross-link, cautiously, the McLuhan/Han power-of-the-medium cluster.)
- **Observer-at-a-distance vs immersed-participant** — the spectatorship tension: judging from a distance vs plunging into the datapool. (Cross-link `Detachment vs Participation` (McLuhan), `Reciprocal Communication`.)

> Ascott resolves all of these explicitly as complementarities ("these oppositions are in fact complementary, and the tensions are energising"), which is itself a notable move — he frames productive tension as the institution's engine.
