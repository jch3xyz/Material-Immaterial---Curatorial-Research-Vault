## Executive Summary

This is the **techno-humanism chapter** of *Homo Deus*. Harari opens Part III's payoff move: the new religions of the twenty-first century "will emerge from research laboratories," not from "the caves of Afghanistan or from the madrasas of the Middle East" ‚Äî the most important place "from a religious perspective" is "Silicon Valley." He divides the coming techno-religions into two types: **techno-humanism** (the conservative creed treated here) and **data religion / Dataism** (deferred to the next chapter). Techno-humanism still "sees humans as the apex of creation," but concedes that *Homo sapiens* "has run its historical course"; its solution is to "use technology in order to create Homo deus ‚Äî a much superior human model" via "genetic engineering, nanotechnology and brain‚Äìcomputer interfaces." Its motive is the **decoupling of intelligence from consciousness**: because "non-conscious intelligence is developing at breakneck speed, humans must actively upgrade their minds if they want to stay in the game." Techno-humanism is framed as "an updated variant on the old dreams of evolutionary humanism," which "already a century ago called for the creation of superhumans" ‚Äî but pursued now "far more peacefully" than Hitler's selective breeding.

The chapter's distinctive contribution is the **"ocean of consciousness" image**: science has mapped only "two tiny sections" of the mental spectrum ‚Äî "the sub-normative and the WEIRD" (Western, educated, industrialised, rich, democratic) ‚Äî while the full spectrum "may be infinite." Harari analogizes the unmapped mind to the electromagnetic spectrum beyond visible light, cites the Henrich/Heine/Norenzayan 2010 WEIRD study, invokes lost premodern states of consciousness (shamans, monks, ascetics), Neanderthal brains, and ‚Äî via **Thomas Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"** ‚Äî the irreducibly alien subjective worlds of bats and whales. "We are living on a tiny island of consciousness within a giant ocean of alien mental states."

Two danger arguments follow. First (**"I Smell Fear"**): because we lack a map of the super-normative, mind-upgrading will follow "political needs and market forces," upgrading abilities "the current economic and political system needs" while downgrading others ‚Äî Harari traces the historical atrophy of smell, attention, and dreaming as the system reshaped minds for large-scale cooperation, and warns the US army's "attention helmet" may strip empathy and the capacity for doubt. "We may successfully upgrade our bodies and our brains, while losing our minds in the process." Second (**"The Nail on Which the Universe Hangs"**): techno-humanism "sanctifies the human will," yet the same technology that would upgrade us can **reshape and engineer the will itself** ‚Äî Ritalin, Prozac, Cipralex, the gay-conversion thought experiment ‚Äî dissolving the humanist belief in an "authentic self." Once "we could design and redesign our will, we could no longer see it as the ultimate source of all meaning and authority." This is the **self-undermining dilemma of techno-humanism**: it deifies the will and then builds the means to manufacture it, leaving the will "just another designer product." The chapter ends by handing the baton to the bolder techno-religion that "seeks to sever the humanist umbilical cord altogether" ‚Äî **Dataism**, which "venerates neither gods nor man ‚Äî it worships data."

This chapter is dense with cross-book resonance: the hard problem / "we don't really understand the mind"; Nagel's bat and qualia; the WEIRD critique of Western psychology's universal claims; the engineering of desire and the death of authenticity (which rhymes hard with Han on the self and with O'Gieblyn/Hayles on the liberal humanist subject); and the explicit naming of **Dataism**, already a canonical note (Han) and the subject of the next chapter.

  - Source passage: > "Just as socialism took over the world by promising salvation through steam and electricity, so in the coming decades new techno-religions may conquer the world by promising salvation through algorithms and genes."
