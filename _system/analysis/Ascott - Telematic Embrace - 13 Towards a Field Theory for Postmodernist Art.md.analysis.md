## Executive Summary

This is the hinge essay of *Telematic Embrace* — the bridge from Ascott's 1960s cybernetic/behaviourist art to his 1980s telematic/network art. Ascott proposes a **field theory of art** (more precisely "a field theory of art and consciousness") to replace the dominant **formalist modernist aesthetic**. The new paradigm "takes as its focus not form but behaviour"; the artwork is not a one-way message to be decoded but "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer)," a "field of psychic probability, highly entropic," in which "the final responsibility for meaning lies with the viewer." The essay is the clearest statement of Ascott's central, career-spanning move: from the artwork-as-object to the artwork-as-system, and from the spectator as passive receiver to the **participator** who completes the work. It transposes the participatory, feedback logic of his cybernetic art into a "connective paradigm" that he names *postmodernist* and explicitly aligns with cybernetics, the new physics (Heisenberg, indeterminacy, quantum observer), Norbert Wiener's information theory ("transmission of alternatives"), Sufi thought, the new linguistics (Eco's semiosis), psi research (Vallée), and Eastern spiritual models of consciousness (Capra's *Tao of Physics*). It announces a transition "from materialist culture" toward "the spiritual in art" — the seed of his later technoetics — and ends on a key methodological commitment: networking as "the antithesis of reductionism," and inconclusiveness as "the essence of the postmodernist condition." For the vault this chapter is the strongest single primary-text anchor (alongside ch. 06's behaviourist material) for EXTENDING the Burnham-realized art cluster — `New Media Art`, `Art as Experiential Technology`, `Reciprocal Communication`, `Systems Esthetics`, `Object and System`, `Material vs Immaterial` — from the Ascott (participatory/behavioural) pole, and for cross-linking to McLuhan (networking/connectivity), Hayles/Haraway (the cybernetic observer, the dissolving boundary), and O'Gieblyn (re-enchantment / art-and-consciousness / the spiritual turn).

## Definitions

- **Field theory of art** — "Art does not reside in the artwork alone, nor in the activity of the artist alone, but is understood as a field of psychic probability, highly entropic, in which the viewer is actively involved ... by interrogating and interacting with the system 'artwork' to generate meaning." (Ascott; explicit functional definition.)
- **The connective paradigm (by negation)** — defined by what formalist modernism "was not": > "It was not connective, inclusive, transactional, associative, referential, interactive, changeable, discontinuous, multilayered, impure, ambiguous; it ignored autobiographical data and questions of personality." Postmodernist art is the encompassing of these qualities.
- **Information (Wiener, via Ascott)** — the cybernetic sense Ascott imports: > "Norbert Wiener recognized the prime concept of semantic alternatives, seeing the transmission of information as 'impossible save as a transmission of alternatives.'" (Bears on the vault's multi-author `Information` note.)
- **The participator** — the viewer redefined operationally: not a receiver completing a message but a generator of meaning "of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system."

## Arguments

- **Claim: Art should be re-founded on behaviour, not form — as a probabilistic field where the viewer generates meaning.**
  - Reasoning structure: replaces the sender→receiver information model with a two-system matrix; meaning is negotiated, not transmitted; therefore the participator is constitutive, not incidental.
  - Evidence: the matrix-of-two-behaviours formulation; Eco's "gambling on the possibility of semiosis"; the "highly entropic" field.
  - Source passage: > "the viewer is actively involved, not in an act of closure in the sense of completing a discrete message from the artist (a passive process) but by interrogating and interacting with the system 'artwork' to generate meaning."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Art is a behavioural field in which the viewer generates meaning` (Ascott).

- **Claim: Formalist modernism, despite claiming to overthrow the Newtonian worldview, is itself "totally Newtonian" — linear, irreversible, progress-bound — and so "faux modernist."**
  - Reasoning structure: parallels art's "arrow to the future" with the discredited linear time-series of classical physics and with the irreversibility of evolutionary biology; the new physics (Heisenberg, indeterminacy) refutes the linear model, so an art still organized on it is not truly modern.
  - Evidence: Heisenberg ("the distribution of possible futures of [a] system"); the "one-way street" / "arrow" image; rejection of classical perspective as only a partial break.
  - Source passage: > "The claim of formalist modernism to have dispensed with the Newtonian worldview was correct only to some extent within its own formal terms ... but the simplest analysis of its cultural form shows a totally Newtonian structure."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Formalist modernism is Newtonian and therefore faux modernist`.

- **Claim: A field theory of art finds its language not in art terms but in cybernetics, the new physics, semiotics, psi research, and Eastern spiritual models of consciousness.**
  - Reasoning structure: the new paradigm needs "new terms, a new language, a new set of metaphors"; because the shift is from an information/intentionalist model to an associative one, the relevant models come from outside art — Wiener's cybernetics, the quantum observer, Eco's semiosis, Vallée's physics of information / psi, Capra's *Tao of Physics*.
  - Evidence: the explicit roll-call of Wiener, Heisenberg, Eco, Vallée, Capra, Allport/Kilpatrick.
  - Source passage: > "The new understanding of art calls for new terms, a new language, a new set of metaphors. We shall not find these terms in formalist modernism; we shall not find them among 'art' terms at all."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The language of a field theory of art comes from cybernetics physics and consciousness research`.

- **Claim: The quantum observer and the art participator are the same structural figure — the observer's consciousness is part of what is observed.**
  - Reasoning structure: imports the measurement-problem insight (observer participates in the observed) and the cybernetic insight (information = transmission of alternatives) to ground the participator ontologically; the artwork-as-matrix interlocks the previously unrelated psychic fields of artist and observer.
  - Evidence: "The apparatus includes the consciousness of the participators"; Wiener on alternatives.
  - Source passage: > "In both physics and cybernetics, the importance of the observer as participator in creating meaning is recognized. The physicist knows that his observational behaviour and consciousness are a part of what he is observing."
  - Confidence: high (interpretive on the equivalence framing; the quoted physics/cybernetics claims are directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The art participator is the quantum observer the consciousness is part of the observed`.

- **Claim: Networking is the antithesis of reductionism; an authentic art theory must remain inconclusive.**
  - Reasoning structure: a connective, multidimensional network of ideas cannot be closed without betraying its form; inconclusiveness is therefore not a failure but constitutive of the postmodernist condition.
  - Evidence: the essay performs this — it "lay[s] out the threads for a multidimensional network of ideas" rather than tying up loose ends.
  - Source passage: > "any field theory of art must be inconclusive. I have not sought to tie up loose ends, so much as to lay out the threads for a multidimensional network of ideas. Networking is the antithesis of reductionism"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Networking is the antithesis of reductionism and the postmodernist condition is inconclusive`.

## Metaphors

- **Art as an arrow / one-way street rushing to the future** — Ascott's figure for formalist modernism's linear temporality: > "Formalist modernism saw itself moving through time like an arrow, directed to the future with simple, clear, and direct messages on its shaft". (durable; the antagonist image of the essay.)
- **The artwork as matrix / field of psychic interplay** — the central positive figure: a matrix between two behaviours, a "field of psychic probability, highly entropic." (the field-theory image itself.)
- **"Mirror, mirror on the wall" (the vertical painting plane)** — the painting as a fixed, answer-giving mirror reflecting given values: > "the painting plane for me can never quite overcome its identification with 'Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all'". (paired with the horizontal table-top/arena counter-figure.)
- **The table-top / arena vs the painting on the wall** — horizontal surface as cosmic, transactional, bird's-eye, open; vertical surface as orthodox, assertive, closed. (Ascott's signature spatial metaphor; "the painting on the wall looks back only at us".)
- **Closing the loop** — a cybernetic/feedback figure used to mean reconnecting postmodern art to early modernism's pre-materialist spiritual aspirations: > "the postmodernist artist is simply closing the loop to the early aspirations of formalist modernism". (connects the feedback vocabulary to the spiritual turn.)
- **Laying out the threads of a network (not tying up loose ends)** — the essay's self-description and method-as-form figure.

## Symbols

- **The horizontal surface / table-top** — recurring object-symbol of participatory, transactional art (as opposed to the vertical "wall").
- **The web / net** — Ascott lists his recurring terminology: "transaction, interplay, net, web, reversibility, association, psychism, multiple meaning, and connectivity" — the net/web is the proto-telematic symbol of the connective paradigm.

- > "In my own work, I recognize the primacy of the arena, the horizontal surface, and most specifically the table-top, as the focus for the processes of mod[elling], imaging, and transacting that constitute my art."
- > "While the painting on the wall looks back only at us, reflecting given values, assertive of orthodoxy ... the arena and the table-top have a more cosmic outlook, facing up, not only to us, but to the whole universe."
- > "I wonder how video, for example, might change and break out of its formal prison if, rather than aping painting in the vertical, it were made to be viewed on the horizontal plane, with its technology extended to permit a more transactional, participative behaviour on the part of the observer."
- > "This essay identifies a search for a field theory of art, which should perhaps be called a field theory of art and consciousness. Its terminology frequently employs ideas of transaction, interplay, net, web, reversibility, association, psychism, multiple meaning, and connectivity."
- > "But, finally, like this essay, any field theory of art must be inconclusive. I have not sought to tie up loose ends, so much as to lay out the threads for a multidimensional network of ideas. Networking is the antithesis of reductionism, and to be inconclusive in this sense is the essence of the postmodernist condition."
