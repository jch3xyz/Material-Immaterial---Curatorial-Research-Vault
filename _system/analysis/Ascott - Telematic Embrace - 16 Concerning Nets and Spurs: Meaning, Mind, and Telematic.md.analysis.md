Originally published in *Artificial Intelligence in the Arts*, ed. Richard Kriesche (Graz: Steirischer Herbst, 1985). Source pages 202–211. This is a 1985 essay — chronologically it sits at the hinge between Ascott's 1960s cybernetic/behaviourist period and his fuller 1980s telematic period; it is one of the first essays to weave poststructuralist literary theory and quantum physics into a single argument for telematic art.

## Executive Summary

This is the densest "theory bridge" essay in Ascott's 1980s telematic writings. Its double title — "Nets" (from the ninth-century Byzantine scholar Photius's summary of Dionysios of Aegeae's *Concerning Nets*) and "Spurs" (from Derrida's *Éperons: Les Styles de Nietzsche*) — encodes the essay's two moves: (1) to recover **weaving / the network** as an ancient metaphor of cosmic creation, and to show that this same metaphor now structures both quantum physics (Capra's "cosmic web," Bohm's "implicate order") and the planetary "mind at large" diffused by computerised telecommunications; and (2) to align telematics with the **poststructuralist / deconstructive** project (Barthes, Foucault, Derrida) as its "necessary (and perhaps inevitable) correlative." Ascott defines **telematics** not merely as the convergence of computers and telecommunications but as "a whole class of consciousness and culture" in which meaning is "not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined." He argues that telematic networks dissolve the sender/receiver model of information theory into "users," dissolve the single "Author God" into "dispersed authorship," and dissolve the discrete object into process and field. Marcel Duchamp is positioned as the seminal precursor (the *3 Stoppages*, the liberation of the text, the spectator who "adds to the creative act"). The essay closes on Ascott's own first major telematic artwork, **"La Plissure du Texte"** (1983) — a planetary collaborative "fairy tale" woven across eleven cities — as the realisation of Barthes's "tissue of quotations." The essay is the keystone link between Ascott's telematic theory and (a) the dematerialization tradition (he cites the "Les Immatériaux" exhibition by name) and (b) the participatory-system lineage shared with Burnham's systems esthetics.

## Arguments

- **Claim: Poststructuralism is the necessary correlative of telematic technology.**
  - Reasoning structure: the deconstructive dissolution of the fixed object, single author, and literal meaning is not merely contemporaneous with networked computing — it is its inevitable cultural twin; both replace fixity/causality/identity with process, intertext, and field.
  - Evidence: the parallel readings of Barthes ("tissue of quotations"), Derrida (*Glas*, *différance*), Nietzsche (*Twilight*), and the "intertext" of deconstruction with telematic dispersed authorship.
  - Source passage: > "The whole poststructuralist project can be seen as the necessary (and perhaps inevitable) correlative to the emergent communications technology that is so radically and rapidly affecting our ways of human interaction, the diffusion and integration of ideas, and our understanding and use of memory, language, and even human identity."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Poststructuralism is the necessary correlative of telematic technology`

- **Claim: In telematic networks meaning is negotiated, not given — there are users, not senders and receivers.**
  - Reasoning structure: telematics is "the system of systems"; it carries images/texts in a flux mediated by computer process; the old information-theory dyad of sender/receiver collapses into participatory "users."
  - Evidence: the contrast with "the old models of information theory"; the "branching, interacting pathways" of the screen of transformations.
  - Source passage: > "Telematic networks can carry images and texts in new forms of conjunction, mediated by computer process in a flux of meanings in which there are no 'senders' and 'receivers' (as in the old models of information theory) but only 'users,' whose intervention will be more or less creative depending on the degree of their participation in the system."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/In telematic networks meaning is negotiated and there are users not senders and receivers`

- **Claim: Telematics realises the death of the author and the birth of dispersed authorship.**
  - Reasoning structure: Barthes already declared the text "a tissue of quotations" with no original "Author God"; telematic process "embodies and amplifies" this insight by literally distributing creation across many users in many places.
  - Evidence: Barthes quote; the *La Plissure du Texte* case study (hundreds of users across eleven cities weaving a "textile" that "could not be classified").
  - Source passage: > "Telematics brings a new order of discourse into being that embodies and amplifies Barthes's earlier insight: 'We now know that the text is not a line of words releasing a single "theological" meaning (the "message" of an "Author God") but a multidimensional space in which a variety of writings, none of them original, blend and clash. The text is a tissue of quotations drawn from innumerable centres of culture.'"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematics realises the death of the author as dispersed authorship`

- **Claim: The work of art resides not in the object but in a system — a negotiation between artist and spectator (Duchamp).**
  - Reasoning structure: Duchamp is the decisive rupture from "the passive pleasure of the contemplation of the beautiful" to the "exegetical/eisegetical" relation; the spectator "adds to the creative act"; this anticipates telematics, "the system of systems."
  - Evidence: Duchamp's creative-act statement; Heisenberg's collapse of the Cartesian observer/observed partition cited in support.
  - Source passage: > "Duchamp understood that the work of art does not reside in the object alone, but in a system, a negotiation between artist and spectator in the creation of meaning: 'All in all the creative act is not performed by the artist alone; the spectator brings the work into contact with the external world by deciphering and interpreting its inner qualifications and thus adds to the creative act.'"
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The artwork resides not in the object but in a system negotiated between artist and spectator` (the participatory-system thesis Ascott shares with Burnham; cross-link to `Object and System`, `Object vs System`).

- **Claim: Advanced technology is not value-free; to interact with telematics is to change the psyche.**
  - Reasoning structure: technology "carries a psychic charge"; when the whole planet engages a technology as widespread as telematics, a "sea-change in world order" follows; understanding its deep meaning requires a new collaborative order of investigation (neither artists nor scientists alone).
  - Evidence: the "psychic charge" assertion; the call for collaboration that "may in time reveal new myths, archetypes, and resonant world images."
  - Source passage: > "Advanced technology is not innocent or value-free; it carries a psychic charge such that to interact with it is to change the psyche. When the whole planet engages with a technology as widespread as telematics, then a sea-change in world order can be expected."
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Advanced technology is not value-free interacting with telematics changes the psyche` (cross-link to McLuhan's "the medium is the message" and "every extension is an autoamputation," and to `Technology as Metaphor`).

- **Claim: Artists must resist the corporate/military colonisation of electronic space and network the Third World's voices.**
  - Reasoning structure: the creative potential of networks is conditional; it "will only be realised to the extent that artists resist the monopolisation and colonisation of electronic space by multinational business and the military."
  - Evidence: the explicit caveat; the "information embrace" of the Third World.
  - Source passage: > "But the creative potential of telematic networks will only be realised to the extent that artists resist the monopolisation and colonisation of electronic space by multinational business and the military."
  - Confidence: medium (a political caveat within the essay, not its central thesis)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The creative potential of networks depends on resisting corporate and military colonisation of electronic space` — Human review whether durable enough; resonates with Haraway's `The Informatics of Domination` and Han's `Digital Panopticon`.

## Metaphors

- **The Net / Weaving as Cosmic Creation** — the central metaphor: Kore "weaves out the universe" in "the roaring loom of time"; the net both knits the new order AND ensnares and pulls down the old.
  - Source passage: > "the work that Kore performs is that of weaving; she plies her shuttle in the 'roaring loom of time,' and weaves out the universe."
  - And: > "in many myths the destruction of the building is linked with a net." (Santillana/von Dechend, *Hamlet's Mill*)

- **The Cosmic Web** (Capra) — particles as "probability patterns, interconnections in an inseparable cosmic web"; the world as "a perfect network of mutual relations" (Avatamsaka Sutra).

- **The Screen of Transformations vs the Mirror of Reflections** — Ascott's own figure (see Key Concepts).

- **The Dance of Data / the Dance of the Pen** — telematic process as "a planetary field for the dance of data," echoing Nietzsche's "thinking itself: a dance of concepts and the pen."
  - Source passage: > "provide a planetary field for the dance of data—much as Nietzsche saw thinking itself: a dance of concepts and the pen."

- **The Spur / Éperon (the ram)** — Derrida's "spurs" as "those hooks into images and texts that enable us to unravel meanings, while at the same time spurring us on to weave new semantic networks." The "ram (éperon)" pushed into the cracks of the old order.

- **The Two Glasses** — Duchamp's *Large Glass* and Derrida's *Glas* "curiously linked (not causally but associatively) by their titles," both bearing "the trace of massive rupture."

- **Strings / Threads of the Universe** — supersymmetry's "strings" as "the modern metaphor of strings or threads woven together to form the fabric of the universe."

## Symbols

- **The Net / Knotted Carpet** — Hamlet (Amlethus) throws "the knotted carpet net prepared by his mother over the drunken crowd and burns down the hall"; Clytemnestra throws a net over the king in his bath (fall of the House of Atreus). The net as instrument of demolition of the old order.

- **The Pleiades and Hyades as Nets** — Kaulu asks Makalii for "the nets, the Pleiades and the Hyades, into which he entangled the evil one before he burned down the house" — the net "in the sky," the celestial matrix of new ideas.

- **La Plissure du Texte (the pleating / textile of the text)** — Ascott's 1983 artwork as the realised symbol of the woven planetary intertext; a "textile, which could not be classified."

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Object vs System / Process** — the discrete object (representation, fixity, causality) vs the network-as-process/field. "Quantum theory has shown that particles are not isolated grains of matter." Directly continuous with the vault's `Object vs System` (Burnham) and `Material vs Immaterial` tensions.

- **Material vs Immaterial** — Ascott names the "Les Immatériaux" exhibition by name; the telematic networks operate in "the virtual 'immateriality'" and the nets are "invisible or at least 'immaterial.'" Lyotard: "Finally there's no such thing as matter, and the only thing that exists is energy." This is a primary-source Ascott contribution to the vault's eight-author `Material vs Immaterial` tension.
  - Source passage: > "Here the nets are invisible or at least 'immaterial,' to draw on the title of a significant exhibition organised by Jean-François Lyotard and Thierry Chaput at the Centre Pompidou in Paris, 'Les Immatériaux,' which registers the dissolution of the old orders of thought based on aesthetics, the legitimacy of the object and the fixity of individual identity."

- **Single Author vs Dispersed Authorship** — the "Author God" / "individual genius" vs the "dispersed authorship" of the network; "The fading of the idea of a single or original author gives way to the idea of multiple authorship."

- **Sender/Receiver vs User** — the information-theory communication model vs the participatory telematic "user."

- **Liberation vs Colonisation of Electronic Space** — the network's emancipatory creative potential vs its monopolisation by "multinational business and the military."

- **Appearance vs Change/Becoming** (Nietzsche) — the metaphysics of "unity, identity, duration substance, cause, materiality, being" exposed by language as a "perpetual advocate" of error, against "Change, mutation, becoming in general." (Prefigures Ascott's later Apparition vs Appearance pairing.)
