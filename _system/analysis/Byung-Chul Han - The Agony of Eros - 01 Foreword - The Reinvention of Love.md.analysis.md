## Executive Summary

Alain Badiou's foreword introduces Han's *The Agony of Eros* by both summarizing it chapter-by-chapter and staking his own philosophical position against it. Badiou frames the book as a "phenomenology of true love" that diagnoses love as "gravely ill," if not already dead, under contemporary globalized capitalism, individualism, and the market's "effort to determine the market value of everything." He restates Han's master thesis: love is not "a simple pact of pleasant coexistence between two individuals" but "the radical experience... of the existence of the Other," and its minimum condition is "sufficient courage to accept self-negation for the sake of discovering the Other." Badiou walks through all eight chapters (Melancholia/von Trier; the critique of Foucault and appreciation of Levinas/Buber; Hegel/Bataille; eroticism vs pornography/Agamben/Baudrillard; the "inferno of the same" with Illouz/Flaubert/Barthes; the politics of love with Plato and Badiou's own "burnout society" praise; and love as the ground of thought). Crucially, Badiou both endorses and *contests* Han: he introduces his own signature thesis — **"love is a Two scene," a dual perspective** — which Han adopts, and then, "after the death of God," questions whether the total self-abolition Han demands is the only route to alterity, proposing instead "a world beginning with the Two of love." He closes on Rimbaud's word: the "reinvention" of love.

## Definitions

- **Love (Badiou, framing Han)** — not a pact of coexistence but the radical experience of the Other's existence.
  - > "love... is not a simple pact of pleasant coexistence between two individuals; rather it is the radical experience, perhaps to the outermost point, of the existence of the Other." (Badiou)
- **Eros / the erotic relation (Badiou quoting Han)** — a relation to the Other beyond achievement, performance, and Can.
  - > "Eros is a relationship to the Other situated beyond achievement, performance, and Can." (Badiou quoting Han)
- **The atopia of the Other (Badiou quoting Han)** — the negativity of Otherness that eludes all ability and constitutes erotic experience.
  - > "The negativity of Otherness—that is, the atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability—is constitutive of erotic experience." (Badiou quoting Han)
- **Eroticism (Bataille, quoted by Badiou via Han)** — assent to life up to the point of death.
  - > "Eroticism, it may be said, is assenting to life up to the point of death." (Bataille, quoted by Badiou via Han)
- **Amorous oblativity (Badiou)** — the vanishing of the self in the Other; a self-abolition with a long mystical history.
  - > "the notion of amorous oblativity—the vanishing of the self in the Other—has a long and glorious history: the mystical love of God, for example, as passionately described in the poems of Saint John of the Cross." (Badiou)
