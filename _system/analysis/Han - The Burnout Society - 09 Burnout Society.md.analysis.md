# Analysis: The Burnout Society / "Burnout Society"

## Executive Summary

This is the title chapter of *The Burnout Society* (*Müdigkeitsgesellschaft*, 2010), Han's foundational diagnosis and the **primary source for much of the vault's existing Han vocabulary** (`Achievement Society`, `Auto-Exploitation`, `Positivity`, `Negativity`, `Can vs Should`). The chapter is a sustained intrapsychic and politico-economic argument that the **disciplinary society** (Freud, Kant, Foucault — built on the negativity of prohibition, repression, and the "Should") has given way to an **achievement society** built on the positivity of "Can," in which the subject is no longer an obedience-subject but a self-projecting **achievement-subject**.

Han reads Kafka's "Prometheus" as an intrapsychic scene of self-exploitation (the eagle as the subject's own alter ego), then mounts an extended polemic against two interlocutors who, in his view, misdiagnose the present by clinging to the negativity scheme: **Alain Ehrenberg** (depression as the failure of the sovereign self, the running target of *The Weariness of the Self*) and **Richard Sennett** (the narcissistic gratification crisis). Against both he argues that depression, burnout, and ADHD are maladies of **excess positivity**, not repression — they cannot be reached by psychoanalysis because they involve no unconscious, no conflict, no transference. He traces the modal/structural shift whereby the **superego positivizes into the ego ideal**, subjection becomes projection, and "**the project turns out to be a projectile**."

The chapter culminates in a re-reading of **Giorgio Agamben**'s *homo sacer* / bare life: Han accepts the figure but rejects Agamben's negativity scheme (exclusion, the state of exception, sovereign vs victim as topologically distinct poles). In achievement society the **injunction is not sovereign but the injunction to achieve** [*Bann der Leistung*], and the achievement-subject becomes "**the homo sacer of his own self**" — perpetrator and victim, master and slave, *homo liber* and *homo sacer* collapsed into one. The closing pages diagnose the **doping society**: the absolutization of survival, bare life stripped of all narrativity and teleology, the mania for health ("**Health is the new goddess**"), and the achievement-subject as one of the undead, "too alive to die, and too dead to live."

This chapter is the keystone for cross-book merges: nearly every Han concept already in the vault (from *Transparency Society* and *Psychopolitics*) originates or is most fully argued here. The dominant recommendation is **update_existing** — extend established notes with their primary-source section rather than minting duplicates.

## Key Entities

- **Byung-Chul Han** — author.
- **Franz Kafka** — "Prometheus" (the opening parable, reworked); also the Hunger Artist and Prometheus elsewhere in the book (figures of self-exploitation).
- **Sigmund Freud** — the psychic apparatus, the ego as "seat of anxiety," the superego, repression, melancholy vs the contemporary depression, the Schreber case, character as "precipitate of abandoned object-cathexes."
- **Immanuel Kant** — the moral/obedience-subject, conscience as superego, the "doubled self," God as the instance of gratification, happiness "distributed in exact proportion to morality."
- **Alain Ehrenberg** — *The Weariness of the Self* (*La fatigue d'être soi*); the principal polemical target — depression as the democratization of melancholy, the exhausted sovereign self, the "deconflictualization" thesis.
- **Richard Sennett** — narcissism as gratification crisis; the "lack of closure"; the narcissist "hungry for Experience"; Han rejects Sennett's conclusion (intentional avoidance of goals).
- **Friedrich Nietzsche** — "sovereign man" vs the "last man"; the man of leisure [*Muße*]; *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* on those "in love with hectic work."
- **Giorgio Agamben** — *homo sacer*, bare life, the sovereign exception, the *Bann*/injunction; critiqued for anachronistic negativity.
- **Carl Schmitt** — "a sign of inner conflict to have more than one real enemy"; the decision; sovereignty; invoked on character, on the friend/enemy, and on depression as democracy's lack of decisive power.
- **Jürgen Habermas** — invoked contra ("the violence of consensus" against Habermasian consensus).
- **Daniel Paul Schreber** — Freud's case of paranoia traced to repressed homosexuality; the exemplar of nineteenth-century disciplinary repression.
- **Facebook / social networks** — "many friends on Facebook" as proof of the late-modern ego's lack of character; "friends" as attention-granting consumers heightening narcissism.
- Technologies/conditions: **new media and communications technology**, the **virtual world** (poor in alterity/resistance), **antidepressants**, **doping**/neuroenhancement.

## Definitions

- **Auto-exploitation (Han)** — "Auto-exploitation is more efficient than allo-exploitation because a deceptive feeling of freedom accompanies it. The exploiter is simultaneously the exploited." — primary-source definition; map to existing `Auto-Exploitation`.
- **Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)** — defined here via Sennett: "Experience [Erfahrung] involves encountering the Other. It alters. Experiencing [Erlebnis], in contrast, expands the ego into the Other, into the world. It com-pares." — maps to existing definition note `Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)`; this is a distinct primary-source articulation (the Erfahrung-alters / Erlebnis-com-pares formulation).
- **Homo sacer / bare life (Agamben, as restated by Han)** — "This life is bare because it stands outside the legal order and therefore can be destroyed at any time." Han then redefines it: the achievement-subject's life is "bare" because, "stripped of all transcendent value, it has been reduced to the immanency of vital functions and capacities, which are to be maximized by any and all means." — candidate for a definition note `Bare Life (Han via Agamben)` if a note is created; otherwise captured in the concept.
- **Character (Freud)** — "a precipitate of abandoned object-cathexes" (quoted from Freud); contrasted with the amorphous, characterless depressive. Supports the negativity/morphology argument; likely plain-text attribution rather than a vault note.

## Arguments

- **Claim: Auto-exploitation is more efficient than allo-exploitation because a feeling of freedom attends it; the achievement-subject is simultaneously perpetrator and victim.**
  - Reasoning structure: economic-functional + intrapsychic. Beyond a certain production level, internalized self-compulsion (posing as freedom) extracts more than external coercion; the exploiter and exploited collapse into one, so "exploitation now occurs without domination."
  - Evidence: capitalist relations of production; the dialectic of freedom (freedom from the Other → new constraints).
  - Source passage: > On the basis of the paradoxical freedom it holds, the achievement-subject is simultaneously perpetrator and victim, master and slave. Freedom and violence now coincide.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Auto-exploitation is more efficient than allo-exploitation because a feeling of freedom attends it`

- **Claim: Depression, burnout, and ADHD are maladies of excess positivity, not repression — so psychoanalysis cannot reach them.**
  - Reasoning structure: structural diagnosis. Psychoanalysis presupposes negativity (repression, the unconscious, conflict, transference, character/morphology); these are absent in the new maladies, which arise from the "inability to say no" / "being-able-to-do-everything." Therefore the conflict model and transference fail.
  - Evidence: depression is objectless, undirected, amorphous; no conversion symptoms (unlike hysteria); no transference; the Schreber case as the contrast (repression-based paranoia).
  - Source passage: > In contrast, the process of repression or negation plays no role in contemporary psychic maladies such as depression, burnout, and ADHD. Instead, they indicate an excess of positivity
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The maladies of the twenty-first century stem from excess positivity not repression`

- **Claim: Ehrenberg misdiagnoses depression by clinging to the negativity scheme (the sovereign self, the unconscious, hidden conflict).**
  - Reasoning structure: immanent critique. Ehrenberg rightly notes deconflictualization and the absent relation to conflict, but still holds the conflict model (a "hidden conflict" obscured by antidepressants), still invokes the unconscious, and equates the depressive with Nietzsche's "sovereign man" — all figures of negativity inapplicable to a society of positivity. He also ignores the economic/systemic dimension (self-exploitation).
  - Evidence: Ehrenberg's quotes on the unconscious, on "limitless possibilities" vs "the uncontrolled," on "deconflictualization," on melancholy/depression as "democratization of the exceptional."
  - Source passage: > Ehrenberg attempts to rescue psychoanalysis even though its conditions have disappeared.
  - Source passage: > Counter to what Ehrenberg claims, Nietzsche advanced "sovereign man" in the name of cultural criticism, as a countermodel to the exhausted achievement-subject.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Ehrenberg misreads depression by holding to the negativity scheme`

- **Claim: Sennett correctly links contemporary disturbance to narcissism but draws the wrong conclusion — the narcissist does not avoid closure to heighten the self; closure has become socially impossible.**
  - Reasoning structure: inversion of Sennett. Sennett says closure is intentionally avoided (it would objectify the self); Han says "precisely the opposite holds" — the socially conditioned impossibility of definitive forms drives self-repetition; the subject cannot reach closure, "loses itself and scatters itself into the open." Economic factors (openness favors growth) underlie this.
  - Evidence: Sennett's quotes on narcissism, closure, continuity, self-denial; the impossibility of a definitive *Werk* under contemporary relations of production.
  - Source passage: > It is not that the narcissistic subject does not want to achieve closure. Rather, it is incapable of getting there.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Closure is not avoided but has become socially impossible`

- **Claim: In the shift from disciplinary to achievement society the superego positivizes into the ego ideal, and the project becomes a projectile.**
  - Reasoning structure: intrapsychic mechanism + image. The repressive superego ("Thou shalt") becomes the seductive, self-projected ego ideal; subjection (sich unterwift) becomes projection (entwirft sich). When the gap between real ego and unattainable ego ideal opens, auto-aggression results; the project the subject hurls itself into becomes a projectile aimed at itself.
  - Evidence: Freud's superego ("harshness and cruelty," "dictatorial 'Thou shalt'"); the dynamic of unattainable ego ideal → self-reproach → ego at war with itself.
  - Source passage: > In view of the ego ideal, the real ego appears as a loser buried in self-reproach. The ego wages war with itself.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The superego positivizes into the ego ideal and the project becomes a projectile`

- **Claim: The achievement-subject is the homo sacer of its own self — Agamben's negativity scheme (exclusion, sovereign exception) cannot grasp the violence of positivity (exhaustion, inclusion).**
  - Reasoning structure: appropriation + critique. Han keeps homo sacer/bare life but rejects Agamben's topology (sovereign and victim as distinct extreme poles, the state of exception as negativity). In achievement society the injunction is the *Bann der Leistung*, not a sovereign ban; the homines sacri inhabit "a totalized state of normality... a state of positivity"; sovereign and homo sacer collapse into one person.
  - Evidence: Agamben's quotes on bare life as "authentically political," "life exposed to death," the *Urphänomen of politics*; Han's counter that Agamben "describes the society of sovereignty" from within achievement society (anachronism).
  - Source passage: > The achievement-subject that considers itself free and fashions itself as homo liber—its own sovereign and self-made [Unternehmer seiner selbst]—stands under the injunction to achieve; thereby, it makes itself into homo sacer.
  - Source passage: > Accordingly, he cannot grasp the violence of positivity, which expresses itself as the exhaustion and inclusion that characterize the society of achievement.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The achievement-subject is the homo sacer of its own self` (rejecting Agamben's negativity scheme)

- **Claim: Violence persists after positivization — it issues from the positivity of consensus and conformism, not only from conflict (the violence of consensus, contra Habermas).**
  - Reasoning structure: redefinition of violence. Positivization de-ideologizes society (no clash of ideologies/classes) but does not abolish violence; the totality of capital is "consensual violence"; conformism produces violence more readily than dissent.
  - Evidence: deconflictualization, de-ideologization, "the totality of capital, which seems to be absorbing everything, represents consensual violence."
  - Source passage: > Violence does not stem from the negativity of clash or conflict alone; it also derives from the positivity of consensus.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Violence issues from the positivity of consensus not only from conflict`

- **Claim: Capitalism absolutizes survival (not the good life); bare life stripped of narrativity makes "Health the new goddess" and dictates the doping society.**
  - Reasoning structure: teleological collapse. With ideal/transcendent values gone, only the body of the ego remains, to be kept healthy at any cost; health becomes self-referential, "purposiveness without purpose"; the inner logic evolves into a doping society. The achievement-subject becomes one of the undead.
  - Evidence: the atomization of society, the erosion of the social, the disappearance of teleology ("every in-order-to"), antidepressants/doping, the undead simile.
  - Source passage: > The mania for health emerges when life has become as flat as a coin and stripped of all narrative content, all value.
  - Source passage: > They are too alive to die, and too dead to live.
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Capitalism absolutizes bare survival and turns health into the new goddess`

- **Claim: New media and virtuality dilute being-for-the-Other; the real is the locus of resistance and support, and its disappearance feeds narcissism.**
  - Reasoning structure: media-ontological. Virtual spaces are poor in alterity/resistance; the narcissistic ego encounters only itself; the real (a "stay" — both interruption/resistance and stopping/support) disappears with virtualization; social-network "friends" heighten narcissism as attention-granting consumers of the ego-as-commodity.
  - Evidence: the reality principle as principle of alterity/resistance; the double meaning of "stay"; Facebook friends.
  - Source passage: > In all the imaginary spaces of virtuality, the narcissistic ego encounters itself first and foremost.
  - Confidence: medium (Directly stated for the claim; the bridge to `Digital Panopticon`/`Hell of the Same` is Strongly implied)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Virtuality dilutes the relation to the Other and feeds narcissism` (or fold into the narcissism note)

## Metaphors

- **Kafka's Prometheus / the eagle as the subject's alter ego** — the intrapsychic scene of self-exploitation: Prometheus bound is the achievement-subject who "deems itself free when in fact it is bound"; the eagle consuming the ever-regrowing liver is the subject's own alter ego; pain of the liver = tiredness.
  - Source passage: > Viewed in this way, the relation between Prometheus and the eagle represents a relation of self-exploitation.
  - Note: distinct from McLuhan's Narcissus-as-narcosis; this is a Han self-exploitation figure. Candidate metaphor note `Prometheus and the Eagle (Self-Exploitation)`.

- **The project as projectile** — "The project turns out to be a projectile that the achievement-subject is aiming at itself." The keystone image of auto-aggression; the self-liberation into a project boomerangs into self-destruction.
  - Recommended: fold into `The Project (Han)` and/or the superego/projectile argument; strong candidate for a metaphor note `The Project as Projectile`.

- **"Health is the new goddess"** — the deification of health/survival once transcendent value collapses; the religion of the doping society.
  - Source passage: > Health is the new goddess.

- **The undead achievement-subject** — "Their life equals that of the undead. They are too alive to die, and too dead to live." Bare life with no death and no good life.

- **The rat race against oneself / locking jaws on oneself** — "It wears out in a rat race it runs against itself"; "it locks its jaws on itself; paradoxically, this leads the self to hollow and empty out." Figures of self-cannibalizing auto-aggression.

- **Life "as flat as a coin"** — "life has become as flat as a coin and stripped of all narrative content, all value." The flattening of life to bare exhibition/health value.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Positivity vs Negativity** — the master tension of the chapter and the book: excess positivity (affirmation, Can, inability to say no, exhaustion, inclusion) vs negativity (prohibition, repression, conflict, the unconscious, exclusion). Existing `tensions/Positivity vs Negativity` — update with primary source.
- **Should vs Can** (Discipline vs Achievement) — the modal/structural opposition; maps to existing `tensions/Discipline vs Control` and the `Can vs Should` concept; the disciplinary obedience-subject vs the achievement-subject.
- **Subject vs Project** — "the change from subject to project"; subjection (to the superego/Other) vs projection (onto the ego ideal). Existing `tensions/Subject vs Project` (Psychopolitics) — update with primary source.
- **Sovereign vs Homo Sacer (collapsed)** — Agamben keeps them as distinct topological poles; Han collapses them into one person ("sovereign and homo sacer still generate each other" but now within the same self). The paradox: *homo liber* = *homo sacer*; master = slave; perpetrator = victim.
- **Allo-Exploitation vs Auto-Exploitation** — external vs self-domination; the latter "more efficient... because the feeling of freedom attends it." Capitalism's switch from one to the other to accelerate.
- **The good life vs survival** — capitalism absolutizes survival, abandons the good life; livingness vs bare vitality/health.
- **Self vs Other / I-tiredness vs world-trusting tiredness** — narcissistic self-reference (worldless, world-destroying) vs encountering/relying on the Other; the healing "tiredness that trusts in the world" vs solitary "I-tiredness."
- **Melancholy vs Depression** — melancholy (negativity: preceded by loss, stands in negative relation to the absent object) vs depression (objectless, undirected, "cut off from all relation," lacking gravity). A within-chapter distinction crucial to the Ehrenberg/Freud polemic.
- **Freedom vs Violence (coincidence)** — "Freedom and violence now coincide"; auto-compulsion presents itself as freedom. Maps to existing `Freedom vs Control` / `Exploitation of Freedom`.

## Candidate Source Citations

> The modal verb that determines achievement society is not the Freudian Should, but Can.

> In contrast, the late-modern achievement-subject is poor in negation. It is a subject of affirmation.

> Instead, they indicate an excess of positivity, that is, not negation so much as the inability to say no; they do not point to not-being-allowed-to-do-anything [Nicht-Dürfen], but to being-able-to-do-everything [Alles-Können].

> In the transition from disciplinary society to achievement society, the superego positivizes itself into the ego ideal.

> The achievement-subject projects itself [entwirft sich] onto the ego ideal, whereas the obedience-subject subjects itself [sich unterwift] to the superego.

> The project turns out to be a projectile that the achievement-subject is aiming at itself. In view of the ego ideal, the real ego appears as a loser buried in self-reproach. The ego wages war with itself.

> Achievement society is the society of self-exploitation. The achievement-subject exploits itself until it burns out.

> Auto-exploitation is more efficient than allo-exploitation because a deceptive feeling of freedom accompanies it. The exploiter is simultaneously the exploited. Exploitation now occurs without domination.

> On the basis of the paradoxical freedom it holds, the achievement-subject is simultaneously perpetrator and victim, master and slave. Freedom and violence now coincide.

> Exogenous violence is replaced by self-generated violence, which is more fatal than its counterpart inasmuch as the victim of such violence considers itself free.

> The injunction that makes us all homines sacri is not a sovereign injunction, but the injunction to achieve [Bann der Leistung].

> The achievement-subject that considers itself free and fashions itself as homo liber—its own sovereign and self-made [Unternehmer seiner selbst]—stands under the injunction to achieve; thereby, it makes itself into homo sacer.

> The sovereign of achievement society, then, is the homo sacer of his own self.

> Agamben utterly fails to notice the topological change of power that lies at the basis of the society of sovereignty's transformation into achievement society.

> Accordingly, he cannot grasp the violence of positivity, which expresses itself as the exhaustion and inclusion that characterize the society of achievement.

> Today violence issues more readily from the conformism of consensus than from the antagonism of dissent. In this sense—contra Habermas—one might speak of the violence of consensus.

> Depression—which often culminates in burnout—follows from overexcited, overdriven, excessive self-reference that has assumed destructive traits.

> It wears out in a rat race it runs against itself.

> In fact, depression defies the conflict model—that is, depression eludes psychoanalysis. Ehrenberg attempts to rescue psychoanalysis even though its conditions have disappeared.

> It is not that the narcissistic subject does not want to achieve closure. Rather, it is incapable of getting there. It loses itself and scatters itself into the open.

> Experience [Erfahrung] involves encountering the Other. It alters. Experiencing [Erlebnis], in contrast, expands the ego into the Other, into the world. It com-pares.

> In all the imaginary spaces of virtuality, the narcissistic ego encounters itself first and foremost.

> The real is a stay in the double meaning of the word. It not only offers interruption and resistance, but also affords stopping and support.

> The capitalist economy absolutizes survival. It is not concerned with the good life.

> The reduction of life to biological, vital processes makes life itself bare and strips it of all narrativity.

> The mania for health emerges when life has become as flat as a coin and stripped of all narrative content, all value.

> The inner logic of achievement society dictates its evolution into a doping society.

> Health is the new goddess.

> Their life equals that of the undead. They are too alive to die, and too dead to live.

> Viewed in this way, the relation between Prometheus and the eagle represents a relation of self-exploitation.

> For Nietzsche, it is not "sovereign man" but the "last man" who exploits himself as his own vassal.

> Carl Schmitt says it is a "sign of inner conflict to have more than one real enemy."
