## Executive Summary

This is the book's concluding chapter, where Hayles gathers the three "stories" the book has told — "how information lost its body, how the cyborg was constructed in the postwar years as technological artifact and cultural icon, and how the human became the posthuman" — and stages a final argument about *which* posthuman we should construct. She frames the chapter around the affective poles she set up in the Prologue: the posthuman "both evokes terror and excites pleasure." The terror she traces to apocalyptic, machine-supremacist visions (Hans Moravec, Warren McCulloch) that imagine humans superseded or downloaded into computers. The pleasure she traces to the prospect of "getting out of some of the old boxes" — escaping the liberal humanist self.

Her central polemical move is to separate two things that are usually fused: the posthuman as such, and the posthuman grafted onto liberal humanism. The disembodied, immortality-seeking, mastery-driven Moravec fantasy is, she argues, not the overcoming of the autonomous liberal subject but its *expansion* — "he is not abandoning the autonomous liberal subject but is expanding its perogatives into the realm of the posthuman." Against this, she offers a "good" posthuman: one "located within the dialectic of pattern/randomness and grounded in embodied actuality rather than disembodied information."

The chapter also completes a philosophical contrast that runs through the book: the shift from **presence/absence** (Logos, origin, plenitude, the metaphysics of presence — including deconstruction as its negative image) to **pattern/randomness**, in which meaning is not "front-loaded" into the system but emerges from recursive processes, contingency, and the creative role of noise/randomness (Langton, Kauffman, Varela, Atlan, Bateson). She marshals **distributed cognition** (Edwin Hutchins's study of ship navigation; his reading of Searle's Chinese Room) to argue that cognition and even judgment are distributed across human and nonhuman actors, dethroning "conscious agency" as the seat of human identity. The chapter closes with the book's signature aphoristic inversion of Bruno Latour: "we have never been modern… we have always been posthuman," and a call to contest the meaning of the posthuman "now, before the trains of thought it embodies have been laid down so firmly that it would take dynamite to change them."

## Definitions

- **Posthuman (as "post-")** — Hayles glosses the prefix.
  - > "'Post,' with its dual connotation of superseding the human and coming after it, hints that the days of 'the human' may be numbered."

- **The body as "congealed metaphor"** —
  - > "the body itself is a congealed metaphor, a physical structure whose constraints and possibilities have been formed by an evolutionary history that intelligent machines do not share."

  - > "In this account, emergence replaces teleology; reflexive epistemology replaces objectivism; distributed cognition replaces autonomous will; embodiment replaces a body seen as a support system for the mind; and a dynamic partnership between humans and intelligent machines replaces the liberal humanist subject's manifest destiny to dominate and control nature."

## Arguments

- **Claim: The lethal thing is not the posthuman but the posthuman grafted onto liberal humanism.**
  - Reasoning structure: separates two conflated things; shows Moravec's downloading fantasy *expands* the liberal subject rather than overcoming it; opens space for a non-apocalyptic posthuman grounded in embodiment.
  - Evidence: Moravec's "you" choosing immortality through downloading.
  - Source passage: > "What is lethal is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self. When Moravec imagines 'you' choosing to download yourself into a computer… he is not abandoning the autonomous liberal subject but is expanding its perogatives into the realm of the posthuman." [OCR: "prerogatives"]
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The lethal posthuman is the posthuman grafted onto liberal humanism`

- **Claim: Conscious agency has never been "in control"; the illusion of control is a story consciousness tells itself.**
  - Reasoning structure: posthuman view dethrones consciousness as a small subsystem ignorant of the emergent dynamics that actually constitute it; mastery is narrative, not fact.
  - Evidence: emergent/chaotic dynamics; feminist critique linking mastery to objectivism and imperialism.
  - Source passage: > "In the posthuman view, by contrast, conscious agency has never been 'in control.' In fact, the very illusion of control bespeaks a fundamental ignorance about the nature of the emergent processes through which consciousness, the organism, and the environment are constituted."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Conscious agency has never been in control`

- **Claim: We have always been posthuman.**
  - Reasoning structure: adapts Latour's "we have never been modern"; the seriated history of cybernetics (materially real, socially regulated, discursively constructed) means the posthuman is not a future event but a retrospective recognition.
  - Source passage: > "Bruno Latour has argued that we have never been modern; the seriated history of cybernetics—emerging from networks at once materially real, socially regulated, and discursively constructed—suggests, for similar reasons, that we have always been posthuman."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/We have always been posthuman`

- **Claim: The body is sedimented evolutionary history that machines do not share, so there is a limit to seamless human-machine articulation.**
  - Reasoning structure: against literary studies' and Moravec's shared "blind spot" about embodiment; embodiment differs in kind from machine embodiment.
  - Source passage: > "there is a limit to how seamlessly humans can be articulated with intelligent machines, which remain distinctively different from humans in their embodiments."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Embodiment differs in kind from machine embodiment` (or fold into `Embodiment`)

- **Claim: Meaning in pattern/randomness is not front-loaded by a coherent origin but emerges from recursive processes.**
  - Reasoning structure: contrasts presence/absence (origin grounds signification) with pattern/randomness (complexity evolves from recursion on simple rules; open, contingent future).
  - Evidence: multiagent simulations; complexity from recursion.
  - Source passage: > "In this dialectic, meaning is not front-loaded into the system, and the origin does not act to ground signification… complexity evolves from highly recursive processes being applied to simple rules."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/In pattern randomness meaning emerges rather than being front-loaded`

- **Claim: Cognition (and even judgment) is distributed across human and nonhuman actors; the Chinese Room "knows Chinese" as a whole.**

  - Source passage: > "it is not Searle but the entire room that knows Chinese." Also: > "Modern humans are capable of more sophisticated cognition than cavemen not because moderns are smarter… but because they have constructed smarter environments in which to work."

## Metaphors

- **The body as "congealed metaphor"** — the body as a metaphor frozen into physical structure, formed by evolutionary history. (Key passage above.)
- **Organisms as "good metaphors for the complexities without"** (Bateson) — survival favors internal structures that metaphorize external complexity; "the metaphors that we ourselves are."
  - > "the distributed cognition of the emergent human subject correlates with—in Bateson's phrase, becomes a metaphor for—the distributed cognitive system as a whole"
- **"Froth of noise" / cybernetics "born in a froth of noise"** — origin image; noise as generative.
  - > "cybernetics was born in a froth of noise when Norbert Wiener first thought of it as a way to maximize human potential in a world that is in essence chaotic and unpredictable."
- **"The noise crashes within as well as without"** — chaos is internal to subjectivity, not just environmental.
- **"Getting out of some of the old boxes"** — the pleasure of the posthuman as liberation from the liberal-humanist enclosure; resonates with Bateson's "box" (the system's confines).
- **"Trains of thought… laid down so firmly that it would take dynamite to change them"** — closing image; the urgency of contesting the posthuman's meaning before it ossifies into entrenched track.
- **"Go gently into that good night" / "joining the dinosaurs"** — humans as a species facing obsolescence (the terror register).
- **"The body's murmuring"** (Atlan) — noise as the somatic ground of biological communication.

- **The Chinese Room** (Searle, via Hutchins) — symbol/figure for distributed cognition; the room, not the person, knows.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Terror vs Pleasure** — the affective frame of the chapter and book; the posthuman "both evokes terror and excites pleasure."
  - > "the prospect of becoming posthuman both evokes terror and excites pleasure."
- **Presence/Absence vs Pattern/Randomness** — the master conceptual tension (origin/plenitude/Logos vs emergence/contingency/noise). Deconstruction is shown to remain *inside* presence/absence.
  - > "deconstruction required a metaphysics of presence to articulate the destabilization of that self."
- **Embodiment vs Disembodied Information** — realizes the committed stub `Embodiment vs Information`; "embodied actuality rather than disembodied information."
- **Liberal Humanist Subject vs Distributed/Emergent Subject** — autonomous will and conscious control vs distributed cognition and emergent subjectivity.
- **Apocalyptic/Antihuman posthuman vs Benign/Embodied posthuman** — "some current versions of the posthuman point toward the antihuman and the apocalyptic, we can craft others that will be conducive to the long-range survival of humans."
- **Reflexivity vs Objectivism** — observer entangled in system vs the dream of mastery and objective distance (tied to the feminist critique of science).
- **Material/Real vs Virtual** — the false binary Hayles wants to dissolve ("the solidity of real life on one side and the illusion of virtual reality on the other").
