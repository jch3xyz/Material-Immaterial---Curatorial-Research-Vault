## Executive Summary

This is the **1971 year-chronicle** (source pages 208–262), a dense documentary anthology of conceptual-art books, statements, exhibitions, and verbatim artist quotations. Lippard's own argumentative voice is sparse; the year speaks mostly through the assembled primary documents. Three through-lines make 1971 a hinge year for the book's title concept and for Lippard's politics:

1. **The Haacke/Guggenheim affair (April–May 1971)** — director Thomas Messer cancels Hans Haacke's show over the "real-time social system" pieces (the *Shapolsky* Manhattan real-estate holdings and the *Visitors' Profile* poll), Edward Fry is fired for defending the artist, and the Art Workers' Coalition demonstrates inside the museum. This is the clearest instantiation of **art and politics / systems aesthetics / art vs. the institution** in the book, and a key moment in Lippard's own political turn.
2. **The document / the photograph as the residue of the dematerialized work** — crystallized in Lippard's long taped conversation with **Robert Morris** ("Photographs function as a peculiar kind of sign... the signifier-signified relation they set up is not at all clear or transparent"), and the Huebler/Burgy conversation on the "information-to-weight ratio" of media and art's residue becoming "information."
3. **Dematerialization named and contested** — Jan Dibbets's 1963 mailbox label "Jan Dibbets Dematerialisateur" and his pushback ("That dematerialization you are speaking about is going to be for me... more and more visual"); Robert Barry's art "so dematerialized that it has no fundamental need" of the gallery; Jeff Wall's *Cine Text* ("the process may become relatively dematerialized... Dematerialization is the truth of materialism").

The year also documents **systems** (LeWitt wall drawings, Darboven's *One Century in One Year*, Merz's Fibonacci series, Rockburne's set theory), the **catalogue/exhibition as medium** (CAYC Buenos Aires, *Arte de Sistemas*, Siegelaub-style city-population catalogues re-presented in Barry's Lambert show), **art vs. the market/commodity** (Kaprow's "stop producing marketable objects," Burgy's "luxury item" critique), and an emergent **feminist** strand (Ukeles's *Maintenance Art*, Antin, Piper, Kozlov, the "Twenty-six Contemporary Women Artists" show).

## Definitions

- **Real-time system** (Haacke) — verifiable social/biological/physical systems presented as art; see quote above.
- **Concept art** (Henry Flynt, via Ken Friedman) — "an art of which the material is 'concepts.'"
- **Dematerialization** (implicit, Wall) — > "Dematerialization is the truth of materialism, whether naive ('doing more with less') or philosophical."

## Arguments

- **Claim: A "real-time social system" presented as art remains a functioning social fact, not neutralized by being called art — which is exactly why institutions censor it.**
  - Reasoning: Haacke frames the museum's refusal as proof that the work operates; the AWC demonstration and Fry's firing dramatize the institution's stake.
  - Evidence: Messer's cancellation; > "Real-time systems are double agents. They might run under the heading 'art,' but this culturization does not prevent them from operating as normal."
  - Confidence: high (directly stated by the documented artist).
  - Recommended wiki note: contributes to `arguments/Dematerialization is a resistance to the art commodity` and a systems-aesthetics note.

- **Claim: Dematerialization is not escape from materialism but its culmination — and is bound up with mass production / information.**
  - Reasoning: Wall's *Cine Text* argues "mass production of physical results is the necessary and minimum condition for the dematerialization of effect."
  - Evidence: > "Dematerialization is the truth of materialism..."
  - Confidence: medium (single artist's text, but title-resonant).
  - Recommended wiki note: candidate citation for `[[Dematerialization]]`; flag the parallel to Hayles's "disembodiment of information."

- **Claim: The dematerialized object reduces art to its document, shifting value from object to information — with ambivalent consequences for the market.**
  - Reasoning: Morris on the photograph as unstable sign; Burgy/Huebler on "information-to-weight ratio" and art-as-residue still caught in the market.
  - Evidence: > "Since our art has turned from making the luxury item and \[t\]urned to the residue or information as the product..."; > "Size and weight become important factors."
  - Confidence: medium (artist dialogue, not Lippard's assertion).
  - Recommended wiki note: feeds `[[The Materiality of Informatics]]` cross-link and `[[Dematerialization]]`.

## Metaphors

- **"Dematerialisateur"** (Dibbets's mailbox label) — the artist as agent of dematerialization; emblematic figure for the whole book's title.
- **"Real-time systems are double agents"** (Haacke) — art that passes as art while continuing to "operate as normal" socially.
- **Information-to-weight ratio** (Burgy) — media history as the progressive shedding of physical mass ("you no longer have to have tons and tons of copper to convey importance").
- **The photograph as "consumable sign... yet something more"** (Morris) — the document as unstable residue of the absent work.

## Symbols

- **The empty/closed gallery** — Barry's "During the exhibition the gallery will be closed"; the immaterial work that doesn't need the gallery.
- **The questionnaire / poll** — Haacke's *Visitors' Profile*, Antin's *Library Science*, Denes's *Psychograph*; the document-as-artwork and data-as-art.
- **The index card / catalogue box** — Lippard's city-population catalogues (557,087; 955,000; 2,972,453) re-presented in Barry's Lambert show: the exhibition reduced to a box of cards.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Material vs Immaterial / object vs idea** — the year's governing opposition; Dibbets resists the immaterial reading ("more and more visual"); Morris refuses "conceptual artist" because his work "initiat\[es\] some process that had an existence other than just a mental one."
  - Key passage: > "I have done certain things that don't have a physical manifesttion, but it always seemed to me that what I was doing was initiating some process that had an existence other than just a mental one." (Morris)
- **Dematerialization as resistance vs. its recuperation by the market** — Kaprow ("stop producing marketable objects") and Burgy ("If art is isolated from culture and becomes a luxury item, it functions in very distorted ways") vs. the persistence of "the sense of property... in the art market."
- **Art vs. the institution / politics** — Haacke/Messer/Guggenheim; the AWC; Kaprow's "the artist of the future must learn how to evade his profession."
- **The work vs. its document** — Morris on whether the photograph "replaces your memory"; Heizer's inaccessible pieces vs. Morris wanting work "there, and available."
