## Executive Summary

"Telenoia" (1993, originally published in *On Line–Kunst im Netz*) is the essay in which Ascott coins his keyword **telenoia** — "networked consciousness, interactive awareness, mind at large" — explicitly as the networked, convivial OPPOSITE of the **paranoia** of the isolated, alienated, single self bred by industrial "smokestack culture." The essay opens by confronting two questions put to him about art in the telematic culture (can computer communications sustain art? will telematics destroy human communication?), then mounts a sweeping case that telematic networking is not merely a new technology but a fundamental evolutionary and spiritual rupture: "Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness; it is spiritual before it is in any way utilitarian."

The chapter is a primary anchor for the keyword **Telenoia** and develops/realizes a dense cluster of Ascott's signature ideas: **distributed authorship** (he dates his coinage to 1983 for *La Plissure du Texte*), the **holomatic principle** ("to be at any one interface is to be in the virtual presence of all other interfaces"), the **datapool** metaphor, the **post-biological body / body-as-interface**, the "**l'homme éclaté**" multiple distributed self (after Virilio), and — most importantly for the vault's vocabulary — the canonical **five-fold path** of Ascott's later aesthetics: **Connectivity, Immersion, Interaction, Transformation, Emergence**, integrated by the concept of **network**. It documents the major networking artworks (*La Plissure du Texte* 1983, the *Planetary Network* at the 1986 Venice Biennale, *Gaia* at Ars Electronica 1989, the *Telenoia* V2 event 1992) and the founding of **CAiiA** (Centre for Advanced Inquiry in the Interactive Arts), the seed of the later Planetary Collegium.

It is exceptionally cross-link-rich for the vault: Ascott explicitly invokes **Donna Haraway's "Cyborg Manifesto"** (calling it more useful for the telematic condition than most male-dominated technoculture conferences), **Hans Moravec** (downloading personality from wetware to hardware — "more to offer us than Freud"), **Gregory Bateson** ("mind at large"), **Maturana and Varela** ("structural coupling"), **Marshall McLuhan** (implicitly — global village → network consciousness, right-hemisphere/mosaic/all-at-once thinking), **Marge Piercy**, **Roland Barthes** (*Le Plaisir du Texte* / plissure), **Lyotard** (*Les Immatériaux*), and **Jakob von Uexküll** (Umwelt). It is also the chapter where Ascott proposes (then half-retracts) "connectivism"/"connectivist" as a replacement for the worn-out word "art."

## Key Entities

- **Roy Ascott** — author; the essay is largely autobiographical, narrating his own networking projects and coinages.
- **Telenoia (V2 event, 1992)** — the 24-hour global networking project at the V2 Organisation, 's-Hertogenbosch, Holland, after which the word is coined. Bulletin boards, e-mail, computer conferencing, file exchange, fax, videophone.
- **La Plissure du Texte: A Planetary Fairy Tale (1983)** — Ascott's project for Frank Popper's "Electra" exhibition at the Musée d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris; used the I. P. Sharp timesharing global network; 14 nodes across 4 continents, each a fairy-tale archetype. The origin of "distributed authorship."
- **Organe et Fonction d'Alice aux Pays des Merveilles (1984)** — Ascott's Minitel piece pleating Lewis Carroll (Henri Bué's French *Alice*) with a Montreal biology treatise; part of Lyotard's "Les Immatériaux" at Beaubourg.
- **Planetary Network (1986)** — the worldwide artist network Ascott initiated as international commissioner of the Venice Biennale, stretching the Biennale's location over the whole globe.
- **Gaia: Digital Pathways across the Whole Earth (1989)** — multimedia interactive project for Ars Electronica, Linz; 100+ artists; "Gaia's womb" data-tunnel interface.
- **CAiiA — Centre for Advanced Inquiry in the Interactive Arts** — base at Newport School of Art and Design, Gwent College, Wales; Ascott's "data garden," seed of the Planetary Collegium.
- **Roland Barthes** — *Le Plaisir du Texte*; the pun "plissure" (pleating) on "plaisir" (pleasure). Forward-stub author.
- **Gregory Bateson** — "mind at large." Forward-stub author (one-off / cited).
- **Donna Haraway** — *Simians, Cyborgs and Women* and "Cyborg Manifesto," cited as more useful for the telematic condition than most technoculture conferences. EXISTING author note.
- **Hans Moravec** — "consummate downloading of personality from wetware to hardware"; "more to offer us than Freud." EXISTING forward-stub author (Hayles/O'Gieblyn/Kurzweil cluster).
- **Sigmund Freud** — set against Moravec; "closet consciousness." EXISTING forward-stub author (Han Burnout pass).
- **Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela** — "structural coupling," the autopoietic self-creating organism that constructs its world. EXISTING forward-stub authors (Hayles cluster).
- **Paul Virilio** — "l'homme éclaté" (the exploded man / multiple distributed self). New forward-stub author.
- **Marge Piercy** — novel *Body of Glass* (1991), the Net as "a great clubhouse with thousands of rooms"; "post-feminist" cyborg-era literature. New forward-stub.
- **P. D. Ouspensky** — we are each composed of many selves. One-off figure (plain text).
- **Richard Rorty** — the novel should replace philosophy in communicating the new pragmatism. One-off figure.
- **William Gibson** — *Neuromancer*; "unidimensional punks," contrasted unfavourably with Piercy. One-off (Ed. note).
- **Jean-François Lyotard** — "Les Immatériaux" exhibition, Beaubourg, 1984. New forward-stub author (and a direct verbal bridge to the vault tension `Material vs Immaterial` — the immateriality theme).
- **Jakob von Uexküll** — Umwelt / milieu, species-dependent world. One-off (Ed. note).
- **Robert Birge** (Center for Molecular Electronics, Syracuse) — bacteriorhodopsin molecular memory; "the emergence of artificial consciousness in the Net."
- **George Gilder, Paul Green (IBM), David Payne** — fibre-optic / dark-fibre prophets; "Erbium Doped Amplifier."
- **Hugh Lusted, Benjamin Knapp, Anthony Lloyd** — "biocontrollers" (direct nerve-signal control of devices).
- **Frank Popper, Kit Galloway and Sherrie Rabinowitz (Electronic Cafe), Richard Branson** — art/network context figures.
- **Marshall McLuhan** — not named, but heavily present: global village, right-hemisphere "visual, mosaic, all-at-once thinking," electric implosion. EXISTING author note.

## Arguments

For each argument:

- **Claim: Telenoia (networked consciousness) is set to replace the paranoia of the isolated, alienated industrial self.**
  - Reasoning structure: the "smokestack culture" of industrial individualism bred paranoia, secrecy, the cult of the single unified self; networking reverses this into shared, distributed, convivial awareness.
  - Evidence: the etymology (tele + nous), the V2 *Telenoia* event, the cult-of-the-individual diagnosis.
  - Source passage: > "Telenoia is thus set to replace the paranoia of the isolated, alienated self of the old industrial society. The cult of the individual was always accompanied by the cult of the unified, indivisible human being, one body, one soul, one self."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telenoia replaces the paranoia of the isolated industrial self`

- **Claim: A work of telematic art generates meaning only through participation from inside the network; it cannot be viewed as a discrete object from outside.**
  - Reasoning structure: contrasts the pre-telematic artifact (surface intended to be viewed, externalising meaning) with the telematic work (process felt only from inside).
  - Evidence: the impossibility of mediating the *Telenoia* event on tape/slides/text.
  - Source passage: > "Unlike a painting, sculpture, or other artifact of the pre-telematic culture, whose surface is absolutely intended to be viewed, whose very purpose is to externalise meaning, a work of telematic art generates meaning through our intimate participation in its evolution. It cannot be viewed, from the outside, as a discrete entity."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The telematic artwork is a process experienced only from inside the network` (this is the Ascott pole of the `Object vs System` tension and a strong extension of `New Media Art` / `Art as Experiential Technology`).

- **Claim: Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness — spiritual before it is utilitarian.**
  - Reasoning structure: the cultural rupture (escape from earth/body) is driven by a longing that also produced the technologies; therefore the technology is fundamentally about consciousness, not utility.
  - Evidence: the "growing together of minds," the displacement/reframing of the body, the shift of perspective to the cosmos.
  - Source passage: > "Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness; it is spiritual before it is in any way utilitarian."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness` (the seed of technoetics; cross-link `Re-enchantment`).

- **Claim: The artist is a system — human and/or artificial — by which meaning is generated; the degree to which it is "human" is of little significance.**
  - Reasoning structure: collapses the artist into a distributed cognitive system (human + artificial); human value resides in the (human) viewer's intervention, not in the maker's humanity.
  - Evidence: the artist/designer as "distributed system," the interface-as-art, the biocontroller post-biological body.
  - Source passage: > "It is in this sense that I say that the artist is a system by which, and in interaction with which, meaning is generated. The degree to which the system is 'human' is of very little significance. The human value of the meaning generated resides in the intervention of the (human) viewer or user of the system."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The artist is a distributed system that generates meaning not a human creator` (Ascott's behavioural/cybernetic inheritance made networked; bridges Burnham's systems esthetics and Hayles's distributed cognition).

- **Claim: The networked erasure of the material body is not disembodiment or immateriality but a re-materialisation — the reinvention of the human being.**
  - Reasoning structure: rejects the dematerialisation/disembodiment reading; insists on a NEW corporeality (bionic, prosthetic, post-biological).
  - Evidence: telepresence, biocontrollers, the body-as-interface, the cyborg culture.
  - Source passage: > "This erasure leads, however, not to a disembodiment of the person, nor to an immateriality, but to the rematerialisation, the redescription, reconstruction— in short, the reinvention of the human being."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Networking re-materialises the body it does not dematerialise it` (CRITICAL for the `Material vs Immaterial` tension — Ascott takes the anti-dematerialisation pole; cross-link `Embodiment`, `The Post-Biological Body`, and explicitly Lippard's `Dematerialization` as a contrast).

- **Claim: Connectivity is a feature of the evolutionary process; the disappearance of the unitary self equals in importance the appearance of the zero.**
  - Reasoning structure: an evolutionary-cosmic framing of the move from the one (isolated self) to the many (distributed selves) and to connectivity.
  - Source passage: > "It could be argued that the appearance of the zero in Western thought has been equalled in importance only by the disappearance of the one, the single and solitary, whose boundary and isolation was hermetically complete. Our impulse is that of connectivity. Connectivity is a significant feature of the evolutionary process."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Connectivity is an evolutionary impulse and the one is disappearing` (anchors the concept `Connectivity` and `The Distributed Self`).

- **Claim: Context must supersede content — the artist constructs a context (a hypercontext), not content; education too becomes interface.**
  - Reasoning structure: in interactive systems content IS interactivity between viewer and interface, so the durable creative act is the design of context.
  - Source passage: > "Just as the artist is concerned no longer with the creation of content but with the construction of a context (the goal is a hypercontext), so, too, the brain seeks its hypercontext in the hyperbody."
  - Source passage: > "Here, too, the telematic rule that context must supersede content applies."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/In telematic art context must supersede content` (cross-link `Interaction`, `The Medium Is the Message` as a McLuhan resonance).

- **Claim: The new art is one of emergence not representation — it abandons the mimetic/symbolic to create worlds rather than imitate them.**
  - Reasoning structure: the biological/constructivist metaphor frame replaces the mind-as-mirror model; art's role shifts from representing the world to inventing alternative realities from which the participant-emergent work arises.
  - Source passage: > "we are witnessing the end of an art in which the mimetic and symbolic were central. Its role in providing representations of the world is shifting to that of creating worlds, of inventing alternative realities, a process in which the observer of art becomes an active participant, a process from which art emerges."
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The new art is emergence not representation` (anchors `Emergence` with an Ascott section; cross-link `The Observer`, Burnham systems esthetics).

## Metaphors

Important analogies, images, figures.

- **The Datapool / the data sea / "plunge into the datapool" / "dive to its depths of meaning"** — the master immersion metaphor; art as a fluid moving stream of data configurations. (See concept above; recommend metaphor note `The Datapool`.)
- **The Data Garden / seedbed / "dynamic organisms rather than fixed and static structures"** — CAiiA and the new institutions as living, growing, data-fed organisms that "blossom." Candidate metaphor `The Data Garden`.
- **Gaia as space platform / friend not mother / "Our eyes are set on the stars"** — the shift from earth-as-host-body to earth-as-space-platform; the longing to escape the body and earth ("We can't wait to go, we need lift-off, and we need it now!").
  - Source passage: > "We see Gaia now more as a friend than as a mother, more as a space platform than as a host body."
- **"In the beginning, the Word was Telenoia"** — the biblical inversion (John 1:1); telenoia as the very first, oldest human word, lost to left-hemisphere linear mechanical thinking.
  - Source passage: > "But if, in the beginning, the Word was Telenoia; it is evident now that we lost it, perhaps in inverse ratio to our gain in verbal intelligence..."
- **"Love in the telematic embrace is a many-splendoured thing... a multisplendoured thing"** — the title-keyword of the book ("the telematic embrace") recast for telepresence/community; cybersex and virtual affection.
- **The cobweb of light / honeycomb of illuminated colour** — the optical-fibre Net; "this cobweb of light, this honeycomb of illuminated colour."
- **The big C — Gaia becomes Caiia / "the connectivity and computerisation of Gaia"** — the pun marking the move from the Gaia project to the CAiiA institution.
- **Memory in a cube / "a few tablespoons of jelly in a glass"** — Birge's bacteriorhodopsin molecular memory; the biological computer.
- **Left vs right hemisphere / patriarchy replacing matriarchy** — the cultural prioritisation of "linear, mechanical thinking" over "visual, mosaic, all-at-once thinking" (a McLuhan-resonant figure).

## Tensions and Contradictions

Conceptual oppositions, paradoxes, unresolved problems.

- **Telenoia vs Paranoia** — THE keystone opposition of the chapter: networked, distributed, convivial consciousness vs the isolated, alienated, secretive single self of industrial culture. Recommend tension note `Telenoia vs Paranoia`.
  - Source passage: > "The secrecy and suspicion sustained by the paradigm of paranoia, which is what the smokestack culture engendered..."
- **Re-materialisation vs Dematerialisation / Immateriality** — Ascott explicitly REFUSES disembodiment/immateriality, insisting on re-materialisation into a new (bionic) corporeality. This is his distinct pole within the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` tension and a direct counter to Lippard's `Dematerialization` and the disembodiment reading of Hayles. (Record in `Material vs Immaterial`; cross-link.)
- **Content vs Context (Context supersedes content)** — the telematic rule; the artist constructs context (hypercontext), not content. Resonant with McLuhan's medium/content. Recommend tension `Content vs Context` (or fold into the `Interaction` / context argument).
- **The One vs The Many (unitary self vs distributed selves)** — the disappearance of the single, hermetically isolated self; "the explosion of the one and the connectivity of the many." Possible tension `The One vs The Many` or fold into `The Distributed Self`.
- **Left Hemisphere vs Right Hemisphere / Linear vs Mosaic thinking** — logic's "authoritative gravity" vs the "dazzling speed of intuition"; the distributed nonlinear mind distrusted by institutionalised authority.
- **Earthbound vs Cosmic / Body vs Out-of-Frame** — "moving not so much out-of-body as out-of-frame"; the longing to escape the earthly plane for the cosmic plane (with its troubling ethical cost — "callous indifference to homelessness"). An unresolved moral tension Ascott himself flags.
- **Representation vs Emergence** — the end of mimetic/symbolic art; art that creates worlds and emerges from participation rather than representing the world.
