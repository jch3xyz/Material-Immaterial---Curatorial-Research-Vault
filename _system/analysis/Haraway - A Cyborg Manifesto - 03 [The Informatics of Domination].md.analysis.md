## Executive Summary

This is the pivotal theoretical engine of *A Cyborg Manifesto*. Having introduced the cyborg as an ironic, blasphemous figure in the preceding sections (the canonical definition — "A cyborg is a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism" — and the slogans "The cyborg is our ontology" and "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess" live in sibling chapters 01 and 06, NOT here), Haraway here names and diagrams the historical condition the cyborg inhabits: **the informatics of domination**. She argues that worldwide social relations are undergoing a transformation "analogous in its novelty and scope to that created by industrial capitalism" — "a movement from an organic, industrial society to a polymorphous, information system—from all work to all play, a deadly game." She renders this as a two-column chart of transitions "from the comfortable old hierarchical dominations to the scary new networks I have called the informatics of domination" (Organics of Domination → Informatics of Domination): representation → simulation, organism → biotic component, depth/integrity → surface/boundary, reproduction → replication, white capitalist patriarchy → informatics of domination, etc.

The chapter's central claim is that **communications sciences and modern biologies are constructed by a common move — "the translation of the world into a problem of coding"** — a search for a common language in which "all resistance to instrumental control disappears and all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange." In communications, this is condensed in the military metaphor **C3I (command-control-communication-intelligence)**; in biology, the organism becomes "biotic components," i.e. "information-processing devices," and "[b]iology here is a kind of cryptography." The material substrate of this regime is microelectronics — "the technical basis of simulacra—that is, of copies without originals."

Two of the chapter's lines are load-bearing for the vault. First, the boundary-collapse thesis: "the difference between machine and organism is thoroughly blurred; mind, body, and tool are on very intimate terms," and "[t]he boundary-maintaining images of base and superstructure, public and private, or material and ideal never seemed more feeble" — a direct material/immaterial entanglement. Second, the explicit displacement of Foucault: "**The cyborg is not subject to Foucault's biopolitics; the cyborg simulates politics, a much more potent field of operations.**" Haraway also introduces, via Rachel Grossman, the figure of **women in the integrated circuit**, and stages the refusal of return to origin or nature ("It's not just that 'god' is dead; so is the 'goddess'"). The chapter closes by insisting this is not technological determinism but "the social relations of science and technology," and that these rearrangements "can make socialist-feminism more relevant to effective progressive politics."

## Arguments

- **Claim: We are living through a shift from organic/industrial society to a coded information system — the informatics of domination — comparable in scope to the rise of industrial capitalism.**
  - Reasoning structure: Periodizing claim. Names a new "system of world order"; renders it as a chart of paired transitions; argues the new order is "[s]imultaneously material and ideological" and that "[w]e cannot go back ideologically or materially."
  - Evidence: the two-column chart (representation → simulation, organism → biotic component, reproduction → replication, white capitalist patriarchy → informatics of domination, etc.); the "mundane, largely economic reality" of microelectronics underwriting "[m]odern states, multinational corporations, military power... the international division of labor."
  - Source passage: > I argue for a politics rooted in claims about fundamental changes in the nature of class, race, and gender in an emerging system of world order analogous in its novelty and scope to that created by industrial capitalism
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The world has shifted from organic domination to the informatics of domination`

- **Claim: Communications sciences and modern biologies share one move — translating the world into a problem of coding — which dissolves all resistance to instrumental control.**
  - Reasoning structure: Identifies a common structural operation across two apparently distinct domains (information theory / cybernetics and molecular biology / immunology), then shows it produces "unhindered instrumental power (called effective communication)."
  - Evidence: cybernetic feedback systems applied to "telephone technology, computer design, weapons deployment, or database construction"; molecular genetics, ecology, sociobiology, immunobiology; the organism as "biotic components"; C3I; "[b]iology here is a kind of cryptography."
  - Source passage: > the translation of the world into a problem of coding, a search for a common language in which all resistance to instrumental control disappears and all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Communications sciences and biology translate the world into a problem of coding`

- **Claim: Cyborg politics supersede Foucault's biopolitics — the cyborg "simulates politics," a more potent field of operations.**
  - Reasoning structure: A pointed displacement. Where biopolitics governs the integrity of natural bodies, the informatics of domination operates on "boundary conditions and interfaces, on rates of flow across boundaries—and not on the integrity of natural objects"; "[n]o objects, spaces, or bodies are sacred in themselves." Hence the cyborg is no longer the object of a power that invests life, but a simulation.
  - Evidence: "'Integrity' or 'sincerity' of the Western self gives way to decision procedures and expert systems"; the privileged pathology is "stress—communications breakdown"; the explicit line.
  - Source passage: > The cyborg is not subject to Foucault's biopolitics; the cyborg simulates politics, a much more potent field of operations.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The cyborg is not subject to biopolitics it simulates politics` (the focus brief frames this as the keystone Foucault engagement; also the spine of the `Donna Haraway and Michel Foucault` reference note). NOTE: the focus brief's fuller phrasing "Foucault's biopolitics is a flaccid premonition of cyborg politics" is from a DIFFERENT passage in the manifesto (not present verbatim in this chapter); do not attribute that exact line to ch.03.

- **Claim: There is no return to nature, the organic, or the goddess; the old naturalistic ground is gone for both god and goddess.**
  - Reasoning structure: Reads the chart's right-hand column to conclude its objects "cannot be coded as 'natural,'" which "subverts naturalistic coding for the left-hand side as well." Therefore essentialist appeals (sex, sex role, race-as-essence) are "unmasked as irrational."
  - Evidence: "Sexual reproduction is one kind of reproductive strategy among many"; race recast as "frequencies of parameters, like blood groups or intelligence scores"; the strange-bedfellows irony (corporate executives + antiporn radical feminists).
  - Source passage: > It's not just that "god" is dead; so is the "goddess." Or both are revivified in the worlds charged with microelectronic and biotechnological politics.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: this supports the symbol `I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess` and the refusal-of-innocence theme — but the slogan itself is in ch.06. Recommend: feed as SECONDARY support to those notes; optionally `arguments/There is no return to nature god or goddess`. Flag for human review whether to mint a distinct argument or fold into the goddess symbol note.

- **Claim: This is not technological determinism but the *social relations* of science and technology — and these rearrangements can renew socialist-feminism.**
  - Reasoning structure: A guarding qualification closing the chapter; insists on "a historical system depending on structured relations among people," not technology as autonomous cause, and reframes the analysis as politically enabling rather than merely diagnostic.
  - Evidence: the "odd circumlocution the social relations of science and technology"; Latour cited; "myth and tool mutually constitute each other."
  - Source passage: > Some of the rearrangements of race, sex, and class rooted in high-tech–facilitated social relations can make socialist-feminism more relevant to effective progressive politics.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/The informatics of domination is a social relation not a technological determinism` (also the seat of the double-vision-of-domination-and-possibility theme the focus brief flags).

- **Stress as the privileged pathology / communications breakdown** — in a coded system, illness is recoded as signal failure; the AIDS-as-boundary-confusion example ("a stressed system goes awry; its communication processes break down; it fails to recognize the difference between self and other").

- **Material vs Immaterial** — the chapter's strongest cross-vault contribution. The informatics of domination is "[s]imultaneously material and ideological"; "[t]he boundary-maintaining images of base and superstructure, public and private, or material and ideal never seemed more feeble"; "the difference between machine and organism is thoroughly blurred." Microelectronics is the material substrate of simulacra ("copies without originals"). This is a Haraway author-section candidate for the existing `tensions/Material vs Immaterial` (currently 5 authors).
- **Domination vs Possibility (double vision)** — the "scary new networks" are simultaneously a "matrix of complex dominations" and the condition that "can make socialist-feminism more relevant"; the chapter holds diagnosis and emancipatory possibility in one frame. (The focus brief's "double vision of domination AND possibility.")
- **Organic / hierarchical vs Networked / coded** — the structuring opposition of the whole chart: depth/integrity vs surface/boundary, organism vs biotic component, reproduction vs replication.
- **Nature/essence vs Design/coding** — "one must think not in terms of essential properties, but in terms of design, boundary constraints, rates of flows, systems logics."
- **Biopolitics vs Simulation** — the cyborg displaces Foucault: power over the integrity of natural bodies (biopolitics) vs power over boundary conditions and rates of flow (the cyborg that "simulates politics"). Direct bridge to the existing `tensions/Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics` (Han) — a three-way conversation about what supersedes Foucault.
- **Myth vs Tool (and their mutual constitution)** — "The boundary is permeable between tool and myth, instrument and concept... Indeed, myth and tool mutually constitute each other." A reflexive tension Haraway refuses to resolve.
