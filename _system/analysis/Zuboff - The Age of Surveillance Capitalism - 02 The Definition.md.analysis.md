## Executive Summary

This is the book's masthead — a single mock-lexicon entry that formally defines the title term. It is set out exactly as a dictionary headword (`Sur-veil-lance Cap-i-tal-ism, n.`) followed by eight numbered senses. Each sense is a one-clause thesis that the rest of the book unpacks across nineteen substantive chapters. The chapter contains no exposition, no examples, and no argumentation; it is the load-bearing definitional spine that names the keystone concept (`Surveillance` / surveillance capitalism) and forward-declares almost every major concept in the corpus: the claiming of human experience as free raw material (extraction → behavioral surplus → prediction), the subordination of goods-and-services production to a "global architecture of behavioral modification," the diagnosis of surveillance capitalism as a "rogue mutation of capitalism," the framing of it as a twenty-first-century threat to human nature parallel to industrial capitalism's nineteenth-/twentieth-century threat to nature, the rise of a new "instrumentarian power," the project of a "new collective order based on total certainty," and the political framing of the whole as a "coup from above" — an "overthrow of the people's sovereignty."

Because this is the definitional kernel, it is the canonical verbatim anchor for the top-level concept `Surveillance` (the committed forward-stub this book realizes) and for the durable concept notes the later chapters will fill out. The analysis below names each of the eight senses as a candidate concept anchor while flagging which ones receive their fuller primary exposition in later chapters (so the planner can EXTEND, not over-create, from a four-line source). The chapter is short and clean (not OCR-corrupted), so every quotation below is a verbatim substring.

## Arguments

- **Claim: Surveillance capitalism is a parasitic, rogue economic logic, not the essence of capitalism — it subordinates the production of goods and services to behavioral modification.**
  - Reasoning structure: by calling it "parasitic" and a "rogue mutation," Zuboff frames it as a contingent deviation that hosts on (and distorts) ordinary capitalism, which makes it diagnosable and contestable rather than inevitable.
  - Evidence: the definitional clauses themselves (senses 2 and 3).
  - Source passage: > A parasitic economic logic in which the production of goods and services is subordinated to a new global architecture of behavioral modification
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Surveillance capitalism is a parasitic rogue mutation not the essence of capitalism`

- **Claim: Surveillance capitalism threatens human nature in this century as industrial capitalism threatened nature in the last.**
  - Reasoning structure: historical analogy positioning the book as the equivalent of an environmental reckoning, but for the interior human.
  - Evidence: sense 5 of the definition.
  - Source passage: > As significant a threat to human nature in the twenty-first century as industrial capitalism was to the natural world in the nineteenth and twentieth
  - Confidence: high (Directly stated)
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Surveillance capitalism threatens human nature as industrial capitalism threatened nature`

- **Claim: Surveillance capitalism is a coup from above — an expropriation of human rights and an overthrow of popular sovereignty.**
  - Reasoning structure: reframes an economic phenomenon as a political-constitutional one; the harm is not merely market concentration but the loss of self-rule.
