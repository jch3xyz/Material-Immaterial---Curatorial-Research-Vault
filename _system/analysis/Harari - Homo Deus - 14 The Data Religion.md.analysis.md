# Analysis: Homo Deus / Part III / Ch. 14 (book Ch. 11) — The Data Religion

> Note on numbering: the raw file's front-matter labels this `chapter: 11` (Harari's own chapter number), but the vault file is `14 The Data Religion.md`. It is the final chapter of *Homo Deus*. As such it functions as the book's synthetic conclusion — it gathers and names the techno-religion (DATAISM) toward which the whole argument has been building and restates the book's three closing questions.

## Executive Summary

This is the culminating chapter of *Homo Deus*. Harari introduces and names **Dataism** — the emerging techno-religion (and, he argues, the new dominant "scientific dogma") that holds that "the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing." He stages Dataism as the convergence of two scientific tidal waves: the life-sciences' reduction of organisms to **biochemical algorithms** (post-Darwin) and computer science's engineering of ever-more-sophisticated **electronic algorithms** (post-Turing). Their fusion — "exactly the same mathematical laws apply to both" — collapses the barrier between animals and machines and predicts that electronic algorithms will eventually decipher and outperform biochemical ones.

The chapter then runs a series of moves that are characteristic of the whole book's endgame:

1. **Dataism as a unifying scientific paradigm** that inverts the old pyramid of learning (data → information → knowledge → wisdom): humans can no longer distil the data deluge, so the work is handed to algorithms, and Dataists grow "sceptical about human knowledge and wisdom."
2. **Everything reread as data-processing** — organisms, societies (beehives, cities), and above all the *economy* and *politics*. Capitalism vs. communism and democracy vs. dictatorship are recast as competing data-processing architectures (distributed vs. centralised); capitalism/democracy "won" the twentieth century not because they were ethical or "good" but because distributed processing handled accelerating technological change better. (Hayek, Lysenko, the apocryphal Gorbachev/London-bread story.)
3. **The hollowing-out of politics** — as technology outpaces politics ("the governmental tortoise cannot keep up with the technological hare"), grand political vision dies; government becomes "mere administration"; power leaks into a complex system that no one — not despots, not a cabal of billionaires — actually controls. "Having power means knowing what to ignore."
4. **History in a nutshell** — the whole human species reread as a single data-processing system whose history is the story of improving its efficiency (more processors, more variety, more connections, freer flow), culminating in the **Internet-of-All-Things**, after which "Homo sapiens will vanish."
5. **A new value: freedom of information** — the first genuinely new value since 1789, given not to humans but to *information* itself. Dataism becomes a missionary religion with commandments (maximise data flow; connect everything; the greatest sin is to block the flow / "What is death, if not a situation when information doesn't flow?"). Aaron Swartz as "Dataism's first martyr." "Record, Upload, Share" as the new motto; experiences are valueless unless shared and converted into free-flowing data.
6. **"Know Thyself" inverted** — humanism commanded "Listen to your feelings!"; Dataism commands "Listen to the algorithms! They know how you feel." Feelings are revalued as evolutionarily-honed-but-now-obsolete biochemical algorithms; self-knowledge migrates from sunsets, museums and private diaries to DNA sequencing, biometric wearables, and Google/Facebook surveillance. The master algorithms themselves become a black box even their makers cannot fully understand.
7. **A ripple in the data flow** — Harari supplies the heretic's objections he gestured at earlier (the chapter twice points back to "Chapter 3" / the consciousness chapters): we have no idea how data flows could produce **consciousness**, and life may not reduce to **decision-making**. But even a *false* Dataism could conquer the world (so did Christianity and communism). The shift from a homo-centric to a **data-centric** worldview would do to Homo sapiens what Homo sapiens did to other animals — reduce us from engineers to chips, to data, "a ripple within the cosmic data flow." The book closes on three questions: (1) Are organisms really just algorithms / is life really just data processing? (2) What is more valuable, intelligence or consciousness? (3) What happens when non-conscious but highly intelligent algorithms know us better than we know ourselves?

For this vault the chapter is enormously important: **Harari is the popularizer/coiner of "Dataism" itself** — the very concept that the already-existing `concepts/Dataism.md` note treats *only through Byung-Chul Han's critique* (Han cites Brooks and Anderson, not Harari, but Harari is the figure who turned the term into a "religion"). This chapter is therefore a textbook cross-book merge: it adds the foundational author-section to a note that currently lacks its own progenitor. It also feeds `The Computational Universe`, `Big Data`, `Information`, `Patternism`, `Algorithmic Determinism`, `The Algorithm as Providence`, `Posthumanism`, `Transhumanism`, and the `Material vs Immaterial` / `Embodiment vs Information` tensions.

Institutions / technologies / movements:
- **Dataism** / **the Data Religion** — the chapter's central subject.
- **The Internet-of-All-Things** — Dataism's eschaton; the cosmic, all-knowing, all-powerful data-processing system "like God."
- **JSTOR**, **MIT computer network**, **RSS protocol**, **"Guerilla Open Access"** — the Swartz episode.
- **Google, Facebook, Science / Nature, Wikipedia, Sotheby's** — the apparatus and emblems of data-flow culture; "Who writes Wikipedia? All of us."

- **The NSA** — "may be spying on your every word," yet Washington "knows what to do with all the data."
- Wearable biometric devices, DNA sequencing, machine learning, artificial neural networks.

Disciplines: biology, computer science, economics, political science, musicology — the disciplines Dataism claims to unify under one paradigm ("King Lear and the flu virus are just two patterns of data flow").

## Arguments

- **Claim: Dataism is the convergence of two scientific waves and collapses the animal/machine barrier.**
  - Reasoning structure: biology (post-Darwin) makes organisms biochemical algorithms; computer science (post-Turing) makes electronic algorithms; Dataism asserts the *same* mathematical laws govern both, so the two are commensurable and the electronic will outdo the biochemical.
  - Evidence: the 150-year and 80-year framings; "King Lear and the flu virus are just two patterns of data flow."
  - Source passage: > Dataism puts the two together, pointing out that exactly the same mathematical laws apply to both biochemical and electronic algorithms. Dataism thereby collapses the barrier between animals and machines, and expects electronic algorithms to eventually decipher and outperform biochemical algorithms.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dataism collapses the barrier between animals and machines`

- **Claim: Capitalism beat communism (and democracy beat dictatorship) because distributed data-processing outperforms centralised processing — not because it is more ethical.**
  - Reasoning structure: reframes ideology as architecture; under accelerating technological change, distributed processing self-corrects and innovates where a single central processor's mistakes are catastrophic.
  - Evidence: Hayek on prices; Lysenko and Soviet wheat imports; the apocryphal "nobody is in charge of supplying bread to London"; "you can produce nuclear bombs by the cartload, but you won't get an Apple or a Wikipedia."
  - Source passage: > Capitalism did not defeat communism because capitalism was more ethical, because individual liberties are sacred or because God was angry with the heathen communists. Rather, capitalism won the Cold War because distributed data processing works better than centralised data processing, at least in periods of accelerating technological changes.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Capitalism won the Cold War because distributed data-processing beats centralised processing`

- **Claim: As technology outpaces politics, grand political vision dies and power dissolves into a system no one controls.**
  - Reasoning structure: politics evolved when it moved faster than technology; now technology has "switched from first gear to fourth" while politics still moves "at the days of steam"; democratic and authoritarian structures alike are "overwhelmed by data"; therefore government becomes "mere administration," and even billionaires/conspiracies cannot run a system "far too complex."
  - Evidence: the unvoted shape of cyberspace; the NSA / poker-player-who-knows-the-cards-yet-loses image; petty-minded contemporary leaders vs. Lenin/Hitler/Mao.
  - Source passage: > The governmental tortoise cannot keep up with the technological hare. It is overwhelmed by data.
  - Source passage: > Government has become mere administration. It manages the country, but it no longer leads it.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/As technology outpaces politics grand political vision dies`

- **Claim: A genuinely new value — freedom of information — has emerged, the first since 1789, and it is given to information rather than to humans.**
  - Reasoning structure: humanity rarely invents new values; the last was the 1789 trio (liberty, equality, fraternity); Dataism's "freedom of information" is the first since; crucially it is *not* the liberal freedom of expression, because the right belongs to information, not the human, and may override human rights to own/restrict data.
  - Evidence: the Brooks-less framing (Harari does not cite Brooks here); Aaron Swartz as "Dataism's first martyr"; JSTOR.
  - Source passage: > Dataism is the first movement since 1789 that created a really novel value: freedom of information.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Freedom of information is the first new value since 1789`

- **Claim: Humanism commanded "Listen to your feelings"; Dataism commands "Listen to the algorithms — they know how you feel."**
  - Reasoning structure: feelings were the best data-processing algorithms for millions of years (evolutionarily honed, the "voice of millions of ancestors"), so humanism rightly sanctified them; but in the twenty-first century external algorithms with vast databases surpass feelings, so authority for self-knowledge should migrate from introspection (sunsets, diaries, Goethe, democratic elections) to data (DNA sequencing, biometric wearables, Google/Facebook).
  - Evidence: the Babylon-stars → Bible → humanist-introspection → Dataist-algorithm sequence; the explicit "practical Dataist guidelines."
  - Source passage: > Whereas humanism commanded: 'Listen to your feelings!' Dataism now commands: 'Listen to the algorithms! They know how you feel.'
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dataism replaces Listen to your feelings with Listen to the algorithms`

- **Claim: Even if Dataism is factually wrong, it can still conquer the world — and would dethrone Homo sapiens as Homo sapiens dethroned the animals.**
  - Reasoning structure: religions (Christianity, communism) spread despite factual mistakes; Dataism's prospects are *better* because it is a single paradigm spreading across all disciplines, so interdisciplinary exchange only reinforces it ("an unassailable dogma"); once authority shifts from humans to algorithms, the humanist projects (health, happiness, power) become irrelevant, and humans are reduced "from engineers to chips, then to data."
  - Evidence: the wolf/poem example (value lies in turning experience into free-flowing data, "a question of survival"); the mammoths and Chinese river dolphins; "a ripple within the cosmic data flow."
  - Source passage: > Dataism thereby threatens to do to Homo sapiens what Homo sapiens has done to all other animals.
  - Source passage: > Looking back, humanity will turn out to be just a ripple within the cosmic data flow.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Even a false Dataism could dethrone Homo sapiens as we dethroned the animals`

- **Claim (the heretic's counter, supplied by Harari himself): life may not reduce to data flows, because we cannot explain how data flows produce consciousness, and life may not reduce to decision-making.**
  - Reasoning structure: a self-administered critique — Dataism's two soft spots are (a) consciousness ("we have no idea how or why data flows could produce consciousness and subjective experiences") and (b) the reduction of life to decision-making ("is that their sole meaning?").
  - Evidence: explicit back-reference "As we saw in Chapter 3"; the three closing questions.
  - Source passage: > In particular, at present we have no idea how or why data flows could produce consciousness and subjective experiences. Maybe we'll have a good explanation in twenty years. But maybe we'll discover that organisms aren't algorithms after all.
  - Confidence: high
  - Recommended wiki note: `arguments/Dataism cannot explain consciousness or reduce life to decision-making`

## Metaphors

- **The governmental tortoise and the technological hare** — "The governmental tortoise cannot keep up with the technological hare." (politics outpaced by technology)
- **The poker player who knows his opponents' cards yet keeps losing** — "It's like a poker player who knows what cards his opponents hold, yet somehow still manages to lose round after round." (the NSA / US foreign policy: data without wisdom)
- **The invisible hand of the data flow** — "Just as free-market capitalists believe in the invisible hand of the market, so Dataists believe in the invisible hand of the data flow." (a deliberate transposition of Adam Smith's figure)
- **The individual as a tiny chip inside a giant system nobody understands** — "The individual is becoming a tiny chip inside a giant system that nobody really understands." Also the species reread as a computer with "individual humans serving as its chips."
- **A ripple within the cosmic data flow / a clump of earth dissolving in a gushing river** — "we might dissolve within the data torrent like a clump of earth within a gushing river"; "humanity will turn out to be just a ripple within the cosmic data flow." (the dissolution of the human)
- **Retiring the horses (not upgrading them)** — "When the car replaced the horse-drawn carriage, we didn't upgrade the horses – we retired them. Perhaps it is time to do the same with Homo sapiens." (obsolescence of the human algorithm)
- **The cosmic data-processing system "like God" / the Internet-of-All-Things as deity** — Dataism's eschatology in explicitly theological terms; Kurzweil's *The Singularity is Near* echoing John the Baptist's "the kingdom of heaven is near."
- **The spider's web becoming the steel-and-asphalt grid** — the four-stage globalization of the data network from Columbus to the twenty-first century.
- **Digital Dadaism** *(Han's phrase, not Harari's)* — relevant only as a contrast at the merge: Harari's Dataism is the *believer's* account; Han calls the same thing "digital Dadaism" and "nihilism." Do not attribute to Harari.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Intelligence vs. Consciousness** — the chapter's pivotal opposition and one of its three closing questions ("What's more valuable – intelligence or consciousness?"); non-conscious algorithms may outperform conscious intelligence at every task.
- **Homo-centric vs. Data-centric (humanism vs. Dataism)** — the "religious revolution": humanism worships human experience/feeling; Dataism declares experiences "valueless if they are not shared" and migrates authority to algorithms.
- **Distributed vs. Centralised processing** — the master schema by which capitalism/communism and democracy/dictatorship are reread; not a moral but an architectural opposition.
- **Freedom of information vs. Freedom of expression** — the new value (given to information) collides with the old liberal value (given to humans, including the right to keep silent / own data).
- **Experience vs. Data (having an experience vs. converting it into free-flowing data)** — "value lies not in having experiences, but in turning these experiences into free-flowing data."
- **Material vs. Immaterial / Embodiment vs. Information** — implicit but strong: the human as embodied, savannah-evolved algorithm vs. the human as recodable/retire-able pattern in the data flow (links the vault's existing `Material vs Immaterial` and `Embodiment vs Information` tensions).
- **Vision vs. Administration (politics)** — grand twentieth-century vision (with its Auschwitz/Hiroshima/Great-Leap dangers) vs. petty-minded but safe contemporary administration; Harari leaves this genuinely unresolved ("Lack of vision isn't always a blessing").
