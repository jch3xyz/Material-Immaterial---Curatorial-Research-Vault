# The No-Sculptures (Goodman)

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Jack Burnham reports a single, specific exhibition: Sam Goodman's *No-Sculptures*, shown at the Gallery Gertrude Stein in the spring of 1964. The works were stone castings of excretions. Burnham describes the show as having "featured twenty-three stone castings of excretions, ranging from small, perhaps human size, samples to a five-foot offering worthy of an unwell pachyderm." He names them flatly as the terminus of a vocabulary: "These No-Sculptures by Sam Goodman were literally the end result of the biomorphic vocabulary." `Directly stated`

The No-Sculptures are not a metaphor Burnham coins; they are an actual object/event he cites as the closing emblem of a tradition. Within the note they function as a symbol — the concrete image that stands for the exhaustion of an entire mode of sculpture. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The coup de grace of vitalism.** Burnham dates the death of Abstract Expressionism precisely ("12 A.M., January 1, 1960") and then offers the Goodman show as the analogous death-blow to [[Vitalism]]: "one suspects that the coup de grace may have been delivered with the Gallery Gertrude Stein's exhibition of No-Sculptures during the spring of 1964." The excrement-castings are the point past which the vitalist faith in an "inner life" buried within organic form cannot continue. `Directly stated`

- **The terminal emblem of [[Form Exhaustion]].** The chapter charts how the biomorphic-mineral idiom reached "a peak during the early 1960's, and with this plethora came the reductio ad absurdum of the vitalistic idiom." The No-Sculptures are that reductio made literal: the biomorphic vocabulary followed to its last organic referent, the body's waste. They mark where invention can push "sculpture" no further within the vitalist domain. `Strongly implied`

- **A scatological reply to reductivism.** Burnham frames the castings as a direct retort to R. H. Wilenski's reductivist dictum. "As tactile, organic counterparts, these elemental offerings were a succinct reply to Wilenski's dictum that (1932, p. 163) 'Essential sculpture is sculpture which has the same kind of meaning as the sphere, cube and cylinder.'" Where Wilenski sought the irreducible Euclidean solid, Goodman offers the irreducible organic residue — the "essential" object of the *biotic* lineage rather than the geometric one. `Directly stated`

## Context

The symbol appears at the hinge of Chapter 4, "Form Exhaustion and the Rise of Phenomenalism," in Burnham's argument that two parallel languages — geometric formalism and organic vitalism — had each run out of moves by the early 1960s. Wilenski (1932) supplies the geometric pole of essential reduction (sphere, cube, cylinder); the No-Sculptures supply the organic pole's terminal answer to it. Burnham reaches the show only after cataloguing, in a single exhausted sentence, the late biomorphic glut of "bubbly cast bronzes with pock-marked openings... bulbous potatolike carvings... limestone randomly caressed to resemble enlarged beach pebbles." The Goodman castings are where that catalogue stops. `Interpretive synthesis`

Read forward, the symbol carries Burnham's larger dialectic: sculpture's "steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality" and away from the "anthropomorphic ideal." The death of vitalism that the No-Sculptures emblematize clears the ground for the phenomenalism the chapter title promises — and, in the vault's terms, for the longer trajectory toward [[Dematerialization]]. `Speculative connection`
