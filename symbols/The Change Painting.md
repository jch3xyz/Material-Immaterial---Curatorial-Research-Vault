# The Change Painting

Ascott's signature founding artwork (c. 1959–63): the Change-Paintings and Hinged Reliefs — works built from interchangeable transparent panels (and reliefs with hinged flaps) that the spectator physically slides, alters, and recombines. The object is unfinished by design; it is completed only in the act of being changed. It is the keystone object — and the deliberate anti-object — of [[Behaviourist Art]] and of Ascott's 1964 essay "The Construction of Change."

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Ascott's own catalogue statements, the Change Paintings are "Change-Paintings and Hinged Reliefs (1959–61)" made of "Interchangeable transparent panels (supporting purely formal images) that slide behind each other within a rectangular framework by the manipulation of the spectator," together with "Structures (wall reliefs) with hinged flaps that can be altered, again by the observer's manipulation." `Directly stated` (ch. 07, Appendix A). A Change Painting is thus literally a moveable, reconfigurable assembly of Plexiglas/wood/oil elements — Shanken's catalogue caption records *Change Painting*, 1959, "Plexiglas, wood, and oil, 66 × 21", Two different states" — not a fixed image but a field of states the viewer actuates.

For Ascott the operative event is not the panels but the manipulation: "The act of changing becomes a vital part of the total aesthetic experience of the participant." `Directly stated` (Ascott statement quoted in ch. 04). The spectator is reclassified as a participant/manipulator; the artwork's meaning is the relationship the participant builds, not the static configuration "at a distance."

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The object as an analogue of an idea.** Ascott frames the Change Painting not as a representation but as a working model: his paintings and constructions "function as analogues of ideas—structures that are subject to change and human intervention in the way that ideas themselves are." `Directly stated` (ch. 05). The symbol stands for thought-in-motion — a thing whose mutability *is* its content.
- **The anti-object / the dematerialized art object.** By relocating the aesthetic experience into the act of changing rather than the fixed artifact, the Change Painting symbolizes the shift from the discrete art commodity toward process, behaviour, and system — a precursor to [[Dematerialization]] and a cousin of [[Conceptual Art]]. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **The viewer made cybernetic.** The transparent panel that the hand slides is the hinge between [[Behaviourist Art]] and [[Cybernetics]]: it makes feedback literal, turning contemplation into a loop of input and response between participant and work. `Strongly implied` (ch. 07 develops the cybernetic vision around exactly this participatory mechanism).

## Context

The Change Paintings recur as the origin point of Ascott's entire trajectory. They are the studio practice from which "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" (1966–67) is drawn — Ascott states that his attitudes "derive from visual/plastic 'thinking' in his studio," and lists the Change-Paintings first among the supporting works (ch. 07, Appendix A). In Shanken's editorial framing (ch. 04), the Change Painting is the seed of the long arc "from cybernetics to telematics": the interchangeable, spectator-actuated panel is the modest physical precursor of the later participatory, networked, [[Reciprocal Communication|reciprocal]] artwork. The motif's persistence — object that exists only in being changed — is what makes it a symbol rather than merely an early work: it encodes Ascott's lifelong thesis that art is [[Art as Experiential Technology|experiential technology]], an instrument for participation rather than a finished image.
