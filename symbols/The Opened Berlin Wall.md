# The Opened Berlin Wall

The opened Berlin Wall — the gates at the Bornholmer Street passage swung wide near midnight on November 9, 1989 — is [[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s emblem of the **synthetic declaration**: the moment a collective certainty dissolves an apparently inevitable order that no amount of individual evasion could touch. Across two decades, courageous East Berliners dug seventy-one tunnels beneath the city; these *counter-declarations* saved several hundred people but "did not bring down the wall or the power that sustained it." The wall fell instead when "those people had become so certain of themselves, and the officials so unsure of themselves, that the answer was We will." The symbol concentrates the book's strategic argument into a single image: against a power that founds itself by [[Conquest by Declaration|declaration]], only a counter-declaration of equal scope — a *synthetic* one that changes the game rather than seeking shelter within it — can transform the field.

## Literal Meaning in the Source

Literally, the figure names the real history of the Berlin Wall as Zuboff narrates it in Chapter 11. From 1961 into the early 1980s, she writes, "courageous East Berliners carved seventy-one tunnels through the sandy soil beneath the city, affording several hundred people a means of escape to West Berlin" — these are her literal *counter-declarations*, real but local acts of evasion (`Directly stated`). The "synthetic declaration gathered force over decades, but its full expression would have to wait until near midnight on November 9, 1989, when Harald Jäger, the senior officer on duty that night at the Bornholmer Street passage, gave the order to open the gates, and twenty thousand people surged across the wall into West Berlin" (`Directly stated`).

The hinge of the image is the exchange at the gate, which Zuboff quotes from a historian: "By the night of November 9, when the people appeared at the Berlin Wall and demanded to know of the border officials, *Will you let us pass?*, those people had become so certain of themselves, and the officials so unsure of themselves, that the answer was *We will*" (`Directly stated`). The wall came down not because it was breached from below but because the certainty that sustained it migrated from the officials to the crowd.

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The synthetic declaration that changes the game.** The opened wall is the literal illustration Zuboff supplies for her central strategic distinction. "If the declaration is 'check,' the counter-declaration is 'checkmate,' and the synthetic declaration changes the game. It asserts an alternative framework that transforms the opposing facts." The tunnels "checkmate" individual positions without ending the game; the opened gate is the game itself transformed. See [[The Counter-Declaration and the Synthetic Declaration]]. (`Directly stated`)
- **Tunnels are not enough — attend to the wall, not the tunnels.** The seventy-one tunnels symbolize the limit of defensive, individual measures — encryption, "opt-out," "data ownership," the "art and science of hiding." They are "testament to the necessity of counter-declarations, but they did not bring down the wall or the power that sustained it." The symbol therefore redirects attention from evasion to the structure: as Zuboff puts it in Chapter 17, "It's the wall, not the tunnels, that requires our attention." (`Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis`)
- **Collective certainty against the inevitable.** The exchange "We will" dramatizes game-changing collective agency — the dissolution of an order that had presented itself as permanent. It is the antithesis of [[Inevitabilism]] (the "full-blown ideology" that "precludes choice and voluntary participation") and of [[A Utopia of Certainty]] in its instrumentarian form: here certainty belongs to the crowd, not to the apparatus, and it opens a future rather than foreclosing one. (`Strongly implied`)
- **The will to will, declared in the future tense.** "We will" is itself an assertion in the future tense — a collective promise that constructs a future against an authority built to forbid it. The symbol thus embodies [[The Right to the Future Tense]] and [[The Will to Will|the will to will]] as a *public, collective* act: not the lone subject's resolve but a crowd's shared declaration of intent. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Context

The opened wall appears at the close of Chapter 11 ("The Right to the Future Tense"), as Zuboff turns from diagnosis to resistance and previews "Part III." Having argued that surveillance capitalism's facts were instituted by self-asserted declarations — [[Conquest by Declaration]] — she frames resistance as itself a matter of declarations and distinguishes its two forms. The Berlin Wall supplies her worked example: the tunnels for the counter-declaration, the opened gate for the synthetic one. The image then carries forward into Chapter 17 ("The Right to Sanctuary"), where it returns as an explicit injunction — "Tunnels under this wall are not enough. This wall must come down" — and grounds her call for "synthetic declarations that are institutionalized in new centers of democratic power."

As a curatorial figure the symbol pairs sharply with [[The Requirimiento]], Zuboff's emblem of conquest-by-declaration: the Requirimiento is the founding declaration imposed from above; the opened wall is the counter-vailing declaration that unmakes such an order from below. Together they bracket the book's strategic arc — declarations made surveillance capitalism's world, and only declarations of equal scope can unmake it. The figure resonates with the vault's surveillance and power cluster — the asymmetries of the [[Digital Panopticon]], the broader [[Surveillance]] and [[Big Data]] notes — by naming the conditions under which an apparently total apparatus can nonetheless be dissolved by collective will. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
