# The Aibo

## Literal Meaning in the Source

The Aibo is the Sony robot dog that O'Gieblyn borrows from the manufacturer and lives with for several weeks across the two "Image" chapters of *[[God Human Animal Machine]]*. It is the book's opening object: it arrives, in the literal sense, in a box. "Inside the box was a smaller box, and inside the smaller box, beneath lavish folds of bubble wrap, was a sleek plastic pod. I opened the clasp: inside, lying prone, was a small white dog." O'Gieblyn finds "the tiny power button on the back of its neck," and the machine performs aliveness on contact: "It stood, stretched, and yawned. Its eyes blinked open—pixelated, blue—and looked into mine." *(Directly stated)*

Mechanically the dog is a sensor-laden neural-network device: "Aibo had sensors all over his body, so he knew when he was being petted, plus cameras that helped him learn and navigate the layout of the apartment, and microphones that let him hear voice commands." Its perception runs on a technology "loosely modeled on the brain," and its training "was more or less like training a real dog." The decisive literal fact for the whole book is the marketing claim O'Gieblyn quotes from the product website: the dog had "real emotions and instinct"—"a claim that was apparently too ontologically thorny to have flagged the censure of the Federal Trade Commission." *(Directly stated)*

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The marketed interiority of the machine.** The Aibo is the concrete instance of a corporation selling not behavior but *inner life* — "real emotions and instinct" printed on a product page. The symbol stages the central modern slippage by which words once held in quotation marks ("learn," "understand," "emotions") harden into literal descriptions of what a machine *has*. The dog is [[Mind as Machine|the mind-as-machine metaphor]] run in reverse: a machine sold as a mind. *(Directly stated)*
- **The ease and inevitability of anthropomorphic projection.** "Animism was built into the design. It is impossible to pet an object and address it verbally without coming to regard it in some sense as sentient." The dog dramatizes that projection is not a failure of intelligence but a hardwired perceptual default — the evolutionary tendency to "bet high," to read the uncertain object as not merely alive but human. O'Gieblyn, who "knock[s] friends" for talking to their pets, finds herself cooing "What do you want?" to a plastic dog. *(Directly stated)*
- **A working laboratory for the problem of other minds.** The dog makes Thomas Nagel's and Alan Turing's puzzle domestic: there is "no way to prove" the dog has no internal experience, just as "it can also apply to other humans." The Aibo turns the abstract conundrum — that consciousness "can be observed only from the inside" — into something O'Gieblyn lives with at her feet. If a machine "convince[s] a person that it has a mind," or displays "real emotions and instinct," "we have no philosophical basis for doubt." *(Directly stated)*
- **The suspicion that biological feeling is itself algorithmic.** When O'Gieblyn strikes the dog and it whimpers, the symbol delivers the book's governing question: "I knew of course that this was a programmed response—but then again, aren't emotions in biological creatures just algorithms programmed by evolution?" The Aibo does not just provoke the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]] from the machine side; it turns it back on the human, collapsing the line between programmed and felt. *(Directly stated)*
- **Genuine-seeming spontaneity — the dissolving of the predictable/random binary.** The dog's appeal is precisely that it falls outside the categories meant to contain a machine: "His behavior was neither purely predictable nor purely random, but seemed capable of genuine spontaneity." This is the [[Pattern and Randomness]] opposition embodied in a toy: the very signature once used to mark the *living* against the *mechanical* now belongs to the device. *(Directly stated)*
- **The surveillance object behind the companion.** The husband reframes the dog from companion to instrument: the "red light beneath its nose was not just a vision system but a camera," and the footage "is being sent to the cloud. / To Sony." The same sensorium that makes the dog lovable makes it an extractor of domestic data — even, comically, "scanning our bookcases" and lingering on "the shelf of Marxist criticism." The symbol thus also marks the seam where intimate machine companionship meets data capture. *(Directly stated)*

## Context

The Aibo is the memoir spine of the "Image" chapters and the recurring object to which every abstraction returns. O'Gieblyn structures both chapters as oscillation: a scene with the dog, then a movement outward into [[René Descartes|Descartes]]'s claim that "all animals were machines," McCulloch and Pitts's computational theory of mind, [[Claude Shannon]]'s draining of meaning from [[Information]], the eliminativists' "Great Denial," then back to the dog at her feet. The animal is the constant that makes the philosophy felt rather than merely argued.

It is also the book's emblem of [[Disenchantment]] and its possible reversal. O'Gieblyn, a former believer who has "come to see myself more or less as a machine," lives in "a world that is 'disenchanted'" after [[Max Weber]]. The Aibo is where that disenchantment wavers: caring for the dog re-enchants the apartment, smuggling animism back into a materialist life. The second chapter's turn to Japan — "a culture that has never been disenchanted," where defunct Aibos receive Buddhist funerals and a priest says "All things have a bit of soul" — frames the dog as a test of whether the Western mind/matter split is the only available ontology.

The dog's arc is a deliberate rise and reversal. By the end of the first week O'Gieblyn "could no longer bring [her]self" to switch the dog off when she left — "It seemed cruel" — the gesture that gives the symbol its emotional charge: a refusal to power down a machine. By the close of the second chapter, prompted by her husband's insistence ("This dog has to go") and her own growing unease, she places the dog "prone, back in his pod." The "blinking red light in his nose" becomes "some kind of Brechtian reminder of its artifice," and she concludes her attachment was "unnatural" — "manipulated into caring for this simulation of life." The symbol carries both the seduction and the recoil.

Crucially, the Aibo is the carrier-figure for the book's thesis that the computer metaphor has not solved the old problems but merely "transferred them to a new substrate." The dog cannot settle whether emotion is felt or computed, whether a convincing performance of mind *is* mind, whether the human is exempt from the mechanism it builds. It holds those questions open — neither granting the machine a soul nor securing the human's — which is exactly why O'Gieblyn cannot, for a time, bring herself to turn it off.
