# Today's Selfie Is Tomorrow's Biometric Profile

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Chapter 17 of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, [[Shoshana Zuboff]] describes a bestselling object on display in the shop of the New Museum for Contemporary Art in Manhattan: a table-top mirror whose reflecting surface is covered with the bright-orange message "Today's Selfie Is Tomorrow's Biometric Profile." The object is the Berlin-based artist Adam Harvey's "Think Privacy Selfie Mirror," and it is the emblematic artifact of what Zuboff names [[The Art and Science of Hiding|the art and science of hiding]]. (`Directly stated`)

The mirror works by reflection in two senses at once. Literally it returns the viewer's image — the vanity surface in which one composes a selfie. Inscribed across that surface, the slogan converts the act of looking into a warning: the self-portrait one takes for pleasure and self-display today is, tomorrow, the raw material from which a [[Quantified Self|biometric profile]] is rendered. The mirror thus catches the user in the very gesture the message indicts, turning a private moment of self-regard into an indictment of the apparatus that harvests it. (`Strongly implied`)

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The face as raw material.** The mirror dramatizes the rendition of the human face — the most intimate index of identity — into [[The Body as Data|extractable data]]. The selfie, the freely given self-image, is reframed as the first link in a supply chain that ends in a permanent biometric record. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **Vanity turned against itself.** The traditional mirror is an instrument of self-display and self-knowledge; Harvey weaponizes it as an instrument of self-warning. The object enacts the reversal at the heart of glass life — that the means of expression have become the means of capture. (`Strongly implied`)
- **The temporal trap of surveillance.** "Today… Tomorrow" names the latency of extraction: the present innocent act and its future, irreversible consequence. The harm is not at the moment of capture but in the data's afterlife, when the face becomes a profile that outlives and exceeds the person's consent. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **Art as defensive feedback device.** As the bestseller of Harvey's anti-surveillance practice — which "begins with 'reverse engineering… computer vision algorithms'" — the mirror is [[Art as Experiential Technology]] turned defensive: a participatory artifact whose medium is the surveilling apparatus itself. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Context

The mirror appears as the entry point of Zuboff's catalogue of the anti-surveillance vanguard — Harvey, Leo Selvaggio, Benjamin Grosser, Trevor Paglen, Ai Weiwei — who, under "the intolerable conditions of glass life," "feel themselves called to create the art and science of hiding." Harvey is the exemplary figure: beyond the selfie mirror, his "Stealth Wear" uses "silver-plated fabrics" that "reflect thermal radiation" and is inspired by traditional Islamic dress, whose idea that "garments can provide a separation between man and God" Harvey "redirects… to create garments that separate human experience from the powers that surveil." (`Directly stated`)

The slogan recurs in the vault as the most quotable distillation of the rendition of the face. Where the Stealth Wear hides the body from thermal and computer vision, the mirror does the opposite work — it makes visible the invisible extraction, holding it up to the user's own reflection. It is, in Zuboff's frame, simultaneously a "brilliant" provocation and a counter-declaration whose limit is adaptation: a message in a bottle "cast from the front lines of mourning and revulsion" rather than the synthetic declaration that would bring the wall down. (`Strongly implied`)
