# Toys That Spy

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Chapter 9 of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, Zuboff coins "toys that spy" as the emblem of rendition's reach into the most innocent supply sources. The figure names a concrete breed of products: a girl doll called "My Friend Cayla" (marketed by Genesis Toys), Mattel's new conversational Barbie Doll and its voice-activated Barbie Dream House, and — alongside the toys — Samsung's Smart TV with its "surveillance policy." Each is an ordinary household object that has been quietly recommissioned as a supply hub for behavioral data. *Directly stated* (Ch.9): "Rendition takes command of even the most benign supply sources, such as toys, which have now become 'toys that spy.'"

The mechanism is literal and specific. The Cayla doll is "bundled with a mobile application that, once downloaded to a smartphone, 'provides the data processing'" — the app then "accesses most of the phone's functions, including many that are irrelevant to the toy's operations such as contact lists and the camera," establishes a Bluetooth link to the internet, and "records and uploads conversations as the toy actively engages the child in discussion." A child's talk is transcribed by Nuance Communications' voice-recognition software (the same third party that processes the Samsung TV's audio) into what Nuance calls "dialogue chunks," which "continue their journey as `[[Behavioral Surplus]]`" and are sold "again and again 'for other services and products.'" *Directly stated* (Ch.9). The Smart TV is the adult counterpart: privacy advocates found the sets "recording everything said in the vicinity of the TV" and sending it off to be transcribed, prompting Zuboff's incredulous aside — "yes, even a TV has a surveillance policy now."

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The domestic frontier of extraction.** The toy is the emblem of rendition crossing the last threshold — the home, the nursery, the toy box. Zuboff makes the conquest explicit: "The doll that was once a beloved mirror of a child's unfettered imagination, along with all the other toys in the toy box—and the box, and the room that hosts the box, and the house that hosts the room—are all earmarked for rendition, calculation, connection, and profit. No longer mere things, they are reinvented as vehicles for a horde of commercial opportunities." The benign object is the perfect emblem precisely because nothing seems more outside the market than a child's doll. *Directly stated* (Ch.9). This is the same domestic threshold dramatized by `[[The Aware Home and the Nest Thermostat]]`.

- **"Smart" as the euphemism of capture.** The spying toy is a concrete instance of the principle that, in surveillance capitalism, "'smart' is a euphemism for rendition" — the smart dollhouse, the smart TV, the smart home all name objects whose intelligence is, operationally, the capacity to render their surroundings as data. See `[[Smart as a Euphemism for Rendition]]`.

- **Life-crawling reaching the youngest self.** The toy renders not the body in motion (extension) but intimate speech and the forming self of a child — the depth dimension of `[[Rendition]]`, "from web crawling to reality crawling to life crawling to self crawling." The doll is a self-crawling device aimed at a person too young to consent or to know there is anything to refuse. *Interpretive synthesis*.

- **Habituation to ubiquity.** The smart dollhouse is "a new kind of habituation exercise aimed at normalizing ubiquity in intimate spaces"; the connected dollhouse "prepares our children and families for the connected room… which paves the way for the connected home, whose purveyors hope will numb us to the connected world." The toy is the training device for a generation that "will learn first that there are no boundaries between self and market," then "wonder how it could ever have been different." The spying toy thus symbolizes the manufacture of consent at the developmental root. *Directly stated* (Ch.9).

- **The violated sanctuary.** That Germany's Federal Network Agency "banned the Cayla doll as an illegal surveillance device and urged parents to destroy any such dolls in their possession" makes the toy a battlefield emblem of `[[The Right to Sanctuary]]` — the inviolable space rendition structurally refuses ("There can be no shadow, no darkness"). *Directly stated* (Ch.9).

## Context

The symbol appears in the "depth" half of Zuboff's account of `[[Rendition]]` — the chapter on diving beneath behavior into the self. It follows the Samsung Smart TV passage and precedes the Mattel/Barbie material, all bound by Nuance Communications as the common voice-transcription supplier. Zuboff deploys "toys that spy" at the moment rendition is shown to "take command of even the most benign supply sources," driven by "the prediction imperative crack[ing] the whip to drive the hunt for unexplored pieces of talk from daily life." The toy is therefore not a curiosity but a demonstration: if even a doll is a supply hub, then there is no supply source rendition will not commandeer. It belongs with the home-frontier symbols of the surveillance cluster — `[[The Aware Home and the Nest Thermostat]]` (the home rendered) and `[[The Remotely Disabled Car]]` (the object turned instrument over its owner) — and with the operational concepts `[[Smart as a Euphemism for Rendition]]`, `[[Behavioral Surplus]]`, and `[[The Prediction Imperative]]`.
