# The Scientific Model as Icon

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In the Introduction to *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Jack Burnham names a single figure for the displacement of the sacred, totemic object by science: where metaphysics and the accepted notion of reality fuse into one operational worldview "as psychologically and intellectually pervasive as any monolithic religion," then "Hence the contemporary icon par excellence becomes the scientific model which interprets principles believed to be operative in the real world." The scientific model — a delicate "web of symbols," offered provisionally — is treated by the culture (and by the sculptor) as a truth-image: the thing one orients belief and form toward, the way an earlier age oriented belief toward a graven or totemic object (`Directly stated`).

Burnham reads modern sculpture itself as caught between these two icons. It "remains a class of visible objects mirroring the process of psychic disbelief in its own being," and "With unmatched pathos it suggests still visible roots in the magic totemic objects of earlier ages while seeking sustenance in the austere soil of the scientific model and technical invention." The old icon (the magic totem, the free-standing human form) is still faintly visible in sculpture's roots; the new icon (the scientific model) is the soil it now feeds on (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The totem replaced by the diagram.** The scientific model occupies the slot the religious or magic object once held — the central image through which a culture interprets "principles believed to be operative in the real world." Belief migrates from the sacred carved object to the symbolic construct of science (`Strongly implied`).
- **Science as a monolithic religion.** Burnham's icon is explicitly framed as the cult-image of an "operational system" that is "as psychologically and intellectually pervasive as any monolithic religion." The figure marks not science's neutrality but its function as a faith — supplying the reality-picture the way a church once did (`Directly stated`).
- **The provisional taken as the eternal.** What is made iconic is a *model*: a construct "put forth provisionally by scientists," subject to revision and eventual discrediting. The icon hardens a tentative web of symbols into a fixed truth, the same slippage diagnosed in [[Scientific Idealism]] and in [[Technology as Metaphor]] (`Strongly implied`).
- **The drive that pulls sculpture off its anthropomorphic course.** For Burnham the rise of this icon "explains why 'thingification' has redirected sculpture away from its traditional anthropomorphic course" — the figure is causal, not decorative (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The icon appears in the Introduction's argument about [[Reification]] ("thingification," *Verdinglichung*), where "reification is the means by which the world is made anew, through sculpture or other artifacts, in a scientific culture." As "the primacy of the scientific world picture has become almost undisputed," sculpture is caught "in a trade-off between its strivings toward science-oriented 'objective' reality and the necessity for retaining some vestiges of idealism for survival's sake." The scientific-model-as-icon is the destination of that drift: one of the detectable trends is "the evolution of sculpture from a psychically-impregnated totemic object toward a more literal adaptation of scientific reality via the model or technologically inspired artifact" (`Directly stated`).

The figure is the symbolic crux of Burnham's larger thesis — that [[Technics]] is "the metaphysics of our century" — applied specifically to belief and the image: when scientific reality becomes "the measure of all reality," the model is what one worships. The recurrence is structural: it sits beside [[Scientific Idealism]] (the sculptural impulse to embody essential reality in scientific prototypes) as the cultural-religious frame within which that impulse operates.

## Related Concepts, Metaphors, and Tensions

- [[Scientific Idealism]] — the sister diagnosis: sculpture seeks "embodiments of the essence of reality in scientific prototypes," and "their use as sculpture or icon transforms them into ideational verities." The scientific model as icon is the broader cultural condition; scientific idealism is its specifically sculptural form.
- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — the cross-author parallel (O'Gieblyn): a scientific or technological construct is mistaken for a literal truth of reality rather than a provisional model — the same idolizing slippage. `Speculative connection`
- [[Reification]] — the mechanism: "thingification" resynchronizes artistic sensibility with "a disclosed form-world of scientific theory," displacing the totemic object with the scientific model.
- [[Technics]] — the metaphysics the icon serves: "technology is the metaphysics of our century," and science "in its role as a speculative agent" makes scientific reality "the measure of all reality."
