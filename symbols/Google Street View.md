# Google Street View

The street-mapping operation that Zuboff turns into the book's sustained, chapter-length case study of surveillance capitalism in action: the "cartoonishly wrapped cars with a large 360-degree camera mount on the roof" launched in 2007, the secret Wi-Fi "payload data" capture exposed in 2010, the "rogue engineer" scapegoat, and the redirection of the once-spurned project into a real-world monitoring infrastructure. It is the book's exemplary surveillance-capitalist artifact — the figure through which the abstract logic of the [[The Dispossession Cycle]] becomes a concrete narrative.

## Literal Meaning in the Source

Literally, Street View is the camera-car fleet and the cartographic "service" it produces. Zuboff describes the apparatus exactly: cars "used cartoonishly wrapped cars with a large 360-degree camera mount on the roof to capture the imagery it sought" (`Directly stated`). Launched in 2007 "without asking permission," it "devoured and datafied the world's public spaces, streets, buildings, and homes," and was later augmented with "a wearable backpack, a three-wheeled pedicab, a snowmobile, and a trolley" to reach places the cars could not traverse.

The 2010 German revelation showed the cars were also "secretly collecting personal data from private Wi-Fi networks" — the so-called "payload data," "personal information grabbed from unencrypted Wi-Fi transmissions," including in some instances "entire emails and URLs… as well as passwords" (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The map as conquest.** Zuboff loads Street View with the cartographer's old imperial power. Quoting John B. Harley ("Maps created empire"), she distills the threat into a single sentence: "if you are not on our map, you do not exist." The camera car is thus a symbol of cartography as a "language of conquest," reducing reality "to only two conditions: the map and oblivion." (`Directly stated`)
- **The full dispossession cycle made visible.** The Street View narrative is Zuboff's chosen vehicle to dramatize all four stages — incursion (2007), habituation (the war of attrition through the 2010–2013 scandals), adaptation (blurring, fines, opt-outs, country bans), and redirection (Ground Truth, Cartographer, indoor positioning, the self-driving car). It symbolizes dispossession as a repeatable, managed political operation rather than an accident. (`Directly stated`) See [[The Dispossession Cycle]].
- **The migration from the online to the real world.** Zuboff treats Street View as the hinge by which surveillance capitalism leaves the screen: "the migration from an online data source to a real-world monitor to an advisor to an active shepherd — from knowledge to influence to control." The camera car is the symbol of [[Behavioral Surplus]] extraction escaping the browser and colonizing physical space. (`Directly stated`) See [[The Reality Business]] and [[Digital Dispossession]].
- **The innocent organism and the "rogue engineer."** The scapegoating of a single engineer — "a single infected cell excised from the flesh of an enormous but innocent organism" — symbolizes corporate misdirection: the body declaring itself "cured of its privacy kleptomania" so it can return "to the streets, born again." (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Street View is introduced as one of several "vivid examples" of the dispossession cycle (alongside Gmail, Buzz, Google+, and Google Glass), but Zuboff chooses it for "a close look at the dispossession cycle and its management challenges." Its narrative organizes the second half of Chapter 5 under explicit stage headings (Incursion, Habituation, Adaptation, Redirection).

As a curatorial figure, Street View is a useful **foil to the earlier "mapping" images in the vault**. Where [[Marshall McLuhan]]'s media are "extensions of man" and the medium reorganizes the sensorium, and where Burnham-era [[Systems Esthetics]] imagined art that maps and models systems as a route to expanded awareness, Street View is mapping inverted into capture: the world rendered, indexed, and re-classified as a private asset ("data compiled through public investments are augmented with data taken from a unilateral transfer of surplus behavior and decision rights… reclassified as private assets"). The camera car turns the modernist dream of total legibility into the infrastructure of the [[Digital Panopticon]]. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

The redirection of Street View feeds directly into [[The Reality Business]] (Chapter 7), the self-driving car, and "Google City" — and, by extension, into the apparatus of [[Big Other]]. The Wi-Fi payload episode and the surveillance ambitions it reveals also resonate with the vault's existing surveillance and data cluster: [[Big Data]], [[Dataism]], and the forward-committed notes [[Surveillance]] and [[Quantified Self]]. (`Strongly implied`)
