# The Forest of Arden

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Chapter 6 ("Media as Translators"), McLuhan reads Shakespeare's pastoral comedy *As You Like It* as a parable of automation. The Forest of Arden — the woodland into which the play's exiles withdraw from court — is for McLuhan "just such a golden world of translated benefits and joblessness as we are now entering via the gate of electric automation." He treats Shakespeare as having "understood the Forest of Arden as an advance model of the age of automation when all things are translatable into anything else that is desired," and quotes Duke Senior's speech in which the exiled life "exempt from public haunt" finds "tongues in trees, books in the running brooks, / Sermons in stones, and good in every thing" (`Directly stated`).

The symbol is grounded in the chapter's controlling thesis that "all media are active metaphors in their power to translate experience into new forms" (see [[Media as Translators]]). The Forest is the literary emblem of a world in which translation has become total: nature played back "in a variety of levels and intensities of style," "complete metamorphoses or translations of nature into human art" (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The automation age as a golden world.** Arden is McLuhan's "advance model of the age of automation" — a pastoral utopia where labour falls away because "all things are translatable into anything else that is desired." The forest stands in for electric automation's promise: abundance produced by the movement of information rather than by toil (`Directly stated`).
- **Joblessness as the shadow of translated benefits.** The same image carries the negative pole. McLuhan pairs "translated benefits" with "joblessness," noting that under electric technology "all forms of employment become 'paid learning,'" and "the problem of discovering occupations or employment may prove as difficult as wealth is easy." The golden world is also a world that has dissolved work into knowing — the precondition of [[Immaterial Labour]] (`Strongly implied`).
- **Programmability of the natural world.** Arden figures a world that can be "play[ed] back" by "programming, as it were." McLuhan reads the pastoral as anticipating the computer: "We are now in a position to go beyond that and to transfer the entire show to the memory of a computer." The forest is a stage that can be re-scored at will — the literary precursor of full simulation (`Strongly implied`).
- **The gate of electric automation.** The phrase "via the gate of electric automation" makes Arden a threshold image: the boundary the electric age is crossing as it converts "all solid goods" into "information circuits set up in the organic patterns that we call 'automation.'" Arden marks the entrance to a translated world (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The Forest of Arden appears at the climax of Chapter 6's argument that media are translators — that "what we call 'mechanization' is a translation of nature, and of our own natures, into amplified and specialized forms," and that "applied knowledge" has always meant carrying experience "across from one kind of material form into another." Having argued that electric media translate the body and nervous system into "information systems," McLuhan reaches for *As You Like It* as the inherited image of what such total translation looks like when "all things are translatable into anything else." The symbol therefore sits at the hinge between the chapter's theory of media-as-metaphor ([[Media as Translators]]) and its economic forecast — wealth detached from work, employment become "paid learning" — which the vault tracks under [[Immaterial Labour]].
