# Pyramids and Skyscrapers

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In the second-to-highest of her twelve [[Leverage Points]] — "The mindset or paradigm out of which the system... arises" — Meadows reaches for a paired architectural image to make the abstract claim concrete. Having just written that "Paradigms are the sources of systems," she names two civilizations by their signature buildings:

> The ancient Egyptians built pyramids because they believed in an afterlife. We build skyscrapers because we believe that space in downtown cities is enormously valuable.

The pyramid and the skyscraper are set side by side as the two visible things that two different [[Paradigms]] extrude. Each is a mass of stone or steel that would be inexplicable without knowing the "great big unstated assumptions" of the culture that raised it — the Egyptian belief in the afterlife, the modern belief in the enormous monetary value of downtown ground.

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The building as the legible surface of a belief.** The pyramid and the skyscraper are readable as sentences: from the material object you can infer the paradigm, because the material apparatus is what a state of thought "clothes itself with." This is the symbol's core work — it lets an immaterial thing (a shared belief) be pointed to through a material thing (a monument).
- **The paradigm as prior to, and generative of, the physical system.** The figure dramatizes Meadows's thesis that beliefs come first and stones follow: shared social agreements about the nature of reality produce "system goals and information flows, feedbacks, stocks, flows, and everything else." The buildings are downstream; the paradigm is the leverage point.
- **The interchangeability and contingency of paradigms.** Two eras, two utterly different sets of "deepest set of beliefs," two utterly different skylines. Placing pyramid and skyscraper in the same breath quietly relativizes both — our skyscrapers are as paradigm-bound as their pyramids, a point that sets up her final leverage point, [[Transcending Paradigms]] ("no paradigm is 'true'").
- **The immaterial made material.** The pair belongs to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis: it is a compact statement that the most material things a society makes — its heaviest, most permanent structures — are the visible "clothing" of its most immaterial contents, its beliefs.

## Context

The image appears at the summit of the leverage-points ladder, where Meadows moves from mechanical parameters (numbers, buffers, stocks) up through feedbacks and rules to the two highest and least tangible points: the paradigm, and the ability to transcend paradigms. Just before the pyramid/skyscraper line she quotes Ralph Waldo Emerson at length — the passage that supplies the symbol's governing metaphor of clothing:

> Every nation and every man instantly surround themselves with a material apparatus which exactly corresponds to... their state of thought.

The pyramid and the skyscraper are Meadows's own gloss on Emerson: two concrete, historically specific instances of a "material apparatus" that "exactly corresponds to" a "state of thought." Emerson supplies the general law ("timber, brick, lime, and stone have flown into convenient shape, obedient to the master idea reigning in the minds of many persons"); Meadows supplies the two examples that make it unforgettable. The figure recurs implicitly whenever she argues that the highest-leverage intervention is not to move stones but to change the mind that arranges them — "there's nothing physical or expensive or even slow in the process of paradigm change."
