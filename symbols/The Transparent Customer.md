# The Transparent Customer

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In the closing movement of "The Society of Control," [[Byung-Chul Han]] names a figure: **the transparent customer**. The figure is identified with two earlier figures Han borrows and re-routes — Bentham's *prisoner* and Agamben's *[[Homo Sacer]]*. Han writes, in the chapter's pivot from moral to economic logic:

> The transparent customer is the new prisoner—indeed, the homo sacer—of the digital panopticon.

The transparent customer is therefore not merely a consumer who happens to be surveilled; he or she is the *inhabitant* of the [[Digital Panopticon]], the one whose self-exposure constitutes the panopticon's working substrate. Crucially, Han names this figure precisely at the point where he distinguishes the digital panopticon from Bentham's: Bentham's panopticon aimed at "morals reformed"; Han's transparent customer is illuminated for an economic, not moral, purpose (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The consumer as inmate.** The transparent customer collapses the older distinction between the disciplined subject (prisoner, patient, schoolchild) and the free consumer. To buy, browse, click, and post is already to be incarcerated in an aperspectival cell whose walls are made of data. The symbol stages consumption itself as a carceral condition.
- **Homo sacer of the digital order.** By invoking [[Homo Sacer]], Han marks the transparent customer as a life that can be exploited without sacrifice — captured, illuminated, monetized — yet stripped of the political standing that would allow protest. The figure is bare life in informational form.
- **The willing exhibit.** Unlike Bentham's inmate, who knows the tower watches, the transparent customer "actively collaborate[s] in [the panopticon's] construction and maintenance by putting [himself] on display and baring [himself]." The symbol thus condenses the dialectic Han pursues throughout the book: freedom *as* control, perpetrator *as* victim (`Directly stated`).
- **Shift of telos: from moral reform to economic exposure.** The transparent customer signals that the panoptic *purpose* has migrated. Where Bentham wanted morals reformed, health preserved, and the Gordian knot of the Poor-Laws untied, the digital panopticon wants maximal attention and maximal profit. The customer is the figure in whom this teleological shift becomes flesh (see the Unveiling passage below).
- **End of the political "we."** As a symbol the transparent customer also marks the impossibility, under transparency, of forming a community in the strong sense. Customers cluster in "brand communities" — additive formations without inner density — rather than assemblies capable of mutual political action.

## Context

The figure appears in Chapter 9, "The Society of Control," at the hinge between Han's diagnosis of the [[Digital Panopticon]] as *aperspectival* — illuminating everyone from everywhere — and his closing claim that "the entire globe is developing into a panopticon" with "no outside space." The transparent customer is named in the paragraph that explicitly contrasts Bentham's moral-biopolitical project with the current economic imperative:

> Above all, the project of the panopticon had a moral—or biopolitical—motivation. According to Bentham, the first result to be expected of panoptic control is seeing "morals reformed." [...] But today's compulsive transparency no longer has an explicitly moral or biopolitical imperative; above all, it follows an economic imperative. People who illuminate themselves entirely surrender to exploitation. Illumination is exploitation. Overexposing individual subjects maximizes economic efficiency. The transparent customer is the new prisoner—indeed, the homo sacer—of the digital panopticon.

The figure is prepared earlier in Chapter 8, "Unveiling," where Han argues that the digital net "lacks a heart" and so cannot host the older moral imperative of transparent conscience; what it hosts instead is an *economic* panopticon — and so the customer, not the penitent, is its proper subject.

Symbolically, the transparent customer also recurs (unnamed but operative) wherever Han speaks of exhibitionism, pornographic self-display, voluntary surrender to the panoptic gaze, and the [[Achievement Society|Achievement-Subject]] who is "the master and entrepreneur of oneself." The customer-prisoner is the achievement-subject seen from the marketing side of the same coin (`Strongly implied`).
