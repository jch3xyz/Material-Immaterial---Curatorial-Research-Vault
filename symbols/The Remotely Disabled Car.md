# The Remotely Disabled Car

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Chapter 7, "The Reality Business," Zuboff develops Google chief economist Hal Varian's own favored illustration of computer-mediated transactions: the "vehicular monitoring system" that, when a driver stops making monthly car payments, can be instructed "not to allow the car to be started" and made "to signal the location where it can be picked up." The image is concrete and material — an internet-connected device planted inside the automobile that both senses the state of the loan and acts on it, shutting off the engine so that "the repo man" can locate "the disabled automobile and its vanquished driver." Zuboff documents that this is not a thought experiment: the telematics firm Spireon's "LoanPlus Collateral Management System" already "pushes alerts to drivers when they have fallen behind in their payments, remotely disables the vehicle when delinquency exceeds a predetermined period, and locates the vehicle for the repo man to recover." `Directly stated`

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The point where data becomes muscle.** The disabled car is Zuboff's emblem of [[Economies of Action]] — the moment when surveillance turns from knowing to doing. As she puts it, "Telematics are not intended merely to know but also to do.... They are hammers; they are muscular; they enforce." The car stands for the whole class of instruments that no longer merely observe behavior but reach into the world to actuate it. `Directly stated`
- **The death of the contract, the birth of the [[The Uncontract|uncontract]].** Where the social contract once depended on promise, trust, and the always-open possibility of breach, the engine lockdown forecloses non-compliance in advance. The car cannot fail to pay because it can no longer be driven; obligation is executed by machine rather than honored by a person. The symbol condenses the substitution of guaranteed outcomes for human dialogue. `Strongly implied`
- **Automated power as "amoral" rather than brutal.** Zuboff frames the device through Herman Kahn's 1967 forecast of a "twenty-first century nightmare," where whoever wields such knowledge is "less immoral than amoral... indifferent to the fate of those who stand in his way." The disabled car is power that does not hate its target; it simply executes. The image's force comes from the questions it raises about the stranded driver — "What if there is a child in the car? Or a blizzard?... A mother on life support in the hospital still miles away?" `Directly stated`
- **The banality of the "lullaby."** Varian's "laid-back, simple prose is a kind a lullaby that makes his observations seem banal." The car symbolizes how the most coercive capabilities are normalized through prosaic, "it's a lot easier" language that asks "a lot easier for whom?" `Directly stated`

## Context

The remotely disabled car recurs at four points across Chapter 7 — as Varian's chosen example, as Zuboff's analytic exhibit of know-and-do architectures, as the real-world Spireon product, and finally as the rhetorical fulcrum of her "lullaby" critique. It is the chapter's keystone concrete image: small, familiar, and material enough to make the abstract claim of [[Economies of Action|economies of action]] palpable. Zuboff explicitly links it to Sidewalk Labs' Dan Doctoroff's "performance-based zoning," noting that those urban processes, "like Varian's vehicular monitoring systems, are indifferent to why you behave as long as they can monitor and control the behavior you produce" — extending the car's logic from the individual to the city. The image therefore operates as the entry point to the chapter's larger argument about the migration of surveillance capitalism from prediction into intervention via [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|the means of behavioral modification]].
