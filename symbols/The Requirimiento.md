# The Requirimiento

The Requirimiento — the *Monarchical Edict of 1513* that conquistadors were tasked with reading aloud, often in Latin, to indigenous villagers before attacking them — is Zuboff's recurring symbol of **conquest-by-declaration and coercive faux-legitimation**. It first anchors Chapter 6 as the founding template for how surveillance capitalism asserts its facts "pulled from thin air," and returns in Chapter 8 to name the structure of the modern abusive contract: "Requirimiento-style relationships" in which the consumer is offered surrender dressed as choice. The figure binds together the book's history of dispossession (the *adelantados* on the beach) with its present (the terms-of-service agreement on the screen).

## Literal Meaning in the Source

Literally, the Requirimiento is a sixteenth-century Spanish legal instrument. Zuboff reports that "the soldiers were tasked with reading the Monarchical Edict of 1513 known as the Requirimiento to indigenous villagers before attacking them" (`Directly stated`). The edict "declared that the authority of God, the pope, and the king was embodied in the conquistadors and then declared the native peoples as vassals subordinate to that authority," summoning them to "take the oath of Allegiance to the Spanish King, as his Vassals."

Crucially, the edict was a piece of legal theater, designed for the Europeans' own audience rather than its nominal addressees. Zuboff stresses that the native people were "summoned, advised, and forewarned in a language they could not fathom to surrender without resistance in recognition of authorities they could not conceive," and that "the approaching invaders often dispatched their obligation by mumbling the edict's long paragraphs into their beards in the dead of night as they hid among the thick vegetation waiting to pounce." Citing Bartolomé de las Casas, she notes that once the declaration was discharged, "the way was clear for pillage and enslavement," and that every act of resistance could then be reframed as "revolt," legitimizing "brutal 'retaliation.'" (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **Conquest masked as legality.** The Requirimiento is the archetype of [[Conquest by Declaration]]: the conqueror imposes a new reality by fiat and then papers it with "a legalistic veneer by citing and following approved precedents." Zuboff makes the analogy explicit — surveillance capitalism's facts "have been carried into the world on the strength of six critical declarations pulled from thin air when Google first asserted them." The edict symbolizes the **declarative word** as an instrument of invasion: words and deeds that "aim to conquer by imposing a new reality." (`Directly stated`)
- **Faux-legitimation / consent that is not consent.** The edict's cruelty lies in its form: a demand for assent issued in terms the addressee cannot understand or refuse on equal footing. It thus symbolizes the cynical ritual of consent — the modern analogue being "cynically conveyed terms-of-service agreements whose stipulations are just as obscured and incomprehensible." This is the symbolic bridge to [[Contracts of Adhesion]] and [[The Uncontract]]. (`Directly stated`)
- **The coercive ultimatum: "Bend the knee."** In Chapter 8 the symbol migrates into the architecture of the [[Rendition]] economy. Of "smart" home products whose function and security degrade if the customer refuses to share data, Zuboff writes: "In these Requirimiento-style relationships, instead of the *adelantados'* message, 'Bend the knee or we destroy you,' the message here is 'Bend the knee or we degrade your purchase.'" The Requirimiento becomes the figure for any relationship in which submission is extracted under the appearance of a voluntary transaction. (`Directly stated`)
- **The unprecedented faced through the lens of the familiar.** Zuboff likens the contemporary public to the Taíno: "like the first unsuspecting Taíno cacique," we "could not have foreseen" the dispossession, and "like them, we risk catastrophe when we assess new threats through the lens of old experience." The edict symbolizes the **structural blindness of the dispossessed** before a genuinely new form of power. (`Directly stated`)

## Context

The Requirimiento recurs at two hinge points of the argument. In **Chapter 6 ("Hijacked: The Division of Learning in Society")** it opens the account of [[Conquest by Declaration]]: Zuboff uses the 1513 edict and the *adelantados* who "appeared out of thin air waving the banner of the Spanish monarchs" to frame Google's six founding declarations as "an age of conquest" inaugurated by "aggressive declaration" and "faux-legitimation practices." In **Chapter 8 ("Rendition: From Experience to Data")** the same figure returns as "Requirimiento-style relationships" and "this same coercive Requirimiento twist," now naming the legal compacts around devices such as the Alphabet-owned Nest thermostat, whose "oppressive privacy and security consequences" are buried in dense end-user agreements. The symbol therefore stitches the book's historical conquest narrative to its analysis of the present-day coercive contract.

As a curatorial figure it resonates with the vault's existing surveillance and power cluster. The Requirimiento is the legalistic ancestor of the modern declarative regime Zuboff diagnoses; its "faux-legitimation" is the historical mirror of the asymmetries captured by the [[Digital Panopticon]] and the broader [[Surveillance]] and [[Big Data]] notes, and its "Bend the knee" ultimatum dramatizes the [[Material vs Immaterial]] migration of coercion from the soldier's sword to the immaterial clause. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
