# Searcher (Seawright)

## Literal Meaning in the Source

*Searcher* (1966) is a cybernetic sculpture by the electronics designer James Seawright — for Burnham, "the most technically sophisticated Cyborg Art produced to date." Seawright, who built equipment for the Electronic Music Center at Columbia University, designed *Searcher* around what he termed "a closed-loop feedback," in which "both environmental stimuli and the actions of the mechanism effect each other to produce complex patterns of movement and light impulses." A change of illumination external to the work precipitates new searching movements; these in turn manipulate the light sensors and/or change the pattern of the work's own light sources, which lead to still other activities, because both the receptor and effector states of the machine have been altered (`Directly stated`).

The decisive structural feature is recursion in time. Rather than reproducing the same program or settling into equilibrium, "the condition of its previous states determines the variety of its future states, just as new stimuli would" (`Directly stated`). Seawright introduces mechanical programming into *Searcher*'s activities deliberately, to alter the efficiency of its feedback loops and ensure the sculpture "does not find itself trapped in a cycle of repetitious responses to the same stimuli."

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The behaviorally unpredictable machine.** *Searcher* stands as the vault's exemplar of art that exhibits a "patterned personality" — Seawright's explicit aim was "not to 'program' them but to produce a kind of patterned personality. Just as a person you know very well can surprise you, so can these machines." The symbol names the threshold at which a made object crosses from determinate program into open-ended, history-dependent behavior (`Directly stated`).
- **Reciprocity over one-way stimulation.** Where Burnham reads almost all prior sculpture and even Kinetic Art as delivering "one-way stimulation for the human party involved," *Searcher* embodies the closed loop in which stimulus and response are mutually constitutive — a working instance of `Reciprocal Communication` (`Strongly implied`).
- **The demystified, non-vitalist artifact.** *Searcher* symbolizes a deliberately unmetaphysical cybernetic art. Seawright, "in reassuring a woman critic for the New York Times that his works are not alive," insisted: "It seems purposeful, but it isn't.... The most remarkable thing about a machine to me is that there's a man behind it." The symbol thus also marks the limit Seawright draws against attributing life or soul to the system (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Burnham treats *Searcher* in the closing exemplary stretch of "The Cybernetic Organism as an Art Form," after the survey of Grey Walter's tortoises (*Machina speculatrix*), Ashby's homeostat, and the artificial-intelligence and machine-consciousness debates. *Searcher* recurs as the case that most fully realizes Burnham's category of `Cyborg Art`: a construction whose "complete behavior patterns" are "not clearly predictable, but neither are they pseudo-random events." It is the technical fulfillment of the chapter's repeated demand — voiced through Norbert Wiener and the New Tendency aesthetics — that art move beyond one-way message toward "a sustained two-way experience" with the observer.

Burnham reads Seawright as singular in his discipline about the medium: "Perhaps more fully than any other artist working today in this direction, Seawright understands the need for a behavioral sculpture which is not falsified by unnecessary mechanisms and fake covers." This positions *Searcher* as a precursor instance within the lineage the vault tracks as `Art as Experiential Technology` — art whose content is its real-time interactive behavior rather than a fixed object (`Interpretive synthesis`).
