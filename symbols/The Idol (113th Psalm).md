# The Idol (113th Psalm)

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Chapter 4 of *Understanding Media*, McLuhan reaches for a Hebrew counterpart to the Greek Narcissus to make the same point about technology and perception. As an extension of the sense life affects "the entire field of the senses," he quotes the Psalmist (`Directly stated`):

> As an extension and expediter of the sense life, any medium at once affects the entire field of the senses, as the Psalmist explained long ago in the 113th Psalm: Their idols are silver and gold, The work of men's hands. They have mouths, but they speak not; Eyes they have, but they see not; They have ears, but they hear not;

The catalogue of dead organs continues — noses that do not smell, hands that do not handle, feet that do not walk — and closes on the verse that carries McLuhan's argument:

> They that make them shall be like unto them; Yea, every one that trusteth in them.

The idol is a man-made artifact, an extension of the maker fashioned in "silver and gold, The work of men's hands," that possesses every sense organ in image yet exercises none. Literally, it is the graven image of pagan worship; for McLuhan it is the figure of any technology a culture beholds, makes, and trusts (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The Hebrew mirror of Narcissus.** McLuhan pairs the idol with the Greek mythic image directly: "The concept of 'idol' for the Hebrew Psalmist is much like that of Narcissus for the Greek mythmaker." Both name the human encounter with an extension of the self in a material other than the self (`Directly stated`).
- **A law of conformation.** Where the Narcissus myth turns on numbness, the Psalm states a moral-perceptual law: the beholder is remade in the likeness of what he beholds and trusts. McLuhan reads "They that make them shall be like unto them" as the claim "that the beholding of idols, or the use of technology, conforms men to them" (`Directly stated`).
- **A fact of sense "closure."** The conformation is not metaphor but physiology for McLuhan: "This is a simple fact of sense 'closure.'" The idol symbolizes the way an extended, isolated sense displaces the others and remakes the whole sensorium — the idolater becomes mouthless, eyeless, earless because his perceptual ratios have closed around the image he serves (`Directly stated`; see [[Sense Ratios]]).

## Context

The idol appears at the hinge of Chapter 4, after McLuhan has built the physiological account of [[Narcosis]] and [[Autoamputation]] from the Narcissus myth and the medical theory of Selye and Jonas. He invokes the 113th Psalm to show that the Hebrew tradition reached the same insight as the Greek by a different route, then immediately escalates it through Blake's *Jerusalem* ("If Perceptive Organs vary, Objects of Perception seem to vary") toward the chapter's governing claim that to behold or use any extension of ourselves "is necessarily to embrace it" and become its [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions|servomechanism]]. The idol is thus the Scriptural authority McLuhan enlists for [[To perceive an extension of ourselves is necessarily to embrace it]] and for [[Every extension of man is an autoamputation that numbs perception]] — the recurring motif that the maker is made over by the made thing (`Interpretive synthesis`).
