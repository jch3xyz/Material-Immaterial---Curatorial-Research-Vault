# The Scanning Tunnelling Microscope

The scanning tunnelling microscope (STM) is Ascott's proof-object: the single instrument in which the prosthesis of vision both *images* and *constructs*, where seeing matter at the atomic scale and building matter atom-by-atom become the same operation. For Ascott it is not a piece of laboratory apparatus so much as a demonstration that, under [[Cyberception]], "the immaterial and material lose their categorical distinction." It is the hardware emblem of his whole technoetic claim that perception and creation have merged.

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In chapter 30 ("Weaving the Shamantic Web") the STM appears as a worked example of [[Cyberception]] — Ascott's term for the technologically extended faculty of perceiving. He introduces it under "our cyberception of matter at the atomic level": the STM "enables us not only to view matter at this level, but also to image individual, single atoms." The decisive move is that imaging does not exhaust the instrument: "Not only can we select and focus on individual atoms, but we can, at the same time, manipulate them, one by one, atom by atom, to construct atomic structures of our own choosing from the bottom up." `Directly stated`

In chapter 32 ("Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions") the STM is given its own dictionary entry, "STMs," which compresses the same point into a slogan: "In scanning tunnelling microscopes, imaging and manipulation of molecular construction combine, thus making the STM the new tool for picosculpture and interface design." Here the instrument is explicitly enrolled into art practice — it is named a sculpting tool ("picosculpture") at the picometre scale. `Directly stated`

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The fusion of imaging and construction.** The STM symbolises the collapse of the gap between seeing and making. To look through it is already to be able to build; perception is no longer passive reception but, in Ascott's phrase, "as much active and constructive as it is receptive and reflective." `Directly stated`
- **The prosthesis of vision as a tool of realisation.** Ascott reads the instrument as proof that "the prosthesis of vision can be at one and the same time instrumental in constructing what is envisioned. To see in the mind's eye is to realize in the material world." The STM is the symbol of the eye that builds — the literalisation of the artist's "mind's eye." `Directly stated`
- **The dissolution of the material/immaterial boundary.** Because envisioning and realising fuse, "the immaterial and material lose their categorical distinction." The STM is Ascott's strongest single piece of evidence for that erosion — it is where the [[Material vs Immaterial]] opposition is shown to be, at the atomic scale, no opposition at all. `Strongly implied`
- **Bottom-up making as a new artistic faculty.** "Atom by atom… from the bottom up" frames the STM as the seed of a constructive, ground-up art — the picometre answer to sculpture. It belongs to the same horizon as the nanotechnological "prospectus" Ascott invokes a few lines earlier ("both dry (pixel) and moist (molecular) artificial organisms and the whole prospectus of nanotechnology"). `Strongly implied`

## Context

The STM recurs at the hinge of Ascott's argument about [[Double Consciousness]] and [[The Double Gaze]]. Historically, he writes, "our command of the material world has been such that we have had little option but to keep the worlds of our double consciousness in separate and distinct categories" — the real, the imagined, the spiritual. The artificial-life sciences and nanotechnology, of which the STM is the concrete instance, point to "eroding the boundaries between states of mind, between conception and construction, between the internalisation and the realisation of our desires." `Directly stated`

The instrument therefore does load-bearing work: it carries the abstract claim that envisioning and constructing have become one act down to a single, citable, real-world device. It is the bridge between the visionary register of [[Cyberception]] and the laboratory, and it is what licenses Ascott's call (in the same passage) for artists to attend to "techno-qualia," "a new kind of relationship between the tools of seeing and building." In chapter 32 the same instrument is folded into the practical vocabulary of telematic art as the tool of "picosculpture and interface design," signalling that for Ascott the STM is not a metaphor borrowed from science but a working medium the artist may take up.
