# Blasphemy

## Literal Meaning in the Source

Blasphemy is the stance Haraway adopts at the opening of *A Cyborg Manifesto* to position her own writing relative to the political traditions she is faithful to. She declares the essay "an effort to build an ironic political myth faithful to feminism, socialism, and materialism. Perhaps more faithful as blasphemy is faithful, than as reverent worship and identification." Blasphemy here is not negation or abandonment of those commitments; it is a mode of fidelity that refuses reverence. As she puts it, "Blasphemy has always seemed to require taking things very seriously," and "Blasphemy protects one from the moral majority within, while still insisting on the need for community. Blasphemy is not apostasy." (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **Faithful irreverence.** Blasphemy figures a fidelity that does not bow — being "more faithful as blasphemy is faithful, than as reverent worship and identification." It marks an allegiance held without piety, sincerity without orthodoxy. (`Directly stated`)
- **Inner critique without exit.** The line "Blasphemy protects one from the moral majority within, while still insisting on the need for community" makes blasphemy a defense against internal dogmatism — the policing impulse that arises inside one's own movements — while refusing to dissolve the bond to collective struggle. It is critique that stays in the room. (`Directly stated`)
- **Not apostasy.** "Blasphemy is not apostasy" distinguishes Haraway's irreverence from desertion: to blaspheme against feminism, socialism, and materialism is not to renounce them but to take them seriously enough to risk offense. (`Directly stated`)
- **The rhetorical-political method of the essay.** Blasphemy names the manifesto's procedure, paired with irony, which Haraway calls "a rhetorical strategy and a political method." Together they license "the image of the cyborg," which sits "at the center of my ironic faith, my blasphemy." (`Strongly implied`)

## Context

Blasphemy appears in the essay's first paragraph, where Haraway sets the terms of the whole manifesto. It is bound to irony: where irony names "contradictions that do not resolve into larger wholes" and "the tension of holding incompatible things together because both or all are necessary and true," blasphemy names the corresponding posture toward inherited political faiths — serious, committed, yet unreverent. The two together prepare the cyborg, which Haraway places "at the center of my ironic faith, my blasphemy." The figure recurs implicitly across the essay's irreverence toward origin myths, salvation history, and holism: "Cyborgs are not reverent; they do not re-member the cosmos." (`Strongly implied`)
