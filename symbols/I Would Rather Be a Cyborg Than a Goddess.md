# I Would Rather Be a Cyborg Than a Goddess

## Literal Meaning in the Source

The line is the final sentence of *A Cyborg Manifesto*, closing the essay's concluding myth in chapter 06, "Cyborgs: A Myth of Political Identity":

> Though both are bound in the spiral dance, I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess.

Literally, Haraway sets two figures side by side — the **cyborg** and the **goddess** — and states a preference between them as the essay's last word. Both are "bound in the spiral dance," so neither is simply rejected; they are co-present, entangled figures, and the sentence registers a choice *within* that entanglement rather than an escape from it (`Directly stated`). The goddess is the figure Haraway associates earlier in the chapter with "ecofeminism and feminist paganism, replete with organicisms," and with feminisms that "insist on the organic, opposing it to the technological" — the line therefore reads as a refusal of organic holism in favor of the hybrid, machine-coupled cyborg (`Strongly implied`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **A choice of political figure over origin myth.** The slogan condenses the manifesto's argument into a preference between two ways of grounding feminist politics: the cyborg, which "takes irony for granted" and "is not innocent," versus the goddess, which Haraway ties to "metaphors of rebirth" and "the resources of reproductive sex." Choosing the cyborg is choosing "regeneration" over "rebirth" (`Strongly implied`).
- **Refusal of the reproductive matrix.** Haraway writes that "cyborgs have more to do with regeneration and are suspicious of the reproductive matrix and of most birthing." The goddess names the maternal, generative wholeness the manifesto declines; the cyborg is "not of Woman born" (`Directly stated`).
- **Refusal of the innocent and all-powerful Mother.** The goddess pole gathers up the figure Haraway names in her myth of writing — the longing for "a deathly oneness that Man has imagined to be the innocent and all-powerful Mother, freed at the End from another spiral of appropriation by her son." To prefer the cyborg is to refuse this fantasy of original innocence and final wholeness (`Strongly implied`).
- **Irony and the refusal of purity.** As the most-quoted line of the essay, it functions as a compressed emblem of the cyborg's "blasphemy" and "irony" — a deliberately impure, half-machine self-image chosen over the sacred, organic, holistic figure of the goddess (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Context

The line appears only once, as the closing sentence of the manifesto's final chapter (chapter 06). It comes immediately after Haraway's image of salamander regeneration and her statement that "we require regeneration, not rebirth," and after she names the "dream not of a common language, but of a powerful infidel heteroglossia." The slogan thus caps the essay's two crucial arguments — against "universal, totalizing theory" and against "an antiscience metaphysics, a demonology of technology" — by leaving the reader with a single chosen figure.

Provenance note: the verbatim sentence "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess" appears in no earlier section of the manifesto; it is exclusive to chapter 06. Any reference to the slogan as the essay's signature line must cite this chapter.

The phrase has circulated far beyond the essay as its best-known formulation — the symbolic shorthand for Haraway's whole project of preferring the hybrid, ironic, boundary-crossing figure over the organicist, holistic one (`Interpretive synthesis`).
