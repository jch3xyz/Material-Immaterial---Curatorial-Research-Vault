# Janet Freed

## Literal Meaning in the Source

Janet Freed was a real person: the "assistant to the conference program" for the [[The Three Waves of Cybernetics|Macy Conferences on Cybernetics]], responsible for converting the participants' speech into the published transcripts on which the historical record — and Hayles's own chapter — depends. [[N. Katherine Hayles]] introduces her at the close of "Contesting for the Body of Information," looking at a photograph from the 1953 conference in which an unnamed woman "sits with her back to the photographer, her arms extended, hands reaching out to a machine I can't quite see."

The symbol turns on a transcription error. The photograph's caption, written down by Stewart Brand from Bateson and Mead's dictation, misnames her:

> The caption identifies her as "Janet Freud," but I know this can't be right either. She must be Janet Freed, listed in the published transcripts as "assistant to the conference program."

Her name slips between **Freed** and **Freud** — "with a flick of a 'u' (the U-shaped table at which she sits?)" — and Hayles preserves both spellings deliberately throughout the section, refusing to correct one into the other (`Directly stated`).

As a literal figure she is the worker who "presided over the physical transformations of signifiers as they went from taperecording to transcript to revised copy to galley to book." Where the men "worried about content," "her focus was the materiality of the processes that make sounds into words, marks into books." She is the one who "strained to catch inaudible strange words," who learned stenotypography, whose transcripts under time-pressure "had more ellipses than words" (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The embodied, erased observer.** Janet Freed is the affirmative material counter-figure to a chapter otherwise occupied by the reification of [[Information]]. "More than anyone else, she qualifies as the outside observer who watches a system that she constructs through the marks she makes on paper, although the system itself has a great deal of trouble including her within the names of those people who are authorized to speak and make meaning." She is the missing body inside [[The Observer]] problem of cybernetics — the one who constitutes the system yet cannot be named within it (`Directly stated`).
- **The sign of the repressed.** The slip Freed/Freud makes her, in Hayles's reading, "this sign of the repressed, this Freudian slip of a female who, with a flick of a 'u' ... goes from Freed to Freud, Freud to Freed." Like Freud's patients she "had no voice in the transcripts, although the transcripts have a voice that we can read only because of her." The symbol literally enacts what cybernetics tried to repress: the laboring, gendered body that information presupposes (`Directly stated`).
- **The living refutation of disembodied information.** Janet Freed is the embodied demonstration of the book's counter-thesis. "On a level beyond words, beyond theories and equations, in her body and her arms and her fingers and her aching back, Janet Freed knows that information is never disembodied, that messages don't flow by themselves." Her aching back is the standing argument against [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy and the fantasy that information "circulates unchanged among different material substrates" (`Directly stated`).
- **The figure of reification's blind spot.** Recalling Dorothy Smith, Hayles makes Freed the test case for why "men of a certain class are prone to decontextualization and reification because they are in a position to command the labors of others." From the dictating man's point of view, "his words fly, by themselves, into books" — because he is not the one performing the labor. Freed names exactly the [[Inscription vs Incorporation|incorporating practice]] that reification renders invisible (`Strongly implied`).
- **The materiality of the signifier.** As the one who "presided over the physical transformations of signifiers," Freed embodies [[The Materiality of Informatics]]: that no message exists prior to its material instantiation, and that "epistemology isn't a word floating through the thin, thin air until it is connected up with incorporating practices" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The figure appears in the final section of "Contesting for the Body of Information," titled "Janet Freud/Freed," which closes a chapter about how, at the Macy Conferences, "information lost its body" and was constructed as "a disembodied medium." The whole chapter charts "the tension between reified models and embodied complexities" — between [[Homeostasis]] (information as a stable, transportable thing) and [[Reflexivity]] (information as an action whose effect on an embodied receiver must be counted). Having shown that "conceptualizing information as a disembodied entity was not an arbitrary decision, but neither was it inevitable," Hayles ends not with a theorem but with a person.

Hayles stages the encounter autobiographically — "I am sitting at my kitchen table in March 1996," mirroring Bateson, Mead, and Brand at theirs — and works from a single photograph. Janet Freed sits at the very U-shaped table that gives her name its phantom "u," her back to the camera, hands on a machine. She is glimpsed only indirectly: "Rarely do we see her directly; we glimpse her largely through her reflections in the speech of others," most pointedly Fremont-Smith's "You write and get a lovely smile," to which she answers only "No" — the single bare "No" in all the Macy transcripts.

The closing argument turns on preserving both spellings exactly. The error is not corrected because the error is the point: the slip from Freed to Freud names the body that the conferences worked to abstract away. Within the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Embodiment vs Information]] arguments, Janet Freed functions as the human anchor of [[Embodiment]] — the laborer whose aching back proves that information is never disembodied. She is, in this sense, the inverse of [[The Body as Data]]: the body that data cannot be reduced to.
