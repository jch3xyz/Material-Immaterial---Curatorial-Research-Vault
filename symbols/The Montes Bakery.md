# The Montes Bakery

## Literal Meaning in the Source

Pasteleria La Dulce is the Montes family's century-old pastry shop in the El Raval section of Barcelona — "a cramped medieval building tucked into a tiny square on one of the few streets that had escaped both the wrecking ball and the influx of yuppie chic." Zuboff opens Chapter 2 with it as a deliberate counter-image, the human prologue against which she will set the stage for surveillance capitalism. Through the 2011 Spanish economic crisis the family is pressured to sell the building "and let the future finally swallow La Dulce"; "just about everyone they knew said that the end was inevitable." The patriarch, Fito Montes, refuses. He insists that "the future will need the past. It will need these marble floors and the sweet taste of my gypsy cakes," and the family resolves to make every sacrifice to keep the bakery rather than accept "a dignified exit." (`Directly stated`)

The bakery is rendered as a thick sensory inventory — churros, buñuelos, flaó "made with fresh milk cheese laced with Spanish lavender, fennel, and mint," blood-orange tarts, "the perfume of honey and yeast" cycled by an old ceiling fan. This material density is not decoration: it is the inheritance Fito means to defend, the continuity that makes a future feel like home rather than exile. (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The immaterial inheritance against the digital future.** The bakery stands for "some things that are elusive, beautiful, surprising, mysterious, inexpressible, and immaterial but without which... life would be mechanical and soulless." In a corpus where "immaterial" usually names data, code, and disembodied information, Zuboff inverts the term: here the *immaterial* is the irreducibly human — scent, taste, memory, mystery — and the *mechanical and soulless* is the digital order that would extract and overwrite it. This makes the Montes bakery the standout anchor for `Material vs Immaterial` in Zuboff, flipping the axis the other twelve authors approach from the data side. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **The future as home rather than as conquest.** Fito's refrain — "The future is our home too" — recasts the future as a tense one *inhabits and inherits*, against the bankers and the digital juggernaut who "believe the future belongs only to them." The bakery is thus the human face of `The Right to the Future Tense`: the right to anticipate, to make and keep promises, to author one's own continuity. (`Strongly implied`)
- **The refusal of inevitability.** "Just about everyone they knew said that the end was inevitable." The Montes' decision to stay is the small-scale rehearsal of Zuboff's larger argument against `Inevitabilism` — that surveillance capitalism's "digital architecture are not, never were, and never would be inevitable" but "made by humans to be unmade and remade." (`Strongly implied`)
- **Sanctuary and the threat of exile.** The bakery is a refuge ("the family's refuge for generations") under pressure to be sold off — to be evicted from one's own continuity. It dramatizes `Home vs Exile`: the existential stakes of being expelled from a future one had assumed as home. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Context

The bakery occupies the opening pages of Chapter 2 ("August 9, 2011 — Setting the Stage for Surveillance Capitalism") and gives the chapter its human voice before Zuboff turns to the structural account. Zuboff links it explicitly to a parallel struggle that same day: "I imagine the two cities linked by invisible ribbons of scent rising from La Dulce... drifting slowly south and west to settle along the austere facade of the building that housed the Agencia Española de Protección de Datos, where another struggle for the right to the future tense was underway." The Spanish Data Protection Agency's defense of ninety citizens' `The Right to Be Forgotten` against Google is, for Zuboff, "the same tenacity, determination, and sentiment that sustained the Montes family." The bakery and the data agency are the two poles of one assertion: that the future is not the property of "indifferent capital." (`Directly stated`)
