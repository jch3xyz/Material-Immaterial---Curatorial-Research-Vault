# The Mole and the Snake

A paired animal figure that Han adopts from Deleuze's 'Postscript on the Societies of Control' to image the passage from disciplinary society to neoliberal control. The burrowing **mole** is the animal of confinement — the labourer, the subjugated subject moving through closed, predetermined spaces. The space-making **snake** is the animal of the neoliberal regime — the entrepreneur, the project that creates its own space by movement, and the bearer of guilt and debt. The pair is the organizing image of ch. 3 and a compact figure for the discipline-to-control, subject-to-project, biopolitics-to-psychopolitics passage.

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In the chapter the two animals are read off Deleuze's contrast between disciplinary and control societies. The mole is the animal proper to milieus of confinement — "The family, schools, prisons, barracks, hospitals and factories" — through which the disciplinary subject "moves within a closed system." Because the mole "cannot bear" the openness demanded by "post-industrial, immaterial and networked forms of production," the snake takes its place: "In contrast to the mole, the snake does not move in closed spaces. Rather, it makes space by means of its own movement." (Directly stated — ch. 3.)

The figure is built on a one-to-one set of oppositions, each stated literally in the text:

- The mole is **a labourer**; the snake is **an entrepreneur** — "The snake is the animal of the neoliberal regime."
- The mole "is a subjugated subject"; the snake "is a project inasmuch as it creates space through the course it steers."
- The mole "subordinates itself to spatial restrictions" of "predetermined spaces"; the snake "eliminates such limitations through new forms of movement."
- The mole's "restricted movements impose limits on productivity"; the snake's new movement serves "to generate more productivity."

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The mole = disciplinary confinement.** Closed space, enclosure, the body ordered "in space and time" — the subject of Foucault's disciplinary apparatus. (Directly stated.)
- **The snake = neoliberal control.** Open, self-generated space; the self-driving entrepreneur whose freedom is the medium of its own exploitation, resonant with the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] who exploits itself voluntarily. (Strongly implied — the chapter names the snake "an entrepreneur" of "the neoliberal regime"; the self-exploiting reading is developed in [[Auto-Exploitation]].)
- **The snake as debtor.** "Above all, the snake embodies the guilt and debts (die Schuld, die Schulden) that the neoliberal regime employs as instruments of domination." The animal of control is also the animal of indebtedness. (Directly stated.)
- **Continuity, not rupture.** The mutation from mole to snake is not liberation but intensification — capitalism "remains one and the same." The snake is a more productive shape of the same system, not its outside. (Directly stated.)

## Context

The figure organizes ch. 3 and anchors Han's reframing of Deleuze for a [[Society of Control]] now governed by the soul rather than the body. Where the disciplinary regime "organizes itself as a 'body'" and "is a biopolitical regime," the neoliberal regime "seems like a 'soul'," and "psychopolitics is its form of government." The snake therefore names the threshold at which biopolitics passes into psychopolitics: domination by "Motivation, projects, competition, optimization and initiative" — "an inexorable rivalry presented as healthy competition . . . wonderful motivation." (Directly stated — ch. 3, quoting Deleuze.)

The passage "from the mole to the snake — from subject to project" pairs naturally with the wider Subject-vs-Project opposition that runs through *Psychopolitics*: the subjugated *subject* of the closed disciplinary space gives way to the self-driving *project* of the open neoliberal space. The mole/snake image is the animal shorthand for that shift. (Interpretive synthesis — the chapter states the subject-to-project passage explicitly; its status as a standing opposition across the book is a synthesis.)
