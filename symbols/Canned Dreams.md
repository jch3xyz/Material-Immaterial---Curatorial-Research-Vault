# Canned Dreams

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In "Movies: The Reel World," [[Marshall McLuhan]] reads cinema as the paradoxical meeting point of the mechanical and the magical: the most rationalized industrial form yet manufactures, as its product, the least material of goods. He writes:

> The movie is not only a supreme expression of mechanism, but paradoxically it offers as product the most magical of consumer commodities, namely dreams.

The image is made concrete — and given its name — when McLuhan describes the worldwide export of Hollywood's product in the 1920s. The dream is literally *canned*, packaged like a tinned good and shipped abroad:

> It meant that in the 1920s the American way of life was exported to the entire world in cans. The world eagerly lined up to buy canned dreams.

The "can" is the film reel itself — the spool on which, in McLuhan's earlier phrase, "we roll up the real world ... in order to unroll it as a magic carpet of fantasy" — but it is also the standardized, exportable container of a commodity. The symbol therefore fuses two registers at once: the literal metal film canister and the metaphor of consumer packaging (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The immaterial commodity.** Canned dreams names the limit case of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] problem: a *dream* — pure fantasy, "without syntax," "an inward world of fantasy" — sold as a packaged good. The most magical of consumer commodities has no material substance of its own; what is canned and shipped is an experience, a daydream. The reel is the material husk of an immaterial product (`Strongly implied`).
- **Cinema as exhibition value made total.** McLuhan's claim that the movie "offers as product the most magical of consumer commodities" turns the image on the screen into something whose worth lies entirely in being seen and consumed. This is the consumer/media-imperial reading of cinema that bridges directly to Han's [[Exhibition Value]]: the film exists to be displayed and bought, "incentive, advertisement and, in itself, a major commodity." McLuhan even calls the movie "monster ad for consumer goods" (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **The aggressive, imperial form.** Canning is not innocent packaging. McLuhan frames the export of dreams as a form of cultural force: "The movie, as much as the alphabet and the printed word, iS an aggressive and imperial form that explodes outward into other cultures." The dream-can is a vector of "the American way of life," hastening "political change in the East." The symbol carries an explosive, expansionist charge — it belongs to the mechanical age of [[Explosion vs Implosion|explosion]], not the electric age of implosion (`Directly stated`).
- **Compensation and deferred desire.** The canned dream is the commodity that "offers poor People roles of riches and power beyond the dreams of avarice." In America the dream functions as "solace and compensation, or as a form of deferred payment by daydreaming"; abroad it reads as a catalogue of deprivation, making "the Oriental ... regard himself as an ordinary person who has been deprived of the ordinary man's birthright." The same can, consumed at home, pacifies; exported, it incites (`Directly stated`).
- **The mechanical that manufactures the magical.** The deepest paradox the symbol holds is that "a supreme expression of mechanism" is the very thing that produces dreams. McLuhan's film "pushed this mechanism to the utmost mechanical verge and beyond, into a surrealism of dreams that money can buy." Canned dreams is the figure in which industrial standardization and fantasy are revealed as the same operation — a connection to [[Technology as Magic]], cinema as the assembly line of enchantment (`Strongly implied`).

## Context

The symbol crowns McLuhan's argument that film "is itself a jerky mechanical ballet of flicks that yields a sheer dream world of romantic illusions" and is therefore "completely involved with book culture" — both the printed page and the film transfer the reader or viewer "from one world, his own, to another," producing fantasy by the same Gutenberg logic of uniform, lineal sequence. Having established cinema as a dream-machine continuous with print, McLuhan names what the machine sells: dreams, canned.

The phrase appears at the chapter's pivot from aesthetics to economics. It follows the reading of *The Great Gatsby* (Daisy and Gatsby in "a tinsel world ... corrupted by power, yet innocently pastoral in its dreaming") and immediately precedes the account of the Hollywood tycoons who "gave the American immigrant a means of self-fulfillment without any delay." It then opens onto the geopolitical claim — President Sukarno's 1956 remark that Hollywood executives were "political radicals and revolutionaries who had greatly hastened political change in the East" — that lets McLuhan read the movie as "a mighty limb of the industrial giant" and "monster ad for consumer goods."

Canned dreams is thus the hinge between McLuhan's media theory and the consumer-society thread: the movie is at once the "supreme expression of mechanism" (a [[Media as Extensions of Man|technological extension]] of [[Explosion vs Implosion|explosion]]) and "the first great consumer age['s]" defining commodity, exported worldwide before the electric implosion of [[The Global Village]] reverses the flow.
