# The Bosworth Memo

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Zuboff's account, "The Bosworth Memo" is a real document: a 2016 internal Facebook memo written by Andrew "Boz" Bosworth, "one of the company's long-standing and most influential executives," titled "The Ugly" and acquired by BuzzFeed in 2018. Its core line — "anything that allows us to connect more people more often is de facto good" — appears in Chapter 18, "A Coup from Above," where Zuboff treats the leaked text as documentary evidence rather than rhetoric. She calls it "an unobstructed view" of `Radical Indifference` "as a managerial discipline" — one of the rare moments when an internal artifact lays bare the operating logic that surveillance capitalism normally keeps shadowed. `Directly stated`

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The smoking gun for radical indifference.** The memo functions in Zuboff's argument as material proof — a confession in the firm's own words — that the connection imperative is pursued without regard to whether connection produces love or death. Bosworth itemizes both ("Maybe someone finds love.… Maybe it costs a life by exposing someone to bullies. Maybe someone dies in a terrorist attack coordinated on our tools") and concludes "And still we connect people." The memo symbolizes the institutionalization of indifference: not callousness as a personal failing, but indifference as policy. `Directly stated`
- **Equivalence without equality, made explicit.** Zuboff reads the memo as the moment "equivalence wins out over equality." Positives and negatives are rendered commensurable — flattened into a single growth metric — "despite their distinct moral meanings and human consequences." The memo thus stands as the canonical illustration of `Equivalence Without Equality`: everything is made equivalent for measurement precisely so that nothing need be treated as morally unequal. `Directly stated`
- **The voice of "an organism among organisms."** Zuboff frames Bosworth's worldview as that of "an organism among organisms" oriented to "totality" and "the growth of surveillance revenues." The memo symbolizes the firm's self-conception as an amoral metabolic system whose only true signal is the metric — "the only area where the metrics do tell the true story as far as we are concerned." `Directly stated`
- **"The best products don't win. The ones everyone uses win."** This line condenses the symbol's meaning into an operational maxim: capture, not quality, is the objective. The memo becomes shorthand for the substitution of reach for worth — the logic by which the public-facing "first text" becomes "vulnerable to corruption" by "lies, systematic disinformation, fraud, violence, hate speech" so long as such content "contributes to 'growth tactics.'" `Strongly implied`

## Context

The memo recurs in Chapter 18 as Zuboff's hinge between the abstract diagnosis of `Radical Indifference` and its concrete corporate practice. She introduces it precisely because radical indifference is normally invisible — "only occasionally do we catch an unobstructed view of its strict application as a managerial discipline." The Bosworth memo is that occasion. From the leaked text she draws two consequences: that the moral commensuration it performs (`Equivalence Without Equality`) is structural rather than incidental, and that the resulting tolerance for "repugnant" content is broken only when corruption "threatens to interrupt the flow of surplus into the second 'shadow' text" — so that content moderation is "best understood as defensive measures, not as acts of public responsibility." The symbol therefore anchors Zuboff's larger claim that `Surveillance Capitalism`'s connection imperative is an economic, not a communicative, project. `Interpretive synthesis`
