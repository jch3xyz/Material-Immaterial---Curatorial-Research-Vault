# The Tortoise (Machina speculatrix)

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Chapter 8 of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Burnham recounts that "[i]n 1948 at the Burden Neurological Institute in Bristol, England, Grey Walter created two automata and awarded them the zoological classification *Machina speculatrix*." The two machines — "Elmer (Electro-Mechanical Robot) and Elsie (Electro-Light-Sensitive- Internal- External) were designed as plastic-clad 'tortoises' with three wheels (the front one for steering), a revolving photoelectric cell for light detection and a knack for producing nearly lifelike emotions" (`Directly stated`).

Built on "the principle of parsimony" — "not a visual imitation of nature, but a functional imitation with the fewest possible components" — the tortoises "are reduced to the equivalent of two nerve cells reacting to stimuli: two vacuum tubes, two relays, two condensers, and two electric motors are their main components," plus "a photoelectric cell (for 'sight' or responses to light sources) and an electrical contact (the sense of touch)" (`Directly stated`). They run on rechargeable batteries and are governed by a voltage-keyed appetite: "below 5.5 volts (the hunger threshold), the tortoises seek their food supply in the form of a strongly lighted 'hutch' containing electrical contacts to which the tortoises move close so as to recharge their batteries. When the charge has reached seven volts (satiation) the tortoises retire in stillness to 'digest' their meal of electrical current" (`Directly stated`).

For Burnham these are "crude but early cybernetic organisms, mechanically constructed to simulate behavior patterns" — "the first mechanical steps in duplicating the purposiveness of living organisms" (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The first true cybernetic organism.** The tortoise stands in Burnham's narrative as the threshold object where the machine stops merely *looking* alive and begins to *behave* alive — a "functional imitation" rather than a "visual imitation of nature." It is the symbol of the cybernetic changeover from the [[Automata|automaton]]'s mimicry of external form to the engineered reproduction of goal-seeking behavior (`Strongly implied`).
- **The benchmark of lifelikeness.** Burnham makes *Machina speculatrix* the measuring stick against which mock robots fail: Castro-Cid's *Anthropomorphic 1*, "reduced to its functional definition, this machine has none of the goalseeking, self-stabilizing ability of even such relatively simple animals as Grey Walter's *Machina speculatrix*; it is, indeed, a mock robot." The tortoise is therefore the positive pole of the opposition the [[The Mock Robot|mock robot]] negatively defines (`Directly stated`).
- **Appetite, homeostasis, and survival as design.** The hunger threshold, the lighted hutch, and the satiation voltage make metabolism into circuitry; "the mechanism is designed to insure its own survival in terms of power," so that when the current is cut the tortoises "die." The tortoise symbolizes self-preserving [[Homeostasis|internal stability]] achieved through a [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] "between the animal and its environment" (`Directly stated`).
- **Personality and unpredictability as emergent properties.** Though "put together nearly identically," the two machines "possess distinctly different personalities" — Elsie "sprightly," Elmer hiding "under the furniture much of the time" — suggesting that minute differences yield individuality. The tortoise becomes a symbol of [[Emergence|emergent]], non-programmed behavior, the very "unpredictability of an organism" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The tortoise appears in the chapter "Robot and Cyborg Art," where Burnham distinguishes the dead image (the doll, the waxwork, the figurative [[The Mock Robot|mock robot]]) from the living system, and traces sculpture's drive toward "the cybernetic organism as an art form." Grey Walter's *Machina speculatrix* is his exhibit A for genuine machine behavior — the standard against which both the mock robot and later [[Cyborg Art]] are judged. Burnham frames the tortoises within a Wienerian account of [[Cybernetics]], drawing on Walter's *The Living Brain* (1953) and its claim that "there is, however, a well-defined difference between the magical and the scientific imitation of life. The former copies external appearances; the latter is concerned with performance and behavior."

The motif recurs across the chapter as a reference point: Schöffer's CYSP I produces, "as in the case of Grey Walter's tortoises, ... the unpredictability of an organism," and the lineage of cybernetic "animals" (Edmund Berkeley's "Squee," Claude Shannon's maze-running "mice") is described as following "the years following the tortoises." Burnham's measured verdict is that "the real merit of Walter's goal-seeking, self-stabilizing automata is that they possess just enough of the colorfulness of humanoid robots to dramatize the possibilities of further cybernetic research."

Within the vault the tortoise is the concrete, historical anchor for the cybernetic-organism cluster: it realizes the long-standing forward-reference in [[The Mock Robot]], and it cross-links the systems vocabulary ([[Cybernetics]], [[Feedback Loops]], [[Homeostasis]], [[Emergence]]) to the art-historical drive toward [[Cyborg Art]] and, beyond Burnham, toward [[The Cyborg]] and [[Posthumanism]]. ## Related Concepts, Metaphors, and Tensions

- [[The Mock Robot]] — the negative counterpart; the tortoise is the goal-seeking, self-stabilizing benchmark whose *absence* defines a mock robot.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the science of goal-seeking, self-stabilizing, feedback-governed systems the tortoise embodies.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — "a feedback loop between the animal and its environment" is the tortoise's principle of self-regulation.
- [[Homeostasis]] — the voltage-keyed hunger/satiation cycle as engineered internal stability and self-survival.
- [[Emergence]] — distinct "personalities" arising from near-identical construction; the unpredictability of an organism.
- [[Cyborg Art]] — the genre the tortoise inaugurates as "the cybernetic organism as an art form."
- [[Automata]] — the older tradition of *visual* imitation the tortoise supersedes with *functional* imitation.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the boundary-breaking figure Burnham's cybernetic organism art-historically anticipates.

Self-organizing and self-stabilizing systems are the broader category to which the tortoise belongs; Burnham's account of "internal stability" and self-survival makes it the paradigm case (`Interpretive synthesis`).
