# The Table

The keystone figure of Ascott's 1975 essay "Table": the domestic table read not as an object but as a behavioural matrix — "an arena for behaviours," "a medium of exchange," "the grounds for change," "a matrix (and so at all levels)," and, in myth and religion, "the mystical matrix" of transformation between two poles. It is Ascott's model of the open-system artwork in miniature — a horizontal surface on which "art resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system" — and the recurring counter-figure to the vertical mirror/picture on the wall. The table is the participatory, behaviourist pole of 1960s cybernetic art: where [[Systems Esthetics]] reads the artwork's object→system shift from the side of the object, the table reads it from the side of behaviour and exchange.

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In the essay's home semiotics, the table is the innermost of a nested set of analogues: "Each person's house is a received or created analogue of his/her perceived or wished-for universe. The table is the analogue of the house." `Directly stated` (ch.10). The kitchen utensils — "a fork, a funnel, a container, a grater, a sieve" — are "all pure ideas, realised in hardware," and using them brings "into conjunction new assemblies of ideas." The table is the arena where these domestic behaviours, "ritual analogue[s] of our ways of dealing with the world," are enacted.

Ascott then defines the table relationally, not as a thing: "A table exists, just as a house exists and our universe exists, only in so far as it is the arena for behaviours." `Directly stated`. We are "never entirely in a table, only at a table" — the table affords a degree of alienation sufficient for transaction across it: "The table is a medium of exchange, the space between behaviours and states, the grounds for change. A table is very properly a matrix (and so at all levels)." `Directly stated`.

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The matrix of transformation (mystical / sacred horizontal).** In myth and religion the table "is always the matrix of transformation and transfer, of change and exchange, between two poles, two worlds (of discourse)" — and "the dialectical surface is necessarily horizontal." The altar is "the sacred horizontal that stands between the vertical polarities of heaven and earth"; "In religion, the table is the mystical matrix." `Directly stated`. The Tarot, whose "most generative card … the magician, has at its centre the table," is named "the most metaphysically refined of table-top behaviours." `Directly stated`.

- **The model of the open-system artwork.** The table-top is the working model of art as transaction: "The table-top supports an open system, in which only the intervention of observers can generate meaning and value. Art, then, resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system." `Directly stated`. This is the object→system / [[Material vs Immaterial]] move in miniature, and Ascott's behaviourist contribution to the [[Object and System|object-to-system]] spine. `Interpretive synthesis`.

- **The many-way against the one-way (dream table vs couch).** The table is a topology of reciprocity: "The dream table, unlike the couch, has infinite lines of communication, enabling transactions to occur between the inner worlds, the dreams or inventions of all those placed around it." `Directly stated`. The analyst's couch "only permitted a one-way line of communication," with the doctor "hidden, disclosing nothing"; the dream table "absolutely replac[es]" it. This is the paradigm case of [[Reciprocal Communication]]: "Reciprocity generates trust and sympathy." `Directly stated`.

- **The horizontal against the vertical mirror.** The table is the structural antagonist of the picture/mirror on the wall — the vertical plane installed by Renaissance perspective "to demonstrate man's isolation from the universe, to close down mystical transactions." "Bonded meanings are actually denied by the convention of the mirror or picture on the wall," which "immobilises the viewer" in a fixed viewpoint, "always totally alienating." The picture on the wall is "the archetypal convention of a whole culture of separation, competition, self-love." `Directly stated`. The full opposition is carried by [[Horizontal vs Vertical]]. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Context

"Table" was first published in *House: The Journal of the London Architecture Club* (vol. 1, no. 1, 1975) and reprinted in *Telematic Embrace*. It sits in Ascott's cybernetic/behaviourist phase, downstream of "The Construction of Change" and "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision," and the table is one concrete, domestic instance of his thesis that art is "a form of behaviour (rather than a simple array of images or objects)" that "aspires to the condition of exchange" — the credo of [[Behaviourist Art]]. `Directly stated`.

The table also folds two recurring motifs of the essay. The first is the **house-and-utensils-as-analogues** motif: the table is "the analogue of the house," domestic objects are "pure ideas, realised in hardware," and the table "provide[s] the arena to construct and rehearse alternative behaviours … a device for divination, a sounding board for new relationships … a test-bed for change." `Directly stated`. The second is the extension of the horizontal/vertical thesis to electronic media: Ascott blames "the bankruptcy and irrelevance of 'video art'" on the vertical monitor and argues that "only when video is created to be viewed on the horizontal, table-top plane, seen all round without top or bottom, will it have the possibility of contributing to our holistic consciousness." `Directly stated`. This anticipates his later anti-spectatorial, network-consciousness arguments — the table-top as a many-to-many transactional surface prefigures the telematic network. `Speculative connection`.

Two further in-text resonances are flagged but not realized here as durable notes, because they belong to the later technoetics chapters: the Sufi "cosmic corporeality" ("Sufi culture, which is concerned with maintaining a continuous daily awareness of the bonded meanings of all acts, a cosmic corporeality") and "psychic instrumentation" (domestic utensils repurposed as "psychic instruments … whether specially made objects or things 'borrowed' from their everyday contexts (fork, funnel, knife, platter)"). These 1975 motifs prefigure Ascott's 1990s technoetic/moistmedia program. `Speculative connection`.
