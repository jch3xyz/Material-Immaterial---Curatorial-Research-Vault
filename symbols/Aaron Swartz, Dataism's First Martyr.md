# Aaron Swartz, Dataism's First Martyr

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In the section "Information Wants to be Free," Harari introduces Aaron Swartz as the human figure who makes [[Dataism]]'s newest value — [[Freedom of Information]] — concrete and consequential. After arguing that "Dataism upholds the freedom of information as the greatest good of all," and that this is "the first movement since 1789 that created a really novel value," Harari names a casualty:

> On 11 January 2013, Dataism got its first martyr when Aaron Swartz, a twenty-six-year-old American hacker, committed suicide in his apartment.

Swartz appears in the text as a biographical sketch with martyrological framing: "a rare genius" who "at fourteen, helped develop the crucial RSS protocol," and "a firm believer in the freedom of information." His creed is quoted directly from his 2008 "Guerilla Open Access Manifesto":

> We need to take information, wherever it is stored, make our copies and share them with the world.

Harari narrates the act that follows from the creed — Swartz used the MIT network to download "hundreds of thousands of scientific papers" from JSTOR to release them freely — his arrest and trial, and his suicide when "he realised he would probably be convicted and sent to jail." The episode closes with the hacker community's reaction (petitions and attacks on "the academic and governmental institutions that persecuted Swartz") and JSTOR's apology and partial opening of its data `Directly stated`.

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The first martyr of a new religion.** Harari treats [[Dataism]] explicitly as a religion with "practical commandments," "missionary" zeal, and "prophets" — and a martyr is the structural completion of that vocabulary. Swartz's death gives the data religion a saint and a founding sacrifice, the way martyrs anchor "Like other successful religions" the data faith Harari describes `Strongly implied`.
- **The creed made flesh.** Swartz embodies the dogma "information wants to be free" — Harari writes that Swartz "believed that information wants to be free, that ideas don't belong to the people who created them." He is the test case where [[Freedom of Information]] (a value "given to information," not to humans) overrides a human's claim to "own data and to restrict its movement" `Directly stated`.
- **Freedom of information against freedom of expression.** The Swartz–JSTOR conflict literalizes Harari's distinction: JSTOR "believes in the freedom of expression of scientists and journal editors, which includes the freedom to charge a fee," while Swartz fought for the freedom of the information itself to circulate. The martyr marks the point where the new value "may impinge on the traditional freedom of expression" `Directly stated`.
- **Self-sacrifice to the data flow.** Read against the book's later claim that "the greatest sin is to block the data flow" and "What is death, if not a situation when information doesn't flow?", Swartz dies resisting blockage — a human extinguished in the name of keeping information moving `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Context

The Swartz episode sits in "Information Wants to be Free," the section where Harari elevates [[Freedom of Information]] from a slogan to "the first movement since 1789 that created a really novel value." Swartz is the section's only named human protagonist; he personalizes an otherwise systemic argument about information, JSTOR, capitalism, and the [[The Internet-of-All-Things]]. The figure recurs in spirit (not name) two sections later under the banner "Record, Upload, Share!", where the same imperative Swartz lived by — "We need to take information... make our copies and share them with the world" — returns as a youth motto: "If you experience something — record it. If you record something — upload it. If you upload something — share it." Swartz is thus the martyr at the threshold of the chapter's turn from the politics of open data to the everyday Dataist ritual of total sharing `Interpretive synthesis`.
