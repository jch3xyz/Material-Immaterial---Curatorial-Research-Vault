# Walden Two and 1984

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Chapter 12 ("Two Species of Power"), Zuboff stages two postwar "utopian" novels as the matched figureheads of two distinct species of power. B. F. Skinner's *Walden Two* (1948) is the behavioral utopia: a fictional community governed not by politics or force but by "behavioral engineering" derived from his `[[Radical Behaviorism]]` and the physicist's viewpoint of `[[The Other-One]]`. George Orwell's *1984* (1949) is the totalitarian dystopia: the soul possessed "from the inside out" by Big Brother. *Directly stated* (Ch.12): "Viewed from a distance, Skinner's *Walden Two*, published in 1948, and Orwell's *1984*, released the following year, had much in common. Each elaborated a complete conception of a distinct logic of power, each imagined a society defined by the full flourishing of that power, and each was utopian from the point of view of the form of power that it described."

The two books, Zuboff notes, "have often been confused with each other." Time magazine's 1971 cover story on Skinner described *Walden Two* as raising "the specter of a 1984 Orwellian society"; Lewis Mumford called it a "totalitarian utopia" and "hell." Against this received reading, Zuboff insists the pairing is a *contrast*, not a likeness: "Although both books have been described as depictions of totalitarianism, the forms of power that each describes are profoundly different. In most respects, they are precise opposites." *Directly stated* (Ch.12). She also marks their opposite public fates: "*Walden Two* was dismissed as a dystopian nightmare and ignored by the general public for more than a decade. Orwell's *1984* was immediately canonized as a dystopian masterpiece."

In Chapter 14 ("A Utopia of Certainty"), the pair returns with the historical clock advanced. *Walden Two*, once met with "revulsion," is now "the real thing" and "inspirational fodder for surveillance capitalist rhetoric." *Directly stated* (Ch.14): "Seven decades ago, Skinner's proto-instrumentarian behavioral utopia, *Walden Two*, was met with revulsion. Today the real thing is inspirational fodder for surveillance capitalist rhetoric as leaders promote the tools and visions that will bring the old professor's ideas to life… to our lives."

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **Two species of power, not two faces of one.** The book-pair is Zuboff's literary device for her central distinction. *1984* figures totalitarianism — power "as a political condition" that "relies on violence," seeking "the ruthless insistence upon the absolute possession of each individual human being… from the inside out." *Walden Two* figures `[[Instrumentarian Power]]` — power that "transcends the use of force and rejects the need to dominate human souls," substituting `[[The Means of Behavioral Modification|behavioral engineering]]` for the boot. To read the two novels as the same thing (Mumford, Time) is, for Zuboff, "a persistent and… dangerous confusion." This is the symbol that anchors the tension `[[Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism]]`. *Directly stated* (Ch.12).

- **"Before" and "after" the scientific reimagining of modernity.** Taking Max Meyer's behaviorist objectification of human experience as the benchmark, Zuboff arranges the two utopias on a timeline of the soul: "Orwell's utopia is the 'before' case; it precedes Meyer as a nightmare of the prescientific compulsion to dominate the soul. Skinner's utopia is 'after' Meyer's reimagining of modernity… Orwell laid bare the disease, and Skinner asserted the antidote." The pair thus symbolizes a historical mutation in the *form* of total power: from the gross domination of consciousness to the elegant administration of behavior. *Directly stated* (Ch.12).

- **The antidote that is itself a danger.** Skinner did not write *Walden Two* as a warning but as "the antidote to totalitarianism" — a "utopia of technique" promising harmony "for which all the ideals of a liberal society—freedom, autonomy, privacy, a people's right to self-rule—must be forfeit." The symbol's irony is that the cure dispenses with exactly what the `[[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal subject]]` holds sacred. Skinner's avatar Frazier insists "We don't use force! All we need is adequate behavioral engineering." The bloodless utopia is precisely the one Zuboff fears most, because it asks for no violence and so meets no resistance. *Directly stated* (Ch.12); *Interpretive synthesis* (the irony).

- **The proto-instrumentarian dream now coming true.** In Chapter 14 the pair stops being a thought experiment and becomes a forecast. Where Orwell's totalitarianism required "soldiers to pave the way in blood," surveillance capitalists are "the rare utopianists who can oversee the translation of their imaginations into fact without soldiers." The *Walden Two* vision — freedom forfeit to others' knowledge — is realized "now in the service of others' wealth and power." The book that was a nightmare in 1948 is the operating manual of `[[A Utopia of Certainty]]` in the present. *Directly stated* (Ch.12, Ch.14).

- **Microsoft's factories as Skinner's labs.** Zuboff's most concrete realization of the symbol is Satya Nadella's 2017 demonstration of an AI-instrumented factory. She reads it as *Walden Two* digitized: "In Microsoft's instrumentarian society, the factories and workplaces are like Skinner's labs, and the machines replace his pigeons and rats. These are the settings where the architecture and velocities of instrumentarian power are readied for translation to society in a digital-age iteration of *Walden Two* in which machine relations are the model for social relations." The hive of confluent machines — and humans rendered as "things in the cloud" — is Frazier's "superorganism" enabled by `[[Big Other]]`. *Directly stated* (Ch.14).

## Context

The book-pair recurs at the two structural hinges of Part III. In Chapter 12 it caps Zuboff's genealogy of behaviorism (Planck → Meyer → Skinner) and her case that today's threat is misnamed when called "digital totalitarianism." The whole rhetorical purpose of the contrast is to break the reflex of reaching for Orwell's Big Brother: "the equation of instrumentarian power with totalitarianism impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist." The two utopias give her a clean image for *what kind of power this actually is* — not domination of the soul but instrumentation of behavior, not the boot but the nudge.

In Chapter 14 the pair reappears as the frame for the surveillance-capitalist endgame, "a utopia of certainty." Nadella's smart factory, Pichai's billion-user platforms that "would have made Professor Skinner glow," and Zuckerberg's "applied utopistics" are read back through *Walden Two*: each is a contemporary instantiation of Frazier's behaviorally engineered superorganism. The symbol therefore bridges the diagnostic half of the book (what surveillance capitalism *is*) and the prophetic half (where it is *taking us*). It is the literary counterpart to the conceptual tension `[[Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism]]` and the concept `[[A Utopia of Certainty]]`, and it grounds the reference `[[Shoshana Zuboff and B. F. Skinner]]`.
