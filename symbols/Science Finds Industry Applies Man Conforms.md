# Science Finds Industry Applies Man Conforms

## Literal Meaning in the Source

The phrase is the motto of the 1933 Chicago World's Fair (the "Century of Progress" exposition), which Shoshana Zuboff invokes in Chapter 1 of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* as the standing emblem of the inevitabilist creed she diagnoses throughout the book. Setting up her later, in-depth treatment of [[Inevitabilism]] in Chapter 7, she writes that "despite all the futuristic sophistication of digital innovation, the message of the surveillance capitalist companies barely differs from the themes once glorified in the motto of the 1933 Chicago World's Fair: 'Science Finds—Industry Applies—Man Conforms'" (`Directly stated`).

The three clauses describe a one-way chain of causation. *Science Finds* and *Industry Applies* locate all agency in discovery and production; *Man Conforms* assigns to humanity only the residual role of adaptation. The motto is literal in the source in that Zuboff quotes it verbatim as a historical artifact, not as a figure she has coined — its rhetorical force comes precisely from its provenance: a nearly century-old advertisement for technological progress that, on her reading, the surveillance capitalists have updated only in vocabulary, not in substance.

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The whole ideology of inevitabilism in three words.** The motto condenses the doctrine Zuboff names [[Inevitabilism]]: the claim that ubiquitous computing and data extraction are simply what technology *does*, an autonomous force to which the only rational response is adaptation. The motto symbolizes that claim in its purest, most candid form — what 2010s tech rhetoric dresses in "futuristic sophistication," the 1933 slogan stated plainly (`Strongly implied`).
- **"Man Conforms" as the deletion of agency.** The third clause is the operative one. It casts the human as the passive *terminus* of a process decided elsewhere — the human who does not choose but only adjusts. This is the same "weaponized virus of moral nihilism programmed to target human agency" that Zuboff says every doctrine of inevitability carries; *Man Conforms* is that virus rendered as a public motto (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **The neutral-instrument alibi.** By placing technology before and above the human, the motto enacts the view of technology as an autonomous, value-neutral given rather than an economic *means* patterned by what Weber called the "economic orientation." It is therefore the symbolic counterpart of the misdirection Zuboff attributes to Eric Schmidt — rhetoric that bewilders the public "by conflating commercial imperatives and technological necessity" and so "makes surveillance capitalism's practices appear to be inevitable when they are actually meticulously calculated and lavishly funded means to self-dealing commercial ends" (`Directly stated`).
- **The continuity of the message across a century.** That a Depression-era fair slogan can stand in for twenty-first-century platform rhetoric symbolizes Zuboff's claim of *continuity*: the surveillance capitalists' message "barely differs" from the old industrial gospel. The novelty is in the technology, not in the ideology of human subordination to it (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The motto appears at the close of Chapter 1's analysis of Eric Schmidt's claim that search engines do not "retain" data — a "classic of misdirection" by which commercial imperatives are passed off as technological necessity. Zuboff caps that analysis with the World's Fair motto, using it to historicize the rhetoric: the inevitabilist message is not new to the digital age but inherited from the industrial one. She then defers the full treatment to Chapter 7 ("The Reality Business") and the summary mechanisms of Chapter 11, where inevitabilism is named and refuted as a doctrine ("technological inevitability does not exist").

As a symbol, the phrase recurs in the vault as the emblem the [[Inevitabilism]] concept is anchored to — its standing image. It is the surveillance-capitalist register of the broader refusal of technological autonomy that the vault also tracks in Marshall McLuhan's verdict that the neutral-instrument view is [[The neutral-instrument view of technology is the numb stance of the technological idiot|the numb stance of the technological idiot]] and in [[The Content Obsession]]. Where McLuhan diagnoses a perceptual numbness (fixation on content blinding us to the medium), the motto's *Man Conforms* names a political-economic numbness (fixation on technology blinding us to the accumulation imperative driving it). The convergence is interpretive: Zuboff does not cite McLuhan, but the two refusals rhyme (`Interpretive synthesis`).
