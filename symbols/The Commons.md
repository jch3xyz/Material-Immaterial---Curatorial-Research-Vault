# The Commons

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Donella Meadows's chapter on system traps, the commons is Garrett Hardin's shared grazing land: "Picture a pasture open to all." Onto this pasture each herdsman, "explicitly or implicitly, more or less consciously," puts the question "What is the utility to me of adding one more animal to my herd?" Because the herdsman "receives all the proceeds from the sale of the additional animal" while "the effects of overgrazing are shared by all," the arithmetic of self-interest always points the same way: "The rational herdsman concludes that the only sensible course for him to pursue is to add another animal to his herd. And another; and another" (`Directly stated`). The single pasture — limited, erodable, and open to all — is the literal object; the herd growing upon it until the grass, the soil, and the herders are ruined is the literal action that gives the image its force.

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The emblem of the [[The Tragedy of the Commons|tragedy-of-the-commons]] archetype.** The pasture stands in for any shared, erodable resource driven to collapse by its own users — Meadows lists the atmosphere, the oceans, groundwater, and public budgets as commonplace commons "being driven, or have been driven, to disaster." The single grazing field becomes the readable picture of a whole class of system.
- **A picture of missing feedback.** The commons dramatizes not a moral failing but a broken loop: the herdsman "has no reason, no incentive, no strong feedback, to let the possibility of overgrazing stop him." The pasture is the symbol of a resource whose deteriorating condition never travels back to the decisions of the people consuming it — "the tragedy of the commons arises from missing (or too long delayed) feedback from the resource to the growth of the users of that resource" (`Directly stated`).
- **[[Bounded Rationality|Bounded rationality]] made visible.** Each herder is locally, individually rational; ruin is the aggregate. The shared field is the stage on which many reasonable private choices compose one collective catastrophe — "Bounded rationality in a nutshell!"
- **The limited world.** The herd grows "without limit — in a world that is limited." The pasture is the finite ground beneath any exponential appetite, the boundary that unrestrained growth cannot see and cannot survive crossing.

## Context

The commons recurs in Meadows's chapter as the exemplar of a "system trap" — an archetypal structure that reliably produces bad outcomes regardless of the intentions of the actors caught inside it. She takes the image from Hardin's 1968 article and re-reads it in her own systems vocabulary: the field's erosion is "another [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop|reinforcing feedback loop]] running downhill" (as grass thins, cows eat the base of the stems, roots no longer hold the soil, less soil grows less grass), and the trap as a whole is a severed link in the network of [[Feedback Loops]] between the resource and its users. The symbolic pasture is what lets Meadows state the three ways out — educate and exhort, privatize the commons, or regulate the commons — as operations on one legible picture. The image is doing the archetype's work: it makes an abstract structural pathology into a scene anyone can hold in mind.
