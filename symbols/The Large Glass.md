# The Large Glass

Marcel Duchamp's *The Bride Stripped Bare by Her Bachelors, Even* (the **Large Glass**) is, for Roy Ascott, the proto-interface of telematic art: a transparent work whose meaning is completed only when the observer's reflection and surrounding environment are folded into it. In chapter 20, *Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?*, Ascott reaches back through the history of Western art to ground his concept of the computer screen as a participatory field, and finds its origin in Duchamp's glass. (`Directly stated` — Ascott names the Large Glass as anticipatory of the computer monitor.)

## Literal Meaning in the Source

Ascott reads the Large Glass as "a field of vitreous reality in which energy and emotion are generated from the tension and interaction of male and female, natural and artificial, human and machine." Because the work is physically transparent, it "always includes both its environment and the reflection of the observer" — the viewer is literally seen within the work and so "comes to be a progenitor of the semantic issue." Its subject, Ascott says, is attraction, "or, we might even say, love"; love "is contained in this total embrace; all that escapes is reason and certainty." (`Directly stated`.)

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The work as proto-interface / proto-screen.** Ascott assigns the Large Glass, "as 'ground,'" a "function and status anticipating that of the computer monitor as a screen of operations—of transformations—and as the site of interaction and negotiation for meaning." The transparent pane becomes the symbolic ancestor of the telematic screen. This is the symbol's load-bearing function for Ascott's art-historical lineage of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]. (`Directly stated`.)
- **The window turned back on itself.** Duchamp is "the first" to take "the metaphor of the glass, of the window onto the world, and [turn] it back on itself to reveal what is invisible." The glass inverts the Renaissance window (the picture as a transparent frame *onto* a depicted world) into a surface that reflects the viewer and the room — a passage rather than a view. This is the lineage the sibling notes [[The Window vs the Doorway]] and [[The Interface as Doorway]] develop. (`Directly stated` for the inversion; `Interpretive synthesis` for the doorway cross-link.)
- **The observer as creator; meaning as unstable.** "By participating in the embrace, the viewer comes to be a progenitor of the semantic issue." Ascott generalizes this beyond the single work: "The very metaphor of networking interaction in a field of uncertainty, in which the observer is creator and meaning is unstable, is implicit in all his work." The glass thus symbolizes a model of art as [[Reciprocal Communication]] rather than one-way transmission. (`Directly stated`.)
- **The horizontal / holistic systems view.** The work's horizontal bar serves "as a metaphor for the all-around viewing, the inclusive, all embracing scope of its vision," set "in opposition to the vertical, head-to-toe viewing of Renaissance space." Ascott aligns this horizontality with "the all-over, all-embracing, holistic systems view of structures, relationships, and events." The glass becomes an emblem of the systems sensibility underpinning telematic art and the broader [[Material vs Immaterial]] turn from object to field. (`Strongly implied`.)

## Context

The Large Glass appears in chapter 20, the keynote essay of *Telematic Embrace*, where Ascott seeks an art-historical precedent for telematic art's defining condition: a work that is not a fixed object delivering content but a transparent, participatory ground that includes its observer and environment. Duchamp's glass is the precedent he names — "in the recent history of Western art, it was Marcel Duchamp who first" performed this inversion. The motif anchors Ascott's claim that telematic networking aspires to a "condition of perception and insight" already latent in the Large Glass: distributed, reflexive, and tolerant of "the ironic, the fuzzy, and the ambiguous." It is the symbolic hinge between [[Marcel Duchamp]] and the computer screen as interface. (`Directly stated`.)
