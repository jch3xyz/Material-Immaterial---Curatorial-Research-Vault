# The Electric Meter

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In an interlude to Chapter 4, Meadows retells a case from a suburb of identical single-family houses near Amsterdam. For no designed reason, some houses had the electric meter installed in the basement and others in the front hall. During the energy crisis of the early 1970s the Dutch discovered that the front-hall houses used *one-third less electricity* than the basement houses — same price, similar families, same physical dwellings. The only difference was where the meter sat: whether the household could see the "little wheel turning around, adding up the monthly electricity bill many times a day."

The device itself is trivial — a glass bubble with a spinning wheel and a dial. What matters is not the meter but its *position*, which decides whether a household's own consumption is visible to it or hidden. The meter is nothing but a channel for a feedback signal; moving it from the basement to the hall changes nothing material and everything behavioral.

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **Information feedback as the highest-leverage, lowest-cost intervention.** Nothing physical was added — no insulation, no smaller appliances, no price change. Only the *delivery of a signal already being generated* was altered. The meter stands for Meadows's thesis that "missing feedback is one of the most common causes of system malfunction," and that restoring the right feedback to the right place at the right time is disproportionately powerful (`Directly stated`).
- **The immaterial governing the material.** The kilowatt-hours are physical; the meter's placement is a matter of pure information geometry. That a rearrangement of *where a signal is seen* outperforms any change to the material apparatus makes the meter an emblem of the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] spine: the immaterial signal is the operative variable, the material house is inert (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **Enlarging [[Bounded Rationality]].** Households are not irrational; they simply cannot act on a bill they never see until month's end. A hidden meter keeps consumption below the horizon of the actor; a visible one folds the consequence back into the moment of the act. The meter dramatizes how behavior changes "with even slight enlargement of bounded rationality, by providing better, more complete, timelier information" (`Directly stated`).
- **Collapsing a [[Delays|delay]].** The basement meter is not merely hidden — it defers the signal to a distant monthly reckoning. The front-hall meter shortens the loop, letting the actor feel the cost "many times a day." The symbol thus also figures the correction of a delayed feedback loop (`Strongly implied`).

## Context

The Dutch-houses interlude closes Chapter 4's argument that missing, distorted, or delayed information routinely makes systems misbehave — and that supplying it is a fix aimed at *structure*, not at people. It sits inside Meadows's larger claim that intractable problems are properties of a system's design rather than of the wickedness or stupidity of the actors in it; the remedy is to redesign the flows of information so that the system's own feedback does the work. The meter is the compact, memorable image she uses to make that abstract point land — a single relocated instrument standing in for the whole doctrine of high-leverage informational intervention.
