# The Strasbourg Cathedral Clock

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Jack Burnham's history of automata, the Strasbourg Cathedral clock is the culminating exhibit. He calls it "the most elaborate masterpiece of the art form" in "the annals of clockwork automata" — a construction "several floors high" that is "not only a clock and many-tiered panoply of automatic pageantry, but it includes many vividly portrayed astronomical functions and an ecclesiastical perpetual calendar." Burnham anchors it historically: "The original clock dates from 1352 and the Schwilgue clock (1842) is a reconstruction with added features. Even now the clock is being rebuilt for a third time." `Directly stated`

The clock is the endpoint of Burnham's narrative of the "jacks" — the jointed figures that replaced the human "keeper of the clock" in the mid-fourteenth century, which "from the fourteenth century onward" grew complex enough that "not only did they announce the time, they evolved into a series of animated vignettes portraying medieval and biblical life." The Strasbourg clock is where this animated machinery reaches its fullest theological and astronomical elaboration. `Directly stated`

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The mechanical, miniature cosmos.** Burnham's central claim is that the clock compressed the whole intellectual order of its age into a single machine: "these planetary and local timekeepers, with astrolabe, astrological recorder, history index, perpetual calendar, and an allegorical world of mundane and heavenly figures were all the stratifications of theological, philosophical, and scientific thinking compressed into one mechanical, miniature cosmos." It is technology serving as a total model of the world — a precursor to [[Technology as Metaphor]]. `Directly stated`

- **A working model of the clockwork universe controlled by God.** The clock is the Renaissance emblem of [[The Clockwork Universe]]: "The great clocks of that age were working models depicting a man-interpreted universe controlled by God." This is the same figure Burnham later traces forward to Newton, where "The world for all earthly intentions was a great clock... The winder of this clock of course was God." The cathedral clock is that cosmology built as a physical object. `Directly stated`

- **A diagram of correspondences between heaven and earth.** More than a scientific instrument, the clock modeled the medieval-Renaissance vision of an ordered, layered reality: the great clocks "were mechanical models representing the subtle array of correspondences which stood between the celestial and terrestrial worlds." `Directly stated`

- **A memento mori and a register of cosmic cycles.** The clock made the relentlessness of time visible and sacred: "the figure of Death and the stages of human life from infancy to senility became stark motifs used repeatedly to denote the passage of time," and "Being God's timekeeper, the automata clocks repeated cycle after cycle of man's stay upon Earth and, naturally, the cycles of both Earth's and heaven's movements." `Directly stated`

- **The threshold to the Age of Reason.** Burnham reads the clock as a hinge: a "Renaissance universal timepiece" whose rational, universal frame of reference "had important implications for future generations of scientists" — "Automata at this point became part of the Age of Reason." It is technology becoming the master metaphor for understanding nature. `Strongly implied`

## Context

The Strasbourg clock appears in Chapter 5, "Sculpture and Automata," in the section on clockwork men before the Enlightenment. Burnham argues that "the development of the mechanical clock must be regarded as the guiding spirit behind early modern automata," and that "The idea of 'clockwork' gradually gave man a physical conception of the universe which lasted until the present century." The Strasbourg clock is the symbol that carries this whole argument: it is where the mechanical clock stops merely measuring time and becomes a representation of the cosmic and theological order itself — a "monumental concession to technology" that set "the living pattern for an entire vicinity." `Directly stated`

For Burnham's larger thesis about sculpture, the clock matters as the historical moment when the machine becomes a vehicle of meaning and a model of the world, prefiguring the later fusion of the mechanical and the fine arts and, eventually, the kinetic and cybernetic sculpture his book builds toward. `Interpretive synthesis`
