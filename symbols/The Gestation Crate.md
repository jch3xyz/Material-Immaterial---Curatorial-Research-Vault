# The Gestation Crate

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In *Homo Deus*, the gestation crate is the metal-and-concrete pen in which a pregnant sow on an industrial farm is held immobilized. Harari describes it in precise, physical terms: a cage `usually measuring two metres by sixty centimetres`, with `a concrete floor and metal bars`, that `hardly allow the pregnant sows even to turn around or sleep on their side, never mind walk`. The sow spends three and a half months in it, is moved to a slightly wider crate to give birth and nurse, has her piglets forcibly weaned within two to four weeks, is immediately re-impregnated, and is sent back to begin the cycle again — five to ten times before she is slaughtered. `Directly stated`

The crate is the literal apparatus of a system Harari numbers at scale: `tens of millions of breeding sows pass almost their entire lives in them.` `Directly stated`

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The objective/subjective gap made concrete.** The crate is Harari's chosen image for the core lesson of evolutionary psychology: that a need can be fully met objectively while being catastrophically unmet subjectively. The farmer supplies everything the sow needs to *survive and reproduce* — food, vaccination, shelter, insemination — so that `from an objective perspective` she needs nothing more; yet `from a subjective perspective` she still feels the urges to explore, socialise, bond and walk, and `if these urges are not fulfilled she suffers greatly.` The two metres by sixty centimetres are the exact distance between those two perspectives. `Strongly implied`
- **The moral test of humanist ethics.** Industrial farming is the practical proving-ground for the question of whether humans possess a `magical spark` that licenses their dominion. The crate stages, in the body of one animal, the gap between humanism's sanctification of human needs and its disregard for the suffering it can measure but chooses not to count. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **Harlow's experiment industrialized.** The crate generalizes a cruelty that science already recognized as cruelty when performed on a handful of subjects. `What Harry Harlow did to a few hundred monkeys, the meat and dairy industries are doing to billions of animals every year.` The crate is the laboratory cage scaled to civilizational throughput. `Directly stated`

## Context

The crate appears at the hinge of the chapter "The Anthropocene," where Harari turns from the *scale* of human dominion over animals to its *moral* texture. He builds toward it by establishing that domesticated pigs inherit the intelligence, curiosity and sociality of wild boars — pigs that, trained by Professor Stanley Curtis, `soon rivalled primates in learning and playing simple computer games` — and then drops the reader into the present: `Today most sows in industrial farms don't play computer games.`

The crate recurs as the emblem to which the chapter's two big arguments attach. First, the evolutionary-psychology lesson that `a need shaped thousands of generations ago continues to be felt subjectively even if it is no longer necessary for survival and reproduction in the present.` Second, the Harlow parallel, which moves the same logic from a few hundred experimental monkeys to billions of farmed animals annually. The crate is thus the symbol that lets Harari hold the abstract (algorithms, selection pressures, subjective experience) and the visceral (concrete, bars, frustration, despair) in a single image. `Strongly implied`
