# Vaucanson's Duck

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Burnham's history of automata (Chapter 5 of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*), Vaucanson's Canard is "probably the most famous automaton of all time" — a mechanical duck built by the eighteenth-century inventor Jacques de Vaucanson (born Grenoble, 1709) and possessing "a fidelity in the imitation of organic functions which surpassed any machine built to that time" (`Directly stated`). The duck could move its body, flex its wings "so that all the feathers work in unison," quack, drink water, eat grain, "and excreting the results — all in perfect mimicry of the living animal." Set on "a great pedestal housing an imposing mechanism," it "actually underwent some of the cruder processes of digestion" (`Directly stated`).

Burnham's decisive gloss: "The Canard was an attempt to produce more than the outward features of an organism." Where earlier image-making aimed at the surface, Vaucanson reached for the *interior* — the organic functions themselves. Burnham concedes the analogy was crude — "If, like Descartes, Vaucanson applied mechanical principles to the internal organs of the body by crude analogy, at least his was a step in the direction of modern physiological analysis" — but treats the duck as a hinge in the mechanization of the living image (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The emblem of the golden age of automata.** As the single most famous automaton, the duck stands metonymically for the entire eighteenth-century efflorescence of mechanical animals and androids — the moment when, as Burnham puts it, "the making of automata was now a matter of popular technology" (`Strongly implied`).
- **The shift from outward mimicry to inner function.** The duck symbolizes the threshold at which the re-creation of life stops imitating appearances and begins imitating *processes* — digestion, excretion, coordinated motion. It is the image of life pursued from the inside out (`Directly stated`).
- **The Cartesian body-as-machine, literalized.** Burnham explicitly pairs Vaucanson with Descartes; the duck is the bête-machine made flesh in brass and springs — the mechanist hypothesis of the organism turned into a working object (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **The aesthetic core of the Faustian urge.** As the high point of literal animation, the duck embodies what Burnham calls "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge" — "Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically" (`Strongly implied`).

## Context

The Canard appears in Burnham's argument that automata are a branch of "subsculpture" whose history "has always run close to that of technology," and whose "progressive mechanization" reveals "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge." Vaucanson is Burnham's exemplary "eighteenth-century mechanical genius" — trained in "anatomy, art, music, and mechanics" — whose flute-playing automaton, silk-weaving machine "regulated by punch cards," and abandoned royal project for "the duplication of a complete man with functioning internal organs, including blood circulation" frame the duck as the centerpiece of a life-making program. After Vaucanson, Burnham notes, automata lost their abrupt, "mechanical" gestures and, "with the development of coordinated cam systems, smaller flexible spring mechanisms and tiny chain drives," "assumed the grace of living beings." The duck thus marks both a technical and a symbolic peak in the Western drive to re-create life nonbiologically (`Directly stated`).
