# The Age of Museums

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In thesis 189 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*, [[Guy Debord]] names the terminal condition of art's history "this age of museums." It is his emblem for the moment when the whole of past art becomes simultaneously available, historically catalogued, and equally acceptable — because none of it communicates any longer. Once "all the art of the past comes to be recognized and appreciated historically, and is retrospectively reclassified as phases of a single 'world art,'" that totality "is incorporated into a global disorder that can itself be seen as a sort of baroque structure at a higher level, a structure that absorbs Baroque art itself along with all its possible revivals" (th. 189). The literal referent is the museum as the institution of wholesale historical consumption — the place where "for the first time in history the arts of all ages and civilizations can be known and accepted together," and where precisely that possibility "marks the end of the world of art" (th. 189). `Directly stated`.

The museum is thus not a neutral container but a symptom. "In this age of museums in which artistic communication is no longer possible, all the previous ex pressions of art can be accepted equally, because whatever particular communication problems they may have had are eclipsed by all the present-day obstacles to communication in general" (th. 189). The equal acceptance of everything is a sign not of catholicity but of a general collapse of communication: art is accepted equally *because* it is equally mute. `Directly stated`. (The phrase "ex pressions" is reproduced verbatim from the raw OCR of the Knabb translation.)

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **Congealed culture / the dead object of contemplation.** The museum concretizes the second of the two forms in which, for Debord, the end of culture manifests itself: not "the project of culture's self-transcendence within total history" but "its preservation as a dead object for spectacular contemplation" (th. 184). In thesis 192 this is named directly: "The spectacular consumption that preserves past culture in congealed form, including co-opted rehashes of its negative manifestations, gives overt expression in its cultural sector to what it implicitly is in its totality: the communication of the incommunicable." The museum is the site where lived, communicative culture is frozen into a thing to be viewed — a case of [[Reification]] in its critical (world-losing) valence. `Directly stated`.
- **The spectacle's cultural sector.** The age of museums is the [[Spectacle]] applied to art: culture "used to bury all historical memory," the museum functioning as "official amnesia" that presents the accumulated past as a pacified, contemplatable surface (th. 192). Where the spectacle in general is "the communication of the incommunicable," the museum is that formula rendered visible in the domain of art. `Directly stated`.
- **Recuperation of the negative.** The museum absorbs even art's revolts. Debord specifies that spectacular consumption includes "co-opted rehashes of its negative manifestations" (th. 192) — Dada, modern poetry, "the most extreme destruction of language" — all of which can be "officially welcomed as a positive development" and repackaged as "modernistic pseudo-innovations." The museum is therefore the machine of [[Recuperation]]: it neutralizes the avant-garde by exhibiting it. `Directly stated`.
- **Equal acceptability as the death of communication.** The museum's defining gesture — accepting "all the previous ex pressions of art" equally — is symbolically the abolition of value-in-communication. When everything can be accepted together, nothing addresses anyone; the equivalence of all past art is the aesthetic face of the general equivalence of commodities. `Strongly implied`.
- **Precursor of the society of exhibition.** As a symbol of art reduced to a thing put on display for contemplation, the age of museums anticipates [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s later diagnosis of a society of exhibition governed by [[Exhibition Value]] — where things exist to be shown and count only insofar as they are exhibited. Debord's museum, in which art survives only as displayed spectacle stripped of communicative content, is a genealogical ancestor of Han's exhibition-value regime. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Context

The symbol appears at the climax of Chapter 8, "Negation and Consumption Within Culture," Debord's account of [[The End of Art]]. The chapter's thesis is that separated culture works out a long "march toward culture's self abolition" (th. 180): art's "declaration of independence is the beginning of its end" (th. 186), and its subsequent history runs from Baroque through "Romanticism to Cubism" as "an ever more individualistic art of negation" that "fragmented and destroyed the entire artistic sphere," closing with Dada and Surrealism (th. 189, 191). The age of museums is where this genealogy arrives: with production of a common artistic language no longer possible, art's whole past is gathered up and consumed together. The symbol therefore concretizes the two-forms structure of thesis 184 — the museum is the emblem of the branch that ends in "the defense of class power" rather than in social critique. `Directly stated`.

It recurs implicitly wherever the vault tracks the fate of the dematerialized or de-objectified artwork: Debord's abolition-and-realization doctrine ("Dadaism sought to abolish art without realizing it; Surrealism sought to realize art without abolishing it… the abolition and realization of art are inseparable aspects of a single transcendence of art," th. 191) is the Situationist ancestor of later attempts to escape the object, which Debord's account would read as one more thing the museum age recuperates. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Related Concepts, Metaphors, and Tensions

- [[The End of Art]] — the age of museums is the consummated symbolic form of this concept: the "preservation as a dead object for spectacular contemplation" pole of art's end (th. 184).
- [[Spectacle]] — the museum age is the spectacle's cultural sector, "the communication of the incommunicable"; culture used "to bury all historical memory" (th. 192).
- [[Recuperation]] — the museum absorbs and neutralizes even art's "negative manifestations," reissuing them as "modernistic pseudo-innovations" (th. 192).
- [[Reification]] — culture "preserve[d]… in congealed form" (th. 192): lived common language turned into a dead, contemplatable thing, in Debord's critical (world-losing) sense of the term.
- [[Exhibition Value]] — [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s later category; the museum-as-display is a precursor of the society of exhibition in which things count only as shown. See [[Byung-Chul Han and Guy Debord]].
- [[Détournement]] — the counter-practice Debord opposes to museal consumption: not the reverent preservation of past culture but its subversive re-use ("the opposite of quotation," th. 208).
