# The Pylon Sign (Las Vegas)

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Chapter 3, Burnham traces "a segment of one sculptural invention" — the hollow, fully enclosed box form credited to David Smith's *Cubi* series (begun 1963) — and names three antecedents. The third, "probably closer to Smith's source," is "American neon box-sign construction." Out of the neon belt and stickout signs that had become "fixtures of the American landscape" since the early 1920's, "a new genre had been invented" by the 1940's: **the roadside pylon sign** — "massively tall, supported by steel H-beams or lally columns," using "neon and incandescent light to great effect," later supplemented by "fluorescent-lighted, plastic-face inserts with enameled and stainless-steel skirts."

Burnham's claim is unhedged: "Without a doubt, the illuminated pylon sign of the 1950's was arch-underground the American art form; its influence on contemporary American sculpture has been overpowering." `Directly stated`

The literal exemplar is Las Vegas — what Burnham, quoting Tom Wolfe, calls "the Mecca of American pylon art," a city seen from Route 91 as "no buildings, no trees, only signs" whose forms exceed "the existing vocabulary." `Directly stated`

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The vernacular underground as the true engine of sculptural form.** The pylon sign stands for an art whose authority does not descend from the studio or museum but rises, anonymously and commercially, from the roadside. Burnham inverts the usual hierarchy: the gallery sculptor's "imposing forms" turn out to derive from the sign-maker's box-aggregation, not the reverse. `Strongly implied`
- **Invention as industrial hybridization.** The pylon is built by "the daring use of box form combinations" — galvanized boxes "bolted together" into aggregates — and Smith's *Cubi*, welding "the faces and edges of his volumes directly together," recapitulates "a similar evolution, though compressed." The sign is thus a symbol of how a "prime invention" proliferates into "hybrids," art and technics merging without acknowledgment. Links to [[Technics]]. `Directly stated`
- **The sign-maker as underground artist.** "The sign designer, as underground artist, had in the past an even sharper eye for new effects and techniques than the average sculptor." The pylon symbolizes a displaced authorship — the unnamed industrial craftsman who "appropriate[s] dated, corny, modern art shapes, revitalizing them," a found commercial object reframed as the source of high-art form, resonant with [[The Readymade]]. `Directly stated` / `Speculative connection` (the readymade connection is interpretive)
- **The "vertical 'display' value" of the monument.** The pylon's massive verticality is a "display" value that the latest, baseless *Cubi* pieces — under Caro's influence, sensitive to the ground plane — move toward eclipsing. The sign symbolizes the upright, advertising monumentality that advanced sculpture both borrows from and abandons. `Directly stated`

## Context

The symbol appears in Burnham's chapter on "Formalism — The Weary Vocabulary," within an argument that sculptural invention now works like corporate "significant breakthroughs," and that "the same proliferation of hybrids from prime inventions takes place in sculpture" as in manufacturing. The pylon sign is the case study: a commercial, anonymous, light-emitting box form whose "overpowering" influence on David Smith and, through him, on contemporary American sculpture, illustrates that the boundaries between "art and science-technology" have "lost their reasoning edge." The Las Vegas signscape — towering, oscillating, soaring "in shapes before which the existing vocabulary is helpless" — is the emblem of an industrialized, mass-cultural source feeding the formalist studio.
