# The Robot

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In Burnham's history of automata, the robot is the modern, debased descendant of the human-looking automaton — the clanking folklore figure of what he calls "the automatic age." It is a *mechanical man* whose difference from earlier androids "lies in its non-human characteristics, that is a lack of skin coloring, hair, clothing, and natural movements" — "an attempt to produce a mechanical servant in the spirit of the modern, ultra-functional machine." (`Directly stated`)

The word itself, Burnham notes, "first coined by Capek, comes from the Czech word meaning 'forced labor,' and implies a lack of autonomy and free will." In Karel Čapek's *R.U.R.* (1921) the robots "were not metallic imitations of men but androids" — only later did the conception undergo "debasement until it implies either a fearsome, cartoon-style, clanking mechanical man, or a person who insensitively follows orders with rigid efficiency." (`Directly stated`)

The image Burnham fixes on is sensory and specific: the robot has become "an often threatening creature clad in polished stainless steel, possessing abrupt motorized motions, metallic voice, and flashing lights for eyes." This is the figure that "burlesqued" the public's "fear of the mechanical unknown." (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The folklore figure of mechanization.** Stripped of divinity and mystery, the robot is the cartoon residue of the long lineage of automata — "a kind of Disney-like folklore for the automatic age." It carries the public's domesticated, "safe" terror of the machine without ever being conscious: it "personified a Luddite fear of the conscious machine without ever really being conscious." (`Strongly implied`)
- **Forced labor / the loss of autonomy.** Because the name means "forced labor," the robot symbolizes servitude and the absence of free will — and, by extension, "the totalitarian mentality" and "an attitude of alienation where machines prevail." The second popular meaning ("a person who insensitively follows orders with rigid efficiency") makes the robot a symbol of the *human being* drained of agency. (`Directly stated`)
- **The corporate-advertising idol.** Burnham's "Televox" / "Electro, the Man of Steel" / "Vocalite" / "Monsurus" / "Sabor" breed are not the brain children of lonely inventors but "the advertising creations of large electrical-appliance corporations." These "steel-clad machines with electrodes projecting from their temples" are "pathetic titans, more helpless and powerful than any human being" — the robot as commodity-spectacle and monstrous other-worldliness. (`Directly stated`)
- **Ennui and modern purposelessness.** In Antonioni's *Il deserto rosso* (*The Red Desert*, 1964) the toy robot in the nursery — which "repeatedly drives into a wall, stops, then backs up and turns, only to crash into the wall again" — becomes an emblem of mechanized futility and the era's "modern purposelessness and ennui." The robot here is a symbol drained even of menace, reduced to compulsive, meaningless repetition. (`Strongly implied`)

## Context

The robot recurs across the closing movement of "Sculpture and Automata," where Burnham traces a "Continuum of Images" from fetishes, idols, and proto-automata down to the cybernetic pseudo-organism. The robot marks the point at which the anthropomorphic automaton is overtaken by the "ultra-functional machine" and degraded into mass folklore. Burnham reads its descent — from the eighteenth-century golden age of Vaucanson's automata, through the nineteenth-century chess players and writers, to the WWI "war of machines" and Čapek's *R.U.R.* — as a parallel to "rising signs of human alienation." After 1945, he notes, the robot is itself superseded: cybernetic "pseudo-organisms" (Grey Walter's tortoises, Ross Ashby's homeostat, the maze-running "mice" of Claude Shannon) are "tenuously linked with life itself" rather than being "clever copies," so the robot's clanking metal body becomes a museum curiosity even as its folklore persists. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

The reduction of the human to a machine is given its starkest form in Bruno Bettelheim's "Joey, the Mechanical Boy," the nine-year-old "who believed himself a machine, rid of the humanity which he finds unbearable" — for Burnham, "still more ghastly" than a machine turned human "is the human who finds security in actually becoming a robot." The robot symbol thus runs in both directions: the machine that mimics man, and the man who hollows himself into machine. (`Directly stated`)
