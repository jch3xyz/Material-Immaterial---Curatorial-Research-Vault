# The Pieta Shipped Across the Ocean

## Literal Meaning in the Source

In the winter of 1964 Michelangelo's *Pieta* was transported from the Vatican across the Atlantic to the New York World's Fair on Long Island, provoking widespread protest. Burnham recounts it as a documented event:

> During the winter of 1964 hundreds of art lovers the world over sharply critized both the Catholic Church and officials of the New York World's Fair for transporting Michelangelo's Pieta from the Vatican to Long Island.

The statue had "rested in place at St. Peter's for over four hundred years" and was widely thought "too fragile to undergo the double ocean voyage." The fear was that "the statue's mass of crystalline stone contained a hidden fracture, one which the vibrations of travel might open needlessly." To move it at all, "elaborate precautions were taken to seal the sculpture in a series of containers surrounded by an enormous amount of shock-absorbing material and sensitive instruments to assure unchanging conditions." `Directly stated`

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The irreplaceable place-bound Object.** Burnham invokes the shipped *Pieta* explicitly as "a dramatic contrast between the handling of place-oriented Object sculpture and the extreme mobility of Systems sculpture." The marble is the emblem of the Object pole of his governing axis (see [[Object and System]]): palpable, finite, place-bound, materially singular, and — because singular — endangered the moment it leaves its place. Its very value resides in the unrepeatable original whose damage cannot be undone. `Directly stated`
- **Material presence as vulnerability.** The "enormous amount of shock-absorbing material" and the climate-monitoring instruments dramatize the cost of physical authenticity: an [[Object Art|Object]]'s presence is precisely what makes it fragile, immobile, and dependent on elaborate maintenance to survive transit. Burnham generalizes: "increasingly the very preservation of art objects depends upon the uses of safety and atmosphere control systems." `Directly stated`
- **The publicity stunt and the loss of place.** "Even after elaborate precautions virtually insured the safety of the work, it was obvious that the sculpture had never been created for an ocean-wide publicity stunt." The motif marks the irony of dragging the place-oriented Object into the circuit of the spectacle for which only the placeless, replicable System is suited. `Strongly implied`

## Context

The *Pieta* image is the negative term in a paired emblem. Burnham sets it directly against the contemporary sculptor Robert Morris, who, when the Art Institute of Chicago asked him for a work for the 1966 "Sixty-eighth Annual Contemporary Americans" show, "sent plans from which the carpenters at the museum constructed two gray L-shaped plywood forms" — and whose logical next step "is to send plans which are mounted for exhibition while the public is invited to 'imagine' the proposed sculptures in three dimensions." Where the *Pieta* must be packed in shock-absorbing containers and shipped at great risk because it is irreplaceable, the Morris L-beam travels as instructions and is rebuilt on site, indifferent to material continuity.

This is the concrete staging of [[For Object Art presence is everything for Systems Art information is the key]]: in Burnham's own words, "It becomes clear that with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." The contrast belongs to the larger logic of replication Burnham builds across the chapter — the science-museum exhibit that "is expected to wear out" and whose "entire assembly can be reduced to blueprint form for future reconstruction," and the [[Dematerialization|art system]] valued by "replication, not duration, of the original." The shipped *Pieta* is thus the symbol of everything Systems Art leaves behind: the irreplaceable artifact whose place is its meaning and whose materiality is its risk. `Directly stated`
