# The 2003 Targeted Advertising Patent

## Literal Meaning in the Source

A single, concrete archival document: a patent submitted in 2003 by three of Google's top computer scientists, titled "Generating User Information for Use in Targeted Advertising." Zuboff selects this one patent out of the many Google filed in its early years and reads it as a primary text — "a treatise on a new political economics of clicks and its moral universe, before the company learned to disguise this project in a fog of euphemism" (`Directly stated`). The patent's stated subject is the elimination of advertising waste through the prediction of which user will click which ad; its actual disclosure, in Zuboff's reading, is the founding logic of [[Surveillance Capitalism]].

The patent describes building "user profile information" ("UPI") not merely from what a user supplies but from data that "may be inferred," "presumed," and "deduced" — including from "the collateral traces of online behavior" — so that UPI "can be determined (or updated or extended) even when no explicit information is given to the system" (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Symbolic Meanings

- **The hidden-in-plain-sight confession.** Filed publicly, the patent is the document that exposes the "economic orientation" baked into the technology before euphemism papered over it. As a symbol it is the moment the project is legible in its own inventors' words — the originating evidentiary artifact for what would otherwise be deniable. (`Strongly implied`)
- **The originating evidence of [[The One-Way Mirror]].** Zuboff reads the patent's method as "an automated architecture that operates as a one-way mirror irrespective of a person's awareness, knowledge, and consent." The patent is thus the textual source of the mirror image — the document where the asymmetry of knowledge and power is first specified as engineering. (`Directly stated` for the quote; `Interpretive synthesis` for "originating evidence")
- **A declaration of war on [[Decision Rights]].** The patent symbolizes a corporate willingness to override the user's right to withhold personal information: it announces that UPI will be deduced "even when no explicit information is given." The symbol carries the moral universe of the extraction project — extraction as entitlement. (`Directly stated`)
- **The crossing-point from service to extraction.** As an emblem it marks the pivot where behavioral data stops being recycled to improve Search for users and becomes [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the "zero-cost asset" diverted toward a market exchange. The patent is the hinge of [[The Extraction Imperative]]. (`Strongly implied`)

## Context

The patent appears in Chapter 3, "The Discovery of Behavioral Surplus," in the section where Zuboff traces Google's path from an "advocacy-oriented founding" through a self-declared "state of exception" toward "behavioral surveillance as a full-blown logic of accumulation." It is the pivotal document in her account of how the [[The Behavioral Value Reinvestment Cycle]] was "subjugated to the service of a new commercial calculation," producing [[Behavioral Surplus]] as the raw material of [[Surveillance Capitalism]].

Zuboff returns to the patent's own language to show that secrecy was structural, not incidental: when Schmidt later instituted the "hiding strategy," employees were told not to speak about what "the patent had referred to as its 'novel methods, apparatus, message formats and/or data structures.'" The symbol therefore frames the larger argument that [[Surveillance]] here is not a side effect but a designed and patented capability — and that our failure to be astonished by it is itself a symptom of [[Psychic Numbing]].

The patent's method also seeds the [[Quantified Self|Quantified Self]] horizon Zuboff develops later: the deduction of "the thoughts, feelings, intentions, and interests of individuals and groups" from online traces, irrespective of the person's awareness. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
