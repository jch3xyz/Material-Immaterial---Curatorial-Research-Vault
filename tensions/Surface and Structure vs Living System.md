# Surface and Structure vs Living System

## The Poles

- **Surface and Structure** — the "edificial" conception of the built environment: architecture as the styling of impermeable façades and inert masses, "the physics of space." Buildings are objects with surfaces and interiors; cities are arenas where stylistic genres fight to "survive." This is the object pole of the [[Object vs System]] opposition applied to the built world — concrete boxes, glass and façade, appearance, representation, "relative inertia."
- **Living System** — the "biology of building": architecture and the city reconceived as evolving, cybernetic organisms — dynamic [[The City as Zone of Transformation|zones of transformation]] built from networks, interfaces, feedback, and apparition. Buildings are "planted" and "grown" rather than designed; the architect fuses "material structures and cyberspace organisms into a new continuum." This is the system pole — process over object, information over thing, [[Cyberception|cyberceived]] flux over linear surface.

## Why It Matters

This is Ascott's architectural restatement of the object→system tension that runs through the vault's [[Systems Esthetics]] cluster, transposed from the gallery to the built environment. Where [[Jack Burnham]] read the history of modern sculpture as the migration from the discrete material object to the responsive physical system, Ascott reads Western architecture as still trapped on the object side: "too much concerned with surfaces and structures and too little concerned with living systems." The opposition matters because it names what blocks a *telematic* city — a planning discourse fixated on the edificial (style, façade, the "physics of space") cannot conceive of an environment that is itself an organism: intelligent, interactive, feedback-driven, growable. Holding the tension exposes architecture's lag behind both art and the changing human subject; resolving it (toward the living system) is, for Ascott, the precondition of "the city of the twenty-first century." `Interpretive synthesis` — the framing as a tension is the system's, drawn from a single contrast Ascott states `Directly stated` (surfaces/structures vs living systems; no biology of building, only the physics of space).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] ("The Architecture of Cyberception") — diagnoses Western architecture as edificial: concerned with surfaces and structures, lacking a "biology of building," knowing only "the physics of space." Against this he proposes the city as [[The City as Zone of Transformation|a zone of transformation]] — an amalgam of "systems interfaces and communications nodes," buildings that are "grown" and "seeded" rather than designed, in which "the architect's new task is to fuse together material structures and cyberspace organisms into a new continuum." `Directly stated`.
- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] — the precedent: the same object→system migration staged for sculpture (see [[Object vs System]]). Ascott extends Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]] from the art object to the built environment. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not hold the tension in balance — he argues for its supersession. The living system is to absorb surface and structure, not coexist with it: buildings should "behave," "grow," and be "intelligent," and the architect must "fuse together material structures and cyberspace organisms into a new continuum." Yet a residue keeps the tension live. Ascott still affirms "intelligent surfaces and structures" and "humanly enriching zones and structures" — the material does not vanish; it is reanimated, made to "react to us as much as we interact with them." So the synthesis is directional rather than dialectical: not surface *versus* living system but surface *become* living system — the object pole metabolized into the system pole, exactly as in [[Object vs System]]. Whether built practice can actually make matter "grow" — "seeding replaces designing" — Ascott leaves as a challenge to architecture, not an accomplished fact. `Strongly implied`.
