# Distributed vs Centralised Processing

## The Poles

- **Distributed processing** — the architecture Harari assigns to free-market capitalism and to democracy: many independent but interconnected processors (producers, consumers, voters, banks, the stock exchange) exchange information freely and make decisions locally, with no single processor in charge. "Capitalism processes data by directly connecting all producers and consumers to one another, and allowing them to exchange information freely and make decisions independently."
- **Centralised processing** — the architecture Harari assigns to state-controlled communism and to dictatorship: "all data is processed and all decisions are made by a single central processor." Information flows to one location (Moscow; the central committee; the autocratic emperor), where the important decisions are taken and from which orders issue.

## Why It Matters

This is Harari's most concrete demonstration of his master thesis — that "organisms are algorithms" and "life is data processing" — applied to political economy. The Cold War, the rise and fall of democracy, the Roman Republic's collapse into empire: all are reframed not as moral or ideological contests but as a comparison of two **data-processing architectures**, judged solely by efficiency. What is at stake is the disappearance of the human normative frame: "Capitalism did not defeat communism because capitalism was more ethical, because individual liberties are sacred or because God was angry with the heathen communists." The victory is reattributed to throughput.

The reframing also carries a sting that cuts against the winner. Distributed processing won, on Harari's account, only "in periods of accelerating technological changes" — it is a *conditional* advantage tied to the speed of change ([[Acceleration]]), not a permanent virtue. The same logic that retired centralised communism predicts that democracy, too, "might decline and even disappear" once "data-processing conditions change again in the twenty-first century" and elections, parties and parliaments "don't process data efficiently enough." The tension is therefore a sub-argument of [[Dataism]]: once value is "determined by its contribution to data processing," even liberal democracy is held only on data-processing parole, and [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] loses the last ground on which it claimed to be sacred rather than merely efficient.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Yuval Noah Harari]] in [[Homo Deus]] — stages the tension explicitly: capitalism/democracy use distributed processing, communism/dictatorship use centralised processing, and the historical winners "won the Cold War" or "gained the upper hand" because the distributed architecture handled accelerating change better. He develops it through Hayek's price system, the Lysenko catastrophe, and the (apocryphal) Gorbachev/London-bread story. (`Directly stated`)
- [[Byung-Chul Han]] (cross-author resonance) — Harari's celebratory-neutral reading of "the invisible hand of the data flow" is the position Han contests: where Harari treats distributed data-processing as efficient and self-correcting, Han's [[Dataism]] reads the same regime as "data totalitarianism" and "nihilism." The architecture Harari praises for throughput, Han indicts for dissolving meaning into the additive. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Dataism]] — this tension is a self-contained corollary of the data religion: political and economic systems become "competing data-processing systems," evaluated by efficiency rather than ethics.
- [[Information]] — the distributed architecture wins by letting "as much information as possible flow as freely as possible"; the centralised architecture chokes on blocked or bottlenecked information.
- [[Acceleration]] — the decisive variable: distributed processing's advantage holds "in periods of accelerating technological changes," and democracy's future is jeopardised precisely when "both the volume and speed of data increase."
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the data-processing frame subordinates human choice to architectural efficiency; "we often imagine that democracy and the free market won because they were 'good'. In truth, they won because they improved the global data-processing system."
- [[Friedrich Hayek]] *(forward-stub)* — supplies the canonical statement of distributed processing: "prices can act to coordinate the separate actions of different people."
- [[Big Data]] / [[Digital Panopticon]] — the twenty-first-century conditions under which Harari predicts the distributed/centralised balance may tip again, possibly past both democracy and dictatorship toward "new and more efficient structures."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Harari does **not** hold the poles as a stable opposition; he relativises both. Distributed processing is not asserted to be permanently superior — its win is explicitly indexed to a historical condition (accelerating change), and Harari forecasts that the same data-processing yardstick may soon discard *both* democracy and dictatorship: "These new structures may be very different from any previous political institutions, whether democratic or authoritarian." The tension thus does not resolve into a winner so much as dissolve the question of winners: "For our current purposes, it doesn't matter whether the theory is correct. The crucial thing is that the theory understands economics in terms of data processing." What remains open — and is the productive residue for this vault — is whether reframing politics as architecture *gains* explanatory power or *loses* exactly the normative dimension (justice, liberty, meaning) that the human stakes of the Cold War were actually about. That is the seam where [[Byung-Chul Han|Han]] re-enters against Harari. (`Strongly implied` that Harari treats the tension as historically contingent rather than settled; `Interpretive synthesis` on the normative residue.)
