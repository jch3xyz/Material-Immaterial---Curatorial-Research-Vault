# Object vs System

## The Poles

- **Object** — sculpture in its traditional physical form: inert, palpable, finite in dimensions, place-bound, durable, and stationary. The object "occupies a specific space: it has place, remaining inert and stationary." It is the means by which environment "has been subdivided into transportable units," akin to real estate in being "real and lasting." Its raison d'etre is that it embodies formal qualities.
- **System** — "an interacting assembly of varying complexity," the means by which sculpture "gradually departs from its object state and assumes some measure of lifelike activity." A system is provisional, evanescent, and may manifest the fundamental characteristics of life: self-organization, growth, mobility, sensitivity, input and output, equilibrium, and eventual death. Its raison d'etre is that it "exists as a physical system including invisible forces."

## Why It Matters

Object vs System is the structural opposition that **titles the two parts of Burnham's book** — modern sculpture is divided "respectively into object and system." It is Burnham's claim that twentieth-century sculpture undergoes a one-directional migration from the durable, place-bound, formally-defined object toward the life-simulating, energy-controlled, ultimately cybernetic system (Kinetic Art, Light sculpture, Environmental Art, Cybernetic/Cyborg Art). The stakes are the survival and very definition of sculpture: as the object loses "its independent status in technological society," the system becomes "a logical extension of the object through intellectual domination of the environment," and "the word viewer seems quite antiquated" (`Directly stated`).

This is the **Burnham-specific framing of the canonical vault tension [[Material vs Immaterial]]** — the inert palpable object versus the immaterial, force-driven, invisible system — and it is cross-linked to that note, not merged with it; Burnham's poles are sculpture-specific (object/base/mass versus interacting assembly/energy/feedback) where the vault tension is general (`Interpretive synthesis`). It also gathers a **matter-versus-energy** pole: once sculpture is "completely liberated from its base it becomes a different animal! Its raison d'etre is no longer that it embodies formal qualities, but that it exists as a physical system including invisible forces. The duality between matter and energy enters a new phase" (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] — the two parts of the book are "object" and "system." The object is traditional palpable sculpture (Part One); the system is the interacting, life-simulating assembly toward which sculpture migrates via Kinetic, Light, Environmental, and Cybernetic Art (Part Two). [[Reification]] ("thingification") is the engine that first carries sculpture toward the object as bare material status, and then beyond the object toward the system that approximates biological activity.

## Related Concepts

- [[Reification]] — the constant "resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory"; its final trend is "the replacement of inanimate sculpture with life-simulating systems through the use of technology."
- [[Systems Theory]] — Burnham's system (the interacting assembly with the attributes of life) is the sculptural application of the broader systems thinking; the engineer "designs his systems in terms of a given life span."
- [[Organicism]] / [[Vitalism]] — the biotic aspirations the object pursued by metaphor and the system pursues literally; "the 'bringing to life' of wood and stone has always been the obsession of the sculptor," now realized "through the technician."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the canonical vault tension this note specifies (cross-linked, not merged).

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham does not hold the poles in balance; he stages an **irreversible directional transition**, not a stable dichotomy. "This book makes a case for at least the temporary survival of sculpture through transition from the object to the system." Yet he concedes the object's enduring advantage — it "has certain properties of endurance which the system does not yet, and perhaps never will, possess" — while the system trades stability for "flexibility of use, adaptability, and the capacity for measured reaction." The boundary itself is admitted to be unclear: "how do we define where the object leaves off and the system begins?" The tension thus resolves asymmetrically (toward the system) at the level of historical thesis, but remains genuinely open at the level of definition and of what is lost (`Strongly implied`).

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

Roy Ascott frames the same opposition from the **participatory / behavioural** side, and earlier than Burnham — his statements run from 1964 through 1988, where Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* is 1968. Ascott does not migrate the autonomous object toward a system; he relocates the system into the **feedback loop between artist, artwork, and participating spectator**. Already in 1964 he gives the tension its methodological form — "Everything changes ceaselessly; we investigate our world best by first seeing the system or process before evaluating the 'thing'" — and reads the modern century as a wholesale reorientation: "The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour, and its function has become less descriptive and more purposive" (1966–67). This is the [[New Media Art]] / [[Art as Experiential Technology]] lineage Burnham's notes were committed to receive, and it is the complementary pole to his [[Systems Esthetics]]: Burnham reads the object→system drift inside the artwork; Ascott reads it as a drift from a finished message to an open, spectator-completed event (`Directly stated`).

By 1975 Ascott makes the pole's "system" face explicitly relational and immaterial: art "clearly established in our present culture as a form of behaviour (rather than a simple array of images or objects), now takes on the quality of a transaction," and so "resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system." In 1988 the spine extends into the networked/telematic register — "Art does not reside in the object alone, nor is meaning fixed or stable within the physical limits of the artist's work. Art is all process, all system." Where Burnham's *system* is the life-simulating interacting assembly that succeeds the object, Ascott's *system* is the open, never-finished field of behaviours completed by participation; the two are the earlier-participatory and the object-historical poles of the same shift, cross-linked through [[Reciprocal Communication]] and the [[Process vs Product]] tension. See [[Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).
