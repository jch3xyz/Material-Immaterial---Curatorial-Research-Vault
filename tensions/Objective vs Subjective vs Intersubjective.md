# Objective vs Subjective vs Intersubjective

## The Poles

This is a three-pole ontology, not a binary. Harari's central move is to insist on a third option that the common-sense map of reality omits.

- **Objective** — reality that exists independently of any mind. "In objective reality, things exist independently of our beliefs and feelings. Gravity, for example, is an objective reality. It existed long before Newton, and it affects people who don't believe in it just as much as it affects those who do."
- **Subjective** — reality that exists only for the single person who feels it. It "depends on my personal beliefs and feelings" — the paradigm case is the headache that every instrument fails to detect: "every objective test has found nothing wrong with me, and even though nobody except me feels the pain, for me the pain is 100 per cent real."
- **Intersubjective** — reality that exists in the space *between* people, sustained by shared belief and communication. "Intersubjective entities depend on communication among many humans rather than on the beliefs and feelings of individual humans." Money, gods, nations, corporations and human rights are intersubjective: real and world-shaping, yet evaporating the moment collective belief withdraws.

## Why It Matters

The tension is generative because of a *category error* Harari diagnoses: "Most people presume that reality is either objective or subjective, and that there is no third option." When people verify that something is not merely their private feeling, they wrongly conclude it must be objective — and so "God, money and nations must therefore be objective realities." The missing intersubjective pole is exactly where the most powerful agents in history live. Getting the ontology right is what lets us see that money has "no objective value" yet runs the world, and that fictions — not genes or geography — explain why "North Korea and South Korea are so different from one another."

For a vault organised around the material/immaterial axis, the stakes are sharper still. The intersubjective is not the immaterial-as-spiritual; it is a *third* mode that is neither brute matter nor private soul, and Harari forecasts that it will overrun the objective: "As human fictions are translated into genetic and electronic codes, the intersubjective reality will swallow up the objective reality and biology will merge with history." (`Directly stated`)

The Sobek/antibiotics contrast (chapter 07) tests the boundary between the intersubjective and the objective. A god is purely intersubjective — efficacious only through collective belief and only on those who participate ("God helps those who help themselves"). An antibiotic is objective — "Antibiotics, unlike God, help even those who don't help themselves. They cure infections whether you believe in them or not." This is the cleanest operational criterion in the chapters for telling the poles apart: does belief alter the effect? (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The poles are not symmetrical and the tension is not meant to be dissolved into one another — that is precisely the error Harari is correcting. The objective and subjective are each *irreducible*: the headache is "100 per cent real" to its bearer and survives every objective test, while gravity is real whether or not anyone believes in it. The intersubjective cannot be collapsed into either; reducing it to the objective is the popular mistake, and reducing it to the merely subjective ("mere fictions") is the one "we don't want to accept... because these are the things that give meaning to our lives."

What Harari offers instead of synthesis is a *directional* prediction: the boundary between objective and intersubjective is becoming porous, with the intersubjective gaining the upper hand — "fiction might thereby become the most potent force on earth, surpassing even wayward asteroids and natural selection." So the tension is held open, but asymmetrically: three distinct ontological registers, with the human-made third pole increasingly able to reshape the first. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
