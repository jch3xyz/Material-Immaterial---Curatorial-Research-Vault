# Truth vs Cooperative Power

## The Poles

- **Truth (accurate world views)** — the standpoint of clear-sighted description: getting the world right. Its exemplars in Harari's account are the Greek historians Herodotus and Thucydides (and the Chinese historian Sima Qian), who explained events through "a plethora of political, social and economic factors" rather than divine reward and punishment, and — in the modern register — the scientist who studies "how the world functions." Accuracy is a real virtue here, but on its own it is politically weak: it does not, by itself, knit large numbers of strangers into a single cooperating body.
- **Cooperative Power (cooperation-enabling fictions)** — the standpoint of order and mobilization: getting people to act together. Its exemplar is the Bible, whose perception of history Harari calls "fundamentally flawed" yet which "managed to spread throughout the world" because it "provided a better basis for large-scale human cooperation." Fictions win not by being true but by enabling masses of people to cooperate. The price, as Harari notes, is that "the same fictions also determine the goals of our cooperation."

## Why It Matters

This tension is the hinge of Harari's theory of how history actually moves. If accuracy and cooperative power coincided, the story would be simple: the truest world view would also be the most powerful, and history would converge on truth. Harari's claim is that they come apart — that "the power of human cooperation networks rests on a delicate balance between truth and fiction" — and that when they collide it is often the *less* accurate but more *binding* story that prevails. The decisive image is the contest he stages between Greek historiography and biblical monotheism: "even though Herodotus and Thucydides understood reality much better than the authors of the Bible, when the two world views collided, the Bible won by a knockout." `Directly stated`

What is at stake for the vault is a deflation of a comforting assumption — that exposure of falsehood is self-correcting, that bad descriptions eventually lose to good ones. Harari denies the automatic mechanism: "Abraham Lincoln said you cannot deceive everybody all the time. Well, that's wishful thinking." A fiction that organizes cooperation can outlast its own refutation for millennia, because what sustains it is not its correspondence to the world but the cooperative apparatus it makes possible. This is why the tension is not merely epistemological (true vs. false) but a tension between two *kinds of value* — accuracy and power — that a society can optimize for, and rarely for both at once. `Directly stated`

Chapter 7 (*The Odd Couple*) sharpens the stakes into a structural diagnosis. There Harari extends the argument from religious myth to *both* of modernity's prestige institutions: "As individuals, scientists and priests may give immense importance to the truth; but as collective institutions, science and religion prefer order and power over truth. They can therefore make good bedfellows." The pole that opposes truth is therefore not superstition versus reason; it is the institutional preference for *order* (religion's specialty) and *power* (science's specialty) that both establishments share. Truth, on this account, is homeless within either — it belongs instead to the lonely "spiritual journey" that "can seldom remain within the confines of either religious or scientific establishments." `Directly stated`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Yuval Noah Harari]] in *Homo Deus* — stages the tension in two moves. In Ch. 6 (*The Storytellers*) the contest is between accurate history (Herodotus, Thucydides, Sima Qian; the modern scientist) and the cooperation-enabling but mistaken biblical world view, which "won by a knockout" because it "provided a better basis for large-scale human cooperation." In Ch. 7 (*The Odd Couple*) he generalizes: as *collective institutions* both science and religion "prefer order and power over truth," so the opposition is not science-vs-religion but truth-vs-the-order-and-power that the two institutions jointly pursue. The deal modern history actually strikes, he argues, is "between science and one particular religion — namely, humanism" (see [[Religion as Social Order]] and [[Humanism (Harari)]]).

## Related Concepts

- [[Intersubjective Reality]] — the ontological home of the cooperation-enabling fiction: money, gods, nations and corporations are "real" in their effects without being accurate, and Harari's "science only makes these myths stronger" move is the direct continuation of this tension into the modern era.
- [[Can It Suffer (The Reality Test)]] — Harari's portable criterion for cutting back through the tension toward the real: when a cooperative fiction's self-invented yardsticks (tax revenue, exam marks, divine commandments) obscure what counts, the suffering test relocates value in entities that "can suffer" rather than in the fiction that mobilizes them. The reality test is the ethical answer to the question this tension raises.
- [[Large-Scale Flexible Cooperation]] — the trait the fiction-pole exists to serve, and the reason the less-accurate story can defeat the more-accurate one; flexible mass cooperation, not accuracy, is what the winning myth purchases.
- [[Religion as Social Order]] — the Ch. 7 redefinition of religion ("anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures") that lets Harari fold science's institutional ally, humanism, into the same order-and-power logic.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Harari does not resolve the tension so much as relocate where its resolution could live. Within either great institution, he holds, it is *not* resolvable in favour of truth: "as collective institutions, science and religion prefer order and power over truth," so any establishment will, under pressure, trade accuracy for cohesion and capability. Nor does he counsel abandoning fiction — "Fiction isn't bad. It is vital," and "without commonly accepted stories... no complex human society can function." `Directly stated`

The unresolved core is therefore a *balance* rather than a victory of one pole: "the power of human cooperation networks rests on a delicate balance between truth and fiction. If you distort reality too much, it will weaken you... On the other hand, you cannot organise masses of people effectively without relying on some fictional myths." Too much truth and no one follows you; too much fiction and reality eventually breaks you. The pole that *can* hold both — the standpoint from which one keeps asking the true question without surrendering it to order or power — is, for Harari, the individual "uncompromising quest for truth," the spiritual journey that lies outside both establishments and is "always tragic, for it is a lonely path fit for individuals rather than for entire societies." The tension is thus productively held only at the scale of the person; at the scale of the institution it remains open, and tilts toward power. `Strongly implied` (the "balance" and "tragic lonely path" formulations are `Directly stated`; reading them as the tension's mode of non-resolution is interpretive synthesis across the two chapters).
