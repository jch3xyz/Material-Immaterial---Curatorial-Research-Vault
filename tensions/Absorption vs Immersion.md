# Absorption vs Immersion

Roy Ascott's polarity for the changed relation between the viewing subject and the image as photography crosses from a stable material medium into [[Post-Photographic Practice]]. It is the perceptual / behavioural counterpart of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] shift: classical photography asks the viewer to *absorb* a bounded image; the digital photograph, floating in [[Hypermedia]], asks the viewer to *immerse* and *navigate*.

## The Poles

- **Absorption** — taking in the content of a bounded, classical image. The viewer "wanted to absorb as much as we could of the beauty of the image, its content, its politics, its culture." Absorption is "compact, bounded, and therefore more intense"; its context is "empathy" and "the transfer of feeling." The image is a finished material object — the photograph "lingered between documentum and monumentum" — and the viewer's relation to it is introspective re-action to a frozen frame. `Directly stated`
- **Immersion** — sensory entry into, and conceptual navigation through, a layered, shifting data field. "The hypermedia systems in which photo data float call for sensory immersion and conceptual navigation on the part of the viewing subject": immersion *in* the images "brought up from the database into our realm of seeing," and navigation *through* "the layers of data, the multiplicity of (HyperCard) routes and (SuperCard) passages, within which the images are enfolded." The viewer is repositioned from absorber to "manipulator of that image, as the one in whose hands the destiny of the image may lie." `Directly stated`

## Why It Matters

Ascott uses the pair to mark a threshold, not merely a difference of degree: "For the viewer, it's the difference between absorption and immersion." Absorption belongs to "the routines of seeing" — a docility that "easily lulls us into a false sense of reality, a reality reaffirmed by its familiarity." Immersion is what happens when, in [[Post-Photographic Practice]], "the routine to which Feyerabend referred breaks down." The figure of the change is spatial: "The window onto a world of analogue actualities gives way to the doorway into a world of digital potentialities." `Directly stated`

What is at stake is the locus of authorship and the ontology of the image. Once the image floats in [[Hypermedia]] it becomes "endlessly manipulated, recombined with other elements, and transformed," so the new discourse "must put as much emphasis on the behaviour of the viewing subject, her inter-action with the apparatus and the image, as on her introspective re-action." Immersion is therefore the perceptual entry-point for Ascott's lifelong thesis that the participating spectator completes the work — the participatory pole the Burnham art cluster already frames as `[[Reciprocal Communication]]` and `[[Art as Experiential Technology]]`. `Strongly implied`

The polarity is also the experiential face of [[Material vs Immaterial]]: the contrast Ascott draws between "the material image on paper and the immaterial apparition at the computer interface" is, on the viewer's side, exactly the contrast between absorbing a bounded object and immersing in an immaterial, navigable field. `Interpretive synthesis`

They share the word "immersion" but name different things. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not resolve the pair into a hierarchy and explicitly refuses a clean opposition: "It's simultaneously a case of both 'both/and' as well as 'either/or.'" He warns that it would be "false to set up the different aspects of photography, digital or analogue, in complete and total opposition" — the analogue is absorbed *within* the digital discourse, not abolished. The tension is thus best held as a productive doubling: immersion does not destroy absorption so much as enclose it, with the decisive new term being not immersion alone but the viewer's resulting power of *transformation* — "the opportunity for its further transformation by the viewing subject." `Directly stated` / `Strongly implied`
