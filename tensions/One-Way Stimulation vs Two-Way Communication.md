# One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication

## The Poles

- **One-Way Stimulation** — the message runs from the artwork to the human observer and stops there. The work is an inert or programmed system that the receptive observer reads, but it cannot receive, register, or respond to anything the observer does. This is the condition of all prior art for Burnham — figure sculpture, motion pictures, symphonic music, the Calder mobile, and even contemporary [[Kinetic Art]].
- **Two-Way Communication** — a sustained, reciprocal exchange in which the artwork itself responds to the human party in some intelligent fashion. The reciprocated message becomes mutual, as in "the kind of interaction of two men talking." This is the still-distant goal Burnham assigns to [[Cyborg Art]], and it requires the work to possess feedback and "some degree of intelligence."

## Why It Matters

This is the defining cybernetic opposition of Burnham's chapter on robot and cyborg art, and it reframes the entire history of sculpture as a problem of communication rather than form. Borrowing Norbert Wiener's image of the music-box automata — figures that receive a message but cannot answer it — Burnham argues that the "inherent defect" of all sculpture and automata, "even today's Kinetic Art," is that "they do not respond to man in any intelligent fashion" `Directly stated`. Even the participatory and kinetic works that *seem* to "act" are, for Burnham, still one-sided: the apparent interaction is no more than "a ball of yarn when it is batted and chased by a kitten" `Directly stated`.

What is at stake is the criterion that separates Cyborg Art from everything before it. To cross from stimulation to communication, the artwork must stop being a "complex, self-stabilizing, goal-seeking system (man) and an inert object" relationship and instead become a second goal-seeking system in its own right — one equipped with [[Feedback Loops]] and intelligence so that it can engage in a series of interactions with a human observer `Directly stated`. The tension thus marks the threshold at which the art object would cease to be an object and become a [[Reciprocal Communication|reciprocating partner]]. See also [[Cybernetics]] and [[Homeostasis]] for the systems framework Burnham imports to draw this line.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham frames the tension as a developmental trajectory rather than a stable opposition: two-way communication is "very gradually becoming the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture," but it is also explicitly a "distant" goal not yet realized in the art of his moment `Directly stated`. The poles are therefore not held in balance so much as ordered in time — one-way stimulation is the entire past and present of art, two-way communication its projected future. The synthesis Burnham envisions is the cyborg artwork that fuses "the geometric propensities of machines with the qualities of living matter," but he is careful to note that present-day participatory and kinetic works do not yet "act" in any genuine sense; to say sculpture acts merely "sets the scene psychologically for the eventuality that it will" `Strongly implied`. The tension thus remains open, awaiting an art form that can actually answer back.
