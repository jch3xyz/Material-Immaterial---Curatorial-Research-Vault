# Home vs Exile

The recurring frame in which Shoshana Zuboff casts the book's deepest political stakes. "Home or exile?" is one of "the oldest political questions" — knowledge, authority, and power posed across an entire civilization — and the whole project of *[[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]]* turns on a single version of it: "Can the digital future be our home?"

## The Poles

- **Home** — sanctuary, mastery, voice, relationship; the *nostos*, "finding home," "among our most profound needs." Home is "where we know and where we are known, where we love and are beloved": "Home is mastery, voice, relationship, and sanctuary: part freedom, part flourishing… part refuge, part prospect." It is the orientation point "from which every species sets its bearings" — without it, "we are lost."
- **Exile** — dislocation, expulsion, the "world of 'no exit.'" Its emotional signature is *saudade*, the Portuguese word for "the homesickness and longing of separation from the homeland," now turned into "a universal story that engulfs each one of us." In Zuboff's later argument exile becomes paradoxical: surveillance capitalism "effectively exiles us from our own behavior," a banishment not *from* the network but *into* it.

## Why It Matters

The tension is the book's master frame, the lens through which Zuboff reads everything that follows. By naming surveillance capitalism's threat as one of *home* and *exile* rather than merely of *privacy* or *monopoly*, she reaches for "the oldest political questions" — "Home or exile? Lord or subject? Master or slave?" — and so refuses the "horseless-carriage" lenses that, she warns, render the unprecedented invisible (Directly stated, Ch.1). The stakes are not a transactable good but the very possibility of a habitable future: "Will this emerging civilization be a place that we can call home?"

The frame is what makes Zuboff's two countervailing rights legible. [[The Right to Sanctuary]] is *home* defended as inviolable refuge ("a sanctuary free from arbitrary intrusion: unplunderable"); its forfeit to a regime of "no exit" is a form of *exile*, an expulsion from the inward space "where a self can be birthed and nurtured." [[The Right to the Future Tense]] is *home* as prospect — the homecoming we set our bearings by — against the foreclosure of tomorrow by [[Behavioral Futures Markets|behavioral futures markets]] (Interpretive synthesis). To grasp surveillance capitalism, on this account, is to recognize that what is at risk is the *nostos* itself.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Shoshana Zuboff]] in [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]] — opens the book with the question "Home or exile in the digital future" (the title of Chapter 1) and the manager's 1981 question that she hears as one of "the oldest political questions." She defines home through the figure of the *nostos* (Odysseus, the green turtles retracing their journey to "the very patch of beach where they were born") and exile through *saudade*. In Chapter 2 the Montes family's bakery — Pasteleria La Dulce — instantiates the home pole as a stubborn, "immaterial" inheritance ("the future will need the past… The future is our home too"), staged against surveillance capitalism's claim to author the future as its own.

- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — home as prospect and homecoming; exile is the foreclosure of the future the Montes family fights to "claw back."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Zuboff leaves the tension deliberately open — "These are eternal themes… that can never be settled for all time. There is no end of history" — but she does not hold the poles symmetrically. The book is a sustained argument *for* home and *against* exile, closing on a conditional homecoming that is also a charge: "If the digital future is to be our home, then it is we who must make it so." Synthesis, on this account, is not a conceptual resolution but a political act — the "crucial friction" of collective action that would reclaim the future as a place to dwell. Until then the tension remains a live "battleground upon which the possibility of a human future at the new frontier of power will be contested" (Directly stated, Ch.1). The Aware-Home parable (the 2000 sanctuary-by-design dissolved into Google's Nest by 2018) shows the default drift toward exile; the Montes bakery shows the countervailing refusal — neither is fated, which is precisely why the question stays the oldest one.
