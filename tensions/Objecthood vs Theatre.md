# Objecthood vs Theatre

## The Poles

- **Objecthood (Fried)** — sculpture must hold itself apart from the beholder and from time, resisting theatricality, in order to survive as an independent art. As Burnham reports Fried's position, "sculpture must resist becoming theatre in order to remain an independent art." Theatre is the condition modernist sculpture must defeat.
- **Theatre (Burnham's inversion)** — the very theatricality Fried treats as a threat is, for Burnham, a symptom of transition: the "acknowledged theatricality of present modes of static sculpture are preparatory steps toward the acceptance of a systems perspective." What Fried diagnoses as a degeneration, Burnham reads as a forward sign that the object is dissolving into the system.

## Why It Matters

This tension is the hinge on which Burnham turns Michael Fried's argument inside out. Fried's "objecthood" names the endpoint of formalist sculpture — the work as an inanimate object — and warns that the slide toward theatre (the work's dependence on a beholder, a duration, a situation) would cost sculpture its autonomy as art. Burnham accepts the diagnosis but reverses the value: the theatrical, situational, time-bound character of recent static sculpture is not a fall but a threshold. It is the point at which the discrete art object begins to give way to the responsive, environmental, man-machine **system**.

Reading the same phenomenon two ways, the tension stages the larger movement of Burnham's book: from the contemplation of detached, materially self-present art objects toward an art of "men enmeshed with and within purposeful responsive systems" (line 342). It also exposes a methodological fault line — whether "theatre" is a category of artistic failure (Fried) or a leading indicator of a paradigm shift (Burnham). The stakes are which frame a critic uses to read the art of the late 1960s: the autonomy of the object, or the logic of the system.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Michael Fried]] (via Burnham) — formulates "objecthood" and the imperative that sculpture must resist theatre to remain art. Burnham cites Fried's Summer 1967 text (pp. 12–23, i.e. "Art and Objecthood") as the source of the term and the position. Fried is reported, not directly ingested; see [[Objecthood (Fried via Burnham)]].
- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] — accepts Fried's account of objecthood ("if we are to obtain aesthetic and spiritual insight from contemporary sculpture, it must be achieved within the context of objecthood") but inverts the verdict on theatre, reading theatricality as preparatory to the [[Systems Theory|systems]] perspective and to the eventual dropping of the term "sculpture" itself. ## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is not so much resolved as **reframed**: Burnham does not refute Fried's claim that theatre erodes the autonomy of the art object — he agrees the object is losing its independence. He simply denies that this is a loss to be resisted. For Fried, theatre marks the boundary art must not cross; for Burnham, crossing it is the historical task. The opposition therefore holds productively only as long as one keeps the autonomous object as the value; once Burnham shifts the criterion of value from the object to the system, the two poles cease to be alternatives and become successive stages of one transition. Whether that shift is itself warranted — whether "systems" really supersedes the object, or whether Fried's defense of objecthood retains force — remains open in the text. `Interpretive synthesis`
