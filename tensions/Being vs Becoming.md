# Being vs Becoming

> Disambiguation: this is Ascott's late-technoetic, *ontological* fork — an art predicated on the **assertion of being** (the fixed, present, finished object) versus an art that favours the **ontology of becoming** (process, flux, emergence). It is the value-laden ontology *behind* the Burnham-anchored [[Object vs System]] tension and the Ascott participation tensions [[Observer vs Participator]] and [[Appearance vs Apparition]]: where those name the *form* of the work, Being vs Becoming names the *mode of existence* the work is built to inhabit. It is distinct from [[Material vs Immaterial]] (which is about substance, not temporality).

## The Poles

- **Being** — an art that rests on "the assertion of being": the artwork as a stable, present, completed thing, "bracketed in space and time," self-identical and self-contained. In Ascott's polemic this is the gallery object viewed by a "second observer," the work whose meaning is fixed and delivered rather than generated. It is representational or narrative — it *depicts* or *recounts* a world that already is.
- **Becoming** — an art "that favours the ontology of becoming," "unstable, shifting, and in flux," that "parallels life, not through representation or narrative, but in its processes of emergence, uncertainty, and transformation." Its meaning is "created out of the viewer's interactions and negotiations on the Internet"; it moves "towards a post-biological re-materialization" and is "an art of enactment, without audience." It does not assert what is; it enacts what is coming into being.

## Why It Matters

The tension stages the **ontological stake of the technoetic aesthetic**. For Ascott the move from the object to the system, and from the spectator to the participant, is finally a move in *ontology*: from an art that asserts being to an art whose very mode of existence is becoming. This is why he frames the question as one of how art "parallels life" — not by mirroring its appearances (representation) or telling its stories (narrative), but by sharing its *processes*: self-organization, indeterminacy, transformation. An art of becoming cannot be a finished object delivered to a viewer, because becoming is by definition unfinished; it must be enacted, negotiated, and distributed within a network of participants.

For the vault, Being vs Becoming is the temporal-ontological hinge of the late-Ascott cluster. It supplies the philosophical ground for [[Object vs System]] (Burnham's object→system spine, now read as being→becoming), for [[Observer vs Participator]] and the "art without audience" thesis, and for the cross-links to [[Emergence]] (the self-assembling processes the art is to parallel) and [[Posthumanism]] / [[The Post-Biological Body]] (the "post-biological re-materialization" toward which the art of becoming moves). It is also the cleanest single statement of why Ascott treats art as a technology of consciousness in flux ([[Technoetics]]) rather than a producer of stable artefacts (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] — in the closing cascade of questions of "Weaving the Shamantic Web" (ch. 30, 1998), Ascott poses the fork directly: an art "that favours the ontology of becoming, rather than the assertion of being," that "parallels life . . . in its processes of emergence, uncertainty, and transformation." See `## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)`.

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

The tension appears in its sharpest form at the close of ch. 30, where Ascott — having paralleled digital and shamanic "technologies of consciousness" and prescribed an "art without audience in its inactive mode" — turns from prescription to a cascade of open questions about the artist's role in "an art that increasingly sees its content and meaning as created out of the viewer's interactions and negotiations on the Internet." Within that cascade the ontological fork is named explicitly: "**An art that favours the ontology of becoming, rather than the assertion of being?**" (`Directly stated`).

Each adjacent question fills in what *becoming* means for art. It is, first, an art whose being is **process**: "An art that is unstable, shifting, and in flux?" — and, crucially, an art that "parallels life, not through representation or narrative, but in its processes of emergence, uncertainty, and transformation" (`Directly stated`). This is the decisive clause: an art of becoming does not *depict* life (representation) or *narrate* it (narrative) — both of which presuppose a stable subject set over against a stable object, an art of *being* — but shares life's own *processes*. The link to [[Emergence]] is therefore not decorative but definitional: the art of becoming is one whose meaning, like a self-organizing system, arises bottom-up and is never finished. Ascott names the corresponding source elsewhere in the same close — the work is the "conservator of what emerges from the complexity of interactions in the Net or from the self-assembling processes of artificial life" (`Directly stated`).

Second, becoming for Ascott is **post-biological**: "An art moving towards a post-biological re-materialization?" (`Directly stated`). The art of becoming is not a flight from matter but a *re*-materialization on the far side of the biological — the data-body, the moist/dry artificial organism, the second body of light. This is why Being vs Becoming cross-links to [[Posthumanism]] and [[The Post-Biological Body]] without collapsing into [[Material vs Immaterial]]: the issue is not whether art is material or immaterial but whether its mode of existence is fixed *being* or ongoing *becoming-other*.

Third, becoming is **enactment without assertion**: "An art of enactment, without audience? An intimate art, the free-flowing outcome of interaction between participant viewers within networks of transformation?" (`Directly stated`). An art that asserts being can be delivered to an audience because it is already complete; an art of becoming must be *enacted* by participants because it comes into being only in and through their interaction. This is the ontological reading of the participation thesis: the removal of the "second observer" and the "phantom audience" follows from the shift from being to becoming, because an audience presupposes a finished object to behold. Hence the cross-link to [[Observer vs Participator]] and to the audience-less "enactment of mind" Ascott prescribes in the same chapter — and the deeper continuity with Burnham's [[Object vs System]], here recast in ontological register: the object is the locus of being, the system the locus of becoming (`Interpretive synthesis` for the Burnham bridge; the Ascott formulations are `Directly stated`).

The fork is **value-laden, not neutral**: Ascott does not hold the two poles in balance but plainly favours becoming. The grammar makes the preference unmistakable — "favours the ontology of becoming, *rather than* the assertion of being" — and the closing thesis seals it: "the technoetic principle will be at the centre of art as it develops, and consciousness in all its forms will be the field of its unfolding" (`Directly stated`). Art, for the late Ascott, is itself a thing in becoming, never an assertion of what already is.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not hold the tension open as a genuine fork; he **resolves it by election**, declaring for becoming. But the resolution is itself an unfinished one, posed as a question rather than a settlement: the chapter does not assert that art *is* an art of becoming but asks whether it *will be* — "These are the questions that will take us into the next century, and artists working at the furthest edge of the technoetic aesthetic are already beginning to ask them." The mode of the resolution thus performs its content: an art of becoming can only be *enacted into existence*, never asserted as accomplished, so the tension is "resolved" precisely by being kept in motion. Read against [[Object vs System]], Being vs Becoming is the ontological key that turns Burnham's mid-century object→system thesis into Ascott's late art-of-process; read against [[Material vs Immaterial]], it shows that the live question for Ascott is no longer substance but *temporality of existence* — fixed being versus ceaseless becoming-other. The vault therefore holds this as a tension **resolved in principle (Ascott elects becoming) yet performatively open** (becoming admits no final assertion) (`Interpretive synthesis`).
