# Material vs Immaterial

## General Orientation

Three authors meet on this axis from different disciplines, and the word "immaterial" carries a distinct sense in each. For **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** the immaterial pole is *information* — the Shannon-Wiener pattern imagined as substrate-independent, contested by [[The Materiality of Informatics]]. For **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** it is *transparency* — language and matter leveled into "smooth streams of capital, communication, and information." For **[[Lucy Lippard]]** it is *dematerialization* — the art-historical episode (1966–1972) in which "the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary," the art object itself becoming the disputed term. Read together, Lippard's dematerialization of the art object is to the gallery what Hayles's disembodiment is to information: a "becoming-immaterial" that, on inspection, never escapes its material and economic substrate.

The titular axis of this vault. It is the most general form of an opposition that recurs under more specific names throughout the collection — [[Embodiment vs Information]], [[Presence Absence vs Pattern Randomness]], body versus pattern, presence versus circulation. Where those tensions stage a particular contest, *material vs immaterial* names the underlying hierarchy itself: the cultural conviction, in [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s words, "that information is in some sense more essential, more important, and more fundamental than materiality." Both authors ingested here work to deny that hierarchy its self-evidence — Hayles by re-embodying information in [[The Materiality of Informatics]], Han by diagnosing the "smooth streams of capital, communication, and information" as a regime that levels matter, language, and the body into frictionless circulation.

## The Poles

- **Material** — the substrate, the body, instantiation, the physical. The pole of presence and resistance: the medium in which any information must be inscribed, the "resistant materiality" of the durable book and the living body, the embodied "lifeworld" that is "instantiated, local, and specific." It is finite, contextual, and — for Hayles — irreplaceable: it "can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated." For Han, it is what *resists*: distance, shame, the threshold, the veil, everything that refuses to be smoothed into circulation.

- **Immaterial** — information, pattern, the "smooth stream," disembodied circulation. The pole of the weightless and the placeless: information "conceptualized as an entity separate from the material forms in which it is thought to be embedded," imagined to "circulate unchanged among different material substrates." It is what an ideology of dematerialization presents as free of friction, body, and locale — Han's "positivized, operationalized language" that flows where "communication meant circulation without interference," Hayles's "Form of ones and zeros" that promises to leave "the body's superfluous flesh" behind.

## Why It Matters

This is the vault's organizing tension because almost every research focus here is a way of asking where the line between material and immaterial gets drawn, who draws it, and what the drawing costs. The user's foci converge on it directly:

- **[[The Body as Data]]** and **the extrapolated body** presuppose the hierarchy at its sharpest — the body recoded as a disposable instantiation of an immaterial pattern ("data made flesh"), flesh demoted to "the derivative manifestation" of an informational "origin."
- **[[Embodiment]]** and **posthumanism** are Hayles's counter-program: the benign posthuman is the one that recovers the material pole rather than the one freed from it, refusing to let "embodiment in a biological substrate" be treated "as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life."
- **Transparency** names Han's version of the immaterial regime: matters "prove transparent when they are smoothed out and leveled, when they do not resist being integrated into smooth streams of capital, communication, and information."

What is at stake is whether the "immaterial" is ever really immaterial. Both authors answer no — but the *way* the immaterial is materially produced, and what that production erases, is exactly the analysis the tension makes possible. To presuppose the hierarchy is to lose the object of inquiry; to excavate it is to recover "the flesh that continues to be erased."

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[N. Katherine Hayles]] in [[How We Became Posthuman]] — the **materiality of informatics**. Hayles's whole project contests "this materiality/information separation," insisting that "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium" and that the apparent disembodiment of information is a "powerful illusion" produced by, and dependent on, the very material infrastructures it appears to obscure. Disembodiment is not a fact to be assumed but an ideology to be excavated: "the body's dematerialization depends in complex and highly specific ways on the embodied circumstances that an ideology of dematerialization would obscure."

- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in [[The Transparency Society]] — **immaterial relations of smooth circulation**. Han reads the immaterial pole as the dream of transparency: a world in which capital, communication, and information flow without friction because everything resistant — negativity, the Other, the body's opacity, the veil — has been smoothed away. "A world consisting only of information, where communication meant circulation without interference, would amount to a machine." Information is for Han the dematerialization of language itself: "positivized, operationalized language," speaking that "turns into information" under the regime of Framing.

## Dematerialization in Conceptual Art (Lippard)

[[Lucy Lippard]]'s *[[Six Years]]* gives this axis its sharpest art-historical instance. "Conceptual art, for me," she writes, "means work in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized.'" Where Hayles tracks information's apparent flight from the body and Han tracks language's leveling into transparency, Lippard documents the art object's attempt to escape its own materiality — to migrate from gallery to mailed catalogue, from canvas to idea-chain, from saleable thing to "easily mailed work, catalogues and magazine pieces." The 1968 Lippard-Chandler thesis put it as a tendency: as "more and more" of the work shifts to the thinking process, "Such a trend appears to be provoking a profound dematerialization of art." This is the immaterial pole arriving in the studio — the object demoted, in [[Sol LeWitt]]'s phrasing she cites, to whatever the "machine that makes the art" leaves behind.

But *Six Years* is unusual among the vault's sources in that it documents its own rebuttal, and so stages **both poles** internally. The Art-Language group wrote to Lippard and Chandler directly contesting the metaphor: conceptual works "may not be an art-object as we know it in its traditional matter-state, but they are nevertheless matter in one of its forms, either solid-state, gas-state, liquid-state." A typewritten sheet is "just as much a solid-state object" as a Rubens. Douglas Huebler pressed the same point against the term: "There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality. To deny it is to descend to irony." Even Lippard conceded the inaccuracy, retaining "dematerialization" only "for lack of a better term." The material pole, in other words, is recovered from *within* the conceptual movement itself — the burden of physicality is never shed, only displaced (from object to paper, from paper to gesture, from gesture to documentation).

The deepest convergence with Hayles and Han is economic, and Lippard names it in the Postface as the dematerialization thesis's failure to escape its substrate. The hope had been that an art freed of object status "would therefore be forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status and market-orientation"; three years later "the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums." "Clearly," she concludes, "whatever minor revolutions in communication have been achieved by the process of dematerializing the object ... , art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries." This is the precise structural rhyme with Hayles's claim that "the body's dematerialization depends in complex and highly specific ways on the embodied circumstances that an ideology of dematerialization would obscure": in both cases the becoming-immaterial is real as a *strategy* and illusory as an *escape*, because the material-economic ground it tried to leave behind is exactly what reabsorbs it. Lippard's dematerialized object and Hayles's disembodied information are the same gesture in two registers — and both, on the book's own testimony, fail to clear their substrate. *(Cross-author parallel: `Speculative connection` / `Interpretive synthesis` — see Sources below; it does not displace the Hayles/Han framing above, but adds the art-object as a third instance of the axis.)*

- [[Virtuality]] — the cultural condition that dramatizes the hierarchy and, in its dominant reading, tilts it toward the immaterial; "the cultural perception that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns."
- [[Embodiment]] — the affirmed material term; instantiated, local, specific, irreplaceable.
- [[Information]] — the pole imagined as weightless and substrate-independent (Hayles), or as positivized, operationalized language (Han).
- [[The Body as Data]] — the fantasy that presupposes and reinforces the hierarchy: the body as a disposable instantiation of an immaterial code.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Neither author lets "immaterial" stand free. This is their deep convergence: for both, the immaterial is materially produced and conditioned, and the appearance of weightless circulation is something manufactured rather than given. Hayles refuses the information/matter split outright — "conceiving of information as a thing separate from the medium instantiating it is a prior imaginary act that constructs a holistic phenomenon as an information/matter duality" — and re-grounds the immaterial pole in the material it had appeared to escape: the posthuman subject "is constituted by the crossing of the materiality of informatics with the immateriality of information," not by the triumph of one over the other. Han, from the other disciplinary door, treats the immaterial regime of transparency as a *violence* done to matter, body, and language — a leveling that "flattens out the human being itself" — and insists that what resists circulation (distance, shame, the veil, the secret) cannot be integrated without remainder.

They differ in what they enlist *against* the immaterial. For **Hayles** the counterweight is **embodiment** — the instantiated, finite, irreplaceable material form, recovered through a positive analytic of bodies, interfaces, and media. For **Han** the counterweight is **negativity** — the gap, the threshold, the Other, the opacity of language ("all understanding is always at the same time a not-understanding") — a more dialectical, loss-oriented resistance that does not rest on the body as such. Hayles wants to keep the productive power of the pattern/information dialectic while denying it the right to erase matter; Han is closer to mourning, reading the smoothing of the material as a depletion that leaves the world "more shameless and more naked." The tension is therefore *held open as a hierarchy to be contested* rather than dissolved into a synthesis: both authors agree the immaterial is parasitic on the material, but they disagree on whether the proper response is to re-embody (Hayles) or to re-introduce the negative (Han).
