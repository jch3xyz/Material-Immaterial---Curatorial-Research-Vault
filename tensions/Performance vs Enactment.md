# Performance vs Enactment

## The Poles

- **Performance** — the work played to an audience, actual or implicit. A presentation bracketed in space and time, presupposing a "second observer" and a "phantom audience" who watch what is shown. The default condition of art in the museum/gallery, which re-objectifies even an interactive work into "pure object" by surrounding it with passive spectators.
- **Enactment** — the *pagé*'s ritual: performative but not a public performance, "never played to an audience, actual or implicit." A "spiritual enactment" that restructures psychic forces rather than displaying anything. Transposed into [[New Media Art]], it becomes the "enactment of mind" — an intimate interaction *within* the system that constitutes the work, "an art without audience in its inactive mode."

## Why It Matters

This opposition grounds the most radical move in [[Roy Ascott]]'s late, [[Technoetics|technoetic]] program: the polemic for **an art without audience**. Ascott argues that networked interactive art, by being staged in a gallery, "presupposes, in spite of itself, an audience of more or less passive observers" — and so is "incarcerated within the very status it despises, that of pure object." The remedy is not better presentation but a different ontology of the work: drawn from the shaman's ritual, where there is no spectator because there is no spectacle, only the structuring of psychic forces. By contrasting *performance* (which requires the gaze of a second observer) with *enactment* (which is complete in the participant's own interaction), Ascott calls for "the removal of the second observer and the phantom audience from the canon of telematic art."

What is at stake is whether interactive art can ever escape the spectator. If the work is a *performance*, the participant is finally just one more thing for someone else to look at, and the open-ended evolution of meaning collapses back into "the closure of an autonomous frame of consciousness." If the work is an *enactment*, the participant's involvement *is* the work, and the dichotomy between observer and participant dissolves into "a kind of widespread intimacy, closeness on the planetary scale." The tension thus carries Ascott's whole shift from the aesthetics of appearance to an aesthetics of consciousness, process, and [[Reciprocal Communication|reciprocal interaction]].

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] — the sole author of this tension. In ch.30 ("Weaving the Shamantic Web," 1998) he invokes the Amazonian *pagé*'s ritual "by way of contrast" to the gallery model, distinguishing "public performance" from "spiritual enactment" and prescribing the removal of the second observer and phantom audience. The ch.32 glossary ("Technoetic Aesthetics," 1996) compresses the same opposition into the lexicon entries "Enactment" and (by way of "Double consciousness" and "Interactivity") the surrounding vocabulary of audience-less, intimate, non-trivial interaction. `Directly stated`.

## Related Concepts

- [[New Media Art]] — the "enactment of mind" is Ascott's refinement of what interactive/networked art *is*.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — art as executed inner experience rather than displayed object.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the intimate interaction "within the system," without a second observer.
- [[The Shamantic Web]] — the bio-telematic practice in which the artist is re-cast as a *pagé*-like figure who "cares for" consciousness rather than performs for a crowd.
- [[Observer vs Participator]] — the closely allied dichotomy this tension seeks to dissolve; *enactment* is the limit-case where the observer is removed entirely.
- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — Ascott's behavioural/cybernetic precursor opposition; the phantom audience is the residue of one-way address.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — McLuhan's media-tactile version of the same axis Ascott pushes to its audience-less extreme.
- [[Object vs System]] — Burnham's keystone; the gallery work is re-objectified ("pure object") exactly where Ascott wants the participant-system to be the work.
- [[Process vs Product]] — enactment is process complete in itself; performance is process delivered as a product for viewing.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not hold the poles in balance — he resolves the tension by abolishing one side. The whole rhetorical force of the *enactment* concept is to **dissolve** *performance*, not to reconcile it: the prescription is to remove the second observer and the phantom audience outright. Yet the resolution is programmatic rather than achieved. Ascott concedes that interactive art, "by its structure, placement, and presentation," still summons the audience "in spite of itself"; the gallery context keeps reinstating the spectator that the theory disowns. So the tension is best read as **unresolved in practice and asymmetrically resolved in principle**: enactment is the ideal the work should approach, while performance is the institutional gravity it cannot yet fully escape. The shamanic analogy is offered "at a great distance from our current hypermediated culture" — a model to aspire to, not a description of where new-media art already stands. `Interpretive synthesis`.
