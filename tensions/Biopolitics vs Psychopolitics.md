# Biopolitics vs Psychopolitics

> [!note] Scope
> This is **Han's own periodizing opposition**, the architecture of the whole book — distinct from the Foucauldian forward-stub `Biopolitics`. Where the Foucault stub names disciplinary power's administration of the biological, Han uses "biopolitics" as the *prior* term in a historical mutation: from a power that disciplines the body to a power that exploits the psyche. Chapter 5 ("Foucault's Dilemma") is the sharpest statement of the transition; chapter 11 ("Big Data") is its digital realization.

## The Poles

- **Biopolitics** — the governmental technology of disciplinary society. It is a power over *life* that administers the body and the population: birth and death rates, levels of health, life expectancy. Its instrument is the census; its medium is statistics and demography; its figure is Foucault's "docile body," the body "calibrated to be a production-unit." It observes only from the outside (Bentham's panopticon is "bound to the optical medium") and so "does not focus on the psyche." Its animal, via Deleuze, is the mole; its regime "organizes itself as a 'body'." (`Directly stated`)
- **Psychopolitics** — the governmental technology of the neoliberal regime. It is a power over the *psyche*, which neoliberalism "has discovered... as a productive force." Its instrument is micro-targeting and prediction; its medium is `[[Big Data]]`, which can "peer into the human soul itself"; its figure is the self-optimizing `[[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]]` practising `[[Auto-Exploitation]]`. Its animal is the snake; the neoliberal regime "seems like a 'soul'." (`Directly stated`)

## Why It Matters

The two poles are not merely a contrast but a **historical mutation** internal to capitalism. Han's claim is that Foucault's apparatus — built on *population* and *biopolitics* — is structurally unable to describe a regime that exploits not the body but the mind, and that this blind spot is "Foucault's Dilemma": late Foucault turned to neoliberalism but "stuck to the concepts of population and biopolitics," failing "to make the turn to psychopolitics." (`Directly stated`)

What is at stake is the *site* of power. Under biopolitics, power "lay hold of individuals directly," disciplining bodies into machinic production. Under psychopolitics, "immaterial and non-physical forms of production... determine the course of capitalism," so power shifts to "optimizing psychic or mental processes" — "physical discipline has given way to mental optimization." Crucially, psychopolitics "does not lay hold of individuals directly" but "ensures that individuals act on themselves so that power relations are interiorized — and then interpreted as freedom." This is why the tension also undergirds Han's account of `[[Auto-Exploitation]]`: the achievement-subject exploits *itself*, "willingly — and even passionately." (`Directly stated`)

The opposition matters methodologically too. Han fixes the divide on a single technical hinge: **demography cannot reach the psyche, but Big Data can.** "Demography is not the same thing as psych-ography." Statistics yields a demographic picture of a population; Big Data yields a *psychogram*, "perhaps even the psychogram of the unconscious itself." This is what makes psychopolitics a genuinely new technology of power rather than an intensified biopolitics — and it is the precise point at which the `[[Digital Panopticon]]` exceeds Bentham's. (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in *Psychopolitics* — stages the opposition as the book's master periodization. Ch. 3 borrows Deleuze's mole/snake and body/soul to date the shift from `[[Society of Control|disciplinary to control society]]`; ch. 4 defines biopolitics as "the governmental technology of disciplinary power"; ch. 5 ("Foucault's Dilemma") argues neoliberalism "is not primarily concerned with 'the biological, the somatic, the corporal'... It has discovered the psyche as a productive force"; ch. 11 ("Big Data") shows micro-targeting as "data-driven psychopolitics," the census's biopolitical successor.
- The opposition is built *against* Foucault: see [[Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault]]. Han reads Foucault's own self-critical admission ("I really did intend to talk about biopolitics, and then... I have ended up talking at length... about neoliberalism") as a symptom of the dilemma, and rejects Giorgio Agamben's speculation that Foucault's early death merely interrupted the development of biopolitics.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Han presents the relation **not as a synthesis but as a supersession** — psychopolitics is "a further development — indeed, a mutated form" of the order biopolitics governed. He explicitly refuses the move (made by Alexandra Rau and Thomas Lemke) of treating psychopolitics as merely "a biopolitical form of government"; for Han the two are categorically distinct because their *objects* differ (body vs. psyche) and only one can be reached by Big Data. (`Directly stated`)

Yet the body does not simply vanish. In Han's account it is "released from the immediate process of production and turning into the object of optimization" — "orthopaedic intervention is yielding to aesthetic intervention," so "sexiness and fitness represent new economic resources." The body thus migrates from the *productive* register of biopolitics to the *optimizing* register of psychopolitics rather than disappearing. Whether this constitutes a clean break or a layering of the two regimes Han leaves as an open consequence of his periodization. (`Strongly implied`) The tension is therefore best held as a **directional, not symmetrical, opposition**: biopolitics is the past from which psychopolitics emerges, and the danger Han names — that psychopolitics "could overtake" free will, heralding "the end of freedom" — is precisely the danger that the older, body-bound critique can no longer see. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
