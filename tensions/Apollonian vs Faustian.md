# Apollonian vs Faustian

## The Poles

- **Apollonian** — the static sculpted image at rest. Burnham, invoking Spengler, casts sculpture as Apollonian: it remains in "quietude arid static order," it "plays with divine madness" but does so "coolly, with a sense of well-defined limit and a knowledge of probable success." Its being is eternal security; the marble god is "implacably serene," aloof, spectral, removed from confrontation. The static object carries the "aloofness and serenity" and "the quietude necessary for aesthetic contemplation."
- **Faustian** — the automaton in motion. Its core is "a Faustian drive to transcend natural limitations." Here "the soul of the artist is tied to mechanical fallibility and madness lies in the indefinite possibilities of technology." The machine "radiates an impertinence for everything that stands still about it" and "revels in drastic change." Its drive is the [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically|Faustian urge]] — to "re-create life nonbiologically."

## Why It Matters

This is the deep cultural axis Burnham places beneath the entire object-to-system turn of his book. Long before kinetic sculpture, cybernetic art, or the dissolution of the discrete object into process, the opposition between the still image and the moving one already encoded two metaphysics: the Apollonian acceptance of limit and the Faustian refusal of it. Reading the history of [[Automata|automata]] as a branch of [[Subsculpture|subsculpture]], Burnham argues that "the history of automata has always run close to that of technology," so that the static-vs-moving image becomes a register for the whole Western "progressive mechanization" — the place where "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge shows itself." Naming the axis this way lets the tension migrate forward: the Faustian pole is what later books in the vault call the drive to [[Transcending Biology|transcend biology]], the impertinence toward the still object resonates with [[Acceleration|acceleration]], and the nonbiological re-creation of life feeds the lineage toward [[The Cyborg|the cyborg]] and [[Posthumanism|the posthuman]]. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

What is at stake is also a verdict on cost. Burnham does not treat the Faustian pole as simple progress: the moving image is "doomed to failure," carries "the pathos of automata," and (citing Toynbee) raises the worry that "mechanics cannot help but erode the soul of art." The tension is therefore not a neutral pairing but a wager about whether transcending the limit forfeits the serenity the limit made possible. (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham presents the poles as historically intertwined rather than reconciled. He notes that the two image-making practices "play with divine madness" by different means, and that "the joint religious and magical functions which they both shared until modern times" once drew sculpture and automata "toward one another" — a fusion he locates as late as the eighteenth century, "a period just prior to the full effects of the technological onslaught," when "the arts freely admitted and encouraged scientific aid" and "no paralytic fear of creative impedimentation yet existed." (`Directly stated`)

But he leaves the tension open, and tilts it toward loss. The Faustian pole is shadowed by pathos and fallibility; sculpture's Apollonian serenity is precisely what the moving machine "would never tolerate." The unresolved question Burnham raises and does not answer — "suppose automata had been raised to the level of a fine art thousands of years ago, would we today have an aesthetic of moving statuary? Or would it have been an impossibility; for, as Arnold Toynbee maintains, mechanics cannot help but erode the soul of art?" — keeps the axis live rather than synthesized. The drift of the chapter, and of the book, is that the Faustian pole increasingly wins, carrying art out of the static object and into the system — but at the cost of the quietude the Apollonian object guaranteed. (`Strongly implied`)
