# Mechanism vs Vitalism

## The Poles

- **Mechanism** — the belief in a physical, usually electrochemical, basis for all phenomena of life, human consciousness included; the view that "life can be interpreted on all levels solely in terms of analyzable physical functions and combinations of matter." See [[Mechanism]].
- **Vitalism** — the doctrine that life is "metaphysically instigated," consisting of "an 'entelechy,' to use Aristotle's word, a nonmaterial center of being making it different in quality and in kind from inorganic matter." See [[Vitalism]].

## Why It Matters

For [[Jack Burnham]] in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (Ch. 2, "The Biotic Sources of Modern Sculpture"), this centuries-long dispute is the hinge between two histories that ran on different clocks. In biology it was *settled* — settled against vitalism. Burnham quotes Ludwig von Bertalanffy: "the history of biology is the refutation of vitalism." The vital essence has been "consistently undermined by biology," not by polemic but by "the weight of new discoveries"; with each solved problem the vitalist was "forced" to "redefine the 'life force' in terms of an ever-receding sphere of influence over the properties of living organisms" (`Directly stated`).

Yet the same losing doctrine became "the reigning aesthetic for many years in modern sculpture." This is the productive scandal Burnham's chapter is built to explain: "Why, if vitalism played such a reactionary role in the biological sciences, did it provide some of the most advanced justification for avant-garde abstract sculpture?" His answer turns on *what kind* of dispute this was outside the laboratory. As he puts it, "the 'vitalist versus mechanist' argument was essentially one, not of the right facts, but of the right sensibilities toward all that makes life bearable as a state of consciousness" (`Directly stated`). In biology it was a contest of facts, and mechanism won; in sculpture it was a contest of sensibilities, where a poetic, scientifically defeated idea could reign precisely because beauty, not proof, was the measure. Understanding this is what lets Burnham read an art history *through* a history of science — and warn that an aesthetic borrowed from the prestige of a scientific theory can survive only as long as that theory remains tenable.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] — narrates the dispute from its seventeenth-century beginnings (Descartes's "animal machine," de la Mettrie's *L'Homme Machine*) through Wöhler's synthesis of urea, Claude Bernard's regulatory systems, and Haeckel's "victorious materialism," to molecular biology; declares it settled in mechanism's favor in biology while it persisted in sculpture as a contest of sensibilities.
- [[Henri Bergson]] in *Creative Evolution* (1907; forward-stub) — Burnham's "high priest" of neo-vitalism, whose [[Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)|elan vital]] is the great poetic restatement of the vital principle; Burnham insists Bergson "must be studied as literature before considering [him] as philosophy."
- [[René Descartes]] (forward-stub) — the body as an "animal machine," the originating modern mechanist gesture that de la Mettrie later extended to thought and the soul.

## Related Concepts

- [[Mechanism]]
- [[Vitalism]]
- [[Organicism]] — Burnham's paired creed, held in *polar opposition* to vitalism but not identical to mechanism: vitalism is "a preeminently poetic view of life," organicism "a beautiful view of the utilitarian evolution of systems." Distinguished by the tension [[Vitalism vs Organicism]].
- [[Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)]] — the canonical neo-vitalist name for the "vital essence"; Burnham frames it as "the old prime mover," displaced at the end of the age by the Greek *kybernetes* (the steersman, root of [[Cybernetics]]) as "a new and even more effective prime mover."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the dispute is a primary-source instance of the vault's keystone tension: whether life is reducible to matter, or requires an immaterial principle invested in inert mass.
- [[Emergence]] — the contemporary scientific account (life arising from material complexity) that *displaced* vitalism in biology; kept distinct, not collapsed.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham presents the tension as *asymmetrically resolved*: dissolved on one side, durable on the other. In biology the synthesis is mechanism's — organic and inorganic matter form "a single continuum," and the vital essence has no remaining sphere of operation. But Burnham does not simply side with the victors. He grants that "essentially, both vitalists and mechanists have based belief in their respective causes on faith," and that vitalism survives wherever the question is one of sensibility rather than fact — pre-eminently in art. The tension therefore cannot be closed by evidence in the domain where it most matters to Burnham: it is *reframed*, not abolished. His larger thesis displaces it onto a third term — the convergence of biology, machine theory, and sculpture "toward a single end result — the understanding of living matter through its creation," in which the *kybernetes* of [[Cybernetics]] replaces both the *elan vital* and the old animal-machine as prime mover (`Strongly implied` / `Directly stated`).
