# Détournement vs Quotation

## The Poles

- **Détournement** — the flexible, self-validating language of anti-ideology. It reuses prior cultural and theoretical material by wrenching it into a present critique, and grounds itself on nothing outside that critique: it "cannot and need not be confirmed by any previous or supracritical reference" and "has grounded its cause on nothing but its own truth as present critique" (thesis 208).
- **Quotation** — the appeal to an external theoretical authority, "inevitably tainted by the very fact that it has become a quotation — a fragment torn from its own context and development" (thesis 208). Quotation defers to a source it enshrines; in doing so it severs the fragment from the historical framework that gave it meaning and turns living thought into a monument.

## Why It Matters

This is the opposition that organizes [[Guy Debord]]'s theory of critical *language* in Chapter 8 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]* — the formal counterpart to everything the book argues against the [[Spectacle]] at the level of society. The stakes are whether a genuinely critical statement can carry its own warrant, or whether it must borrow legitimacy from an authority outside itself.

For Debord the two poles are not merely different techniques but opposed *stances toward truth and history*. Quotation reproduces the very structure of [[Separation]] that the spectacle lives on: it tears a fragment from "the general framework of its period" and re-presents it as a self-standing, contemplated object of deference — the operation of the spectacle applied to thought. It is thus continuous with [[Recuperation]], the process by which critical material is captured, honored, and neutralized into "respectable truths and thus transformed into lies" (thesis 206). Détournement is the counter-operation: it "reradicalizes previous critical conclusions" that recuperation has petrified, returning reified thought to use. Understanding the tension is what lets one read *The Society of the Spectacle* as a *performance* of its own theory — a text stitched from détourned Hegel, Marx, Lukács and Lautréamont, whose style "includes a critique of itself" (thesis 206), rather than a treatise that quotes authorities. Missing the tension flattens Debord's method into ordinary citation and misses that, for him, *how* you handle prior thought is already a political act. `Interpretive synthesis` for the framing of quotation as continuous with separation and recuperation; `Directly stated` for the re-radicalization and self-validation claims (theses 206, 208).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Guy Debord]] in *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]* (1967), Chapter 8, theses 204–209 — the sole and originating locus. Debord states the opposition flatly in thesis 208 ("Détournement is the opposite of quotation") and builds it out of a lineage he explicitly détournes: Hegel's "reversing the genitive," "the young Marx, inspired by Feuerbach's systematic reversal of subject and predicate," who "answers 'the philosophy of poverty' with 'the poverty of philosophy'" (thesis 206), and Lautréamont's maxim that "plagiarism is necessary" (thesis 207). The distinction is inseparable from his parallel demand that critical theory "communicate itself in its own language, the language of contradiction" — "not a negation of style, but the style of negation" (thesis 204).

## Related Concepts

- [[Détournement]] — Pole A as a concept in its own right; the note that isolates the method Debord counterposes to quotation.
- [[Recuperation]] — the spectacle's capture of critique into "respectable truths"; quotation is the citational form this capture takes, and détournement is its inverse.
- [[Spectacle]] — quotation reproduces spectacular separation in the domain of language; détournement is the anti-ideology to the spectacle's materialized ideology.
- [[Separation]] — quotation is a mode of separation (a fragment torn from its context and set up as authority); détournement returns fragments to living use.
- [[Reification]] — a petrified, quotable "truth" is reified critical thought; détournement is the de-reifying counter-move.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Debord does not present the two poles as a dialectical pair awaiting synthesis; he presents them as *opposites* and comes down entirely on one side. Détournement is endorsed precisely because it refuses the resolution quotation offers — the comfort of an external, "supracritical" guarantee. The tension is therefore held open by design: détournement's whole claim is that it needs no settling reference, that "its own internal coherence and practical effectiveness are what validate the previous kernels of truth it has brought back into play" (thesis 208).

Yet the opposition contains a live instability the concept note on [[Détournement]] also raises. If détournement validates itself "on nothing but its own truth as present critique," what prevents that self-certainty from hardening into a new authority — from being, in turn, recuperated as a mere *style*, absorbed by the advertising and spectacle it opposes? And, sharply for this vault: is careful, verbatim, provenance-first citation itself a form of the "quotation" Debord refuses, or does context-preserving citation do the opposite of the decontextualizing "fragment torn from its own context" he attacks? The tension thus remains genuinely unresolved — not because Debord failed to choose a side, but because the winning pole's refusal of external warrant is exactly what leaves it exposed to becoming the thing it negates. `Interpretive synthesis`.
