# Art vs Technics

## The Poles

- **Art** — for Burnham, "art has always been a subtle balance between irrational and rational human characteristics." Art demands "a certain degree of psychic involvement, an autonomous capitulation to a not always rational visual order"; it lives by symbol, ambiguity, and irrationality. Its last inviolable region is madness — the Dada strain (Duchamp, Picabia, Klee, de Chirico) that mixes "extremes of deranged fantasy" with the materiality of the machine.
- **Technics** — the welded unity of science and technology Burnham names [[Technics]]: "self-aggrandizing," "irreversible," possessed of "the property of 'self-augmentation,'" proceeding by an "ultra-rationality" with "no psychic or ethical strictures." Its "compelling logic and almost authoritarian ethos" either undermines the psychic structure of irrationality or forces it into "stronger and more antithetical terms."

## Why It Matters

The tension is the hinge of Burnham's chapter on Kinetic Art, "The Unrequited Art." Kinetic sculpture is the artistic recognition of "the drive toward total mechanization," yet it is precisely there that art and technics prove "inherently at odds." If the machine is, in Reuleaux's phrase, "a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies," and if art requires "an autonomous capitulation to a not always rational visual order," then an art *made of* machines is close to a contradiction in terms — "like an 'art of advertising' or an 'art of scientific optics.'" `Strongly implied`

What is at stake is whether irrationality — long "a much more integrated and culturally compatible force in the lives of men" — can survive a culture given over to technics. Burnham's answer is grim: technics does not merely *stand opposed* to art's irrational pole; it actively erodes it, so that the balance art once held becomes unholdable. The sharpened, almost lethal form of this opposition is the argument [[Technics is self-augmenting and only madness escapes the machine]]: every art form "can be reduced to technique" except madness, leaving the artist a single "hiding place from technological proliferation." `Directly stated`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] (ch. 6, "Kineticism: The Unrequited Art") — frames the Kinetic revival as maintaining "internal contradictions" because it unites art "with forces inherently at odds with artistic endeavor"; relays Jacques Ellul's verdict that the artist's only refuge from technics is madness, and reads Dada as "a breath of sanity" against the perversity of applying "ultra-rational technology to areas which have always defied rational explanation."
- Jacques Ellul (via Burnham, *The Technological Society*) — the forward-stub author whose thesis Burnham applies: technical apparatus "replace[s] traditional symbolism in the human mind," and he "strongly doubts whether any art as we know it can stem from the ultra-rationality of technics." `Directly stated`

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham records a genuine three-way split rather than a resolution. One camp (Schaffer) "see[s] no incompatibility between art and advanced technology," holding "that the artist has always used what technical facilities were at hand." A second view — Burnham's own provisional sympathy — holds that "art has always been a subtle balance between irrational and rational human characteristics," so technics might shift rather than abolish the balance. But the chapter's tendency is toward the third, Ellulian pole: technics "either tends to undermine the psychic structure of irrationality, therefore destroying it, or forces it into a position where it must manifest itself in stronger and more antithetical terms." `Directly stated`

On that reading the tension cannot be synthesized so much as survived: art's only durable foothold is the one faculty technics cannot reduce to method — madness. The synthesis Kinetic artists attempt (the "joyous," "free" machine; the machine "tamed or humanized") is, in Burnham's diagnosis, a perpetual diversion of "the essential restrictive nature of the machine," never a stable reconciliation. The tension stays open, and "The Unrequited Art" is its name. `Interpretive synthesis`
