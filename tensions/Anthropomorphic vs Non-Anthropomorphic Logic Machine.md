# Anthropomorphic vs Non-Anthropomorphic Logic Machine

## The Poles

- **Anthropomorphic (the amusing android)** — the maker copies the *outward form* of a human or animal. This is for Burnham "the traditional objective of constructing amusing facsimiles of men and animals" — the eighteenth-century flute-player and digesting duck of Vaucanson, the nineteenth-century chess players and conjurers, and finally the clanking metal robot. Its ambition is mimetic surface: skin, hair, clothing, "natural movements," the illusion that the machine is a man. The branch wanes; the public learns that "machines normal; they should look like / were machines."
- **Non-Anthropomorphic Logic Machine** — the maker copies not the body but the *behavior*: thought, problem-solving, the logic of a system, with no attempt to preserve human appearance. This is "the designing of logic systems and programming techniques for the solution of problems put into mathematical form." Its lineage runs from Pascal's and Leibniz's calculators (the first machines to perform "the human act of abstract problem solving" while doing so "without looking human") through the postwar cybernetic "pseudo-organisms," "power amplifiers," and "adaptive, complex, role-playing automata" that "will be regarded as machines, first and foremost, with no attempt to preserve anthropomorphic appearances." This branch becomes "progressively more important for modern automata."

## Why It Matters

This is the bifurcation Burnham plants at the historical center of his automata chapter and then defers — "The implications for both will be set forth in the last chapter of this book" (the Cybernetic/Cyborg Art chapter). It is therefore the **structural seed of computational and new-media art** inside a book ostensibly about sculpture: the moment the figural image of the machine (the body it wears) splits off from the *logic* of the machine (the thought it performs), and Burnham's whole "object to system" thesis tracks the second branch winning. (`Directly stated` that the lineage splits in two branches and that the consequences are reserved for the final chapter; `Interpretive synthesis` that this bifurcation is the seed of [[Systems Esthetics]] and computational/new-media practice.)

What is at stake is *where the lifelikeness of the artifact resides*. The anthropomorphic android locates it in resemblance — it "fails to match the qualities of higher living organisms" because it can only imitate a surface. The non-anthropomorphic logic machine relocates lifelikeness into *function and feedback*: Pascal's calculator simulates "the logic of the Arabic number system," Leibniz becomes "the spiritual father of modern cybernetics," and the postwar "pseudo-organisms" are "working models, generally analogues of various aspects of the nervous system." Once the goal is to model behavior rather than copy form, the human shell becomes not just unnecessary but a hindrance — a piece of obsolete styling, like "steamboats with sails, and trains that looked like stagecoaches." Understanding this split is what lets Burnham move sculpture "beyond" the body altogether; missing it makes the cybernetic art of his final chapter look like a curiosity rather than the destination of a 10,000-year genealogy.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] — Burnham dates the bifurcation to "the work of Pascal and Leibniz," after which "the problem of designing automata… has been divided into two branches." He names Pascal's calculating machine the possible "first nonanthropomorphic automata" because "it performed the human act of abstract problem solving… without looking human." He contrasts the anthropomorphic android — which "maintain[s] anthropomorphic illusionism at all costs" — with the postwar [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] "electronic pseudo-organisms quite unlike robots in appearance" (Grey Walter's tortoises, Ross Ashby's homeostat, Claude Shannon's maze-running mice) and the "power amplifiers" that "look nothing like human beings but enable a man to perform the work of ten or twenty men." The fate of the two branches is reserved for the book's last chapter.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham does not hold the poles in balance — like [[Object vs System]], this is an **irreversible directional transition**, not a stable dichotomy. The anthropomorphic android is staged as a doomed, "pathetic," debased lineage ("doomed to failure… by a final sense of inconviction"), while the non-anthropomorphic logic machine is "progressively more important." Yet the resolution is asymmetric rather than clean: the two branches do not merge so much as the first is *abandoned* as obsolete styling once the public accepts that "machines… should look like… machines." The tension is genuinely *opened* here and explicitly left open — Burnham defers its consequences to the final chapter — so within this chapter it functions as a seed, not a resolution (`Strongly implied`).
