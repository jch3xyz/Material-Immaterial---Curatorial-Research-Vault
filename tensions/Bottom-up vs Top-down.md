# Bottom-up vs Top-down

## The Poles

- **Bottom-up** — creation, organization, and meaning that arise from a multiplicity of local interactions, without central design: the emergent, cultivated, self-organizing *organism*. Ascott takes the term directly from the Artificial Life program — Chris Langton's "bottom-up, distributed, local determination of behavior" — and transfers it wholesale onto networked art and onto pedagogy. Such a process is "planted," "grown," "fed," and "nourished," never engineered.
- **Top-down** — order that descends from a hierarchical center: the planned and implemented institution, the "classical top-down imposition of ideology or aesthetic." This is the atelier, the canvas-on-its-easel, the "tarnished ivory tower," the academy organized around the material object — the forms Ascott declares to be in "terminal decline."

## Why It Matters

This is the structural axis of Ascott's late program — of both the [[The Planetary Collegium|Planetary Collegium]] and of telematic art generally. The opposition is what lets Ascott argue that art education "has to be reconstructed from the bottom up": if art has become connective, interactive, and emergent (the five defining features — connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, [[Emergence|emergence]]), then a top-down, object-centred pedagogy is not merely outdated but categorically mismatched to the thing it claims to teach. `Directly stated`.

The axis also names *how the new institution comes into being*. The Planetary Collegium is not founded but cultivated: it "cannot be planned and implemented top-down" and is instead "already emerging, bottom-up from the infinity of interactions within the Internet." `Directly stated`. The opposition thereby doubles as a theory of cultural change — chaotic, non-linear, where tiny differences in initial conditions yield massive later divergence — and as an ethics of stewardship (feed it, support it, nourish it) rather than control.

Underwriting all of this is [[Artificial Life]]. Ascott borrows Langton's definition that AL "views life as a property of the organization of matter, rather than a property of the matter which is so organised," and reads bottom-up emergence as the generative metaphor for a non-material, behavioural art. `Strongly implied` that the bottom-up/top-down axis is, for Ascott, the same opposition as life-as-organization versus matter-as-substrate — which is why it sits alongside the [[Material vs Immaterial]] spine and the participatory pole of [[Object vs System]].

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] ("The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-Biological Era," 1994) — the locus classicus. Ascott imports the bottom-up principle from Langton's Artificial Life and opposes it to the "classical top-down imposition of ideology or aesthetic," making it the structural axis of telematic art and of the emergent Planetary Collegium.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

For Ascott the tension is not held in balance but **resolved by displacement**: the bottom-up process is presented as already superseding the top-down one ("the bottom-up process of creation replacing the classical top-down imposition"), so the work is to *cultivate* the winning pole rather than reconcile the two. `Directly stated`.

Yet the chapter leaves an unresolved residue. The same emergent, market-like infinity of interactions Ascott celebrates is also what he fears will be captured top-down by "the politicians, merchants, and entrepreneurs" if artists do not act first. The bottom-up organism thus has no built-in immunity to top-down enclosure; cultivation is also a race. `Interpretive synthesis` that the tension remains genuinely open at the level of power and institution even as Ascott treats it as settled at the level of aesthetics.
