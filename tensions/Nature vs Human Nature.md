# Nature vs Human Nature

## The Poles

- **Domination of nature (industrial civilization)** — Industrial capitalism took the natural world as its object of exploitation and control: raw material to be extracted, fashioned, and consumed. Its machines were built to overcome the limits of human bodies. Its catastrophic by-product is the *sixth extinction*, the disappearance of vertebrate species — an "unintended consequence" of market forms that "did not hold it to account."
- **Domination of human nature (information civilization)** — Surveillance capitalism takes *human nature* as its object: behavior rendered as raw material, packaged as a commodity, and modified at scale. Its machines no longer overcome bodies; they *modify behavior*. Its threatened by-product is the *seventh extinction* — not of nature, but of "what has been held most precious in human nature: the will to will, the sanctity of the individual, the ties of intimacy."

## Why It Matters

This tension is the historical hinge of Zuboff's argument: surveillance capitalism is not a continuation of the industrial project but a *repetition of its arrogance against a new object*. Industrial civilization conquered nature; information civilization sets out to conquer human nature. Naming the difference matters because it relocates the stakes — from the external, physical world to human inwardness, autonomy, and the first-person voice (`Directly stated`).

Holding the two poles together exposes a structural parallel that the rhetoric of "personalization" and "connection" conceals: both arcs treat their object as inert material exalted as inevitable, and both produce extinction-scale harm as an unintended consequence. The shift from *machines that overcome bodies* to *machines that modify behavior* is what makes [[Instrumentarian Power]] categorically new — it is power aimed not at the body (as in discipline or biopolitics) but at behavior itself, "more like a taming" (`Directly stated`).

The asymmetry between the two extinctions also carries Zuboff's wager. In the conquest of nature, "industrial capitalism's victims were mute"; those who would conquer human nature "will find their intended victims full of voice, ready to name danger and defeat it" (`Directly stated`). The book itself is offered as a contribution to that voice.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is staged as an unresolved historical danger rather than a paradox to be dissolved. Zuboff's framing implies a continuity (the "same dangerous arrogance," now turned inward) and a discontinuity (a different object, a different extinction). The two poles are not reconciled but ranked: domination of human nature is the more intimate and, for Zuboff, more answerable conquest — answerable precisely because its victims, unlike mute nature, can speak. The synthesis she gestures toward is therefore *political* rather than conceptual: the seventh extinction "will be just as unintended as any other" only if it is allowed to proceed unnamed. Whether the parallel to the sixth extinction is structural law or rhetorical warning is left open (`Interpretive synthesis`).
