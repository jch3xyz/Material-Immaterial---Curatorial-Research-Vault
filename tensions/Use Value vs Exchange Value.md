# Use Value vs Exchange Value

## The Poles

- **Use Value** — the concrete usefulness of a thing, its capacity to satisfy an actual human need; in Debord's account, an original ground that exchange value was once merely meant to *represent*, and which the commodity economy has since hollowed out until use survives only as a proclaimed alibi.
- **Exchange Value** — the abstract, quantitative equivalence a commodity commands on the market; for Debord it begins "like a condottiere in the service of use value" but wins autonomous power and ends up "waging the war for its own sake," subordinating use entirely to itself.

## Why It Matters

This is the Marxian pair, but Debord narrates it not as a static structural distinction so much as a **conquest**: a historical drama in which one pole overthrows the other. The stakes are the reality of experience itself. As long as exchange value merely *represented* use, the commodity remained "an implicit aspect" of a real need (th.48). Once exchange value "mobiliz[es] all human use value and monopoliz[es] its fulfillment" (th.46), use is no longer the master exchange serves but a hostage "completely at its mercy." The consequence is a felt impoverishment inside abundance: "The constant decline of use value that has always characterized the capitalist economy has given rise to a new form of poverty within the realm of augmented survival" (th.47). *Directly stated.*

The tension folds a second axis Debord makes explicit — **quality versus quantity**. "The commodity form reduces everything to quantitative equivalence. The quantitative is what it develops, and it can develop only within the quantitative" (th.38). Use value is the register of quality (the particular, the incomparable, the actually-needed); exchange value is the register of quantity (equivalence, the market's single measure). The loss of use value is therefore also a "loss of quality that is so evident at every level of spectacular language." *Directly stated.*

What makes the tension acute in Debord rather than merely inherited from Marx is its terminal twist: use value does not simply disappear, it is *retained as a lie*. "Within the upside-down world of the spectacle, use value must be explicitly proclaimed... because it serves as a necessary pseudo-justification for a counterfeit life" (th.48). The [[Spectacle]] is then diagnosed as the general form of this counterfeit: "The spectacle is not just a servant of pseudo-use, it is already in itself a pseudo-use of life" (th.49). Understanding the tension this way explains why the spectacle is not a distortion added on top of the economy but the economy's own completed self-image — "money one can only look at" (th.49). *Strongly implied.*

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Karl Marx]] — the source of the use-value / exchange-value distinction that Debord inherits and radicalizes. Debord treats it as given (the chapter opens under an epigraph from Lukács on [[Reification]] and the "fetishism of the commodity," th.36) and does not re-derive it; his contribution is to historicize the pair as a war exchange value wins. *Interpretive synthesis.* (Marx is a forward-stub author in the vault — cited across multiple ingests, not yet ingested from a primary source.)
- [[Guy Debord]] in [[The Society of the Spectacle]] (Chapter 2, "The Commodity as Spectacle," theses 46–49) — narrates the pair as a conquest in which exchange value, "starting out like a condottiere in the service of use value," ends "waging the war for its own sake" (th.46), leaving use value to be "explicitly proclaimed... as a necessary pseudo-justification for a counterfeit life" (th.48). *Directly stated.*

## Related Concepts

- [[Commodity as Spectacle]] — the chapter's governing thesis; the completed dominion of exchange value *is* the commodity's colonization of "all living experience."
- [[Spectacle]] — named as "already in itself a pseudo-use of life" (th.49); the general form the victory of exchange value takes.
- [[Pseudo-needs]] — the demand side of the same overthrow: with use value hollowed, "the satisfaction of primary human needs" is replaced "with an incessant fabrication of pseudo-needs" (th.51).
- [[Reification]] — the Lukácsian frame of the chapter's epigraph; the congealing of living activity into thing-like, quantitative form that the triumph of exchange value carries out.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Debord does not offer a stable synthesis — the tension is staged precisely as a defeat, not a balance. Exchange value has already won; use value survives only as proclaimed alibi. Yet the chapter is not fatalist: it closes by pointing past the antinomy rather than reconciling it. The economy's "triumph as an independent power at the same time spells its own doom" (th.51), and the counter-project — "consciousness of desire and desire for consciousness" seeking "the workers' direct possession of every aspect of their activity" (th.53) — would dissolve the tension by abolishing the commodity regime that produced it, not by re-balancing the two poles within it. The tension is thus **held as historically real but not permanent**: resolvable only by the supersession of the spectacle, otherwise open. *Strongly implied.*
