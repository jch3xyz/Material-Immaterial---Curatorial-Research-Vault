# Councils vs Party

## The Poles

- **[[Workers' Councils]]** — proletarian self-organization as its own political *form*. Debord names it as "the long-sought political form through which the working class could carry out its own economic liberation," realized "in the form of revolutionary workers councils that assume all decision-making and exec utive powers and that federate with each other by means of delegates who are answerable to their base and revocable at any moment" (th.116). The council is defined by three refusals folded into its structure — no permanent hierarchy, no specialization, no separation of the deciders from the decided-for — so that it becomes "the terrain where active direct communication is realized, marking the end of specialization, hierarchy and separation" (th.116). It does not *represent* the class; it *is* the class organizing itself, "the pro letarian movement becomes its own product… nothing other than the producers themselves" (th.117). `Directly stated`.
- **The Vanguard/Hierarchical Party** — professional revolutionaries directing the class *from outside*, ideological authority substituting for self-activity, and separation reproduced internally until it hardens into a bureaucratic ruling class. Its exemplar is Bolshevism: "The Bolshevik practice of directing the proletariat from outside, by means of a disciplined under ground party under the control of intellectuals who had become 'professional revolutionaries,'" which "became a new pro fession" and "ended up practicing the profession of totalitarian social domination" (th.98). By "seizing a state monopoly as sole representative and defender of working-class power," this form makes the party into "the party of the owners of the proletariat" (th.103) — a separate power standing over the class in the class's own name. `Directly stated`.

The axis between them is a single sentence Debord sets at "the heart of the modern spectacle's domination": "the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class" (th.100). The council is the self-organization that dissolves separation; the party is the representation that reinstalls it.

## Why It Matters

This is the organizational tension around which the whole of Chapter 4 turns, and it is the one place where Debord's diagnosis of the [[Spectacle]] issues in a positive political prescription rather than a critique. Everything the book says about [[Separation]] — that it is "the alpha and omega of the spectacle," that the spectacle is the [[Reification|reified]] social relation in which people are dispossessed of their own activity and confront it as an alien power — is here posed as a question of *revolutionary form*. If the spectacle is separation perfected, then a revolution that reproduces separation inside its own organization does not negate the spectacle; it renationalizes it. Debord's wager is that the council is the only form whose *structure* enacts the negation of separation, and the party the form whose structure — hierarchy, specialization, [[Representation|representation]] — smuggles the enemy back in. `Interpretive synthesis` grounded in th.98–121.

What is at stake is therefore not efficiency or tactics but whether the proletariat can be a *subject* at all, or only ever a represented object. Debord historicizes this as a wrong turn taken at the movement's origin: "the organizational question became the weakest aspect of radical theory, a confused terrain lending itself to the revival of hierarchical and statist tactics borrowed from the bourgeois revolution" (th.97). The Bolshevik answer to that weakness — the disciplined party of intellectuals — did not correct the flaw but consummated it, producing "a revolutionary bureaucracy that placed itself at the head of the prole tariat, seized state power, and proceeded to impose a new form of class domination" (th.104). The party, "modeled on the hierarchical separations of bourgeois organizations" (th.104), becomes "a substitute ruling class for the commodity economy" (th.104): separation reproduced internally hardens into a class. `Directly stated`.

This tension must be kept **distinct** from the other control-oppositions in the vault, which it superficially resembles:

- It is not the Deleuzean [[Discipline vs Control]]: that pair contrasts two *historical modes of domination* (enclosure vs. modulation), whereas Councils vs Party contrasts two *proletarian revolutionary forms* — self-organization vs. representation — as answers to how domination is to be abolished.
- It is not the Meadows [[Central Control vs Autonomy]]: that is a systems-design trade-off about where regulatory capacity should sit in a resilient system, a question of governance efficiency. Debord's opposition is not a dial between centralization and local autonomy but a categorical refusal of the very *separation* that "central control" presupposes.
- It is not the broad [[Freedom vs Control]]: Councils vs Party is not liberty against constraint. The council is not "freedom from" organization — it is a *maximally* organized form ("assume all decision-making and exec utive powers," th.116). The distinction is between two *organizations*, one of which dissolves separation and one of which perpetuates it, not between organization and its absence.

The specificity is the self-organization-vs-representation opposition of proletarian revolutionary *form*.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Guy Debord]] in [[The Society of the Spectacle]] — Chapter 4 (theses 90–121) stages the tension as the strategic core of the book. Debord traces the party pole through the defeat of the workers' movement — social democracy that "crushed the Spartakist revolutionaries," Bolshevism "directing the proletariat from outside" (th.98), [[Bolshevism directs the proletariat from outside and becomes totalitarian domination|the party as "the owners of the proletariat"]] (th.103), and Leninism as "the highest voluntaristic expression of revolutionary ideology — a coherence of the separate governing a reality that resisted it" (th.112). Against it he sets the council pole (theses 116–121): [[Workers councils are the only undefeated form and negate the spectacle|the councils as "the only undefeated aspect of a defeated movement"]] (th.118). The pivot between the two is [[The representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class|"the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class"]] (th.100). The council is where [[The proletariat can only take power by becoming the class of consciousness|the proletariat becomes the class of consciousness]] rather than the object of a representation.

## Related Concepts

- [[Workers' Councils]] — Pole A; the self-organized, federated, revocable-delegate form that "assume[s] all decision-making and exec utive powers" (th.116).
- [[Representation]] — Pole B's mechanism and Debord's name for the fatal category: the party as the *representation* of the class that turns against it (th.100).
- [[Separation]] — the stake: the council "mark[s] the end of… separation" (th.116); the party "reproduce[s]" the "conditions of separation and hierarchy… within itself" (th.121) unless it refuses to.
- [[Spectacle]] — the wider regime of separation the council's negation is aimed at: "Only in this way can the spectacle's negation of life be negated in its turn" (th.117).
- [[Reification]] — the party bureaucracy as the reified, alienated power of the class standing over it — "its own representation which had become externalized and alienated in the form of a working-class party" (th.104).
- [[Unity vs Separation]] — the deeper dialectic the tension serves: the council as unity-in-activity against the party as division installed as unity.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Debord does not hold the two poles as symmetrical alternatives to be balanced; he **decides** the tension in favor of the councils — but with a crucial qualification that keeps the party pole from returning through the back door. A revolutionary organization *before* the councils exist is admitted as necessary, yet forbidden the party's defining gesture: "it cannot claim to represent the working class. Its task, rather, is to embody a radical separation from the world of separation" (th.119). The organization is legitimate only as a critique, never as a representation, and it carries a self-abolishing clause: "At the revolutionary moment when social separations are dissolved, the organization must dissolve itself as a separate organization" (th.120). It must "see to it that the dominant society's conditions of separation and hierarchy are not reproduced within itself" (th.121).

So the "synthesis" is not a middle term between council and party but the **subordination of any transitional organization to the council-form and its eventual self-dissolution** — the party pole is admitted only in a version stripped of representation, hierarchy, permanence, and separation, i.e. only in a version that has ceased to be the vanguard party. Whether such a non-representational, self-abolishing organization is historically achievable Debord leaves open: the councils "have as yet provided no more than a rough hint of their possibilities" (th.116), having been "immediately… opposed and defeated" (th.116), and, citing Pannekoek, he grants that opting for council power "'poses problems' rather than providing a solution" — "But it is precisely within this form of social organization that the problems of prole tarian revolution can find their real solution" (th.116). The tension is thus *resolved in principle* (against the party, for the councils) and *left unresolved in practice* (the councils remain "the only undefeated aspect of a defeated movement," th.118 — undefeated because never yet victorious). `Strongly implied` as a reading of theses 116–121.
