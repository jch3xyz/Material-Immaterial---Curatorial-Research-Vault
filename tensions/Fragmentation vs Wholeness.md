# Fragmentation vs Wholeness

## General Orientation

This is the perceptual-cultural pole of [[Marshall McLuhan]]'s apparatus in *[[Understanding Media]]* — the axis along which his historical argument actually moves. Where [[Mechanical vs Electric]] names the two *technological eras* and [[Detachment vs Participation]] names the two *postures of the self*, *fragmentation vs wholeness* names the two *modes of perception and social organization* those eras produce: on one side the literate, print, mechanical self — specialized, detached, sequential, lineal, organized by "the mere viewpoint"; on the other the electric self — integral, participatory, mythic, organized by "the inclusive image" or "total field," aspiring (in the Introduction's keynote) to "wholeness, empathy and depth of awareness." The three tensions are facets of one diagnosis and should be read together; this note carries the perceptual facet, and cross-links to [[Mechanical vs Electric]] for the technological substrate and [[Material vs Immaterial]] for the vault-wide axis (McLuhan's claim that it is the immaterial *form* of a medium, not its material content, that reorganizes the sensing body and the social field — see his presence in that note).

The crucial McLuhan move is that fragmentation is not decay but *technology*. It is the specific work of the [[The Phonetic Alphabet|phonetic alphabet]] and its hotting-up into print: the "fragmenting and analytic power of the printed word in our psychic lives" that "split literate man out of the tribal world." Wholeness, correspondingly, is not a moral ideal recovered by effort but the automatic perceptual *effect* of electric speed, which by "the substitution of the inclusive image for the mere viewpoint" restores the "total, unified 'field' character" that tribal-oral culture never lost. The tension is therefore historical and reversible rather than eternal: the West fragmented itself for three thousand years, and electricity is now — for good or ill — re-totalizing it.

## The Poles

- **Fragmentation** — the literate/print/mechanical condition: "the old, fragmented space and time patterns of the pre-electric age." Its signature is specialism, the division of functions, the sequential and lineal, the "private point of view," detachment, and the homogenized individual produced by typographic uniformity. It is the gift and curse of "the technology of literacy," which gave "the power to act without reacting" by fragmenting the self into a single, isolated, manageable function (the surgeon's detachment, the specialist's narrow competence). For McLuhan its origin is precise — "Phonetic letters and number were the first means of fragmenting and detribalizing man" — and Blake's verdict on it is the section's emblem: "Blake, in a word, sees man as fragmented by his technologies."

- **Wholeness** — the electric/integral condition: "wholeness, empathy and depth of awareness," the "total field," the "inclusive image," the "integral being." Its signature is participation in depth, synesthesia and "the close interinvolvement of the other senses," the mythic and configurational rather than the lineal, and the "organic interlacing of all functions" that automation substitutes for "the divisibility of process." It is the perceptual world of oral-tribal man — who "had freely extended the form of his body to include the universe" — re-imposed on the literate West by "the instant quality, and overlay in depth, of electric speed."

## Why It Matters

This axis carries the book's central historical claim and is the hinge on which McLuhan's relevance to the vault turns. Its keynote is the Introduction's: "The aspiration of our time for wholeness, empathy and depth of awareness is a natural adjunct of electric technology" (`Directly stated`). Three things are at stake in it.

First, it explains *why the medium is the message*. McLuhan dates the very intelligibility of his thesis to the perceptual shift from fragmentation to wholeness: "Specialized segments of attention have shifted to total field, and we can now Say, 'The medium is the message' quite naturally." A fragmented, point-of-view culture literally could not see the form of its media; only a culture restored to "instant total awareness" (his example is Cubism, which "by seizing on instant total awareness, suddenly announced that the medium is the message") can perceive the medium as such. The diagnosis and the method are the same insight.

Second, it locates the human cost. The fragmenting of the self is described in the most physical terms — as [[Autoamputation|autoamputation]] and [[Narcosis|narcosis]]: each extension "numbs" the sense it amplifies, so the gain in specialist power is paid for in perceptual wholeness. The famous lag — "We live mythically but continue to think frag-mentarily and on single planes" — is the condition of a self whose technologies have outrun its self-understanding.

Third, it is the upstream source for the vault's posthuman and surveillance foci. McLuhan's wholeness — "we have extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace, abolishing both space and time" — is the perceptual ancestor of [[Posthumanism|the posthuman]], [[Distributed Cognition|distributed cognition]], and [[The Cyborg|the cyborg]]: the body recoded as one node in a total field. And his warning that this wholeness is achieved by leasing the integral self to the medium (the [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System|central nervous system worn outside the skull]]) is why "wholeness" here is never simply benign — the inclusive field can be a global embrace or a global capture.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Marshall McLuhan]] in [[Understanding Media]] — the whole book is an argument from this axis. McLuhan periodizes Western history as a three-thousand-year career of self-fragmentation by literate-mechanical technology, now reversing under electric speed into an "implosive" wholeness. He treats fragmentation as the *active power* of the [[The Phonetic Alphabet|phonetic alphabet]] and print — the maker of [[Typographic Man|typographic man]], specialism, nationalism, and the private point of view — and wholeness as the *automatic perceptual effect* of electricity, which restores the "total field," the mythic, and the integral. He is ambivalent about both poles: fragmentation gave the West "detachment and noninvolvement—the power to act without reacting," its "unique strength," even as it produced "that 'dissociation of sensibility'"; wholeness restores "empathy and depth of awareness," even as it can numb, retribalize, and inundate.

## Fragmentation as the Work of Literacy and Print (McLuhan)

McLuhan's strongest and most repeated claim is that fragmentation is *caused*, and caused specifically by phonetic literacy intensified into print. In "[[The Medium Is the Message]]" he derives the whole mechanical principle from it: "mechanization is achieved by fragmentation of any process and by putting the fragmented parts in a series," and "The restructuring of human work and association was shaped by the technique of fragmentation that is the essence of machine technology." In "Number: Profile of the Crowd" he names the origin point flatly — "Phonetic letters and number were the first means of fragmenting and detribalizing man" — and in "[[Print as the Architect of Nationalism|The Printed Word: Architect of Nationalism]]" he gives the consequence its sharpest formulation: "The fragmenting and analytic power of the printed word in our psychic lives gave us that 'dissociation of sensibility' which in the arts and literature since Cezanne and since Baudelaire has been a top priority for elimination." The fragmented self is thus the literate self — and "Literacy creates very much simpler kinds of people than those that develop in the complex web of ordinary tribal and oral societies" ("Hybrid Energy"). `Directly stated`.

That fragmentation is, for McLuhan, simultaneously a *strength* and a *wound* is essential to the tension's structure. The strength: literate detachment "made possible the discovery of individualism, introspection and so on," and the typographic gift of "detachment and noninvolvement—the power to act without reacting" is "the chief gift of typography." The wound: Blake "sees man as fragmented by his technologies," and the specialist's "intense concentration on minute segments and specialist tasks" — "the specialist is one who never makes small mistakes while moving toward the grand fallacy" — leaves him "near to a state of disintegration by putting us out of touch with ourselves" ("Number"). McLuhan does not simply condemn the fragmented pole; he reads it as the price of Western power, an embarrassment only now, "in the electric age, in which all people are involved in all others at all times." `Directly stated` for the claims; the strength/wound ambivalence is `Strongly implied`.

## Wholeness as the Automatic Effect of Electric Speed (McLuhan)

Against fragmentation McLuhan sets wholeness — but, characteristically, as an *effect* of technology rather than an achievement of will. The keynote is the Introduction's: "The aspiration of our time for wholeness, empathy and depth of awareness is a natural adjunct of electric technology." Electricity restores the total field because it "ended sequence by making things instant," substituting "the inclusive image for the mere viewpoint" and shifting "Specialized segments of attention" to "total field." In "[[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]]" he gives the industrial form of the same reversal: "automation in industry replaces the divisibility of process with the organic interlacing of all functions in the com-plex. The electric tape succeeds the assembly line." The fragmented, serial, lineal world (the assembly line, the point of view, the specialist) is replaced by the integral, configurational, field-organized world (the electric tape, the inclusive image, the [[The Global Village|global village]]). `Directly stated`.

The wholeness pole carries McLuhan's lag-diagnosis, which is the section's most quoted line: "We live mythically but continue to think frag-mentarily and on single planes" ("Media Cold and Hot"). The self is already, technologically, in the total field — "In the electric age we wear all mankind as our skin" — but its *habits of thought* remain those of the fragmented page. Wholeness is thus not yet *understood*, only *undergone*; the work of the book is to close that gap. McLuhan locates the same restored unity at the world scale — "When we have achieved a world-wide fragmentation, it is not unnatural to think about a world-wide integration" ("Number") — and at the level of dwelling and the body: "Literate man, civilized man, tends to restrict and enclose space and to separate functions, whereas tribal man had freely extended the form of his body to include the universe" ("Housing"). The integral pole is always the tribal-oral pole returning by electric means. `Directly stated`.

A caution belongs to this pole, and McLuhan supplies it himself: wholeness is not automatically benign. The same electric implosion that restores empathy also "retribalizes," inundates ("Mental breakdown of varying degrees is the very common result of uprooting and inundation with new information"), and numbs — and it is achieved by extending "our central nervous system itself" outside the body, which elsewhere in the book becomes the ground of capture (the nerves "leased" to private corporations). The wholeness pole is the perceptual face of the [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System|central nervous system extended]] — which is why this tension cross-links to [[Material vs Immaterial]]: the "total field" is an immaterial form imposing itself on the material, sensing body. `Strongly implied`.

## Related Concepts

- [[Typographic Man]] — the fragmented self as a historical type: specialist, detached, lineal, organized by the private point of view; the product of print and the phonetic alphabet. The named occupant of the fragmentation pole.
- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the general theory beneath the axis: every extension reorganizes the [[Sense Ratios|sense ratios]], and the literate/electric difference is a difference in which sense is amplified and which are numbed.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — the mechanism by which fragmentation and wholeness are produced: a medium that intensifies one sense (the visual, for print) fragments the sensorium; electric speed "dethrones the visual sense and restores us to the dominion of synesthesia."
- [[The Phonetic Alphabet]] — McLuhan's named cause of fragmentation: "the first means of fragmenting and detribalizing man," the "break boundary between tribal and individualist man."
- [[Explosion vs Implosion]] — the spatial-dynamic form of the same axis: the fragmenting "explosion" of mechanical technology reversing into the unifying "implosion" of the electric.
- [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] — the social form of the axis: literacy detribalizes (fragments the tribe into individuals), electricity retribalizes (restores the integral, participatory group).
- [[The Global Village]] — the wholeness pole at world scale: the total field as a re-tribalized, integral planetary community.
- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the technological substrate of this perceptual tension; read together.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the postural face of the same opposition: the fragmented self is detached, the integral self participates "in depth."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the vault-wide axis; McLuhan's "total field" / "inclusive image" is the immaterial *form* of a medium restructuring the material, sensing body.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

McLuhan holds the two poles as a *historical reversal in progress* rather than as a contradiction to be resolved or a balance to be struck. He does not call for a return to fragmented literacy, nor for a simple surrender to electric wholeness; both poles carry, for him, an ambivalence — fragmentation is the West's "unique strength" *and* a "dissociation of sensibility"; wholeness restores "empathy and depth of awareness" *and* retribalizes, inundates, numbs. The nearest thing to a synthesis in the book is *understanding itself*: the gap named in "We live mythically but continue to think frag-mentarily and on single planes" is to be closed not by choosing a pole but by raising the electric whole into conscious awareness — the artist as "the man of integral awareness" who "can correct the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures" ("Challenge and Collapse"). The tension is therefore held *open and directional*: the West is moving from fragmentation toward wholeness whether it understands the move or not, and the only available agency is in perceiving the transition rather than in arresting or reversing it. The desirable outcome is not a return to either pole but the rare middle ground of an electric wholeness that is also *thought* and not merely *undergone* — the "depth of awareness" of the keynote, achieved consciously rather than imposed subliminally. `Interpretive synthesis`.
