# Inevitability vs Contingency

## The Poles

- **Technological inevitability** — the claim that the digital order (ubiquitous computing, total data extraction, pervasive watching) is simply what technology *does*: an autonomous, self-driving force whose only rational human response is to adapt. In its purest form it reduces the human to the passive terminus of a process decided elsewhere — *Man Conforms*.
- **Historical contingency** — the counter-claim that this order "is not and never can be a thing in itself, isolated from economics and society"; that technology is always an economic *means* and never an end; and that the digital future, being humanly made, can be unmade and remade by democratic processes. The future is open.

## Why It Matters

This is the tension over *who decides the future*. If the surveillance-capitalist order is inevitable, then resistance is irrational, regulation is futile, and politics yields to engineering — the posture Shoshana Zuboff calls [[Inevitabilism]]. If it is contingent, then the order is a set of "meticulously calculated and lavishly funded" commercial choices that other choices could undo, and the future tense is reclaimable. The stakes are agency itself: Zuboff argues that "every doctrine of inevitability carries a weaponized virus of moral nihilism programmed to target human agency and delete resistance and creativity from the text of human possibility." `Directly stated` To name the future *contingent* is therefore not a metaphysical nicety but a political act — it restores the standing of democratic institutions against private power.

The tension also draws the line between description and ideology. Inevitabilist rhetoric *presents itself* as neutral observation of where technology is heading; Zuboff reads it instead as "a Trojan horse for powerful economic imperatives," a misdirection that "make[s] surveillance capitalism's practices appear to be inevitable when they are actually meticulously calculated and lavishly funded means to self-dealing commercial ends." `Directly stated` Understanding the tension is what lets one see the alibi as an alibi.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Shoshana Zuboff]] in [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]] — stages the tension as her foundational polemic. Her thesis is the contingency side: "That surveillance capitalism is a logic in action and not a technology is a vital point because surveillance capitalists want us to think that their practices are inevitable expressions of the technologies they employ." `Directly stated` She refutes inevitability structurally — "technological inevitability does not exist" because technology's "DNA comes already patterned by what the sociologist Max Weber called the 'economic orientation'" — and dramatizes it through the Spanish "right to be forgotten" struggle (see [[The Right to the Future Tense]]), where the Data Protection Agency and the European Court of Justice "demonstrated the unbearable lightness of the inevitable." Her summary epigram folds both poles into a single image: "technological inevitability is as light as democracy is heavy." `Directly stated`
- [[Marshall McLuhan]] in [[Understanding Media]] — supplies a precursor refusal of technological autonomy from the *perceptual* side. His verdict on the "neutral instrument" view of technology — [[The neutral-instrument view of technology is the numb stance of the technological idiot|the numb stance of the technological idiot]] — rejects the idea that media are inert tools whose effects are decided only by their content. Where Zuboff locates the alibi in political economy, McLuhan locates it in perception, but both deny that technology is a self-explaining thing-in-itself. The convergence is interpretive: Zuboff does not cite McLuhan here, yet the two arguments rhyme as twin refusals of the inevitable. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the order whose claim to inevitability is at stake; inevitabilist rhetoric is its protective ideology.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Within Zuboff's own argument the tension is not symmetrical and not meant to be *held* — it is meant to be *resolved* in favour of contingency. Inevitability, on her account, is not a real feature of the world but a manufactured doctrine; the only thing "inevitable" about the digital future is the rhetoric insisting that it is. Her empirical check confirms the asymmetry: among fifty-two practitioners, "nearly every interviewee regarded inevitability rhetoric as a Trojan horse for powerful economic imperatives." `Directly stated` So for Zuboff the tension dissolves once the alibi is exposed — what remains is a political question (will democratic institutions act?) rather than a metaphysical one.

But the tension stays genuinely open at the vault scale, because the corpus contains avowed inevitabilists who would take the other pole sincerely rather than ideologically. [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s "law of accelerating returns" and [[Yuval Noah Harari]]'s [[Dataism]] both treat the trajectory as effectively unstoppable — and largely welcome it. Read against them, Zuboff's contingency thesis is one disputant in an unresolved argument about whether the future has a direction we can only ride or a shape we can still choose. The unresolved residue is the leverage question her own framing raises: even granting that the future is "contingent and contestable by democratic action," whether courts and individuals can in fact bend population-scale economic power remains, in her words, a matter the "next decades" will decide. `Interpretive synthesis`
