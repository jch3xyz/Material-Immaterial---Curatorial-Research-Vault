# Cyborg vs Goddess

## The Poles

- **The Cyborg** — the technological hybrid of organism and machine: ironic, partial, perverse, blasphemous, "not of Woman born," without origin story, garden, or Fall. It "takes irony for granted," is "suspicious of the reproductive matrix," and seeks regeneration rather than rebirth. It is the figure of seizing the tools that marked one as other and surviving on the boundaries.
- **The Goddess** — the organic, holistic counter-figure mobilized by ecofeminism and feminist paganism: the body written as wholeness, the matrix of reproductive sex and metaphors of rebirth, the recovery of original unity and natural standpoint. In its mythic limit she is "the innocent and all-powerful Mother," the prelapsarian wholeness the cyborg refuses.

## Why It Matters

This is the closing opposition of the manifesto and its single most quoted line: "Though both are bound in the spiral dance, I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess." It crystallizes Haraway's wager about where a late-twentieth-century feminist politics should be grounded. The stakes are not technophilia versus technophobia in the ordinary sense; they are two rival *myth systems* for feminist imagination. The goddess pole — the insistence on the organic against the technological — grounds politics in original innocence, holism, victimhood, and "closeness to nature." Haraway argues this is "an oppositional ideolog[y] fitting the late twentieth century," intelligible only against the machines and consciousness of late capitalism, and therefore "part of the cyborg world" even in its refusal of it. The cyborg pole instead grounds politics in irony, partiality, non-innocence, and responsibility for the boundaries one is made of. What understanding this tension enables is the recognition that Haraway is not destroying the organic but *displacing the priority of innocence* as the foundation of political authority — choosing the blasphemous over the reverent, regeneration over rebirth, the spliced and noisy over the whole and pure (`Interpretive synthesis`). Choosing the goddess, on this reading, would re-secure the very "natural matrix of unity" the cyborg myth exists to crack.

The opposition is doubled in ch.03 at the level of the sacred itself: in the move from the "Organics of Domination" to the "Informatics of Domination," neither god nor goddess survives as a natural ground — "It's not just that 'god' is dead; so is the 'goddess.'" The objects of the new information order "cannot be coded as 'natural,'" which "subverts naturalistic coding for the left-hand side as well." The goddess therefore cannot be a refuge *outside* the system; she is dead as a natural standpoint, or "revivified" only inside microelectronic and biotechnological politics — the same condition that produces the cyborg (`Directly stated`, ch.03).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Donna Haraway]] in *A Cyborg Manifesto* — stages the opposition explicitly. Ch.06 ("Cyborgs: A Myth of Political Identity") supplies the closing slogan and the substantive contrast: organic/holistic politics "depend on metaphors of rebirth and invariably call on the resources of reproductive sex," whereas "cyborgs have more to do with regeneration and are suspicious of the reproductive matrix and of most birthing." Ch.03 ("The Informatics of Domination") supplies the ontological underwriting: both god and goddess die as natural categories in the shift to an information system.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is **deliberately not symmetrical and not dissolved** — it is a *preference*, openly avowed. Haraway does not abolish the goddess pole; she explicitly concedes that "both are bound in the spiral dance," granting the organic-holistic figure a shared mythic ground with the cyborg even as she refuses its priority. The "spiral dance" image (drawn from the language of feminist paganism it opposes) signals that the two are entangled rather than cleanly separable: the goddess myth is "part of the cyborg world," and the cyborg is itself a myth, "a kind of disassembled and reassembled, postmodern collective and personal self." So the synthesis on offer is not a reconciliation of the poles but a *re-grounding of feminist politics on the non-innocent pole* while acknowledging the other's continued pull (`Interpretive synthesis`). What remains genuinely open is whether a politics of irony and partiality can supply the affective and mobilizing force that origin myths and organic wholeness have historically supplied — Haraway offers "a powerful infidel heteroglossia," not "a common language," and leaves the wager's success undecided (`Strongly implied`).
