# Effort vs Result

## The Poles

- **Effort** — throughput, activity, spending, the quantity of measurable exertion a system pours in: money spent per student, dollars of military spending, IUDs implanted, "flows of stuff made and purchased in a year." What is easy to count and easy to name as a goal.
- **Result** — the achievement or real welfare the effort was supposed to buy: good education, national security, human flourishing, "the source of real wealth and real pleasure." What the system was actually meant to produce, and what a proxy indicator so often fails to measure.

## Why It Matters

This tension is the polarity behind [[Seeking the Wrong Goal]], the [[Systems Theory|system trap]] Donella Meadows treats in Chapter Five of *Thinking in Systems*. Because a system's goal is "the direction-setter of the system," a goal defined as effort makes the system obediently produce effort — and only effort. The danger is not malfunction but fidelity: the machinery works perfectly toward the wrong target. When a society names throughput (GNP) as its goal, it faithfully maximizes throughput while welfare, equity, and justice go unproduced. Meadows calls confusing the two "one of the most common mistakes in designing systems around the wrong goal," which makes this the diagnostic signature of the whole trap: wherever you find a system optimizing a proxy for activity rather than the outcome it stands in for, you have collapsed result into effort. Understanding the polarity is what lets you catch a badly defined goal before the system dutifully bends everything below it — its rules, information flows, and feedback loops — toward producing exertion instead of welfare.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Donella Meadows]] in [[Thinking in Systems]] — stages the opposition as the core error inside [[Seeking the Wrong Goal]]. Her examples — military spending as security, money-per-student and test scores as education, IUDs implanted as family planning, and above all GNP as national success — all "confuse effort with result." Her closing rule for escaping the trap: "Be especially careful not to confuse effort with result or you will end up with a system that is producing effort, not result."

## Related Concepts

- [[Seeking the Wrong Goal]] — the system trap this tension is the internal polarity of; confusing effort with result is its defining error.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — "System behavior is particularly sensitive to the goals of feedback loops"; naming effort as the goal aims every loop at the wrong reference point.
- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — the goal is "the indicator... toward which balancing feedback loops work," so an effort-defined goal points the corrective machinery at exertion rather than welfare.
- [[The Growth Imperative]] — Harari's economy-must-grow mandate is the value-laden twin of Meadows's structural critique: a society whose goal is throughput (GNP) produces throughput, not the result it was a proxy for.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Meadows offers a way out rather than a dissolution: "Specify indicators and goals that reflect the real welfare of the system." The tension is resolvable in principle — measure and report the result you actually want (welfare, equity, justice, the lowest infant mortality, the smallest gap between rich and poor) rather than the effort that is merely easy to count. But it is not resolvable once and for all, because every measurable indicator is a proxy, and any proxy can slide back into standing for effort rather than result. The polarity is therefore best held as a standing discipline — a question to ask of any goal — rather than a contradiction to be closed: does this indicator measure what we exert, or what we are trying to achieve?
