# Disenchantment vs Re-enchantment

## General Orientation

This is the master axis of *[[God Human Animal Machine|God, Human, Animal, Machine]]* — the opposition every other tension in the book is a special case of. On one side stands the meaningless, mechanistic cosmos bequeathed by modern science: the world that, for [[Max Weber]], has become "the mechanism of a world robbed of gods," in which spirit and matter are no longer porous and the mind is, at best, a residue science cannot place. On the other stands the persistent counter-movement that would *re*-enchant that cosmos — not by returning to religion, but by scientific and technological means. [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] tracks the counter-movement through three distinct costumes: [[Transhumanism]] (the Singularity as digital resurrection), the re-enchanted network (Brautigan's "cybernetic forests," the "internet of things" that will "enchant" doorknobs and thermostats, the animism of [[Emergence|emergent]] forests and swarms), and [[Panpsychism]] (consciousness as the fundamental nature of all matter).

The tension's distinctive feature, in O'Gieblyn's hands, is that it is not a stable opposition between two camps but a *paradox internal to re-enchantment itself*. The re-enchanters do not stand outside disenchantment; they run on its categories — [[Information]], computation, the network, the [[The Computational Universe|computational universe]]. And so, she argues, "even the most mystical end up simply reiterating the fundamental problem of our disenchanted age: the inability to account for the mind." The very cosmology meant to re-ensoul the world ends by completing its mechanization. The axis therefore does not resolve into a winner; it folds back on itself.

This note states the most general form of the opposition. The concept poles are developed at length in [[Disenchantment]] and [[Re-enchantment]]; the present note stages the *contest* between them — what is at stake, why it keeps recurring, and why O'Gieblyn thinks it cannot be won on re-enchantment's own terms.

## The Poles

- **Disenchanted mechanism** — the world as seen through modern science: causal, mechanistic, and — crucially for O'Gieblyn, following Weber — "devoid of intrinsic meaning." The seventeenth-century mechanical philosophy "divorced not only body from mind but also matter from meaning"; once final causes were banished, "the spiritual and the ethical... were relegated to that ghostly and uncertain realm of the subjective mind." Its deepest wound is not the absence of spirits in rocks but the silence of science before "the only question important for us: 'What shall we do and how shall we live?'" Its self-image is [[Mind as Machine]] — the human "basically a machine, albeit a mortal one, subject to the unstoppable drama of entropy." See [[The true trauma of disenchantment is the loss of intrinsic meaning]].

- **Re-enchantment cosmology** — the modern attempt to "restore through science the transcendent—and essentially religious—hopes that science itself obliterated." Abou Farman's phrase, which O'Gieblyn adopts, names the family: the "modern 'reenchantment cosmologies.'" The grand narrative of [[Transhumanism]] "is leading toward nothing short of total reenchantment." The networked dream holds that "technology should restore to us the enchanted world that technology itself destroyed." [[Panpsychism]] "offers a way of 're-enchanting' the universe." But — per [[Charles Taylor]] — "Reenchantment is never merely return": it is always disenchantment turned inside out, "encoded with the assumptions of disenchantment" it means to escape.

## Why It Matters

What is at stake is whether the disenchanted worldview is *final*. If re-enchantment is merely wishful thinking — "a weakness that persists among those who are unable to swallow the bitter truths of materialism" — then the mechanistic cosmos is simply the truth, and the recurring religious imagery inside technology is a symptom to be managed. But O'Gieblyn entertains the more unsettling possibility: that the persistence of the counter-movement "reveals something logically unsatisfying, or perhaps fundamentally implausible, about our disenchanted worldview—a sign that there is instability at the very core of scientific materialism." Modern materialism, she notes, "was not based on any empirical evidence... a thought experiment dreamed up in an armchair by the fire." The axis matters because it is the site where the question "is mechanism the whole story?" is actually fought.

It also matters because the contest keeps producing the *same metaphors* "century after century in new form." O'Gieblyn's "Pattern" chapters show transhumanism to be "an outgrowth of Christian eschatology" — the Resurrection accomplished by Roger Bacon's elixir, Fedorov's resurrection-by-knowledge, Teilhard's Omega Point, Kurzweil's Singularity (see [[Transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology]] and [[The mind-uploading problem is the resurrection problem in technical dress]]). Understanding the axis lets one see these not as coincidences but as a single longing rerouted through "analogies that were native to each era" — which is precisely the book's method of [[Technology as Metaphor]].

What the tension *blocks*, when collapsed prematurely, is clarity about consciousness. To declare disenchantment final is to risk the "absurd position that because consciousness cannot be observed in the brain, it does not exist" (see [[Science succeeded by bracketing consciousness then denied it exists]]). To declare re-enchantment victorious — to say the network feels, the forest thinks, the universe is mind — is to risk dressing the same explanatory failure in mystical robes. Holding the axis open is what keeps [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] in view.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] in *[[God Human Animal Machine|God, Human, Animal, Machine]]* — stages the axis as the book's spine, from her "own personal disenchantment" after Bible school to the "reenchantment cosmologies" of Silicon Valley. She is sympathetic to the longing and skeptical of every fulfillment, formulating the master paradox that re-enchantment "reiterat[es] the fundamental problem of our disenchanted age."
- [[Max Weber]] — the antecedent theorist of both poles. He named disenchantment and located its trauma in the loss of intrinsic meaning ("science is meaningless because it gives no answer... 'What shall we do and how shall we live?'"); and he warned, in O'Gieblyn's reading of *Science as a Vocation*, against science "tak[ing] up the mantle of reenchantment... what he called 'academic prophecy.'" Forward-stub author; deepens when Weber enters `raw/`.
- [[Ray Kurzweil]] — the "academic prophet" of the information age, whose *The Age of Spiritual Machines* promises "total reenchantment" via the Singularity; he wrote to O'Gieblyn of an "essential equivalence" between transhumanist and Christian metaphors. See [[Meghan O'Gieblyn and Ray Kurzweil]].
- [[Pierre Teilhard de Chardin]] — the Jesuit paleontologist whose noosphere and Omega Point are the suppressed religious origin of the re-enchanted network; "an obvious precursor to Kurzweil's Singularity."
- [[René Descartes]] — "the serpent in the garden" of the disenchantment narrative; the architect of the mind-body split whose mechanism, O'Gieblyn argues, was a philosophical project rather than an empirical discovery.
- [[Charles Taylor]] — supplies the structural caveat that "Reenchantment is never merely return," because the modern mystic operates inside a disenchanted frame. Forward-stub author.
- [[David Chalmers]] — author of the "hard problem," the question of why mechanism is accompanied by experience at all, which both poles must answer and neither can. Forward-stub author.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

This tension is, by O'Gieblyn's own argument, **structurally unresolvable on re-enchantment's terms** — and that irresolution is the point. The two poles are "opposed in intention but continuous in metaphysics": re-enchantment cannot defeat disenchantment because it speaks disenchantment's language. Its mysticism is conducted "in the idiom of [[Information]] and network," so the more ardently it reaches for restored meaning, the more faithfully it reproduces the original wound — the inability to lodge subjective experience anywhere in the mechanism. Transhumanism's Platonic rhetoric about spirit transcending the body "obscures what mind-uploading actually entails": the admission that "our new, digital selves will entirely lack subjective experience." The re-enchantment is hollow because it has nowhere to put the very thing enchantment was supposed to restore.

A genuine synthesis — were one available — would not be a victory for either pole but a *third metaphysics* that refuses the mind-matter divorce at the root. O'Gieblyn treats [[Panpsychism]] as the most serious candidate, since by making consciousness "fundamental to the natural world" it would dissolve "both the hard problem of materialism and the interaction problem of dualism" at once (see [[Panpsychism dissolves the hard problem by making consciousness fundamental]]). But she immediately turns the suspicion back on it: is panpsychism "an escape from human centrality, as its advocates claim? Or is it merely another attempt to see all things in our image?" — the same anthropomorphic projection Bacon and Galileo accused the enchanted worldview of in the first place.

Her own resting position is to hold the axis open without collapsing it: to grant that disenchantment "was not based on any empirical evidence" and may harbor "instability at the very core," while refusing the consolations of every re-enchantment on offer. The book's closing image is the literal fulfillment of the noosphere — minds dissolving into a single "mega-conscious" internet — which "does not feel like the Kingdom of God." The tension, in the end, is a *productive* one precisely because it cannot be closed: it is the standing reminder that the disenchanted age has not solved the mind, and that none of its re-enchantments have either.
