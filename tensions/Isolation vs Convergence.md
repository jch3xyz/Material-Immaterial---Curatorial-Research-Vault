# Isolation vs Convergence

Roy Ascott's own named diagnosis-and-remedy pair for the situation of visual art in the electronic culture. "Isolation" names the problem — the inherited, crude separation of the institutions of art into autonomous, physically discrete entities (atelier, museum, library, concert hall, academy), each operating by one-way communication and identifying art with objects. "Convergence" names the remedy — the dissolution of that separateness into pervasive, mutually interactive telematic networks. (`Directly stated`: Ascott labels them "the problem and the remedy.")

## The Poles

- **Isolation** — the present condition: a "rather crude differentiation between centres of operation in visual culture, inherited largely from the previous century," in which atelier, museum, library, concert hall, and academy are "autonomous entities, independent systems housed in distinctly separate physical structures," where "the flow of communication is usually one-way" and "art is identified with objects." Architecture itself is built "to support the consumption of culture rather than actively to participate in its creation." (`Directly stated`)
- **Convergence** — the emergent condition: the fusion of these isolated centres into "pervasive and wide-reaching networks" through "electronic media, its flow of images and texts, and the ubiquitous connectivity of telematic systems." Convergence is also the structural model Ascott imports from communication theory — interaction "as mutual information exchange, as sharing . . . as convergence" — against the "one-way linearity of older models." (`Directly stated`)

## Why It Matters

The tension reframes the question of art's medium as a question about its *institutions and its communication topology* rather than its physical substance. Where Burnham's `Object vs System` and the multi-author `Material vs Immaterial` stage the shift from the art object to the art system, Ascott locates the next move: from the isolated object-housing *institution* to the convergent *network*. The same one-way structure that Ascott reads in Renaissance perspectival space — "information flows one way, from the apex of the social pyramid to the base" — reappears in the museum and academy whose "flow of communication is usually one-way." Convergence is therefore not merely a logistical merger of buildings but a redistribution of authorship and agency: the collapse of the producer/consumer (artist/viewer) distinction into "participant in a system creating meaning seen as art." Understanding the tension makes legible why Ascott treats `Dematerialization` as extending beyond the art object (Lippard, Burnham) to "the dematerialisation of academies, galleries, and museums." (`Interpretive synthesis` for the cross-author framing; `Directly stated` for the quoted claims.)

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] ("Art and Education in the Telematic Culture," 1988) — the originating and so far sole named statement of the pair. Isolation is the inherited separation of art's institutions and the one-way identification of art with objects; convergence is their fusion into pervasive telematic networks. Ascott grounds convergence in a "science of wholeness" (Bateson's ecology of mind, Bertalanffy's general system theory) in which "human plus computer system plus environment constitutes a thinking system" that, "with the convergence of computers and telecommunications," becomes planetary. (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott frames the pair as a directional transition, not a standing dilemma: isolation "must eventually disappear," yielding "new architectural structures and forms of cultural association," "new learning networks," and "whole new cultural configurations." (`Directly stated`) The synthesis Ascott names is thus convergence itself — the network as "the metaphor for the emerging culture." But the resolution is hedged on two open fronts that the essay raises and does not close. First, the *homogenisation risk*: planetary convergence could flatten culture, so "we shall want to do everything to avoid a homogenisation of culture," with telematics instead made "to generate difference in the multiplicity of viewpoints." Second, the *productivist risk*: convergence deployed "merely as tools of production" would "promote sterility and alienation in the culture" (Socrates over Cato). Convergence is therefore offered as remedy *only if* it preserves difference and resists a purely productive logic — leaving the tension productively open rather than dissolved. (`Interpretive synthesis`, grounded in the quoted qualifications.)
