# Gravity vs Mobility

## The Poles

- **Gravity** — the base as gravitational and biological anchorage. Traditional sculpture is "virtually life that cannot move"; the base ordains a "traditional biological immobility," a "fixity," and "the sense of gravitational dependence so inherent in the anatomy of man." It is "a biological reaffirmation that man is constructed to walk the ground, to gain his mobility through successive contacts with the earth."
- **Mobility** — the avant-garde "collective consciousness" working to overcome biological immobility through spatial mobility, lift, flight, levitation, and orbit. Sculpture is freed "from all earthly contact or means of visible support, making sculpture not so much airborne as gravity defying," up to the literal proposal of "shooting sculpture into orbit."

## Why It Matters

For [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]], the opposition between gravity and mobility is the *motor* of [[The Vanishing Base]]. The base is not a neutral support but an "orientational device" — a biological reaffirmation that man has a top and bottom, a front and back, and is built to walk the earth. To dissolve the base is therefore to refuse the anatomy of man, to attack "biological immobility" itself. The "sculptural will toward spatial mobility" is what makes the base "lose its reason for being" (Directly stated). Reading the base this way reframes a craft detail — how a statue is mounted — as a symptom of a far larger transformation: sculpture's migration from the earthbound, idealized object toward forms freed of mass, gravity, and even spatial orientation. The poles matter because Burnham treats the will to overcome gravity as continuous with the will to overcome biology — the chapter's bridge to [[Transcending Biology]] and away from the [[Vitalism]] and [[Organicism]] of traditional figurative sculpture (Interpretive synthesis).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] (Ch. 1, "Sculpture's Vanishing Base") — locates a "collective consciousness" among avant-garde sculptors, "from the second half of the nineteenth century," working to disturb biological immobility and gravitational dependence; traces the escalation from floor-bound sculpture, through Constructivist "gravity defying" mounts, to air-borne, levitating, and orbital works in which "the base has vanished altogether!"

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Within the chapter the tension is not held in balance but driven progressively toward one pole: mobility wins, and gravity (with the base that signified it) "had lost its reason for being." Yet Burnham notes the resistance is never total — Object sculptors reassert that "sculpture is eminently three-dimensional," "solid, palpable, and real," so that "when sculpture is less and less tangible, the base becomes the only aspect which continues to show presence and substantiality." The tension thus folds into the larger one of [[Material vs Immaterial]]: as sculpture sheds mass and gravity, "the duality between matter and energy enters a new phase," and the question becomes whether sculpture can exist as "a physical system including invisible forces" rather than an earthbound object. Burnham leaves the trajectory open — "the evolutionary pattern is not always clear" — but reads the direction as unmistakable (Strongly implied).
