# Vitalism vs Organicism

## The Poles

- **Vitalism** — "a preeminently poetic view of life, a celebration of the natural condition." Life is held to be instigated by a non-material principle (Aristotle's "entelechy," Bergson's *elan vital*), a "nonmaterial center of being making it different in quality and in kind from inorganic matter." For Jack Burnham this is the doctrine that, though defeated in biology, supplied modern sculpture's reigning aesthetic.
- **Organicism** — "a beautiful view of the utilitarian evolution of systems, both biological and non biological." It is "the awareness of the interrelation between systems and their components within larger systems so that behavior of the whole ensemble can be understood and manipulated" — a systems-sense that runs from biology through machines, factories, and social organization into postwar systems analysis.

## Why It Matters

Burnham stages these two "modes of awareness" as the paired but divergent ways a technological culture can imagine life. The stakes are which sense survives. Vitalism's authority was borrowed from a science it was losing: as a non-material life-essence, it could be invoked by the sculptor to "create 'life'" without copying it, but it had no functional theory and was steadily refuted in biology. Organicism, by contrast, names a structural intuition — the behavior of a whole ensemble exceeding and governing its parts — that proved transferable to machines and systems. Burnham's larger argument is that this systems-sense is what carries forward into the cybernetic age, while vitalism becomes a transitional, object-bound stage left behind. Understanding the opposition clarifies the book's arc from the sculptural *object* toward the *system*, and locates the moment the "organic" detaches from biotic appearance and attaches to biotic *functioning* via the machine. `Strongly implied`

The tension is not a clean opposition. Burnham insists the two creeds "have influenced each other" even as "in some essential ways they remain in polar opposition." Abstract vitalist sculpture (Rodin, Brancusi, Arp, Hepworth, Henry Moore) drew on a heightened, quasi-scientific awareness of organic structure, while organicism in its biological sense aimed at the very "attributes of life" that vitalism mystified. The opposition is therefore generative rather than static — a difference of *sensibility* (poetic essence vs. utilitarian system) more than of subject matter. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] (1968), ch. 2 "The Biotic Sources of Modern Sculpture" — coins the paired distinction and holds the two creeds in deliberate polar tension: vitalism as the poetic, life-as-essence aesthetic of modern sculpture; organicism as the systemic, utilitarian account of evolving systems realized most fully in postwar systems analysis. Henry Moore is noted as one of the few sculptors who "saw the inappropriateness of the word" *organic* "and supplied 'vitalistic' in its stead," sharpening the distinction.

## Related Concepts

- [[Vitalism]] — the poetic pole; life as a non-material essence (the *elan vital*).
- [[Organicism]] — the systemic pole; the utilitarian evolution of systems, the working objective of which is "any arrangement possessing the attributes of life."
- [[Systems Theory]] — where Burnham says organicism is most fully realized; "the largest and most comprehensive attempt to use the analogues of biological organization."
- [[Cybernetics]] — the science that, for Burnham, lets a culture "literally fus[e] organic activities with the linear-geometric precision of machines"; the *kybernetes* succeeds the *elan vital*.
- [[Mechanism (Burnham)]] — vitalism's direct antagonist in biology; organicism partly absorbs and supersedes the mechanist-vitalist dispute by recasting life as system rather than essence.
- [[Emergence]] — the systems-organicist intuition that the whole ensemble's behavior exceeds its components.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the vault-spanning tension this instances: vitalism invests an immaterial "psychic energy" into inert mass, while organicism treats matter as connected energy states forming a single continuum.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham does not hold the tension symmetrically open; he resolves it historically. Vitalism is read as a *transitional step* — a surrogate that anticipates, in metaphor, what organicism and its successor sciences (cybernetics, systems analysis) pursue literally. The organicist systems-sense is what survives: the modern meaning of the organic, Burnham argues, "lies in the gradual moving away from biotic appearances toward biotic functioning via the machine; vitalism is a transitional step in this process from inanimate object to system." Yet the resolution is asymmetric, not total — the two "have influenced each other" and "remain in polar opposition," so vitalism's poetic claim is superseded in authority without being simply refuted as a sensibility. The synthesis Burnham foresees is the *fusion* of the organic and the geometric in the systems age — "neither amorphic or representational... nor linear or abstract... but a fusion of the two which transcends the existing stylistic polarity" — a transcendence in which organicism, not vitalism, provides the operative model. `Strongly implied`
