# Intelligence vs Consciousness

## The Poles

- **Intelligence (data-processing capability)** — the ability to solve problems and perform tasks: "playing chess, driving cars, diagnosing diseases or identifying terrorists." In Harari's frame this reduces to pattern recognition that "non-conscious algorithms may soon excel human consciousness in recognising." Intelligence is, on the Dataist account, what a system *does* — and it requires no inner life.
- **Consciousness (subjective experience)** — "experiencing emotions and sensations"; the inner felt life of "a flesh-and-blood taxi driver" who "can enjoy music," whose "mind may expand in awe as he looks up at the stars," as against "a self-driving car, which feels absolutely nothing." Consciousness is what it is *like* to be a system — the dimension a purely functional account leaves out.

The tension is the wedge Harari drives between these two. For all of history they were welded together; he argues they are now coming apart.

## Why It Matters

This opposition is the hinge of *Homo Deus*'s closing argument and the engine of its "decoupling" thesis. Harari's claim is not that machines will become conscious but the reverse: that "high intelligence" had "always went hand in hand with a developed consciousness," and that we are now "developing new types of non-conscious intelligence that can perform such tasks far better than humans." If intelligence and consciousness separate, then the economic and political value once attached to conscious humans can migrate to non-conscious algorithms — "intelligence is mandatory but consciousness is optional" (`Directly stated`).

What is at stake is the source of human worth. The liberal-humanist order ascribed value to conscious individuals because only they could do intelligent work; sever the two and that justification dissolves, which is why Harari frames the decoupling as the obsolescence of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] (`Directly stated`). The tension also reframes the book's three culminating questions — most directly the second: "What’s more valuable – intelligence or consciousness?" Holding the poles apart turns a "pastime for philosophers" into "an urgent political and economic issue" (`Directly stated`).

The deeper difficulty is that science can measure and reproduce intelligence but cannot yet account for consciousness at all. Harari concedes that "at present we have no idea how or why data flows could produce consciousness and subjective experiences" — which is the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]] viewed from the data-religion's blind spot. The tension therefore marks the exact boundary of the Dataist worldview: everything inside is data-processing; consciousness is the residue it cannot reduce (`Strongly implied`).

- [[Integrated Information Theory]] — the leading attempt to make consciousness a measurable, intelligence-independent quantity.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Harari leaves the tension **deliberately unresolved**, and the leaving-open is the point. He stages it as a genuine question rather than a thesis: "which of the two is really important, intelligence or consciousness?" and, at the book's very end, "What’s more valuable – intelligence or consciousness?" The honest answer the book offers is split-level. *For the system* — "armies and corporations" — the question is already answered: "intelligence is mandatory but consciousness is optional." *For philosophy and ethics* it is wide open, because no one can yet say "what, if anything, would be lost by replacing conscious intelligence with superior non-conscious algorithms" (`Directly stated`).

A genuine synthesis is blocked by the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem]]: until we can say "how or why data flows could produce consciousness," we cannot weigh the poles against each other, only observe that the market has already chosen one. Harari's own gesture toward holding the tension open is the "ocean of consciousness" image — the warning that consciousness is a vast, barely-mapped territory that the system, optimising only for intelligence, may "neglect and even downgrade." The tension thus stands as a watch-post: an opposition to be *held*, lest the optionality of consciousness become its erasure (`Interpretive synthesis`).
