# Presence Absence vs Pattern Randomness

This is the tension *between two dialectics*, not the tension *inside* one. The internal coupling of pattern with randomness — information as both at once, noise driving reorganization — is developed in the concept note [[Pattern and Randomness]]. This note stages the historical *displacement* of one entire dialectic (presence/absence) by another (pattern/randomness): an epistemic shift across cultural sites, which [[N. Katherine Hayles]] first frames as antagonism and later revises into complementarity.

## The Poles

- **Presence/Absence** — the dialectic that, in Hayles's account of [[Jacques Derrida|Derrida]], allies presence "with Logos, God, teleology—in general, with an originary plenitude that can act to ground signification." Here "the metaphysics of presence front-loaded meaning into the system. Meaning was guaranteed because a stable origin existed." Absence is the privative of this plenitude (lack, desire, castration). Crucially, the pole has a long monopoly: "the long tradition of dominance that presence and absence have enjoyed in the Western tradition."
- **Pattern/Randomness** — the dialectic with "deep ties" to information, "underlaid by a very different set of assumptions. In this dialectic, meaning is not front-loaded into the system, and the origin does not act to ground signification." Pattern is information-as-form ("a pattern rather than a presence"); randomness is reframed from mere lack into "the creative ground from which pattern can emerge," even "invested with plenitude." Its catastrophe is *mutation* rather than castration; its semiotics is the [[Flickering Signifier|flickering signifier]] rather than the floating one.

## Why It Matters

What is at stake is the ground of meaning itself, and therefore the status of the body, the artifact, and the original. Under presence/absence, signification is anchored to a stable origin and a located, plenitudinous presence; under pattern/randomness, signification "is made possible (but not inevitable) by the blind force of evolution finding workable solutions within given parameters," with systems evolving "toward an open future marked by contingency and unpredictability." Hayles calls the felt pressure of this shift "the contemporary pressure toward dematerialization, understood as an epistemic shift toward pattern/randomness and away from presence/absence" — which is precisely why this tension is the historical engine behind [[Embodiment vs Information]] and the [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy (Directly stated).

The displacement is observable, not merely theoretical: "discursive formations based on pattern and randomness jostle and compete with formations based on presence and absence," and "the surprise is not that formations based on them continue to exist but that these formations are being displaced so rapidly across a wide range of cultural sites." Money as informational pattern rather than cash, access rather than possession, the avatar that "both is and is not present" — each is a site where "Questions about presence and absence do not yield much leverage" and "the focus shifts to questions about pattern and randomness" (Directly stated).

The sharpest stake is Hayles's claim about *deconstruction*. She argues that the very theory that destabilized the self could only do so from *inside* presence/absence — it could not escape the dialectic it critiqued: "Just as the metaphysics of presence required an originary plenitude to articulate a stable self, deconstruction required a metaphysics of presence to articulate the destabilization of that self." Deconstruction is therefore "the child of an information age," forced into visibility *because* presence/absence was already "losing its constitutive power to form the ground for discourse." Understanding this tension blocks a category error — treating pattern/randomness as one more move *within* the old dialectic rather than as a rival dialectic with its own ground (Directly stated).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[N. Katherine Hayles]] in [[How We Became Posthuman]] — the primary and originating source. She poses the two as "the two central dialectics involved in the formation of the posthuman," tracks the ascendancy of pattern/randomness as information saturates everyday life, diagnoses deconstruction as trapped inside presence/absence, and across the book moves from an explicit "antagonistic relation" to the proposal that the two are "complementary."
- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in [[The Transparency Society]] — *interpretive link.* The thematic contrast is suggestive rather than asserted by either author: Han's complaint that [[Hyperinformation|hyperinformation]] "brings no light into darkness" and that the positive accumulation of data dissolves the [[Negativity|negativity]] of meaning reads as a defense of the *presence/absence* side — Logos, the narrative that front-loads meaning — against an additive informatic order. Where Hayles diagnoses the shift neutrally as an epistemic displacement, Han diagnoses a closely related shift as loss. The pairing is a possible resonance across authors, not a claim that Han uses Hayles's pattern/randomness vocabulary (Speculative connection).

## Related Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the substantive fight that *this* epistemic shift makes possible; the privileging of informational pattern over material instantiation. Hayles is the primary source for both.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the "pressure toward dematerialization" is this tension felt as a hierarchy of value; Hayles insists the pattern/randomness dialectic "does not erase the material world."
- [[Flickering Signifier]] — the semiotic instantiation of the shift: floating signifiers belong to presence/absence, flickering signifiers to pattern/randomness.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the figure the conjoined dialectics produce; "located within the dialectic of pattern/randomness and grounded in embodied actuality," the posthuman of emergence and contingency rather than originary presence.
- [[Inscription vs Incorporation]] — the theoretical pairing Hayles conjoins with the two dialectics in the mapping chapter's semiotic square.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Hayles deliberately revises the tension from antagonism toward complementarity, but does not collapse it. In chapter 2 the working assumption is hostile: "presence and pattern are opposites existing in antagonistic relation. The more emphasis that falls on one, the less the other is noticed and valued." She then proposes a reinterpretation — "seeing pattern and presence as complementary rather than antagonistic." By the mapping chapter this becomes a method: "pattern/randomness can profitably be seen as complementary to presence/absence rather than as antagonistic," related on the semiotic square by "exclusion rather than opposition," because "pattern/randomness tells a part of the story that cannot be told through presence/absence and vice versa." The two dialectics are made to *generate* synthetic terms together — materiality, information, mutation, hyperreality — rather than to cancel one another (Directly stated).

The synthesis is therefore real but asymmetric and open. Pattern/randomness is ascendant, yet presence/absence retains explanatory power "for it connects materiality and signification in ways not possible within the pattern/randomness dialectic." The Conclusion frames the goal not as the victory of one pole but as transforming both "from the inside to arrive at new kinds of cultural configurations, which may soon render such dualities obsolete if they have not already." Held rather than dissolved: the tension is productive precisely while both dialectics remain in play, and Hayles's wager is that pattern/randomness can be embraced without "a systematic devaluation of materiality and embodiment" (Strongly implied).
