# Individual vs Interface

## The Poles

- **The Individual** — the unitary, bounded mind and the solitary consciousness of the old order of Western thought: a single self "locked socially and philosophically into the solitary body," the discrete person presupposed by the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]].
- **The Interface** — the self as node and connection: a collaborative mind and connected consciousness, a set of selves "made up of many individuals," dispersed across the network. Identity is no longer something one *has* but a point at which one *connects*. As Ascott puts it, "We are all interface."

## Why It Matters

For Ascott this is not a metaphor but an ontological shift in human identity itself. The computer-mediated, telematic condition does not merely give the individual new tools; it dissolves the individual *as the unit of being* and replaces it with the interface. Where modernity grounded knowledge, rights, autonomy, and creativity in the self-possessed solitary subject, Ascott argues that "the sense of the individual is giving way to the sense of the interface" — the collaborative mind and connected consciousness replacing the unitary mind and solitary consciousness of the old order of Western thought.

The stakes are large. If the bearer of mind is the interface rather than the individual, then the entire inherited apparatus of Western thought — the autonomous author, the original idea, the bounded inner life, the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist]] picture of a self that precedes its relations — loses its ontological footing. Ideas "come from the interactions and negotiations of minds"; minds that were once locked in the solitary body "now float free in telematic space." Understanding the tension is what lets one see [[New Media Art|networked]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology|telematic art]] not as art *about* connection but as art that enacts a new, [[The Distributed Self|distributed]] mode of being a self. Blocking the tension — clinging to the individual as the only possible unit — leaves us, on Ascott's account, unable to conceive of [[Telepresence|telepresence]], [[Reciprocal Communication|reciprocal]] authorship, or the [[The Post-Biological Body|post-biological body]] that the cybernet redefines.

This pole-shift sits beside, but is not identical to, [[Material vs Immaterial]]: the question here is not what art is *made of* but who or what *has the experience* — the discrete person or the connective node.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] — names the opposition directly in "The Architecture of Cyberception": telepresence is "the disseminated self," and "the collaborative mind and the connected consciousness replacing the unitary mind and solitary consciousness of the old order of Western thought." The interface, not the individual, becomes the locus of identity: "We are all interface. We are computer-mediated and computer-enhanced."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

For Ascott the tension is not held in balance but historically *resolved in favor of the interface* — he frames the individual pole as belonging to a passing "old order." Yet his own language preserves a residue of the individual: the self is "a set of selves," "many individuals," not no individual at all; the interface is still *somebody's* interface ("each individual interface to the net is an aspect of a telematic unity"). So the productive reading is that the individual is not abolished but *redistributed* — recast as one access node within a connective whole, present at any single interface yet "in the virtual presence of all the other interfaces throughout the network." The tension is best held, not dissolved: the individual persists as the local form the interface takes. `Interpretive synthesis`
