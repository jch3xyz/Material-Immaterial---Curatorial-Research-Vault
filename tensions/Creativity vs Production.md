# Creativity vs Production

## The Poles

- **Creativity (the Socratic principle)** — telematic networks and computer systems used as creative, convivial, exploratory media: instruments of critical reflection, personal development, and sustained enquiry. On this side the network is a [[Reciprocal Communication|two-way medium]] in which meaning is co-created, not a conduit for output.
- **Production (the Catonic principle)** — the same networks used "merely as tools of production," estimating everything by what it produces. On this side the network promotes, in Ascott's words, "sterility and alienation in the culture."

## Why It Matters

This is [[Roy Ascott]]'s diagnosis of the fork facing a culture that has acquired powerful telematic and computational means. The hardware is neutral — "the universal machine . . . can contain as much creative thought and express as much emotion as we put into it" — so the decisive question is the *purpose* to which the network is bent. Ascott frames the choice through two figures from antiquity: **Socrates**, who stands for critical reflection, personal development, and sustained enquiry, and **Cato**, who estimates everything by what it produces. Pointed at production, the network sterilises and alienates; pointed at enquiry, it becomes the convivial, meaning-generating medium that the whole of his telematic-art programme depends on.

What is at stake is whether the new media of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] are absorbed into a productivist logic or kept open as instruments of creativity. The polarity is most acute in *education* (the immediate subject of the source essay): Ascott insists the Socratic principles "must not be undermined in this new technological environment by the principles of Cato." Read against the rest of the vault, the warning is a sympathetic precursor to [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s critique of the productivity-driven [[Achievement Society]]: where Han diagnoses a subject that exploits itself in the name of performance, Ascott names, decades earlier, the cultural sterility produced when networks are valued only for their yield. (`Interpretive synthesis` — Ascott does not cite Han; the achievement-society resonance is a vault-internal bridge.)

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not present the poles as symmetrical or reconcilable: production is the danger and creativity the calling. There is no synthesis on offer — the tension is to be *resolved by choice*, by subordinating the Catonic principle to the Socratic one in how networks are designed, taught, and used. The polarity therefore stays open as a standing imperative rather than a paradox to be held: a telematic culture is healthy only insofar as it keeps refusing to estimate everything by what it produces. (`Strongly implied` — Ascott prescribes Socrates over Cato but does not claim the two can be fused.)
