# Prediction vs Exploration

## The Poles

- **Prediction** — the demand that a model tell us *what will happen*: a single stated future, validated by how closely its numbers match the world when the future arrives. This is the register of "an economic prediction, a corporate budget, a weather forecast, future climate change, a stockbroker saying what is going to happen." It rests on the first testing question — *Are the driving factors likely to unfold this way?* — which, Meadows insists, "can't be answered factually. It's a guess about the future, and the future is inherently uncertain."
- **Exploration** — the use of a model to see *what would happen if* a number of driving factors "unfold in a range of different ways": to run scenarios and "ask 'what if' questions." This pole is validated not by numerical accuracy but by the second and more scientific question — *If they did, would the system react this way?* — i.e., whether the model "captures the inherent dynamics of the system" and "responds with a realistic pattern of behavior."

## Why It Matters

The whole discipline of dynamic-systems modeling hangs on refusing the first pole's promise. "Dynamic systems studies usually are not designed to predict what will happen. Rather, they're designed to explore what would happen, if a number of driving factors unfold in a range of different ways." `Directly stated` Mistaking a systems model for a prediction machine is a category error: it asks the model to answer a question ("what will happen?") that no model — however good — can answer, because the answer depends on driving factors that are "a guess about the future," and "there's no way to prove you're right until the future actually happens." `Directly stated`

What is at stake is the *criterion of a model's value*. If a model is judged by prediction, it is judged by numbers — and a systems model will always look like a poor prophet, since "the numbers are off." If it is judged by exploration, it is judged by realistic behavior: whether it "responds the way a real population would, growing under the conditions when a real population would grow, declining when a real population would decline." `Directly stated` The population model Meadows uses in the [[The Systems Zoo|Systems Zoo]] is deliberately simple and its numbers are wrong, yet "the basic behavior pattern is realistic" — and that, not accuracy, is what makes it good. `Directly stated` Understanding the tension is what lets a reader use a model correctly: as an instrument for testing scenarios and understanding structure, not as an oracle. It is also what protects against a subtler failure — treating any confident scenario (a budget, a forecast) as knowledge of the future rather than as one exploration among the possible.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Donella Meadows]] in [[Thinking in Systems]] — states and decides the tension in the [[The Systems Zoo|Zoo]] chapter, and decides it for exploration. She reframes the value of a model around three "questions for testing the value of a model," of which only the second (would the system react this way?) is "more scientific... a question about how good the model is." A model earns trust not by predicting but by capturing "the inherent dynamics of the system" so that it "explore[s] possible futures and ask[s] 'what if' questions." ## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

For Meadows the tension is not held in balance so much as *reassigned to its proper tool*. Prediction and exploration are not two equal uses of a model but two different questions, only one of which a model can honestly answer. Her synthesis is a division of labor: a model cannot tell you what *will* happen (that depends on the ungovernable first question, "are the driving factors likely to unfold this way?"), but it can rigorously tell you what *would* happen if they unfolded a given way — and running many such scenarios is "usually one purpose of a systems analysis." The judgment of "which scenario, if any, should be taken seriously as a future that might really be possible" is handed back to the human: "you have to be the judge." `Directly stated`

The seam that stays open is exactly there. Exploration produces a fan of realistic possible behaviors, but choosing among them — deciding which "what if" is the likely "what will" — returns the analyst to the very prediction problem the model was built to avoid. Meadows does not pretend the model closes this gap; she insists it *shouldn't*. The residue is a discipline of humility: use models to understand structure and explore possibility, and never mistake a good exploration for a forecast. The tension is therefore productively held rather than dissolved — the two poles belong to two different acts (modeling and judging), and the error is only ever to collapse one into the other. `Interpretive synthesis`
