# Art vs Commerce

## The Poles

- **Art** — the world of learning, research, and creativity; specifically Ascott's "models of on-line collaborative creativity and learning," embodied in the non-hierarchical, bottom-up [[The Planetary Collegium]]. Art is the party with the imagination to author the coming "environmental and ecological digitalisation" of culture, but only if poets, artists, and musicians are *ready* with strategies.
- **Commerce** — "the opportunistic corporate world of commerce and business"; the big media and telecommunications corporations that have identified global education as "the next big market," treating cultural "content" merely as the "value added" component needed to secure "market share." Commerce is the default victor by inertia: if art abdicates, the politicians, merchants, and entrepreneurs simply move in.

## Why It Matters

This is the stakes-setting opposition of Ascott's late argument about who will own the digital future of education and culture. The telematic infrastructure — networks, integrated multimedia, environments that respond to body and voice — is being built either way. The open question is *whose values* will structure it: collaborative inquiry and shared creative experience, or the financial bottom line. Ascott frames this not as a balanced standoff but as a race against a head start: the corporate sector is already turning toward "global education as the next big market" (he cites figures at the 1994 Seybold Digital World Conference), while many universities are "indifferent to the telematic culture" and so cede the field. The tension matters because it specifies the *political* condition on which the redemptive promise of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] depends: their "creative potential" is not automatic but contingent on artists actively resisting market (and, elsewhere in Ascott, military) colonisation. (`Interpretive synthesis` — Ascott stages the opposition explicitly in ch. 26; the framing as a contingent race is drawn together across the chapter's passages.)

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] — In "The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-Biological Era," Ascott warns of "a completely crazy take-over of education by these commercial telecommunications industries" and answers it with [[The Planetary Collegium]] as a counter-model of "on-line collaborative creativity and learning." His closing imperative — that if "the poets, artists, and musicians of the world are not ready," then "the politicians, merchants, and entrepreneurs will" — makes the opposition a call to action rather than a lament. (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not propose a synthesis so much as a *condition for victory*: art does not negate commerce but must out-compete it on its own emergent terrain — providing models "whose originality, effectiveness, and appeal outshine the more cynical manipulations of the market." The tension is therefore left **open and contingent**, with a clear default outcome (commerce wins) unless artists act first. He even concedes the poles are already contaminated — "all too many universities and academies have themselves become big business" — so the line is not institutional (academy vs. corporation) but axiological (imagination, excellence, and insight vs. the financial bottom line). The chapter's parting formulation, that "art schools have a clear necessity to put up or shut down," registers the tension as unresolved and urgent rather than dialectically reconciled. (`Strongly implied`)
