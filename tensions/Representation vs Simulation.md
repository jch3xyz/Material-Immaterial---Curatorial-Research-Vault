# Representation vs Simulation

> **Disambiguation:** "Simulation" here is Roy Ascott's *aesthetic* sense (see [[Simulation (Ascott)]]) — the mode of telematic art, defined entirely by opposition to representation. It is DISTINCT from [[The Simulation Hypothesis]] (O'Gieblyn), the cosmological thesis that reality itself is a computer simulation. Ascott is not claiming the world is unreal; he is naming how networked art makes meaning.

## The Poles

- **Representation** — the mode of "the old order": art as fixed **object**, a fixed separation of **creator and observer**, coherence and consistency in space and time, an art of **assertion** that demands a **passive** observing subject, and which "always masks the inherent instability, the chaos, of process in the world."
- **Simulation** — the mode of [[Telematic Art]]: art as **process** and negotiation, **participation** and collaboration in place of assertion, the rendering of "what is invisible visible," and the deliberate reproduction of "the unstable, chaotic, fleeting, and numinous quality of its subject."

## Why It Matters

This is the keystone polarity of Ascott's ch.19 ("Beyond Time-Based Art: ESP, PDP, and PU") and the hinge on which his theory of networked art turns. The single sentence — "Telematic art rejects representation in favour of simulation" — names the whole stake: a shift from an art that *asserts* a coherent picture of the world to an art that *enacts* the world's actual indeterminacy. (`Directly stated`.)

What the tension makes legible is that "representation" and "simulation" are not two styles but two *ontologies of the artwork and its audience*. Representation requires a stable object, a perceiving system anchored at "a fixed point," and a subject who receives; it is bound up, for Ascott, with "an assertive (aggressive, consuming, demanding) culture." Simulation dissolves the object into a "free-flowing continuum," disperses authorship across a network, and converts the spectator into a "user" who is "neither autonomous creator nor passive viewer." (`Directly stated`.) The polarity thus carries Ascott's politics of perception: representation is, for him, a kind of falsehood — its "insistence on clarity and coherence… always masks the inherent instability, the chaos, of process in the world" — while simulation is the only mode honest to a quantum, networked, "schizo-time" reality. (`Strongly implied` — Ascott does not use the word "falsehood," but frames representation as a masking and an exhausted, "suicidal art of solipsistic inversion.")

It also clarifies what telematic art is moving *beyond*: not just representation but [[Object Art]] and time-based art alike. The tension is the art-theoretic counterpart, inside [[New Media Art]], to the wider object→system shift this vault tracks. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] (ch.19, 1990) — coins the polarity and resolves it in one direction: telematic art *is* the rejection of representation for simulation. He builds the "representation" pole as a cluster of demands (object, fixed creator/observer, coherence, assertion, passivity) and the "simulation" pole as their inverse (process, negotiation, participation, rendering the invisible/numinous, the chaotic actual).

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not hold the poles in balance — he **decides** the tension. For him representation is exhausted ("led inexorably to representations of representation itself, a suicidal art of solipsistic inversion, de constructivist postmodernism"), and simulation is the way out toward "a radically constructive art." (`Directly stated`.) So in his own text the tension is *resolved by supersession*: simulation does not negate representation so much as render it obsolete for art adequate to a networked, quantum world.

Held open against the rest of the vault, the tension is less settled. Representation's defenders would note that Ascott's "simulation" still produces images, sound, and text — it does not escape mediation, only relocates the locus of meaning from the object to the network. And the very word "simulation" carries, elsewhere in the vault, the opposite charge: where Ascott treats simulation as *more* faithful to the chaotic actual than representation, [[The Simulation Hypothesis]] and Baudrillardian usage treat the simulacrum as a *loss* of the real. The productive synthesis may be to read Ascott's polarity not as true-vs-false but as *object-fidelity vs process-fidelity* — two answers to "what should art be faithful to?" — which keeps both poles live. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
