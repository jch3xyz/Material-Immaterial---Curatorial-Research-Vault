# Numbness vs Awareness

## The Poles

- **Numbness** — the necessary narcosis by which the [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System|central nervous system]] survives its own amplification. Every technology is an [[Autoamputation|autoamputation]] of an organ, and "such amplification is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception." The numbness is not optional but physiological: "we have to numb our central nervous system when it is extended and exposed, or we will die." Its consequence is a refused self-recognition — "self-amputation forbids self-recognition" — which keeps man in the [[Narcosis|Narcissus]] role of "subliminal awareness and numbness in relation to these images of ourselves." The medium "lulls attention while the new form slams the gates of judgment and perception."
- **Awareness** — the integral consciousness that snaps the trance. It arrives along several routes: the [[Hybrid Energy|media hybrid]], whose crossing is "a moment of freedom and release from the ordinary trance and numbness imposed by them on our senses"; the prospect of a [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness|programmed consciousness]] that "cannot be numbed nor distracted by the Narcissus illusions"; the artist's [[The Artist as Integral Awareness|integral awareness]] that "can correct the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures"; and Bertrand Russell's "technique of the suspended judgment," which holds open "the possibility of rejecting the narcotic." Paradoxically, the same electric age that produces apathy also produces its antidote: it is "the age of consciousness of the unconscious."

## Why It Matters

This is the master tension that organizes McLuhan's whole physiology of media. If [[Media as Extensions of Man|every medium is an extension of man]], then every extension carries with it a narcosis — the price of bearing the amplified function is a numbing of perception to it, so that "the area of impact and incision is numb" precisely where the change is most total. This explains the central paradox of *Understanding Media*: media are "make happen" agents, "but not 'make aware' agents." The very success of an extension cancels the possibility of perceiving it — technologies "are put out long before they are thought out. In fact, their being put outside us tends to cancel the possibility of their being thought of at all" (`Directly stated`). The stakes are therefore diagnostic and almost clinical: numbness is what makes a culture a [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions|servomechanism]] of its own gadgetry, unable to recognize that it has [[Leasing the Central Nervous System|leased its central nervous system]] to commercial interests. Awareness — the artist's, the suspended judgment of the physicist, the shock of the [[Hybrid Energy|hybrid]] — is the only form of immunity McLuhan offers, and he raises it to the level of survival: "Knowledge of this simple fact is now needed for human survival" (`Directly stated`).

The tension also reframes the modern mood. McLuhan does not deny the diagnosis of an "age of anxiety" and "apathy"; he relocates it. The age of numbness is "strikingly the age of consciousness of the unconscious, in addition" — the very narcosis of the [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System|electrically extended nervous system]] has "hoicked up into full view" the subliminal life it once hid. Apathy and awareness are produced by the same electric event (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Marshall McLuhan]] in [[Understanding Media]] — the numbness pole is established in Ch. 4 ("The Gadget Lover: Narcissus as Narcosis"), where the Greek *narcosis* and [[Autoamputation|autoamputation]] are made the physiological law of all extension. The awareness pole is built across Ch. 5 (the [[Hybrid Energy|hybrid]] as "release from the ordinary trance"), Ch. 6 (the [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness|programmed un-numbable consciousness]]), and Ch. 7 (the [[The Artist as Integral Awareness|artist]] of "integral awareness" and Russell's "suspended judgment"). The opposition is then tested through Part II's case studies — print's self-protective numbing (Ch. 18), the hypnotic intensification of a single sense (Ch. 11), the literate viewer "numb and vague in the presence of film or photo" (Ch. 20), the ad as "subliminal pills for the subconscious" (Ch. 23), and the film viewer who accepts the spell "subliminally and without critical awareness" (Ch. 29).

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

McLuhan does not resolve the tension into a stable balance; he treats it as a **reversal that can be intercepted but not abolished**. Numbness is not a flaw to be corrected once and for all — it is the standing condition of any creature that extends itself, and the electric extension of the nervous system makes the narcosis total rather than partial. What he offers instead is a *practice of awareness* that can be applied at the threshold: Russell's "suspended judgment" that postpones "indefinitely the operation of inserting the new technology in the social psyche"; the artist's correction of "the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures"; the hybrid moment of "freedom and release from the ordinary trance." These are interventions, not cures. There is even a counter-current in McLuhan's own appeal to Nietzsche — "understanding stops action" — which suggests that full awareness may be paid for in the loss of the unselfconscious capacity to act (`Interpretive synthesis`). The tension is therefore held open: the electric age is simultaneously "the age of the unconscious and of apathy" and "the age of consciousness of the unconscious," and McLuhan's wager is that understanding can convert the second into a defense against the first without ever ending the narcosis itself (`Directly stated` for the two characterizations of the age; `Interpretive synthesis` for the wager).
