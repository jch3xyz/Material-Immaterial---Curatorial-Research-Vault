# Telenoia vs Paranoia

This is the organising opposition of Ascott's "Telenoia" chapter and a recurring axis across *Telematic Embrace*: **telenoia** (a networked, distributed, convivial consciousness) is set to **replace** **paranoia** (the isolated, alienated, secretive single self of "smokestack" industrial culture, with its cult of the unified, indivisible human being). Ascott coins "telenoia" from the Greek *tele* ("far off") and *nous* ("mind"): consciousness-at-large.

## The Poles

- **Telenoia** — "computer-mediated, distributed mind-at-large: asynchronous global connectivity"; the "networked consciousness of global connectivity"; the convivial, collaborative, open-ended mind of [[Network Consciousness]] released by [[The Telematic Embrace]]. It tolerates — indeed welcomes — the permeable, multiple, distributed self.
- **Paranoia** — "the anxious, alienated, secretive, and neurotically private" condition of "the old industrial culture": the cult of the individual and "the cult of the unified, indivisible human being, one body, one soul, one self." It treats the divided or distributed self as a threat to order — as lunacy or schizophrenia.

## Why It Matters

The opposition stakes Ascott's whole project: he reframes the move into networked, telematic media not as a technical or stylistic shift but as a transformation of consciousness and of the **self**. The single, bounded, secretive ego that organised industrial modernity (and that [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanism]] enshrined) is exactly what global connectivity dissolves. So the tension governs what is at stake in [[Telematic Art]], [[Reciprocal Communication]], and [[New Media Art]]: the value placed on the **distributed, multiple self** versus the **unified, indivisible** one. To read the network as merely a tool while keeping the paranoid self intact is, for Ascott, to miss the point; telenoia is the new subjectivity the medium calls for. `Strongly implied`

The pole names also reverse the usual cultural-anxiety script: where "paranoia" about surveillance and connectivity is the default critical posture (cf. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]]), Ascott makes **paranoia the disease of the old order** and connectivity-plus-**transparency** the cure — "the telenoia of universal connectivity and transparency." This is the optimistic counter-reading of the very same networked transparency that other authors in the vault read as domination. `Directly stated` (Ascott's valuation) / `Interpretive synthesis` (the contrast with other authors).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

Recurs across the "Telenoia" chapter, "The Mind of the Museum," "The Planetary Collegium," the "Technoetic Aesthetics" glossary, and "Art @ the Edge of the NET."

## Related Concepts

- [[Network Consciousness]] — telenoia is the affective/psychological name for the distributed mind Ascott elsewhere calls network consciousness.
- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the connective act that "releases feelings of solidarity and co-operation" and so moves culture from paranoia toward telenoia.
- [[Telematic Art]] — the art practice in which the distributed, multiple self is most "imaginatively explored."
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the two-way, participatory communication structure that telenoia presupposes (against the broadcast/monologue order).
- [[The Global Village]] — McLuhan's retribalised, electrically interconnected community is the precursor Ascott extends; telenoia is its psychological condition. `Interpretive synthesis`
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the "unified, indivisible human being, one body, one soul, one self" Ascott names is the same bounded subject the vault tracks as liberal humanism; telenoia dissolves it. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not hold the poles in balance — he frames telenoia as **superseding** paranoia ("set to replace," "the very opposite of," "moving from … into"), so for him the tension is directional rather than open. The unresolved residue is external to his text: the same networked **transparency** he celebrates as the end of paranoia is, for other authors in the vault, precisely the engine of a new domination (Han's [[Digital Panopticon]]; the [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Transparency vs Opacity]] axes). Whether universal connectivity yields convivial telenoia or a smoother, friendlier control is the question Ascott's optimism leaves open. `Interpretive synthesis`
