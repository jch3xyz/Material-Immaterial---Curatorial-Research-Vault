# Internal Structure vs External Event

## The Poles

- **Internal Structure ("in here")** — the cause of a system's behavior is latent within its own configuration: its stocks, its interconnections, its [[Feedback Loops|feedback loops]]. An outside force may *trigger* a behavior, but the behavior itself is a property of the structure. On this reading recessions are "inherent in the structure of the market economy," a company "creates its losses at least in part through its own business policies," and "the flu virus does not attack you; you set up the conditions for it to flourish within you." The disposition it demands is uncomfortable: "see the system as the source of its own problems, and find the courage and wisdom to restructure it."
- **External Event ("out there")** — the cause is an outside agent that can be named, blamed, and acted upon: the political leader who caused the recession, the competitor who took the market share, the OPEC nations, the virus, the drug. This is the reflex of reductive, cause-and-effect analysis — "to look for the control knob, the product, the pill, the technical fix that will make a problem go away." Meadows does not dismiss it (it "solved" smallpox and famine), but she diagnoses it as the pole we retreat to by temperament and politics rather than by evidence.

## Why It Matters

This is Meadows's foundational reorientation, and the whole systems lens turns on it. The tension is not merely epistemic (where *is* the cause?) but psychological and political (where do we *prefer* to put it?). Her key admission is that the two poles are not weighed neutrally: "Psychologically and politically we would much rather assume that the cause of a problem is 'out there,' rather than 'in here.' It's almost irresistible to blame something or someone else, to shift responsibility away from ourselves." The stakes are that this preference is *diagnostic error with consequences* — the problems most rooted in internal structure are exactly the ones that "have refused to go away" (hunger, poverty, environmental degradation, addiction, war), because we keep attacking external agents that were only triggers. The tension supplies a *test* for which pole is operative: "the same outside event applied to a different system is likely to produce a different result," and behavior that "persist[s] in a variety of circumstances" is the signature of an internal structure, not an external cause. Persistence, in short, is how you know you are on the wrong pole. *(Directly stated.)*

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Donella Meadows]] in [[Thinking in Systems]] ("Introduction: The Systems Lens") — stages the opposition with the Slinky demonstration (the identical hand-gesture bounces the spring but leaves the empty box inert, so "the answer clearly lies within the Slinky itself"), generalizes it to the "heretical" claim that "the system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior," and then names the human bias toward the external pole as the obstacle that keeps the "real messes" unsolved. *(Directly stated.)*

## Related Concepts

- [[A system causes its own behavior through its structure]] — the argument on the internal-structure pole; this tension is the framing that argument overturns the default of.
- [[Intractable problems persist because they are systems problems not external failures]] — the consequence when we stay on the external-event pole: the persistent "messes" that yield only to restructuring, not to blame.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the structural mechanism that lets a system "cause its own behavior"; feedback is what makes the internal pole causally real rather than metaphorical.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the discipline built on relocating cause from event to structure; this tension is its entry gate.
- [[Homeostasis]] / [[Emergence]] — internal-structure phenomena (self-maintenance; whole-behavior irreducible to parts) that only appear once cause is sought "in here."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Meadows does not abolish the external pole — she rebalances it. Her final position is explicitly *complementary*, not a victory for one side: "I don't think the systems way of seeing is better than the reductionist way of thinking. I think it's complementary, and therefore revealing" — one lens among the eye, the microscope, and the telescope, each showing something that "is actually there." She concedes that "an outside event may unleash" behavior (the trigger is real) and that "serious problems have been solved by focusing on external agents." So the synthesis is a division of labor keyed to *persistence*: for one-off, tractable problems the external-agent lens works; for the recurring messes that "persist in spite of the analytical ability and technical brilliance directed toward eradicating them," the internal-structure lens is required, because those problems are "intrinsically systems problems—undesirable behaviors characteristic of the system structures that produce them." The tension is therefore productively held rather than dissolved — but with a thumb on the scale, since the pull toward "out there" is a standing bias to be corrected, not a symmetric option. *(Strongly implied — Meadows states the complementarity `Directly`; the persistence-keyed division of labor is the system's reading of her examples.)*
