# Living vs Nonliving Matter

## The Poles

- **Living Matter** — organic, biological life: the animal, the organism, the conscious being, traditionally held to differ in *kind* from the inert stuff the sculptor shapes; the side the vitalist guards as instigated by a "nonmaterial center of being."
- **Nonliving Matter** — inert, mechanical, artificial substance: stone, metal, circuitry, the machine; the "dead souls made alive through 'art' and prearranged mechanized motion," traditionally barred from sensation, intelligence, or consciousness.

The tension is the boundary itself — whether the line between the living and the nonliving is a difference of *quality and kind* or only a difference of *degree*. In Jack Burnham's [[Beyond Modern Sculpture|Beyond Modern Sculpture]] (Chapter 8, "Robot and Cyborg Art") the boundary is collapsed: living and nonliving matter "differ only as 'an ascending scale of complexity in the organization of matter,'" so that "organisms artificially created may possess consciousness."

## Why It Matters

This is the ontological floor of the entire systems/cyborg argument. If the living/nonliving boundary is a difference of kind, then sculpture — inert matter — can only ever *imitate* life ("indicate, imitate, simulate"); the cybernetic organism, machine consciousness, and the cyborg are categorically impossible, and the vitalist's "few life-centered properties remain out of the reach of the scientist to recreate." If the boundary is a difference of degree — a single continuum of organizational complexity — then nothing about life is in principle off-limits to synthesis: the machine can be made to sense, to learn, to "think," and ultimately to be conscious, and sculpture can "seek its own obliteration by moving toward integration with the intelligent life forms it has always imitated." *(Interpretive synthesis grounded in directly stated passages)*

For the vault this is the grounding text for [[The Cyborg|machine life and the cyborg]]: it is the premise that makes [[Cyborg (Burnham)|Burnham's "cybernetic organism as an art form"]] more than metaphor. It is also the point where Burnham's 1968 mechanism meets the later corpus — [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] on [[Artificial Life]], [[Ray Kurzweil|Kurzweil]] on substrate-independent pattern, [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] on whether [[Emergence|complexity]] alone can yield mind — and the standing dispute over [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]]. *(Speculative connection across the corpus)*

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] (Chapter 8, "Robot and Cyborg Art") — relays the bio-electronics consensus that "there are no qualitative physical differences between living and nonliving matter," redescribes the difference as "an ascending scale of complexity in the organization of matter," and draws the consequence that "organisms artificially created may possess consciousness." The chapter stages the collapse through the cyborg ("any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism"), Grey Walter's tortoises (machines showing hunger, frustration, self-survival), and Condillac's marble statue endowed with senses, against the canvassed "vitalist hope" that some life-centered property stays beyond synthesis.

## Related Concepts

- [[Emergence]] — the contemporary scientific account the chapter leans on: life and mind as arising from the organization/complexity of matter rather than from a superadded vital principle; the "ascending scale of complexity" is an emergence claim.
- [[Vitalism]] — the position the tension's collapse negates: that "life at its core is metaphysically instigated," an "entelechy... a nonmaterial center of being making it different in quality and in kind from inorganic matter." Burnham canvasses the "vitalist hope" only to set it against the mechanist continuum.
- [[The Cyborg]] / [[Cyborg (Burnham)]] — what the collapsed boundary makes possible: "the cybernetic organism as an art form," the literal fusion of organism and machine.
- [[Artificial Life]] — the synthesis target: if matter is a single continuum, "the simulation of the structure of life literally" is in principle attainable.
- [[Mechanism (Burnham)]] — the pole the chapter sides with: "a belief in a physical basis, usually electrochemical, for all phenomena of life including human consciousness."
- [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] — the closing qualification (subjectivity and intelligence as graded outcomes of organizational degree) bears on whether consciousness is separable from intelligence.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the vault-spanning tension this is a special case of: whether mind/life is something immaterial added to matter, or a configuration *of* matter.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham presents the collapse as a *report* — "it is generally acknowledged by scientists working in the field of bio-electronics" — and hedges the consequence ("may possess consciousness"), so within the chapter the tension is dissolved in principle but left open in fact. He keeps the vitalist counterposition standing in the same pages ("there are large numbers of educated people-scientists included-who see no possibility, ever, for a machine to approach a true state of consciousness") and notes that the threshold is partly definitional — "the problem becomes one of defining what attributes one will accept as being consistent with true mental processes." James Culberson's physicalist theory of consciousness is explicitly unproven "by the construction of physical models." *(Directly stated)*

The book's closing pages even offer the inverse: the boundary may dissolve not because the nonliving can be made to live, but because life and intelligence might be coextensive — "would it not be ironic if organic life and 'intelligence' were the same thing... the outermost limits of reasoning would fall eternally within the boundaries of life." So the tension can be held three ways: collapsed upward (the nonliving achieves consciousness), collapsed downward (logic at its lowest levels is "inorganic"), or left genuinely open. Burnham's own verdict elsewhere is that the dispute "was essentially one, not of the right facts, but of the right sensibilities" — i.e., it may be undecidable on evidence. *(Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis)*
