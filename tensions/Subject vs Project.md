# Subject vs Project

## The Poles

- **Subject** — the *subjectum*, "literally, the 'one who has been cast down'." To be a subject is to be subjugated from without: ordered in the closed spaces of disciplinary confinement, moving "from one milieu of confinement to the next." Its animal is the **mole** — "a subjugated subject" and "a labourer" that "moves through predetermined spaces" and "subordinates itself to spatial restrictions."
- **Project** — the contemporary I that "do[es] not deem [itself] subjugated subject, but rather project[], always refashioning and reinventing [itself]." Its animal is the **snake** — "the animal of the neoliberal regime," "an entrepreneur" that "does not move in closed spaces" but "makes space by means of its own movement." The project deems itself free of external limitation while "subjugating itself to internal limitations and self-constraints."

## Why It Matters

This is the hinge of *Psychopolitics*. Han's diagnosis and his emancipatory turn both run through one false antithesis: the *project* presents itself as the overcoming of the *subject*, the passage from being-cast-down to free self-fashioning — and "a sense of freedom attends" that passage. The book's critical work is to show that this is not liberation but "a more efficient kind of subjectivation and subjugation": the project re-subjugates itself, now to itself, under the very affect of freedom. The opposition that *feels* like an escape from the subject-form is in fact its intensification (`Directly stated`).

What is at stake is whether emancipation can be sought *inside* this opposition at all. If the project is only a mutated subject — subjection that has learned to feel like freedom — then becoming a freer, more self-possessed "project" deepens capture rather than escaping it. This is why the same etymology that grounds the *subject* pole (*subjectum*, the cast-down) drives the book past **both** poles toward [[Beyond the Subject]]: liberation is not a better subject and not a freer project, but a mode of existence past the subject-form entirely (`Strongly implied`). Reading the tension wrong — taking *project* as the answer to *subject* — is precisely the neoliberal trap the book is built to expose.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in *Psychopolitics* — In "The Crisis of Freedom," Han stages the opposition phenomenologically: passing from subject to project is *felt* as freedom, yet this projection "amounts to a form of compulsion and constraint." In "The Mole and the Snake" he gives it Deleuze's bestiary: the disciplinary mole (subjugated subject) yields to the neoliberal snake (project), but "the passage from the mole to the snake — from subject to project — does not amount to setting out for an entirely new way of life; instead, it represents a mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is not meant to be *resolved* by choosing a pole; Han's whole argument is that the project pole is the false resolution neoliberalism already supplies. Nor is it a stable dialectical pair: "the passage from the mole to the snake" is "a mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same" — the two poles share a single substance (subjection), differing only in how subjection is administered and how it feels (`Directly stated`). The opposition is therefore productively *dissolved* rather than synthesized: the book's move is to step outside the subject/project frame altogether toward [[Beyond the Subject]], a "de-psychologized" existence that is neither cast-down subject nor self-fashioning project. Until that step is taken, the tension remains the live engine of capture — held open precisely so the reader does not mistake the snake for freedom (`Interpretive synthesis`).
