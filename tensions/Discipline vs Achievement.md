# Discipline vs Achievement

## The Poles

- **Discipline** — the regime of the [[Disciplinary Society]], governed by prohibition and the negative modal verbs *May Not* and *Should*. Its subject is the **obedience-subject** disciplined from outside by walls, commandments, and law; its negativity "produces madmen and criminals."
- **Achievement** — the regime of the [[Achievement Society]], governed by the positive modal verb *Can* and by "projects, initiatives, and motivation." Its subject is the **achievement-subject**, an "entrepreneur of itself" that exploits itself voluntarily; its positivity "creates depressives and losers."

## Why It Matters

This is Han's central periodizing claim in *The Burnout Society*: the disciplinary society Foucault described has been superseded — but **not by a society of control**. Han explicitly rejects "control society" as the name of the successor regime because it "still contains too much negativity" (`Directly stated`). The regime that supersedes discipline is **achievement**, not control. What is at stake is the diagnosis of late-modern suffering: if power still worked by prohibition, the characteristic pathologies would be transgression, hysteria, and paranoia; because power now works by limitless positivity (*Can*), the characteristic pathologies are depression, burnout, and ADHD — disorders of "excessive positivity," of "not negation so much as the inability to say no" (`Directly stated`). Mistaking the regime (reading the present through discipline, or through control) leads to misdiagnosing the violence as external/repressive when it is in fact internalized and self-generated.

Crucially, the supersession is a continuity of *productivity*, not a clean break: "the Can does not revoke the Should. The obedience-subject remains disciplined. It has now completed the disciplinary stage" (`Directly stated`). Discipline raises productivity to a limit; achievement breaks through that limit because "the positivity of Can is much more efficient than the negativity of Should."

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in [[The Burnout Society]] — stages the opposition as **obedience-subject vs achievement-subject**, **Should vs Can**, **prohibition vs positivity**, and **madmen/criminals vs depressives/losers**. The transition is also an intrapsychic restructuring: the repressive **superego** (the "dictatorial 'Thou shalt'") positivizes itself into the seductive **ego ideal** onto which the achievement-subject projects itself (`Directly stated`). Han pointedly contrasts this with the control-society reading: the successor to discipline is achievement, not [[Society of Control]].

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is **not symmetrical or static** — Han presents it as a one-directional supersession with a residue. Discipline is not abolished; it is "completed" and carried forward inside achievement (continuity of the productivity imperative), while the *mode* of power inverts from negativity to positivity. The limiting case is Melville's Bartleby: living in a society "still governed by no," he "still represents an obedience-subject" who "does not develop symptoms of depression" — the disciplinary subject seen precisely at the threshold the achievement-subject has crossed (Ch. 7, `Directly stated`).

**Flag for review — relation to [[Discipline vs Control]]:** this note is deliberately distinct from the existing `Discipline vs Control` tension (anchored in [[Psychopolitics]] and the Deleuzian society of control). That note frames the successor as *control*; *The Burnout Society* names the successor as *achievement* and explicitly rejects "control society" as still too negative. The two notes should be cross-read as two of Han's namings of the post-disciplinary regime — a possible inconsistency or a deliberate refinement across books that a future synthesis pass should adjudicate.
