# Domination vs Possibility

## The Poles

- **Domination** — seen from one vantage point, "a cyborg world is about the final imposition of a grid of control on the planet, about the final abstraction embodied in a Star Wars apocalypse waged in the name of defense, about the final appropriation of women's bodies in a masculinist orgy of war." Same technologies, read as the consummation of "the 'West's' escalating dominations of abstract individuation" — `[[The Informatics of Domination]]`.
- **Possibility** — seen from the other, the same world "might be about lived social and bodily realities in which people are not afraid of their joint kinship with animals and machines, not afraid of permanently partial identities and contradictory standpoints" — the ground of new couplings, new coalitions, and "powerful possibilities."

The crux is that these are not two worlds but **one**, read twice. For Haraway the cyborg is "the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism" *and* the most potent available myth "for resistance and recoupling." The poles are not a choice between optimism and pessimism; they are two simultaneous truths about a single configuration of `[[The Cyborg]]`, and the political task is to refuse to let either one swallow the other. (Directly stated.)

## Why It Matters

This is the manifesto's signature method, not merely one of its claims. Haraway names irony at the outset as "a rhetorical strategy and a political method": "Irony is about contradictions that do not resolve into larger wholes, even dialectically, about the tension of holding incompatible things together because both or all are necessary and true." Domination vs Possibility is that method applied to technoscience itself. (Directly stated.)

What is at stake is the cost of *single* vision. "The political struggle is to see from both perspectives at once because each reveals both dominations and possibilities unimaginable from the other vantage point. Single vision produces worse illusions than double vision or many-headed monsters." To see only domination is to be trapped in "an antiscience metaphysics, a demonology of technology"; to see only possibility is to forget that the very same machines are "a matrix of complex dominations." Double vision is therefore an epistemological and tactical requirement: it is what lets one "contest for meanings, as well as for other forms of power and pleasure in technologically mediated societies." (Directly stated; the demonology line and the matrix line are verbatim.)

The tension also reframes complicity. Haraway refuses to sort consciousness into "clear-sighted critique grounding a solid political epistemology" versus "manipulated false consciousness," asking instead for "subtle understanding of emerging pleasures, experiences, and powers with serious potential for changing the rules of the game." The point is that the analyst is "fully implicated in the world"; there is no innocent ground of pure critique outside the grid from which domination could be named without also naming a possibility. (Directly stated.)

This is why the "Cyborg Double Vision" idea is folded into the tension rather than minted as its own concept: the double vision *is* the tension held as a method, and a separate note would near-duplicate this one.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

**Held open by design — there is no synthesis, and that is the point.** Haraway is explicit that irony names "contradictions that do not resolve into larger wholes, even dialectically." She rejects the dialectical resolution by name: "dialectics too is a dream language, longing to resolve contradiction," kin to "the feminist dream of a common language" she calls "a totalizing and imperialist one." The two perspectives are not to be reconciled into a third, higher truth; they are to be inhabited at once. The closest thing to a "resolution" is the discipline of double vision itself — survival "in the diaspora," a politics of "permanently partial identities and contradictory standpoints" rather than a settled verdict on whether technology is good or bad. (Directly stated.)

This makes it the **methodological parent** of Haraway's other oppositions rather than a rival to them: it is the stance from which she refuses to collapse any of the manifesto's dualisms.

- **Distinct from [[Promise vs Peril]]** (Kurzweil): that tension is about *outcomes* — the same self-replicating technologies as both deliverance and gravest weapon, with the entanglement managed (not dissolved) by vigilance and defensive technologies. Domination vs Possibility is about *vision* — not which future arrives, but the demand to read one present from two irreconcilable standpoints at once. Kurzweil wants to keep the promise and engineer away the peril; Haraway refuses any vantage point clean enough to do that sorting. (Interpretive synthesis.)
- **Distinct from [[Freedom vs Control]]** (Han): Han's axis diagnoses a society where freedom *is* the form control takes — the two poles collapse into one another. Haraway's poles do not collapse; they stay incompatible and both true, and the work is to hold them. Where Han's tension is a trap to be exposed, Haraway's is a method to be practiced. (Interpretive synthesis.)
