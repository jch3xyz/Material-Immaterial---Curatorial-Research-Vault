# Natural vs Artificial

## The Poles

- **Natural** — that which was given, grown, biologically inherited; the body and mind as products of genetic endowment, "what made us." The pole of origin, of the organic substrate prior to technological intervention.
- **Artificial** — that which is made, engineered, technologically constructed; the body and mind as projects open to amplification, prosthesis, and redesign, "what can be made of ourselves." The pole of construction, of the technologized self.

## Why It Matters

This is a dichotomy that [[Roy Ascott]] does not so much resolve as **declare obsolete**. For most of the modern tradition the line between the natural and the artificial organized fundamental judgments — about authenticity, about the sanctity of the body, about what counts as life and what counts as machine. Ascott's wager is that the telematic, networked, nanotechnologically and robotically augmented self has dissolved that line entirely: once the self is understood as a system continuous with all the technological systems it connects to, asking which part is natural and which artificial "becomes meaningless." (`Directly stated`)

What is at stake is the entire frame within which a humanist art and ethics located value. If the distinction collapses, then the old defense of the "natural" body against technological encroachment loses its ground; the question shifts from *preserving* a given nature to *composing* one. This is why Ascott reframes the matter not as loss but as orientation: "We are only interested in what can be made of ourselves, not what made us." (`Directly stated`) The collapse is the enabling condition of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — practices premised on the artist working *with* the artificial extension of the human as a continuous field rather than against it. (`Strongly implied`)

The tension is closely entangled with — but distinct from — [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Object vs System]]. Where those stage the dissolution of the discrete object into informational systems, *Natural vs Artificial* stages the parallel dissolution of the given organism into engineered, prosthetically extended, networked systems. Ascott's "systems metaphor" is the common solvent: within it, "everything connects." (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] — declares the debate "no longer relevant"; the self as a "complex system that interconnects with all other systems" renders the natural/artificial line "meaningless," with the human "becoming, to a greater or lesser extent, bionic." Cross-links nanotechnology (Drexler) and advanced robotics (Hans Moravec) as evidence of the "interdependency and connectedness of the natural and artificial." (`Directly stated`)
- [[Donna Haraway]] in [[A Cyborg Manifesto]] — the cyborg is the figure of exactly this breakdown: among "the breached boundaries" she names the dissolution of the organism/machine boundary. Ascott's "we are all becoming bionic" converges with Haraway's claim that "the machine is us, an aspect of our embodiment." The convergence is documented in [[Roy Ascott and Donna Haraway]]. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

For Ascott the tension is **dissolved rather than held**: it is not a productive opposition to be sustained but a defunct dichotomy to be set aside. The synthesis is the **bionic self** within the systems metaphor — a self for which "natural" and "artificial" are no longer separable categories but co-extensive aspects of one connected, evolving system. (`Directly stated`)

Whether this dissolution is as complete as Ascott declares is the open question. He asserts the irrelevance of the debate; he does not argue that the underlying difference (the given vs. the made) has vanished, only that it has ceased to be *interesting* or *operative* for a network culture oriented toward self-construction. The dichotomy may thus survive as an ontological distinction even where Ascott pronounces it dead as a value-distinction. Read alongside [[Donna Haraway]], the collapse is better described as a productive *confusion* of boundaries (a stance to inhabit) than as a clean erasure — a difference of register that the [[Roy Ascott and Donna Haraway]] note holds open. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
