# Magical vs Scientific Imitation of Life

## The Poles

- **Magical imitation of life** — the copying of *external appearances*. What seems most alive is what most looks like a living being; vitality is "a function primarily of its form." This is the tradition of the sacred image, the carved or painted likeness, the figurative statue, and (in Burnham's extension) the [[The Mock Robot|mock robot]] and [[Vitalism|vitalist sculpture]] — objects that *resemble* the living without behaving as the living do.
- **Scientific imitation of life** — the concern with *performance and behavior*. Here the model is judged not by how it looks but by what it does: whether it adapts, learns, seeks goals, maintains its own stability through feedback. This is the project of [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] and, as art, of [[Cyborg Art|Cyborg Art]] — the [[The Cyborg|cybernetic organism]] that simulates the *structure* of life rather than its surface.

## Why It Matters

This distinction — which Burnham takes verbatim from the neurophysiologist Grey Walter's *The Living Brain* (1953) — is the organizing hinge of his entire history of life-simulating art. It cuts the long Western tradition of "the artist-magician . . . fabricating images" in two: everything before the cybernetic era falls on the magical side (form), and the cybernetic organism inaugurates the scientific side (behavior). *Directly stated*.

For Burnham the stakes are art-historical and almost cosmological. The magical pole names the limit of all sculpture "in principle" since "the paleolithic age": images "do not respond to man in any intelligent fashion. They are dead souls made alive through 'art' and prearranged mechanized motion." The scientific pole names what comes after — the moment sculpture stops imitating the *look* of life and begins imitating the *behavior* of life, which Burnham calls "the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture." The distinction therefore separates [[The Mock Robot|the mock robot]] (a static figure that only *simulates machineness*) from the true [[Cyborg Art|cybernetic organism]] (a system "capable of intelligent intercourse with their creators"). *Strongly implied*.

Crucially, the two poles are tied to two different criteria of *life* itself. On the magical side, the watchmaker's automaton — "a boy actually writing a letter or a girl playing a real keyboard instrument" — fails to convince because its "performance being limited to a planned series of motions" is *predictable*. On the scientific side, what reads as alive is exactly the *unpredictable*, behaviorally adaptive response of a feedback-governed system, "neither sacred nor, like life, unpredictable." Walter's distinction thus quietly relocates the criterion of "aliveness" from resemblance to responsiveness. *Directly stated*.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] (Chapter 8, "Robot and Cyborg Art") — quotes Grey Walter's "well-defined difference between the magical and the scientific imitation of life" and makes it the spine of his account of the move from automata and [[Vitalism|vitalist sculpture]] (form) to [[Cyborg Art]] (behavior). Walter himself notes that the maker of lifelike images "could be a magician, artist, or priest," and that the difficulty is "persuading the public that the behavior-simulating models created by the experimental scientist are not related to these earlier disciplines dominated by extra-logical concerns." Burnham, tellingly, refuses to take the separation entirely at face value — "We can only wonder if they are so different." *Directly stated*.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Mock Robot]] — the purest occupant of the magical pole: a sculpture that *looks* mechanical but possesses none of the goal-seeking, self-stabilizing behavior of a cybernetic organism.
- [[Cyborg Art]] — the scientific pole realized as art: the cybernetic organism that imitates performance and behavior, not appearance.
- [[Vitalism]] — the biotic-geometric sculptural impulse that "yearns" toward the organic but stays on the side of form.
- [[The Robot]] · [[The Cyborg]] — the two poles' anthropomorphic icons; Burnham argues the cyborg relationship "goes beyond the anthropomorphic anatomy of the classic robot."
- [[Cybernetics]] · [[Feedback Loops]] · [[Homeostasis]] · [[Systems Theory]] — the scientific apparatus by which behavior, not form, becomes the criterion of life.
- [[Automata]] — the historical hinge: clockwork automata mimic motion (form-side performance) but, being predictable, remain magical rather than scientific by Walter's test.
- [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] — the underlying drive that both poles serve.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham presents the two poles as a *genuine historical succession* rather than a stable opposition: the scientific imitation is what sculpture becomes once cybernetics gives it the means to imitate behavior, so the magical pole reads as the long pre-history of the scientific one. Yet he does not let the distinction harden into a clean break. His parenthetical doubt — "We can only wonder if they are so different" — keeps the tension open: the scientist reproducing life and the totem-forming animal projecting life into inert matter may share one urge under two names. Walter's own admission that the image-maker "could be a magician, artist, or priest" concedes a common ancestry. The tension is therefore best held as *productive and unresolved*: a real difference in *criterion* (appearance vs. behavior) overlaid on a possibly continuous human impulse to "create" life. *Interpretive synthesis*.
