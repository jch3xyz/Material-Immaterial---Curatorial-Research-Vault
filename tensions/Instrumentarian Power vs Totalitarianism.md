# Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism

## The Poles

- **Instrumentarian Power** — the transformation of the *market* into a project of total *certainty*. It operates through [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|behavioral modification]], not violence. It is *radically indifferent* to the soul: it wants only measurable, predictable behavior, which it tunes, herds, and conditions toward others' profitable ends. Its materialization is [[Big Other]]; its endgame is [[A Utopia of Certainty]].
- **Totalitarianism** — the transformation of the *state* into a project of total *possession*. It operates through violence and terror, and it wants the soul from the inside out — Orwell's Big Brother as "a panvasive consciousness that infects and possesses each individual soul." It seeks totality as a *political* condition.

## Why It Matters

This is Zuboff's defining and most insistent distinction: the pole-pair that keeps Big Other separate from Big Brother. She argues that critics reflexively reach for the lens of totalitarianism — "digital totalitarianism," Orwell's *1984* — to name the new menace, and that this equation *impedes* understanding and resistance (`Directly stated`, Ch. 12). Naming the difference correctly is the political stake of the whole book: if instrumentarian power is mistaken for an old, recognizable tyranny, we will fight it with the wrong weapons and fail to see what is genuinely unprecedented.

The contrast is exact along three axes:

- **What it wants.** Totalitarianism demands the soul (total possession); instrumentarianism is "profoundly and infinitely indifferent to our meanings and motives" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 12) — it wants only behavior it can render, calculate, modify, and monetize. Zuboff names this stance *radical indifference*.
- **By what means.** Totalitarianism clears its path through violence; instrumentarian power clears its path through [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|the means of behavioral modification]] and its "control over the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning in society]]" enforced by [[Big Other]] (`Directly stated`, Ch. 14).
- **Toward what totality.** Both "yearn toward totality" but in profoundly different ways — totalitarianism toward *totality as a political condition*, instrumentarianism toward *totality as a condition of market dominance* (`Directly stated`, Ch. 14). This is why Zuboff calls instrumentarian power "the precise antithesis of Orwell's Big Brother" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 12) rather than its descendant.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Shoshana Zuboff]] in [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]] — frames the entire third part of the book around this contrast, anchoring it in [[B. F. Skinner|radical behaviorism]] (instrumentarianism) versus the historical regimes of Nazi Germany and Stalin's Russia (totalitarianism). The China social-credit case is, for her, the disturbing point where the two species *fuse*: "a comprehensive fusion of instrumentarian and state power" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 13).

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The poles are held as **distinct species of power, not opposite ends of one spectrum** — Zuboff is emphatic that instrumentarianism is *unprecedented*, with "no historical precedent" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 12), so it cannot be dissolved into totalitarianism without losing what is dangerous about it. The tension is therefore productively *kept open* as a diagnostic: the value is in resisting the collapse of one pole into the other.

Yet Zuboff does not treat them as permanently sealed off. Her China analysis shows the two can converge into a single regime when instrumentarian means (economies of action, behavior modification at scale) are placed in the service of guaranteed *social* outcomes by an authoritarian state (`Strongly implied` — Zuboff presents this as one possible future, "one version of a future," not the necessary destiny of instrumentarian power). The unresolved question the tension leaves open: whether market-driven instrumentarianism and state-driven totalitarianism are stable alternatives or whether, absent countervailing democratic power, the former tends toward fusion with the latter.
