# Fantasy vs Information

## The Poles

- **Fantasy** — the imaginative faculty that lives in the *undefined*. It is negative, withdrawn, atopic: it works by privation, by what is held back and left unseen. Fantasy needs thresholds, mystery, and the negativity of absence to bring forth [[The Other]]; it is the medium of [[Eros]], the erotic detour that spares us the frontal glare of the visible.
- **Information** — high-definition positivity. Computerized imagery that eliminates vagueness and ambiguity, saturating perception with visual data. Information is immediacy without distance; as [[Positivity]] it dismantles the negativity on which the Other, and therefore fantasy, depend. Its telos is hypervisibility — the smoothed, transparent space of the society of [[Transparency]].

## Why It Matters

This is the master opposition of Han's *Fantasy* chapter, and his sharpest reversal of a commonplace: that richer, denser media *liberate* the imagination. Han argues the reverse — the higher the information density, especially in visual terms, the more fantasy is *stifled*, not freed. "New communications media do not give flight to fantasy. Their high information density … does precisely the opposite." (`Directly stated`)

What is at stake is nothing less than the survival of the erotic and of the arts. Fantasy is not a *quantity* of images but a *relation to the undefined*; it constructs the Other precisely where information is thin. Flood that space with high-definition positivity and the Other collapses into the [[Hell of the Same]] — this is why, for Han, "the contemporary crisis in literature and the arts stems from a crisis of fantasy: the disappearance of the Other. This is the agony of eros." (`Directly stated`)

Han stages the opposition against Eva Illouz. Illouz claims the premodern imagination was "information-thin," and that today's information-rich imagination, unable to idealize, produces disappointment. Han accepts none of the causal chain: there is no such thing as an "information-thick" imagination *incapable* of idealizing, because "the construction of the Other does not depend on whether more or less information is available." Only the *negativity of withdrawal* brings forth the Other. (`Directly stated`) Han relocates the true cause of contemporary disappointment from heightened fantasy to heightened *expectation* — a distinction Illouz's sociology, he charges, fails to draw. (`Directly stated`)

The tension is the visual/erotic register of Han's larger [[Positivity vs Negativity]] program, and it clarifies why [[Pornography (Han)]] is his limit-case: pornography maximizes visual information and thereby *destroys* erotic fantasy — "Hypervisibility is not conducive to imagination." (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in [[The Agony of Eros]] ("Fantasy," ch. 6) — the sole and originating treatment. Fantasy inhabits undefined space; information eliminates the vague; the two are "opposing forces." Han corroborates the reading through Flaubert (the coach-ride scene in *Madame Bovary* that "offers practically nothing to see"), Ballard's "The Gioconda of the Twilight Noon" (the blinding that trades sight for vision, read *against* Žižek's Platonic gloss), and Barthes-via-Kafka ("My stories are a way of shutting my eyes"). (`Directly stated`)

The opposition Han is answering belongs to **Eva Illouz** (*Why Love Hurts*), whose "information-thin" / "information-thick" imagination Han quotes in order to refute. See the reference note [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz]]. (`Directly stated`)

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Han holds the poles as a strict opposition, not a dialectic to be reconciled: "Information and fantasy are opposing forces." There is no synthesis on offer and, on Han's terms, none is possible — an "information-thick" imagination is a contradiction, since fantasy is constituted by the very negativity information erases. (`Directly stated`)

If there is a way to hold the tension productively, it is *counter-cyclical* rather than synthetic: the artist's deliberate cultivation of visual privation. Han's exemplary cases — Flaubert withholding what the lovers do, Ballard's protagonist destroying his eyes "in order to see more," Kafka's shutting of the eyes — are all techniques for *subtracting* information to restore the space of fantasy. The synthesis, such as it is, is negative: not more images but the reintroduction of the threshold, the veil, the closed eye. (`Interpretive synthesis`) Whether that gesture can survive a culture of "compulsive hypervigilance," where "the rapid succession of images leaves no time" to close one's eyes, Han leaves open — and pessimistic. (`Strongly implied`)
