# Determinism vs Randomness

## The Poles

- **Determinism** — strictly determinate kinetics: detailed, precise, fully programmed motion, the artist controlling "motion and power in a determinate fashion" (the engineering ideal of the [[The Closed Kinematic Chain|closed kinematic chain]]). It promises rich, structured, repeatable sequence — but demands engineering ability most artists lack, and tends to produce either cumbersome machinery or "simplistic motion patterns which do not sustain interest."
- **Randomness** — random, pseudo-random, aleatoric, or statistical field motion: the artist lets chance "run its course," structuring a homogeneous field of small unstable events given "a statistical appearance of semi-order." It is technically cheap and theoretically fashionable (the postwar musical avant-garde), and is legitimated by recasting the "statistical laws of nature" as the work's "programmer."

## Why It Matters

This is, for [[Jack Burnham]] in *[[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]*, the central unsolved problem of programmed art — the practical hinge on which the whole [[Kinetic Art|kinetic]] / [[The New Tendency and Field Kinetics|New Tendency]] project turns. The decision between a determinate program and a random one is never purely aesthetic: it is forced by the gap between artistic ambition and engineering competence. "A fully deterministic control, in fact any kind of detailed and precise programming, demands more engineering ability from artists than a decision to let randomness run its course" — so the technical poverty of the kineticists, reinforced by the contemporaneous vogue for aleatoric music, pushes the movement toward chance. (`Directly stated`)

The tension also exposes a sleight of hand in the word *programmed* itself. The Olivetti-sponsored "Programmed Art" exhibition (1962–65) used the term, but "it has been devoted mainly to aleatoric techniques"; to make this consistent, "a little bit of word juggling has been necessary." Burnham's evidence is Umberto Eco, who in his introduction "defined the word programmed so that we can accept the statistical laws of nature as a legitimate 'programmer.'" Randomness, in other words, is rebranded as program — chance laundered into law. This matters for the vault's larger axis because the substitution of statistical field motion for determinate mechanism is exactly the move that lets [[Kinetic Art]] dissolve the inert object into a fluid energy field — the kinetic route to [[Material vs Immaterial|immateriality]]. (`Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis` for the immateriality link)

Understanding the tension blocks a common misreading: that "programmed art" was a triumph of cybernetic control. Burnham shows the opposite — that determinate control was mostly beyond reach, that "deterministic Kinetics either produce simplistic motion patterns which do not sustain interest... or they are reduced to a mesmerizing effect such as light dancing across a body of water," and that the field/raster was the device by which "the impasse of stagnant, deterministic mechanical motion was given new life and meaning" precisely by going statistical. (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in *[[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]*, ch. 6 ("Kineticism: The Unrequited Art") — names the problem outright: strict determinate programming demands engineering most artists lack, so "much New Tendency Kineticism has placed both a practical and theoretical reliance on random or aleatoric techniques." He maps the spectrum from "strict aleatoric processes" through serial/mathematically-determined techniques, and reports Eco's redefinition of *programmed* to admit "the statistical laws of nature as a legitimate 'programmer.'" Julio Le Parc's series is literally titled *Determinism and Indeterminism*; the G.R.A.V.'s "General Propositions" call "[t]o state the existence of indeterminate phenomena in the structure and visual reality of the work."
- [[Jack Burnham]] in *[[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]*, ch. 7 ("Light as Sculpture Medium") — restates the poles for light: for Nicolas Schöffer (rendered "Schaffer" in the source's OCR), "his vision of space-time aesthetics is based on the statistical leveling-off of random phenomena," and "the indeterminacy of unstructured light sequences merged with revolving sculptures is regarded by the artist as being superior to a repetitive determinate program." Schöffer's resolving figure is **continuous discontinuity**: light programs that "go on indefinitely, never quite the same but always appearing the same."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham presents the tension as **unresolved in practice** but with two attempted mediations. The first is **conceptual**: Eco's redefinition of *programmed* to admit the "statistical laws of nature" as programmer dissolves the opposition by definition — but Burnham treats this as "word juggling," a rationalization of technical inability rather than a genuine reconciliation. The second is **formal**: Schöffer's *continuous discontinuity* and the permutational program (a "very long nonrepetitive cycle from a set of much shorter sequences") hold both poles at once — determinate sub-programs whose combination is effectively unpredictable, "never quite the same but always appearing the same." Yet even here Burnham is sceptical that the result is "that much further toward the twenty-first century." The deeper diagnosis is structural: determinate kinetics is dull or unbuildable, pure randomness "the least causal and directed motion for any observer" (it produces "the same statistical leveling-off as the random noise on an unused television channel"), so interest must be smuggled back in through the active spectator's own movement. The tension therefore stays open — a real, unsolved engineering-and-aesthetic problem that, for Burnham, marks kineticism as "The Unrequited Art." (`Interpretive synthesis`, on `Directly stated` passages)
