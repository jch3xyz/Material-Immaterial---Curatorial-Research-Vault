# Citizen vs User

## The Poles

- **Citizen** — A member of a self-governing public: a bearer of [[Decision Rights]] who can deliberate, contest, withhold consent, and "try to reclaim" the authority over their own lives. The citizen is *informed* in order to govern, and is therefore a potential source of resistance, regulation, and refusal.
- **User** — Google's "ideal society": "a population of distant users, not a citizenry." The user is empowered — but "not too much"; informed — but "only in the ways that the corporation chooses." The telos of the user is to be "docile, harmonious, and, above all, grateful." Where the citizen holds decision rights, the user has had them quietly pilfered.

## Why It Matters

This tension names the *political form* that surveillance capitalism prefers its subjects to take. It is not a tension between two technologies or two products, but between two models of the person and two distributions of power. Zuboff states it flatly as the firm's own social ideal: "Google's ideal society is a population of distant users, not a citizenry" (`Directly stated`). The substitution of "user" for "citizen" is the substitution of a managed population for a self-governing public.

The deeper stake sits inside the word "empower." The empowerment-rhetoric is genuine but rationed: "it's great to empower people, but not too much, lest they notice the pilfering of their decision rights and try to reclaim them." Empowerment is permitted precisely up to — and not past — the threshold of *noticing*. This is the affective and epistemic mechanism by which a citizenry is converted into a user-base: people are "enable[d]... to make better choices, but not if those choices impede Google's own imperatives." The user is the citizen with the governing function amputated and the gratitude function installed (`Directly stated`).

This is why the tension is the political face of two others. Against [[Empowerment vs Diminishment]], the citizen/user split is the *governance* axis of the same amalgam: the diminishment that the empowerment conceals is, specifically, "the pilfering of their decision rights." And against [[The Coup from Above]], the production of users-not-citizens is the coup's social precondition — a population that has ceded sovereignty without registering the cession cannot mount the popular response that would constrain surveillance capitalism's logic of accumulation (`Interpretive synthesis`).

Held against the rest of the corpus, the user-not-citizen completes a recurring figure. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and Harari's [[Dataism]] describe subjects who volunteer their own exposure; Zuboff sharpens the point politically: the volunteer is not merely an exposed subject but a *de-citizened* one, "informed... only in the ways that the corporation chooses," whose informedness is engineered to be loyal rather than sovereign (`Speculative connection`).

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

For Zuboff the tension is not a stable equilibrium but a *directional* substitution: surveillance capitalism is actively converting citizens into users, and the conversion is engineered to be unfelt — empowerment stops short of the point at which citizens "notice the pilfering of their decision rights and try to reclaim them." The synthesis Zuboff gestures toward is therefore not a middle term but a *reversal*: the reclamation of decision rights is exactly the act by which a user re-becomes a citizen. Because she frames the loss of decision rights as a contingent product of an unconstrained logic of accumulation rather than a feature of the digital as such (see [[Empowerment vs Diminishment]], [[The Coup from Above]]), the tension is in principle dissolvable in the citizen's favor — through "noticing," contestation, and democratic constraint. Absent that response, the docile, grateful user is the default settlement, and the citizenry it displaced does not get a vote on its own displacement (`Interpretive synthesis`).
