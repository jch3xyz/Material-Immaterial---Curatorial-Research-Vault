# Discipline vs Control

## General Orientation

This note carries two distinct, mildly conflicting periodizations of the post-disciplinary present, both authored by [[Byung-Chul Han]] across two books — read them as different framings of the same hinge, not as one settled thesis.

- **The Deleuzean framing (Discipline → Control), from *Psychopolitics*.** Here Han adopts Deleuze's "Postscript on the Societies of Control": disciplinary society (the mole, closed space, the body) has *yielded* to a neoliberal **control** society (the snake, open self-made space, the psyche). This is the framing the body of this note develops. `Directly stated`
- **The Burnout framing (Discipline → Achievement), from *The Burnout Society*.** There Han explicitly *rejects* the term "control society" as still too negative and names **achievement** ([[Achievement Society]]) — not control — as the successor regime. By that framing the correct successor tension is [[Discipline vs Achievement]], not Discipline vs Control. `Directly stated`

The friction is real but narrow: it concerns what to *call* the successor and which negation it carries, not whether a break from discipline occurred. *The Burnout Society* (2010) predates *Psychopolitics* (2014); the later book's embrace of Deleuze's vocabulary is a shift in framing, while the underlying diagnosis — positivity, auto-exploitation, the self-exploiting [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] — is continuous across both. The Burnout-specific objection is recorded below and flagged for review. `Interpretive synthesis`

## The Poles

- **Disciplinary regime** — power that operates through milieus of confinement (family, school, prison, barracks, hospital, factory), in closed and rigid space. Its animal is the mole, which moves through predetermined spaces and subordinates itself to spatial restrictions. It works by negativity: norms, commandments, prohibitions, coercion, training and drills (*Abrichtung*), a "concerted orthopaedy" that fashions the body into a machine. Its emblematic technique is shock — violent, paralyzing, annihilating. Han, following Foucault, periodizes it as biopolitical: it administers bodies and populations, but has no access to the psyche.
- **Neoliberal control regime** — power that operates in open, borderless space adapted to "post-industrial, immaterial and networked forms of production." Its animal is the snake, which does not move in closed space but *makes* space through its own movement. It works by positivity: it seduces rather than coerces, flatters rather than shocks, enlists *Liking*, and modulates desires instead of repressing them. Han names its governmental form psychopolitics: it exploits the psyche and the soul, not the body.

## Why It Matters

This tension periodizes the two power-forms and supplies the historical hinge of *Psychopolitics*. It is the distinction Han borrows from Deleuze's "Postscript on the Societies of Control" and reworks: a "general crisis affecting all milieus of confinement" forces the closed disciplinary order to yield to an open control order. Naming the passage correctly is what lets Han diagnose the present — for him, the contemporary regime is *not* disciplinary, so critiques that still target confinement, coercion and shock (he means Naomi Klein) misread the actual mechanism, which is positive, seductive and pre-emptive. `Directly stated`

It is distinct from the [[Freedom vs Control]] tension. That tension Han *dissolves*: under neoliberalism freedom and control coincide, because the subject exploits itself in the name of freedom. *This* tension is not dissolved but *historical* — it marks a real before-and-after (mole → snake, body → soul, negativity → positivity), a "mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same." The two power-forms are genuinely opposed in their technique even though they serve the continuous logic of capital. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in *Psychopolitics* — stages the opposition across three chapters: the mole and the snake (ch. 3), discipline as biopolitics over the body (ch. 4), and shock as a strictly disciplinary, not neoliberal, technique (ch. 7).
- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in *The Burnout Society* — names a *different* successor. He rejects "control society" as carrying too much negativity and periodizes the present as Discipline → **Achievement** ([[Achievement Society]]), reading depression, not docility, as the late-modern symptom. See the section below and the [[Discipline vs Achievement]] tension.
- [[Gilles Deleuze]] — Han draws the periodization directly from Deleuze's "Postscript on the Societies of Control," which "diagnoses a general crisis affecting all milieus of confinement." `Directly stated`

## Related Concepts

- [[Society of Control]] — the open, borderless regime named by Deleuze and adopted by Han as the present power-form.
- [[Psychopolitics]] — the governmental technology proper to the control regime; it exploits the psyche where biopolitics could only administer bodies.
- [[Biopolitics]] — "the governmental technology of disciplinary power"; it makes use of population statistics but "has no access to the psychic realm."
- [[Disciplinary Society]] — Foucault's milieu-of-confinement order, which Han says has "yielded" to neoliberal control society.
- [[Positivity]] / [[Negativity]] — the technique-axis along which the two regimes divide: shock's "essential trait is negativity," whereas "neoliberal psychopolitics is dominated by positivity."

## The Burnout Society (Han) — achievement, not control, as the successor

In *The Burnout Society* (2010) Han stages the same break from discipline but draws the line elsewhere, and in doing so he explicitly *declines* the very term this note is built around. He grants that the present is "no longer a disciplinary society," but adds: "Nor does the commonly employed concept of 'control society' do justice to this change. It still contains too much negativity." The successor he names is not control but **[[Achievement Society]]** ([Leistungsgesellschaft]) — its inhabitants "are no longer 'obedience-subjects' but 'achievement-subjects.' They are entrepreneurs of themselves." The diagnostic axis is the same [[Positivity]]/[[Negativity]] one as in *Psychopolitics* — "Disciplinary society is a society of negativity. … Unlimited Can is the positive modal verb of achievement society" — but here positivity disqualifies "control" as a name, where the later book will reclaim Deleuze's "control" precisely as the open, positive regime. `Directly stated`

The criterion of the break is pathological, not architectural. Discipline "creates depressives and losers" only after it has become achievement; "Its negativity produces madmen and criminals. In contrast, achievement society creates depressives and losers." Han uses the Bartleby case as the test: Melville's scrivener "still represents an obedience-subject. He does not develop symptoms of depression, which is a hallmark of late-modern achievement society." Bartleby belongs to discipline — walls, partitions, the "Tombs" — and so cannot be the figure of the present; the present's subject is the one who exhausts itself under "Unlimited Can." Continuity across the break is conceded — "no break exists between Should and Can; continuity prevails" in the drive to maximize production — which is the same "mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism" claim the *Psychopolitics* framing makes, only routed through achievement rather than control. `Directly stated`

This is the friction the kickoff flagged, and it is recorded here rather than resolved: the periodization in the body of this note (Discipline → Control, after Deleuze) **diverges in name** from *The Burnout Society*'s Discipline → **Achievement**, because the earlier book treats "control" as still too negative to name a regime defined by positivity. The Burnout-Society-specific framing has its own tension note, [[Discipline vs Achievement]]; this note's authors and sources now span both books so the divergence is visible in one place. Flag in review: confirm the cross-link to [[Discipline vs Achievement]] and whether the two tension notes should be merged or kept as deliberately distinct framings. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is **historical, not dialectical**: Han does not hold the two poles together so much as periodize them, asserting that disciplinary power "has yielded" to neoliberal control. Yet the two do not cleanly succeed one another. Han insists the passage is "a mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same," so the same capital persists across both power-forms; and his reading of shock therapy as "a genuinely disciplinary technique" implies the disciplinary repertoire survives *within* the neoliberal present even as he denies it is the dominant mechanism. The unresolved edge is therefore whether discipline is truly past or merely subordinated — Han's argument needs it gone (so that the neoliberal regime can be diagnosed as positive and seductive) while his examples keep it alive. `Interpretive synthesis`
