# Regeneration vs Rebirth

## The Poles

- **Regeneration** — reconstitution after injury without return to an original whole: the regrowth of structure and function at the site of damage, carrying "the constant possibility of twinning or other odd topographical productions," so that what comes back may be "monstrous, duplicated, potent." It is partial, ironic, and non-innocent; it does not pass through reproductive sex or the demand for flawlessness. The cyborg's mode.
- **Rebirth** — the organicist/holistic mode that depends on "metaphors of rebirth" and "invariably call[s] on the resources of reproductive sex": the founding myth of original wholeness, the return to a prelapsarian unity, "rebirth without flaw, perfection, abstraction." It is the goddess's mode, the reproductive matrix, the dream of being made whole again.

## Why It Matters

This tension is the affective and political hinge of the *Cyborg Manifesto*'s conclusion. For [[Donna Haraway]], the choice between regeneration and rebirth is the choice between two opposed structures of feeling about damage, identity, and the body. Rebirth is bound to "a reproductive politics — rebirth without flaw, perfection, abstraction" and to the longing for "the return to wholeness"; it underwrites origin stories, the Garden, the Fall, and the apocalyptic "return to a deathly oneness." Regeneration accepts injury as the starting condition and refuses both flawlessness and the return to an original unity — it is how the cyborg, "not of Woman born," reconstitutes itself without the founding myth of wholeness (`Interpretive synthesis`).

What is at stake is whether feminist and emancipatory politics must be grounded in organic holism, the privileged closeness to nature, and the reproductive matrix — or whether they can instead be grounded in partial, technologically entangled, monstrous reconstitution. Holding the salamander's regrown limb (monstrous, potent) against the demand for a flawless rebirth lets Haraway value technology, partiality, and "illegitimate fusions" without nostalgia for an organic Eden. To choose regeneration is to make injury survivable on terms other than purity (`Strongly implied`). This opposition is the conceptual twin of [[Cyborg vs Goddess]]: the goddess is the figure of rebirth and the reproductive matrix; the cyborg is the figure of regeneration.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Haraway does not hold the poles in balance — she chooses. The tension is asymmetrically resolved: regeneration is affirmed and rebirth is rejected, just as in the closing slogan the cyborg is preferred to the goddess. Yet the resolution is itself unstable rather than triumphant: the regenerated limb is "monstrous, duplicated, potent" — restoration without guarantee of fidelity or wholeness — and the affirmation rests on the prior admission of universal injury ("We have all been injured, profoundly"). What is dissolved is not the difference between the poles but the *value* attached to wholeness: regeneration refuses the demand that reconstitution end in a flawless, unified, reborn self. The tension stays productively open as the difference between two political imaginaries — the partial and the holistic — that Haraway insists remain "bound in the spiral dance" even as she takes a side (`Interpretive synthesis`).
