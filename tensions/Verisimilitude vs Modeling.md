# Verisimilitude vs Modeling

## The Poles

- **Verisimilitude (truth to appearance)** — the photographic ideal that an image is judged by its fidelity to a pre-existing real; the "lust for verisimilitude." This pole carries the whole documentary apparatus of the photograph as evidence, and with it the anxiety about retouching, "faking," and the betrayal of the real. It presupposes a world that the image either records faithfully or falsifies.
- **Modeling (construction of realities)** — the post-photographic disposition in which the image is no longer a faithful record but a constructed datum, one move in the building of "a reality, of multiple realities." Truth-to-appearance is displaced by the "love of modeling": the participating viewer and artist construct frameworks "from which image worlds can emerge."

## Why It Matters

Ascott uses this polarity to **dissolve**, rather than adjudicate, the long-running documentary-truth debate — the worry, native to analogue photography, about retouching, manipulation, and whether a digital image can be trusted. That debate is staged on the verisimilitude pole: it assumes a real that the photograph is obliged to match. Ascott's move is to deny the premise. If "reality is built by us," then asking whether an image is true *to appearance* is the wrong question; the live question becomes how realities are *constructed*, and how the image participates in building them. `Directly stated`

What is at stake is the status of the photograph as proof. Once verisimilitude gives way to modeling, the photograph stops being measured against an external real and becomes instead an instrument for constructing realities — the epistemological hinge of Ascott's `Radical Constructivism (Ascott)`. This is why the tension is not a contest with a winner: Ascott does not say modeling is *more accurate*; he says the appearance/truth frame is obsolete, replaced by a frame in which construction, not correspondence, is the operative value. `Strongly implied`

The polarity also reroutes attention from content to operation: "our interest nowadays is less in what photographs mean than in how they mean" — a shift from the photographed object to the process of its construction. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] (ch. 21, "Photography at the Interface," 1992) — Ascott frames the tension as a historical passage: classical photography pursued verisimilitude; "post-photographic practice" abandons it for modeling. He grounds the modeling pole in radical constructivism (Paul Watzlawick) and an anti-foundationalist register he borrows from Richard Rorty ("contingency, irony, and solidarity"), and he periodizes the whole shift as moving "from the light and shade of the mimetic, through the trough of deconstruction, towards the radiance of a radical constructivism." The viewer is recruited into the modeling — admitted "as participating viewers, into the process of construction." `Directly stated`

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is best read as **dissolved rather than balanced**. Ascott does not hold verisimilitude and modeling in productive equilibrium; he declares the verisimilitude frame — the truth/faking debate — obsolete, and lets it be "absorbed within the digital discourse." Yet he explicitly resists a clean either/or: "it would be false to set up the different aspects of photography, digital or analogue, in complete and total opposition… It's simultaneously a case of both 'both/and' as well as 'either/or.'" `Directly stated` So the synthesis is asymmetrical: the analogue/verisimilitude pole survives, but only as a deployable strategy *inside* a constructivist field that has reframed what truth and the real even mean. Modeling does not defeat verisimilitude on its own terms; it changes the terms. The documentary-truth question is not answered — it is retired.
