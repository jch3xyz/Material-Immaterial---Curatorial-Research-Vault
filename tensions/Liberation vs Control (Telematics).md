# Liberation vs Control (Telematics)

> Disambiguation: this is the **media / network-technology** version of the freedom-vs-power question — will computer-mediated telecommunications *liberate* or *dominate*? The canonical suffix `(Telematics)` marks it as distinct from (and cross-linked to) Han's [[Freedom vs Control]], which is about neoliberal subjectivity rather than the network medium as such. It is also distinct from the Deleuzean [[Society of Control]] and from [[Discipline vs Control]].

## The Poles

- **Liberation** — telematic networks "enhance adaptability, freedom, and communication," let "every citizen and every group" be responsible for itself, and emancipate art and mind from the unique object, the single location, and the solitary author. The medium is multidirectional, decentralized, and participatory.
- **Control** — the very same networks can "reinforce the mechanisms of rigidity, authority, and domination": governments and "controlling institutions" monitor access strictly to control constituencies; the network's military pedigree (ARPANET, the Cold War radar screen) and its capacity for surveillance can turn the connective medium into an instrument of power.

## Why It Matters

The tension stages the **political ambivalence of telematic technology**. The same infrastructure — computer-mediated communications networking — is the condition both for the utopian [[Network Consciousness]] Ascott proposes and for a pervasive apparatus of monitoring and command. The question is not which the technology *is*, but which it will be *made* to be. This is why Ascott folds the medium into a moral and aesthetic mission rather than a technical given: the artist's task is to envision and prototype the liberatory scenario so that the instruments of control are turned toward "collaboration" and "more harmonious planetary relations."

For the vault, this is the media/network-technology counterpart to Han's neoliberal [[Freedom vs Control]] and the surveillance-side [[Digital Panopticon]]. Where Han argues that *freedom itself* becomes coercive, the telematic framing keeps the fork genuinely open — control and liberation are rival uses of one neutral-seeming medium, decided by who builds and governs it (`Interpretive synthesis`). The framing descends, via the volume's editor, from Simon Nora and Alain Minc, who coined *télématique* (1978) and posed the fork as a policy choice about centralization versus decentralization.

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

In Shanken's editorial introduction (ch. 04, "From Cybernetics to Telematics"), the political ambivalence of the network medium is posed most sharply through **Simon Nora and Alain Minc**, the French inspectors who coined the term *télématique*. Shanken reports that they "were keenly aware of the desire of governments and other powerful interests to monitor access to technologies strictly in order to control constituencies," and then quotes their fork directly: a society that uses the technology "to reinforce the mechanisms of rigidity, authority, and domination" versus one that knows "how to enhance adaptability, freedom, and communication" so that "every citizen and every group can be responsible for itself" (`Directly stated`).

The **liberation** pole is dramatized by **Nam June Paik's** "Good Morning Mr. Orwell" (a satellite telecast on New Year's Day, 1984), staged as "a liberatory and multidirectional alternative to the threat posed by 'Big Brother' surveillance of the kind that George Orwell had warned of." Paik's own line — "I see video not as a dictatorial medium but as a liberating one" — is the surveillance-skeptic counter-symbol, asserting that the *same* broadcast medium Orwell feared can be inverted into a two-way, emancipatory one (`Directly stated`). This connects directly to the surveillance-side note [[Digital Panopticon]] and to the McLuhanite frame of the [[The Global Village]].

The **control** pole is not external to Ascott's vision but internal to it. Ascott "tempered this utopian vision" by citing Foucault's *L'Ordre du discours* on "the inextricability of texts and meaning from the institutional powers" they reflect, warning that "in the interwoven and shared text of telematics . . . meaning is negotiated—but it too can be the object of desire. . . . We can expect a growing . . . interest in telematics on the part of controlling institutions" (`Directly stated`, Ascott via Shanken). Shanken adds the biographical key: Ascott's "service as a radar officer in the mid 1950s, at the height of the Cold War" sharpened his "mindfulness of the potency of communications and surveillance technologies," and motivated his "search for alternative scenarios in which these instruments of control promote collaboration" (`Directly stated`).

The decisive feature of Ascott's position is that he keeps the fork **open** rather than collapsing it to either determinism. The medium is "double-edged" — Shanken's gloss on cyberspace as "a double-edged sword that reproduces the physical world, simultaneously intensifying and dematerializing it" — so the liberatory outcome is an *aesthetic and ethical task*, "a self-defined creative and ethical mission to formulate constructive visions of the future" (`Strongly implied` for the synthesis; the quoted phrases are `Directly stated`). The liberatory pole is the project of [[Telematic Art]] and [[Network Consciousness]]; the controlling pole is the diagnosis those projects are built against. This is the [[Decentralization]] vs centralization fork Nora and Minc posed, recast as an artistic program.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is held **open, not resolved** — and Ascott's contribution is precisely to insist that it *cannot* be resolved by the technology itself. Because telematics is a "double-edged sword," its political valence is underdetermined by its hardware; whether the network becomes an "embrace" or a panopticon is a matter of who governs access and to what ends (Nora and Minc's centralization-vs-decentralization fork). Ascott's practical answer is not a proof that liberation will win but a *wager* on it: build the liberatory, decentralized, participatory forms ([[Telematic Art]], [[Network Consciousness]], [[Reciprocal Communication]]) so as to occupy and re-purpose the medium before "controlling institutions" do. Read against Han's [[Freedom vs Control]] and the [[Digital Panopticon]], Ascott's optimism looks contestable — the network that promises the embrace is the same one that affords the surveillance — and Shanken stages exactly this skeptical counter (the medium's military pedigree, the institutions' "growing interest"). The vault therefore holds this as an **unresolved, productively open** tension: a genuine fork, not a dialectical synthesis (`Interpretive synthesis`).
