# Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman

## The Poles

- **[[The Liberal Humanist Subject]]** — the Enlightenment model of the human as an autonomous, rational, self-possessing, free-willed individual, located in the mind and bounded by the skin; [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s named "reference point for the human."
- **[[Posthumanism]]** — the cybernetic amalgam in which the self is a [[Distributed Cognition|distributed cognitive system]] spliced through [[Feedback Loops|feedback loops]] into its environment and "seamlessly articulated with intelligent machines," its boundaries "constructed rather than given."

This is the *dynamic* between the two concept notes, not either pole on its own. Hayles tells the opposition as a historical succession — "transforming the liberal subject, regarded as the model of the human since the Enlightenment, into the posthuman" — but the interest of the tension lies in how it refuses to resolve into clean rupture.

## Why It Matters

At first glance the poles look like simple opposites: the bounded, self-owning individual versus the porous, networked amalgam; autonomous will versus emergent process; the man who ends at his skin versus the cyborg whose blind man's cane is "part of the man." Reading the opposition this way authorizes a familiar story of supersession in which the posthuman simply leaves the liberal subject behind.

Hayles's intervention is to complicate that story with three claims that the concept notes for either pole cannot, on their own, hold:

1. **The two share the erasure of [[Embodiment]].** What looks like the deepest opposition is in fact a shared inheritance. "Identified with the rational mind, the liberal subject possessed a body but was not usually represented as being a body." When the cybernetic posthuman recodes the body as informational pattern — Gibson's "data made flesh" ([[The Body as Data]]) — it "continues the liberal tradition rather than disrupts it." The successor inherits the predecessor's contempt for the [[Material vs Immaterial|material]] body.

2. **The author of the dissolution recoiled to protect the very subject it dissolved.** [[Norbert Wiener]] built the [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] mirror that shows the human as a control-communication device "differing in no substantial regard from our mechanical siblings" — yet, glancing over his shoulder and seeing himself as a cyborg, he "makes a horrified withdrawal." He wanted the cybernetic machine "designed so that it did not threaten the autonomous, selfregulating subject of liberal humanism," to *extend* that self "into the realm of the machine." The cybernetic logic undermined the liberal subjectivity its own father meant to preserve.

3. **The lethal thing is the graft, not the posthuman as such.** "What is lethal is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self." When [[Hans Moravec]] imagines "you" downloading yourself into a computer for immortality, he "is not abandoning the autonomous liberal subject but is expanding its perogatives into the realm of the posthuman." The danger is not the dissolution of the bounded subject but the bounded subject's autonomy and mastery surviving the dissolution and being projected, disembodied, into the machine.

Understanding the tension this way blocks two errors: treating the posthuman as a triumphant break from humanism (it secretly carries humanism's worst assumption), and treating it as simple apocalypse (its lethality is contingent on the graft, which can be refused).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[N. Katherine Hayles]] in [[How We Became Posthuman]] — the primary and structuring source for this tension. Hayles stages the succession from liberal subject to posthuman in the [[The Turing Test as a Magic Trick|Turing test]] of the prologue, narrates Wiener's ambivalent recoil in the chapter on cybernetic anxiety, and delivers the "lethal graft" diagnosis in the conclusion. Her distinctive move is the **continuity thesis**: the opposition is "supersession-with-inheritance," not clean rupture, because both poles erase [[Embodiment]].
- [[Norbert Wiener]] (via Hayles) in *Cybernetics* and *The Human Use of Human Beings* — the historical figure in whom the tension is enacted as biography: he fashioned "human and machine alike in the image of an autonomous, self-directed individual," then withdrew when the cybernetic dissolution of boundaries threatened to dissolve that individual.
- [[Hans Moravec]] (via Hayles) — the antagonist whose download fantasy is Hayles's exhibit of the lethal graft: liberal autonomy and the "ultimate privilege" of mastery, smuggled intact into the posthuman.
- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in *The Transparency Society* — *(interpretive)* a thematic, not textual, contrast. Han's account of the [[Hell of the Same|hell of the same]] and the dissolution of [[Negativity]] in the transparency society describes a different route to a comparable end — the smoothing-away of the bounded, opaque interior self under regimes of [[Information]] and visibility. Han does not engage Hayles, cybernetics, or the liberal/posthuman frame; the resonance is curatorial, marked here as a [[Material vs Immaterial|material/immaterial]] family resemblance rather than a shared argument. Label: `Speculative connection`.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

The tension is **productively held, not dissolved** — and Hayles wants it held open precisely so that the graft can be refused. Two unsatisfactory resolutions are available and both are foreclosed in the text. One can *recuperate* the posthuman back into liberal humanism (Moravec's download, autonomy extended into immortality) — this is the lethal graft. One can read the posthuman as *antihuman* apocalypse (Moravec and McCulloch's "machines take over") — this is the terror Hayles works to defuse. "Yet the posthuman need not be recuperated back into liberal humanism, nor need it be construed as antihuman."

Hayles's own path is a third option that does not so much synthesize the poles as **re-ground the second one**: "Located within the dialectic of pattern/randomness and grounded in embodied actuality rather than disembodied information, the posthuman offers resources for rethinking the articulation of humans with intelligent machines." In this *benign* posthuman, "emergence replaces teleology; reflexive epistemology replaces objectivism; distributed cognition replaces autonomous will; embodiment replaces a body seen as a support system for the mind." The liberal subject is not mourned — "I do not mourn the passing of a concept so deeply entwined with projects of domination and oppression" — but its erasure of the body must not be allowed to survive its passing. The tension therefore remains **open by design**: "the question is not whether we will become posthuman ... Rather, the question is what kind of posthumans we will be," and "the best possible time to contest for what the posthuman means is now."
