# Representation vs Virtualisation

[[Roy Ascott]]'s art-historical restatement of his governing turn, drawn from "Back to Nature II" (ch.28). Where the title essay frames the shift as one from [[Appearance vs Apparition|appearance to apparition]], here the same pivot is named in the idiom of media and consciousness: art and culture cease to *represent* the world and instead *virtualise* it. "We are less interested in representations of the world than in its virtualisation." The figure recasts the self and human presence not as material things to be depicted but as "ongoing dynamic processes" distributed across telematic systems — a [[Virtuality|virtual]] presence rather than a represented one.

## The Poles

- **Representation** — art (and perception) as the depiction, imaging, or mirroring of a pre-given world; "things as they are"; the surface look of reality re-presented in a finite object. In Ascott's media reading this is the "rigorous story line" of literacy and "a high visual culture," with its "linear systems, rigid causality, and analytical certitude." It is the stance the telematic artist leaves behind: "we artists are no longer concerned either with magic or representation." `Directly stated`
- **Virtualisation** — the rendering of the world (and of the self) as a process within [[Data Space|electronic space]] — interactive, distributed, mediated, immaterial; presence "distributed across the connection matrix." Virtualisation "epitomise[s] our current concerns" in telematic culture and corresponds to "a more playful interaction with non-linear systems, layered language, the navigation of meaning, heightening intuition, and self-creation." `Directly stated`

## Why It Matters

This is the figure of [[Appearance vs Apparition]] stated in its consequences for *presence* and *selfhood* rather than for the surface look of things. Representation presupposes a stable, finished subject and a stable, given world standing apart from each other, the one picturing the other. Virtualisation dissolves both into process: "the self and human presence as ongoing dynamic processes rather than as a material finality." The stake is ontological as much as aesthetic — to virtualise the self is to refuse the "material finality" of the body-as-object and to relocate identity in the interactive, distributed field of [[Telematic Art]], where, as Ascott puts it elsewhere in the chapter, "our presence [is] distributed across the connection matrix." `Strongly implied`

It also names what the [[Material vs Immaterial]] turn looks like from the side of the observer. Representation is bound to the retinal/visual and to the material object; virtualisation is bound to the [[Cyberception|cyberceptive]], the [[Posthumanism|post-biological]], and the immaterial. Notably, Ascott does *not* leave the figure at pure immateriality: "Back to Nature II" pairs virtualisation with an emergent "process of materialisation" (nanotechnology, molecular engineering), forecasting a "rematerialization of art" and "a marriage of the immaterial and the material." Virtualisation is thus the dominant present-tense pole, not a terminus — the world is being virtualised *now*, with a re-materialised, "radically revised" nature anticipated to follow. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in *[[Telematic Embrace]]* — the originating and sole source. "Back to Nature II" (ch.28) restates the move from appearance/representation to apparition in the vocabulary of virtualisation: as the interface between self and environment becomes "increasingly digitalised" and perception "computer-mediated," interest shifts from representing the world to virtualising it, and the self is reconceived as an ongoing dynamic process rather than a material finality.

## Related Concepts

- [[Apparition]] — virtualisation is apparition seen from the side of mediation and presence; both name the displacement of the represented/given by the emergent/processual.
- [[Appearance vs Apparition]] — the keystone parent tension; "representation vs virtualisation" is its media-and-consciousness restatement (appearance ≈ representation; apparition ≈ virtualisation).
- [[Virtuality]] — the broader cross-author category (Hayles et al.) of which Ascott's virtualisation is the art-historical, process-oriented inflection ("a state of virtual presence beyond the constraints of Euclidian space and Newtonian time").
- [[Telematic Art]] — the art form that virtualises rather than represents; presence "distributed across the connection matrix."
- [[Data Space]] — the electronic/virtual space into which the world and the self are virtualised.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — representation is bound to the material object; virtualisation to the immaterial — though "Back to Nature II" expressly seeks "a marriage of the immaterial and the material."
- [[Representation vs Simulation]] — the adjacent figure: where simulation copies/displaces the real, virtualisation processualises it; both stand against representation, by different routes.
- [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]] — the epistemology underwriting the turn: if reality is constructed in consciousness, there is no given world to represent, only worlds to virtualise.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

As with [[Appearance vs Apparition]], Ascott does not hold the poles in balance: he sides decisively with virtualisation — "we artists are no longer concerned either with magic or representation, but with virtualisation." But "Back to Nature II" is the one place where the turn is explicitly *not* a one-way march into immateriality. Ascott forecasts that virtualisation will converge with a coming "molecular revolution" to yield "new processes of materialisation" and a "rematerialization of art," "a marriage of the immaterial and the material." The tension's near-term resolution is therefore lopsided (virtualisation displaces representation now), but its long-term horizon is a *synthesis of a different pair* — immaterial virtualisation rejoined to a re-materialised, "radically revised" nature. What stays open is whether that promised re-materialisation reinstates anything of representation, or merely supplies a new, "moist," biologically-engineered substrate for an art that remains, in concern, virtualising rather than depicting. `Interpretive synthesis`
