# Observer vs Participator

> Disambiguation: this is the **art-and-meaning** version of the activity-of-the-viewer question — does the viewer *decode* a meaning the artist fixed, or *co-create* meaning in interaction? It is borrowed from John Wheeler's "participatory universe" and from second-order cybernetics, and it is the spine polarity of [[Telematic Art]]. Keep it DISTINCT from (and cross-linked to) McLuhan's [[Detachment vs Participation]] (which concerns the *stance of the actor* under hot/cool media) and Burnham's [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] (which concerns whether the *artwork itself* can answer back). This note anchors the concept [[The Participator]].

## The Poles

- **Observer** — the viewer of the old order: a "skilled decoder or interpreter of the artist's 'meaning,' or, at worst, simply a passive receptacle of such meaning." On this model the artist is "sender and therefore originator of meaning," "creator and owner of images and ideas," "controller of context and content," and the artwork is "a fixed semantic configuration" whose "beauty and truth" are "out there" and "as fixed and immutable as the material universe appears to be." Representation "demands . . . a fixed separation of creator and observer of the work," in which "the observing subject must be essentially passive." This is the pole Ascott crosses out.
- **Participator** — the viewer of the telematic order: "the observer in an interactive telematic system is by definition a participator." Here "meaning is not created by the artist, distributed through the network, and received by the observer"; rather, "meaning is the product of interaction between the observer and the system, the content of which is in a state of flux." The participator "actively negotiates for meaning," is "as much creative in the act of the viewer's perception as . . . in the artist's production," and "comes to be a progenitor of the semantic issue." At the interface, "content is created rather than received."

## Why It Matters

This is the spine polarity of Ascott's argument about content in [[Telematic Art]] (ch. 20), and it reframes the whole question of artistic meaning as a question of *who makes it*. Ascott explicitly borrows the opposition from physics. Quoting John Wheeler on the quantum principle, he reports that the act of measurement "destroys the concept of the world 'sitting out there'": "To describe what has happened one has to cross out that old word 'observer' and put in its place 'participator.' In some strange sense the universe is a participatory universe" (`Directly stated`). Ascott then maps this directly onto art: "the parallel shift in art of the status of 'observer' to that of 'participator' is demonstrated clearly if in accounts of the quantum principle we substitute 'data' for 'quanta'" (`Directly stated`).

What is at stake is the location of meaning, and with it the status of the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|sovereign author]]. The observer model underwrites "the industry of criticism and exegesis, in which those who 'understand' this or that work of art explain it to those who are too stupid or uneducated to receive its meaning unaided" — a regime of authority, determinism, and "a fixed semantic configuration." The participator model dissolves that authority into [[Distributed Authorship]]: "the polarities of maker and user become destabilized," and the work becomes "a cultural communications system . . . in which the viewer actively negotiates for meaning." Ascott grounds the shift in **second-order cybernetics** — "the (second-order) cybernetics of observing systems" — so that the participator is not merely an active reader but a node within an [[Network Consciousness|observing system]] that is itself part of what it observes (`Directly stated`). For the vault's material/immaterial spine, the shift is also a shift in the ontology of content: meaning is no longer carried by a stable object but "embodied in data that is itself immaterial . . . pure electronic différance, until it has been reconstituted at the interface" (cross-linked to [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Virtuality]]).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

'participator,'" and recast as the "canon of the immaterial and participatory." It is developed further in ch. 19 ("Beyond Time-Based Art: ESP, PDP, and PU"), where the same physics — "it is the observer who creates the reality of the act by observation" — is mapped onto the telematic network, against an "art of representation" in which "the observing subject must be essentially passive." ## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott frames the opposition not as a stable dialectic but as a **historical and ontological supersession** — the observer is to be "crossed out" and replaced, exactly as in Wheeler's account of the quantum principle. Yet the synthesis is not the abolition of the artist but the *redistribution* of creativity: "Creativity is shared, authorship is distributed, but not in a way that denies the individual her authenticity or power of self-creation." The participator does not destroy meaning; meaning simply ceases to be a thing transmitted and becomes "the product of interaction" held "in a state of flux, of endless change and transformation." The tension is therefore best read as a **resolved-toward-the-participator** polarity at the level of Ascott's theory (he treats the participatory turn as already accomplished in physics and as the telos of telematic art), while remaining genuinely *open* at the level of practice — the observer model persists wherever the interface "binds us to past practices," "forces [the user] into the posture of a clerk," or makes the work "an internally lit painting . . . of no artistic value." Ascott's wager is that the interface, made flexible and "invisible in its immanence," will complete the passage from observer to participator (`Interpretive synthesis`; the quoted phrases are `Directly stated`). The deeper, unresolved edge — whether the same participatory network is also the surveillance apparatus — is carried by the sibling tension [[Liberation vs Control (Telematics)]].
