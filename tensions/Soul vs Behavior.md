# Soul vs Behavior

## The Poles

- **Soul** — the *inside*: subjectivity, interiority, the inner life, "soul," "self," "mind," "consciousness," freedom, will, lived experience. The locus that totalitarian power sought to claim, dominate, and remake from the inside out.
- **Behavior** — the *outside*: observable, measurable action devoid of subjective attribution; "what an organism is doing — or more accurately what it is observed by another organism to be doing." The only object that radical behaviorism (Watson, Meyer, Skinner) admits as scientifically real, and the only thing instrumentarian power touches.

The tension is the behaviorist axis Max Meyer named in 1921 and Zuboff carries into the present: a shift *from soul to other, inside to outside, lived experience to observable behavior* — and the political payoff that shift acquires once it is operationalized by surveillance capitalism's `Big Other`.

## Why It Matters

This tension is how Zuboff distinguishes her unprecedented "second species of power" from the twentieth-century totalitarianism it superficially resembles. The distinction is exact and load-bearing:

- **Totalitarianism engineers the soul.** It "cannot succeed by remote control" — "each individual inner life must be claimed and transformed," remaking "every aspect of the individual from the inside out: heart, mind, sexuality, personality, and spirit" through handmade terror. It wants our conformity, our belief, our submission. *(Directly stated — Ch.12)*
- **`Instrumentarian Power` is indifferent to the soul.** It "does not care what we think, feel, or do" so long as it can observe, render, and instrumentalize our behavior. Its signature is `Radical Indifference`: it reduces experience to observable behavior while remaining "steadfastly indifferent to the meaning of that experience." Zuboff's keystone formulation: Big Other produces instrumentarian power "**replacing the engineering of souls with the engineering of behavior**." *(Directly stated — Ch.13)*

The political payoff is that severing inside from outside removes the friction that totalitarian soul-engineering generated. Because instrumentarianism "does not claim our bodies for some grotesque regime of pain and murder, we are prone to undervalue its effects and lower our guard." It does not need our belief or our conformity; it needs only our behavioral data. By leaving the soul formally untouched — "your own business," in Meyer's phrase — it "**severs our insides from our outsides, our subjectivity and interiority from our observable actions**," exiling us from our own behavior while presenting itself as no threat to the inner self at all. *(Strongly implied / Directly stated — Ch.12, Ch.13)*

Understanding the tension blocks the false comfort of the Orwell analogy (China's social credit system "is often compared to the world of Orwell's *1984*" but "is better understood as the apotheosis of instrumentarian power"). It also exposes the cost: the inner life is "formally exempt" not because it is respected but because, from `Big Other`'s point of view, we are "strictly Other-Ones: organisms that behave." *(Directly stated — Ch.12, Ch.13)*

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Zuboff stages the opposition not as a paradox to be dissolved but as a *severance* to be resisted. Where totalitarianism's assault on the soul was at least legible *as* assault, instrumentarianism's indifference is its stealth: it leaves the inner life nominally intact ("the soul is your own business") precisely while it datafies and conditions everything outward. The synthesis Meyer and Skinner imagined — a rational "global harmony of organisms" purchased by forfeiting freedom for knowledge — is, in Zuboff's account, exactly the danger, because under surveillance capitalism "the knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us." The tension therefore remains open and adversarial: holding the inside and the outside together — refusing the behaviorist severance — is the human capability instrumentarian power erodes, and that Zuboff calls on us to defend. *(Interpretive synthesis)*
