# Free Will vs Determinism

## General Orientation

This is the canonical free-will problem, but the vault's authors enter it in different registers, and the tension is sharper if they are kept distinct.

- **O'Gieblyn** approaches the deterministic pole through *prediction*. Her "automaton/predestination problem" is less a flat denial of freedom than a worry about whether agency can be told apart from "the inexorable logic of the clickstream" once forecasting systems grow accurate enough to "enact the very fate" they foresee. She holds the tension open as a question and refuses both libertarian free will and surrender to the machine.
- **Harari** pushes the deterministic pole to its *eliminativist* terminus. For him there is no tension to hold open: "determinism and randomness have divided the entire cake between them, leaving not even a crumb for 'freedom'," so the word "freedom," like "soul," is simply "an empty term." His distinctive lever is not prediction but desire — we can act on but cannot *choose* our desires — and the political payoff is that engineerable desire makes "the supposedly free will of customers" a purchasable product. See [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]].
- **Zuboff** is the vault's clearest *defender* of the spontaneous-volition pole, and she defends it precisely against the others' deterministic apparatus. Where Harari abolishes freedom by argument and O'Gieblyn fears its erosion by prediction, Zuboff treats "freedom of will" as the existential ground of an "elemental right to the future tense," names behaviorism's "knowledge displaces freedom" axiom as the intellectual root of surveillance capitalism's power, and reframes the whole problem in political-economic terms: the deterministic picture is not (only) a metaphysical truth but an *operational program* whose aim "is no longer enough to automate information flows about us; the goal now is to automate us." See the Zuboff section below.

Where O'Gieblyn fears the *appearance* of determinism is partly manufactured by the apparatus that reads behavior, Harari argues determinism (or randomness) is the literal, exhaustive truth of the case, with no remainder for freedom at all. Zuboff agrees with O'Gieblyn that the determinism is partly an artifact of the apparatus — but, unlike both, she refuses the deterministic pole as a *truth* even while taking the apparatus building it with deadly seriousness: the loss of freedom is real not because freedom was always an illusion but because a proprietary power is actively dismantling it.

## The Poles

- **Spontaneous volition** — the conviction that a choice originates with the agent who makes it, that the self is the genuine author of its acts. In O'Gieblyn's recovery of an older meaning, this is what the word "automaton" once named: not passive machinery but a thing that acts "of itself," that exhibits "freedom and spontaneity." In Zuboff this pole is given its strongest defense: "I recognize my direct experience of freedom as an inviolate truth that cannot be reduced to the behaviorists' formulations of life as necessarily accidental and random."
- **Deterministic algorithm** — the view that "the decisions we believe to be spontaneous or freely chosen are merely the end of a long and rigid causal chain of events." In the book's sharpest form, the chain is no longer the soul's hidden mechanism or the brain's neurons but the predictive model that anticipates a behavior before it occurs — fate relocated from God's decree to a proprietary system. In Zuboff's behaviorist genealogy this pole has a creed of its own: "Knowledge and freedom are necessarily adversaries," so that freedom shrinks as measurement grows.

## Why It Matters

This is the canonical free-will problem, and O'Gieblyn's distinctive move is to show that it does not arrive in her book as an abstract metaphysical puzzle but as the same dilemma "in different guises." It is "the algorithm/predestination problem": the disquiet of the addict who learns her theft was "already accounted for" in a store's budgeted "shrinkage," the anxiety of the Calvinist child who cannot know "whether the doubts themselves were fated or freely chosen," and the dread of the profiled subject who finds that "the act of prediction" may "enact the very fate it foresees." What is at stake is whether agency survives — whether there remains "the difference between individual agency and the inexorable logic of the clickstream."

The tension folds in two facets the planner names. **Foresight vs freedom:** the very accuracy of a prediction "suggests—at least intuitively—that human behavior is deterministic," because a future that can be foreseen looks like a future already fixed. **Prediction vs control:** O'Gieblyn's worry is that foresight does not stay separate from causation. Calvin's God "not only foresees but controls" the future; the predictive analytics that "actively modif[y] real behavior" threaten to do the same, until "the line between prediction and control will have completely dissolved." Once prediction enacts what it foresees, the appearance of determinism is partly an artifact of the prediction itself — and the question of whether behavior *is* determined becomes inseparable from the apparatus claiming to read it.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] in [[God Human Animal Machine]] — stages the tension across two registers. In "Image" (Ch1) she excavates the buried sense of "automaton" to show that mechanism and freedom were not always opposites, framing the whole disenchanted picture in which "I have come to see myself more or less as a machine." In "Algorithm" (Ch12) she recasts free will as the algorithm/predestination problem, threading her own loss of faith over Calvinist predestination through Laplace's omniscient intellect, Weber's Protestant anxiety, and the predictive systems that make "prudent guesswork into a kind of oracular power."
- [[Yuval Noah Harari]] in [[Homo Deus]] — does not hold the tension open but tries to abolish one pole outright. In "The Time Bomb in the Laboratory" (Ch11) he argues that the deterministic and the random exhaust the field of brain processes, leaving "freedom" an "empty term"; in "The Great Decoupling" (Ch12) he draws the practical consequence, that "the algorithms constituting a human are not free" and so can be known, predicted, and ultimately steered by external algorithms better than the person knows themselves. See the section below and [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]].
- [[Shoshana Zuboff]] in [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]] — defends the spontaneous-volition pole as "an essential condition of free will," grounds it in an "elemental right to the future tense," and diagnoses the deterministic pole's behaviorist creed ("freedom is ignorance," "knowledge and freedom are necessarily adversaries") as the very ideology that licenses surveillance capitalism's power. For her the danger is not that determinism is true but that "the goal now is to automate us." See the section below.

## Free Will as an Empty Word (Harari)

Where O'Gieblyn keeps the tension as a live question, [[Yuval Noah Harari]] pushes the deterministic pole to its eliminativist extreme: there is no contest between freedom and determinism, because freedom never had a place on the board. Opening up the "Sapiens black box," scientists found "neither soul, nor free will, nor 'self' – but only genes, hormones and neurons that obey the same physical and chemical laws governing the rest of reality." Every brain event is "either deterministic or random or a combination of both – but they are never free." From this Harari draws the line that gives the tension its starkest form: "To the best of our scientific understanding, determinism and randomness have divided the entire cake between them, leaving not even a crumb for 'freedom'." The word "freedom," like "soul," is "an empty term that carries no discernible meaning," and "free will exists only in the imaginary stories we humans have invented." (`Directly stated`)

His decisive lever is not the physics of neurons but a distinction about desire — and it is what lets him deny the usual escape from the deterministic pole. Harari concedes that if "free will" means merely "the ability to act according to your desires," then humans have it, "and so do chimpanzees, dogs and parrots." But "the million-dollar question is not whether parrots and humans can act out their inner desires – the question is whether they can choose their desires in the first place." His answer is that we cannot: "I don't choose my desires. I only feel them, and act accordingly," and the belief otherwise "results from faulty logic," the slide from feeling a desire to authoring it. The spontaneous-volition pole of this tension thus collapses inward — what feels like self-authored choice is a wish that "well[s] up within me because this is the feeling created by the biochemical processes in my brain." (`Directly stated`)

The political sting, which is what makes Harari's contribution more than a metaphysical footnote, is that an unauthored desire is an engineerable one. "If organisms indeed lack free will, it implies we could manipulate and even control their desires using drugs, genetic engineering or direct brain stimulation." Once such manipulation "become[s] routine, the supposedly free will of customers will become just another product we can buy" — the attention helmet that leaves you "downright aching to play the piano" is his paradigm case. In "The Great Decoupling" this hardens into a forecast: because "the algorithms constituting a human are not free," an "external algorithm could theoretically know me much better than I can ever know myself," and, as Harari puts it, "Liberalism will collapse on the day the system knows me better than I know myself." Determinism here is not a cosmic abstraction but a market and a technology; freedom does not lose an argument so much as become, in Harari's phrase, a thing "we can buy." (`Directly stated`) For the standalone treatment of this position, including the evolutionary "last nail in freedom's coffin" and the dissolution of the unitary self, see [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]].

## Shoshana Zuboff (The Age of Surveillance Capitalism)

[[Shoshana Zuboff]] stands at the opposite end of this tension from Harari: she is the vault's most emphatic *defender* of the spontaneous-volition pole, and she defends it not as a metaphysical thesis to be proven but as a primary human fact to be protected. Refusing the embarrassment that "centuries of debate" have instilled, she writes: "I recognize my direct experience of freedom as an inviolate truth that cannot be reduced to the behaviorists' formulations of life as necessarily accidental and random, shaped by external stimuli beyond my knowledge or influence." On this foundation she names a new claimed right — "[[The Right to the Future Tense]]" — anchored explicitly in free will. Surveillance capitalism's operations are "a challenge to the elemental right to the future tense, which accounts for the individual's ability to imagine, intend, promise, and construct a future. It is an essential condition of free will." Where the future tense for O'Gieblyn is what prediction threatens to foreclose and for Harari is an empty grammar of an illusory self, for Zuboff it is the very habitat of will: "Most simply put, there is no freedom without uncertainty; it is the medium in which human will is expressed in promises." (`Directly stated`)

Zuboff's signal contribution to this tension is to identify the deterministic pole's *creed* and trace its lineage as an operational ideology rather than a discovered truth. In "Two Species of Power" she reconstructs the behaviorist axiom — Planck through Max Meyer to B. F. Skinner — that "the liberal idea of freedom persists in an inverse relationship to the growth of scientific knowledge," so that "[k]nowledge and freedom are necessarily adversaries." On this account freedom is merely "the void that we fill with the fantasy of freedom," a residue of ignorance destined to melt under measurement. This is recognizably Harari's eliminativism and O'Gieblyn's predictive determinism stated as a *program*; but Zuboff adds the twist that detonates it. Skinner imagined the conquest of freedom by knowledge as a collective good; under surveillance capitalism the asymmetry is the whole point: "The knowledge that now displaces our freedom is proprietary. The knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us." The deterministic pole, for Zuboff, is less a fact about the universe than a power relation about who knows and who is shaped. (`Directly stated`)

This recasts the stakes of the tension in a way that converges with O'Gieblyn's prediction-becomes-control worry and sharply diverges from Harari's resignation. The danger is not that the self is an illusion but that a power has set out to overwrite it: surveillance capitalists' interests "have shifted from using automated machine processes to know about your behavior to using machine processes to shape your behavior according to their interests… this decade-and-a-half trajectory has taken us from automating information flows about you to automating you." This is the same hinge O'Gieblyn names as the dissolution of "the line between prediction and control," but Zuboff gives it an economic motor and a named adversary, and — crucially for this tension — she does not concede the metaphysics that would make the project legitimate. The right to the future tense "is not extinguished, but rather it is usurped"; the loss of freedom is a theft, not a disenchantment. Against Harari's "free will… exists only in the imaginary stories we humans have invented," Zuboff insists that to surrender the deterministic premise is already to capitulate, since "in the absence of this freedom, the future collapses into an infinite present of mere behavior, in which there can be no subjects and no projects: only objects." (`Directly stated`; the Han/Harari/O'Gieblyn convergences are `Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the precise modern name for the deterministic pole: prediction built from past behavior that "traps" people "within the mirror of their digital reflection," so the forecast helps cause the outcome it predicts. This tension is the metaphysical question that note's concept makes concrete.
- [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — the structural analogy that lets predestination and predictive analytics rhyme: the model occupies the place once held by the foreseeing, ordaining God.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the figure the deterministic pole dissolves. Spontaneous volition is the liberal premise that "an individual knows what is best for herself"; "Dataism" undermines it from outside.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the broader metaphysics in which "everything in modern life, from our minds to the rotation of the planets, can be reduced to the causal mechanism of physical laws." The tension is disenchantment's home contradiction.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the reflexive endpoint of the deterministic pole: "To concede that one's mind is controlled by God is to become a machine."
- [[The Black Box]] — what determinism becomes when its cause is hidden: "the divine will was a black box," and so is the proprietary model; the agony is the impossibility of knowing whether one is fated.
- [[Theodicy]] — the older theological key (Job, Ivan Karamazov) in which O'Gieblyn first met the problem of a justice meted out for things "entirely out of my control."
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — the cybernetic ontology that licenses describing a person "purely in terms of pattern and probabilities, without any concern for interiority," making the deterministic forecast possible.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the condition toward which the deterministic pole tends: a subject whose preferences are read off the clickstream rather than chosen.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

O'Gieblyn does not resolve the tension; she sharpens it into a question and refuses the easy exits. She rejects naive libertarian free will (she "come[s] to see [her]self more or less as a machine") and equally rejects surrender to the deterministic picture, recovering instead from Ivan Karamazov a third move: not to disprove the system but to "insist on the validity of human morality," to "return [the] ticket." The strongest synthetic thread is her suspicion that the foresight-vs-freedom intuition is partly an illusion of its own making — that determinism *looks* total precisely because prediction "actively modifies real behavior," so the loop manufactures the fixity it then reports. On that reading the poles are not two settled metaphysical states between which we must choose but a feedback relation: the more we treat behavior as deterministic and act on the forecast, the more deterministic behavior becomes. The tension is therefore best held open, with the practical stakes foregrounded — its danger lies less in whether the universe is "fixed and unchangeable" than in our building systems that make it so, and then forgetting we built them.

Zuboff radicalizes O'Gieblyn's "feedback relation" reading and refuses Harari's resignation. For her the loop is not a metaphysical accident but an engineered, owned, profit-driven program: the deterministic pole is something a power is *building* ("the goal now is to automate us"), so the right response is neither proof nor surrender but resistance — naming the apparatus and reasserting "the elemental right to the future tense." This sets up the vault's clearest internal disagreement on the question. Harari treats the deterministic verdict as settled science and the only honest stance as acceptance; Zuboff treats acceptance of that verdict as itself the surrender, since to grant that "knowledge and freedom are necessarily adversaries" is already to license the project that would "author us." The unresolved status, across all three, is whether the appearance of determinism is a discovery about human nature (Harari), an artifact of the prediction apparatus that may be partly illusory (O'Gieblyn), or a deliberate usurpation that can and must be contested (Zuboff). (`Interpretive synthesis`)
