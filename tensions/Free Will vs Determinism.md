# Free Will vs Determinism

## The Poles

- **Spontaneous volition** — the conviction that a choice originates with the agent who makes it, that the self is the genuine author of its acts. In O'Gieblyn's recovery of an older meaning, this is what the word "automaton" once named: not passive machinery but a thing that acts "of itself," that exhibits "freedom and spontaneity."
- **Deterministic algorithm** — the view that "the decisions we believe to be spontaneous or freely chosen are merely the end of a long and rigid causal chain of events." In the book's sharpest form, the chain is no longer the soul's hidden mechanism or the brain's neurons but the predictive model that anticipates a behavior before it occurs — fate relocated from God's decree to a proprietary system.

## Why It Matters

This is the canonical free-will problem, and O'Gieblyn's distinctive move is to show that it does not arrive in her book as an abstract metaphysical puzzle but as the same dilemma "in different guises." It is "the algorithm/predestination problem": the disquiet of the addict who learns her theft was "already accounted for" in a store's budgeted "shrinkage," the anxiety of the Calvinist child who cannot know "whether the doubts themselves were fated or freely chosen," and the dread of the profiled subject who finds that "the act of prediction" may "enact the very fate it foresees." What is at stake is whether agency survives — whether there remains "the difference between individual agency and the inexorable logic of the clickstream."

The tension folds in two facets the planner names. **Foresight vs freedom:** the very accuracy of a prediction "suggests—at least intuitively—that human behavior is deterministic," because a future that can be foreseen looks like a future already fixed. **Prediction vs control:** O'Gieblyn's worry is that foresight does not stay separate from causation. Calvin's God "not only foresees but controls" the future; the predictive analytics that "actively modif[y] real behavior" threaten to do the same, until "the line between prediction and control will have completely dissolved." Once prediction enacts what it foresees, the appearance of determinism is partly an artifact of the prediction itself — and the question of whether behavior *is* determined becomes inseparable from the apparatus claiming to read it.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] in [[God, Human, Animal, Machine]] — stages the tension across two registers. In "Image" (Ch1) she excavates the buried sense of "automaton" to show that mechanism and freedom were not always opposites, framing the whole disenchanted picture in which "I have come to see myself more or less as a machine." In "Algorithm" (Ch12) she recasts free will as the algorithm/predestination problem, threading her own loss of faith over Calvinist predestination through Laplace's omniscient intellect, Weber's Protestant anxiety, and the predictive systems that make "prudent guesswork into a kind of oracular power."

## Related Concepts

- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the precise modern name for the deterministic pole: prediction built from past behavior that "traps" people "within the mirror of their digital reflection," so the forecast helps cause the outcome it predicts. This tension is the metaphysical question that note's concept makes concrete.
- [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — the structural analogy that lets predestination and predictive analytics rhyme: the model occupies the place once held by the foreseeing, ordaining God.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the figure the deterministic pole dissolves. Spontaneous volition is the liberal premise that "an individual knows what is best for herself"; "Dataism" undermines it from outside.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the broader metaphysics in which "everything in modern life, from our minds to the rotation of the planets, can be reduced to the causal mechanism of physical laws." The tension is disenchantment's home contradiction.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the reflexive endpoint of the deterministic pole: "To concede that one's mind is controlled by God is to become a machine."
- [[The Black Box]] — what determinism becomes when its cause is hidden: "the divine will was a black box," and so is the proprietary model; the agony is the impossibility of knowing whether one is fated.
- [[Theodicy]] — the older theological key (Job, Ivan Karamazov) in which O'Gieblyn first met the problem of a justice meted out for things "entirely out of my control."
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — the cybernetic ontology that licenses describing a person "purely in terms of pattern and probabilities, without any concern for interiority," making the deterministic forecast possible.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the condition toward which the deterministic pole tends: a subject whose preferences are read off the clickstream rather than chosen.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

O'Gieblyn does not resolve the tension; she sharpens it into a question and refuses the easy exits. She rejects naive libertarian free will (she "come[s] to see [her]self more or less as a machine") and equally rejects surrender to the deterministic picture, recovering instead from Ivan Karamazov a third move: not to disprove the system but to "insist on the validity of human morality," to "return [the] ticket." The strongest synthetic thread is her suspicion that the foresight-vs-freedom intuition is partly an illusion of its own making — that determinism *looks* total precisely because prediction "actively modifies real behavior," so the loop manufactures the fixity it then reports. On that reading the poles are not two settled metaphysical states between which we must choose but a feedback relation: the more we treat behavior as deterministic and act on the forecast, the more deterministic behavior becomes. The tension is therefore best held open, with the practical stakes foregrounded — its danger lies less in whether the universe is "fixed and unchangeable" than in our building systems that make it so, and then forgetting we built them.
