# Leverage vs Resistance

## The Poles

- **Leverage** — the effectiveness of an intervention: how large a shift in system behavior a given change produces. In [[The Twelve Leverage Points|Meadows's ranked list]], leverage rises as one climbs from numbers and buffers toward information, rules, [[Self-Organization|self-organization]], [[System Goals|goals]], [[Paradigms|paradigms]], and finally [[Transcending Paradigms|transcending paradigms]].
- **Resistance** — a system's disposition to defend the very place where an intervention would do the most. The two poles are not independent variables but the same axis read in opposite directions: as Meadows states it, "The higher the leverage point, the more the system will resist changing it."

## Why It Matters

This is the self-limiting principle that qualifies Meadows's entire twelve-point catalogue of "places to intervene in a system." Read naively, a ranked list of leverage points is an operating manual: find the highest rung, push there, transform the system. Meadows's closing warning inverts that promise. Leverage and accessibility are *inversely* correlated — the interventions that would change the most (paradigms, and above them transcending paradigms) are precisely the ones a system most fiercely defends, so that "societies often rub out truly enlightened beings" (`Directly stated`). What is at stake is whether effective change is structurally rare. If the most powerful place to intervene is also the one power structures most violently suppress, then the map of leverage doubles as a map of foreclosure: knowing where the high-leverage points are does not make them reachable. "Magical leverage points are not easily accessible," Meadows writes, "even if we know where they are and which direction to push on them" (`Directly stated`). The tension reframes systems mastery away from force — from "pushing leverage points" — toward a very different competence: [[Resilience|flexibility]], humility, and letting go.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Donella Meadows]] in [[Thinking in Systems]] (2008), Chapter 6 ("Leverage Points") — the sole and originating anchor. Meadows builds the ascending ladder of [[Leverage Points|leverage]] over the chapter, then closes it with the resistance principle as a deliberate qualification: the list "is a tentative list and its order is slithery," and, more consequentially, its top rungs are the least attainable. She notes that "you could say [[Paradigms|paradigms]] are harder to change than anything else about a system, and therefore this item should be lowest on the list, not second-to-highest," resolving the apparent contradiction by distinguishing *cost* from *resistance*: paradigm change is not "physical or expensive or even slow" — "in a single individual it can happen in a millisecond" — yet "whole societies are another matter—they resist challenges to their paradigms harder than they resist anything else" (`Directly stated`).

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Meadows does not resolve the tension so much as convert it into a different stance toward action. She refuses two easy exits. She will not soften the leverage ranking to make it operable ("There are no cheap tickets to mastery. You have to work hard at it"), and she will not treat resistance as a wall that forecloses change — the same chapter records paradigm shifts that "totally transform systems" and describes Kuhn's own method for effecting them ("You keep pointing at the anomalies and failures in the old paradigm… You insert people with the new paradigm in places of public visibility and power"). The synthesis she offers is neither pushing harder nor giving up but a third posture: "In the end, it seems that mastery has less to do with pushing leverage points than it does with strategically, profoundly, madly, letting go and dancing with the system" (`Directly stated`). The tension thus remains productively open. It is held, not dissolved: leverage really does rise toward the top of the list, and resistance really does rise with it, and the competent systems actor lives inside that contradiction rather than escaping it — working "rigorously" while "throwing yourself into the humility of not-knowing." Whether "letting go" genuinely defeats resistance or merely reconciles the intervener to its persistence is a question Meadows leaves — characteristically — unanswered (`Interpretive synthesis`).
