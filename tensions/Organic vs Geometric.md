# Organic vs Geometric

## The Poles

- **Organic** — the *representational* and *amorphic* tendency: forms that look plantlike, rocklike, or animal-like; nature's "organicism," which Burnham describes as "amorphic or representational."
- **Geometric** — the *linear* and *abstract* tendency: the precision of "classical machines," which Burnham describes as "linear or abstract."

This is the art-historical polarity Burnham takes from Wilhelm Worringer and Arnold Hauser — "two of a number of art historians who attempt to explain stylistics and its underlying meaning for a culture in terms of how cultures gravitate toward one tendency or another" — and then dissolves. `Directly stated`

## Why It Matters

The opposition is the inherited grammar of art history (the Worringer/Hauser scheme that periodizes whole cultures by whether they lean organic or geometric), but Burnham shows it breaks on the case of modern abstract sculpture, "which is obviously organic in origin" yet abstract in appearance — forcing "semantic acrobatics" when the geometric is equated with abstraction and the organic with naturalism. `Directly stated`

What is at stake for Burnham is the larger thesis of the book: the polarity is not merely reconcilable in art but is being *literally* fused — for the first time outside of metaphor — by the new sciences. Where Herbert Read could only resolve the dichotomy aesthetically (naming abstract organic forms "vitalistic art"), technology can fuse organic activity with linear-geometric machine precision through cybernetics, information theory, and systems analysis, producing a "no-style" aesthetic that "transcends the existing stylistic polarity." Understanding the tension thus marks the hinge of Burnham's argument from `Vitalism` (the sculptor's metaphor of life in matter) toward `Organicism` / `Systems Theory` (the technician's actual making of living systems). `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] — invokes the organic/geometric polarity via Worringer and Hauser, shows [[Herbert Read]] and Henri Focillon attempting to merge the two (Read naming the merger "vitalistic art"), and argues that cybernetics, electronic circuit theory, information theory, and systems analysis can now fuse the poles literally, transcending style into an "anti-style or no-style aesthetic."

## Related Concepts

- [[Vitalism]] — Read's resolution: the merger of "organic appearing forms with abstraction" called "vitalistic art." The poetic counterpart to the technical fusion.
- [[Organicism]] — "the awareness of the interrelation between systems and their components within larger systems"; the systems-side meaning toward which the fused poles converge.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the disciplinary frame ("systems analysis") that, with cybernetics and information theory, performs the literal fusion.
- [[Cybernetics]] — named by Burnham as one of the sciences making the literal fusion possible.
- [[Information]] — "information theory," likewise named as an enabling science of the fusion.
- [[Object and System]] — kept distinct: that note tracks Burnham's shift from the sculptural *object* to the *system*; this tension is the stylistic polarity (organic vs geometric forms) the fusion dissolves.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Burnham presents the tension as **dissolving**, not merely held. He states that "as separate categories the organic and geometric nearly vanish with the emergence of abstract vitalistic sculpture," and that "for the first time in history our culture has the option of literally fusing organic activities with the linear-geometric precision of machines." The synthesis is twofold: (1) aesthetic — Read's "vitalistic art" merges organic-appearing form with abstraction; (2) technological — cybernetics, information theory, and systems analysis produce "neither amorphic or representational … nor linear or abstract … but a fusion of the two which transcends the existing stylistic polarity," absorbed into a "no-style aesthetic." Whether this fused "no-style" is genuine transcendence or a new style of its own Burnham leaves open ("Certainly every culture has a style"). `Directly stated`
