# Appearance vs Apparition

The title figure of [[Roy Ascott]]'s *[[Telematic Embrace]]* and the keystone opposition of its title essay (ch.24, "From Appearance to Apparition," 1993). Ascott argues that Western art's millennial preoccupation with **appearance** — the surface look of things, representation, the retinal gaze, things as they are — is dying, displaced in a systems-based culture by an art of **apparition** — emergence, becoming, construction, the coming-into-being of things as they could be. This tension is the default canonical home for the figure; the [[Apparition]] concept note carries the constructive/emergent pole.

## The Poles

- **Appearance** — surface, representation, the outward and visible look of things; the retinal/optical gaze; "ready-made reality," things as they are; *inertia*. Ascott's pejorative for the millennia-long Western concern with the surface image, a concern he calls "a fraud" because "the mirror always lies." `Directly stated`
- **Apparition** — emergence, becoming, transformation, "the inward and emergent processes of becoming"; the coming-into-being of a new identity; "things as they could be"; *action*. The defining quality of art in a systems-based, networked culture. `Directly stated`

## Why It Matters

The opposition stages Ascott's core claim about what art is *for* in a post-biological, telematic culture. If appearance-art served church and state ideologies and a classical, clockwork-determinist science that saw man as a material object, then a culture "progressively concerned with the complexity of relationships and subtlety of systems, with the invisible and immaterial" has no use for representation. The shift is not stylistic but ontological: from a finite, composed object reflecting a given reality (`appearance`) to an open-ended cybernetic system whose meaning the observer constructs in interaction (`apparition`). It reframes the artist from a maker of content to a designer of context, and reframes the artwork as "art in a state of endless becoming." `Strongly implied`

It is also the participatory/behavioural pole of 1960s–90s cybernetic art beside [[Jack Burnham]]'s systems esthetics of the object: where Burnham tracks sculpture's passage from [[Object vs System|object to system]], Ascott tracks art's passage from object to *process*, completed by the observer. The two are the two poles of the same cybernetic-art lineage. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in *[[Telematic Embrace]]* — the originating and sole source; the figure recurs across the collected writings (ch.24, 26, 27, 29, 31, 32), each restating that apparition (emergence, coming-into-being) is displacing appearance (representation, the look of things) as the defining concern of art.

## Related Concepts

- [[Apparition]] — the constructive/emergent pole as a standalone concept.
- [[Telematic Art]] — the networked, interactive art whose defining quality is apparition.
- [[Emergence]] — apparition glossed as "emergence and… coming into being"; the shared cross-author term (Hayles/O'Gieblyn/Harari/Burnham).
- [[Object vs System]] / [[Object and System]] — Burnham's object→system spine; Ascott's "from object to process" is its participatory/behavioural reading.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — appearance = the retinal surface/object; apparition = "the invisible and immaterial"; Ascott names "the interspace between material and immaterial worlds."
- [[Dematerialization]] — Lippard's art-historical strand of the same turn from the object.
- [[Representation vs Simulation]] — appearance-as-representation against the constructed/emergent; cf. Ascott's "Can representation co-exist with constructivism?"
- [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]] — the epistemology underwriting apparition: reality is constructed in consciousness, so the processes of apparition are "authentic" and appearance is "a fraud."
- [[Conceptual Art]] — the precursor lineage of art as action/process rather than finite object.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does **not** hold the poles in balance: he resolves the tension decisively against appearance. Representation is declared "mendacious, illusory, and counterfeit," and apparition is named "authentic." The two are framed as successive paradigms (a Kuhnian shift), not co-existing options — appearance-art "is dying" and apparition is "replacing" it. The figure is therefore better read as a directional thesis than a held antinomy: the tension is meant to *collapse*, the surface giving way to the emergent. What stays genuinely open in Ascott is whether any residue of appearance survives the turn (he concedes the question — "Can representation co-exist with constructivism?" — but answers it in the negative). `Interpretive synthesis`
