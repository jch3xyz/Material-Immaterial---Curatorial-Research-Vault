# Deep Attention vs Hyperattention

## The Poles

- **Deep, contemplative attention** — the immersive, lingering mode of awareness that can dwell on what is before it. For Han it is not a private preference but the very ground of civilization: "We owe the cultural achievements of humanity—which include philosophy—to deep, contemplative attention. Culture presumes an environment in which deep attention is possible." It is bound up with [[Profound Boredom|profound boredom]] as "the peak of mental relaxation" and the "dream bird that hatches the egg of experience," and only it has access to "whatever is floating, inconspicuous, or fleeting" and to "phenomena that are long and slow." Its exemplar is Cézanne, "a master of deep, contemplative attention," in whom "the landscape thinks itself."
- **Hyperattention** — the scattered, accelerated mode that is displacing deep attention. Han defines it by its restlessness: "A rash change of focus between different tasks, sources of information, and processes characterizes this scattered mode of awareness." Crucially, "[s]ince it also has a low tolerance for boredom, it does not admit the profound idleness that benefits the creative process." It is continuous with [[Multitasking|multitasking]] and video games, which "produce a broad but flat mode of attention, which is similar to the vigilance of a wild animal" — an attentive technique Han ranks not as progress but as "regression."

## Why It Matters

The opposition is the attention-regime axis of Han's *The Burnout Society*: it names what the "excess of stimuli, information, and impulses" does to the human interior. Excessive [[Positivity|positivity]] "radically changes the structure and economy of attention," so that "[p]erception becomes fragmented and scattered" (`Directly stated`). What is at stake is nothing less than the precondition of culture. If "[c]ulture presumes an environment in which deep attention is possible," then the displacement of immersive reflection by hyperattention is not a neutral shift in habit but the erosion of the soil in which philosophy, the genuinely new, and the "gift of listening" can grow (`Directly stated`).

Han's decisive move is to deny that the hyperattentive, multitasking subject represents an advance. "The attitude toward time and environment known as 'multitasking' does not represent civilizational progress. ... Rather, such an aptitude amounts to regression." He grounds this in an ethological reversal: multitasking is "commonplace among wild animals," "an attentive technique indispensable for survival in the wilderness," where the animal must eat while guarding prey, young, and mate and so "is incapable of contemplative immersion." The late-modern shift toward "the simple concern for survival" therefore drives "human society deeper and deeper into the wilderness" — a re-animalization, not a refinement (`Directly stated`). The genuinely new can only come from the slack side of the opposition: "A purely hectic rush produces nothing new. It reproduces and accelerates what is already available" (`Directly stated`). The creative act depends on the very idleness hyperattention cannot tolerate.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in *The Burnout Society* — in "Profound Boredom" Han stages the opposition directly: deep, contemplative attention as the ground of culture and philosophy, displaced by hyperattention as "an entirely different form of attention." He links the deep pole to [[Profound Boredom|Benjamin's deep boredom]] (the "dream bird that hatches the egg of experience"), to the [[Vita Contemplativa|vita contemplativa]] and its "marveling at the way things are," and to Cézanne's and Merleau-Ponty's account of contemplative perception; he links the shallow pole to [[Multitasking|multitasking]], video games, the "hyperactive ego," and the deadly hyperactivity Nietzsche warned of, in which "our civilization is turning into a new barbarism."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Han does not hold the two modes in balance; he stages a **displacement and a loss**, and calls for a corrective. Hyperattention is "displacing" deep, immersive reflection, and the trend runs one way — toward fragmentation, regression, and "a new barbarism." Yet the chapter is not simply elegiac. Its closing gesture is recuperative: Han enlists even Nietzsche, who "replaced Being with Will," to argue that "one of the most necessary corrections to the character of mankind ... is a considerable strengthening of the contemplative element in it." The synthesis Han points to is therefore not a reconciliation of the two attentions but the deliberate cultivation of the deep pole against the grain of the achievement society — the recovery of "the profound idleness that benefits the creative process," of tolerance for boredom, of the step "from walking to dancing" that "escapes the achievement-principle entirely" (`Directly stated` for the call to strengthen the contemplative element; `Interpretive synthesis` for reading the chapter as displacement-plus-corrective rather than balance).
