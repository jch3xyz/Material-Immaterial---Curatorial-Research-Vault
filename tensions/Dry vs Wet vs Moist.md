# Dry vs Wet vs Moist

## The Poles

This is a **tripartition**, not a binary: Ascott stages a two-term opposition (dry vs wet) only to resolve it in a third term (moist) that synthesizes the other two.

- **Dry** — the digital, virtual, silicon-based world: pixels, bits, code, virtual reality. "Dry reality is found in the arid spaces of VR." Immaterial, computational, exact.
- **Wet** — the biological, organic world: molecules, cells, flesh, "the nature that we nurture." Material, living, fluid.
- **Moist** — the third interspace where the dry and the wet converge: the substrate of artificial life, where "dry pixels and wet molecules converge." Neither purely digital nor purely biological — and, for Ascott, also "spiritually numinous," so that the convergence is finally a *threefold* one of the digital, the biological, and the spiritual.

## Why It Matters

The tension is the structural skeleton of Ascott's late-period thought and the opposition underlying his keystone concept [[Moistmedia]]. It reframes the vault's long-running [[Material vs Immaterial]] question in a way none of the prior eight authors do: rather than choosing the immaterial pole (information, pattern, the dematerialized art object) or defending the material pole (embodiment, the wet body), Ascott declares the binary itself obsolete. The twentieth century, he argues, drove art toward the dry and immaterial — virtuality, telematics, the dematerialization of the art object; the twenty-first century loops that immateriality back into the wet via biotechnology, "as silicon and pixels merge with molecules and matter." The *moist* is what that loop produces.

What the tripartition enables is a positive name for a synthesis that the binary can only gesture at. It lets Ascott treat artificial life, nanotechnology, and genetic intervention not as the digital *invading* the biological (or vice versa) but as a genuinely new ontological register — the ground of a "transformative art concerned with the construction of a fluid reality." It also carries Burnham's 1968 [[Object and System]] thesis forward: where Burnham tracked art's movement from the discrete material object to the immaterial system, Ascott pushes that same trajectory into the biological and spiritual, so that "system" becomes a *living*, self-organizing, sentient substrate (`Interpretive synthesis`).

What it can *block*, if taken too literally, is critical scrutiny: by christening the convergence "moist" and "spiritually numinous," Ascott folds the surveillance- and control-laden digital — what Han diagnoses as the [[Digital Panopticon]] — into an emancipatory, utopian register, leaving open whether the biological substrate actually changes the political stakes (`Speculative connection`).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Unlike most tensions in the vault, this one **arrives pre-synthesized**: the third pole *is* the synthesis. Ascott does not hold dry and wet in unresolved opposition — he resolves them, by fiat and by extrapolation, in the moist. The opposition is staged precisely in order to be dissolved: "Just as the development of interactive media in the past century… replaced the cult of the objet d'art with a process-based culture, so, at the start of this century, we see a further artistic shift, as silicon and pixels merge with molecules and matter" (`Directly stated`, ch.31).

What remains genuinely open is whether "moist" names a real third ontological state or is a rhetorical bridge that re-describes the convergence of computation and biotechnology under a more evocative word (`Interpretive synthesis`). The companion claim in ch.32 — that "the dematerialisation of art, telemedia, and virtual reality leads inexorably to the re-materialisation of culture in the form of artificial life" — treats the moist as the *necessary* outcome of the dry; but the inevitability is asserted, not demonstrated. And the spiritual third pole ("spiritually numinous," "MOIST MIND is technoetic multiconsciousness") sits uneasily beside the wet/dry pair as a different *kind* of term — less a state of matter than a claim about consciousness — so the "tripartition" is arguably a dyad (dry/wet) plus a qualifier (numinous), rather than three commensurable poles (`Interpretive synthesis`). The synthesis Ascott offers is therefore confident in its statement and unresolved in its grounding.
