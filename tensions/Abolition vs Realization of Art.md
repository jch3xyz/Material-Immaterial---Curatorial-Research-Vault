# Abolition vs Realization of Art

## The Poles

- **Abolition of art (Dadaism)** — the attempt to destroy the autonomous artistic sphere from within, to negate art as a separate activity. For [[Guy Debord]], Dada "sought to abolish art without realizing it": it carried the negation to the point of dissolving the artwork, but left the promise contained in art — the "community of dialogue" and the reconciled "game with time" (th. 187) — unfulfilled, so its destruction remained one-sided.
- **Realization of art (Surrealism)** — the attempt to fulfill art's utopian content, to carry the imagination of the artwork over into life. Surrealism "sought to realize art without abolishing it": it tried to make good on art's promise while preserving art as a separate sphere, so its fulfillment likewise remained one-sided — the realized dream still lodged inside the very "artistic sphere whose decrepitude they had denounced" (th. 191).

## Why It Matters

This is the internal contradiction of the historical avant-garde as Debord diagnoses it — the two "most important and radical contributions" of Dada and Surrealism, which "also revealed the internal inadequacy of their one-sided critiques" (th. 191). It matters because it names the trap of any art that tries to negate itself: to *abolish* the artwork without carrying over its content is to leave the world unchanged; to *realize* the content while keeping art autonomous is to leave the content quarantined in a sphere that no longer communicates. Debord frames both movements as "contemporaries of the last great offensive of the revolutionary proletarian movement," so the failure is not merely aesthetic — the "defeat of that movement... was the fundamental reason for their immobilization" (th. 191). The avant-garde's aesthetic impasse and the workers' movement's political defeat are the same event read twice.

The Situationist wager is that the two poles are not a choice but a false separation: "The critical position since developed by the Situationists has shown that the abolition and realization of art are inseparable aspects of a single transcendence of art" (th. 191). Held apart, each pole self-defeats; held together, they name a single supersession (*Aufhebung*) in which art is at once destroyed *as* a separate activity and preserved *as* the content — dialogue, play, the shaping of lived time — released into life. This is the same doubled logic Debord gives [[The End of Art]], whose "two opposing forms" (self-transcendence within total history versus preservation "as a dead object for spectacular contemplation," th. 184) map onto realization and abolition respectively. Understanding the tension is therefore the key to why Debord treats the museum, the neoliterature that "does nothing but contemplate the written word for its own sake," and the "complex neoartistic environments" of urbanism (th. 192) not as art's continuation but as its recuperated corpse — the [[Spectacle|spectacle]] preserving "past culture in congealed form" (th. 192).

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Guy Debord]] in [[The Society of the Spectacle]] (thesis 191, Chapter 8, "Negation and Consumption Within Culture") — stages the opposition as the defining contradiction of Dada and Surrealism and supersedes it with the Situationist thesis that abolition and realization are "inseparable aspects of a single transcendence of art." The whole argument sits inside Debord's account of [[The End of Art|the end of art]]: autonomous art's "declaration of independence is the beginning of its end" (th. 186), and the two avant-gardes are the terminal moment of that decomposition. The supersession is carried in practice by [[Détournement|détournement]] — "the flexible language of anti-ideology" (th. 208) — which neither preserves the artwork nor merely negates it but reuses its fragments in a present critique.

An **ancestor tension** for the vault's later art-abolition attempts. Debord's 1967 formulation is the direct forerunner of the [[Dematerialization|dematerialization]] of the art object that [[Lucy Lippard]] chronicles a few years later (roughly 1966–1972): [[Conceptual Art|conceptual art]]'s bid to make work "free of art-world commodity status" is another attempt to abolish art-as-commodity without abolishing art as such — and, like the avant-gardes before it, it ends in [[Recuperation|recuperation]] ("art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries"). The lineage is *interpretive synthesis*: Lippard does not cite Debord, and dematerialization deemphasizes the material *object* rather than negating the *institution* of art, but both sit on the same [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis and both discover that immateriality is no escape from commodity status. See [[Jack Burnham and Lucy Lippard]] and [[Roy Ascott and Lucy Lippard]] for the object→system→network continuation of the same drive.

## Related Concepts

- [[The End of Art]] — the containing concept; this tension is the specific avant-garde form of Debord's larger "end of art," whose "two opposing forms" (th. 184) are abolition and realization.
- [[Spectacle]] — the recuperating force: the spectacle "preserves past culture in congealed form" (th. 192), turning both the abolished and the realized artwork into "a dead object for spectacular contemplation" (th. 184).
- [[Détournement]] — the Situationist practice that performs the supersession, reusing cultural fragments rather than either preserving or merely destroying them (th. 208).
- [[Recuperation]] — why the one-sided poles fail: the spectacle can absorb "co-opted rehashes of its negative manifestations" (th. 192), neutralizing an abolition that does not pass into life.
- [[Dematerialization]] / [[Conceptual Art]] — the vault's later, art-historical descendants of the abolition impulse (Lippard, Burnham, Ascott); an ancestor-descendant link, not a citation.
- [[Reification]] — the Lukácsian substrate: separated culture congeals into a contemplated thing, which is precisely what both abolition and realization try, one-sidedly, to break.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the broader axis on which the later dematerialization attempts play out.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Debord offers an explicit synthesis, not an open aporia: the tension is **dissolved by supersession**. Abolition and realization are not two exclusive programs but "inseparable aspects of a single transcendence of art" (th. 191) — a Hegelian *Aufhebung* that at once negates art as a separate sphere and preserves its content by realizing it in a transformed everyday life. Crucially the synthesis "can no longer be a *cultural* negation": "The real values of culture can be maintained only by actually negating culture. But this negation can no longer be a cultural negation. It may in a sense take place within culture, but it points beyond it" (th. 210). The transcendence is therefore conditional on something outside art — "the resumption of revolutionary class struggle" (th. 203) — so the resolution is programmatic rather than accomplished. On Debord's own terms the synthesis has *not* been realized in fact: the avant-gardes were "immobilized" by the workers' movement's defeat (th. 191), and the spectacle continues to recuperate every partial negation. The tension is thus theoretically resolved and historically unresolved at once — which is exactly why it recurs, in a new key, in every later attempt to abolish the art object.
