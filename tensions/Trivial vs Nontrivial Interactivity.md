# Trivial vs Nontrivial Interactivity

## The Poles

- **Trivial interactivity (FDS)** — a *closed system with a finite data set*: action confined to pre-designed limits, offered as a "so-called interactive experience" while the **artist remains in control**. The viewer chooses among options the artist has already determined; the work cannot exceed its own authored boundaries.
- **Nontrivial interactivity (OES)** — an *open-ended system* with the capacity to accommodate new variables: interaction within networked or evolutive systems that **put the user or the environment in control**. The work has no fixed terminus; it absorbs the unforeseen and is co-authored by participation.

## Why It Matters

This is Ascott's sharpest critical refinement of the participation thesis. Much art that *claims* to have abolished the passive spectator merely substitutes a menu — a finite, branching, artist-controlled data set — for the canvas, and so retains "the inert viewer" in a new disguise (`Strongly implied`; cf. his "phantom audience" entry). The FDS/OES distinction supplies the criterion that separates genuine open-endedness from its simulation: not whether the user *does* something, but whether the system can *receive what was not pre-designed* and surrender authorial control to the user or the environment.

For the vault this draws a fault line *through* the category of interactive art rather than around it. It cuts directly across [[Observer vs Participator]] — Ascott's point is that the participator can still be trapped inside a closed FDS, so mere participation is not yet emancipation. And it extends Burnham's systems-esthetics lineage: where Burnham staged [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] as the move from kinetic spectacle to [[Reciprocal Communication]], Ascott specifies that even two-way exchange is "trivial" if the loop is closed and finite. Nontrivial interactivity is the condition Ascott's [[New Media Art]] and his program of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] aim at: an art of "open-ended, evolutive systems in the Net."

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not present the poles symmetrically — the distinction is *evaluative*. Trivial interactivity is a deficient, "so-called" form that the rhetoric of participation tends to disguise; nontrivial interactivity is the genuine telos of telematic art. The tension is therefore less a balance to be held than a threshold to be crossed: an FDS becomes nontrivial only by relinquishing artist control and acquiring "the open-ended capacity to accommodate new variables" (`Directly stated`). It nevertheless remains a live, unresolved fault line in practice, because — as Ascott's "phantom audience" warning makes clear — even works that proclaim the interactive user can smuggle the passive spectator back in, leaving most "interactive" art on the trivial side of the line (`Interpretive synthesis`).
