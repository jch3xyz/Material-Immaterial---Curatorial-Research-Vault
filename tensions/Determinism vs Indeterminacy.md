# Determinism vs Indeterminacy

## The Poles

- **The determinism of programmed machine processes** — the closed, guaranteed execution of automated procedures that "preempt and foreclose action alternatives." Outcomes are not predicted but *compelled*; certainty is manufactured by removing the very possibility of deviation. This is the engineered side of [[Economies of Action]] and [[The Uncontract]].
- **The indeterminacy of social processes** — the open, unfinished texture of human relations: promise, dialogue, dispute, trust, and the unsettled future they leave room for. Indeterminacy here is not a defect to be cured but the *raison d'être* of institutions like the contract, which exist precisely because the future is not yet decided.

## Why It Matters

Zuboff's framing is deliberately ontological rather than psychological. The phrase she chooses — replacing "the indeterminacy of social processes with the determinism of programmed machine processes" — favors **Determinism vs Indeterminacy** over the softer **Certainty vs Uncertainty** (`Directly stated`, Ch. 7). The difference is load-bearing: uncertainty names a *feeling* that prediction might relieve, whereas indeterminacy names a *property of social reality itself* — its openness, its capacity to be otherwise — which surveillance capitalism does not merely reduce but abolishes. What is at stake is therefore not better forecasting but the **substitution of machine action for society**: "This is not the automation of society, as some might think, but rather the replacement of society with machine action dictated by economic imperatives" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 7).

The tension exposes what is lost when "certainty" is manufactured. The indeterminate social processes that the uncontract overwrites are not noise; they are "promises, dialogue, shared meaning, problem solving, dispute resolution, and trust: the expressions of solidarity and human agency that have been gradually institutionalized in the notion of 'contract' over the course of millennia" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 7). To swap their indeterminacy for machine determinism is to bypass "all that social work in favor of compulsion" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 7). The open future — the space in which a person could still act, renege, repair, or surprise — is foreclosed so that outcomes can be "guaranteed" for the sake of prediction products. Understanding the tension this way lets us see the uncontract not as a contract at all but as "the annihilation of contract" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 7) — a unilateral execution that makes human agency unnecessary.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Shoshana Zuboff]] in [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]] — names the substitution directly in the discussion of [[The Uncontract]] (Ch. 7, "Executing the Uncontract"): the machine determinism of [[Economies of Action]] replaces the indeterminacy of the social, dissolving promise and trust into compelled outcomes. She frames this against Langdon Winner's warning about surrender to "technological determinism" as accumulated, unexamined "drift" (`Directly stated`, Ch. 7).

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Zuboff stages this as a substitution, not a balance — and she does not treat it as a tension to be *held*. For her the indeterminacy of social processes is the very thing worth defending; its replacement by machine determinism is a loss, not a synthesis. The "certainty" produced is therefore counterfeit: it is achieved only by destroying the social conditions (promise, trust, the open future) that made certainty meaningful as a human achievement in the first place. The tension remains **unresolved by design** — surveillance capitalism resolves it unilaterally, in favor of determinism, and Zuboff's critique is precisely a refusal of that resolution. Any genuine synthesis would have to restore the right to an indeterminate future — what she later develops as "the right to the future tense" — rather than trade it away for guaranteed outcomes (`Interpretive synthesis`, reading Ch. 7 forward to the book's broader argument).
