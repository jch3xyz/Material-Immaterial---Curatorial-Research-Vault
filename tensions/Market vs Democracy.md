# Market vs Democracy

## The Poles

- **Private market power** — the prerogative of capital to concentrate knowledge, freedom, and the power to order society within its own domain, answerable to no one. In its surveillance-capitalist form this becomes a "collectivist orientation" in which "it is the market, not the state, which concentrates both knowledge and freedom within its domain" — a privatized social order that "imposes the social relations of a premodern absolutist authority" and is "a form of tyranny that feeds on people but is not of the people."
- **Popular sovereignty** — the countervailing claim of the demos: "a people's inalienable right to rule themselves." For Zuboff this is materialized historically in the [[Organic Reciprocities]] that once bound capitalism to the rise of democracy (the consumer reciprocities of the American Revolution, the employment reciprocities behind Britain's expanding franchise), and politically in the institutions — law, the vote, "democratic institutions that produce durable and effective countermeasures" — that can still mitigate even "abnormal" dynamics of accumulation.

## Why It Matters

For Shoshana Zuboff this is the master political axis of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*: the diagnosis of the whole as [[The Coup from Above]] is precisely the diagnosis of private market power overrunning popular sovereignty. She names surveillance capitalism's "antidemocratic and antiegalitarian juggernaut" a "market-driven coup from above" — "not a coup d'état in the classic sense but rather a coup de gens: an overthrow of the people." The stake is therefore the location of sovereignty itself: whether the "central principle of social ordering in the twenty-first century" is decided by a self-governing people or privatized by an unaccountable market power. `Directly stated`

The tension is **historical, not eternal**. Zuboff argues that market capitalism and democracy were once interdependent, knit together by [[Organic Reciprocities]] — firms relying on people as both employees and customers, so that "economic power translated into political power." It was the *violation* of those reciprocities (the colonists' "empire of goods" turned into "symbols of imperial oppression") that "awakened an unstoppable march toward liberty." What makes surveillance capitalism distinctive is that it "formally rescinds any remaining reciprocities with its societies": [[Hyperscale]] firms employ almost no one and treat users as raw material rather than customers, so capital no longer materially depends on the prosperity of the people. Severed from reciprocity, market power has nothing left to bind it to democracy — and so it tilts toward [[Radical Indifference]] and the [[The Hive|hive collective]] rather than toward the shared institutions of self-rule. `Directly stated`

Understanding the axis this way blocks the framing of the conflict as state versus market, or socialism versus capitalism. Zuboff's "new form of collectivism" is *market*-based, not state-based — Hayek's derided "termite ants" reappearing inside the firm. The opposition is not between two economic systems but between **private power and public power**: between a market that "concentrates both knowledge and freedom within its domain" and a people's claim to author the conditions of its own life. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Shoshana Zuboff]] in [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]] (Chapter 18, "A Coup from Above") — stages the opposition as the book's concluding political diagnosis: surveillance capitalism as a "market-driven coup from above," a *coup de gens* that overthrows popular sovereignty by privatizing the principle of social ordering, after severing the [[Organic Reciprocities]] that once bound capitalism to democracy.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Coup from Above]] — the name for the outcome when private market power prevails over popular sovereignty: an overthrow of the people (*coup de gens*), not of a state.
- [[Organic Reciprocities]] — the historic mutual dependencies that bound market capitalism to the rise of democracy; their rescission is what unmoors the market pole from the democratic one.
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the "antidemocratic and antiegalitarian" market form whose extractive logic drives the coup.
- [[Radical Indifference]] — the asocial mode of operation that replaces reciprocity once structural independence "breeds exclusion rather than inclusion."
- [[Hyperscale]] — the discontinuous business form (massive infrastructure, minimal workforce) that materializes capital's structural independence from people.
- [[The Hive]] — the market-based "new form of collectivism" toward which surveillance capitalism aims us, displacing the values of "market capitalism and market democracy."
- [[Neoliberalism]] — the ideological habitat that recast democratic regulation as a threat, preparing the ground for market power's claim against popular sovereignty.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Zuboff leaves the tension **open and actively contested** — a wager, not a guarantee. The market pole is, in her account, structurally driven away from democracy: having rescinded its reciprocities, surveillance capital has no internal incentive to subordinate itself to collective self-government, and aims instead at the hive. Yet she refuses resignation. The resolution she offers is *external and normative*: a reassertion of popular sovereignty through democratic institutions — "democracy remains our only channel for reformation… a people's inalienable right to rule themselves." She invokes Piketty's insistence that even abnormal accumulation "can again be — mitigated by democratic institutions," concluding "If we are to regain control of capital, we must bet everything on democracy." The synthesis, if it comes, is not a balance to be struck but a victory to be won by the demos over private power. `Interpretive synthesis`

Distinguish this from [[Freedom vs Democracy]]. That tension concerns the *corporation's claim to a lawless space* — the "Cry Freedom" demand to operate where democratic law cannot reach. *Market vs Democracy* names the broader and more structural axis beneath it: not the firm's freedom *from* law, but private market power *as such* set against the people's right to govern themselves — the axis of the coup de gens and of the organic reciprocities that once held the two together. The two are intimately linked — the founders' lawless freedom is one expression of unaccountable market power — but they name different oppositions and should not be merged. `Speculative connection`
