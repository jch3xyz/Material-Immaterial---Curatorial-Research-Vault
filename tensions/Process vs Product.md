# Process vs Product

## The Poles

- **Process** — art conceived as behaviour, system, and software: an open, temporal, unfolding transaction with no fixed terminus, in which meaning is generated *between* the parts of the system rather than fixed in a made thing. For [[Roy Ascott]] this is the side cybernetic, behaviourist art declares for.
- **Product** — art conceived as the finished, bounded object: form, structure, and hardware — the discrete, completed artefact of the formalist modernist aesthetic, with its "final outcome" and "absolute resolution."

## Why It Matters

This is the founding inversion of [[Roy Ascott]]'s behaviourist art and the axis on which his entire program turns: when "art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware," and so "[p]rocess replaces product in importance" (*Directly stated*). It reorients value away from the made artefact and toward the temporal system in which the viewer participates — the precondition for [[New Media Art]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], and [[Reciprocal Communication]].

The axis is the **behaviourist twin** of Jack Burnham's `Object vs System` (see [[Object vs System]]) and is intimately bound to [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott pairs "process/system/software" against "product/structure/hardware" in a single move. But it is **distinct**: where Burnham's spine runs from the discrete object to the systems-relational field, Ascott's axis privileges the **temporal/processual** dimension specifically — the "no final outcome, no ultimate climax... irreversible conclusion" of an art that "aspires to the condition of exchange" (*Directly stated*). And critically, Ascott refuses the eliminative reading: "Process culture and behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object" (*Directly stated*) — the object survives, but only as a "behaviourable," a node in a process, not as a terminal product. To collapse this tension into mere dematerialization or object-abolition is to miss that Ascott keeps the object while changing what it is *for*.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Roy Ascott]] in [[Telematic Embrace]] — stages the tension as the core wager of behaviourist art: "a shift, in short, towards an art of system and process," where appreciation "resides in our perception and awareness of a system rather than an object" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/07 Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision|ch.07]]); "software predominates over hardware... Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/08 Behaviourables and Futuribles|ch.08]]). His "Field Theory for Postmodernist Art" takes "as its focus not form but behaviour" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/13 Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art|ch.13]]).

> Cross-reference: Jack Burnham's [[Object vs System]] tension (in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]) is the sculptural/object-historical counterpart of this behaviourist axis; both feed the seven-and-more-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension. Ascott's privileging of *time/process* and his insistence that the object survives differentiate his axis from Burnham's object→system spine.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not present this as a tension to be resolved in favour of one pole but as a *re-valuation*: the product is not destroyed, it is re-functioned. "Process culture and behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object, as long as it means the beginning of new values for art" — the object persists as a "behaviourable," read "like the palm of your hand," i.e. as something to be acted with rather than contemplated as a closed whole (*Directly stated*). The synthesis Ascott offers is the **open system**: "Art, then, resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system" (*Directly stated*, ch.10). The tension is thus held productively rather than dissolved — product becomes a moment within process, never abolished, but no longer the locus of value (*Interpretive synthesis*).
