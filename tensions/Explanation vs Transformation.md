# Explanation vs Transformation

## The Poles

- **Explanation (science)** — the reductionist project of *accounting for* consciousness: explaining, understanding, reporting it as an object of knowledge. For Ascott this is the scientific relation to mind, and it is failing — consciousness "poses the most intractable of problems."
- **Transformation (art)** — the artistic relation to consciousness: not to explain it but to *explore, transform, and reframe* it. The artist does not stand outside consciousness reporting on it; the artist works *within* it, changing what it is. This is the polemical hinge of Ascott's [[Technoetics|technoetic]] program.

## Why It Matters

This tension is the load-bearing claim that lets Ascott put consciousness "at the centre of art" without competing with neuroscience or philosophy on their own ground. Science approaches mind from the outside, seeking a *description* that closes the gap of [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|the hard problem]] — and, on Ascott's reading, fails. The artist's mode is categorically different: not explanatory but generative. "Technology plus mind," he argues, can "lead to distinctly new forms of art, new qualities of mind, new forms of cognition and perception" — that is, art does not report on consciousness as it is, it *makes new states of consciousness*.

The stakes are method and authority. If consciousness is to be *explained*, art is at best illustration and at worst the "dazzling special effects" of a digital craft that has replaced meaning-making. If consciousness is to be *transformed*, art becomes a first-order technology of mind — the [[Technoetics|technoetic]] aesthetic — with its own irreducible claim. The contrast also reframes [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]]: where reductionist science would dissolve the mystery of mind, the artist preserves and works it, treating consciousness as a field to be navigated (the [[Cyberception|psi-perception]]/cyberception pole) rather than a problem to be solved. Understanding the tension clarifies why Ascott's late work turns to shamanism and altered states: these are *technologies of transformation*, not instruments of explanation.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Ascott does not seek to *resolve* the tension so much as to *divide the labor* across it: science may keep trying to explain consciousness, while art takes a wholly different, non-competing relation to it. The two are not rival answers to one question but two modes — one explanatory, one transformative. Yet the synthesis is unstable, because Ascott's deeper claim (that "technology plus mind" can "explore consciousness more thoroughly") quietly lets art back onto science's epistemic ground: an art that *explores* consciousness is, at the limit, also producing knowledge of it. The tension is therefore best held open as a polemic — a programmatic insistence on the artist's distinct, transformative vocation — rather than dissolved into a clean division of intellectual labor. It remains **productively unresolved**: the value of the contrast is precisely the pressure it puts on what counts as knowing a mind.
