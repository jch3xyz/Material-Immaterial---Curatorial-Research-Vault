# Counting vs Recounting

In the "Big Data" chapter of *[[Psychopolitics]]*, [[Byung-Chul Han]] condenses his critique of [[Dataism]] into a single play on words: **"Counting is not recounting."** The opposition turns on the gap between the additive operation that produces data (*counting* — enumeration, measurement, accumulation) and the narrative operation that produces meaning, self-knowledge, and memory (*recounting* — giving an account, narrating, drawing a conclusion). It is the [[Psychopolitics]]-register extension of the temporal-aesthetic opposition Han stages elsewhere as [[Narration vs Addition]]: where that tension contrasts narrative time with additive time, this one isolates the epistemic and ethical claim that no quantity of data, however total, can recount a self, a meaning, or a memory.

## The Poles

- **Counting** — The additive operation of data and numbers: measurement, enumeration, accumulation, storage. "Data and numbers are not narrative; they are additive." Counting absolutizes the measurable ("everything that can be measured should be measured"), and it underwrites [[Dataism]], the [[Quantified Self]], and the "total memory of the Digital." Counting fills the senseless void but produces no meaning: "Data simply fills up the senseless void." Its temporality is undead — "Digital memory consists of indifferent — as it were, undead — points of presence." On Han's reading of [[Hegel]], counting is "purely additive; it never comes to an end, to a conclusion."
- **Recounting** — The narrative operation of meaning, memory, self, and theory: narration, account-giving, the drawing of a conclusion (*Schluss*/syllogism). "Meaning, on the other hand, is based on narration." Recounting is what yields self-knowledge ("A sense of self derives from giving an account"), what constitutes living memory ("Human memory is a narrative, an account"), and what theoretical thinking does that data cannot ("theoretical thinking represents a narrative form of knowledge"). Recounting concludes; it excludes further addition.

## Why It Matters

This single opposition grounds three of the chapter's most consequential claims:

1. **Dataism abolishes meaning.** Because "[d]ata and numbers are not narrative; they are additive," and because "[m]eaning ... is based on narration," the rise of data-driven knowledge is for Han not a gain in meaning but its evacuation: "Dataism is nihilism. It gives up on any and all meaning." Big Data does not interpret; it correlates. The counting/recounting gap is why Han can equate "digital Dadaism" with the emptying of sense (`Directly stated`).

2. **The [[Quantified Self]] yields no self-knowledge.** The motto "Self Knowledge through Numbers" is, on Han's account, a category error: "no insight into the self can result from data and numbers alone, no matter how exhaustive they are. Numbers do not recount anything about the self." Self-knowledge is an effect of recounting — of *Publicatio sui*, of writing and account-giving — not of measurement. Exhaustiveness of data is no remedy, because the deficiency is not quantitative but kind: counting cannot become recounting by accumulating (`Directly stated`).

3. **Digital memory is undead addition.** Living [[Memory vs Storage|memory]] is "a narrative, an account," subject to constant "retranscription" (Freud); digital memory is "a matter of seamless addition and accumulation." Stored data "admit counting, but they cannot be recounted." A timeline "recounts nothing. It simply enumerates and adds up events." Hence the diagnosis that "digitalized life lacks animacy. Digital temporality belongs to the undead" (`Directly stated`).

Curatorially and methodologically, the tension names what is lost when an archive becomes a database, when a life becomes a feed, and when interpretation is replaced by correlation: the recounting that turns enumerated traces into an account capable of meaning.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in *[[Psychopolitics]]* — explicitly coins "Counting is not recounting" in the "Big Data" chapter and threads it through three sub-arguments: [[Dataism]] (meaning), [[Quantified Self]] (self), and "Forgetting" ([[Memory vs Storage|memory]]).
- Han mobilizes [[Hegel]] to extend the opposition into the register of *Spirit*: the syllogism (*Schluss*, conclusion) is "not addition, but narration," and "Big Data is purely additive; it never comes to an end, to a conclusion." See [[Byung-Chul Han and Hegel]]. This converts the counting/recounting gap into the claim that "[t]otalized data-knowledge amounts to absolute ignorance: the absolute zero of Spirit" (`Directly stated`).
- The opposition borrows the German wordplay (*zählen* vs *erzählen*) that Han first deploys in *[[The Transparency Society]]* — making this tension the [[Psychopolitics]] sibling of [[Narration vs Addition]].

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Han does **not** offer this as a balanced polarity to be held in tension. As with [[Narration vs Addition]], the two poles are asymmetrically valued and the relation is one-directional: recounting can do everything counting does and more (a narrative still contains its enumerated events), but counting can never become recounting, "no matter how exhaustive" the data. Accumulation does not converge on a conclusion: "constant addition yields no conclusion, no syllogism." The deficit is therefore structural, not a matter of insufficient data — which is precisely why "the second Enlightenment" of Big Data cannot, on Han's account, recover the meaning it dissolves.

The tension is **diagnostic rather than dialectical**: Han names with precision what counting forecloses (meaning, self, living memory, Spirit) but specifies no mechanism internal to the data regime by which recounting might be restored. The implied remedy lies outside counting altogether — in narrative, account-giving, and the syllogistic closure of theory — and whether such recounting can survive inside additive digital infrastructures is left open (`Interpretive synthesis`).
