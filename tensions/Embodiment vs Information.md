# Embodiment vs Information

The master opposition of *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*. Across the whole book [[N. Katherine Hayles]] tracks the postwar fantasy that the human self is, at bottom, an informational pattern that could in principle be peeled off its material substrate and re-instantiated in another — downloaded into a computer, jacked into cyberspace, evolved inside a [[The Computational Universe|computational universe]] — and sets against it her own counter-thesis that information has no existence apart from the embodiment that brings it into being. This is the axis on which her account of [[Posthumanism|the posthuman]] turns: the "good" posthuman is not the one freed from the body but the one that recovers it.

## General Orientation

This is a multi-author tension, and the authors do not all occupy the same ground. The axis runs between two poles — identity as a disembodied **informational pattern** versus identity as enacted, material **embodiment** — but the writers gathered here stand in different relations to it:

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** *(the critic of the information pole).* Hayles names the disembodiment fantasy, traces its postwar construction, and argues it against — her whole book is written *against* the reduction of the self to pattern. She is the author who articulates the tension as a tension and resolves it asymmetrically: embodiment is the *condition* of information, not its rival.
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** *(the proponent of the information pole).* Kurzweil asserts as a goal precisely the disembodiment Hayles names a "dream or nightmare." For him the living body is "version 1.0" overhead — "frail," "cumbersome," its neuronal complexity mostly "life-support, not information-processing" — and the real self is the pattern, to be ported off onto a "more suitable computational substrate." This is the cleanest head-to-head in the vault: the position Hayles spends a book refuting, stated affirmatively and as a destiny.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** *(a thematic resonance, not a direct participant).* Han is not writing about cybernetics or the body's data-pattern; his contact with the axis is oblique, defending opacity and lived bodily [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)|experience]] against the regime of total informational [[Transparency]]. Treat his presence here as resonance, not as a claim about uploading.

The sections below give the poles in Hayles's own terms, then Kurzweil's affirmative occupation of the information pole, before returning to Hayles's resolution and the internal friction (the Edelman caveat) that Kurzweil himself records.

## The Poles

- **Information** — the dream (or "nightmare," as Hayles names it) that "human identity is essentially an informational pattern rather than an embodied enaction." On this pole, identity is a [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] defined by a probability distribution, "conceptually distinct from the markers that embody it," a "bodiless fluid that could flow between different substrates without loss of meaning or form." The body becomes mere "meat," a disposable support system; consciousness can be stored "ona little disk." This is the pole of [[The Body as Data]] — the erasure of embodiment performed at the inaugural moment of the computer age in the [[Turing Test (Hayles)|Turing test]] and made literal in the Moravec scenario of downloading.

- **Embodiment** — the counter-claim that identity is "an embodied enaction," "always instantiated, local, and specific." Embodiment is not the body-as-concept but the lived, material, enacting body: "thought is a much broader cognitive function depending for its specificities on the embodied form enacting it." On this pole, "information, like humanity, cannot exist apart from the embodiment that brings it into being as a material entity in the world." See [[Embodiment]], [[The Materiality of Informatics]].

## Why It Matters

Everything in the book's politics of the posthuman rides on this tension. If identity is information, then the human is in principle transferable, replicable, and obsolete — Moravec's machines simply inherit the pattern, and "the days of the human race are numbered." If identity is embodiment, then the human is "the net result of thousands of years of sedimented evolutionary history," irreducibly local, and "embodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated." Hayles's wager is that the first reading is not a neutral scientific fact but a historically constructed *choice* — information "is a socially constructed concept" that "could have been, and was, given different definitions" — and that the way to "intervene constructively" is to refuse the antagonism between the poles and read them as "complementary rather than antagonistic." The danger she names is not the posthuman as such but "the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self" (see [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]]): downloading "you" into a computer does not abandon the autonomous liberal subject but expands its prerogatives. The tension, properly held, is what lets the posthuman become "leverage to avoid reinscribing, and thus repeating, some of the mistakes of the past."

## The Body as Overhead (Kurzweil)

Where Hayles writes against the reduction of the self to pattern, [[Ray Kurzweil]] in *[[The Singularity Is Near]]* writes *for* it — and states as a goal exactly the disembodiment she names a "dream or nightmare." His argument occupies the information pole at its sharpest. For Kurzweil the living body is not the condition of the self but its drag: "Our version 1.0 biological bodies are likewise frail and subject to a myriad of failure modes, not to mention the cumbersome maintenance rituals they require." The crucial move — the one that makes the whole [[Mind Uploading|uploading]] program coherent — is a partition of the body into signal and overhead. Surveying the [[The Computational Universe|computational]] estimate of the brain, he concludes: "Most of the complexity of a human neuron is devoted to maintaining its life-support functions, not its information-processing capabilities. Ultimately, we will be able to port our mental processes to a more suitable computational substrate. Then our minds won't have to stay so small." On this reading the neuron's living biology is mere life-support — overhead, not self — so the information-processing [[Patternism|pattern]] *is* the real self and the body is discardable. `Directly stated`.

This is the precise inverse of Hayles's thesis. Where she holds that "embodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated," Kurzweil treats replication onto a new substrate as the destiny of mind, and reframes the loss of the body as a *liberation* rather than a death. He even rescues the word the critics use against him: against the objection that an uploaded or "virtual body is not a real body," he answers that "the word 'virtual' is somewhat unfortunate. It implies 'not real,' but the reality will be that a virtual body is just as real as a physical body in all the ways that matter." The body's materiality, in other words, is demoted to one substrate among possible substrates — exactly [[The Body as Data|the body as data]] that Hayles's whole book argues against. `Directly stated`.

Kurzweil also records, and rebuts, the strongest version of the embodied objection — the internal friction on the information pole. He cites Gerald Edelman's caveat that "there is a difference between a capability and a description of that capability. A photograph of a person is different from the person herself, even if the 'photograph' is very high resolution and three-dimensional" — i.e. a description is not the capability, the static pattern is not the living enaction. This is, in effect, Hayles's point arriving from inside Kurzweil's own text. But he turns it: uploading "goes beyond the extremely high-resolution scan," because the scan "needs to be instantiated into a working computational medium that has the capabilities of the original," so that "the neural details ... interact with one another (and with the outside world) in the same ways that they do in the original." The rebuttal concedes Hayles's premise — that pattern without enaction is inert — and then claims enaction can itself be re-instantiated on a non-biological substrate. The tension thus does not dissolve: it relocates to a single disputed question — whether the embodied enaction Hayles calls irreplicable is, as Kurzweil insists, portable. `Directly stated` (that Kurzweil makes the argument); `Interpretive synthesis` (that this is the same friction Hayles names, restaged inside the proponent's own text). See the cross-link [[Ray Kurzweil and N. Katherine Hayles]] for the head-to-head.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[N. Katherine Hayles]] in *[[How We Became Posthuman]]* — the primary source. Hayles stages the opposition at the [[Turing Test (Hayles)|Turing test]] ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/02 Prologue|Prologue]]), gives it its sharpest formulation as the "dream or nightmare of the body as information" ([[Flickering Signifier|Virtual Bodies and Flickering Signifiers]]), finds her resolution in [[Autopoiesis]] ("information without a body does not exist"), in [[The Observer|the observer]] who can never be left out of [[The Three Waves of Cybernetics|Artificial Life]] ("bodies can never be made of information alone"), and in the [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/13 Conclusion - What Does It Mean to be Posthuman|Conclusion]], where the benign posthuman is "grounded in embodied actuality rather than disembodied information."
- [[Ray Kurzweil]] in *[[The Singularity Is Near]]* — the **proponent of the information pole**. Kurzweil occupies the exact position Hayles argues against: the living body is "version 1.0" overhead, "frail" and "cumbersome" ([[raw/Kurzweil - The Singularity Is Near/05 Ch1 - The Six Epochs|The Six Epochs]]); most of a neuron's complexity is "life-support, not information-processing," so the [[Patternism|pattern]] is the self and the body can be "port[ed]" off onto "a more suitable computational substrate" ([[raw/Kurzweil - The Singularity Is Near/07 Ch3 - Achieving the Computational Capacity of the Human Brain|Achieving the Computational Capacity of the Human Brain]]); a re-instantiated "virtual body is just as real as a physical body in all the ways that matter," and Edelman's "a description is not the capability" caveat is recorded and rebutted via re-instantiation ([[raw/Kurzweil - The Singularity Is Near/08 Ch4 - How to Reverse Engineer the Human Brain|How to Reverse Engineer the Human Brain]]). This is the cleanest head-to-head in the vault — Kurzweil asserting as a goal exactly the disembodiment Hayles names a "dream or nightmare." See [[Ray Kurzweil and N. Katherine Hayles]].
- [[Byung-Chul Han]] in *[[The Transparency Society]]* — *(interpretive)*. Han is not writing about cybernetics or the body's data-pattern, so the contact is thematic, not direct. But Han's diagnosis of [[Hyperinformation]] — the accumulation of additive, transparent [[Information (Han)|information]] that "brings no light into darkness" and hollows out [[Narrativity|narrative]], [[Negativity|negativity]], and the bodily, erotic [[The Veil|veil]] — runs parallel to Hayles's worry that the triumph of pattern produces a "systematic devaluation of materiality and embodiment." Where Hayles defends the enacting body against the informational pattern, Han defends opacity, distance, and lived [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)|experience]] against the regime of total informational [[Transparency]]. Both stage the immaterial-informational against the embodied-material, from opposite disciplinary doors. Mark this connection as a thematic resonance, not a claim of influence.

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Hayles does not leave the tension open; she resolves it asymmetrically. The two poles are not equal partners — embodiment is the *condition* of information, not its opposite. "Information, like humanity, cannot exist apart from the embodiment that brings it into being as a material entity in the world." Autopoietic theory makes the point sharpest: "information without a body does not exist other than as an inference drawn by an observer." So the synthesis is not a midpoint but a re-grounding: pattern and presence are to be read "as complementary rather than antagonistic," but only because the informational pole is shown to be derivative of, and dependent on, the embodied one — "it is precisely because material interfaces have changed that pattern and randomness can be perceived as dominant." The benign posthuman is therefore "grounded in embodied actuality rather than disembodied information." The tension is *held* rather than dissolved: Hayles wants to keep the productive power of the pattern/randomness dialectic while denying it the right to erase the body. This is the resolution that distinguishes this tension from the broader cross-author axis of [[Material vs Immaterial]], which spans the vault rather than restating Hayles's specific verdict.
