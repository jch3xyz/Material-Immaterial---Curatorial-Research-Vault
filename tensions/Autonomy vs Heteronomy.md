# Autonomy vs Heteronomy

The opposition that Shoshana Zuboff places at the moral center of chapter 10 ("Make Them Dance"). When surveillance capitalism completes its "means of behavior modification" — what she calls [[Economies of Action]] — it does not merely predict behavior, it acts on it. The result is a contest between two ancient ways of regulating action: **autonomy**, "regulation by the self," and **heteronomy**, "regulation by others." Zuboff's claim is that the competitive logic of economies of action drives surveillance capitalists to *supplant the first with the second*, exiling us from our own behavior.

## The Poles

- **Autonomy** — "regulation by the self." Zuboff traces the word to its Greek root and aligns it with the cluster philosophers call "freedom of will": "self-regulation," "self-determination," "autonomy." It is the precondition for "empathy, volition, reflection, personal development, authenticity, integrity, learning, goal accomplishment, impulse control, creativity," and for the self-regulating choices of "agreement and disagreement, participation and withdrawal, resistance or collaboration." In the future tense, autonomy is the voice that says **"I will."**
- **Heteronomy** — "regulation by others." Behavior is shaped from the outside — by tuning, herding, and conditioning that "bypass human awareness, individual decision rights, and the entire complex of self-regulatory processes." Heteronomy "shifts the locus of control over the future tense from 'I will' to **'You will.'**" Its endgame, Zuboff warns, is "a new regressive age of autonomous capital and heteronomous individuals."

## Why It Matters

The tension reframes the stakes of behavior modification from privacy to *will*. Zuboff's argument is that human awareness is structurally incompatible with the surveillance-capitalist project: "There is no autonomous judgment without awareness," and so "human consciousness itself is a threat to surveillance revenues" (`Directly stated`). Because economies of action need outcomes to be *guaranteed*, awareness — the seat of autonomy — must be circumvented rather than persuaded. This is what distinguishes [[Telestimulation]] from older forms of influence like advertising: it aims not at the mind that chooses but at behavior that can be actuated beneath choice.

The pole-pair is therefore the engine of Zuboff's larger claim about the future. Autonomy is the capacity to author one's own "I will"; heteronomy installs a foreign "You will" in its place. The struggle for social power, she argues, is no longer over the "hidden facts of class and its relationship to production" but over "the hidden facts of automated engineered behavior modification" (`Directly stated`). The asymmetry is what makes it "regressive": the *firm* becomes autonomous (free to experiment on populations for profit) while the *individual* becomes heteronomous — the reverse of the arrangement on which "democratic flourishing and human fulfillment depend."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

Zuboff does not hold the poles in productive balance — she treats their reversal as a political emergency to be resisted, not a paradox to be savored. The book's recovered counter-history (the 1970s US mobilization "to resist, regulate, and control the means of behavioral modification," the Common Rule, Chomsky's defense of "autonomous man" against Skinner) is offered precisely so that autonomy might be reasserted against an ascendant heteronomy. The tension is thus **structurally unresolved by design**: it stands open as a call to restore the conditions of self-regulation — awareness, decision rights, the "I will" — before "autonomous capital and heteronomous individuals" becomes the settled order. Synthesis, in Zuboff's terms, would not be a blending of the poles but the political re-subordination of heteronomous power to autonomous human ends (`Interpretive synthesis`).
