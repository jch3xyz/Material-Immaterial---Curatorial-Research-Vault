# Form vs Light

## The Poles

- **Form** — sculpture conceived as solid physical mass: a coherent, self-contained body whose meaning resides in its shape, proportion, structure, and the interior psyche "buried within the object itself." The classical-Euclidean and vitalist understanding of the work as a unified material presence.
- **Light** — sculpture conceived through its surface optics: reflection, transparency, color, and emission. Here "light-not form-determines our perception," the object is reduced to "a modulator of light," and what matters "lies on its surface for optical examination" rather than in any inner mass.

## Why It Matters

This tension is the optical turn at the close of [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]'s Chapter 3, and it is the hinge on which Burnham's whole argument swings from sculpture-as-object toward sculpture-as-system. After tracking the exhaustion of the formalist vocabulary — proportion, scale, structure, mass — Burnham names what has quietly replaced it: not a new repertoire of *forms* but a different *perceptual basis* altogether. If "in the past decade the tacit conviction has arisen that light-not form-determines our perception of sculpture" (`Directly stated`), then the locus of the work migrates from the body of the object to the optical event on its skin — and, finally, to the perceiving subject.

What is at stake is the substantiality of the art object itself. The optical conviction is, "in the broadest sense," a polemic against [[Vitalism]] — "a reaction against the vitalist myth that the psyche of sculpture is buried within the object itself" (`Directly stated`). Once significance is relocated to the reflective/emitting surface, "form and matter lose their substantiality." Burnham's most dematerializing sentence states the resolution of the tension in light's favor: "Form, in effect, is what we make it appear to be" (`Directly stated`). This is why the tension is inseparable from [[Dematerialization]] and feeds directly into [[Material vs Immaterial]]: the optical work is a step in matter's withdrawal from the art object.

## Authors and Books That Discuss It

- [[Jack Burnham]] in [[Beyond Modern Sculpture]] (Ch. 3, "Formalism: The Weary Vocabulary," section "Optical Properties of Sculpture") — stages the opposition explicitly. The "greatest sculptors of the recent past saw their works quite analytically, in fact dispassionately, for their optical effects created purely as light-reflecting sources." The mechanism is a reversal of priority: "As the need to control light (for we perceive forms in terms of their light-emitting properties) gained priority, the tendency grew to construct works solely designed as modulators of light." Burnham gathers the fragmented-reflective tendency under Herbert Read's term, the "glitter principle" — Lippold, Lassaw, and Bertoia scattering reflection across many small, oddly pitched surfaces "instead of deriving reflection from a continuous, coherent mass of metal."

## Possible Synthesis or Unresolved Status

In Burnham's own text the tension is **not held in equilibrium — it is resolved in light's favor**, and that resolution is itself the problem. "Form, in effect, is what we make it appear to be" dissolves the object into the perceiver's optical act, which is a genuine liberation of sculpture from inert mass but also, Burnham warns, risks "a cult devoted to optical deception and excitation" — kin to the spectacle of the neon sign, the pylon, and advertising he repeatedly invokes (`Strongly implied`). The unresolved residue: once meaning "lies on its surface for optical examination," what distinguishes optical sculpture from pure display? The tension therefore remains productively open as a question about *where* the work is located — in the mass, on the surface, or in perception — even though Burnham has tipped its weight decisively toward light (`Interpretive synthesis`).
