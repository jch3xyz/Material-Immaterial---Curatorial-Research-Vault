# Money — Marshall McLuhan

## Author's Definition

For Marshall McLuhan, money is not primarily a unit of value or a medium of exchange in the economist's sense but a **medium of communication** — a technology that, like any other, is an extension of man and obeys the same laws as writing, the clock, and the phonetic alphabet. He states the definition most compactly in the chapter's culminating sentence, where money is figured as a relation rather than a thing:

> "Money talks" because money is a metaphor, a transfer, and
a bridge. Like words and language, money is a storehouse of communally achieved work, skill,
and experience.

So defined, money has two faces. As a **storehouse** it accumulates and holds the work, skill, and experience of a community; as a **metaphor, transfer, and bridge** it translates one kind of human effort into any other and carries it across distance. McLuhan immediately classes it with the visual technologies that fragment and specialize, pairing it against writing and the clock:

> Money, however, is also a specialist technology like writing; and as writing
intensifies the visual aspect of speech and order, and as the clock visually separates time
from space, So money separates work from the other social functions.

The translating function is concrete: money is "a language for translating the work of the farmer into the work of the barber, doctor, engineer, or plumber." And because it bridges and translates, its essential character is reach — the capacity to act where the body is not, in both dimensions at once:

> It is action at a distance, both in space and in time.

Money is therefore defined less by what it *is* than by what it *does*: it stores communal work and translates it across the social field, in space and in time, exactly as language and writing do. `Directly stated`

## Context

The definition appears in Part II's chapter "Money: The Poor Man's Credit Card," one of the case studies in which McLuhan applies the book's master equation — that every medium is an [[Media as Extensions of Man|extension of man]] — to a single technology. He opens by setting aside the psychoanalytic derivation of money from "filthy lucre" and the anal, on the ground that it "does not correspond sufficiently to the nature and function of money in society," and proceeds instead to read money as a staple, "a natural resource," and "a corporate image, depending on society for its institutional status." The bare-noun definition extracted here — money as storehouse, metaphor, transfer, and bridge — is the hinge on which the chapter's longer historical argument turns; that development (commodity money → representative/paper money → credit card, and money's eventual eclipse by the pure "movement of information") lives in the paired concept [[Money as a Medium]]. `Directly stated`

What makes the definition do work in the larger thesis is its alignment of money with the **specialist, visual, fragmenting** technologies. McLuhan repeatedly insists that "money, like writing, has the power to specialize and to rechannel human energies and to separate functions, just as it translates and reduces one kind of work to another." He makes money "an adjunct of that specialist alphabetic technology" of [[The Phonetic Alphabet|the phonetic alphabet]], noting that "uniformity of commodities, combined with a fixed-price system such as we now take for granted, does not become possible until printing prepares the ground." Money thus enters McLuhan's account of [[Detribalization and Retribalization]]: like writing and number it pulls the individual out of the tribal whole into a world of fragmented, specialized "work," which (he claims) "does not exist in a nonliterate world." `Directly stated`

The "action at a distance" clause is the definition's second load-bearing element. Because money stores and translates communal work, it "speeds up exchange and tightens the bonds of interdependence in any community" and "gives great spatial extension and control to political organizations, just as writing does, or the calendar." In a literate society this collapses into the formula "Time is money," where "money is the store of other people's time and effort." The chapter's closing movement then turns the definition against itself: in the electric age, "as work is replaced by the sheer movement of information, money as a store of work merges with the informational forms of credit and credit card," and "electric technology puts the very concept of money in jeopardy." Money, having begun as a storehouse of work, dissolves into [[Information]]. `Directly stated`

## Differences from Other Uses

- This is the **definition** note for money-as-medium. It sits beside the broader concept [[Money as a Medium]] and the master definition [[Extensions of Man (McLuhan)]], applying McLuhan's equation to the single case of money; it should not be collapsed into them. The bare-noun definition lives here; the historical and argumentative development (commodity → paper → credit, and the electric dissolution into information) lives in the concept. `Interpretive synthesis`
- McLuhan's money is conspicuously **not** the economist's or Marx's money. He brackets value-theory and exchange-theory and treats money as a *communications* medium governed by the same sensory laws as writing and the clock — its meaning depends on "communal participation" ("Apart from communal participation, money is meaningless, as Robinson Crusoe discovered"), and its study, he says following Keynes, must be dynamic because "money is not a closed system, and does not have its meaning alone." `Directly stated`
- Against the vault's Dataist and posthuman authors, McLuhan describes the *same* terminal tendency they diagnose but from the medium's side. Where [[N. Katherine Hayles]], [[Ray Kurzweil]], and [[Yuval Noah Harari]] (in [[The Body as Data]], [[Dataism]], [[Organisms Are Algorithms]]) treat the reduction of value and the world to manipulable [[Information]] as a metaphysical or epistemic claim, McLuhan reads money's collapse into "the movement of information itself" as a media-historical event — a medium losing its old function as a "store of work" as the electric age dethrones the visual, fragmenting sense. The credit card, for him, "approaches once more the character of tribal money." `Interpretive synthesis`
- Money as "a storehouse of communally achieved work, skill, and experience" anticipates, in McLuhan's own idiom, what Han would later call [[Immaterial Labour|immaterial labour]] and the broader shift from the storage of work to its acceleration ([[Acceleration]]) — but McLuhan's frame is sensory and technological, not a critique of neoliberal power; money's "action at a distance" is offered descriptively, as the signature of every extension of man, not as domination. `Speculative connection`
