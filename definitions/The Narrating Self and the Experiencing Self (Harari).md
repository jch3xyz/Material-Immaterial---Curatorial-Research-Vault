# The Narrating Self and the Experiencing Self — Yuval Noah Harari

## Author's Definition

In *Homo Deus* (Ch. 8, "The Time Bomb in the Laboratory"), Harari draws on Daniel Kahneman's cold-water experiment to argue that there is no single self but "at least two different selves within us: the experiencing self and the narrating self." He defines them as a contrasting pair.

The **experiencing self** is consciousness as it happens — the bare stream of momentary feeling, with no memory and no voice:

> The experiencing self is our moment-to-moment consciousness.

It registers each sensation as it occurs but plays almost no part in how a life is remembered or decided. As Harari puts it: "the experiencing self remembers nothing. It tells no stories, and is seldom consulted when it comes to big decisions" [Directly stated].

The **narrating self** is the story-telling entity that monopolises memory, narration and choice — and that we mistake for the "I":

> Retrieving memories, telling stories and making big decisions are all the monopoly of a very different entity inside us: the narrating self. The narrating self is akin to Gazzaniga's left-brain interpreter. It is forever busy spinning yarns about the past and making plans for the future.

The narrating self does not record experience faithfully. It is **duration-blind** and evaluates episodes by averaging two sampled points — the most intense moment and the final moment — a heuristic Harari, following Kahneman, names the **peak-end rule**:

> Every time the narrating self evaluates our experiences, it discounts their duration, and adopts the 'peak-end rule' – it remembers only the peak moment and the end moment, and evaluates the whole experience according to their average.

Hence its signature operation: "The narrating self doesn't aggregate experiences – it averages them" [Directly stated]. Crucially, the narrating self is the referent of *I*. When we identify with anything, it is with the story, not the stream:

> most people identify with their narrating self. When they say 'I', they mean the story in their head, not the stream of experiences they undergo.

## Context

The distinction does specific argumentative work in the chapter Harari calls "the time bomb in the laboratory." Having first dismantled free will (brain processes are "deterministic or random or a combination of both – but they are never free"), Harari turns to liberalism's *second* factual pillar: the belief in "a single and indivisible self." The experiencing-self / narrating-self split is his demolition charge under that pillar. If a person already contains two selves with opposed interests, then the "single authentic self" that liberalism needs — the one voice we are told to consult "in the polling station, in the supermarket and in the marriage market" — does not exist. "Humans aren't individuals. They are 'dividuals'" [Directly stated]. This places the definition at the hinge of Harari's larger thesis that the self, like nations, gods and money, is an imaginary story (see [[Intersubjective Reality]]).

Harari builds the pair from borrowed science. The narrating self is explicitly modelled on Michael S. Gazzaniga's split-brain studies of the **left-brain interpreter** — "an internal interpreter that constantly tries to make sense of our life, using partial clues in order to concoct plausible stories" — which Harari dramatises with his "private CIA and State Department" metaphor for confabulation. The experiencing / narrating distinction itself, together with the peak-end rule, duration-neglect, and the cold-water and colonoscopy experiments, is taken from Daniel Kahneman (with Donald Redelmeier). The colonoscopy data make the duration-blindness vivid: a twenty-four-minute test ending on a mild note was remembered as *less* painful than an eight-minute test ending on a sharp one, because "the narrating self doesn't aggregate experiences – it averages them" [Directly stated].

The two selves are not fully separable and not always aligned. Harari stresses they "are not completely separate entities but are closely intertwined" — the narrating self uses experiences as "raw materials for its stories," and those stories in turn "shape what the experiencing self actually feels" (the same hunger felt differently when fasting for Ramadan, for a medical test, or from poverty). Yet they have "different interests": the experiencing self can "sabotage the best-laid plans of the narrating self" (the New Year's gym resolution defeated by the sofa and the pizza). Most "critical life choices – of partners, careers, residences and holidays" are nonetheless made by the narrating self, "which cares only about stories and has zero interest in even the most mind-blowing experiences if it cannot remember them" — the point of the Jamestown-versus-memory-wiped-Vegas thought experiment [Directly stated].

## Differences from Other Uses

**Versus Han's *Narrativity*.** Harari's narrating self rhymes with [[Byung-Chul Han|Han's]] [[Narrativity]] and the [[Narration vs Addition]] tension — both privilege *story* over mere stream or accumulation — but the objects differ. Han's narrativity is a *media-historical and temporal* category: the selective, directional "gravity" that ritual, pilgrimage and the novel possess and that the transparency society's additive "counting" dissolves. Harari's narrating self is *intra-psychic and neuropsychological*: a story-telling faculty inside one skull, opposed not to "addition" but to a second self (the experiencing self). For Han, narrativity is largely a *good* whose erosion he mourns; for Harari, the narrating self is a *fabricator* whose "yarns" are "full of lies and lacunas" — the very mechanism that sustains the liberal illusion of a unified "I." The two should be cross-linked, not merged [Interpretive synthesis — Han is not cited by Harari].

**Versus Harari's own *experience*.** This pair is distinct from Harari's definition of [[Experience (Harari)|experience]] in the humanist-revolution chapter, where "an experience is a subjective phenomenon that includes three main ingredients: sensations, emotions and thoughts." That definition supplies the *content* the experiencing self registers moment-to-moment; the present definition adds that this stream is precisely what the narrating self discards and overwrites. Read together, they sharpen Harari's critique: humanism enthrones "experience" as the source of meaning, yet the self that actually decides — the narrating self — has "zero interest" in experience as such, only in its averaged, story-shaped residue.

**Versus the liberal humanist subject.** The pair is Harari's life-sciences route to the same target [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] and [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] reach from cybernetics and metaphor: the dissolution of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] as a unified, self-present, self-authoring locus. The narrating-self account also resonates with [[Distributed Cognition]] — cognition without a single central controller — since the "I" turns out to be one confabulating subsystem among a "cacophony of conflicting voices," not the sovereign chooser liberalism assumes [Interpretive synthesis].
