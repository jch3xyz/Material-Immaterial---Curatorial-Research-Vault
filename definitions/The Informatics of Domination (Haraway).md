# The Informatics of Domination — Donna Haraway

## Author's Definition

Haraway coins "the informatics of domination" to name the new system of world order she sees emerging after the Second World War — a shift from a hierarchical, organic mode of power to a networked, coded one. She introduces the term as the destination of a chart of one-to-one transitions, "from the comfortable old hierarchical dominations to the scary new networks I have called the informatics of domination" (`Directly stated`). The opposed columns are headed *Organics of Domination* versus *Informatics of Domination*, running through pairs such as representation → simulation, organism → biotic component, reproduction → replication, mind → artificial intelligence, and — as the chart's own summary line — white capitalist patriarchy → informatics of domination.

The defining operation underneath the chart is a single epistemic move shared by the communications sciences and the modern biologies: the recoding of the world as information. In Haraway's verbatim formulation, these fields "are constructed by a common move—the translation of the world into a problem of coding, a search for a common language in which all resistance to instrumental control disappears and all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange" (`Directly stated`). The informatics of domination is thus not merely a new set of dominations but a new *medium* of domination: power exercised through code, where any component "can be interfaced with any other if the proper standard, the proper code, can be constructed for processing signals in a common language" (`Directly stated`).

Haraway compresses the technical kernel of this order into a borrowed military acronym: the fundamentals "can be condensed into the meta phor C3I, command-control-communication-intelligence, the military's symbol for its operations theory" (`Directly stated`). The world becomes a field "subdivided by boundaries differentially permeable to information," and the privileged pathology of the whole system is "stress—communications breakdown" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The definition appears in section 3 of "A Cyborg Manifesto," the section titled "The Informatics of Domination." Haraway frames it as part of "an epistemological and political position" rooted in "socialist and feminist principles of design," responding to "rearrangements in worldwide social relations tied to science and technology" (`Directly stated`). She names the stakes as a wholesale epochal transition: "we are living through a movement from an organic, industrial society to a polymorphous, information system—from all work to all play, a deadly game" (`Directly stated`). The new order is "analogous in its novelty and scope to that created by industrial capitalism" (`Directly stated`) — a successor formation, not a reform of the old one.

The concept does two kinds of work in the larger argument. First, it diagnoses why an older feminist analysis is inadequate: the "organic, hierarchical dualisms ordering discourse in 'the West' since Aristotle" — mind/body, animal/human, organism/machine, nature/culture, men/women — have been "cannibalized" or "techno-digested," so they can no longer ground politics (`Directly stated`). Second, it sets up the cyborg as the figure adequate to the new conditions: "The cyborg is a kind of disassembled and reassembled, postmodern collective and personal self. This is the self feminists must code" (`Directly stated`). The informatics of domination is therefore the *condition* the [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] inhabits and the system the cyborg both belongs to and might turn against. Haraway pointedly distinguishes this regime from an earlier one: "The cyborg is not subject to Foucault's biopolitics; the cyborg simulates politics, a much more potent field of operations" (`Directly stated`) — marking a break from the disciplinary/[[Biopolitics|biopolitical]] model of power.

This section is also the documentary source of the term "informatics" as it later travels through the vault. Haraway's "informatics of domination" is the proximate coinage from which N. Katherine Hayles draws the noun [[Informatics]] (and her phrase [[The Materiality of Informatics]]); Hayles explicitly defines informatics by citing this passage (`Strongly implied` for the vault-internal lineage; `Interpretive synthesis` that the curatorial pairing here is the documentary anchor).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. [[Informatics|informatics]] (Hayles).** Hayles takes the bare noun "informatics" — which she traces to Haraway — and uses it for "the technologies of information as well as the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes that initiate, accompany, and complicate their development." Haraway's phrase keeps the genitive *of domination* attached: for her it is irreducibly a regime of *power*, a system of "production/reproduction and communication" into which women and others are integrated and exploited (`Directly stated`). Hayles abstracts the descriptive field; Haraway insists on the political charge.
- **vs. [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] (Wiener/Hayles).** Cybernetics is the science of control and communication that supplies the *mechanism* (feedback, the translation of the world into "rates, directions, and probabilities of flow of a quantity called information," `Directly stated`). The informatics of domination is the *social order* that this science, fused with biotechnology and capital, installs — the politics of the cybernetic, not the cybernetic as such.
- **vs. Marxian market exchange.** Haraway marks her concept as exceeding Marx's analysis: "Exchange in this world transcends the universal translation effected by capitalist markets that Marx analyzed so well" (`Directly stated`). Code, not the commodity form alone, becomes the universal solvent of heterogeneity.
- **vs. [[Dataism]] (Han / Harari) and the [[Digital Panopticon]] (Han).** Haraway's 1985 coinage prefigures the later vault diagnoses of a world remade as information, but its valence differs: where Han treats the recoding of life as data as a regime to be resisted and Harari treats Dataism as an ascendant techno-religion, Haraway holds the informatics of domination and the cyborg in deliberate ambivalence — "a deadly game" that is nonetheless the only ground on which an oppositional, coding feminism can now stand (`Interpretive synthesis`).
