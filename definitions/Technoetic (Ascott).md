# Technoetic — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

Ascott coins **technoetic** as a portmanteau of two Greek-derived roots — *techne* (art, craft, skill, technology) and *noetic* (from *nous*, mind or consciousness). The term names the emergent culture in which technology and consciousness converge:

> the emergence of what I call technoetic culture (techne + noetic, as I just defined it, concerning mind and consciousness).

`Directly stated`. The qualifier "as I just defined it" points back, in the same passage, to his gloss on *telenoia* ("from the Greek roots tele, 'far off,' and nous, 'mind,' or consciousness-at-large"), from which he carries the *noetic* root forward into this new compound.

## Context

Ascott introduces the word in "The Mind of the Museum" (a 1996 Art Institute of Chicago address) as the culminating name for what the twentieth century's two divergent strands of art — the technological/scientific and the spiritual/conceptual — were, in his reading, jointly preparing. He frames it as a deliberate succession away from the visual: in his words, "the 'game of images is over' [Deleuze 1983]; our concern now is with conceptual strategies, ideas and cognitive behaviour, and with the emergence of new consciousness" `Directly stated`. The technoetic is thus an art and a culture concerned with mind itself — "the exploration of mind, both human and artificial, perhaps unmediated by visual forms and representation" — rather than with the surface appearance of objects.

The term anchors a triad Ascott offers for "the location of culture at this turning of the millennium": he distinguishes the adjective *technoetic* from the cognate noun **technoetics**, which he glosses as "everything that concerns cyberception and the technology of consciousness," set beside artificial life and intelligent architecture `Directly stated`. The technoetic is therefore tightly bound to his faculty of `[[Cyberception]]` (technologically amplified perception and cognition) and to the networked condition he names `[[Telenoia]]` (mind-at-large). It also extends the vault's `[[New Media Art]]` and `[[Art as Experiential Technology]]` clusters from Burnham's systems-esthetics frame toward an explicitly consciousness-centred horizon: where Burnham's spine ran object → system, Ascott's runs system → mind `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Differences from Other Uses

No other author in the vault uses the word "technoetic" — it is Ascott's coinage `Directly stated`. Its conceptual neighbours, however, are held by others, and the contrasts are sharp:

- Against `[[Posthumanism]]` and the Hayles/Kurzweil/Moravec line on `[[Disembodied Information as the Soul]]`, Ascott's technoetic is not primarily about uploading or substrate-independence. He is explicit that "we are not junking the body" but rejecting "the definition of the body in the orthodoxy of its Western meaning," reaching instead for "new metaphors of embodiment" `Directly stated` — closer to a redistribution than a discarding of `[[Embodiment]]`.
- Against Burnham's `[[Systems Esthetics]]`, which keeps art on the axis of physical/informational systems, the technoetic relocates art's domain to "the space of consciousness" — what Ascott calls a noetic rather than a merely systemic shift `Interpretive synthesis`.
- It overlaps with O'Gieblyn's `[[Re-enchantment]]` and the broader `[[The Computational Universe]]` motif in foregrounding "issues of consciousness, mind, and spirit" after "the relentless materialism of the industrial age," but Ascott routes this through `[[Artificial Life]]`, `[[Autopoiesis]]`, `[[Emergence]]`, and `[[Distributed Cognition]]` rather than through metaphysics or theology `Strongly implied`.

It also feeds directly into the vault's keystone `[[Material vs Immaterial]]` tension: Ascott names art as inhabiting "the interspace between material and immaterial worlds," making the technoetic a position *on* that opposition rather than a resolution of it `Directly stated`.
