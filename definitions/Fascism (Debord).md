# Fascism — Guy Debord

## Author's Definition

In thesis 109 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*, [[Guy Debord]] compresses fascism into a single lapidary formula:

> Fascism is a technologically equipped primitivism. (th. 109)

The formula is set inside a longer definitional sentence that supplies its content. Fascism, Debord writes, unlike the bourgeois ideology it defends, "is not itself fundamentally ideological":

> It presents itself as what it is-a violent resurrection of myth calling for participation in a community defined by archaic pseudo values: race, blood, leader. Fascism is a technologically equipped primitivism. (th. 109)

Fascism, on this account, is a **violent resurrection of archaic myth** — race, blood, leader — that does not disguise itself as reasoned ideology but presents itself frankly *as* myth, and that stages this "primitivism" through the most advanced technical means available. It is "primitivism" in its content (the return to prerational, tribal bonds of blood and leader-worship) and "technologically equipped" in its form (the modern apparatus of mass conditioning). The definition's second half names the consequence for Debord's central term: fascism's "factitious mythological rehashes are presented in the spectacular context of the most modern means of conditioning and illusion," making it "a significant factor in the formation of the modern spectacle" and "one of the founding forces of present-day society" (th. 109).

## Context

The definition sits at the close of Chapter 4 ("The Proletariat as Subject and Representation"), Debord's long account of how the twentieth-century revolutionary workers' movement was defeated — first by the Stalinist bureaucracy (theses 101–108), then, in the same historical breath, by fascism (th. 109). The two are treated as twinned catastrophes: "Between the two world wars the revolutionary working-class movement was destroyed by the joint action of the Stalinist bureaucracy and of fascist totalitarianism" (th. 109). Fascism enters the argument not as an ideology to be refuted but as a *form of appearance* to be diagnosed. Debord first gives its economic function — fascism "was a desperate attempt to defend the bourgeois economy from the dual threat of crisis and proletarian subversion... an emergency dose of rationalization in the form of massive state intervention" — and immediately undercuts it: "this rationalization is hampered by the extreme irrationality of its methods" (th. 109). The definition resolves that contradiction. Fascism rationalizes the economy through irrational means because its methods are not really rational at all; they are the deliberate reanimation of archaic myth, "equipped" with modern technology.

The definition therefore does specific work in the book's larger architecture. It folds fascism into the theory of the [[Spectacle]] — the [[Guy Debord|Debord]] thesis that under modern conditions "everything that was directly lived has receded into a representation" (th. 1). Fascism is one of the *founding forces* of the modern spectacle precisely because it perfects the technique of substituting a staged, contemplated myth for lived political reality: the leader, the rally, the icons of "family, private property, moral order, patriotism" (th. 109) are consumed as spectacle. Debord also notes fascism's historical obsolescence within his own frame — "since it is also the most costly method of preserving the capitalist order, it has generally ended up being pushed to the back of the stage and replaced by the major capitalist states, which represent stronger and more rational forms of that order" (th. 109) — which recasts fascism as a crude, expensive early version of a spectacular domination that later capitalism accomplishes more smoothly.

## Differences from Other Uses

Debord's is a compressed, deliberately paradoxical definition that differs from the surrounding critical tradition in emphasis:

- **Against the "irrationalist" reading.** Where a standard account treats fascism as a lapse into unreason opposed to modern rationality, Debord insists the two are welded together: the "primitivism" is *technologically equipped*. Archaism and the most modern conditioning apparatus are not opposites in fascism but a single mechanism. This is the definition's whole force — the oxymoron is the point.
- **Against the purely ideological reading.** Debord expressly denies that fascism "is itself fundamentally ideological" (th. 109). It does not, in his view, operate primarily by false theory but by the spectacular re-presentation of myth; it "presents itself as what it is." This distinguishes his account from critiques that treat fascism as a body of doctrine to be intellectually refuted.
- **Within the vault.** No other ingested author defines fascism as a term, so there is no rival definition note to reconcile. The nearest resonance is with the [[Byung-Chul Han]] cluster: Debord's "technologically equipped" staging of myth through "the most modern means of conditioning and illusion" anticipates the later diagnosis of a domination that works through media, display, and the manufactured image rather than through open coercion or doctrine. This is a read connection, not a citation — Han does not cite Debord on fascism — and is developed as an interpretive bridge in [[Byung-Chul Han and Guy Debord]]. It should not be overstated: Debord's target is a specific interwar political form, not a general theory of media power.

Note also that Debord's earlier concept `Gleichschaltung` (in the Han cluster) names a *forced synchronization* under Nazism; that is a Han-anchored term about conformity, distinct from this Debord definition of fascism as spectacularized primitivism. They are not variants and are kept separate.
