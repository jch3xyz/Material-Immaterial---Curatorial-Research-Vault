# Hyperscale — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Zuboff fixes the term in chapter 18 ("A Coup from Above"), in the section "After Reciprocity," as the name for a historically discontinuous business form (*Directly stated*):

> Second, by historical standards the large surveillance capitalists employ relatively few people compared to their unprecedented computational resources. This pattern, in which a small, highly educated workforce leverages the power of a massive capital-intensive infrastructure, is called “hyperscale.”

The defining ratio is therefore between two unequal magnitudes: a *small, highly educated workforce* on one side, a *massive capital-intensive infrastructure* on the other. The term names the asymmetry, not the company. Zuboff is explicit that the category is broader than her object: "Not all hyperscale firms are surveillance capitalists, however, and our focus here is restricted to the convergence of these two domains" (*Directly stated*).

## Context

Hyperscale appears as the **second** of two "decisive break[s] with capitalism's past" that together constitute what Zuboff calls "After Reciprocity." The first break is the abandonment of consumer reciprocity ("surveillance capitalists no longer rely on people as consumers"); the second is the abandonment of *employment* reciprocity. Hyperscale is the structural form of that second rupture: it is how surveillance capitalism severs the "organic reciprocities with people" that, "symbolized in the twentieth century by Ford's five-dollar day," had long underwritten capitalism's endurance (*Directly stated*).

Zuboff establishes the "historical discontinuity of the hyperscale business operation" empirically, by contrast. Google reached \$532 billion and Facebook \$332 billion in market capitalization "without Google ever employing more than 75,000 people or Facebook more than 18,000," whereas General Motors took four decades to reach \$225.15 billion "when it employed 735,000 women and men" — indeed "GM employed more people during the height of the Great Depression than either Google or Facebook employs at their heights of market capitalization" (*Directly stated*). The GM pattern stands for the institutions of [[The Double Movement (Polanyi via Zuboff)]] — "fair labor practices, unionization, and collective bargaining"; hyperscale is what remains once those reciprocities are "formally rescind[ed]."

The stakes are political, not merely economic. Because hyperscale firms "operate at hyperscale or outsource to hyperscale operations," they "dramatically diminish any reliance on their societies as sources of employees, and the few for whom they do compete… are drawn from the most-rarefied strata of data science." For Zuboff this matters "in light of the historical relationship between market capitalism and democracy," whose origins "have been traced to these very reciprocities" of consumption and labor (*Directly stated*). Hyperscale is thus a precondition of the chapter's title thesis: a "coup from above" is feasible precisely because the surveillance firm no longer needs the people it governs as either customers or workers. This is the immaterial-infrastructure dimension of the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension cast in political economy: value concentrates in computational capital and a thin layer of data-science labor rather than in a mass workforce (*Interpretive synthesis*).

Quantitatively, Zuboff dates and sizes the phenomenon: "In 2017, 24 hyperscale firms operated 320 data centers with anywhere between thousands and millions of servers (Google and Facebook were among the largest)" (*Directly stated*).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Industry sense vs. Zuboff's sociological sense.** In data-center engineering "hyperscale" usually denotes raw scale of computing capacity (the count of servers and data centers a firm can elastically operate — the "320 data centers… thousands and millions of servers" Zuboff cites). Zuboff borrows that quantitative usage but redefines hyperscale *relationally*, as the workforce-to-infrastructure asymmetry and the rupture of employment reciprocity it enables. The novelty in her hands is the social consequence, not the server count (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Not co-extensive with surveillance capitalism.** Zuboff insists "Not all hyperscale firms are surveillance capitalists"; her interest is "the convergence of these two domains." Hyperscale is a *necessary structural condition* that surveillance capitalism exploits, not a synonym for it. [[Surveillance Capitalism]] is the economic logic; hyperscale is the labor-and-capital form within which that logic can dispense with people (*Strongly implied*).
- **Against the Marxian image Zuboff herself invokes.** Where classical critique reads exploitation as the extraction of surplus value from *labor*, hyperscale describes a form that needs almost no labor at all: the asymmetry is the firm's independence from people-as-employees. This converges with [[Radical Indifference]] — the surveillance firm's formal unconcern with the human meaning of its sources — extended from consumers to the workforce (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Adjacent to but distinct from Han/Harari "immaterial" diagnoses.** Han's [[Immaterial Labour]] and Harari's [[The Useless Class]] describe, respectively, the dematerialization of work and the redundancy of human labor before machine intelligence. Hyperscale names the concrete *business architecture* that makes such redundancy a competitive advantage: a tiny elite workforce plus capital-intensive computational infrastructure. Where the Useless Class is the projected social effect, hyperscale is the present-tense firm structure producing it (*Interpretive synthesis*).
