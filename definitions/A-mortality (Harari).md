# A-mortality — Yuval Noah Harari

## Author's Definition

Harari coins **a-mortality** as a precise correction to loose talk of immortality. The superhumans that the war against death might produce would not be immortal in the theological sense — invulnerable, beyond all death — but merely *without an expiry date*. The load-bearing sentence:

> In truth they will actually be a-mortal, rather than immortal. Unlike God, future superhumans could still die in some war or accident, and nothing could bring them back from the netherworld. However, unlike us mortals, their life would have no expiry date.

The hyphenated *a-mortal* carves out a third category between the two terms it sits between. The mortal has an expiry date; the immortal (God) cannot die at all; the a-mortal has *no expiry date* yet *remains killable*. Death is not abolished — only its inevitability. "So long as no bomb shreds them to pieces or no truck runs them over, they could go on living indefinitely" (`Directly stated`). The condition is contingent survival, not invulnerability.

## Context

The distinction arrives at the end of the immortality section of the opening chapter, "The Last Days of Death," and is the definitional pivot of the whole immortality project that anchors [[The New Human Agenda]]. Harari has just rehearsed the modern reframing of death — for modern science and culture "death is a technical problem that we can and should solve," because "humans always die due to some technical glitch" and "every technical problem has a technical solution" (`Directly stated`). He has surveyed the believers (Aubrey de Grey, Ray Kurzweil, Google's Calico, Bill Maris, Peter Thiel) who think the elixir is within reach. *A-mortality* is the term that disciplines that whole discourse: even total victory over the biological causes of death would not deliver godhood, only the removal of the built-in deadline.

The definition does specific argumentative work. Because the a-mortal can still die by accident or violence but has everything to lose, Harari draws an immediate psychological consequence:

> Which will probably make them the most anxious people in history. We mortals daily take chances with our lives, because we know they are going to end anyhow. So we go on treks in the Himalayas, swim in the sea, and do many other dangerous things like crossing the street or eating out. But if you believe you can live for ever, you would be crazy to gamble on infinity like that.

This is the sting of the distinction. The immortal would have nothing to fear; the a-mortal has *infinity* to lose to a stray truck, and so would rationally refuse every risk. The promise of the death-conquering project thus terminates not in serene godhood but in maximal anxiety — a corrosion of ordinary risk-taking life (the Himalayan trek, the swim, crossing the street). The term is therefore Harari's lever for turning the upbeat immortality narrative into a warning (`Directly stated` for the anxiety claim; `Interpretive synthesis` for reading it as the chapter's critical hinge).

A-mortality also belongs to Harari's wider story of [[Disenchantment]]: death stripped of metaphysical meaning becomes "some technical glitch," and the best the technical fix can yield is not the immortal soul of religion but the expiry-date-free, still-killable body — divinity downgraded to maintenance (`Strongly implied`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Against everyday "immortality."** This is the usage Harari defines *against*. Popular and Silicon-Valley talk of "solving death," "living to 500," or "eternal youth" slides toward the theological sense of immortality — deathlessness as such. Harari's correction is that the technical project, even fully successful, cannot reach that target: "Unlike God, future superhumans could still die in some war or accident." A-mortality is the most the engineers can promise (`Directly stated`).

- **Against the theological immortal (God / the immortal soul).** The contrast term Harari names explicitly is **God**: God cannot die, and what dies cannot be brought back. The a-mortal lacks both properties — it can die, and there is no resurrection ("nothing could bring them back from the netherworld"). The note thus distinguishes a *technological* deathlessness from the *metaphysical* immortality of the religions Harari elsewhere describes, for whom death was "a sacred metaphysical experience exploding with meaning" (`Directly stated`).

- **Within [[Transhumanism]] and the vault's allied terms.** A-mortality is the sober underside of the transhumanist immortality dream that O'Gieblyn anchors in the vault (mind uploading, patternism, the singularity). Where those routes often imagine escape from death altogether, Harari's term insists that bodily life-extension yields only an open-ended but still-vulnerable existence — and one purchased at the price of permanent dread. Link by intent; Harari and the transhumanists share the project but Harari's *a-mortal* names its ceiling (`Interpretive synthesis`).

- **Relation to [[Homo Deus]] and the divinity project.** A-mortality is the immortality branch of the upgrade, not the whole of it. The Homo deus Harari imagines aims at "divine powers of creation and destruction"; *a-mortality* clarifies that the "deathlessness" component of that divinity is partial — the upgraded being approaches the powers of a god while remaining, on this one axis, conspicuously unlike God. The agenda names the target *immortality*; this definition names what is actually deliverable (`Directly stated` for the divinity framing; `Interpretive synthesis` for the partial-divinity reading).

## Related Concepts

- [[The New Human Agenda]] — the immortality project is the first member of the agenda's triad; a-mortality is its definitional pivot, the correction that says the project's ceiling is no-expiry-date, not deathlessness.
- [[Homo Deus]] — the upgraded being; a-mortality specifies the (partial) sense in which it would be deathless, unlike the immortal God it otherwise approaches.
- [[Disenchantment]] — death reduced from metaphysical mystery to "technical glitch"; a-mortality is the disenchanted afterlife — an expiry-date-free but still-killable body in place of the immortal soul.
- [[Transhumanism]] — the movement of which the immortality/a-mortality project is an articulation; Harari's term names its sober ceiling and its psychological cost.
