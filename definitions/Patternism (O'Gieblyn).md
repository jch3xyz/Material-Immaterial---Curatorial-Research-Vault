# Patternism — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

O'Gieblyn defines patternism by reporting Ray Kurzweil's self-description: the self is a persisting pattern of information rather than the material substrate (cells, atoms, neurons) that carries it, and is therefore in principle transferable to another medium. In her gloss:

> Transhumanists do not believe in the soul, but they subscribe to a concept that is not so dissimilar. Kurzweil calls himself a "patternist." He believes consciousness is a pattern of information, a biological configuration of energy and matter that persists over time. It does not reside in the hardware of our brains—the cells and atoms and neurons, which are always changing—but in the computational patterns that make up our sensory systems, our attention system, and our memories, which together form the distinctive algorithm that we think of as our identity. This is essentially a functionalist account of the mind—the popular view that consciousness does not reside in the physical material of the brain but rather in its organization and causal relations—though Kurzweil opts for a more organic metaphor.

The decisive corollary — that the pattern, being computational, is substrate-independent and so transferable — O'Gieblyn states directly:

> But for Kurzweil, patternism was precisely what made possible the most decisive form of permanence: immortality. A pattern, after all, is essentially computational, which means it can, at least in theory, be transferred onto a computer.

`Directly stated` — O'Gieblyn explicitly names "patternist" as Kurzweil's self-applied term and characterizes it as "essentially a functionalist account of the mind."

## Context

The definition arrives in the central chapter of *God, Human, Animal, Machine*, in which O'Gieblyn narrates her drift from fundamentalist Christianity, through her own "personal disenchantment," to Kurzweil's *The Age of Spiritual Machines*. Patternism is introduced as the secular structural replacement for the soul: it gives the convert "the notion that my interior life was somehow real—that the purely subjective experience that I had once believed to be my soul was not some ghostly illusion but a process that contained an essential and irreducible identity." The term does the load-bearing work for the chapter's larger thesis that [[Transhumanism]] reanimates Christian eschatology — the [[Disembodied Information as the Soul|soul recoded as disembodied information]] — under cover of Enlightenment rationalism.

The functionalist content licenses what follows in the chapter: [[Mind Uploading]]. Because the pattern is "essentially computational," it can be "transferred onto all sorts of different substrates: our new bodies might be supercomputers, robotic surrogates, or human clones," up to "the mind as pure information, pure spirit." O'Gieblyn embeds Kurzweil's own organic figure for the doctrine — "I am rather like the pattern that water makes in a stream as it rushes past the rocks in its path…The actual molecules of water change every millisecond, but the pattern persists" — and traces it backward to [[Norbert Wiener]] ("we are but whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water…patterns that perpetuate themselves") and behind Wiener to [[Heraclitus]]. The genealogical point is an inversion: where Wiener and Heraclitus used the river image to mark the *transience* of identity, Kurzweil uses it to underwrite *permanence*. `Directly stated`.

O'Gieblyn frames patternism as the engine of [[The Algorithm as Providence|a teleological cosmic narrative]] in which "all the matter of the universe will become saturated with intelligence." She also registers the standard objection — Susan Schneider's "continuity of identity" problem, that an uploaded copy "will be a zombie with no subjective experience" — and Kurzweil's circular rebuttal that "the subjective reality of the new person will depend solely on how convincing the behavior is." `Strongly implied` — these flank the definition as its principal stake and its principal vulnerability. Her closing move historicizes the doctrine: she identifies patternism's nearest ancestor not in Enlightenment philosophy but in [[Origen]] of Alexandria's *eidos*, "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter," which she judges to resemble "more than anything…Kurzweil's understanding of consciousness as a pattern of information." `Directly stated`.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Vs. the concept [[Patternism]] (and Kurzweil's own usage).** This note is the author's *definitional* report; the concept note carries the cross-author analysis. O'Gieblyn does not coin or endorse patternism — she names it as [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s self-description and treats it diagnostically, as a quasi-religious longing in functionalist dress. Where Kurzweil affirms substrate-independence as fact, O'Gieblyn brackets it as ideology, citing N. Katherine Hayles: transhumanism is a worldview in which "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form."

- **Vs. [[N. Katherine Hayles]] on [[Pattern and Randomness]] and [[The Body as Data]].** Hayles analyzes the dialectic of *pattern and randomness* (displacing presence/absence) as the constitutive semiotic of the informatic era and critiques the erasure of [[Embodiment]] in [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal-humanist]] and [[Posthumanism|posthumanist]] thought alike. O'Gieblyn's "patternism" is narrower and personified: it is one transhumanist's named doctrine of personal identity, not Hayles's structural category. O'Gieblyn borrows Hayles's critique ("disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form") as the verdict on patternism rather than as her own primary apparatus. `Interpretive synthesis`.

- **Vs. functionalism in [[The Mind-Body Problem]] more broadly.** O'Gieblyn flags that patternism *is* "essentially a functionalist account of the mind" but with "a more organic metaphor," distinguishing Kurzweil's water-in-a-stream image from the standard mind-as-software analogy. She notes that critics like Schneider grant the computational premise yet hold that "most mind-as-software analogies, including patternism, take the metaphor too far." Patternism is thus a specific, immortality-seeking species of functionalism, not functionalism as such. `Strongly implied`.

- **Vs. the theological *eidos* ([[Origen]]) and the soul.** O'Gieblyn's central comparative claim is that patternism occupies the structural slot of the Christian soul and the Origenist *eidos* — "the resurrected body…characterized by the same essential pattern that characterized the mortal flesh." The difference from the soul is metaphysical packaging, not function: both promise an "essential and irreducible identity" surviving the turnover of matter. `Directly stated`.
