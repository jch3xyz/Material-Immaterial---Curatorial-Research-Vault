# Second-Order Cybernetics — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

For Ascott, second-order cybernetics is the form of cybernetics that emerged once the discipline could no longer hold the observer outside its systems and had to fold the observer back into the system being studied. In "Back to Nature II" he narrates the move as a self-correction internal to cybernetics' own history: first-order cybernetics, "the study of control and communication in living and artificial systems, found itself inadequate to the study of such systems until it included the observer of a system as a part of the system itself." Out of that inclusion comes the second order, defined by the changed status of every boundary it draws:

> Thus was born second-order cybernetics, which recognised the boundaries around all its objects of study as being intrinsically fuzzy, permeable, uncertain, and transformable, and utterly dependent on the position, attitude, and intention of the observer involved in such study.

`Directly stated`

The definition is, in essence, a definition of boundaries: in second-order cybernetics the edge around any object of study is no longer fixed, sharp and given but **fuzzy, permeable, uncertain, transformable**, and — the decisive clause — **dependent on the observer** (their "position, attitude, and intention"). Where first-order cybernetics models a system from the outside, second-order cybernetics admits that there is no outside; the one who studies the system is part of it, and so are the boundaries they draw. `Directly stated`

## Context

Ascott introduces the term inside a larger argument about art, technology and a redefined relationship to nature ("Nature II"). He uses the fifty-year arc of cybernetics — from first-order to second-order — as a "useful parallel to the evolution of our relationship with nature." The observer's entry into the system is the hinge: once boundaries become observer-dependent, "rather than being outsiders looking in on nature or antagonists opposed to nature, we become participants in the creative processes of nature." Second-order cybernetics thus supplies the theoretical license for participation — the same move Ascott makes in his art, where the spectator is folded into the work as a participant rather than held outside it as a contemplator. `Strongly implied`

The passage also extends the insight beyond cybernetics proper. Ascott calls it "applicable as much to the quantum level of experiment as to the social, cultural, or even aesthetic level of experience" — explicitly reaching toward [[Observer-Created Reality]] (the quantum sense that observation participates in what is observed) and toward an aesthetics of participation. The definition is therefore not a technical aside but a worldview claim: a single observer-dependent ontology spanning physics, society and art. `Directly stated`

This note sits beside [[Cybernetics (Ascott)]], where Ascott requisitions cybernetics generally for art (the "coordinator of science"). Second-order cybernetics specifies *which* cybernetics underwrites his participatory programme: the reflexive kind that has already dissolved the spectator/system boundary. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the concept [[Cybernetics]]** — that note is the shared, multi-author concept page (Wiener's founding formulation, and the later readings by Hayles, Haraway, Burnham and McLuhan). This note isolates Ascott's verbatim definition of the *second* order specifically: the reflexive turn in which boundaries become observer-dependent. The two are companion notes, not duplicates (the `Information` / `Information (Han)` pattern). `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Cybernetics (Ascott)]]** — the sibling definition pins Ascott's general claim on cybernetics (the integrative "coordinator of science," the science of "the perfectibility of self-organising systems"). This note pins the narrower, later concept — second-order cybernetics — which Ascott invokes to ground observer-dependence and participation. First-order Ascott annexes the discipline for art; second-order Ascott uses its reflexive form to dissolve the line between observer and observed. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s [[The Three Waves of Cybernetics|three waves]]** — Hayles narrates second-order (reflexive) cybernetics as the historical wave in which the observer was brought into the system, and reads that reflexivity critically, tracking how "information lost its body" across the seriation. Ascott takes the same reflexive turn affirmatively and *prescriptively*: observer-dependent boundaries are not a problem to be audited against [[Reflexivity|embodiment]] but an opening toward participation in nature. He compresses the history Hayles unfolds into a single before/after — first-order inadequate, then second-order born — rather than three measured waves. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Cybernetics (Burnham)]]** — Burnham (1968) defines cybernetics as "the science of organization" recruited to explain how the sculptural object migrates into a system; his orientation stays inside the artwork-as-system and predates the explicit second-order vocabulary. Ascott's second-order cybernetics turns outward, locating the observer (and the artist, and the viewer) *within* the system whose boundaries they help constitute. `Interpretive synthesis`
