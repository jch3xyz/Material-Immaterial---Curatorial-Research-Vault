# Cyborg — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

For Burnham, the cyborg is defined by a feedback coupling, not by anatomy. In *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) he gives the term twice in close succession — first reporting the coinage as a man-machine system in which the human partner is unaware of the connection, then collapsing it into a single general formula:

> A cyborg, then, is any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism.

The fuller gloss of the borrowed term:

> the term cyborg (i.e., cybernetic organism) was created to mean a man-machine system in which the human partner functions unaware of his connection with the machine component; the machine behaves automatically through feedback connections with the muscular or nervous system of the human component

`Directly stated`. The defining feature is **reciprocal feedback** between an artificial system and a living organism; the defining illusion is the **elimination of the sense of separation** between the two — "Unlike an automobile and driver, the sense of physical and perhaps psychic separation between man and machine is eliminated" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The definition arrives in the chapter "Robot and Cyborg Art," in the book's Part Two, "Sculpture as System." Burnham's larger argument is that sculpture is moving from the inert, durable, free-standing object toward the **system** — and that the next, "perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture" is what he calls [[Cyborg Art]] (the cybernetic organism as an art form). He builds the term out of the cybernetic vocabulary he has just laid down: [[Cybernetics]] as the science of organization, [[Systems Theory]] (von Bertalanffy), Cannon's [[Homeostasis]], and the negative/positive [[Feedback Loops]] that Wiener, Rosenblueth, and Bigelow modeled across animals and machines.

Crucially, Burnham frames the cyborg as a **scale of relationship**, not a single object. He reads existing machines as "at least partial cyborg relationships" — General Electric's Handyman manipulator and the IBM "1500" teaching machine — noting they "retain an element of awareness between machine and operator; as yet they do not represent an unconscious symbiotic relationship" (`Directly stated`). The term, he says, "refers to both electromechanical systems with lifelike behavior and man-machine systems which parallel (through feedback) some of the properties of single biological organisms" (`Directly stated`). The cyborg thus names the horizon where the one-way "stimulation" of traditional and Kinetic art gives way to genuine two-way communication between work and observer.

This is the **vault's earliest primary-text use of the word "cyborg"** (1968), predating Haraway's 1985 *A Cyborg Manifesto* and Hayles's later secondhand account. Burnham notes the coinage itself was recent — "Less than a decade ago the term cyborg ... was created" — placing the source coinage in the early 1960s (`Directly stated`). (The book attributes much of the cybernetic frame to Norbert Wiener, *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine*, 1948.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. [[Cyborg (Haraway)]] (1985):** Haraway's cyborg is a political-mythic figure — "a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction" — mobilized to refuse origin stories, organic wholeness, and innocence. Burnham's cyborg is a **technical-aesthetic** definition: an engineering condition (reciprocal feedback between artificial system and organism) that he projects as the destiny of sculpture. Where Haraway makes the boundary breakdown an occasion for "the confusion of boundaries," Burnham makes the *erasure of the sense of separation* the literal design criterion. `Interpretive synthesis`.

- **vs. the concept [[The Cyborg]] (Hayles cluster):** The concept note carries Hayles's secondhand cultural-historical account of the cyborg as the joining of human and machine through informatic feedback; Burnham supplies a contemporaneous **primary-source definition** from 1968 with a strict feedback criterion and an art-historical telos (sculpture's "own obliteration by moving toward integration with the intelligent life forms it has always imitated"). `Strongly implied`.

- **vs. the robot:** Burnham explicitly separates the cyborg from the anthropomorphic robot — "the cyborg relationship goes beyond the anthropomorphic anatomy of the classic robot; and it has even less to do with static robot sculpture." The robot is a body that imitates man; the cyborg is a feedback coupling, and need not be man-shaped at all. `Directly stated`.
