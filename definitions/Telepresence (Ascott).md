# Telepresence — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

Ascott self-glosses **telepresence** in his "100 Terms and Definitions" for a [[Technoetic Aesthetics|technoetic aesthetics]]. He sets it against the long history of art's concern with presence, then names the specifically *telematic* mutation of that concern (`Directly stated`):

> Telepresence Art has always concerned itself with presence: of gods in religious art, heroes in classical art, the artist in romantic art; of presence itself in abstract art. In telematic art, our telepresence is distributed throughout the Internet. We are both here and there, out-of-body and reembodied, dematerialised, and reconfigured at one and the same time.

Telepresence is therefore not a thinner or surrogate presence (presence-at-a-distance as mere remote operation), but a *distributed* presence: the self spread across the network so that being is simultaneous in multiple locations. Its signature is the set of paired, non-exclusive states Ascott names — **here and there**, **out-of-body and reembodied**, **dematerialised and reconfigured** — held "at one and the same time" rather than as a sequence or a trade-off. Presence is reconceived as a condition of the network rather than a property of a single located body (`Strongly implied`).

## Context

The definition appears in chapter 32, *Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions*, Ascott's late, aphoristic glossary of the vocabulary he developed across *Telematic Embrace*. Telepresence is one term in a dense constellation he glosses there — alongside *Telenoia* (networked consciousness against industrial paranoia), *Apparition*, *Net-extent* ("Our net-extent defines us in telematic space just as the material body defined us in the old biological culture"), and the *Cyberself* ("We are each made up of many selves: de-centred, distributed, and constructively schizophrenic"). Read together, these entries make telepresence the presence-correlate of Ascott's [[The Distributed Self|distributed self]]: if the self is many and de-centred, its presence is correspondingly disseminated.

Telepresence is what [[Telematic Art|telematic art]] stages and depends on. Where Ascott's [[Cyberception (Ascott)|cyberception]] names the augmented *faculty* by which the networked subject perceives, telepresence names the *condition of distribution* that faculty perceives within — being "both here and potentially everywhere else at the same time." It is also the human stake of the network's [[Apparition (Ascott)|apparitional]] ontology, in which entities (and selves) come into virtual presence rather than possessing a fixed material location. For Ascott this distributed presence is celebrated, not mourned: the same connectivity that disperses the body is the substrate of [[Technoetics|technoetic]] consciousness and of an [[Art as Experiential Technology|experiential, interactive art]] of mind and being. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

The entry's opening move — locating telepresence within art's perennial "concern with presence" — is itself the argument: telematic distribution is presented as the latest, network-native form of an old artistic preoccupation, not a break from art but its reconfiguration for [[Cyberspace (Ascott)|cyberspace]]. (`Strongly implied`)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Telepresence vs. remote presence / teleoperation.** The everyday engineering sense of "telepresence" is presence-*at-a-distance*: a single subject projected to a remote site (a teleoperated robot, a video link). Ascott's sense is presence-*distributed*: not one self relocated but a self spread "throughout the Internet," "both here and there... at one and the same time." Simultaneity and multiplicity, not relocation, are definitional. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

- **Versus Hayles on embodiment.** Ascott's "out-of-body and reembodied... dematerialised, and reconfigured" affirmatively welcomes the dispersal of the body across the net, aligning with the patternist / [[The Body as Data|body-as-data]] strand of the corpus and the [[Material vs Immaterial|immaterialist]] reading of the network. This sits in tension with Hayles's claim that *there is only different embodiment* — that information never truly loses its body. Where Hayles resists disembodiment, Ascott embraces a distributed re-embodiment as the very form of telematic presence. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

- **Versus McLuhan's extensions.** Telepresence is heir to [[The Global Village|McLuhan's]] electric collapse of distance — the central nervous system extended outward so that everyone is everywhere at once. But McLuhan frames the extension as autoamputation and numbness; Ascott frames distributed presence as a gain, an enrichment of being. The dispersal that McLuhan reads as loss, Ascott reads as expansion. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

- **Telepresence vs. the dematerialization of the art object.** Lippard's [[Dematerialization|dematerialization]] and Burnham's object-to-system shift dematerialize the *artwork*; Ascott's telepresence dematerializes the *artist/viewer*. The same word "dematerialised" recurs, but here it qualifies the distributed *self* rather than the vanishing *object* — though both belong to the corpus's larger [[Material vs Immaterial|material-to-immaterial]] movement. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
