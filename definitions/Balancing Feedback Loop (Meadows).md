# Balancing Feedback Loop — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

For [[Donella Meadows]], a balancing feedback loop is the stabilizing, change-opposing kind of feedback: a loop that reads the gap between a stock and a goal and adjusts flows to close it. She names it as she introduces its diagram symbol:

> This kind of stabilizing, goal-seeking, regulating loop is called a balancing feedback loop, so I put a B inside the loop in the diagram. Balancing feedback loops are goal-seeking or stability-seeking. Each tries to keep a stock at a given value or within a range of values. A balancing feedback loop opposes whatever direction of change is imposed on the system. If you push a stock too far up, a balancing loop will try to pull it back down. If you shove it too far down, a balancing loop will try to bring it back up.

The margin caption compresses the definition into a single double-edged sentence — its clean tail clause the sharpest statement of the concept:

> ... goal-seeking structures in systems and are both sources of stability and sources of resistance to change.

So the defining marks are three: the loop is *equilibrating / goal-seeking* (it carries a target — a stock value or range); it *opposes imposed change* in either direction (correction, not amplification); and it is therefore *at once a source of stability and a source of resistance to change* (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Meadows introduces the balancing feedback loop in Chapter 1 ("The Basics") as one of the two elementary building blocks from which every system is assembled — its counterpart being the amplifying [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]]. Having defined a stock (an accumulation) and a flow (a rate that fills or drains it), she needs the feedback that couples them: a [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] forms when changes in a stock affect the flows into or out of that same stock. The *balancing* loop is the sub-type where that coupling is corrective. She grounds it in worked examples — a coffee drinker topping up caffeine to hold "a desired stock level (energy for work)," and a cup of coffee cooling (or iced coffee warming) toward room temperature — and points to the "homing" behavior common to both: "Whatever the initial value of the system stock … whether it is above or below the 'goal' … the feedback loop brings it toward" the goal, the change "faster at first, and then slower, as the discrepancy between the stock and the goal decreases."

The definition does load-bearing work later in the book. Because a balancing loop *resists imposed change*, it is exactly what frustrates a well-intentioned intervention: the same mechanism that lets a system mend injuries and buffer disturbance also makes a system hard to move when one wants it to move. That symmetry — stability and resistance as one property — is why balancing loops recur through Meadows's account of system traps, and why a loop's "breakdown point," "where other loops pull the stock away from its goal more strongly than it can pull back," matters for which loop dominates a system's behavior (see [[Shifting Dominance]]).

## Differences from Other Uses

The vault meets this same stabilizing circuit under several names; the differences are ones of discipline and generality, not of mechanism.

- **vs [[Homeostasis]] (Cannon via [[N. Katherine Hayles]]).** Homeostasis is the equilibrium-seeking regulation Walter Cannon named for the body and cybernetics carried forward. Meadows's balancing loop is the *general-systems mechanism that produces homeostatic behavior*: negative, goal-seeking feedback is what *does* the homeostatic work, whether the stock is blood sugar, a reservoir level, or a thermostatted room. Meadows's is the native-discipline account; the biological term is the special case (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **vs [[Negative Feedback (Burnham)]].** Jack Burnham's "negative feedback" is the cybernetic/servomechanical framing — error-correcting control in Wiener's lineage — imported into an art-and-systems context. Meadows names the identical corrective principle in the plain vocabulary of systems dynamics (stocks, flows, goals) rather than of control engineering. "Balancing" and "negative" pick out the same loop; the labels track the discipline.
- **vs the everyday word "balance."** Meadows's balancing loop is not about a static equilibrium already achieved but about the *dynamic process* that seeks one — an active, error-reading correction, which is why she prefers "goal-seeking" and "stability-seeking" to "balanced."

Two qualifications keep the term precise. A balancing loop's goal must be tuned to competing flows or the stock settles at the wrong level: "A stock-maintaining balancing feedback loop must have its goal set appropriately to compensate for draining or inflowing processes that affect that stock." And a balancing loop is no guarantee of stability — "The presence of a feedback mechanism doesn't necessarily mean that the mechanism works well"; feedback can arrive late, weak, or unclear, so "The goal of the feedback loop may never be reached by the actual stock" (`Directly stated`).
