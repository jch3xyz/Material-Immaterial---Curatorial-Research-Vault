# Cyberception — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

For Ascott, **cyberception** is a new, *post-biological faculty* of technologically augmented perception and conception — the faculty that emerges when the connectivity of telematic networks becomes part of our sensory and cognitive apparatus.

He names it as a *qualitative change in our being* rather than a quantitative change in how we see, and defines it through the convergence it effects (`Directly stated`):

> Cyberception involves a convergence of conceptual and perceptual processes in which the connectivity of telematic networks plays a formative role.

Its two component faculties are themselves redefined by the network. Perception, in Ascott's gloss, is *physical sensation interpreted in the light of experience* — but the **cybernet** (his word for "the sum of all the interactive computer-mediated systems and telematic networks in the world") *is part of our sensory apparatus*, so perception becomes networked. Conception — *the process of originating, forming, or understanding ideas* — is likewise unbound from the solitary body, because "minds now float free in telematic space." Cyberception is the **augmentation of our capacity to think and conceptualise, and the extension and refinement of our senses** (`Directly stated`).

A second, later formulation compresses the same idea (`Directly stated`):

> cyberception, the technologically amplified faculties of perception and cognition.

The faculty's distinguishing character is *pattern* — an all-at-once grasp of system and whole, opposed to the linear, object-by-object scan of pre-telematic perception (`Directly stated`):

> It is an all-at-once perception of a multiplicity of viewpoints, an extension in all dimensions of associative thought, a recognition of the transience of all hypotheses, the relativity of all knowledge, the impermanence of all perception.

Crucially, cyberception lets us apprehend the *immaterial* as immediately as the material: "We cyberceive transformative relationships and connectivity as immaterial process just as palpably and immediately as we commonly perceive material objects in material locations" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Cyberception is the keystone term of the late, most futurological stretch of *Telematic Embrace*. Ascott introduces it in the 1994 essay "The Architecture of Cyberception" (ISEA94, Helsinki) as part of an argument that human beings — *body and mind* — are being actively transformed by their immersion in networks, such that "the old debate about artificial and natural is no longer relevant." The faculty is what equips the "new human being" to inhabit "both the real and virtual worlds at one and the same time," to be "both here and potentially everywhere else at the same time." It is the perceptual correlate of telepresence, the disseminated/distributed self, and the dissolution of the unitary subject into "a set of selves" — "the sense of the individual is giving way to the sense of the interface."

For Ascott this is not merely a neurological claim but an *aesthetic* and *curatorial* one. Cyberception "is the defining behaviour of a transpersonal art"; it gives the artist "X-ray vision and the optics of outer space" to fulfil the century-old "project of art... to make the invisible visible." It is the faculty that an [[Art as Experiential Technology|interactive, experiential art]] both depends on and cultivates, and the faculty that the institution of art must catch up to: in "The Mind of the Museum" (1996) he argues that the museum "must learn the cultural consequences of our newly acquired faculty of cyberception" — that engaging [[New Media Art|art in the Net]] "requires a new kind of perception, cyberception." Cyberception thus supplies the perceptual ground for Ascott's larger program of *technoetics* (techne + noetic), [[Distributed Cognition|mind-at-large]], and the [[Emergence|emergent]], "bottom-up, distributed" art of the network.

Ascott explicitly frames the faculty as *revived* rather than wholly invented — "us finding ourselves again, after the human waste and loss of the age of reason" — and as continuous with the human transformation accelerated "by our advances in genetic engineering and post-biological modelling," part of his broader [[Posthumanism|post-biological]] horizon.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus McLuhan's extensions of man.** Cyberception is Ascott's most direct heir to [[Media as Extensions of Man|McLuhan's media-as-extensions]] thesis (he quotes McLuhan approvingly in "The Mind of the Museum" on electric media freeing "nomadic gatherers of knowledge"). But where McLuhan describes the electric medium as *extending the central nervous system outward* into a [[The Global Village|global village]] — an autoamputation that numbs even as it extends — Ascott casts cyberception as a *gain*: an amplified, more-than-natural faculty, "an awakening of our latent psychic powers," with the network experienced as an enrichment of being rather than a self-amputation. The numbness pole of McLuhan's account is absent. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

- **Versus the dispersed/networked cognition of the vault's posthumanists.** Cyberception overlaps with [[Distributed Cognition]] (Hayles) and with the *disembodied information* lineage, but Ascott's stance toward the body diverges. He insists cyberception does not mean "junking the body" — yet he embraces "leaving behind embodiment as the primary measure and sign of reality" and a "disembodied reconstruction of ourselves." This affirmative welcome of disembodiment sits in tension with Hayles's argument that *there is only different embodiment*, and aligns Ascott closer to the patternist/[[The Body as Data|body-as-data]] view he shares with the [[Material vs Immaterial|immaterialist]] strand of the corpus. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

- **Versus the surveillance/control reading of connectivity.** What Han diagnoses as the [[Digital Panopticon|digital panopticon]] of total transparency and what Harari calls Dataism, Ascott reads in an emancipatory register: the same telematic connectivity is "telenoia" (networked consciousness, the opposite of paranoia), "consciousness-at-large." The faculty that registers the all-connected whole is, for Ascott, a liberation, not a capture. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

- **Cyberception (the faculty) vs. telepresence / technoetics (its conditions and culture).** Within Ascott's own vocabulary, cyberception is specifically the *perceptual-conceptual faculty*; telepresence/the disseminated self is the *condition of distribution* it perceives within, and technoetic culture is the *cultural formation* it makes possible. They are cross-defined but not identical.

## Related Concepts

- [[New Media Art]] — the network-based, telematic art whose viewing "requires a new kind of perception, cyberception"; Ascott extends this Burnham-realized note from his own (later) standpoint.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — interactive, immersive art that both depends on and trains the faculty of cyberception.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — "mind-at-large"; minds that "float free in telematic space" rather than being "locked... into the solitary body."
- [[Emergence]] — the "bottom-up, distributed" behaviour of art-on-the-Net that cyberception is tuned to apprehend.
- [[Posthumanism]] — cyberception as a "post-biological faculty" of the "new human being."
- [[Virtuality]] — the faculty by which we "perceive the apparitions of cyberspace, the coming into being of their virtual presence."
- [[Embodiment]] — cyberception "redefines our individual body" and presses Ascott toward "new metaphors of embodiment" and the "disembodied self."
- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — McLuhan's extensions thesis, of which cyberception is the explicit telematic-era successor.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the "computer-mediated and computer-enhanced" being ("We are all interface") whose perceptual faculty cyberception names.
