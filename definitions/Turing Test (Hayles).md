# Turing Test — N. Katherine Hayles

## Author's Definition

For Hayles, the Turing test is the text-only "imitation game" proposed by [[Alan Turing]] in his 1950 paper, in which an interrogator, communicating by terminal alone, must distinguish a man from a woman — or, in its better-known version, a human from a machine — on the basis of typed responses. The test's evidentiary logic is what defines it:

> If you cannot tell the intelligent machine from the intelligent human, your failure proves, Turing argued, that machines can think.

What the test stages, in Hayles's reading, is "the erasure of embodiment," performed "so that 'intelligence' becomes a property of the formal manipulation of symbols rather than enaction in the human lifeworld." The game is rigged at the level of its setup: because the interrogator can see only "flickering signifiers scrolling down the computer screens," thought is decoupled from the body enacting it and reconceived as pattern alone. *(Directly stated)*

## Context

Hayles opens *[[How We Became Posthuman]]* with the Turing test as the Prologue's "primal scene" — the inaugural moment of the computer age and of [[Posthumanism]]. She treats it less as a passed-or-failed experiment than as "a magic trick" that "relies on getting you to accept at an early stage assumptions that will determine how you interpret what you see later." The decisive move comes not when the interrogator guesses, but earlier, when the test "puts you into a cybernetic circuit that splices your will, desire, and perception into a distributed cognitive system in which represented bodies are joined with enacted bodies through mutating and flexible machine interfaces." *(Directly stated)*

Two features of Hayles's reading do specific work in her larger argument. First, she recovers Turing's *first* example — distinguishing a man from a woman — which she says "often forgotten," and refuses Andrew Hodges's verdict that gender is "a red herring." For Hayles the two cases are "rhetorically parallel"; gender, like thought, turns out to be a "contingent production" rather than something that "embodiment secures." This is how the test "necessarily makes the subject into a cyborg," since enacted and represented bodies "are brought into conjunction through the technology that connects them." See [[The Cyborg]]. *(Directly stated)*

Second, Hayles coins **the Moravec test** as the Turing test's "logical successor." Writing four decades later, [[Hans Moravec]] proposed that human identity "is essentially an informational pattern rather than an embodied enaction," demonstrable by downloading human consciousness into a computer:

> the Moravec test was designed to show that machines can become the repository of human consciousness—that machines can, for all practical purposes, become human beings. You are the cyborg, and the cyborg is you.

Where Turing's game shows machines can *think*, Moravec's shows machines can *hold* a human self — completing the slide from [[Embodiment]] to disembodied [[Information]] that the test inaugurates. Both rest on the [[Claude Shannon]] / [[Norbert Wiener]] definition of information as "an entity distinct from the substrates carrying it," "a kind of bodiless fluid that could flow between different substrates without loss of meaning or form." See [[The Body as Data]] and [[Embodiment vs Information]]. *(Directly stated)*

The deeper point is reflexive: to ask "what can think" is, "in a reverse feedback loop," to change "the terms of 'who can think.'" The test thus dismantles [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] from within, and the reader who watches the flickering signifiers "ha[s] already become posthuman." See [[Feedback Loops]] and [[Distributed Cognition]]. *(Strongly implied)*

## Differences from Other Uses

In its ordinary use within computer science and analytic philosophy of mind, the Turing test is an operational *criterion for machine intelligence*: the question is whether a machine's verbal behavior is indistinguishable from a human's, and a pass is treated as evidence of thinking. Hayles does not contest this framing so much as read *through* it. Her object is not "can machines think?" but the prior cost of the test's design — its built-in "erasure of embodiment" and the conversion of thinking subjects into a "cybernetic circuit." Where the standard account treats the screen-only channel as a neutral leveling device that isolates intelligence, Hayles treats it as a substantive ideological move that produces the [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] and severs pattern from materiality. *(Interpretive synthesis)*

Hayles also diverges sharply from Andrew Hodges's biographical reading: where Hodges dismisses the gender game as "a red herring" whose point is that "a successful imitation of a woman's responses by a man would not prove anything," Hayles reads the man/woman and human/machine cases as deliberately parallel and "explosive," each proving "the same thing" — that the overlay of enacted and represented bodies is "a contingent production." Hodges's reading, she argues, is a "misreading" that does "violence upon the text" to keep embodiment securing "the univocality of gender." *(Directly stated)*

This note is the definitional anchor for the imitation game itself. For the figure the test produces — the human/machine hybrid and Moravec's "you are the cyborg" — see the distinct concept [[The Cyborg]]. *(Directly stated)*
