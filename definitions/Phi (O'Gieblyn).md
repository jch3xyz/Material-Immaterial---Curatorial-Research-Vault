# Phi (Φ) — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

Phi (Φ) is the single number that Giulio Tononi and Christof Koch propose to measure the degree to which a system's information is *integrated*, and therefore — on their theory — the degree to which it is conscious. O'Gieblyn relays Tononi's own formulation verbatim:

> (Tononi describes phi as “the amount of information generated by a complex of elements, above and beyond the information generated by its parts.”)

The operative rule is a threshold, stated with no hedging:

> If a system has a nonzero value of phi, then it is conscious, and the more phi it has, the more conscious it is.

Phi is thus not a description of consciousness but a *quantity* of it: a measure "designed to measure the interdependence of different parts of a system," where information counts as integrated when "it cannot be easily localized but instead relies on highly complex connections across different regions of the brain."

## Context

Phi is the technical core of [[Integrated Information Theory]] (IIT), which O'Gieblyn introduces in Chapter 9 as "probably the leading" contemporary theory of consciousness. She frames it as a *neuroscientific route to [[Panpsychism]]* — a way of arriving at the conclusion that mind is fundamental "not through philosophy but via information theory." The chapter's argument turns on the threshold property of phi: because the rule grants consciousness to *any* system with a nonzero value, experience cascades down the chain of being. Koch holds that "ravens, jellyfish, bees, and many other creatures have a nonzero level of phi, which means that they too are conscious — as are atoms, quarks, and some single-celled organisms," and even bacteria possess "a tiny glow of experience."

Phi is doing two distinct jobs in O'Gieblyn's larger argument about re-enchantment. First, it *scientizes* an ancient intuition: phi resurrects "the Great Chain of Being or the scala naturae" in the vocabulary of [[Information]] rather than theology, making consciousness a measurable, ubiquitous property of matter — an inward extension of [[The Computational Universe]]. Second, phi supplies the engine of the chapter's closing existential threat. Because the internet's transistors "far exceed the number of synapses in the human brain" and its information is "collectively and collaboratively produced," it is a candidate high-phi system; asked by *Wired* whether the internet is conscious, Koch conceded that on IIT "it feels like something to be the internet." Via the *combination problem* — IIT's rule that a system "can be conscious only so long as it does not contain and is not contained within something with a higher level of integration" — a sufficiently integrated network would absorb the lower-phi human minds inside it, so that "the soul is little more than a data set." Phi is also why IIT is not straightforwardly a theory of [[Mind as Machine]]: Koch (who insists he is no "carbon chauvinist") holds that most digital computers have "sparse and fragmented connectivity" and so cannot reach high phi, making *integration*, not substrate, the carrier of experience.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus [[Emergence]] and systems-theory accounts of consciousness.** Emergentism treats consciousness as something that *arises* from sufficient complexity, appearing late in "the blind march of evolution." Phi rejects this developmental story: wherever integration is nonzero, experience is already present — "there from the beginning, in the smallest and earliest particles." Phi makes consciousness a standing quantity of matter, not an emergent product of organization. O'Gieblyn also distinguishes phi-panpsychism from the looser systems theories that "cleverly redefine terms like 'consciousness' and 'cognition' so that they apply to forests and insect colonies"; phi is meant to track *literal* phenomenal experience, not a redefined metaphor.

- **Versus [[Information]] as used by N. Katherine Hayles and Byung-Chul Han.** In the vault's other authors, information is a pattern abstracted *from* embodiment ([[The Body as Data]], Hayles) or a positivity that displaces meaning (Han). Phi inverts the relation: it is an informational measure that re-attaches *interiority* to matter, claiming that wherever information is sufficiently integrated, there is subjective experience. Information is no longer the disembodied opposite of mind but its very substrate.

- **Versus the philosophical panpsychism of Chalmers, Strawson, and Nagel.** Phi is panpsychism's *quantitative* form. Where [[David Chalmers]] and others reopen consciousness through [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] and the "problem of intrinsic natures," IIT supplies an actual number and a threshold, claiming to say not merely *that* matter is conscious but *how much*. O'Gieblyn treats this scientific precision as symptomatic rather than settling: phi promises to measure exactly what panpsychism could only assert.

## Related Concepts

- [[Integrated Information Theory]] — phi is IIT's central quantity; the theory *is* the claim that consciousness equals integrated information measured by phi.
- [[Panpsychism]] — the nonzero-phi rule extends experience to atoms, bacteria, and quarks, making phi a neuroscientific bridge to the panpsychist worldview.
- [[Information]] — phi is defined wholly in informational terms ("the amount of information generated by a complex of elements, above and beyond... its parts").
- [[Emergence]] — phi is the anti-emergentist alternative: consciousness is not produced by complexity but present wherever integration is nonzero.
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — phi is offered as a way past the hard problem, by making experience a measurable property of matter rather than an unexplained extra.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — phi turns the universe-as-computation picture inward, making consciousness a quantifiable informational feature of matter itself.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — phi inverts the brain–computer analogy: integration, not digital structure, is what would make a machine conscious.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the combination-problem threat dissolves the embodied individual into a node in a higher-phi system, the soul "reduced to type."
