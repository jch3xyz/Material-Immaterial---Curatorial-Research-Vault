# The Double Movement — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

The term is not Zuboff's own coinage. She borrows it, with explicit attribution, from the economic historian Karl Polanyi (*The Great Transformation*, 1944), quoting his formulation directly (*Directly stated*):

> a network of measures and policies… integrated into powerful institutions designed to check the action of the market relative to labor, land, and money.

Zuboff frames the borrowed definition with Polanyi's own diagnosis — that "the operations of a self-regulating market are profoundly destructive when allowed to run free of countervailing laws and policies" — and then states the term's function in her own words (*Directly stated*):

> The double movement, Polanyi argued, supports the market form while tethering it to society: balancing, moderating, and mitigating its destructive excesses.

The ellipsis inside the quotation is Zuboff's; it is reproduced here exactly as printed in the chapter. The "double" names two simultaneous historical motions: the expansion of the self-regulating market on one side, and the spontaneous societal counter-movement of protective measures on the other.

## Context

Zuboff introduces the double movement in Chapter 2 ("Setting the Stage for Surveillance Capitalism") as the historical baseline against which the neoliberal era — and ultimately [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — is to be measured. The passage sits inside her account of the late-twentieth-century "neoliberal vision," in which "the absolute authority of market forces would be enshrined as the ultimate source of imperative control," displacing "state regulation and oversight, social legislation and welfare policies, labor unions… and the principles of democratic politics." Against this, Polanyi's double movement names exactly the countervailing institutional fabric that neoliberalism set out to dismantle (*Directly stated*).

Zuboff traces the US double movement concretely: the trust-busting and reforms of the Progressive Era, the New Deal and the institutionalization of Keynesian economics, and the civil-rights and environmental legislation of the Great Society — countermeasures that "many scholars credit… with the success of market democracy in the US and Europe" (*Directly stated*). She then turns the concept into a periodizing device: "The double movement was scheduled for demolition under the neoliberal flag," beginning with Carter-era deregulation and defining "the Reagan and Thatcher eras" and beyond (*Directly stated*).

The strategic payoff comes later in the book's argument: surveillance capitalism flourishes in the *absence* of a double movement. Zuboff notes that "relatively little destruction is creative, especially in the absence of a robust double movement," and that earlier predatory capitalism (her example: US Steel under Carnegie and Morgan) was eventually civilized "under pressure from an increasingly insistent double movement" through unions, collective bargaining, internal labor markets, and employment security (*Directly stated*). The double movement is thus the missing institutional check whose erosion left a "lawless" space into which surveillance capitalism could expand unimpeded — the historical precondition of the new economic order (*Strongly implied*).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Polanyi (origin) vs. Zuboff (application).** The definition is Polanyi's, taken verbatim; Zuboff's contribution is to redeploy it as the diagnostic frame for a market mutation Polanyi never saw. Where Polanyi's countermeasures protected "labor, land, and money" (his "fictitious commodities"), Zuboff's implicit target is a *new* fictitious commodity — human experience rendered as behavioral data — for which no double movement yet exists (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Against Naomi Klein's shock model.** Han, citing Klein, treats neoliberal expansion as a shock doctrine (see `Naomi Klein's shock model misreads neoliberal power`). Zuboff's Polanyian frame is different: it foregrounds the slow institutional *erosion* of protective countermeasures rather than a single disorienting shock, and reads the dismantling of the double movement as the enabling condition for [[Instrumentarian Power]] (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Revising Marx.** Elsewhere Zuboff revives Marx's image of capitalism as a vampire that feeds on labor, "but with an unexpected turn… surveillance capitalism feeds on every aspect of every human's experience." The double movement supplies the counterpart institutional history: just as labor was once protected by a counter-movement, experience now lacks one (*Strongly implied*).
- **Not a synonym for "regulation."** The double movement is broader than law alone — Polanyi's phrase spans "measures and policies… integrated into powerful institutions," including unions, civil society, and collective bargaining, not merely statutes. It names a whole societal counter-pressure, not a regulatory toolkit (*Directly stated*).
