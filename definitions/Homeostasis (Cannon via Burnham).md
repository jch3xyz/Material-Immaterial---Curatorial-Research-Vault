# Homeostasis — Cannon via Jack Burnham (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*)

## Author's Definition

[[Jack Burnham]] does not coin *homeostasis*; he reports Walter B. Cannon's coinage and then carries it into art. In the "Cybernetic Changeover" section of "Robot and Cyborg Art," Burnham credits the term to Cannon's *The Wisdom of the Body* (1932): Cannon "explained the balanced plumbing of the human body, those ever present involuntary mechanisms maintaining the health of the whole organism." Burnham then gives the definition verbatim:

> He called this system of automatic valves and thermostats homeostasis. Defined, this is the ability of the body to keep its quantities ofliquids, solids, and gases in a state of equilibrium. Cannon found that the body is constantly forcing such a balance by various strategies, which include feedback mechanisms.

So defined, homeostasis is the body's automatic maintenance of internal balance — its quantities of liquids, solids, and gases held in equilibrium — achieved through [[Feedback Loops|feedback mechanisms]], specifically negative feedback (confidence: `Directly stated`). Burnham makes the negative-feedback link explicit later in the chapter: "Negative feedback (as was mentioned with Cannon's homeostasis) is the process whereby any number of coupled systems are designed to maintain a relatively constant input-output relationship" (confidence: `Directly stated`).

## Context

The definition appears as one term in Burnham's compressed primer on [[Cybernetics]] — the chapter's "Cybernetic Changeover," which runs through Wiener, Rosenblueth, Bigelow, McCulloch and Pitts, Shannon, Bertalanffy, Cannon, and Ashby on the way to declaring that "the system becomes the unit by which all levels of fluid organization are analyzed up through and including living organisms." Burnham folds Cannon's biological concept into the engineering vocabulary of [[Systems Theory]]: machines become "a series of 'servo systems'; or systems with closed-loop communication regulated as are Cannon's homeostasis functions for biological systems." The biological steady-state and the servo-mechanical steady-state are treated as the same logic operating at different complexities — exactly the animal–machine equivalence on which [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] is built (confidence: `Strongly implied`).

Burnham's purpose is art-historical, not biological. Homeostasis underwrites his master argument that all sculpture from the paleolithic through present-day [[Kinetic Art]] suffers an "inherent defect" — it is "a one-way message," it "does not respond to man in any intelligent fashion" — and that the "perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture" is an art capable of reciprocal, two-way relationship grounded in feedback. The equilibrium-seeking self-stabilizing system is, for Burnham, the model of organized life that responsive art must approximate. He applies the concept to art in two concrete instances:

1. **Schöffer's homeostatic balancing device.** In Nicolas Schöffer's cybernetic sound tower at the Parc Saint-Cloud (1954), environmental inputs selected tape fragments; to keep the output coherent, "a homeostatic device balanced the final sound pattern so that discordant combinations were avoided." Here a Cannon/Ashby steady-state mechanism is built into a work of art to hold its output in equilibrium (confidence: `Directly stated`).

2. **Ashby's homeostat.** Burnham describes W. Ross Ashby's "homeostat" — "a device with a set of four pointers" each guided by internal feedback, where "stability is only achieved for one pointer when it is gained by all." Ashby conceived it "as a model for some of the stabilizing operations in the human brain" (*Design for a Brain*, 1954) and called "a system seeking equilibrium by mutual stabilization of all of its subsystems" *ultrastability*. The homeostat is the mechanized, art-adjacent realization of Cannon's biological homeostasis (confidence: `Directly stated`).

Through these examples homeostasis becomes, in Burnham, an *art principle*: equilibrium-seeking is the perception of organized life, and an artwork that holds or seeks its own equilibrium under environmental disturbance reads as "alive." This is the conceptual bridge from Cannon's biology to Burnham's [[Cyborg Art]] — "the cybernetic organism as an art form."

## Differences from Other Uses

This is a **definition note** (Cannon's coinage as Burnham reports it). It is distinct from the multi-author concept note [[Homeostasis]], to which Burnham's reading is added; consult that note for the [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]], [[Norbert Wiener|Wiener]], and [[Marshall McLuhan|McLuhan]] treatments and their cross-comparison.

- **vs. [[N. Katherine Hayles]] (via Wiener).** Hayles historicizes homeostasis as the *period-defining constellation* of first-wave cybernetics — the contingent wager that allied information-as-thing with stable, medium-independent value, "won" because it was quantifiable rather than true, and surviving into the second wave only as a skeuomorph. Burnham, writing in 1968, takes homeostasis *un-historicized and affirmatively*: not a constellation to be denaturalized but a working principle to be built into art. Where Hayles reads homeostasis as the moment embodiment is abstracted away (it opposes [[Reflexivity]], see [[Homeostasis vs Reflexivity]]), Burnham reads it as the route by which an artwork might become *more* organism-like — equilibrium-seeking as the mark of life (confidence: `Interpretive synthesis`).
- **vs. [[Marshall McLuhan]].** McLuhan generalizes homeostasis into a standing law of the *social body* — the city as body politic answering "irritations" with "counter-irritant" new extensions to preserve "staying power, constancy, equilibrium." Burnham keeps the term closer to its Cannon/Ashby technical sense (servo systems, negative feedback, the homeostat) and applies it to *the artwork as a cybernetic system*, not to media or the body politic (confidence: `Interpretive synthesis`).
- **vs. equilibrium-seeking generally.** Burnham uses "homeostasis" interchangeably with the broader idea that any self-stabilizing system "seeks equilibrium" after disturbance (the Calder mobile, the servo-controlled mechanism, the homeostat). The Cannon definition is the *biological* anchor for that wider equilibrium-seeking principle running through his account of responsive art (confidence: `Strongly implied`).
