# Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)

## Author's Definition

Burnham reports Henri Bergson's *elan vital* as the "vital impetus" — in George Bernard Shaw's gloss, the "life force": an essence which makes life possible in inert matter, and which *is* life itself. It is the principle by which "inert matter is entered by a life force in order to instill animation, like a block of uncut stone on the sculptor's bench." In Burnham's account this idea is "vitalism regenerated and made the rallying cry for one of the twentieth century's most powerful aesthetics" (`Directly stated`).

Verbatim:

> Best remembered of Bergson's ideas is the phrase elan vital, the vital impetus, or, in George Bernard Shaw's words, the "life force." This was vitalism regenerated and made the rallying cry for one of the twentieth century's most powerful aesthetics, a term raised in authority by some of the most impressive names in science.

> Furthermore, the concept of an essence which made life possible in inert matter-which was life itself-possessed a persuasive dignity and charm which made it difficult even for a die-hard materialist to resist.

## Context

The definition appears in Chapter 2, "The Biotic Sources of Modern Sculpture," at the point where Burnham reconstructs the philosophy of [[Vitalism]] as "the sculptor's counterpart to an aesthetic based on organicism." Bergson is its "high priest." Burnham introduces the *elan vital* not as a settled scientific fact but as a *literary and aesthetic* force — he insists Bergson "must be studied as literature before considering [him] as philosophy" — whose power lay in its appeal to "sensitivity" rather than to proof: "Bergson's appeal to sensitivity made it as difficult to deny directly the presence of the elan vital as to prove its existence" (`Directly stated`).

What the term *does* in the larger argument is supply the metaphysical license for abstract sculpture. From *Creative Evolution* (1907), Burnham extracts the sculptor's working analogy directly: matter is "a block of uncut stone on the sculptor's bench," and "this analogy received literal acceptance by some of the next generation of sculptors, whose task it became to psychically force the shape of life into the fragments of material which came their way" (`Directly stated`). The *elan vital* thus becomes the "neo-vitalist aesthetic" — "ready-made for a sculpture that seemed not to be carved but to grow from an inner direction," an idea "in the air" for the Parisian avant-garde of the first two decades of the century (`Directly stated`).

The term also carries the book's larger arc toward the system. Burnham's thesis is that vitalism "lasted as long as it remained tenable as a scientific philosophy"; he closes the *elan vital* passage by noting that "in the last fifteen years the development of molecular biology has given us the beginning of a poetic view of living systems which may well in time far outshine the delicate imprecisions of Bergson." Elsewhere in the book the *elan vital* is named "the old prime mover," soon to be displaced by the *kybernetes* — the steersman, root of [[Cybernetics]] — "a new and even more effective prime mover" (`Strongly implied` / `Directly stated`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **A reported, not original, definition.** This is Bergson's term as Burnham *reports* it (with Shaw's "life force" gloss). The note is filed under Burnham because the verbatim wording, the sculptor's-block analogy, and the "rallying cry … aesthetic" framing are Burnham's. The concept [[Vitalism]] is the broader doctrine; [[Henri Bergson]] (forward-stub) is the source figure; this note is the specific term.
- **Distinct from [[Vitalism]] the concept.** Vitalism is the whole doctrine — and, for Burnham, the fifty-year *aesthetic* of modern sculpture; the *elan vital* is its single "best remembered" coinage, the "vital impetus" itself. Same relation as `Cyborg (Haraway)` (definition) to `The Cyborg` (concept).
- **The opposite of mechanistic explanation.** Burnham sets the *elan vital* squarely against [[Mechanism]]: "in contrast to the mechanistic theories of physiology and evolution, in no way did the elan vital denigrate the existence of man, nor nullify the divinity of his origins." This is the [[Mechanism vs Vitalism]] tension in its sharpest single term. Where the mechanist sees "physical laws" governing "every living entity on the earth," Bergson's force "had the power of cutting into the temporal continuum on its own volition." (`Directly stated`)
- **Not [[Organicism]].** Burnham keeps the *elan vital* (poetic, life-as-essence, vitalist) distinct from the organicist "view of the utilitarian evolution of systems." The two influenced each other but "remain in polar opposition."
- **Not material complexity / emergence.** The *elan vital* is precisely the *non-material* principle that the contemporary scientific account of life arising from material organization displaced. Burnham reads molecular biology as what will "far outshine" it — the two are cross-linked, not equated.
