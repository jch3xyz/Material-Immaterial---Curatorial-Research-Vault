# Automata — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

Burnham gives a deliberately broad, working definition in the opening pages of his chapter on sculpture and automata:

> Automata may be defined as all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men.

`Directly stated`. The definition is intentionally capacious: it gathers under one term not only the celebrated clockwork androids of the eighteenth century but an entire "vast substratum of figures" — what Burnham calls **subsculpture** — that art history "used to rank far below sculpture as a fine art."

## Context

Burnham introduces the term within *Beyond Modern Sculpture*'s larger project of reading the history of the human image as a history of technology. He places automata as "one particular branch of subsculpture," the category of "types of images aesthetically ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts" — alongside "Fetishes, idols, amulets, funeral images, dolls, waxworks, manikins, puppets." `Directly stated`.

What singles automata out, for Burnham, is not realism (he notes "waxworks and funeral images come closer to mimetic perfection than even the best classical sculpture or automata") but **literal movement**. The chapter's governing claim is that the history of automata "has always run close to that of technology," and that their "progressive mechanization" exposes "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge": "Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically." `Directly stated`.

The definition therefore does double duty. It is a taxonomic boundary (self-animation is the differentia that separates automata from inert subsculpture and from sculpture proper), and it is the entry point to Burnham's thesis that the drive behind every anthropomorphic image is "to 'bring to life' the human-shaped entity, though not through natural biological creation" — by art, by mimesis, by supposed magic, or, "as in the case of automata, by literal movement and animation." `Directly stated`. Burnham himself flags the larger claim as "an assumption which may be disputed," while holding firm to the narrower point that the maker's "ultimate concern" is animation. `Directly stated`.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus the technical "theory of automata."** Burnham is explicit that his anthropomorphic-image sense is not the cybernetic one: "The theory of automata — which has nothing to do with mechanical men — has become one of the most fascinating and vital branches of communication and cybernetic study." `Directly stated`. His definition names the *images*; the postwar discipline (`Cybernetics`) names abstract logic systems. The chapter traces how the two branches split after Pascal and Leibniz — "the traditional objective of constructing amusing facsimiles of men and animals" versus "the designing of logic systems and programming techniques."

- **Versus Hayles's and Haraway's cyborg.** Burnham's automaton is the genealogical ancestor of `The Cyborg`: both name the figure that breaches the organism/machine boundary and the drive "to re-create life nonbiologically." But where Donna Haraway and N. Katherine Hayles theorize the cyborg as a condition of the embodied subject under informatics, Burnham defines automata as *made images* — externalized, doomed to "physical disintegration" and to "a final sense of inconviction" in the beholder's mind. `Interpretive synthesis`. The shared stake is the question of `Embodiment` and the `Material vs Immaterial` line that runs through the vault.

- **Versus the everyday "robot."** Within the same chapter Burnham distinguishes the robot (the de-anthropomorphized "mechanical servant in the spirit of the modern, ultra-functional machine," from Čapek's Czech word for "forced labor") from the older human-looking automata his definition centers on. The definition's "images of animals or men" deliberately privileges the anthropomorphic line that the robot abandons. `Directly stated`.
