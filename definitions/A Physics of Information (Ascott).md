# A physics of information — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

In "Gesamtdatenwerk: Connectivity, Transformation, and Transcendence" (1989) Ascott coins **a physics of information** by mapping the structure of quantum physics term-for-term onto computer science. The analogy is stated as a substitution:

> replace the term “quanta” with “data” and a physics of information is invoked. For it is in the quantum world that reality is the production of observation.

The mapping is made explicit a few lines later, with **data** named as the object whose **transformation** is the proper subject of computer science, exactly as **quanta** are the object whose transformation is the subject of quantum physics:

> The subject of quantum physics is the transformation of energy, and quanta are its object. The subject of computer science is the transformation of information, and data are its object. Data exist in streams; data flow is ephemeral, transient, shifting. Data is everywhere and nowhere. A physics of information would talk about the phase space, the virtual space that data occupy.

Two further properties complete the analogy. Data are **time-free** — asynchronous and non-linear — even though they are processed in metered time, and they are **discontinuous**, "jumping" between states the way quanta jump levels:

> Processed in time—the beat of the computer’s pulse measured in nanoseconds—data are nevertheless time-free and time-resistant in so far as their transformation within computer networking is concerned. […] In this sense, data are asynchronously managed and networking becomes a non-linear creative medium. And, as with quantum behaviour, data are discontinuous; they “jump” between semantic states.

A physics of information is therefore not a metaphor of resemblance but a structural homology: data are to computer science what quanta are to physics — the ephemeral, asynchronous, discontinuous object whose transformation, not whose storage, is the discipline's true subject. (`Directly stated`)

## Context

The definition is the analytic engine in the middle of the essay's three-stage arc (Connectivity → Transformation → Transcendence). Ascott has just argued that immersion in electronic networks forces "a re-evaluation of the status of reality" as "provisional," plural, and "constructed—'virtual' in a sense." A physics of information is what licenses that constructivist conclusion: because in the quantum world "reality is the production of observation" — "the apparatus we use, the measuring system we employ, and the language we articulate, all condition the nature of the reality we perceive" — the data world inherits the same participatory, observer-dependent ontology. The physics of information thus grounds two things at once: it grounds [[Data Space|data space]] (the "phase space, the virtual space that data occupy") as the medium of telematic art, and it grounds Ascott's constructed-reality argument and his "radically constructive repudiation of the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought." (`Directly stated`)

It also does work in Ascott's redefinition of the computer. If data are ephemeral, "everywhere and nowhere," and time-free, then the computer cannot be understood as a discrete thing or apparatus; it is "the agent of the data field, a construct of data space." The physics of information is the technical underpinning of the larger telematic claim that art's medium has moved from the material object to the immaterial, asynchronous, planetary data flow. This is Ascott's behaviourist-telematic recasting of Burnham's earlier [[Object vs System|object→system]] move (the artwork becomes a system) into a further system→network move (the system becomes a planet-wide participatory data field). (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. Shannon / information theory.** A physics of information is not Shannon's quantitative measure of uncertainty reduction in a signal. Ascott's interest is ontological and processual: data as a *kind* of entity (ephemeral, discontinuous, observer-dependent) whose transformation constitutes a medium, not a quantity carried by a channel. (`Directly stated`)
- **vs. Han's [[Information]] (and the def [[Information (Han)|Information (Han)]]).** Han defines information negatively, as "positivized, operationalized language" that "lacks all negativity" — a flattening, accelerative, anti-transformative form. Ascott's data is the opposite valence: its very discontinuity and indeterminacy make it a "fecund," generative, "non-linear creative medium." Where Han's information forecloses, Ascott's data opens. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **vs. The [[The Computational Universe|computational universe]] / Dataism.** Other vault authors (Kurzweil, Harari, Wolfram via O'Gieblyn) treat the world as *already* made of information or computation — an objective, given substrate. Ascott's physics of information is explicitly **constructivist**: data, like quanta, do not pre-exist their observation; "reality is the production of observation." Reality is built in the act of participatory measurement, not discovered. This aligns Ascott with [[Idealism|idealism]] / [[Intersubjective Reality|constructed reality]] rather than with computational realism. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **vs. quantum physics proper.** The physicists Ascott invokes (Heisenberg, Schrödinger, Bell, Aspect) supply the source domain, not the target; he is not doing physics but importing its observer-dependence, indeterminacy, and discontinuity as a model for the behaviour of data. The transfer is deliberate and avowed ("it is easy to see a comparison to quantum physics"). (`Directly stated`)
