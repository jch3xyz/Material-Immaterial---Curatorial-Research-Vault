# Reification (Burnham)

## Author's Definition

Burnham takes over Marx's *Verdinglichung* — "thingification" — and then deliberately redirects it. He first states the inherited Marxian sense verbatim:

> For Marx "thingification" was a term which described how certain societies transformed all ideas into objects. By this means, man himself became an exchangeable object, a commodity.

He then announces a different vantage — "Reification will be described from another point of view" — and supplies his own operative definition for the history of modern sculpture:

> Thus the process of "thingification" which has given birth to modern sculpture is the constant resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory.

For Burnham, reification is therefore not chiefly the alienating conversion of persons into commodities but the mechanism by which "the world is made anew, through sculpture or other artifacts, in a scientific culture" — sculpture's repeated re-tuning of its sensibility to whatever form-world the reigning scientific paradigm has disclosed (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The term anchors the Introduction to *Beyond Modern Sculpture*. Burnham frames sculpture not as "a segregated endeavor" but as "a vestigial biological activity closely related to the technological drive," and he needs a single concept to name how sculpture's forms track the surrounding "scientific world picture." He borrows Marx's most "dynamic concept" — "the dialectical convenience of reification or 'thingification' (Verdinglichung)" — but explicitly brackets Marx's economic frame ("This Introduction will not consider if Marx's economic solutions could reverse the effects of reification to restore man's sense of wholeness") so the word can do art-historical work instead (`Directly stated`).

Reification, so redefined, becomes the engine of his whole periodization. It runs parallel to scientific paradigm shifts: "Each paradigm of major importance has produced not only a scientific but a subsequent cultural revolution," and "Similarly, reification is the means by which the world is made anew." Echoing Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962), Burnham distinguishes "'major reifications' by which sculpture comes to entirely new modes of form consciousness" from "'minor reifications' where a given form consciousness is elaborated," both "dependent on the evolving scientific world picture." The same process carries sculpture "away from its traditional anthropomorphic course," dissolves the sculpture base, draws sculptors toward vitalism and then toward "Object or Minimal sculpture … sculpture de-idealized," and ultimately "toward more precise approximations of the systems which underlie operational reality" (`Directly stated`). This last move — sculpture aligning itself with *systems* rather than objects — is the seed of Burnham's later systems aesthetic and his thematic kinship with the vault's [[Systems Theory]] and [[Dematerialization]] clusters (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus Marx (and the standard critical-theory sense).** In the Marxian/Lukácsian tradition reification is a pathology: the freezing of social relations and human capacities into thing-like, commodified objectivity — "man himself became an exchangeable object, a commodity," accomplishing "his own alienation from self." Burnham quotes this sense exactly and then sets it aside. His reification is largely *productive and cognitive*: a "resynchronization of artistic sensibility" with science, the way "the world is made anew." Where Marx's reification estranges, Burnham's chiefly *discloses* — though he preserves an ambivalent residue, noting "an ironic twist to reification in modern sculpture," since the very scientific paradigms sculpture chases become "increasingly difficult to approximate through the traditional means of sculpture" (`Directly stated`).

- **Versus the vault's base concept [[Reification]].** This note records Burnham's idiosyncratic, art-historical redefinition; the concept note holds the broader, primarily critical (object/commodity-making) sense. They share the Marxian root and are cross-linked, but the (Burnham) qualifier marks the deliberate divergence — the shift of emphasis from "transforming all ideas into objects" to re-tuning artistic form to the disclosed world of scientific theory (`Directly stated`).

- **Resonances elsewhere in the vault.** Burnham's image of an "icon par excellence" that "interprets principles believed to be operative in the real world," and of "scientific reality" having "become the measure of all reality," rhymes with the vault's recurring [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension and with science-as-worldview motifs in O'Gieblyn's [[Disenchantment]]/[[Re-enchantment]] pair — but Burnham makes the claim specifically about *sculpture's form*, not consciousness or meaning at large (`Speculative connection`).
