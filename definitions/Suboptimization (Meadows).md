# Suboptimization — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows coins the term in a single sentence, at the end of a run of examples of hierarchies gone wrong:

> "When a subsystem's goals dominate at the expense of the total system's goals, the resulting behavior is called suboptimization." (*Directly stated*)

Suboptimization is thus not incompetence in a part, nor a defect in any element, but a *misalignment of goals across levels of a [[Hierarchy (Systems)|hierarchy]]* — a subsystem optimizing for its own local end while the larger system it belongs to loses. Meadows makes the abstraction concrete with four parallel cases, each sharing the shape of a part maximizing itself against the whole:

> "If a team member is more interested in personal glory than in the team winning, he or she can cause the team to lose. If a body cell breaks free from its hierarchical function and starts multiplying wildly, we call it a cancer. If students think their purpose is to maximize personal grades instead of seeking knowledge, cheating and other counterproductive behaviors break out. If a single corporation bribes the government to favor that corporation, the advantages of the competitive market and the good of the whole society are eroded." (*Directly stated*)

The cancer cell is the limit case: a subsystem that has severed its service to the whole entirely, optimizing its own replication until it kills the body that hosts it (*Strongly implied*).

## Context

The definition sits inside Chapter 3, "Why Systems Work So Well," in Meadows's account of [[Hierarchy (Systems)|hierarchy]] as one of the three properties — with [[Resilience]] and [[Self-Organization]] — that let dynamic systems function well. Her model of hierarchy is bottom-up in origin and top-down in purpose: subsystems come first and self-organize into larger wholes, and "the original purpose of a hierarchy is always to help its originating subsystems do their jobs better" — something "both the higher and the lower levels of a greatly articulated hierarchy easily can forget" (*Directly stated*). Suboptimization is the name for the failure of the bottom-to-top direction of that service: the part stops serving the system and serves only itself.

Crucially, Meadows presents suboptimization as only *one* of two symmetrical malfunctions, and refuses to let central coordination be read as the cure:

> "Just as damaging as suboptimization, of course, is the problem of too much central control." (*Directly stated*)

Overcontrol is the mirror image — the whole strangling the autonomy its parts need (a brain policing each cell so tightly it cannot self-maintain; central rules stopping students and faculty from exploring freely; economic overcontrol from the top that has caused "some of the great catastrophes of history"). The resolution the definition points to is therefore balance, not the victory of either pole: "there must be enough central control to achieve coordination toward the largesystem goal, and enough autonomy to keep all subsystems flourishing, functioning, and self-organizing" (*Directly stated*). This is why the term belongs on the [[Central Control vs Autonomy]] axis — it names the failure at the *autonomy* pole, with overcontrol as its counterpart at the control pole.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Not the optimization-theory / operations-research sense.** In mathematics, engineering, and management science "suboptimization" often means optimizing a subproblem in isolation, or settling for a merely locally optimal (rather than globally optimal) solution. Meadows's usage is adjacent but specifically *systemic and behavioral*: it names an emergent malfunction of a multi-level system in which a subsystem's *goals* — read off its behavior, not its rhetoric — override the total system's goals. Her diagnostic frame is part-vs-whole goal conflict inside a living hierarchy, not a solver stuck at a local optimum.

- **A relational failure, not a defective part.** Suboptimization is not a flaw in any one element's competence — "the glory-seeker may be a superb player." It is a misalignment of *whose* goal is being served across levels. This distinguishes it from ordinary notions of malfunction as broken parts, and links it to Meadows's insistence that a system's [[Elements Interconnections and Purpose|purpose]] — the least visible component — is the most crucial determinant of its behavior.

- **Distinct from its symmetrical opposite.** Where much political and managerial thinking treats decentralization or self-interest as the primary danger and central authority as the fix, Meadows deliberately pairs suboptimization with *overcontrol* as equally damaging. The term is defined by imbalance; its true opposite is not central control but the *balanced* hierarchy that avoids both failures.
