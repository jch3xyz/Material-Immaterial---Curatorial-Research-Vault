# Apparition — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

For Roy Ascott, **apparition** names the inward and emergent process of *becoming* — the coming into being of a new identity — set against **appearance**, the outward and visible look of things, surface reality, representation. Where appearance fixes "things as they are," apparition is the emergence of "things as they could be." `Directly stated`

The defining contrast is stated explicitly:

> If appearance is claimed as the face of reality, of things as they are, apparition is the emergence of things as they could be.

And again, as the axis of a whole shift in art:

> Art is no longer about appearance, and certainly not about representation, but is concerned with apparition, the coming into being of what has never before been seen or heard or experienced.

The shift between the two terms is, in Ascott's own formulation, a shift "from the outward and visible look of things to the inward and emergent processes of becoming." `Directly stated`

## Context

Ascott introduces apparition as the conceptual pivot of his account of art in the cyberculture — most fully in the essay whose very title states the movement, "From Appearance to Apparition." The Western tradition of art, he argues, has always been concerned with appearance: with the look of things, with surface reality, with representation, keeping art "in line with classical science, looking no further into things than their outward forms allow." That art of appearance is, for Ascott, "dying," because it is no longer relevant to "a culture that is progressively concerned with the complexity of relationships and subtlety of systems, with the invisible and immaterial, the evolutive and the evanescent, in short, with apparition." `Directly stated`

Apparition is therefore the temporal and ontological signature of an art of process rather than product. "Apparition implies action, just as appearance implies inertia"; it is "about the coming into being of a new identity, which is often, at first, unexpected, surprising, disturbing." Cyberspace, for Ascott, "is the space of apparition, in which the virtual and real not only co-exist, but co-evolve." This connects apparition directly to [[Cyberception]] — the faculty that "enables us to perceive the apparitions of cyberspace, the coming into being of their virtual presence" — and to the larger move "from object to process" that underwrites [[Systems Esthetics]] and his vision of [[New Media Art]] as an art of "transformation, growth, and change." `Directly stated`

The pairing is the heart of the [[Appearance vs Apparition]] tension and a primary contribution to the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis: appearance belongs to the material object and its surface, apparition to "the invisible and immaterial," "the inward and emergent processes of becoming." `Strongly implied`

## Differences from Other Uses

In ordinary usage "apparition" denotes a ghost or spectral visual appearance — an unexpected *thing seen*. Ascott pointedly inverts this: his apparition is precisely *not* about the seen surface but about emergence, becoming, and the construction of new realities; it is opposed to, not a species of, visible appearance. `Interpretive synthesis`

Within the vault, Ascott's apparition is the affirmative, art-making counterpart to several diagnostic notes. Where [[Dematerialization]] (Lucy Lippard) and [[Systems Esthetics]] (Jack Burnham) trace the historical passage of the art object toward system, process, and information, Ascott supplies the metaphysical claim that art's proper concern is the *coming into being* of what has never existed before, not the depiction of what is. His "emergence of things as they could be" rhymes with the vault's broader concept of [[Emergence]] (the bottom-up coming-into-being of new order) but is specifically aesthetic and constructivist — grounded in his [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]], in which "to construct art is to construct reality." `Interpretive synthesis`

Apparition's stress on the virtual co-evolving with the real also aligns it with [[Virtuality]] as theorized elsewhere in the vault, but Ascott treats the virtual not as a loss or simulation of the real (the worry in much posthuman discourse) but as the very medium of becoming. `Interpretive synthesis`
