# Function vs Purpose — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Under the heading "A NOTE ON LANGUAGE," Meadows draws a terminological distinction between the two words she uses for the third component of a system — the component she elsewhere calls its [[Elements Interconnections and Purpose|function or purpose]]:

> "The word function is generally used for a nonhuman system, the word purpose for a human one, but the distinction is not absolute, since so many systems have both human and nonhuman elements." (*Directly stated*)

The distinction is deliberately soft. It is a convention of usage, not an ontological divide: because "so many systems have both human and nonhuman elements," the same third component may be called a *function* or a *purpose* depending on which face of the system is in view. Meadows illustrates each usage from her own examples — "The function of a thermostat-furnace system is to keep a building at a given temperature. One function of a plant is to bear seeds and create more plants. One purpose of a national economy is, judging from its behavior, to keep growing larger" (*Directly stated*).

## Context

This note-on-language appears inside Meadows's opening account of what a system *is*. A system, she writes, "must consist of three kinds of things: elements, interconnections, and a function or purpose" — and of the three, "the least obvious part of the system, its function or purpose, is often the most crucial determinant of the system's behavior" ([[The purpose of a system is the most crucial determinant of its behavior]], *Directly stated*). Having just argued that this component is the hardest to see, Meadows pauses to clarify her own vocabulary before proceeding, so the reader does not mistake "function" and "purpose" for two different things.

Two further moves in the surrounding text keep the pair from hardening into human-versus-machine dualism. First, a system's function or purpose "is not necessarily spoken, written, or expressed explicitly, except through the operation of the system"; the way to find it is to watch behavior — "if a frog turns right and catches a fly… the purpose of the frog has to do… with catching flies" ([[A systems purpose is deduced from its behavior not its stated goals]], *Directly stated*). Because purpose is read off behavior rather than declared, the same behavioral-deduction method applies whether the system is a frog, a furnace, or a government. Second, "system purposes need not be human purposes and are not necessarily those intended by any single actor within the system"; the word "purpose" does not smuggle in conscious human intention (*Directly stated*). This decouples "purpose" from mind and lets Meadows speak of the *purpose* of an economy or a drug-and-crime system without implying anyone intends it — indeed, "the purposes of subunits may add up to an overall behavior that no one wants" (*Directly stated*).

For this vault the pair matters because it is Meadows's disciplinary way of refusing the human/nonhuman boundary at the level of *behavior*: the same explanatory category — the thing a system is *for*, deduced from what it does — spans thermostats and universities alike. That refusal runs parallel to the posthumanist and cybernetic sources' erosion of the same boundary, but Meadows reaches it from systems dynamics rather than from theory of the subject.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Purpose as intention vs. purpose as behavior.** In ordinary and much philosophical usage, "purpose" names a conscious human aim. Meadows explicitly severs that tie: "System purposes need not be human purposes." Her "purpose" is a *behavioral* attribute deduced by observation ("Purposes are deduced from behavior, not from rhetoric or stated goals"), closer to a teleonomic than a teleological sense. This is why she can call growth the "purpose" of a national economy — "judging from its behavior" — without attributing intent to anyone.

- **Function (Meadows) vs. function elsewhere in the vault.** Meadows's "function" is the *for-what* of a whole system read off its operation. It is not the mechanical-cause sense, nor the mathematical sense of a function/mapping (Meadows keeps her primer "nontechnical"), nor the sociological structural-functionalist sense of a part's contribution to social order. Her nonhuman example — the thermostat-furnace system whose function is temperature regulation — is a homeostatic control loop, cross-linking this pair to [[Homeostasis]], [[Feedback Loops]], and [[Cybernetics]], but the word "function" here labels the *goal the loop holds*, not the loop mechanism itself.

- **Not a hard human/machine dualism.** Where much of the vault's material treats human vs. machine as a contested boundary (the cyborg, embodiment vs. information), Meadows's function/purpose split is explicitly *not absolute* and dissolves in "systems [that] have both human and nonhuman elements." It is a labeling convenience, not a claim about two kinds of being — a contrast with dualist uses of the same terms.

- **Distinct from the system component it names.** This is the terminological *pair*; the underlying component of a system is the concept [[Elements Interconnections and Purpose]]. The related concepts [[Self-Organization]] and [[System Goals]] name what a system's purpose does and where it sits in Meadows's [[Material vs Immaterial|leverage hierarchy]]; this note only fixes the *words* function and purpose.
