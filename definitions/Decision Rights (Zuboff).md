# Decision Rights — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

> This power is a crucial illustration of the difference between “decision rights” and “privacy.” Decision rights confer the power to choose whether to keep something secret or to share it. One can choose the degree of privacy or transparency for each situation. US Supreme Court Justice William O. Douglas articulated this view of privacy in 1967: “Privacy involves the choice of the individual to disclose or to reveal what he believes, what he thinks, what he possesses.…”

For Shoshana Zuboff, *decision rights* are the power to choose whether to keep something secret or to share it — the self-determining authority to set, situation by situation, one's own degree of privacy or transparency. The definition is explicitly a reframing of *privacy*: drawing on Justice William O. Douglas's 1967 formulation of privacy as "the choice of the individual to disclose or to reveal," Zuboff relocates the concept from a possession one *has* to an authority one *exercises*. Privacy, on this account, is not a quantity of concealed information but the right to decide what is disclosed. (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Zuboff introduces the term in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*'s chapter on the discovery of behavioral surplus, at the point where she examines Google's culture of secrecy. The fact that "Google had the power to choose secrecy is itself testament to the success of its own claims" — and that observation becomes "a crucial illustration of the difference between 'decision rights' and 'privacy.'" The definition is therefore not abstract: it is deployed to make a *power* analysis visible where a *privacy* analysis would obscure it. (`Directly stated`)

The work the definition does in the larger argument is to convert the standard complaint — that privacy is being *eroded* — into a diagnosis of *redistribution*. "Surveillance capitalism lays claim to these decision rights," Zuboff writes; "privacy is not eroded but redistributed, as decision rights over privacy are claimed for surveillance capital." The rights do not vanish into thin air; they migrate, concentrated within [[Surveillance Capitalism]] rather than held by individuals who decide "how and what they will disclose." Defining decision rights precisely is what licenses this reframe: it names the unit of power that is being transferred. (`Directly stated`)

The deepest twist Zuboff gives the definition is temporal. Because rendition is ubiquitous and obscure, decision rights "vanish before one even knows that there is a decision to make." The right is defined as a power to *choose*, but surveillance capitalism's design forecloses the moment of choice in advance — the 2003 Google patent's methods deduce user profile information "even when no explicit information is given to the system," overriding the user who withholds. The definition thus carries its own negation: a right to decide that one is structurally never positioned to exercise. (`Strongly implied`)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the everyday / legal concept of *privacy*.** The vault's Han cluster treats transparency and exposure largely from the side of the subject; see [[Transparency (Han)]], where transparency is a coercive social demand. Zuboff's contribution is orthogonal: she does not argue about *how much* privacy exists but insists the right vocabulary is *decision rights*, the authority over disclosure. "The typical complaint is that privacy is eroded, but that is misleading." This is the substance of the [[Privacy vs Decision Rights]] tension — less an opposition of values than a contest over the correct description of the harm.
- **vs. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]].** Han diagnoses subjects who *voluntarily* exercise their decision rights toward total self-exposure; Zuboff diagnoses decision rights *taken* "even when we say 'no.'" The two describe the same disclosure problem from opposite sides — the willing chooser (Han) and the chooser whose choice is expropriated before it can be made (Zuboff). (`Speculative connection`)
- **vs. the concept note.** This definition note isolates Zuboff's explicit definitional move (the Douglas-grounded "power to choose secrecy or disclosure"); the concept [[Decision Rights]] carries the fuller account of redistribution, the patent evidence, and the political stakes. The `(Zuboff)` suffix marks this as a real definition note, paired with — not a duplicate of — the concept.
