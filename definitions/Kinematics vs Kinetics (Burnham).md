# Kinematics vs Kinetics — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

Burnham draws an explicit distinction between two sciences of motion inherited from nineteenth-century machine theory. Tracing the term to André Ampère's 1830 *Essay on the Philosophy of Sciences*, he defines kinematics as the abstract, geometric study of motion considered apart from the forces and bodies that produce it:

> Kinematics (or cinematique as Ampere called his new science) consequently became the study of motion unattached to forces or objects

He further specifies that, applied to machinery, kinematics treats only the visually apparent geometric relationships of contacting parts — "Essentially kinematics has remained the geometry of touching surfaces." (`Directly stated`)

Against this he sets kinetics as the science of motion bound to physical force and to the systems in which that force acts:

> Kinetics, on the other hand, is concerned with motions resulting from forces directly connected to physical systems.

(`Directly stated`)

## Context

The distinction appears in Chapter 6, "Kineticism: The Unrequited Art," where Burnham reconstructs the genealogy of [[Kinetic Art]] out of machine theory. He situates the pair within Ampère's foundational move: in 1830 Ampère "define[d] the nature of machine relationships so that they could be subjected to mathematical analysis and planning," concluding that "the direction and velocity of motions, not forces, was the core of machine study." Kinematics is thus the theoretical, ideal half of motion-study — the one that lets a designer "propose the translation of forces primarily on the strength of visually apparent geometric relationships." (`Directly stated`)

Burnham's load-bearing observation is a point of nomenclature with conceptual consequences. The twentieth-century art of visual motion took the *materially* oriented name rather than the theoretical one:

> For that reason, perhaps, it is interesting that an art of visual motion became connected with the more materially orientated word kinetic, instead of the theoretical kinematic.

He attributes this partly to the medium's tangibility: because the concept of the kinetic "was necessarily attached to sculpture, because of its tangible properties, perhaps it was inevitable that the physical side of motion would be stressed over the ideal." (`Directly stated`) The choice of "kinetic" over "kinematic" is therefore, for Burnham, a symptom of kinetic art's commitment to the physical, material side of motion over the abstract geometry of pure relationship — a tension that runs through his account of the form as "unrequited." (`Interpretive synthesis`)

The pair also frames Burnham's adjacent discussion of Franz Reuleaux's machine, defined as a "closed kinematic chain" — the determinate control of motion and power that Kinetic Art, at its roots, celebrates (see [[The Closed Kinematic Chain]]). (`Strongly implied`)

## Differences from Other Uses

This is the only author-anchored treatment of the kinematics/kinetics distinction in the vault; the term is Burnham's via Ampère and Reuleaux. Burnham's usage is historical and technical rather than physics-pedagogical: he is less interested in defining the two sciences for their own sake than in using their *naming* to diagnose where kinetic art placed its emphasis — on tangible, force-bearing physical systems rather than on the ideal "geometry of touching surfaces." Where the vault's other motion- and machine-related notes ([[Kinetic Art]], [[The Closed Kinematic Chain]]) concern the artistic and mechanical realizations of controlled motion, this note isolates the prior conceptual fork — theoretical versus material — from which those realizations descend. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
