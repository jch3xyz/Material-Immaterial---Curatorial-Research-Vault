# Surveillance Exceptionalism — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

For Zuboff, *surveillance exceptionalism* is a "unique historical deformity": the post-9/11 condition in which surveillance capitalism's fledgling practices were allowed to root and grow with little regulatory or legislative challenge because the state came to covet — rather than contest — Google's emerging surveillance capabilities. It names the public-private *elective affinity* (Max Weber) that, under a *state of exception* (Giorgio Agamben), sheltered the surveillance-capitalist mutation from the privacy legislation that had been imminent before the September 2001 attacks.

The term is introduced verbatim as the product of that affinity:

> The elective affinity between public intelligence agencies and the fledgling surveillance capitalist Google blossomed in the heat of emergency to produce a unique historical deformity: surveillance exceptionalism. (`Directly stated`)

Its consequence — the substance of the definition — is stated just as plainly:

> Surveillance exceptionalism helped to shape the evolutionary course of information capitalism by creating an environment in which Google's budding surveillance practices were coveted rather than contested.… one unanticipated consequence of this public-private "elective affinity" was that the fledgling practices of surveillance capitalism were allowed to root and grow with little regulatory or legislative challenge. (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Zuboff introduces the term in Chapter 4 ("The Moat Around the Castle"), Section IV, as the *second* of the historical circumstances that sheltered the young surveillance market form — the first being the neoliberal capture of regulatory machinery (Section III). It belongs to the chapter's "third set of answers" to the book's recurring question, "How did they get away with it?": the political and cultural circumstances that "advanced surveillance capitalism's claims and protected them from fatal challenge" (`Directly stated`).

The setup is a near-collision. In 2000 — before Google's behavioral-surplus discovery had been understood and one year before 9/11 — a majority of FTC commissioners, having concluded that self-regulation had failed, recommended federal legislation to regulate online privacy. Zuboff's counterfactual is that "had these been translated into law, it is quite possible that many of the foundational elements of surveillance capitalism would have been either plainly illegal or at least subject to public examination and contest" (`Directly stated`). The attacks intervened: "With the attacks of September 11, 2001, everything changed. The new focus was overwhelmingly on security rather than privacy." Privacy provisions "vanished from the conversation more or less overnight," displaced by the Patriot Act and the warrantless-collection regime.

Two borrowed concepts do the analytic work. Via surveillance scholar David Lyon, Zuboff invokes Agamben's *state of exception* — "In this environment of trauma and anxiety, a 'state of exception' was invoked to legitimate a new imperative: speed at any cost" — because the slow, checks-and-balances tempos of a democratic state had become intolerable to agencies "scrambling" for "information dominance" (`Directly stated`). From Weber she takes the *elective affinity*, "a mutual magnetism that originates in shared meanings, interests, and reciprocities": the constitutionally constrained intelligence community and the lawless firm discovered a shared craving — certainty — that each was "determined to fulfill … in their respective domains at any price." Surveillance exceptionalism is the name Zuboff gives to the deformity that this affinity produced. Concretely it ran through In-Q-Tel, Poindexter's Total Information Awareness, the NSA/CIA Google contracts, the Keyhole acquisition, and the NSA's "Google-like" ICREACH — culminating in the stark claim that "had it not been for surveillance exceptionalism, it is possible that these data would not even exist, at least not in their current volume and detail."

The decisive move in the definition is *evolutionary*, not merely episodic: by making surveillance "coveted rather than contested," the exception meant Google's leaders "were not compelled to undertake the arduous and risky work of inventing an exchange-based advocacy-oriented market form when the surveillance model was so lucrative." The exception did not merely tolerate the mutation — it *selected for* it, and once "surveillance revenues set the bar for venture capitalists and Wall Street analysts," every other internet business followed (`Strongly implied`). This is why Zuboff later names it "the realpolitik of surveillance exceptionalism," listing it alongside second-modernity needs and the neoliberal inheritance as a historical condition that "birthed, sheltered, and nurtured" the surveillance project (`Directly stated`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the concept note [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]].** This definition note isolates the *definitional* core — the verbatim coinage and its stated consequence — and the two borrowed terms (Weber's elective affinity, Agamben's state of exception) that constitute it. The concept note carries the fuller mechanism (In-Q-Tel, TIA, ICREACH), the counterfactual, and the cross-author tensions. Same pattern as `Surveillance Capitalism` / [[Surveillance Capitalism (Zuboff)]] elsewhere in the vault.
- **vs. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[Dataism]].** Han diagnoses a *steady-state* condition of voluntary self-exposure and the dictatorship of data; Zuboff's term is a *genesis* story — a discrete historical emergency that explains how the apparatus those diagnoses describe was allowed to take root unchallenged. Han is synchronic phenomenology; Zuboff is diachronic political economy.
- **vs. Deleuze's [[Society of Control]].** Control society is continuous, ambient modulation. Surveillance exceptionalism is the *opposite genesis* — a discontinuous emergency that ruptures the slow democratic order ("the tempos of democracy are slow by design, weighted by redundancies, checks and balances, laws and rules") so that the control regime could install itself. The exception is the crisis through which the steady state arrived.
- **vs. the generic "American exceptionalism" / security-state literature.** Zuboff's term is narrower and economic: it is not the state's exemption of *itself* from constraint but the state's *coveting and sheltering of a private firm's* extraction capabilities — a public-private fusion in which "the boundaries of private and public melt in the intense heat of new threats."
