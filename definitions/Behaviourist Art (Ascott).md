# Behaviourist Art — Roy Ascott

> [!note] Definition note
> This is the **definition** note — Ascott's own functional, point-of-coinage definition of the term. For the fuller concept, its variants, related arguments, and its place in the vault's 1960s cybernetic-art cluster, see the concept note [[Behaviourist Art]].

## Author's Definition

Ascott's most compressed functional definition closes the theoretical first half of "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" (1966–67):

> Behaviourist art constitutes, as we have seen, a retroactive process of human involvement, in which the artifact functions as both matrix and catalyst.

He then unpacks each term and states the operating principle:

> As matrix, it is the substance between two sets of behaviours; it exists neither for itself nor by itself. As a catalyst, it triggers changes in the spectator's total behaviour. … The basic principle is feedback. The artifact/observer system furnishes its own controlling energy: a function of an output variable (observer's response) is to act as an input variable, which introduces more variety into the system and leads to more variety in the output (observer's experience).

So defined, behaviourist art is: (a) **retroactive** (feedback-governed) rather than one-directional; (b) a **process of human involvement** rather than a finished object; (c) constituted by an artifact that is at once **matrix** (the "substance between two sets of behaviours" — the artist's and the spectator's) and **catalyst** (a trigger of changes in the spectator's "total behaviour"); whose (d) **basic principle is feedback**, the artifact/observer system supplying "its own controlling energy" in a self-organising loop. *Directly stated.*

Folded into the definition is Ascott's **terminological proposal** — that "behavioural" replace "visual" as the inclusive name for the new art:

> a more inclusive term than "visual" art must be found, and the one I propose is "behavioural."

Because the channel of communication is no longer only visual but "tactile, postural, aural," and because "the artist, the artifact, and the spectator are all involved in a more behavioural context," Ascott renames the field itself: the art is constituted not by what is seen on a surface but by the conduct of the whole artist/artwork/observer triad. *Directly stated.*

The 1967 broadside "Behaviourables and Futuribles" reduces the definition to a credo:

> Art is now a form of behaviour. Message ends.

## Context

Ascott introduces this definition to mark a migration of art's very field — "The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour, and its function has become less descriptive and more purposive" — and to displace what he calls the "deterministic" aesthetic, in which a "clearly defined message" is pushed at a "more or less passive receptor." Against that one-way channel, behaviourist art makes the work "exist in a perpetual state of transition, where the effort to establish a final resolution must come from the observer" — the game "never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play." The definition is therefore doing the work of *re-grounding* aesthetics in cybernetic feedback ([[Cybernetics]], [[Feedback Loops]]): the criterion of art becomes the sustained two-way loop, i.e. [[Reciprocal Communication]], not the styling of an object. The "matrix and catalyst" formula is the precise functional statement of that re-grounding — it tells you what the artwork *is for* once art is behaviour. *Directly stated.*

Crucially, Ascott does not equate behaviour with the abolition of the object. The companion manifesto insists "Process culture and behaviourist art need not mean the end of the object, as long as it means the beginning of new values for art" — he **revalues** rather than dematerializes the object, a nuance that distinguishes his definition from a pure dematerialization reading. *Directly stated.*

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus the concept note [[Behaviourist Art]].** This note is Ascott's terse functional definition (matrix/catalyst + feedback + the "behavioural"-for-"visual" proposal); the concept note carries the fuller exposition, the sub-terms ("behaviourable," the 1964 Change Painting practice), the related arguments, and the cluster placement. Same `(Author)` disambiguation pattern as `Transparency` / `Transparency (Han)`. *Directly stated.*
- **Versus [[Jack Burnham|Burnham's]] [[Systems Esthetics]] (1968).** Burnham defines the new art by the autonomous art *object's* internal migration from [[Object and System|object to system]] in sculpture's formal history; Ascott (1966–67, the earlier text) defines it by the **behavioural loop** between artist, work, and participant. Both put process before object and both make [[Reciprocal Communication]]/feedback the criterion, but Ascott reaches it from the side of participation. The two are best read as the two poles of one moment, with Ascott the participatory pole. *Interpretive synthesis.*
- **Versus the psychological "behaviourism" of B. F. Skinner.** Ascott borrows the *behaviourist* register but explicitly pushes "beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering" — toward open, unpredictable, participant-completed play (and, in the same manifesto, toward "psychotechnology"), not stimulus-response conditioning. His "behaviour" is the spectator's free decision-making completion of the work, not engineered response. *Directly stated.*
- **Versus the dematerialization reading (Lippard) and the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension.** Where dematerialization tends toward the disappearance of the object, Ascott's definition explicitly retains the object while reassigning its function ("matrix and catalyst"); "software predominates over hardware" without the object's "end." *Directly stated.*
