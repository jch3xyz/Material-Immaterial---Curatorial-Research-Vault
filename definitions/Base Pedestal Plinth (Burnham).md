# Base, Pedestal, Plinth — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

In the first chapter of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Jack Burnham fixes a precise nomenclature for the substructure of sculpture, distinguishing three terms that were, before him, used loosely and interchangeably. He prefaces the definitions by noting the imprecision of the existing vocabulary:

> Nomenclature for the substructure of sculpture is ill-defined. Plinth, base, and pedestal are the most commonly used terms, and sculpture may employ any or all of these elements.

He then supplies the working distinctions:

> These terms may be defined as such: the base, the greatest mass upon which a sculpture rests, it refers to the support as a whole; the pedestal, a shaftlike form which elevates the sculpture; the plinth, a flat, planar support which separates the sculpture from the ground or from a pedestal.

So the three terms are nested and complementary rather than synonymous: the **base** is the support *as a whole* — the greatest mass; the **pedestal** is the *shaftlike elevating* member within or beneath that support; and the **plinth** is the *flat, planar* member that separates the sculpture either from the ground or from a pedestal. A single work "may employ any or all of these elements." (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Burnham introduces this nomenclature near the opening of "Sculpture's Vanishing Base," after observing how little sustained attention the substructure has historically received: "While practical and aesthetic questions of mounting sculpture have confronted artists for thousands of years, it is revealing how little attention the problem receives in historical texts and technical treatises... the base has remained a minor detail of the craft." (`Directly stated`) Defining the terms exactly is therefore a deliberate corrective: Burnham is elevating a neglected craft detail into a diagnostic instrument for the whole development of modern sculpture.

The definition is load-bearing for everything that follows. Having distinguished base / pedestal / plinth, Burnham reads each through his case studies — Degas's "slab of polished hardwood under the Dancer's feet that serves as both a plinth and a replica of a ballet practice floor"; Boccioni's striding figure "supported on rectangular impost blocks... These two pedestals"; Rodin's *Burghers of Calais*, where each figure was "created to stand on its own plinth" before being "cast... into a single eight-inch-thick plinth," and which the town council insisted "be mounted on a tall base." The precision of the vocabulary lets Burnham track exactly *which* element of the substructure each innovator manipulates, dissolves, or promotes. (`Directly stated`)

Conventionally, Burnham notes, students "were taught that a base should be unobtrusive while providing maximum enhancement for any sculpture set on top of it. Bases consequently tended to be strong geometric shapes with an architectonic simplicity separating them from the organic activity above." (`Directly stated`) This taught unobtrusiveness is the baseline whose negation — incorporation into the work, then outright disappearance — drives the chapter's argument about `The Vanishing Base`.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **A controlled vocabulary against ordinary loose usage.** Burnham's whole point is that "Plinth, base, and pedestal" are "commonly used" interchangeably and the nomenclature is "ill-defined." His definition is a stipulative correction: it reserves *base* for the support-as-a-whole / greatest mass, restricting *pedestal* and *plinth* to specific members (shaftlike-elevating; flat-planar-separating). Where everyday usage flattens the three into rough synonyms for "the thing a statue stands on," Burnham makes them a graded set with distinct functions. (`Directly stated`)

- **The nomenclature note vs. the function note.** This definition is deliberately narrower than the companion concept `The Sculpture Base`. That note treats the base's *functions* — rooting art to reality while letting it stand apart, conferring "presence," "an aura of distance and dignity," and a biological/orientational reaffirmation of human anatomy. This note isolates only the *terminology* Burnham supplies for the substructure's parts. The two should be read together: the function note explains what the support *does*, this note specifies what its members *are called* and how they differ. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
