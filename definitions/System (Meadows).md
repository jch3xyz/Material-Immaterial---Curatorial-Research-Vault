# System — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows defines "system" twice, once in each of the book's opening sections, and the two glosses are meant to be read together. The Introduction gives the behavioral definition:

> A system is a set of things—people, cells, molecules, or whatever—interconnected in such a way that they produce their own pattern of behavior over time.

`Directly stated`

Chapter 1 ("The Basics") gives the structural definition and then decomposes it:

> A system is an interconnected set of elements that is coherently organized in a way that achieves something. If you look at that definition closely for a minute, you can see that a system must consist of three kinds of things: elements, interconnections, and a function or purpose.

`Directly stated`

A system, for Meadows, is therefore not a mere aggregate. It is an *interconnected* set whose organization is *coherent* — organized "in a way that achieves something" — and which, by virtue of that organization, "produce[s] [its] own pattern of behavior over time." The two definitions name the same object from two sides: the structural side (**elements, interconnections, and a function or purpose**) and the behavioral side (a thing that generates its own dynamics rather than merely reacting to outside forces). `Directly stated`

The distinguishing test is subtraction. What is *not* a system is "a conglomeration without any particular interconnections or function": "Sand scattered on a road by happenstance is not, itself, a system. You can add sand or take away sand and you still have just sand on the road." By contrast, "Arbitrarily add or take away football players, or pieces of your digestive system, and you quickly no longer have the same system." `Directly stated`

## Context

The definition is the founding move of *Thinking in Systems*, the pivot on which the whole primer turns. Meadows introduces it to shift the reader's attention from events and external causes to internal structure. The Introduction's most heretical corollary follows directly from the behavioral definition: because a system produces "[its] own pattern of behavior over time," "The system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior! An outside event may unleash that behavior, but the same outside event applied to a different system is likely to produce a different result." This is the seed of the vault concept [[Structure Determines Behavior]]. `Directly stated`

The Chapter 1 definition supplies the anatomy the rest of the book anatomizes: the three "kinds of things" — [[Elements Interconnections and Purpose]] — are ranked by their power to change a system, with purpose the least visible and most decisive. A system's coherent organization lets it do more than sit still: it "may exhibit adaptive, dynamic, goal-seeking, self-preserving, and… evolutionary behavior" (the ellipsis marks an OCR margin-caption intrusion in the raw). Those behaviors are what later chapters trace to [[Feedback Loops]], to [[Self-Organization]], and to the system's capacity for self-stabilization (Meadows's native-discipline treatment of what the vault holds under [[Homeostasis]]). `Directly stated`

The definition is deliberately scale-free. "Systems can be embedded in systems, which are embedded in yet other systems" — a digestive system, a football team, a school, a city, a corporation, a national economy, a tree, a forest, the solar system, a galaxy. This nesting is why the same definition can ground both the local diagram and the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis the vault runs across its whole corpus: the "things" interconnected may be people, cells, or molecules, but what makes them a system is the immaterial pattern of interconnection and purpose, not the matter of the parts. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the concept [[A System]]** — that note holds the full working concept as it operates across the primer (the three parts, the behavior-over-time claim, the least-visible-most-powerful ranking of purpose, the nesting of systems within systems). This note isolates and pins the two verbatim *defining sentences*. They are companion notes, not duplicates (same pattern as `Information` / `Information (Han)`, and as the sibling `System (Burnham)` beside `Object and System`). `Directly stated`
- **vs. [[System (Burnham)]]** — Burnham's "system" is an art-historical and ontological category for sculpture, "an aggregate of components" whose parts are "mutually dependent" and which "may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life." Meadows's is the *native-discipline* definition from general systems thinking: an interconnected, coherently organized set with elements, interconnections, and a purpose that produces its own behavior over time. Both insist a system is more than a heap and both reach for life-like behavior (Burnham's "self-organization, growth… irritability"; Meadows's "adaptive, dynamic, goal-seeking, self-preserving… evolutionary behavior"), but Burnham defines a *destination for sculpture* while Meadows defines a *general analytic unit*. Where Burnham's stake is the object→system migration and the substitution of information for matter, Meadows's stake is leverage: understanding structure to change behavior. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Systems Theory]] (Hayles, cybernetics lineage)** — the vault's `Systems Theory` note tracks the interdisciplinary formal apparatus (the three waves of cybernetics, the observer, reflexivity). Meadows works in that lineage's applied, ecological, management-facing wing; her "system" is the plain-language operational definition that grounds the formal apparatus rather than the apparatus itself. This book GROUNDS the systems/cybernetics cluster in its native discipline. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Emergence]] and [[Self-Organization]]** — a system's capacity to "produce [its] own pattern of behavior" is the precondition for both, but Meadows keeps them as distinct downstream properties: emergence is the appearance of behavior at the level of the whole, self-organization the system's power to elaborate its own structure. The definition names the object; those concepts name things systems *do*. Cross-linked, not collapsed. `Strongly implied`
