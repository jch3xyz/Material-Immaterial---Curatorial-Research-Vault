# Inevitabilism — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Zuboff uses **inevitabilism** to name a full-blown *ideology* — not merely a mood or a forecast — that circulates among the architects of ubiquitous computing: the article of faith that everything will soon be connected, knowable, and actionable, and that this state of total information is the autonomous, unstoppable destiny of digital technology. She fixes the term in Chapter 7, "The Reality Business," where the rhetoric of necessity is so pervasive that it hardens into doctrine (`Directly stated`):

> The same “phase shift” that is understood by its architects as the universal antidote to uncertainty is anticipated with absolute certainty. Paradiso is not alone here. On the contrary, the rhetoric of inevitability is so “ubiquitous” that within the tech community it can be considered a full-blown ideology of inevitabilism.

Its creedal content — the precise object of faith — is ubiquity and total information (`Directly stated`):

> Among high-tech leaders, within the specialist literature, and among expert professionals there appears to be universal agreement on the idea that everything will be connected, knowable, and actionable in the near future: ubiquity and its consequences in total information are an article of faith.

So defined, inevitabilism has three marks: it is *universal* among the apparatus's builders ("universal agreement"); it is *creedal* rather than argued ("an article of faith," "rarely discussed or critically evaluated"); and it is *deterministic* — it "presents the new apparatus of ubiquity as the product of technological forces that operate beyond human agency and the choices of communities, an implacable movement that originates outside history" (`Directly stated`). Zuboff locates its geographic and spiritual center precisely: "Silicon Valley is the axis mundi of inevitabilism."

## Context

This definition note isolates the *Chapter 7 / Reality Business* sense of the term — inevitabilism as the proselytizing **faith of the apparatus of ubiquity** — beside the broader concept note [[Inevitabilism]], which gives Zuboff's full refutation (the contingency thesis: "technological inevitability does not exist" because technology is always an economic means patterned by Weber's "economic orientation").

In Chapter 7 the term does specific work. Having established that surveillance capitalism's "extraction imperative" now demands that sensing and rendition spread everywhere — into homes, bodies, and cities — Zuboff confronts the question of *why no one stops it*. Her answer is inevitabilism: the rhetoric that recasts a deliberate economic project as a law of nature. She marshals Google's own leaders as exemplary inevitabilists — Eric Schmidt and Jared Cohen open *The New Digital Age* with "Soon everyone on Earth will be connected" and invoke "Moore's Law" as "this new iron law of necessity" — and notes that when criticized they doubled down: "Many of the changes we discuss are inevitable. They're coming" (`Directly stated`). She situates this faith in a lineage of modern utopianism (even Marx's "equally inevitable" victory of the proletariat), arguing that "inevitability is the opposite of politics and history," and that "apostles of the apparatus routinely hijack historical metaphors that lend a veneer of gravitas to their claims" (`Directly stated`).

The polemical payoff is that the faith is *functional*: "Inevitabilism enshrines the apparatus of ubiquity as progress but conceals the realpolitik of surveillance capitalism at work behind the scenes." It "precludes choice and voluntary participation" and "leaves no room for human will as the author of the future" — which is exactly why the concept note's contingency thesis is the necessary rejoinder (`Directly stated`).

## Differences from Other Uses

This is the vault's first definition of *inevitabilism as ideology* — a named belief-system, with adherents ("passionate inevitabilists"), a creed (ubiquity + total information), and an axis mundi (Silicon Valley). It must not be collapsed into the vault's other determinisms, which it both resembles and indicts (`Interpretive synthesis`):

- **[[Dataism]] / [[Big Data]]** (Han / Harari) name a *worldview* that worships data flow and treats the data-fication of everything as the natural order. Inevitabilism is the *rhetorical-ideological form* that worldview takes when it asserts its own necessity; Zuboff reframes Dataism's air of scientific inevitability as an economic imperative dressed as a law of nature. The data faith supplies the content; inevitabilism supplies the claim of necessity.
- **Kurzweil's "law of accelerating returns"** is, on Zuboff's terms, inevitabilism stated as a *welcomed* law — the technological optimists of the vault (Kurzweil, Harari's Dataism) treat the trajectory toward total connection as both inevitable *and* good, which is precisely the position Zuboff names and refuses. Schmidt and Cohen's invocation of "Moore's Law" as an "iron law of necessity" is the same gesture.
- **Han's [[Digital Panopticon]]** explains *how* subjects come to participate (voluntary self-exposure, the exploitation of freedom); Zuboff's inevitabilism explains *why the apparatus goes unopposed* at the level of public belief. The two are complementary diagnoses — psycho-political consent within, ideological resignation without.

Zuboff's own usage is itself doubled: in Chapter 7 (this note) inevitabilism is the *faith* — the ubiquity creed; in the concept note it is the *fraud* — "a Trojan horse for powerful economic imperatives." The definition here captures the believed thing; the concept captures Zuboff's verdict on it.
