# The Singularity — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

In [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]'s account, the Singularity is [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s name for the eschatological threshold at which the cumulative organization of [[Information]] into ever-higher intelligence reaches its culmination — the moment when, in her gloss, "intelligence would merge with the universe and we would become divine." It is the predicted endpoint of the universe's evolution as Kurzweil narrates it, the instant after which, "eventually — inevitably — all the matter of the universe will become saturated with intelligence." O'Gieblyn fixes its definitional core in a single sentence:

> Kurzweil insists that this threshold, which he calls the Singularity, will happen by the year 2045.

This is not, for O'Gieblyn, a neutral technical forecast but the climactic event of a "grand narrative of history" that is "leading toward nothing short of total reenchantment" — a secular Apocalypse dated to a year.

## Context

O'Gieblyn introduces the Singularity in Chapter 3 ("Pattern") through her own conversion narrative: as a former fundamentalist who lost her faith and with it her belief that "history was an arc bending toward a point of final redemption," she discovers in Kurzweil's *The Age of Spiritual Machines* a structure she recognizes. The Singularity is the term that names the culminating "threshold" of the eschatology she reconstructs — the point toward which Kurzweil's "cumulative process of revelation" (the epoch of physics and chemistry, of biology, of brains) ascends. It is the technological hinge on which her central thesis turns: that [[Transhumanism]] is secularized Christian eschatology (see [[Transhumanism is secularized Christian eschatology]]). The Singularity occupies the structural position of the biblical Resurrection and the new age — the dead rising, the body made incorruptible, the earth remade as paradise, the human "saturated with intelligence" and rendered divine.

The definition is completed in Chapter 4, where O'Gieblyn supplies the genealogy that, for her, demystifies the term. Digging into the etymology of "transhuman," she traces it through Dante's coined *transumanar* to the French Jesuit [[Pierre Teilhard de Chardin]], who envisioned evolution converging through "the noosphere" toward "an ' 'etherised' universal consciousness'" that culminates in what he called the Omega Point — an "intelligence explosion" that would let humanity "break through the material framework of Time and Space" and "merge with the divine." O'Gieblyn then states the lineage directly:

> (The Omega Point, as many critics have noted, is an obvious precursor to Kurzweil's Singularity.)

This is the load-bearing move of the definition: the Singularity is not a novel product of "modern rationalism" but the latest form of a Christian prophecy passed "from one age to the next." Its technical preconditions are [[Patternism]] (the self as a transferable "pattern of information"), [[Mind Uploading]], and the [[Disembodied Information as the Soul|sacralization of disembodied information]]; its cosmology is [[The Computational Universe]], the cosmos read as "information becoming organized into increasingly complex forms of intelligence." O'Gieblyn ultimately turns the threshold against itself: the same Singularity that promises divinization carries a "darker irony," since "the only way we can survive the Singularity is to become machines ourselves" — a degradation, not an exaltation, of the human.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus the popular/engineering "technological singularity."** In transhumanist and Silicon Valley usage the Singularity is typically defined as the point of runaway, recursively self-improving machine intelligence — a forecast extrapolated from Moore's Law and presented, in [[Nick Bostrom]]'s framing, as "sober, disinterested" rationalism. O'Gieblyn does not dispute the technical vocabulary but redescribes the term's *meaning*: she reads it as an "eschatology," a "point of final redemption," and "academic prophecy" in [[Max Weber]]'s sense. Her definition is genealogical and critical where the standard one is predictive and self-assured.

- **Versus Teilhard's Omega Point.** O'Gieblyn treats the two as essentially the same vision in different idioms — the Omega Point is the Singularity's "obvious precursor." The difference she marks is one of disclosure: Teilhard openly held this to be "how the biblical Resurrection would take place," whereas the Singularity arrives stripped of its overt theology, since "most transhumanists are outspoken atheists." The Singularity is the Omega Point with its religious narrative disavowed.

- **Versus the philosophical "singularity" of singleness/uniqueness.** This note's term must not be confused with the unrelated sense of *singularity* (the singular, irreducible uniqueness of a person or thing) that appears elsewhere in the vault's Han cluster. O'Gieblyn's Singularity is Kurzweil's dated eschatological threshold, hence the disambiguating `(O'Gieblyn)` title.

- **Versus [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s posthuman.** O'Gieblyn relies on Hayles to diagnose the disembodiment the Singularity requires — "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form" — but where Hayles offers [[Posthumanism]] as critical *diagnosis*, the Singularity is *prophecy*: a future to be believed in and pursued, not an ideology to be unmasked.
