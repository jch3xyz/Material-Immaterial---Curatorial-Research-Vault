# The Fourth Fictional Commodity — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

The term is an extension Zuboff builds on Karl Polanyi's framework rather than a phrase Polanyi himself used. Reading surveillance capitalism "Using Karl Polanyi's lens," she names a *new* commodity that completes the series Polanyi opened. She states it directly (*Directly stated*):

> Using Karl Polanyi's lens, we see that surveillance capitalism annexes human experience to the market dynamic so that it is reborn as behavior: the fourth "fictional commodity." Polanyi's first three fictional commodities—land, labor, and money—were subjected to law. Although these laws have been imperfect, the institutions of labor law, environmental law, and banking law are regulatory frameworks intended to defend society (and nature, life, and exchange) from the worst excesses of raw capitalism's destructive power. Surveillance capitalism's expropriation of human experience has faced no such impediments.

The definition has three load-bearing parts (*Directly stated*):

1. **The fourth item in Polanyi's series.** Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) argued that capitalism converts three things into commodities that were never produced for sale — land, labor, and money — and called them "fictitious" (in Zuboff's spelling, "fictional") commodities. Zuboff adds a fourth: human experience.
2. **The mechanism: annexation and rebirth.** Human experience is "annexe[d] … to the market dynamic," and in that annexation it "is reborn as behavior." The thing sold is not experience itself but experience rendered, abstracted, and traded as behavioral data — the substance the vault elsewhere names [[Behavioral Surplus]] (*Strongly implied*).
3. **The decisive asymmetry: no law.** The first three fictional commodities were eventually "subjected to law" — labor law, environmental law, banking law — the regulatory residue of a societal counter-movement. The fourth has "faced no such impediments." This absence is the definition's sharpest claim: what distinguishes the fourth commodity is not its nature but its lawlessness (*Directly stated*).

## Context

Zuboff introduces the fourth fictional commodity in Chapter 18 ("A Coup from Above"), in the passage where she characterizes surveillance capitalism as "a boundary-less form that ignores older distinctions between market and society, market and world, or market and person." It is "a profit-seeking form in which production is subordinated to extraction," whose actors "unilaterally claim control over human, societal, and political territories extending far beyond the conventional institutional terrain of the private firm or the market" (*Directly stated*).

The Polanyian framing does specific argumentative work here. It lets Zuboff measure surveillance capitalism against the historical pattern by which earlier market excesses were eventually checked. Land, labor, and money each provoked a societal counter-pressure — Polanyi's [[The Double Movement]] — that produced the laws and institutions defending "nature, life, and exchange." The fourth commodity has so far provoked no comparable counter-movement; its "expropriation of human experience has faced no such impediments." The definition therefore points to a structural gap: a fictional commodity exists, but the second half of Polanyi's double movement — the protective response — has not yet materialized for it (*Strongly implied*).

This is why the concept sits at the heart of the chapter's titular argument. The "coup from above" — a "market-driven coup," a *coup de gens* rather than a *coup d'état* — succeeds precisely because human experience has been quietly converted into a tradable thing without the legal architecture that civilized the first three commodities. Zuboff's earlier point that "relatively little destruction is creative, especially in the absence of a robust double movement" finds its sharpest application here: the fourth fictional commodity is destructive *because* it is unimpeded (*Interpretive synthesis*).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Polanyi (origin) vs. Zuboff (extension).** "Fictitious commodities" is Polanyi's category, naming things produced for purposes other than sale (land = nature, labor = human life, money = a token of purchasing power). Zuboff keeps the category and adds a member Polanyi never named — human experience reborn as behavior — and shifts the spelling to "fictional." Her contribution is the fourth term and, crucially, the observation that it alone escaped subjection to law (*Directly stated*).
- **Distinct from, but built on, [[Behavioral Surplus]].** Behavioral surplus is the *raw material* — the behavioral data exhausted beyond what is needed to improve a service. The fourth fictional commodity is the *categorical claim about its market status*: that this material has been installed as a tradable commodity on the model of land, labor, and money. The first is an extraction mechanism; the second is a political-economic diagnosis of what that extraction has produced (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Companion to [[The Double Movement]].** The two definitions are halves of one Polanyian argument. The double movement names the historical counter-pressure that subjected the first three commodities to law; the fourth fictional commodity names the commodity for which that counter-pressure has not yet arisen. Read together, they frame surveillance capitalism as the exploitation of a missing double movement (*Strongly implied*).
- **Against the commodification of labor (Marx) and immaterial labour (Han).** Earlier vault notes treat labor's commodification (Marx) and the rise of [[Immaterial Labour]] (Han). Zuboff's fourth commodity is adjacent but not identical: it is not labor — paid or immaterial — that is sold, but lived experience involuntarily rendered as behavior, "everything about you and me that is personal," extracted whether or not one is working (*Interpretive synthesis*).
