# The Law of Accelerating Returns — Ray Kurzweil

## Author's Definition

Kurzweil names the law of accelerating returns (LOAR) as the engine that drives technology toward the Singularity. His core definition appears at the opening of Chapter 2:

> what I call the law of accelerating returns, which describes the acceleration of the pace of and the exponential growth of the products of an evolutionary process.

`Directly stated`

In Chapter 1 he glosses the same law more compactly, foregrounding its claim that technology is a *continuation* of biology rather than a break from it:

> the law of accelerating returns (the inherent acceleration of the rate of evolution, with technological evolution as a continuation of biological evolution)

`Directly stated`

The decisive feature of LOAR, for Kurzweil, is not merely that the products of evolution grow exponentially but that the growth is itself accelerating — what he calls **double exponential** growth:

> we often find not just simple exponential growth, but "double" exponential growth, meaning that the rate of exponential growth (that is, the exponent) is itself growing exponentially

`Directly stated`

He locates the mechanism of this second-order acceleration in a positive-feedback loop between cost-effectiveness and reinvestment:

> the more effective a particular evolutionary process becomes—for example, the higher the capacity and cost-effectiveness that computation attains—the greater the amount of resources that are deployed toward the further progress of that process. This results in a second level of exponential growth; that is, the rate of exponential growth—the exponent—itself grows exponentially.

`Directly stated`

## Context

LOAR is the central theoretical claim of *The Singularity Is Near* — the law from which Kurzweil derives the Singularity as an "inexorable result." He introduces it in order to argue that the acceleration of technology is not contingent or coincidental but the structural signature of an evolutionary process, and that this acceleration "extends substantially beyond the predictions made by what has become known as Moore's Law." Kurzweil treats Moore's Law as only the fifth in a series of computing paradigms, each an S-curve; LOAR is the higher-order trend that spans and supersedes any individual paradigm's eventual limit. `Directly stated`

The law is grounded in his account of evolution as a builder of order: "A primary reason that evolution—of life-forms or of technology—speeds up is that it builds on its own increasing order, with ever more sophisticated means of recording and manipulating information." Crucially, Kurzweil insists evolution is not a closed system and so does not contradict the second law of thermodynamics — it "draws upon the chaos in the larger system in which it takes place for its options for diversity." `Directly stated`

The polemical thrust of LOAR is methodological: it opposes what Kurzweil calls the "intuitive linear" view of history to the "historical exponential" view, arguing that "people tend to overestimate what can be achieved in the short term ... but underestimate what can be achieved in the long term (because exponential growth is ignored)." `Directly stated`

## Differences from Other Uses

**Versus Han's Acceleration.** The `(Kurzweil)` qualifier on this definition is mandatory because the bare concept [[Acceleration]] in the vault is Byung-Chul Han's — and the two are near-exact inversions. For Han, acceleration is a pathology: the loss of narrative tension, the additive "bad infinity" of the same, a symptom of a society that can no longer conclude or tarry. For Kurzweil, the very same acceleration is a *law* of evolution to be celebrated — a "marvelous quantitative example of the exponentially growing returns from an evolutionary process" that promises transcendence of biological limits. LOAR is therefore the **celebratory antonym** of Han's critique: where Han diagnoses acceleration as the engine of the [[Hell of the Same]], Kurzweil reads the same exponential curve as the trajectory toward the Singularity. `Interpretive synthesis`

**Versus mere exponential / Moore's Law.** LOAR is broader and deeper than simple exponential growth. Where Moore's Law "narrowly refers to the number of transistors on an integrated circuit," LOAR claims a *double* exponential — the exponent itself rising — and asserts that this trend "applies to all of technology, indeed to any evolutionary process," not just to computation. `Directly stated`

**Versus a closed-system / entropic reading.** Kurzweil explicitly distinguishes LOAR from any law that would imply technological progress is constrained by entropy: "the law of accelerating returns pertains to evolution, which is not a closed system." This separates his use from thermodynamic pessimism and from theories that treat order as inevitably decaying. `Directly stated`
