# Functionalism / Multiple Realizability — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

O'Gieblyn defines functionalism by where it locates consciousness — not in the brain's stuff but in its structure (`Directly stated`):

> This is essentially a functionalist account of the mind—the popular view that consciousness does not reside in the physical material of the brain but rather in its organization and causal relations.

The corollary, which she names in the following chapter, is *multiple realizability* — the thesis that because mind is organization rather than matter, it can be instantiated in any medium whatsoever (`Directly stated`):

> The relationship between the mind and the body became even more tenuous in the 1960s with the rise of functionalist theories of consciousness, which insist that mental states can be "multiply realizable," instantiated by any medium, biological or mechanical.

Taken together these supply the substrate-independence thesis: if a mental state is constituted by the *role* it plays in a causal web rather than by the physical material that plays it, then the same state can be realized by neurons, silicon, or anything else that reproduces the organization. This is the philosophy-of-mind machinery that licenses [[Mind Uploading|mind-uploading]] — the mind, being pattern rather than meat, can in principle be transferred to "any medium, biological or mechanical."

## Context

O'Gieblyn introduces functionalism as the conceptual hinge of her two "Pattern" chapters, the chapters that ask how the computer metaphor "has returned to us these ancient religious ideas about physical transcendence and the disembodied spirit." Functionalism is the answer: it is the doctrine that lets [[Mind as Machine|the mind-as-machine figure]] be taken literally, the formal premise underneath [[Ray Kurzweil|Kurzweil]]'s self-described [[Patternism|patternism]]. When she calls patternism "essentially a functionalist account of the mind," she is naming the academic respectability that patternism borrows: Kurzweil's claim that consciousness is "the distinctive algorithm that we think of as our identity" is functionalism dressed in an organic metaphor.

She situates the thesis historically, drawing on [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*. The ground was prepared at the Macy Conferences, where, as Hayles argues, "information came to take precedence over materiality" and came to seem "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates" — [[Claude Shannon|Shannon]]'s [[Information|information theory]] having first "stripped information of meaning." Functionalism, arriving "in the 1960s," completes the abstraction: having decoupled information from meaning and then from matter, it now decouples *mind* from matter, making information "the materialist's substitute for the soul." This is why O'Gieblyn treats functionalism not as a neutral technical position but as a load-bearing part of a [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]] narrative — the apparatus by which a [[Disenchantment|disenchanted]], meaning-stripped materialism smuggles back in the promise of an immortal, [[Disembodied Information as the Soul|disembodied self]].

Crucially, O'Gieblyn marks where transhumanism *exceeds* the philosophy. Functionalism's classical statement is often compared to Aristotle, who held "that identity resided in the body's organization rather than its matter." But she stresses the divergence: "proponents of mind-uploading take this notion of identity much further than Aristotle himself did, arguing that the patterns of our minds are not only abstract and irreducible but potentially immortal as well." Aristotle still maintained "that a person who died could not be re-created or reincarnated as the same person." The leap from multiple realizability to *immortality* is patternism's, not functionalism's — and it is precisely the leap the "continuity of identity" objections (the philosopher Susan Schneider's "zombie with no subjective experience") are designed to challenge.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. [[Patternism]] (Kurzweil).** Functionalism is the general philosophical thesis (mind is organization, hence multiply realizable); patternism is Kurzweil's specific, immortalist *application* of it. O'Gieblyn's own gloss — patternism is "essentially a functionalist account of the mind" — fixes the relation: patternism presupposes functionalism but adds the substrate-transfer-into-permanence move that functionalism alone does not entail. **Do not collapse the two.**
- **vs. Aristotle (the classical precedent).** O'Gieblyn explicitly notes that "this view of the mind is often compared to Aristotle's philosophy," who located identity in the body's organization rather than its matter. But she draws the boundary: Aristotle denied that the dead could be re-created as the same person, whereas mind-uploaders extend multiple realizability into a claim of immortality "much further than Aristotle himself did" (`Directly stated`).
- **vs. [[N. Katherine Hayles]].** Hayles supplies O'Gieblyn the *genealogy* of substrate-independence — the Macy-Conference story in which "information came to take precedence over materiality" — but as critique, not endorsement. For Hayles the dream that information "can flow unchanged between different material substrates" is a contingent, ideological erasure of [[Embodiment|embodiment]]; O'Gieblyn cites her precisely to mark functionalism's blind spot, the point at which [[The Materiality of Informatics|materiality]] is illegitimately discarded.
- **vs. [[Embodiment]] (the counter-thesis).** Functionalism's central claim — that the substrate is incidental, the organization everything — is exactly what embodiment denies. Where functionalism says mind is "instantiated by any medium, biological or mechanical," the embodiment thesis holds there is no disembodiment, only different embodiment: the medium is constitutive, not interchangeable.
