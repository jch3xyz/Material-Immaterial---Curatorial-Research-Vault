# Subsculpture — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

In the opening section "A Continuum of Images" of Chapter 5 ("Sculpture and Automata"), Burnham coins and pins the term in a single sentence:

> The term subsculpture refers here to types of images aesthetically ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts.

`Directly stated`

The definition fixes the category by two negatives: it is *aesthetically ranked below* sculpture, and it is *excluded from* the fine arts. The very next sentences make the boundary historical and porous rather than fixed: "Already some of this subsculpture has crossed the boundary line of scholarship into the category of major art. In time the psychological motives behind the drive to create human images will assume greater importance than the supposed hierarchical levels of beauty which separate those images into minor and major art." `Directly stated`

The membership of the category is enumerated just before the definition, framed as a "continuum of images" and a "vast substratum":

> Fetishes, idols, amulets, funeral images, dolls, waxworks, manikins, puppets, and, most dramatically, automata, all play their part in the vast substratum of figures which historians used to rank far below sculpture as a fine art.

`Directly stated`

So subsculpture is defined less by *form* than by *standing* and *function* — many of its artifacts are "rooted, not in any Western concept of beauty, but in some very practical purpose" (magical, religious, votive, substitutive). `Directly stated`

## Context

Burnham introduces *subsculpture* to license the chapter's central move: following "one particular branch of subsculpture: automata" — the "seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men" — across the whole Western history that art history had refused to treat as art. The term is a polemical instrument. By naming the excluded field, Burnham can argue that "a 'history of human images,' free of what the art historian Wilenski refers to as 'the Greek prejudice,' has yet to be written," and that the hierarchy separating "minor and major art" is a contingent "supposed" ranking, not a natural law. `Directly stated`

The category does double duty in the book's argument. First, its boundary is shown to move: the decisive event Burnham narrates is subsculpture's migration *into* fine art around 1910 — "The theme of the pseudo-man, as manikin or robot, became a central issue of art after 1910" — culminating in his verdict that "The doll, the manikin, and the robot precariously began to find their place in the fine arts" (Archipenko, Picabia, Duchamp, de Chirico, Schlemmer, Bellmer). `Directly stated` Second, the branch he singles out, automata, carries the book's deeper thesis: their "progressive mechanization" reveals "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge," "a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically." `Directly stated` This is the hinge by which subsculpture feeds Burnham's larger object-to-system story: a manikin or automaton is prized not as a static contemplative form but as a working, animated, behaving thing, so its rise destabilizes the autonomous sculptural object. `Interpretive synthesis`
