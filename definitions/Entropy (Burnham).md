# Entropy — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

Burnham borrows entropy from the physicists, who, he notes, have used the term "since the mid-nineteenth century" to "express the work potential in a measurable system." His own gloss is a dissipation factor measuring disorder:

> Entropy is a curiously negative term, a kind of dissipation factor which expresses the level of disorganiza tion to be found in a system.

He extends the definition along two axes — work potential and probability of existence:

> The more disorganized or chaotic the system, the less work it can do, the higher its level of entropy will be. Entropy also measures the probability that a system exists; the more structured the system, the less probable its existence.

*Directly stated.* (The OCR break "disorganiza tion" is preserved verbatim from the raw chapter.)

## Context

Entropy enters *Beyond Modern Sculpture*'s Introduction as a deliberately cooler, "less emotionally charged" alternative to Jacques Ellul's pessimistic account of *technique* as a "pernicious disease." Where Ellul treats the spread of artificial systems as a terminal sickness, Burnham reframes the same phenomenon thermodynamically: technology is not simply destruction but a local *ordering* of energy against a rising background of disorder. *Strongly implied* (Burnham introduces entropy explicitly as "another way to look at the inroads created by artificial systems").

This sets up the book's pivotal move from **object** to **system**. Burnham reads sculpture's drift away from the inert, palpable object toward the lifelike, energy-controlled system as one instance of a universal trend — the building of improbable, highly structured "pockets" within an entropic universe. He quotes a cyberneticist's definition to make the point:

> such a trend has led one cyberneticist to define technology as the development of pockets of decreasing entropy on a planet where the general level of entropy is rising.

*Directly stated.* The Earth, "running downhill, out of energy," becomes "a more probable system," while technology "reorganized energy stores into compact usable sources so that materials could be ordered into more improbable and structured forms" (*Directly stated*).

He then borrows the economist Kenneth Boulding's sculptor analogy — the statue is "much less probable than that of the stone," but its order "has been bought at the cost of disorganization in the chips" — to seat the carved object itself on a gradient of "systems of decreasing entropy" running from "a pail of sand, a spring-wound machine, a plant, and a human being." For Burnham this licenses the book's thesis: sculpture in a technological society is "a tiny microcosm of the entire socio-technical-biological evolution," each stage lowering "its entropy rate at the expense of the general environment." *Interpretive synthesis* (the linkage of entropy to the object→system arc is assembled across the closing pages of the Introduction).

The term thus does load-bearing work: it gives Burnham a value-neutral, physicist's vocabulary for the negentropic ambition that culminates in his final chapter on Cyborg Art, where the "kybernetes" (steersman) replaces the "elan vital" as art's "new and even more effective prime mover." See [[Cybernetics]].

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. Hayles's information-theoretic frame.** In the vault, entropy is otherwise approached through cybernetics and information theory, where (after Shannon) entropy is bound up with the measure of [[Information]] and the [[Pattern and Randomness]] dialectic — disorder as *unpredictability*, i.e. message content. Burnham's emphasis is older and more thermodynamic: entropy as a *dissipation factor* / work potential and as a *probability of a system's existence*, with technology as local negentropy. The two senses are cognate (both descend from physics into [[Cybernetics]] and [[Systems Theory]]) but Burnham foregrounds the energetic, cosmological reading rather than the signal-theoretic one. *Interpretive synthesis.*

- **vs. Ellul's *technique*.** Burnham explicitly offers entropy as the "less emotionally charged" counter-frame to Ellul's moralized diagnosis of technique as disease — the same expansion of artificial systems, redescribed as ordering rather than corruption. *Directly stated.*
