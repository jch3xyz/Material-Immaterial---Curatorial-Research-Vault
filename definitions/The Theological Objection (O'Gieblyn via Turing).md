# The Theological Objection — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

The **theological objection** is [[Alan Turing|Turing]]'s own name for the religious argument against machine intelligence: that thinking requires an immortal soul, which God grants to humans alone, so no machine (and no animal) can think. [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] reproduces Turing's compressed statement of it from his 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence":

> God has given an immortal soul to every man and woman, but not to any other animal or to machines. Hence no animal or machine can think.

O'Gieblyn's interest is not in the objection as Turing states it but in the *failure of his reply*. Turing, "a stark materialist" who "confessed that he was 'unable to accept any part of this,'" nonetheless answered the objection on its own terms — by arguing that an omnipotent God could grant a soul to a machine as readily as to an elephant, so that the engineers building thinking machines would be merely "instruments of His will providing mansions for the souls that He creates." O'Gieblyn's definitional move is to read this rebuttal as a *self-refutation*: the term names not just the religious argument but the trap it sets for any materialist who tries to dispel it. *(Directly stated)*

## Context

The objection appears in Chapter 6 ("Network"), where O'Gieblyn is tracing how "this fear of black magic has haunted modern robotics from its inception" — the suspicion that "to summon life from inert matter" is "paganism, witchcraft, or idolatry." Turing addressing the theological objection in a landmark engineering paper is her evidence that the fear "was still strong enough by the middle of the last century" to demand an answer from the discipline's founders. *(Directly stated)*

The chapter's argumentative payload is O'Gieblyn's verdict on Turing's reply: **"As arguments go, this is remarkably clumsy."** Her charge is that the "mansions for the souls" defense concedes the very thing the objection asserted. By imagining the machine as a *body waiting to be inspirited* — a mansion built for a soul that God, not the engineer, supplies — Turing implicitly grants that intelligence requires "something else — a soul, consciousness — that was not accounted for in Turing's mechanistic understanding of personhood." A materialism that has to outsource the decisive ingredient to divine intervention has admitted it cannot manufacture mind from matter alone. As O'Gieblyn puts it, "all Turing's response proves is what the theological objection held in the first place: that humans are incapable of creating intelligent life." *(Directly stated)*

The deeper irony, which connects this note to the book's central thesis, is that Turing did not actually believe in the mansions or the souls that fill them. "Turing and his colleagues, after all, were not creating 'mansions' to be supernaturally inhabited by an immortal soul; they believed their machines amounted to life itself." So the reply is doubly hollow: it borrows a theological scaffold the author rejects, and in borrowing it concedes that mechanism alone is insufficient. This is the same crypto-theological pattern O'Gieblyn diagnoses across the book — the [[Mind as Machine|computer metaphor]] that "was expressly designed to avoid the notion of a metaphysical soul" yet keeps smuggling the soul back in (see [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]], and the argument [[The computer metaphor reintroduces the disembodied soul it was built to abolish]]). The theological objection is the moment where that smuggling becomes visible at the very origin of AI. *(Strongly implied)*

It is also, for O'Gieblyn, an instance of the wider problem she presses against [[Emergence|emergentism]]: that "digital technologies were built on the assumption that consciousness played no role in the process of intelligence," so a machine "designed specifically to elide the notion of the conscious subject" cannot then be expected to "develop an interior life." Turing's reply tacitly reintroduces the conscious ingredient — under the name *soul* — that his mechanism had bracketed out. The objection thus belongs to the same family as the [[The Mind-Body Problem|mind-body problem]] and the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem of consciousness]]: each marks the place where a thoroughgoing materialism finds it has nothing to say about the one thing the question was about. *(Interpretive synthesis)*

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus Turing's own use.** For Turing the "theological objection" is one item on a checklist of nine objections to be cleared away en route to the imitation game; he treats it as a confusion he can dissolve by appeal to God's omnipotence and move past. O'Gieblyn inverts the valence: the objection is not refuted by Turing's reply but *vindicated* by it. What Turing files under "objections answered," she files under "concessions made." The term, in her hands, names the rebuttal's backfire, not the religious premise. *(Directly stated)*

- **Versus the standard "religious objection to AI."** In popular framing the theological objection is simply the claim that machines lack souls — a piece of pre-modern resistance that secular science has outgrown. O'Gieblyn refuses the progress narrative: the objection is interesting precisely because the *materialist* answer to it reinstates the soul as an unpaid debt. The argument is not religion-versus-science but a materialism that cannot fund its own promissory note without theological credit.

- **Versus Hayles's [[Turing Test (Hayles)|Turing test]].** [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] reads Turing's imitation game as the scene where embodiment is *erased* — intelligence redefined as "the formal manipulation of symbols" so that the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal subject]] slides into the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]. O'Gieblyn's theological objection is the complementary scene a few pages over in the same 1950 paper: where Hayles sees the body deleted, O'Gieblyn sees the *soul* readmitted. The two readings are not rivals but the two faces of the same disavowal — the body is dropped and the soul is smuggled back as the missing ingredient. *(Interpretive synthesis)*

- **Versus the golem tradition in the same chapter.** O'Gieblyn sets Turing beside the Kabbalistic [[Imago Dei|imago dei]] logic of the golem-makers, for whom animating clay was an explicit "repetition of God's act of creating us." The golem-builders openly avow the theology Turing only borrows for the length of one rebuttal. The contrast sharpens her point: the modern roboticist's predicament is to need the golem's theology while disowning it. *(Strongly implied)*
