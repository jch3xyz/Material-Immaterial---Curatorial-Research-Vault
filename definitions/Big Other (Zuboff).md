# Big Other — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Big Other is Zuboff's name for the **apparatus** of surveillance capitalism: a ubiquitous, sensate, networked, computational infrastructure that both *knows* (renders, monitors, computes) and *does* (modifies) human behavior. She introduces the term twice, defining it in successively sharper form.

In the book's overview (Chapter 1):

> its expression in a ubiquitous sensate, networked, computational infrastructure that I call Big Other

And in the keystone definitional passage (Chapter 13):

> Surveillance capitalism is the puppet master that imposes its will through the medium of the ubiquitous digital apparatus. I now name the apparatus Big Other: it is the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior. Big Other combines these functions of knowing and doing to achieve a pervasive and unprecedented means of behavioral modification.

The definition is built on a deliberate distinction between the **will** and the **instrument**: surveillance capital is the *puppet master*; Big Other is the *puppet*. The apparatus does not author the aims it serves — it is "the medium of the ubiquitous digital apparatus" through which capital "imposes its will." `Directly stated`.

## Context

Chapter 13 is the theoretical keystone of the book's Part III. Having established the economic *logic* of surveillance capitalism in earlier chapters, Zuboff here names the *form of power* it produces — `Instrumentarian Power` — and the *apparatus* through which that power operates — Big Other. The two coinages are paired: "Surveillance capitalism's economic logic is directed through Big Other's vast capabilities to produce instrumentarian power, replacing the engineering of souls with the engineering of behavior" (`Directly stated`).

Big Other is staged explicitly as the **counter-figure to Orwell's Big Brother**. Where Big Brother names a regime of terror, intimacy, and relationship — domination of the soul — Big Other names a regime of indifference and non-relationship: it does not care who we are, only that we can be observed and instrumentalized. Zuboff makes the contrast direct: "There is no brother here of any kind, big or little, evil or good; there are no family ties, however grim. There is no relationship between Big Other and its otherized objects." From Big Other's vantage, "we are strictly Other-Ones: organisms that behave" — the source of the name. `Directly stated`.

The apparatus is also positioned as a **Marxian inversion**: "Power was once identified with the ownership of the means of production, but it is now identified with ownership of the means of behavioral modification that is Big Other" (`Directly stated`). Big Other is thus both a material infrastructure (the "millions, billions, and trillions of sensate, actuating, computational eyes and ears") and the new locus of power, the thing whose ownership now defines domination.

A further feature of Big Other in Zuboff's account is its tendency to **disappear**: she rereads Eric Schmidt's "The internet will disappear" as "The internet will disappear into Big Other," aligning the apparatus with ubiquitous-computing visions in which the instrument vanishes into the background of all things (`Directly stated`). This is the same pervasive, embedded, disappearing computation that Kurzweil celebrates and that Han, from the subject's side, diagnoses as the `Digital Panopticon`.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. Orwell's Big Brother / Han's `Friendly Big Brother`** — Big Other is Zuboff's deliberate anti–Big Brother. Han's symbol `Friendly Big Brother` keeps the *figure* of the watching Brother while softening his face (surveillance that pleases rather than represses); Zuboff dissolves the relationship entirely — there is no brother at all, only indifferent Otherness. The two are in productive dialogue: Han retains a relationship (a friendly Brother), Zuboff insists on radical *non-*relationship. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **vs. `Digital Panopticon` (Han)** — Han's digital panopticon theorizes ubiquitous, frictionless, voluntary-feeling surveillance from the *subject's* side (self-exposure, auto-exploitation). Big Other is the *apparatus*-side, capital-driven counterpart: the same pervasive surveillance, but theorized through the economic logic and *ownership* behind it. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **vs. `The Panopticon` (Bentham/Foucault)** — Bentham's panopticon disciplines through the *internalized possibility* of being seen by a central watcher. Big Other does not need the subject to internalize a gaze or to conform at all: "Our conformity is irrelevant to instrumentarianism's success." It modifies behavior directly through `Radical Indifference`, not through disciplinary self-watching. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **vs. `Big Data` (Han + Harari) / `Dataism`** — Big Data and Dataism name the *worldview* and the *flows of data*; Big Other names the *material apparatus* that produces and acts on them. Big Other is the infrastructure in which Dataism's "substitution of certainty for society" is operationalized. `Interpretive synthesis`.
- **The "Big" prefix is not a family resemblance** — Big Other is a distinct concept, not a variant of `Big Data`, `Friendly Big Brother`, or `Digital Panopticon`. It is cross-linked to all three but names something none of them does: the sensate computational *infrastructure* itself. `Directly stated`.
