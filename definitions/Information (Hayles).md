# Information — N. Katherine Hayles

## Author's Definition

The definition [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] works *from* — and works *against* — is the one she inherits from Claude Shannon and [[Norbert Wiener]], the founding formula of information theory:

> Shannon’s theory defines information as a probability function with no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning. Itisa pattern, not a presence.

Information, in this technical sense, is not a thing but a *measure*: a probability function quantifying the choice of one message from a set of possible messages. It has no dimensions and no material existence of its own — a signal acquires "material form" only when it is encoded into a medium — and, crucially, no necessary connection with meaning. Hayles underlines the second feature as deliberate:

> Note that the theory is formulated entirely without reference to what information means. Only the probabilities of message elements enter into the equations.

This is the definition the rest of *[[How We Became Posthuman]]* contests. Hayles accepts it as a precise piece of engineering and rejects its elevation into a worldview. (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Hayles introduces the Shannon/Wiener definition to locate the historical moment at which, in her phrase, "information lost its body" — when information came to be theorized as an entity conceptually distinct from, and "more essential, more important, and more fundamental than," materiality. She dates the move to 1948 and Shannon's mathematical theory of communication, and she is careful to explain *why* meaning was excised. Shannon and Wiener "wanted information to have a stable value as it moved from one context to another"; because context affects meaning, tying information to meaning would force its value to change with every new context. To secure a context-independent quantity, "they divorced it from meaning." (`Directly stated`)

The decisive interpretive claim is that this divorce was a *local engineering convenience* that was subsequently universalized into an ontology. Out of its proper context, the definition "allowed information to be conceptualized as if it were an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates" — the premise that licenses fantasies like downloading a mind into a computer. Hayles names the slide explicitly:

> Thus, a simplification necessitated by engineering considerations becomes an ideology in which a reified concept of information is treated as if it were fully commensurate with the complexities of human thought.

She stresses that Shannon himself "was meticulously careful," repeatedly insisting that the theory concerned "only the efficient transmission of messages through communication channels, not what those messages mean." The reification is therefore not Shannon's error but a cultural appropriation of his theory. This definition thus functions in Hayles's book as the technical hinge of the [[The Materiality of Informatics|materiality-of-informatics]] argument: by exposing information-as-pattern as a constructed, costed abstraction rather than a self-evident fact, she reopens the question of [[Embodiment|embodiment]] that the abstraction had foreclosed. The conceptual development of this critique lives in the Hayles sections of [[Information]] and in [[The Body as Data]]; this note records the technical definition itself. (`Directly stated` for the engineering rationale; `Strongly implied` for the structural role in the book.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. [[Information (Han)|Information (Han)]].** [[Byung-Chul Han|Han]] and Hayles both treat "information" critically and both fasten on its severance from meaning, but the two senses are genuinely distinct and coexist. For Han, *information* is a **condition of language** under the [[The Transparency Society|transparency society]] — "positivized, operationalized language" defined by its lack of [[Negativity|negativity]], a cultural-linguistic diagnosis. For Hayles, *information* is in the first instance a **mathematical quantity** with a precise technical pedigree (a probability function), which only *becomes* an ideology when reified beyond its engineering home. Han defines information by what it lacks (negativity, transformative power); Hayles defines it by what its founders *built out* (dimension, materiality, meaning) for calculational stability. The convergence is the shared verdict that information divorced from meaning flattens thought; the divergence is that Hayles's term is anchored in Shannon's equations and Han's in a phenomenology of language. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **vs. the everyday and the popular-futurist sense.** In ordinary usage "information" means meaningful content — what one learns. The Shannon definition that Hayles foregrounds is counterintuitive precisely because it *excludes* meaning: the same message ("It's raining") carries different meaning but, by design, the same information value across contexts. The popular-futurist sense Hayles criticizes (e.g. "the overthrow of matter") inflates the technical quantity into a metaphysics in which the immaterial pattern is the true reality and matter a residue. (`Directly stated`)
- **vs. the Wiener variant within cybernetics.** Hayles notes that within [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] itself the definition was unstable: where Shannon (and later choices at the Macy Conferences) aligned information *with* entropy, Wiener at times aligned it with *negentropy* — information as the opposite of disorder. Hayles reads this disagreement as evidence that "information" was a constructed entity contested in the making, not a found fact. (`Strongly implied`)
