# Experience — Yuval Noah Harari

## Author's Definition

For Harari, *experience* is the elementary unit of value in the humanist worldview — the thing humanism puts in the place once occupied by God's commandments and the laws of nature. He defines it as a strictly subjective, internally compounded phenomenon. In *Homo Deus*, in the chapter on the humanist revolution, he writes:

> an experience is a subjective phenomenon that includes three main ingredients: sensations, emotions and thoughts. At any particular moment my experience comprises everything I sense (heat, pleasure, tension, etc.), every emotion I feel (love, fear, anger, etc.) and whatever thoughts arise in my mind.

Experience is explicitly *not* empirical data: "An experience is not made of atoms, molecules, proteins or numbers" [Directly stated]. It is the inner, first-person register — the only register, on the humanist account, from which meaning, ethics, politics and aesthetics can be drawn.

Crucially, experience never stands alone in Harari's account. It is one half of a paired formula for ethical knowledge — *Knowledge = Experiences × Sensitivity* — and is inert without its partner. He defines the second term immediately after:

> And what is 'sensitivity'? It means two things. Firstly, paying attention to my sensations, emotions and thoughts. Secondly, allowing these sensations, emotions and thoughts to influence me.

Sensitivity is thus *attention plus permeability*: noticing one's sensations, emotions and thoughts, and letting them change one's views, behaviour and personality. Harari adds that the two terms are mutually constitutive — "Experiences and sensitivity build up one another in a never-ending cycle. I cannot experience anything if I have no sensitivity, and I cannot develop sensitivity unless I undergo a variety of experiences." Sensitivity is "a practical skill" honed only by undergoing experiences, illustrated by his parable of learning to taste tea [Directly stated].

## Context

The definition appears within Harari's account of the **humanist revolution** as a shift in the source of meaning and authority — "from the sky to human feelings" [Directly stated]. Where the medieval formula for knowledge was *Knowledge = Scriptures × Logic*, and the Scientific Revolution proposed *Knowledge = Empirical Data × Mathematics*, Harari argues that the latter "could not deal with questions of value and meaning." Humanism's answer is a third, distinct formula for *ethical* knowledge: [[Knowledge = Experiences × Sensitivity]]. Experience is the load-bearing term in that formula — it is what one consults, instead of scripture or data, to determine "what is good, what is right and who I really am."

The definition therefore does specific argumentative work. By construing experience as a purely subjective compound of sensations, emotions and thoughts, Harari can make human feeling the "ultimate source of meaning and authority" across every domain: "the voter knows best" (politics), "beauty is in the eye of the beholder" (aesthetics), "the customer is always right" (economics), "if it feels good — do it" (ethics), "think for yourself" (education) [Directly stated]. The same construal sets up Harari's later critique: because experience is here defined as subjective phenomenon rather than empirical data, the dataist claim that experiences are merely biochemical algorithms (data after all) becomes the threat that, in his argument, eventually dissolves humanism from within [Strongly implied]. The definition also grounds the *humanist schism* — [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)|liberal]], socialist and [[Evolutionary Humanism|evolutionary]] humanism all agree that "human experience is the supreme source of authority and meaning," and differ only in how they interpret and weigh experiences [Directly stated].

## Differences from Other Uses

The sharpest contrast in the vault is with **Byung-Chul Han's** distinction between *Erfahrung* and *Erlebnis* (see [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)]]). The two thinkers use "experience" in nearly opposite valences:

- **Harari's "experience" is closest to Han's *Erlebnis*, not his *Erfahrung*.** Harari defines experience as a *subjective phenomenon* — "everything I sense ... every emotion I feel ... whatever thoughts arise in my mind" — i.e. a self-referential inventory of one's own inner states "at any particular moment." Han's *Erlebnis* is precisely this: self-encounter, the subject "encountering oneself everywhere," a consumable stimulus that "leaves what exists as it stands." Where Harari celebrates the inward turn ("get in touch with themselves and their feelings"), Han diagnoses it as narcissistic closure with "no negativity ... to make change possible."

- **Han's *Erfahrung* — the term he honours — is almost the inverse of Harari's "experience."** *Erfahrung* "means facing the Other," is "founded on discontinuity," and "means transformation"; it "tears the subject out from subjection." Harari's humanist experience faces *inward*, not toward an Other, and its political function is the reverse of Han's: for Harari the appeal to inner experience *liberates* (humanism frees us from external authority), whereas for Han "the neoliberal psychopolitics of experiencing or emotion ... only ensnares the subject deeper and deeper in the state of subjection." What Harari treats as the emancipatory ground of modern selfhood, Han treats as the very medium of contemporary domination [Interpretive synthesis].

A further difference of method: Harari frames experience descriptively and historically (as the founding myth of modern industries "from tourism to art"), and he does so to set up his own critique of humanism from the side of the life sciences — the point being that if "experience" is just sensations, emotions and thoughts, and those are biochemical, then the humanist edifice rests on data after all. Han's distinction is normative and diagnostic from the outset: *Erfahrung* is the good that the [[The Transparency Society|transparency society]] forecloses. Both writers also differ from the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]] as theorized by Hayles, for whom the prior question is whether any such interior, self-present locus of "experience" exists at all [Speculative connection].
