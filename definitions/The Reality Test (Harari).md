# The Reality Test (Can It Suffer?) — Yuval Noah Harari

## Author's Definition

Harari offers a single, deliberately blunt operational criterion for telling a real entity apart from a fiction. It is the one diagnostic he supplies to cut through the vast intersubjective web of gods, money, nations and corporations that the chapter has spent its length describing:

> How do you know if an entity is real? Very simple – just ask yourself, ‘Can it suffer?’

The test is then exercised directly on the chapter's central examples, with the capacity to suffer drawn as the line between fiction and reality:

> When people burn down the temple of Zeus, Zeus doesn’t suffer. When the euro loses its value, the euro doesn’t suffer. When a bank goes bankrupt, the bank doesn’t suffer. When a country suffers a defeat in war, the country doesn’t really suffer. It’s just a metaphor. In contrast, when a soldier is wounded in battle, he really does suffer. When a famished peasant has nothing to eat, she suffers. When a cow is separated from her newborn calf, she suffers. This is reality.

`Directly stated` — both the criterion and its application are quoted verbatim from *The Storytellers*.

## Context

The test arrives at the close of *The Storytellers*, after Harari has built his case that "really powerful human organisations" — pharaonic Egypt, communist China, the European empires, the modern school system — rule not by clear sight but by "their ability to force their fictional beliefs on a submissive reality." Having shown that intersubjective fictions are causally dominant agents in history (see [[Intersubjective Reality]]), he needs a counterweight to keep the reader from mistaking that dominance for full ontological standing. The reality test is that counterweight. He prefaces it with a methodological instruction: "When examining the history of any human network, it is therefore advisable to stop from time to time and look at things from the perspective of some real entity." The capacity-to-suffer criterion is the tool for locating that perspective. `Directly stated`

Crucially, the test does not demote fiction to worthlessness. Harari is emphatic that "Fiction isn’t bad. It is vital. Without commonly accepted stories about things like money, states or corporations, no complex human society can function." The point of the test is to keep the ontological ledger straight — to remember that "Corporations, money and nations exist only in our imagination. We invented them to serve us" — so that suffering capable beings are not sacrificed to entities that cannot suffer. The chapter's last line drives this home: "how come we find ourselves sacrificing our lives in their service?" The test, in other words, is ethical as much as ontological: it marks who can be a victim. `Directly stated`

A subtle but load-bearing point: for Harari the cause of suffering can be fictional while the suffering remains fully real. "The cause of war is fictional, but the suffering is 100 per cent real. This is exactly why we should strive to distinguish fiction from reality." The reality test is therefore not a claim that fictions have no effects — it is precisely because fictions inflict real suffering on suffering capable beings that the line must be drawn and held. `Directly stated`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the everyday sense of "real."** Common usage opposes "real" to "imaginary/fake," and would happily call a corporation real (it has offices, employees, a bank balance). Harari refuses that test. For him a corporation is a paradigm of the *unreal*-yet-powerful: it acts, owns and commands but cannot suffer, and so fails the criterion. He replaces a materiality test ("does it have a body / occupy space?") with a sentience test ("can it suffer?"). Note this is not a materiality criterion — a wounded soldier and a hungry cow pass, but so would any sentient being; the test is keyed to felt experience, not to physical substance, which is what makes it cut *across* the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis rather than along it. `Interpretive synthesis`

- **vs. the utilitarian / animal-ethics tradition the phrase echoes.** "Can it suffer?" deliberately recalls Jeremy Bentham's question about animals ("The question is not, Can they reason? nor, Can they talk? but, Can they suffer?"). Harari does not cite Bentham in this chapter, and he is not building a calculus of pleasure and pain; he repurposes the sentience criterion as a *demarcation* tool between the intersubjective and the real, and extends it pointedly to non-human animals (the cow separated from her calf) on the same footing as the wounded soldier and the famished peasant. The inherited phrasing does ethical work, but in the service of an ontological argument about fictions. `Interpretive synthesis` — Bentham is not named in the raw text; the resonance is noted, not asserted as a citation.

- **vs. the vault's other realist/anti-realist criteria.** Where Hayles (in the vault's posthuman material) and O'Gieblyn worry about what is *lost* when bodies are read as information or pattern, Harari's test is the inverse move: a one-line guard against losing the body altogether. It re-grounds value in embodied sentience at the very moment his own argument has shown immaterial fictions to be the most powerful agents in history. The test thus sits in productive tension with [[Intersubjective Reality]] — the same author who insists fictions are history's chief agents also insists they cannot suffer and so are not, in his sense, real. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Related Concepts

- [[Can It Suffer]] — the companion concept note. *The Reality Test* names the criterion as a definitional move; *Can It Suffer* develops it as a recurring idea (the ethical and ontological work the question does across the book). The two are deliberately kept distinct and cross-link to each other.
- [[Intersubjective Reality]] — the entire class of entities (gods, money, nations, corporations) the test is built to exclude; the test is the foil that keeps intersubjective dominance from being mistaken for full reality.
- [[The Web of Meaning]] — the fabric of mutually confirming fictions that the reality test is meant to let us periodically step outside of, "from the perspective of some real entity."
- [[Society as Algorithm]] — the chapter's account of bureaucracy as a fiction-driven calculating machine that can "force their fictional beliefs on a submissive reality"; the reality test is Harari's corrective to the harms such systems inflict on suffering capable beings.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the test cuts across this axis: it grounds reality in sentience rather than in matter, so an immaterial fiction fails it while an embodied animal passes.
