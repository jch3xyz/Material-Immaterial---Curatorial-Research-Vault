# Nominalism — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

Nominalism is the late-medieval doctrine — associated with William of Ockham — that universals such as "justice" and "humanity" name nothing eternal: they are only labels for perceived commonalities, not realities the mind discovers. O'Gieblyn states it directly:

> This rather narrow theological dispute eventually helped eradicate from Western philosophy the idea of universals—the notion that concepts in the mind correspond to eternal truths, like the Platonic forms—and succeeded in making the world, as Blumenberg puts it, "radically contingent." The doctrine that emerged from these debates is often called "nominalism." For the nominalist, "justice" and "humanity" are just names we give to commonalities we perceive between objects, not universal truths. (John Stuart Mill once described nominalism as the belief that "there is nothing general except names.")

Three consequences follow in O'Gieblyn's account. First, the world becomes "radically contingent" — one of many possible worlds rather than the rational order Aquinas had assumed. Second, human reason is demoted from a reflection of the cosmic order to "a construct specific to our species," so that "the mind was a hall of mirrors whose relationship to the external world was suddenly uncertain." Third, the doctrine produces a voluntarist God who is "limited neither by morality nor by rational laws" and whose only justification for any decree is, in Ockham's words, that "He willed it, and no other reason is to be expected."

## Context

O'Gieblyn introduces nominalism in [[Ch11 Algorithm|Chapter 11]] as the hidden genealogy of algorithmic authority. Her argument, following [[Hans Blumenberg]]'s *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age*, is that the inscrutable "black box" deity invoked by deep-learning's enthusiasts is not a relic of the Dark Ages but "the God of Calvin and Luther—a thoroughly modern entity," and that this God was born when the Scholastic dispute over whether God could have created other worlds dissolved the Platonic universals on which medieval reason rested.

The move is genealogical, not merely historical. Nominalism's three effects map onto the present: a [[The Computational Universe|world too contingent and chaotic]] for theory (Chris Anderson's "correlation is enough"); a reason reduced to a species-specific construct (the machines' "alien" intelligence that need not resemble human thought); and a voluntarist sovereign whose verdicts cannot be questioned (the loan denied, the sentence handed down, with no answer to *why*). O'Gieblyn's claim is that we have "chosen to raise this God from the dead" in the form of the algorithm — see [[The Algorithm as Providence]] and [[Algorithmic Determinism]]. Nominalism is thus the conceptual root of the [[Disenchantment]] that, in her reading, the algorithm now reverses into a strange [[Re-enchantment]]: a return to revelation without understanding.

The term carries the chapter's pivotal causal claim — that nominalism, spreading through Protestant theology, "led to the Enlightenment, disenchantment, and the scientific revolution," provoking the crisis of certainty that [[René Descartes]] tried to escape by grounding the world in human thought alone.

## Differences from Other Uses

In the standard philosophical lexicon nominalism is a position in the problem of universals — opposed to Platonic realism — and is treated as an epistemological or metaphysical thesis about whether abstract entities exist. O'Gieblyn invokes that core sense (her gloss via Mill, "there is nothing general except names," is the classical one), but her interest is theological and genealogical rather than analytic: she reads nominalism through Blumenberg as the *origin* of a voluntarist God and, downstream, of modern disenchantment and algorithmic authority. The doctrine matters to her not as a debate to be adjudicated but as the inherited "site of the crime" we keep returning to blindly.

This distinguishes her usage from the broadly Platonic strain elsewhere in the vault. Where the Hayles cluster treats information as something abstracted from its material substrate — [[The Computational Universe]], [[Pattern and Randomness]], [[The Body as Data]] — those positions resemble a *realism* about pattern (the pattern is the real thing; the flesh is contingent). O'Gieblyn's nominalism is the opposite vector: it dissolves the eternal universals and leaves only contingent names and an unfathomable will. The two meet in [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] and the dream of [[Mind as Machine]], where reason is recast as computation; but for O'Gieblyn the recasting is precisely the nominalist demotion of reason to "a construct specific to our species," not a triumph of it.
