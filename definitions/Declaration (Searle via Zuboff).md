# Declaration — Shoshana Zuboff (after John Searle)

## Author's Definition

A **declaration**, in [[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s usage, is a borrowed term: she takes it directly from the philosopher of language **John Searle**'s theory of speech acts and deploys it as the engine of [[Conquest by Declaration]], surveillance capitalism's founding strategy of self-asserted legitimacy. The verbatim definition she quotes is:

> According to the philosopher of language John Searle, a declaration is a particular way of speaking and acting that establishes facts out of thin air, creating a new reality where there was nothing.

`Directly stated` (Ch.6)

The distinctive mechanism is that a declaration *fuses* the two ordinary functions of speech — describing the world and changing it — into a single move. As Zuboff glosses Searle: "sometimes we speak to simply describe the world—'you have brown eyes'—or to change it—'Shut the door.' A declaration combines both, **asserting a new reality by describing the world as if a desired change were already true**: 'All humans are created equal.' 'They are yours to command.'" The compressed formula is Searle's own: "**We make something the case by representing it as being the case**" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6).

A declaration need not be spoken. It can be enacted: we "create a reality by representing that reality as created." Zuboff illustrates with Searle's soup-bowl example — a waiter who, "without saying anything," sets one bowl before each diner has *declared* that the bowls are not the same, and the diners "strengthen the facts of his declaration" by eating only from "their" bowls. From this follows the property Zuboff most needs: **declarations rise or fall on the strength of others' acceptance**. "In this way declarations rise or fall on the strength of others' acceptance of the new facts" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). And, in Searle's largest claim, which Zuboff quotes to set the stakes: "**All of institutional reality, and therefore… all of human civilization is created by… declarations**" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6).

## Context

Zuboff introduces the declaration at the opening of Chapter 6 ("Hijacked: The Division of Learning in Society"), in the section "The Google Declarations." It is the conceptual hinge between two halves of her argument: the colonial **conquest pattern** (Columbus's three-phase template of legalistic gloss, declaration of claims, and town-founding; the Spanish *Requirimiento* read aloud to villagers who could not understand it) on one side, and **Google's six self-asserted rights** on the other. Searle's theory supplies the *philosophical mechanism* that makes the analogy more than rhetorical: it explains *how* a unilateral assertion can become an institutional fact. Surveillance capitalism's facts, Zuboff argues, "have been carried into the world on the strength of six critical declarations pulled from thin air when Google first asserted them" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6).

The term does specific work in the larger argument. First, it names the *mode* of [[Digital Dispossession]]: experience is not negotiated or licensed away but simply *declared* to be "raw material free for the taking," and then defended until the declaration stands as fact. Second, it identifies the *vulnerability* of the whole edifice — because a declaration depends on acceptance, it can be unmade by refusal: "each declaration builds on the one before it. If one falls, they all fall." Third, it diagnoses the *invasiveness* Zuboff attributes to surveillance capital: "Declarations are inherently invasive because they impose new facts on the social world while their declarers devise ways to get others to agree to those facts" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). The [[Inevitabilism|rhetoric of inevitability]] and the unread terms-of-service agreement are, in this reading, the contemporary instruments by which acceptance is manufactured after the fact — "faux-legitimation practices" standing in for the mumbled *Requirimiento*.

This definition note holds the **Searlean machinery itself** (the speech-act theory, the describe-versus-change distinction, the soup-bowl example, the institutional-reality claim); the companion concept note [[Conquest by Declaration]] holds the *use* surveillance capital makes of that machinery (the six-declaration cascade, the colonial template, the click-through edict). The two are designed to be read together.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Searle's own scope vs. Zuboff's deployment.** For Searle the declaration is a neutral category of speech act — the constituting ground of *all* institutional reality (money, marriage, property, borders, "human civilization"). Zuboff borrows the apparatus intact but turns it to a *critical* end: she is interested in the declaration as a weapon of conquest, in its "inherently invasive" character, and in the asymmetry between declarer and the public whose acceptance is engineered rather than freely given. The term is value-neutral in Searle and indicting in Zuboff. `Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis`

- **Adjacent vault notions of reality-by-assertion.** Harari's [[Intersubjective Reality]] and [[Writing Reshapes Reality]] (in *Homo Deus*) describe a closely related phenomenon — shared fictions (money, nations, gods) that become real because enough people believe them, and the way inscription "reshapes" the world by rewriting it. The Searle/Zuboff declaration is the *micro-mechanism* version of the same insight: a single performative act that institutes a fact, which then holds only so long as others accept it. Where Harari emphasizes the *durability* of intersubjective fictions across millions of minds, Zuboff emphasizes the *unilateral founding move* and its dependence on (manufactured) consent. The notes are cross-linked, not merged; Zuboff cites Searle, not Harari. `Interpretive synthesis`

- **Not a synonym for "decree" or "edict."** Although Zuboff maps the declaration onto the Spanish Monarchical Edict (*Requirimiento*), the Searlean declaration is broader and more basic than a legal pronouncement: it includes wordless acts (the soup bowls) and everyday institutional facts. The edict is one historically loaded *instance* of a declaration, not the definition of the term. `Strongly implied`
