# Cyborg — Donna Haraway

## Author's Definition

For [[Donna Haraway]], the cyborg is defined in a single compact sentence at the opening of "A Cyborg Manifesto," immediately after she names it as the image at the center of her "ironic faith":

> A cyborg is a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction.

The definition is deliberately doubled. The cyborg is not only a literal coupling of machine and organism but equally "a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction" — and Haraway insists these registers are inseparable, because "the boundary between science fiction and social reality is an optical illusion" (`Directly stated`).

She extends the definition from the individual figure to a collective ontological claim about the late-twentieth-century condition:

> By the late twentieth century, our time, a mythic time, we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism—in short, cyborgs. The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics.

The cyborg, that is, is not a special or exotic being but the form of everyone's existence now — "we are all chimeras" — and from that ontology Haraway derives a politics (`Directly stated`).

In the concluding chapter she glosses the figure negatively, by what it refuses, in the line that fixes its character as a political myth:

> A cyborg body is not innocent; it was not born in a garden; it does not seek unitary identity and so generate antagonistic dualisms without end (or until the world ends); it takes irony for granted.

The cyborg therefore has no origin story and no Garden of Eden to return to; it "skips the step of original unity," refuses "the founding myth of original wholeness," and treats irony — "the tension of holding incompatible things together because both or all are necessary and true" — as its native condition (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Haraway introduces the cyborg as the central image of "an ironic political myth faithful to feminism, socialism, and materialism." It is not offered as a neutral term but as a polemical and utopian figure: "my cyborg myth is about transgressed boundaries, potent fusions, and dangerous possibilities, which progressive people might explore as one part of needed political work" (`Directly stated`). The definition is doing argumentative work — it is meant to provide socialist-feminism with a figure that does not depend on an "imagined organic body" or a "myth of original unity."

The definition's force comes from three "boundary breakdowns" Haraway names. First, "the boundary between human and animal is thoroughly breached"; second, "the second leaky distinction is between animal–human (organism) and machine," now that "late twentieth-century machines have made thoroughly ambiguous the difference between natural and artificial, mind and body, self-developing and externally designed"; and third, "the boundary between physical and nonphysical is very imprecise for us," since "our best machines are made of sunshine; they are all light and clean because they are nothing but signals, electromagnetic waves" (`Directly stated`). The cyborg is precisely the creature that inhabits these breached boundaries: "The cyborg appears in myth precisely where the boundary between human and animal is transgressed."

The cyborg is also avowedly impure in its parentage. "The main trouble with cyborgs, of course, is that they are the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism, not to mention state socialism. But illegitimate offspring are often exceedingly unfaithful to their origins" (`Directly stated`). This illegitimacy is the source of its political promise, not a flaw to be overcome. By the closing chapter the definition becomes an ethics of fusion: "The machine is not an it to be animated, worshipped, and dominated. The machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment." Haraway aligns the figure with regeneration rather than rebirth, and closes the essay with its most-quoted line: "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess" (`Directly stated`).

Notably, Haraway also positions the cyborg against the dominant frame for thinking power-over-life in this period: "Michel Foucault's biopolitics is a flaccid premonition of cyborg politics, a very open field" — locating the cyborg as a successor to, and intensification of, [[Biopolitics]] (`Directly stated`).

## Differences from Other Uses

The vault's existing concept note [[The Cyborg]] derives the figure largely secondhand, through [[N. Katherine Hayles]], who borrows it from Haraway. This note is the primary-source definition that concept was missing. The differences in emphasis are real:

- **Haraway vs. Hayles.** [[N. Katherine Hayles]] adopts Haraway's cyborg but reframes it historically and epistemologically — as a postwar "technological artifact and cultural icon," and as the figure produced when the Turing test splices the enacted body and the represented body. Hayles foregrounds the three violated distinctions (human/machine, human/animal, animate/inanimate) and the question of whether the cyborg confirms or refutes disembodiment ([[Embodiment vs Information]]). Haraway's emphasis is the reverse: hers is first a *feminist and socialist political myth* — "a fiction mapping our social and bodily reality" — built to escape the "myth of original unity" and the organicist feminism that "insist[s] on the organic, opposing it to the technological." Where Hayles asks what the cyborg means for the fate of [[Embodiment]] and [[Informatics]], Haraway asks what oppositional politics it makes possible. For Haraway the cyborg is explicitly anti-innocence ("it was not born in a garden"); the irony, illegitimacy, and "pleasure in the confusion of boundaries" are constitutive, not incidental.

- **Against Foucault's biopolitics.** Haraway treats [[Biopolitics]] as merely "a flaccid premonition" of cyborg politics — the cyborg is not the disciplined or administered body but the body whose boundaries are already fused with machines and signals, "as hard to see politically as materially."

- **Against the liberal humanist subject.** The cyborg "does not seek unitary identity"; it refuses "the One who is not dominated," the autonomous self of "the tradition of the appropriation of nature as resource for the productions of culture." This places Haraway's cyborg in pointed opposition to [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]], though by a feminist-political rather than cybernetic-historical route.
