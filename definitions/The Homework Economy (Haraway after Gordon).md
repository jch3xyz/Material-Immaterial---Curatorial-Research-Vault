# The Homework Economy — Donna Haraway (after Richard Gordon)

## Author's Definition

The term is not Haraway's coinage. She attributes it explicitly to Richard Gordon and then takes it over as a conceptual structure:

> Richard Gordon has called this new situation the “homework economy.”

Haraway is careful to distinguish what the term does *not* primarily mean. Although Gordon includes literal homework — piecework done in the home — the concept is not reducible to it:

> Although he includes the phenomenon of literal homework emerging in connection with electronics assembly, Gordon intends homework economy to name a restructuring of work that broadly has the characteristics formerly ascribed to female jobs, jobs literally done only by women. Work is being redefined as both literally female and feminized, whether performed by men or women.

The core of the definition, then, is a *restructuring of work* so that all work acquires the characteristics historically assigned to women's jobs — that work is "redefined as both literally female and feminized, whether performed by men or women" (`Directly stated`). Haraway glosses what "feminized" means in this technical sense:

> To be feminized means to be made extremely vulnerable; able to be disassembled, reassembled, exploited as a reserve labor force; seen less as workers than as servers; subjected to time arrangements on and off the paid job that make a mockery of a limited workday; leading an existence that always borders on being obscene, out of place, and reducible to sex.

She then fixes the boundaries of the concept against two narrower readings. It is not merely deskilling, and it is not merely literal homework:

> Deskilling is an old strategy newly applicable to formerly privileged workers. However, the homework economy does not refer only to large-scale deskilling, nor does it deny that new areas of high skill are emerging, even for women and men previously excluded from skilled employment. Rather, the concept indicates that factory, home, and market are integrated on a new scale and that the places of women are crucial—and need to be analyzed for differences among women and for meanings for relations between men and women in various situations.

The positive content of the term is therefore the *integration of factory, home, and market on a new scale*, with women's positions as the analytic key (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Haraway introduces the homework economy in the section "The Homework Economy 'Outside the Home,'" as part of mapping the social-material conditions produced by what she calls the "New Industrial Revolution." The term names the labor side of the same transformation that, on the conceptual side, she calls [[The Informatics of Domination]] — the shift from an industrial, organic, hierarchical order to a dispersed, networked, "polymorphous information system." The homework economy is the form work takes inside that system (`Strongly implied`).

Crucially, Haraway insists the new arrangement is *enabled* by, not *caused* by, the new technologies:

> The homework economy as a world capitalist organizational structure is made possible by (not caused by) the new technologies. The success of the attack on relatively privileged, mostly white, men’s unionized jobs is tied to the power of the new communications technologies to integrate and control labor despite extensive dispersion and decentralization.

This is a deliberately anti-technologically-determinist framing: the communications technologies furnish the capacity to "integrate and control labor despite extensive dispersion and decentralization," but the political defeat of unionized, mostly white male labor is a strategy of capital, not an automatic effect of machines (`Directly stated`).

The concept carries a sharp account of who bears the cost. Haraway ties it to "the feminization of poverty—generated by dismantling the welfare state, by the homework economy where stable jobs become the exception," and she stresses that its effects are differentiated by race, class, and sexuality rather than uniform: the situation of U.S. black women, of "teenage women in industrializing areas of the Third World," and of white women losing access to "the family (male) wage" are distinct positions within one structure (`Directly stated`). She also schematizes a third family form correlated with this stage of capital: "the 'family' of the homework economy with its oxymoronic structure of women-headed households and its explosion of feminisms and the paradoxical intensification and erosion of gender itself" (`Directly stated`).

The homework economy is thus doing two jobs in the larger argument. It grounds the cyborg's politics in a concrete labor analysis — the cyborg is "the awful apocalyptic telos of the 'West's' escalating dominations" precisely as it is lived through this restructured, surveilled, feminized work — and it supplies the material basis for the companion concept [[Women in the Integrated Circuit]], the mapping of women's dispersed positions across home, market, paid workplace, school, clinic, church, and state (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Coinage vs. expansion (Gordon vs. Haraway).** The filename's parenthetical — "(Haraway after Gordon)" — records a real division of authorship. Richard Gordon coined "homework economy"; Haraway adopts the term and supplies its conceptual reach, explicitly narrating his intent ("Gordon intends homework economy to name...") before generalizing it into her own analysis of feminized work and the integration of factory, home, and market. The note is filed under Haraway because the vault's source is her text, but the term is not original to her.

- **Not literal homework.** The most common misreading the concept guards against is the literal one — homework as piecework done at the kitchen table. Haraway concedes Gordon "includes the phenomenon of literal homework" but insists the term "does not refer only to" it. The homework economy names a quality that work *as such* acquires, "whether performed by men or women," not a location where work is done.

- **Not deskilling alone.** Equally, it is not a synonym for proletarianization or deskilling. Haraway grants that deskilling is "newly applicable to formerly privileged workers" but states flatly that "the homework economy does not refer only to large-scale deskilling, nor does it deny that new areas of high skill are emerging." The concept's distinctive content is *integration on a new scale*, not loss of skill.

- **Against the vault's other labor concepts.** This is distinct from [[Immaterial Labour]] as that concept appears elsewhere in the vault (Han, after Negri): immaterial labour names the immaterialization of the *product* and the colonization of affect and communication by capital. Haraway's homework economy is narrower and earlier in framing — it names the *gendered restructuring* of the labor relation under the new communications technologies, with feminization (vulnerability, disassembly, the reserve-labor condition) as its defining mark, and women's differentiated positions as the analytic key. The two illuminate each other but are not the same term; the resonance is `Interpretive synthesis`.
