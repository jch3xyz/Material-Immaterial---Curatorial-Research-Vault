# Visionary Pragmatism — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

Ascott coins **visionary pragmatism** as the name for what artists do, and have always done: the fusion of "the visionary impulse" with "pragmatism of the kind that Rorty espouses." It is, in his own gloss, "about flying with your feet on the ground… about working with the future in the present… living life acausally but not casually" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). At its most efficacious it is a synthesis across the divide between spiritual and scientific knowledge: it "combines, within the artistic domain, the perennial wisdom of shamans and gnostics with contemporary insights of scientists, engineers, and philosophers" (`Directly stated`, ch.31).

Visionary pragmatism is named under the section heading "CONSTRUCTIVE LANGUAGE AND VISIONARY PRAGMATISM." Ascott derives it from his constructivist account of language: language is "not merely a device for communicating ideas about the world but rather a tool for bringing the world into existence," and art is accordingly "a form of world building, of mind construction, of self-creation" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). He routes this through Richard Rorty, whose pragmatism "eschews the sanctity of philosophy in favour of the artist's visionary impulse and search for metaphor that leads to the continual construction of reality and of the self, thereby denying the passive acceptance of any canonical description" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). Visionary pragmatism is the artist's version of that stance: it "is guided by the need to bring dreams to earth," it "is an attribute to be fostered in all aspiring artists… a quality least understood but most in need of support," and "finds in moistmedia its creative ally, with its triad of computational exactitude, biological fluidity, and technoetic complexity" (`Directly stated`, ch.31).

## Context

The term appears in the late essay "Art @ the Edge of the NET: the Future Will Be Moist!" (ch.31), Ascott's manifesto for a coming "moist culture" at "the convergence of the digital, biological, and spiritual." Visionary pragmatism is the *disposition* proper to that culture, paired with [[Moistmedia]] (its preferred medium) and [[Technoetics]] (its account of mind/consciousness). Ascott credits it with the very developments he champions: it "has led to what is known as ubiquitous computing, where intelligence spreads throughout the network and into every facet of the environment," and it is what "has pushed the consequences of a screen-based, immaterial world into the re-materialisation of culture involving molecules and atoms, nanotechnology and neurons" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). This last point makes the concept a direct hinge on the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension: Ascott has visionary pragmatism carry art *out of* the immaterial screen-world and *back into* matter (a "re-materialisation"), rather than treating dematerialization as art's terminus (`Strongly implied`).

The concept does concrete institutional and practical work across the essay. Ascott names [[The Planetary Collegium]] "an example of visionary pragmatism that seeks to develop advanced research in the interspace between artistic, technological, scientific, and spiritual disciplines"; he makes architecture "a useful focus of visionary pragmatism" that "must embrace the anticipatory design science advocated by Buckminster Fuller"; and he closes by calling it "perhaps the most useful way to inform the artist's participation in building worlds we would want to live in," able to "take the love inherent in the telematic embrace and create new relationships, new societies, and new culture" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). It is thus both Ascott's self-description of the artistic act and the explicit antidote to "the nihilism and despair of late postmodernism" — the stance he opposes to Baudrillard ("a case of 'bye-bye Baudrillard'") and to "the sense of terminal decline that characterised art at the end of the preceding millennium" (`Directly stated`, ch.31).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Rorty's pragmatism** is Ascott's explicit, named source and foil (a `Directly stated` reference). Rorty supplies the anti-foundationalism — the artist as the maker of new vocabularies, "to create one's mind is to create one's own language" — and the refusal of any "canonical description." Visionary pragmatism *adds* the visionary, shamanic-gnostic pole that Rorty's secular philosophy does not contain: Ascott explicitly yokes the perennial wisdom of "shamans and gnostics" to the pragmatist's world-construction, producing a stance that is at once spiritual and technical (`Interpretive synthesis`).

- **Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]]** (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*, 1968) locates the artist's work in the shift from object to system. Visionary pragmatism is a later, dispositional companion to that move: where systems esthetics describes the *form* the artwork takes, visionary pragmatism names the *attitude* — "flying with your feet on the ground" — required to make systems-, network-, and moist-art (`Interpretive synthesis`). Kept DISTINCT from `Systems Esthetics`.

- **Lippard's [[Dematerialization]]**: conceptual art's dematerialization carried art *away* from the object. Visionary pragmatism, by Ascott's account, reverses the terminal reading of that trajectory — it "pushed the consequences of a screen-based, immaterial world into the re-materialisation of culture" — so the immaterial network is a passage toward new matter (molecules, atoms, nanotechnology, neurons), not an endpoint (`Interpretive synthesis`).

- It is not utopian futurism unmoored from practice: the "pragmatism" half is load-bearing. Ascott insists on "living life acausally but not casually" and on "the need to bring dreams to earth" — the term holds the visionary and the grounded together, against both disenchanted technical instrumentalism and ungrounded mysticism (`Strongly implied`).
