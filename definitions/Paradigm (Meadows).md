# Paradigm — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows fixes the term while ranking the twelve "places to intervene in a system." Paradigms are leverage point #2, glossed in the list as "The mind-set out of which the system—its goals, structure, rules, delays, parameters—arises." The defining sentence follows, extending Jay Forrester's tax-law example:

> The shared idea in the minds of society, the great big unstated assumptions, constitute that society's paradigm, or deepest set of beliefs about how the world works.

`Directly stated`

She adds immediately why these beliefs escape notice:

> These beliefs are unstated because it is unnecessary to state them—everyone already knows them.

`Directly stated`

A paradigm, for Meadows, is therefore not a scientific model in the narrow sense but a culture's tacit metaphysics — the layer of self-evident assumptions ("Money measures something real"; "Growth is good"; "Nature is a stock of resources to be converted to human purposes"; "One can 'own' land") that looks obvious from inside and "utterly dumbfounded other cultures, who thought them not the least bit obvious." `Directly stated`

The decisive claim is one of priority. The paradigm is not one system element among others but the wellspring of all of them:

> Paradigms are the sources of systems. From them, from shared social agreements about the nature of reality, come system goals and information flows, feedbacks, stocks, flows, and everything else about systems.

`Directly stated`

## Context

The definition appears at the near-top of Chapter 6, "Leverage Points," in Meadows's countdown of interventions from least to most powerful. Placing paradigms at #2 — above the goals of the system, above its rules and information flows and stocks — is the chapter's most counterintuitive move, and the definition exists to justify it. Because a paradigm is "the source of systems," intervening there is upstream of everything a modeler could otherwise adjust. This inverts the ordinary intuition that a system is built from its material parts: the [[Feedback Loops|feedbacks]], stocks, and goals one diagrams are downstream expressions of a prior worldview. Meadows underwrites the inversion with Emerson — "every nation and every man instantly surround themselves with a material apparatus which exactly corresponds to... their state of thought" — and with the pyramids (built out of a belief in the afterlife) and skyscrapers (built out of a belief that downtown space is valuable) as the material residue of paradigms. `Directly stated`

The definition also carries a paradox that the surrounding text unpacks: a paradigm is at once the hardest thing to change and, in principle, the cheapest. "You could say paradigms are harder to change than anything else about a system," yet "there's nothing physical or expensive or even slow in the process of paradigm change. In a single individual it can happen in a millisecond. All it takes is a click in the mind, a falling of scales from the eyes, a new way of seeing." Whole societies "resist challenges to their paradigms harder than they resist anything else," which is why Meadows borrows Thomas Kuhn's tactics — point at the anomalies of the old paradigm, act loudly from the new one, place its holders in visibility and power. `Directly stated`

This note isolates and pins the verbatim *defining sentences*; the working concept as it operates across the primer — the ranking, the paradox, Emerson, Kuhn, and the still-higher move of not clinging to any paradigm at all — lives in the concept note [[Paradigms]] and its sibling [[Transcending Paradigms]] (leverage point #1). They are companion notes, not duplicates, on the pattern of `Information` / `Information (Han)` and `System (Meadows)` / `A System`.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the concept [[Paradigms]]** — that note holds the full working concept (the twelve leverage points, the difficulty/cost paradox, the Emerson and Kuhn material, the ordering above goals and below Transcending Paradigms). This note pins the two verbatim defining sentences that anchor it. Cross-linked, not merged. `Directly stated`
- **vs. [[Transcending Paradigms]]** — Meadows names one leverage point higher than *changing* a paradigm: staying "unattached in the arena of paradigms," realizing that "no paradigm is 'true'" — "to 'get' at a gut level the paradigm that there are paradigms." That is a stance *toward* paradigms, not a definition *of* one; it presupposes this note's definition and moves beyond it. `Directly stated`
- **vs. Ray Kurzweil's [[Paradigm Shift and the S-Curve]]** — the sharpest distinction in the vault, because the two authors share only the word. For Kurzweil a "paradigm" is a narrowly technical unit — "a method or approach to solving a problem; for example, shrinking transistors on an integrated circuit" — that runs a three-stage S-curve until "its potential is exhausted," whereupon "a paradigm shift occurs, which enables exponential growth to continue." Kurzweil's paradigm shift is a mechanical hand-off internal to the machinery of accelerating technology (electromechanical → relays → vacuum tubes → transistors → integrated circuits → 3-D molecular computing). Meadows's paradigm is a culture's deepest metaphysical worldview, and *changing* it is the second-highest leverage a person can wield over a system. Kurzweil's paradigm turns over automatically and lawfully; Meadows's is fiercely resisted and must be fought for. Deliberately not collapsed. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. the Kuhnian source** — Meadows takes the term explicitly from Thomas Kuhn ("who wrote the seminal book about the great paradigm shifts of science") but generalizes it from the scientific-community sense to the whole-society sense: not just what a discipline holds about its object, but "shared social agreements about the nature of reality" that structure an entire culture. `Directly stated`
