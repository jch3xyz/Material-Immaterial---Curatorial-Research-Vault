# Reality Mining — Shoshana Zuboff (reporting Pentland and Eagle)

## Author's Definition

Zuboff reports the term as coined by MIT's Alex (Sandy) Pentland and his doctoral student Nathan Eagle in 2005. In her account the definition is given through their own words:

> The team saw that it would be possible to exploit the increasingly "ubiquitous infrastructure" of mobile phones and combine those data with new streams of information from their wearable behavioral monitors. The result was a radical new solution that Pentland and Eagle called "reality mining." Mentor and student demonstrated how the data from cell phones "can be used to uncover regular rules and structure in the behavior of both individuals and organizations."

Reality mining, then, is the method of exploiting the *ubiquitous infrastructure* of mobile phones together with *wearable behavioral monitors* to *uncover regular rules and structure in the behavior of both individuals and organizations*. (`Directly stated`)

For Zuboff its defining significance lies in what it extends. Reality mining furthers "the progress of behavioral surplus capture and analysis," and points "the way toward the larger shift in the nature of behavioral dispossession from virtual, to actual, to social experience." (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Zuboff introduces reality mining in Chapter 15, "The Instrumentarian Collective," as her central case study of the worldview she calls [[Instrumentarian Power|instrumentarianism]]. Her stated aim is "to infer the theory behind the practice, as surveillance capitalists integrate 'society' as a 'first class object' for rendition, computation, modification, monetization, and control." Pentland — director of MIT's Human Dynamics Lab and "the rare applied utopianist" — supplies that theory.

The definition sits at a specific historical hinge. Earlier surveillance capitalism mined the *virtual* exhaust of online clicks and searches; a 2005 collaboration with Eagle confronted "the bias, sparsity of data, and lack of continuity" in social science's grasp of behavior, and Pentland insisted that even "data mining" could not capture "the 'real action' of conversations and face-to-face interactions." The cell phone changed that. Combining its "ubiquitous infrastructure" with wearable sensors (the "sociometer," and later the sociometric badge) let researchers instrument the body and the street. Reality mining is therefore, for Zuboff, "a technological and cultural landmark": the announcement that "reality" was "now fair and feasible game for surplus capture, search, extraction, rendition, datafication, analysis, prediction, and intervention," forging "a path toward the new practices that would eventually become the 'reality business.'" (`Directly stated`)

Crucially, Zuboff stresses that the term was never offered as neutral science. "From the start, Pentland understood reality mining as the gateway to a new universe of commercial opportunities," asserting in 2004 that wearable devices with "computational horsepower" would provide the "foundation" for reality mining as "an exciting new suite of business applications." The companion concept note [[Reality Mining]] develops this commercial and instrumentarian arc in full. (`Directly stated`)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Diagnostic vs. celebratory framing.** Pentland and Eagle coin "reality mining" as an enthusiastic name for a breakthrough technology (the *MIT Technology Review* listed it among its "10 Breakthrough Technologies" in 2008). Zuboff adopts their exact coinage but inverts its valence — quoting them in order to expose, not endorse, the "God's-eye view" their work serves. The word stays the same; the evaluative charge reverses.
- **Against "data mining."** Pentland explicitly contrasts reality mining with the established term *data mining*, which he held "could not capture the 'real action' of conversations and face-to-face interactions." Reality mining names a step *past* mining databases of records — toward mining lived, embodied, social behavior in real time. (`Directly stated`)
- **Beyond the [[Quantified Self|Quantified Self]].** The self-tracking culture of the [[Quantified Self]] uses wearable monitors for *voluntary self-measurement*. Reality mining repurposes the same class of instruments toward *involuntary commercial surplus*, with "reality" itself — not the consenting self — as the object. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **Beyond Han's and Harari's data terms.** Where [[Dataism]] (Han, Harari) names a *worldview* that worships data flow, reality mining names a concrete *capture technique*. It is the kind of ubiquitous, sensor-based [[Surveillance|surveillance]] that Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and Harari's [[Big Data|big data]] describe at the level of social theory, here specified as a named MIT research method. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
