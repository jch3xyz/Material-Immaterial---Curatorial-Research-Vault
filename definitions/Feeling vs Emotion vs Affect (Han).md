# Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect — Byung-Chul Han

## Author's Definition

In the chapter "Emotional Capitalism" of *Psychopolitics*, Han draws a precise semantic distinction between three terms that, he complains, "seem interchangeable for many researchers": *feeling* (Gefühl), *emotion* (Emotion), and *affect* (Affekt). The distinction is the analytic engine of his account of [[Emotional Capitalism|emotional capitalism]]: it is what lets him locate the *emotion* — not feeling, not affect — as the exploitable unit of neoliberal production.

The master axis is **objectivity versus subjectivity**:

> Affect and emotion refer to strictly subjective matters, whereas feeling refers to something objective.

**Feeling (Gefühl)** is the *objective, durational, narratable, constative* term. One can "have a feel for language" (*Sprachgefühl*) or "feel for others" (*Mitgefühl*); feeling "can be recounted" and "has narrative length, or breadth"; it "admit[s] duration." Grammatically it is constative — "we say: 'I have a feeling that.'" It need not be intentional: "Often, the feeling of anxiety has no concrete object." Feeling resists exploitation precisely because it "ha[s] no performativity."

**Emotion (Emotion)** is the *subjective, fleeting, performative, intentional* term — the unit emotional capitalism seizes:

> Emotions are not constative, but performative. They refer to actions and deeds. Furthermore, they are intentional and goal-oriented.

Emotions "prove significantly more fleeting and short-lived than feelings," they "do not represent a state" ("there is no emotion of rest"), and they "are dynamic, situative and performative." Their directionality is *whither* — a direction — as against feeling's *wherefore* ('why'). Because emotion calls forth action — "[a]s inclinations, they represent the energetic — the sensory, or even sensuous — basis for action" — it is what neoliberal psychopolitics can steer [Directly stated].

**Affect (Affekt)** is the *eruptive, momentary, discharge-oriented* term. Like emotion it is "strictly subjective," but where emotion is performatively directional, affect is not: it "is often limited to a single moment," and "affects are not performative so much as eruptive; they lack performative directionality." Affect "does not open up space"; instead "it steers a linear path in order to discharge, to unload itself" — *Affektabfuhr*, catharsis. Han names digital communication an "affect-medium," and "[s]hitstorms are streams of affect" [Directly stated].

To these three Han adds a fourth, *even more objective* term:

**Atmosphere / mood (Stimmung)** "possesses even more objectivity than feeling." It "expresses a way-it-is," is "neither intentional nor performative," and is "static and constel-lative — whereas emotion is dynamic and performative." Its grammatical marker is *where* — "a state, a disposition" (*Befindlichkeit*) — completing Han's grid: atmosphere = *where*, emotion = *whither*, feeling = *wherefore* [Directly stated].

## Context

Han introduces the tripartite distinction to correct what he calls the "utter conceptual confusion" of the contemporary "emotional 'boom'" in academic research, and to explain that boom economically: "the emotional 'boom' stems from an economic process, above all." His running polemical target is Eva Illouz's *Cold Intimacies: The Making of Emotional Capitalism*, which "equates feelings and emotions without drawing any conceptual distinction at all." Only by separating the terms can Han make his central claim: that capitalism exploits *emotion* specifically.

The pay-off of the distinction is the thesis that **only emotion is exploitable**:

> Emotional capitalism exploits precisely these qualities. Feelings, in contrast, cannot be readily exploited inasmuch as they have no performativity.

Feeling is too objective, too durational, too constative to be converted into productive energy; affect is too eruptive and undirected; emotion alone is "dynamic, situative and performative," and so can be "deploy[ed] ... as resources in order to bring about heightened productivity and achievement." This connects the definition to Han's wider apparatus: emotion supplies the affective "raw material" of immaterial production, it is what consumer capitalism sells ("today we do not consume things so much as emotions"), and it operates on the "pre-reflexive, half-conscious, physico-instinctual level" that neoliberal psychopolitics targets in order to "cut and operate deep inside" [Directly stated].

The temporal sub-distinction (feeling admits duration; emotion is fleeting; affect is momentary) places the definition alongside Han's broader diagnoses of [[Acceleration|acceleration]] and the erosion of [[Narrativity|narrativity]]: because "[r]ationality is slower than emotionality," "the pressure of acceleration now is leading to a dictatorship of emotion," and "the narrative theatre of feelings is yielding to a clamorous theatre of affects" [Directly stated]. The opposition of objective feeling to subjective emotion/affect also rhymes structurally with Han's earlier opposition of [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)|Erfahrung to Erlebnis]] — durational, transformative, narratable experience against fleeting, self-referential, consequence-free experiencing [Interpretive synthesis].

## Differences from Other Uses

Han's tripartition is deliberately set *against* the dominant usage in affect theory and the sociology of emotions, where "feeling," "emotion," and "affect" are often treated as near-synonyms or arranged on a different axis. In much affect theory (following Massumi's reading of Spinoza/Deleuze), *affect* is the impersonal, pre-subjective intensity and *emotion* is the subjective capture of it — so that affect is the more "objective" term. Han inverts the salient contrast: for him it is *feeling* (and still more *atmosphere/mood*) that carries objectivity, while *both* emotion and affect are "expressions of subjectivity." Noting this inversion relative to the wider affect-theory tradition is an [Interpretive synthesis]; Han himself does not stage the comparison in this chapter.

His named foil is **Eva Illouz**, whose *Cold Intimacies* he faults for "equat[ing] feelings and emotions without drawing any conceptual distinction at all" and for mislocating emotional capitalism at the "inaugural stages" of capitalism. Against Illouz's invocation of **Max Weber**'s Protestant ethic — where "anxiety provoked by an inscrutable divinity" drives "the capitalist entrepreneur's frantic activity" — Han objects that "[i]t is mistaken to understand anxiety in terms of emotion. Anxiety is a feeling," whose durational temporality is what could underwrite sustained activity; Weber's ascetic accumulation "obeys rational logic more than it follows emotional logic" and so "does not feed into consumer capitalism" [Directly stated]. The Stimmung material draws (without explicit citation here) on the Heideggerian sense of mood as *Befindlichkeit*, a way-one-finds-oneself; attributing that lineage as Han's source would be a [Speculative connection] beyond this chapter.

Within the vault, the objective/subjective and durational/fleeting structure of this distinction parallels [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)]]; the claim that emotion (not use value) is what consumer capitalism sells links to [[Exhibition Value]] and to [[Cult Value vs Exhibition Value (Han via Benjamin)]], since Han here says "[i]t is not use value but emotive or cultic value that plays a constitutive role in the economy of consumption" [Directly stated].
