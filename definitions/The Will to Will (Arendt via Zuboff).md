# The Will to Will — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

The term is not Zuboff's own coinage. She borrows it, with explicit attribution, from the philosopher Hannah Arendt — whose *The Life of the Mind* (the "entire volume" Zuboff names) examines will as the human faculty oriented toward what is not yet. Zuboff quotes Arendt's formulation directly (*Directly stated*):

> The philosopher Hannah Arendt devoted an entire volume to an examination of will as the “organ for the future” in the same way that memory is our mental organ for the past.

The defining property of this organ is that, unlike memory, it deals with what has no existence at all. Zuboff quotes Arendt (*Directly stated*):

> The power of will lies in its unique ability to deal with things, “visibles and invisibles, that have never existed at all. Just as the past always presents itself to the mind in the guise of certainty, the future’s main characteristic is its basic uncertainty, no matter how high a degree of probability prediction may attain.”

Will, so defined, is bound to freedom by definition. Zuboff closes the Arendt account with the philosopher's own conclusion (*Directly stated*):

> “A will that is not free,” Arendt concludes, “is a contradiction in terms.”

Zuboff then glosses the borrowed term in her own voice as both organic and existential. Will is "the organ with which we summon our futures into existence"; Arendt's "metaphor of will as the 'mental organ of our future' suggests that it is something built into us: organic, intrinsic, inalienable" (*Directly stated*). And it is the bone structure of the promise: "The freedom of will is the existential bone structure that carries the moral flesh of every promise" (*Directly stated*).

The phrase **"will to will"** is Zuboff's own compression of the Arendtian faculty into an act. Her promise to finish the book "is the product of my will to will and a compass that steers my course toward a desired future that is not yet real"; if the imagined book "is to exist in the future, it must be because I will to will it so" (*Directly stated*). The doubling — willing one's willing — names the reflexive self-commitment by which an agent makes and keeps a promise, turning a prediction about the future into a fact.

## Context

Zuboff opens Chapter 11 ("The Right to the Future Tense") with two sections titled "I Will to Will" and "We Will to Will," staging the chapter's central claim before naming surveillance capitalism's threat to it. The Arendtian definition is the philosophical anchor: will is "the organ for the future" as memory is the organ for the past, and where memory meets only "objects," will meets "projects," things that are yet to be. With "freedom of will we undertake action that is entirely contingent on our determination to see our project through" — acts we "could have 'left undone' but for our commitment" (*Directly stated*).

The will to will grounds the chapter's headline right. "The assertion of freedom of will also asserts the right to the future tense as a condition of a fully human life," because "through my will I can influence the future… only over my piece of it" (*Directly stated*). Zuboff reinforces the Arendtian account with John Searle (whose "declaration" she treats in Chapter 6 — see [[Declaration (Searle via Zuboff)]]): Searle locates a "causal gap" between the reasons for an action and its enactment, and "the traditional name of this gap in philosophy is 'the freedom of the will'"; "the notion of making and keeping promises presupposes the gap" (*Directly stated*).

The strategic payoff is the diagnosis of [[The Uncontract]]. Surveillance capitalism's "uncontract" — Hal Varian's example of remotely disabling a car the moment a payment is missed — "abandons the human world of legally binding promises and substitutes instead the positivist calculations of automated machine processes," eliminating "the messy, unpredictable, untrustworthy eruptions of human will" (*Directly stated*). The will to will is precisely the faculty that [[Economies of Action]] and guaranteed outcomes aim to render irrelevant. In its absence, Zuboff writes, "the future collapses into an infinite present of mere behavior, in which there can be no subjects and no projects: only objects" (*Directly stated*). The definition thus does double duty: it names what is distinctively human (the self-authored future) and what surveillance capitalism threatens to extinguish ([[The Right to the Future Tense]]).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Arendt (origin) vs. Zuboff (application).** The definition of will as the "organ for the future" is Arendt's, quoted verbatim from her late philosophy of mind. Zuboff's contribution is to redeploy it as a *political-economic* category: the faculty that surveillance capitalism's prediction and action imperatives are built to bypass. Where Arendt theorized the will against a backdrop of totalitarian domination, Zuboff sets it against the new "instrumentarian" power that "aims… simply to author us and to profit from that authorship" (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Against the behaviorist reduction.** Zuboff explicitly defends the will to will against "the behaviorists' formulations of life as necessarily accidental and random, shaped by external stimuli beyond my knowledge or influence" (*Directly stated*). This is the direct contrary of B. F. Skinner's program, which the book elsewhere treats as the intellectual ancestor of [[Instrumentarian Power]].
- **Against Harari's "free will as fiction."** Harari argues that the liberal belief in a free, authoring will is a story science has dissolved (see [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]]). Zuboff takes the opposite stance: "I recognize my direct experience of freedom as an inviolate truth," and the centuries of debate over free will too often "silence our own declarations of will, as if we are embarrassed to assert this most fundamental human fact" (*Directly stated*). For Zuboff the will to will is not a comforting illusion but a "hard-won achievement" and a normative ground (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Against Harari's narrating-vs-experiencing self.** Where Harari decomposes the self into [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self]] to undercut the authoring "I," Zuboff treats the first-person author of the future as the irreducible unit: "This is how we claim our right to speak in the first person as the author of our futures" (*Directly stated*).
- **Searle's "gap" vs. Arendt's "organ."** Searle and Arendt arrive at the same place — freedom of will is the precondition of the promise — by different routes: Searle through the "causal gap" we "cannot shake off" even if it is an illusion, Arendt through the will as a built-in organic faculty. Zuboff folds both into one defense (*Strongly implied*).
- **Not the everyday "willpower."** Zuboff's will to will is not motivational grit but the reflexive, freedom-presupposing capacity to bind oneself to a future through a promise — "an anchor that girds me against the vagaries of my moods and temptations" (*Directly stated*).
