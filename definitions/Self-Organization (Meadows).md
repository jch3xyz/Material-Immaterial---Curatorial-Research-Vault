# Self-Organization — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows gives self-organization a single defining sentence, set off by a passage of examples that establishes its scope before naming it. She first describes the property — "The most marvelous characteristic of some complex systems is their ability to learn, diversify, complexify, evolve" — running through the fertilized ovum that generates "the incredible complexity of a mature frog, or chicken, or person," nature diversifying "millions of fantastic species out of a puddle of organic chemicals," and a society developing burning coal and specialized labor "into an automobile assembly plant, a city of skyscrapers, a worldwide network of communications." Then she names it:

> This capacity of a system to make its own structure more complex is called self-organization.

`Directly stated`

The definition has two load-bearing words. It is a capacity **of a system** — the source and the object of the action are the same system; nothing external imposes the new structure. And what the capacity produces is a structure **more complex** than before — self-organization is not maintenance or repetition but the elaboration, the *complexification*, of a system's own organization. `Directly stated`

In Chapter 6 Meadows supplies the definition's operational, cross-domain gloss, ranking self-organization as the fourth-most-powerful place to intervene in a system:

> 4. Self-Organization—The power to add, change, or evolve system structure

`Directly stated`

and she fixes its meaning by naming its aliases across fields:

> In biological systems that power is called evolution. In human economies it's called technical advance or social revolution. In systems lingo it's called self-organization.

`Directly stated`

Read together, the two passages define self-organization as **the power of a system to add to, change, or evolve its own structure so as to make itself more complex** — the systems-vocabulary name for what biology calls evolution and what human history calls technical advance or social revolution. `Directly stated`

## Context

The definition appears in Chapter 3, "Why Systems Work So Well," where Meadows names the three properties that make good systems robust: resilience, self-organization, and hierarchy. Self-organization is the middle and, for her, the most astonishing of the three. She introduces it to correct a blind spot — "we are… nearly blind to the property of self-organization" and "take it for granted" — and to argue that we "would do better at encouraging, rather than destroying, the self-organizing capacities of the systems of which we are a part." `Directly stated`

The definition carries two claims that give it its charge in the vault. First, its signature counterintuitive corollary: unbounded self-organizing complexity does not require a complex cause. "New discoveries," she writes, "suggest that just a few simple organizing principles can lead to wildly diverse self-organizing structures" — the Koch snowflake, the fractal-ruled fern, the four-letter genetic code. Second, its political edge: because self-organization "produces heterogeneity and unpredictability" and "requires freedom and experimentation, and a certain amount of disorder," it is "often sacrificed for purposes of short-term productivity and stability," and the conditions that encourage it "often can be scary for individuals and threatening to power structures." `Directly stated`

In Chapter 6's ranking of leverage points, the definition acquires its stakes for intervention. Self-organization is leverage point number four — "The power to add, change, or evolve system structure" — outranked only by a system's goals, its paradigm, and the power to transcend paradigms, and it is tied directly to survival: "The ability to self-organize is the strongest form of system resilience. A system that can evolve can survive almost any change, by changing itself." The definition thus does double duty: it names a property (Ch. 3) and a place to intervene (Ch. 6). `Directly stated`

- **vs. the concept [[Self-Organization]]** — that note holds the concept as it operates across the whole primer (the property, the leverage point, the "few simple organizing principles," the political suppression, the equivalence of evolution / technical advance / social revolution). This note pins the two verbatim *defining passages*. Companion notes, not duplicates. `Directly stated`
- **vs. [[Emergence]]** — kept **strictly distinct** per the vault's ingest rules. Meadows's self-organization is a *system's own restructuring of itself into greater complexity* (the source and object are the same system). Emergence is the *appearance of unanticipated higher-order pattern* at the level of the whole. In [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s reading of second- and third-wave [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]], self-organization is the "springboard to emergence" — the two are cross-linked, never collapsed. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Autopoiesis]]** ([[Humberto Maturana]] and [[Francisco Varela]], via [[N. Katherine Hayles]]) — also kept **distinct**. Autopoiesis is *self-production*: a system continually regenerating its own components to *conserve* its organization (organizational closure, homeostatic identity). Meadows's self-organization is the *complexification and evolution* of structure — by definition it *changes* the organization rather than merely maintaining it. Neighbors in the systems/cybernetics cluster, cross-linked. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Homeostasis]]** — the stability-preserving counterpart. Homeostasis holds a system at a set point; self-organization changes the set points and the structure itself. Meadows lists both among the reasons systems "work so well," but they pull in opposite temporal directions — one conserving, one evolving. `Strongly implied`
- **vs. [[Systems Theory]] / [[Cybernetics]] usage** — the term entered general systems theory and cybernetics (Meadows epigraphs the biologist Ludwig von Bertalanffy; Hayles tracks the term across the three waves of cybernetics) as a technical property of complex systems. Meadows works in that lineage's applied, ecological, management-facing wing: her definition is the plain-language operational one — a *capacity to complexify one's own structure* and a *leverage point for intervention* — rather than the formal cybernetic apparatus. This book GROUNDS the vault's systems/cybernetics cluster in its native discipline. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Jack Burnham]]'s "self-organization"** — Burnham lists "self-organization, growth… irritability" among the life-like characteristics a systems-based sculpture "may manifest," borrowing the word as an ontological marker for the object→system migration in art. Meadows's is the native-discipline definition: a general analytic property of complex systems, not a destination for the art object. `Interpretive synthesis`
