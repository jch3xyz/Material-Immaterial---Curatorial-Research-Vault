# Algorithm — Yuval Noah Harari

## Author's Definition

Harari defines an algorithm not as a particular computation but as the *method* a computation follows — a content-neutral procedure that can be carried by any substrate (mechanical gears, electric circuits, biochemistry, bureaucracy):

> An algorithm is a methodical set of steps that can be used to make calculations, resolve problems and reach decisions. An algorithm isn’t a particular calculation, but the method followed when making the calculation.

He frames this not as a technical aside but as a master-concept for the present:

> ‘Algorithm’ is arguably the single most important concept in our world.

`Directly stated` — both the definition and the status claim are quoted verbatim from *The Anthropocene*.

## Context

The definition is introduced in *The Anthropocene*, in the section "Organisms are Algorithms," at the precise moment Harari needs to cash out his claim that "emotions are biochemical algorithms that are vital for the survival and reproduction of all mammals." He pauses the argument to explain the term, flagging its reach forward: "this key concept will reappear in many of the following chapters, but also because the twenty-first century will be dominated by algorithms." The illustrations he chooses are deliberately substrate-spanning — the averaging procedure, the soup recipe, the beverage vending machine, and finally the human being and the baboon — so that "the man pressing the buttons and drinking the tea is also an algorithm," merely "a much more complicated algorithm than the vending machine." `Directly stated`

The definition is doing two distinct jobs across the book, and this note is the shared anchor underneath both:

1. **Biological reapplication** — in *The Anthropocene*, the definition grounds [[Organisms Are Algorithms]]: "What we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms," and "Humans are algorithms that produce not cups of tea, but copies of themselves." `Directly stated`
2. **Social reapplication** — in *The Storytellers*, Harari explicitly re-invokes the same definition ("we... defined it as a methodical set of steps...") to ground [[Society as Algorithm]]: "Writing has thus enabled humans to organise entire societies in an algorithmic fashion," so that "each person is only a small step in a huge algorithm, and it is the algorithm as a whole that makes the important decisions. This is the essence of bureaucracy." `Directly stated`

The single concept therefore bridges Harari's two scales — the body as calculator and the bureaucracy as calculator — which is why he elevates it to "the single most important concept in our world." `Strongly implied` — the bridging role is the structural function of the repeated, verbatim re-definition across the two chapters.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the everyday/computational sense.** Harari's definition is the standard textbook one (a method, not a particular calculation), but he weaponises its substrate-neutrality: because an algorithm is *only* the method, it can be embodied indifferently in silicon, flesh, or paper. The everyday word treats "algorithm" as something computers do; Harari treats computation as something algorithms do, and computers, organisms and bureaucracies as three of its carriers. `Interpretive synthesis`

- **vs. N. Katherine Hayles.** Hayles, across the vault's [[Pattern and Randomness]] and [[The Computational Universe]] notes, treats the move from material body to abstract pattern as a *contingent historical construction* to be resisted ("the disembodiment of information was contingent not inevitable"). Harari runs the identification in the other direction, taking "organisms are algorithms" as a working scientific consensus rather than a contestable narrative. Where Hayles asks what embodiment is lost when life is read as information, Harari mostly asserts the equivalence and builds on it. `Interpretive synthesis` — Hayles is not cited by Harari; this is a vault-internal contrast.

- **vs. Meghan O'Gieblyn.** O'Gieblyn's [[Algorithmic Determinism]] and [[The Algorithm as Providence]] treat the algorithmic worldview as a re-enchantment — a secular theology in which the algorithm inherits God's omniscience and providence. Harari's definition is the bare procedural kernel beneath that critique: O'Gieblyn diagnoses the *metaphor* ("the algorithm as providence") whose literal core is exactly Harari's "methodical set of steps." The two are complementary rather than opposed — Harari supplies the definition; O'Gieblyn supplies the theological reading of what gets done with it. `Interpretive synthesis` — O'Gieblyn does not cite Harari; vault-internal.
