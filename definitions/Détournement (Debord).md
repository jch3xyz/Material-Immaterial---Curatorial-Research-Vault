# Détournement — Guy Debord

## Author's Definition

Debord defines détournement negatively, by contrast with quotation, in thesis 208 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*:

> Derournement is the opposite of quotation, of appealing to a theoretical authority that is inevitably tainted by the very fact that it has become a quotation-a fragment torn from its own context and development, and ultimately from the general framework of its period and from the particular option (appropriate or erroneous) that it represented within that framework. Detournement is the flexible language of anti-ideology. It appears in communication that knows it cannot claim to embody any inherent or definitive certainty. It is language that cannot and need not be confirmed by any previous or supracritical reference. On the contrary, its own internal coherence and practical effectiveness are what validate the previous kernels of truth it has brought back into play. Derournement has grounded its cause on nothing but its own truth as present critique.

(The forms "Derournement" / "Detournement" reproduce the raw scan's rendering of *détournement*; the term is one and the same.)

The definition works in three moves. First, détournement is the **opposite of quotation**: where quotation defers to an external authority — and by that deference "taints" it, tearing a fragment loose from its own context and development — détournement refuses any such supracritical reference. Second, it is the **"flexible language of anti-ideology"**: the mode of communication proper to a critique that knows it "cannot claim to embody any inherent or definitive certainty." Third, its warrant is purely **immanent** — "its own internal coherence and practical effectiveness are what validate the previous kernels of truth it has brought back into play," so that détournement "has grounded its cause on nothing but its own truth as present critique." (`Directly stated` — thesis 208.)

## Context

The definition sits inside Chapter 8's account (theses 204–209) of the *style* a genuinely critical theory must adopt. Critical theory, Debord argues, "must communicate itself in its own language, the language of contradiction" (thesis 204); its "style, which includes a critique of itself, must express the domination of the present critique over its entire past" (thesis 206). It is at this point that Debord introduces détournement as the concrete technique of that self-critical, historical style.

The passage that precedes the definition anchors it in a lineage Debord himself détournes. In thesis 206 he cites "Hegel's characteristic practice of reversing the genitive" and "the young Marx, inspired by Feuerbach's systematic reversal of subject and predicate," who "answers 'the philosophy of poverty' with 'the poverty of philosophy,'" and states the operative function of the method: "Détournement reradicalizes previous critical conclusions that have been petrified into respectable truths and thus transformed into lies." Thesis 207 then adopts Lautréamont's provocation — "Plagiarism is necessary. Progress depends on it" — as a maxim of method. The definition in thesis 208 is thus the culmination of a two-thesis build-up: détournement is corrective reuse (not homage), self-validating (not authorized from outside), and directed at the re-radicalization of critical truths that recuperation has "petrified." (`Directly stated` — theses 206–208.)

In the architecture of the book, détournement is the linguistic and methodological counterpart to the negation of the [[Spectacle]]. The spectacle materializes ideology and freezes life into a separately contemplated image; détournement is "anti-ideology," the operation that wrenches already-existing material back out of its authorized meaning and returns it to living, present use. Debord's own text performs the method — a book stitched from détourned Hegel, Marx, and others, whose style "includes a critique of itself." (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Détournement vs. quotation.** The whole force of Debord's definition is the contrast captured in the vault's [[Détournement vs Quotation]] opposition. Quotation, for Debord, reproduces [[Separation]] — it tears a fragment from its development and enshrines it as authority; détournement de-separates, returning fragments to use and validating them only by "present critique." This is not the everyday sense in which quotation is neutral scholarly practice; Debord treats the citational gesture itself as ideologically compromised. (`Directly stated`.)
- **Against recuperation.** Détournement is defined in this vault as the inverse of [[Recuperation]]. Recuperation is the spectacle's capture of critical material into "respectable truths"; détournement is the counter-operation that "reradicalizes" what recuperation has petrified. The definition therefore has a temporal dimension absent from ordinary notions of appropriation: it is specifically the *reactivation of decayed critique*, a de-reification (see [[Reification]]) of thought that had hardened into a lie. (`Directly stated` for re-radicalization, thesis 206; `Interpretive synthesis` for the inverse-pairing.)
- **Against the vault's other appropriation practices.** The Situationist "plagiarism is necessary" stands in pointed tension with the ordinary sense of citation as fidelity to a source — and, self-referentially, with a provenance-first research method built on verbatim quotation. Debord's target is not accurate attribution as such but the *deference* that turns a source into an untouchable authority. Détournement keeps the borrowed words while stripping them of their sanctioned meaning; careful quotation preserves the meaning while marking the words as another's. The two are near-opposite orientations toward the same borrowed material. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
- **Note on the companion concept note.** This definition isolates Debord's verbatim thesis-208 formulation; the broader treatment of the practice — its lineage in Hegel and Marx, its relation to the abolition-and-realization of art, and the questions it raises — lives in the concept note [[Détournement]], on the same pattern as [[Information (Han)]] beside the concept `Information`.
