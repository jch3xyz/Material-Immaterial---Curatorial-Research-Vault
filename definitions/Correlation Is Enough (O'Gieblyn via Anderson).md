# Correlation Is Enough — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

"Correlation Is Enough" is not O'Gieblyn's coinage but a slogan she takes from the *Wired* editor Chris Anderson and makes load-bearing for her account of the [[The Black Box|black-box]] [[Algorithm|algorithm]]. It names the epistemic wager of "the end of theory": that at petabyte scale we should abandon causal models and hypotheses and simply trust the patterns that statistical machines extract from raw data. O'Gieblyn quotes Anderson's 2008 article verbatim (`Directly stated`):

> "Petabytes allow us to say: 'Correlation is enough,' " he wrote. "We can stop looking for models. We can analyze the data without hypotheses about what it might show. We can throw the numbers into the biggest computing clusters the world has ever seen and let statistical algorithms find patterns where science cannot."

The phrase compresses a complete inversion of the scientific method. Where the Enlightenment held that "more empirical evidence would yield more knowledge," Anderson argues that information is now produced on a scale that "could not be 'visualized in its totality,'" so that "no human mind could make sense of it." The remedy is not better theory but the abandonment of theory: "when you have data on this scale, you no longer need a theory at all. You can simply feed the numbers into algorithms and let them make predictions based on the patterns and relationships they notice." Google Translate, O'Gieblyn notes, "'learned' to translate English to French simply by scanning Canadian documents that contained both languages, even though the algorithm has no model that understands either language" — Peter Norvig "once boasted that not a single person who worked on the Chinese translation program spoke Chinese."

The decisive consequence, for O'Gieblyn, is the price the slogan exacts. Data "can't tell us why something happens," and Anderson's response is to treat the question *why* as obsolete: "maybe our need to know why was misguided." Deep learning fulfills the wager exactly. These algorithms, she writes, "have fulfilled Anderson's call for objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension. To obtain the superior knowledge the machines possess, we must give up our desire to know 'why' and accept their outputs as pure revelation." "Correlation is enough" is therefore the formula by which *understanding* — comprehension, the answer to *why* — is reclassified as an anthropocentric luxury the machine can elide.

## Context

O'Gieblyn opens the "Algorithm" chapter with Anderson because his essay names, in a "blunt, well-circulated" form, the trade the rest of the book interrogates. "Correlation is enough" is the hinge between two of her central concepts. On one side it expresses the metaphysics of [[The Computational Universe]]: a world handed to algorithms as raw data and read back as prediction, with no model in between. On the other it terminates in [[The Black Box]] — the deep-learning system whose "internal nodes... correspond to [no] terms in human language," whose verdicts are "no more reducible to explanation than the human brain." The slogan is the *epistemology* of the black box: it licenses, in advance, a knowledge that works without being understood.

The phrase also drives O'Gieblyn's argument that "'understanding,' like meaning, is an anthropocentric concept — one that information technologies were deliberately built to elide." She reads the engineering of [[Information]] after Shannon as having severed signal from meaning by design, so that an algorithm built only to find correlations is the consistent endpoint of that severance. It "knows" only [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] — "even algorithms that are preternaturally good at recognizing, say, dogs in a photo have no idea what a dog actually is." This is why she frames the AlphaGo move "that's not a human move" as the slogan made visible: a correlation strong enough to win and "totally unlike human reason."

O'Gieblyn does not endorse the wager; she dramatizes its cost. She marshals the objections — Jaron Lanier's "science is about understanding," the observation that "the simple fact that there is a correlation between two things does not explain anything" — only to "feel the futility of such objections" against a technical logic that has already "seeped into the real world we inhabit." She then folds the whole development into her larger thesis on [[Disenchantment]]: David Weinberger's reading of prediction-without-explanation as "a return to a premodern epistemology," James Bridle's "New Dark Age," and Kelly Clancy's image of subjects "forced to accept the decisions of our algorithms blindly, like Job accepting his punishment." "Correlation is enough" is, in this arc, the modern world's quiet surrender of the question *why* — the conceptual seed of [[The Algorithm as Providence|the algorithm as a fate-decreeing god]].

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. Chris Anderson (the source).** Anderson advances "correlation is enough" as a *prescription* — an enthusiastic call to "stop trying to understand the world and instead trust the wisdom of algorithms." O'Gieblyn quotes him faithfully but reads the slogan against the grain, as a *diagnosis*: the moment human comprehension is priced out of knowledge and *why* becomes a discardable, "anthropocentric" want. She treats his flamboyance as symptom rather than argument (`Directly stated` for the quotation; `Interpretive synthesis` for the reframing as a cost rather than a triumph).
- **vs. [[N. Katherine Hayles]].** Hayles never uses Anderson's phrase, but her account of [[Information]] as having "lost its body" — meaning stripped from signal so that a pattern can travel without a referent — is the precondition "correlation is enough" cashes out. Hayles diagnoses the contingent severance of information from [[Embodiment]]; O'Gieblyn shows the operational endpoint, a knowledge machine that needs no model, no body, and no meaning. The connection is O'Gieblyn's-via-the-vault (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **vs. the everyday statistical maxim "correlation is not causation."** The folk warning treats correlation as *insufficient* — a placeholder until a causal mechanism is found. Anderson's slogan inverts the maxim into a doctrine: at sufficient scale, correlation does not await causation but *replaces* it. O'Gieblyn's interest is precisely in this inversion, and in what is lost when the demand for a mechanism — the scientist's *why* — is declared unnecessary (`Directly stated`).
- **vs. the vault's [[The Black Box]] and [[The Computational Universe]] concept notes.** This is the *definitional* anchor for one verbatim phrase and the epistemic claim it carries; those concept notes synthesize the broader machinery (the opacity of the model; the data-metaphysics of the world). The `(O'Gieblyn via Anderson)` qualifier marks a real definition note attributing the phrase to its originator, not a variant of either concept.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Black Box]] — "correlation is enough" is the epistemology of the black box; the slogan licenses the model whose internal logic is, by design, beyond explanation.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the metaphysics that lets the world be fed to algorithms as raw data and read back as prediction "without hypotheses about what it might show."
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — the algorithm "knows" only correlations and patterns, having been built to "find patterns where science cannot."
- [[Information]] — the slogan is the endpoint of information theory's deliberate elision of meaning; comprehension becomes "an anthropocentric concept... information technologies were deliberately built to elide."
- [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — abandoning *why* and accepting outputs "as pure revelation" is the conceptual seed of the algorithm recast as an inscrutable, fate-decreeing god.
- [[Disenchantment]] — prediction without explanation is, for O'Gieblyn (via Weinberger and Bridle), the disenchanted world's return to "revelation without true understanding."
- [[Mind as Machine]] — once "the human brain is also a black box," it appears "just as reasonable to trust the opaque logic of an algorithm as it is to trust our own minds."
- [[Posthumanism]] — the price of "correlation is enough" is the demotion of *understanding* as a merely human value the machine can do without.
