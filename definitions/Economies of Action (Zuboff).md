# Economies of Action — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

[[Shoshana Zuboff]] coins the term as the third and most aggressive of the three "economies" that drive surveillance capitalism's hunt for predictive certainty — after economies of scale and [[Economies of Scope]]. The keystone definitional sentence isolates economies of action by their distinguishing move: they do not merely extract behavior, they reach into the world and *change* it (*Directly stated*, Ch.7):

> The processes invented to achieve this goal are what I call economies of action. In order to achieve these economies, machine processes are configured to intervene in the state of play in the real world among real people and things.

Zuboff immediately fixes the purpose and the verbs (*Directly stated*, Ch.7):

> These interventions are designed to enhance certainty by doing things: they nudge, tune, herd, manipulate, and modify behavior in specific directions by executing actions as subtle as inserting a specific phrase into your Facebook news feed, timing the appearance of a BUY button on your phone, or shutting down your car engine when an insurance payment is late.

So economies of action are, exactly: **machine processes configured to intervene in the state of play in the real world among real people and things, in order to enhance the certainty of behavioral predictions by *doing* things — nudging, tuning, herding, manipulating, and modifying behavior toward guaranteed outcomes.** Where the earlier economies make prediction *vast and varied*, economies of action make it *certain*, by shaping the very behavior being predicted at its source.

## Context

The definition is minted at the opening of Chapter 7, "The Reality Business," as the answer to what Zuboff calls the [[The Prediction Imperative|prediction imperative]]: the competitive pressure to supply "the highest quality of prediction products able to sustain competitive advantage in the new markets for future behavior." She states the underlying logic plainly — "Behavioral surplus must be vast and varied, but the surest way to predict behavior is to intervene at its source and shape it" (*Directly stated*, Ch.7). Economies of scale and [[Economies of Scope]] widen and deepen the extraction of [[Behavioral Surplus]]; economies of action close the remaining gap between prediction and reality by acting on the world rather than only observing it. This is the chapter where surveillance capitalism crosses from knowing to intervening — from a "reality *learning*" business to a "reality *business*."

Zuboff grounds the term genealogically in the work of biologist Donald MacKay on animal telemetry, whose "reverse process" of telestimulation she reads as the template for the new phase (*Directly stated*, Ch.7):

> His "reverse process" of telestimulation is resurrected in the economies of action that automatically stimulate behavior, not to save the human herd from catastrophe but rather to heighten the predictability of its behavior.

The chapter's recurring image makes the active, coercive character explicit. Where telematics were once meant only to render the world legible, economies of action turn them into instruments of force (*Directly stated*, Ch.7):

> Telematics are not intended merely to know but also to do (economies of action). They are hammers; they are muscular; they enforce.

Economies of action are the operational core of what Zuboff later names [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the apparatus that "leverages proprietary behavioral surplus to preempt and foreclose action alternatives, thus replacing the indeterminacy of social processes with the determinism of programmed machine processes." Her automobile-insurance case study shows the mechanism end to end: behavioral data drawn from drivers "return to the drivers, executing procedures to interrupt and shape behavior in order to enhance the certainty, and therefore profitability, of predictions (economies of action)," via the asymmetric instrument she calls [[The Uncontract]] (*Directly stated*, Ch.7). The aim is not the automation of society but, in her sharp formulation, "the replacement of society with machine action dictated by economic imperatives." Economies of action thus connect [[Behavioral Surplus]] to guaranteed [[Prediction Products]] sold in [[Behavioral Futures Markets]], and supply the executional muscle of [[Instrumentarian Power]] and its operating architecture, [[Big Other]].

This note isolates the explicit definitional passages; the cross-chapter and cross-author development of the idea lives in the concept note [[Economies of Action]], following the `Information` / `Information (Han)` precedent.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Distinct from economies of scale and [[Economies of Scope]] (the other two "economies").** Scale concerns the *volume* of surplus extracted; scope concerns its *extension and depth* (more surfaces, more intimate detail). Economies of action are categorically different in *kind*, not degree: they are not about acquiring more data but about *acting on the world* to make behavior conform to prediction. Zuboff presents the three as a sequence in which each becomes "necessary but insufficient" for the next, culminating in action (*Directly stated*, Ch.7).
- **Sharper than the "nudge" of behavioral economics.** Zuboff borrows the vocabulary of nudging but radicalizes it: where Thaler-and-Sunstein nudges are framed as choice architecture for the chooser's own benefit, economies of action are tuned, herded, and at the limit coercive interventions ("engine lockdowns," "real-time rate hikes," "financial penalties, curfews") executed for the profit of self-authorizing commercial actors, "outside the driver's awareness while she still thinks that she is free" (*Strongly implied*; the coercion instances and the awareness claim are *Directly stated*).
- **Not Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] or [[Psychopolitics]].** Han's psychopolitics works on the subject from the inside, through seduction and voluntary self-exposure — the subject exploits itself. Economies of action work on the world from the outside, through machine processes that "intervene in the state of play... among real people and things." Han names a phenomenology of complicity; Zuboff names an externalized, executional architecture of behavioral enforcement (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Beyond surveillance as watching.** Conventional senses of [[Surveillance]] — and Foucault's disciplinary gaze — turn on visibility: the watched internalize control because they might be seen. Economies of action mark Zuboff's break from the optical model: this is surveillance that does not merely observe and discipline but actively reaches in and modifies. "They are hammers; they are muscular; they enforce" (*Interpretive synthesis*; the quotation is *Directly stated*).
