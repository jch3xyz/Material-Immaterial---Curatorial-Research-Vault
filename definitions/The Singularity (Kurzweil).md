# The Singularity — Ray Kurzweil

## Author's Definition

[[Ray Kurzweil]] gives the term its primary, proponent's definition in the first chapter of [[The Singularity Is Near]]. Posing the question outright — "What, then, is the Singularity?" — he answers in a single sentence:

> It's a future period during which the pace of technological change will be so rapid, its impact so deep, that human life will be irreversibly transformed.

He is careful to add that this epoch is "neither utopian nor dystopian," but one that "will transform the concepts that we rely on to give meaning to our lives, from our business models to the cycle of human life, including death itself." In the same passage he defines the term for the person who holds the view:

> I regard someone who understands the Singularity and who has reflected on its implications for his or her own life as a "singularitarian."

Later in the chapter Kurzweil supplies the structural definition — the Singularity as the merger of human and machine intelligence: "The Singularity will represent the culmination of the merger of our biological thinking and existence with our technology, resulting in a world that is still human but that transcends our biological roots. There will be no distinction, post-Singularity, between human and machine or between physical and virtual reality." He also names its mathematical-astrophysical pedigree (the term "adopted by mathematicians to denote a value that transcends any finite limitation," and by astrophysicists for the black-hole singularity that "constitutes a rupture in the fabric of space and time"), while insisting that, properly understood, "from a mathematical perspective, there is no discontinuity, no rupture, and the growth rates remain finite, although extraordinarily large."

The definition has an **operational, dated form**. In Chapter 3 Kurzweil compresses the same concept into a quantitative threshold and fixes its year:

> I set the date for the Singularity—representing a profound and disruptive transformation in human capability—as 2045.

He ties the date to a price-performance milestone: by the mid-2040s "one thousand dollars' worth of computation will be equal to 1026 cps, so the intelligence created per year (at a total cost of about $10 12) will be about one billion times more powerful than all human intelligence today" — the raw text renders the exponents as `1026` (i.e. 10^26) and `10 12` (i.e. 10^12). A still more compact gloss appears in the same chapter, defining the Singularity by its magnitude: "an expansion of human intelligence by a factor of trillions through merger with its nonbiological form." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

## Context

This is the keystone definition of [[The Singularity Is Near]] — the term the entire book exists to develop, document, and defend. Kurzweil places it in the foundational first chapter ("The Six Epochs"), immediately after describing the Singularity as "a transforming event looming in the first half of the twenty-first century" that, "just as a black hole in space dramatically alters the patterns of matter and energy accelerating toward its event horizon," is "increasingly transforming every institution and aspect of human life, from sexuality to spirituality." The definition is the hinge between two larger structures of the book:

- The engine — [[The Law of Accelerating Returns]]. The Singularity follows from Kurzweil's claim that technological (and, before it, biological) evolution proceeds at an *exponential*, indeed "double exponential," rate, so that "the key idea underlying the impending Singularity is that the pace of change of our human-created technology is accelerating and its powers are expanding at an exponential pace." The whole rhetoric of the chapter — the lily-pad parable, the "knee of the curve," Kasparov, the genome, the Internet — is designed to convert the reader from the "intuitive linear" view of history to the "historical exponential" view that makes the Singularity legible.
- The cosmology — [[The Six Epochs]]. The Singularity is dated *within* Kurzweil's master narrative of evolution as six stages of "patterns of increasing order": it "will begin with Epoch Five" (the merger of human technology with human intelligence) "and will spread from Earth to the rest of the universe in Epoch Six" (the "universe wakes up"). Its endpoint is the saturation of all matter with intelligence — "the 'dumb' matter and mechanisms of the universe will be transformed into exquisitely sublime forms of intelligence" — which Kurzweil calls "the ultimate destiny of the Singularity and of the universe."

The Chapter 3 material grounds the dated form in hardware: Kurzweil's order-of-magnitude estimates that functional emulation of the human brain requires on the order of 10^16 cps, that "it is reasonable to expect human brain capacity, at least in terms of hardware computational capacity, for one thousand dollars by around 2020," and that by 2045 a thousand dollars of computation reaches 10^26 cps. The definition is thus deliberately *engineered*: a metaphysical claim (human life "irreversibly transformed," intelligence merging with the universe) welded to a falsifiable-looking forecast (a year, a dollar figure, a calculations-per-second figure). The Singularity's defining quantitative promise is that "by the end of this century, the nonbiological portion of our intelligence will be trillions of trillions of times more powerful than unaided human intelligence." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

For Kurzweil the term is fundamentally affirmative. Against "many commentators" who "focus on what they perceive as a loss of some vital aspect of our humanity," he insists the Singularity's "most important implication is this: our technology will match and then vastly exceed the refinement and suppleness of what we regard as the best of human traits," and that "we will transcend biology, but not our humanity." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus [[The Singularity (O'Gieblyn)]] — the proponent's primary definition vs. the critic's secondhand reading.** This note must not be collapsed into the existing [[The Singularity (O'Gieblyn)]] definition. They name the same forecast from opposite positions. Kurzweil's is the *primary source*, stated in the first person by the term's chief proponent, affirmative in valence ("neither utopian nor dystopian," "we will transcend biology, but not our humanity"). [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]'s is a *secondhand, critical-eschatological* reading that redescribes the same threshold as "academic prophecy," a secularized Christian Apocalypse "dated to a year," whose "rather depressing gospel message" is that "the only way we can survive the Singularity is to become machines ourselves." The two notes share the date (2045) and the content (matter saturated with intelligence) but disagree entirely on its meaning; the `(Kurzweil)` and `(O'Gieblyn)` suffixes mark this proponent/critic split. (Confidence: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

- **Versus Vinge's and Good's "technological singularity."** Kurzweil situates his term against precursors he quotes directly: John von Neumann's report that accelerating progress "gives the appearance of approaching some essential singularity in the history of the race beyond which human affairs, as we know them, could not continue"; I. J. Good's "intelligence explosion" from "ultraintelligent" machines; and Vernor Vinge's 1993 "technological singularity" as a "runaway phenomenon … beyond any hope of control" driven by "entities with greater than human intelligence." Kurzweil accepts the lineage but *softens the rupture*: where Vinge sees "a fundamental discontinuity … the likes of which I can't even begin to predict," Kurzweil insists "there is no discontinuity, no rupture, and the growth rates remain finite," and that "we do have sufficient powers of abstraction to make meaningful statements about the nature of life after the Singularity." His Singularity is a continuation, not a wall. (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

- **Versus the philosophical/astrophysical "singularity."** Kurzweil explicitly distinguishes his usage from the source senses he borrows the word from: the mathematical singularity ("a value that transcends any finite limitation," as in $y = 1/x$ as $x \to 0$) and the astrophysical singularity at the center of a black hole ("apparently zero volume and infinite density," "a rupture in the fabric of space and time"). His Singularity is *named after* these — it "appears to be an acute and abrupt break in the continuity of progress" only "from our currently limited framework" — but is, on his own account, finite and continuous. This note's term must also not be confused with the unrelated sense of *singularity* as singleness/uniqueness that appears elsewhere in the vault's Han cluster. (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

- **Versus Han's [[Acceleration]] — a false friend.** Kurzweil's exponential acceleration (celebratory, mathematical, the engine of redemption) is the opposite in valence of [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s [[Acceleration]] (a diagnosis of pathology — speed stripped of narrative sense and direction). The same word names contrary stances; the two are a deliberate contrast, not a synonym. (Confidence: `Interpretive synthesis`.)
