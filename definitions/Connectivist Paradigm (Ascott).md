# Connectivist Paradigm — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

For Ascott, the **connectivist paradigm** is the metaphysical principle of total interconnection that underwrites his whole telematic project: the doctrine that everything is connected and that everything mutually affects everything else. He states it as a worldview shared across science and "second-order nature" (`Directly stated`):

> Second-order cybernetics and secondorder nature share in what can be called the connectivist paradigm, which holds that everything is connected, everything interacts with and affects everything else.

He immediately glosses the paradigm as a vision of *undivided wholeness* and *non-local interaction* (`Directly stated`):

> This connectedness, this undivided wholeness, unmediated action-at-adistance, capable of transcending the laws of space and time with non-local interaction, is reflected in the telematic environment of computer-mediated networks of data transfer, interactive videoconferencing, remote sensing, and telerobotics, where communication can also in a sense be "non-local" and asynchronous, although in different ways and with different outcomes.

The single most important move in the definition is the *fork* Ascott runs from the one paradigm into two named practices — one in science, one in art (`Directly stated`):

> The connectivist paradigm thus embraces both connectionism in science and what it is appropriate to call connectivism in art.

Connectionism, in his usage, is the scientific side: "These explorations into the microstructure of cognition, known as connectionism, parallel distributed processing, or neural networks, have developed out of a re-evaluation of some of the earliest ideas of cybernetics, of neural nets and the Perceptron, rejecting the linear, symbolic systems of the old AI." [[Connectivism]] is its artistic counterpart. Both are expressions of the one paradigm, not synonyms (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Ascott states the connectivist paradigm in "Back to Nature II: Art and Technology in the Twenty-First Century" (ch. 28), the chapter where he argues that "the logical outcome of the immateriality of the information society is the reinstatement in the twenty-first century of a natural materiality" — a "second-order nature, emergent nature, [[Nature II]]." The paradigm is the conceptual bridge that lets him claim that immersion in electronic space "can bring us back to nature": if everything is connected, then the most artificial of technologies (electronic and molecular) are not a departure from nature but a deeper participation in it.

The paradigm is sourced, for Ascott, from the convergence of two histories. The first is the history of cybernetics: he notes that cybernetics found itself "inadequate to the study of such systems until it included the observer of a system as a part of the system itself. Thus was born second-order cybernetics," which recognized all boundaries as "fuzzy, permeable, uncertain, and transformable." The connectivist paradigm generalizes this observer-implicating wholeness from the laboratory to "the social, cultural, or even aesthetic level of experience." The second is the lineage Ascott himself names as authority for the principle: "an idea as old, as traditional as oriental religions and as new as the quantum physics of David Bohm, John Stewart Bell, and Alain Aspect" — and, later in the chapter, Rupert Sheldrake's "morphic resonance" and James Lovelock's Gaia hypothesis. The connectivist paradigm is thus the load-bearing premise beneath Ascott's [[Re-enchantment|re-enchanted]] horizon: a "rematerialization of art" that marries the immaterial (telematic networks) and the material (nanotechnology, molecular engineering, [[Artificial Life]]) into "an holistic environment of mind and matter, as much spiritual as material."

The paradigm also does polemical work. Much computer art, Ascott charges, fails to honour it: "this work is not connectivist in its attitudes, and it is not telematic in its use of media," because "the computer is still too much contained, finitely located, isolated as a black box." The connectivist paradigm is therefore not only a description of how the world is, but a *normative* test of whether a given art participates in that interconnection or merely "replays" older, isolated forms.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Paradigm vs. practice (Ascott's own vocabulary).** This note is the *definition* of the principle; the concept note [[Connectivism]] holds the broader practice — including Ascott's second, terminological sense in which "connectivism" is his proposed *replacement for the word "art."* The connectivist paradigm names the metaphysics ("everything is connected"); connectivism (in art) is one of the two children that paradigm "embraces." Do not collapse the paradigm into the art-term. (`Directly stated`)

- **Connectivism (art) vs. connectionism (science).** Ascott insists the paradigm "embraces both connectionism in science and... connectivism in art." Connectionism — the neural-net / parallel-distributed-processing modeling of cognition — is the paradigm's scientific expression; connectivism is its artistic one. They are siblings, not the same thing, and connectionism is not a vault note (it renders here as plain text). (`Directly stated`)

- **Versus cybernetic interconnection in [[Cybernetics]] / [[Systems Theory]].** The connectivist paradigm grows out of *second-order* cybernetics (the observer included in the system), but Ascott pushes it past the descriptive systems-thinking of Wiener or Burnham into a frankly metaphysical and spiritual register — "as old, as traditional as oriental religions," "undivided wholeness," morphic resonance, Gaia. Where Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]] keeps interconnection as an *aesthetic of process and relation*, Ascott's paradigm asserts interconnection as a *cosmology*. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

- **Versus the surveillance reading of total connection.** What Han diagnoses as the [[Digital Panopticon|digital panopticon]] — pervasive connectivity as control — Ascott reads in the opposite key: the same all-connectedness is "undivided wholeness," the ground of [[Network Consciousness]] and planetary transformation. The connectivist paradigm is, for him, a liberation, not a capture. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
