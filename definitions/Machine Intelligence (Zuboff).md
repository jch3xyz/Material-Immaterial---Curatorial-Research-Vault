# Machine Intelligence — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

For Zuboff, "machine intelligence" is a deliberately chosen **umbrella phrase**, not the name of any single technology. It designates the entire complex of computational systems by which raw behavioral material is converted into predictions. She introduces it as a gloss on the "Analysis" term in Hal Varian's account of big data:

> "Analysis" refers to the complex of highly specialized computational systems that I will generally refer to in these chapters as "machine intelligence." I like this umbrella phrase because it trains us on the forest rather than the trees, helping us decenter from technology to its objectives.

The phrase deliberately gathers under one heading what the industry splinters into separate jargon — "machine learning," "classical" algorithmic production, "predictive analytics," and "artificial intelligence" (`Directly stated`). She borrows it from Google itself, which describes itself "at the forefront of innovation in machine intelligence," a term in which it includes machine learning, classical algorithmic production, and operations "often referred to with other terms such as 'predictive analytics' or 'artificial intelligence'" — spanning language translation, speech recognition, visual processing, ranking, statistical modeling, and prediction (`Directly stated`).

In the architecture of [[Surveillance Capitalism]], machine intelligence is the **means of production**: "Its specific technologies and techniques, which I summarize as 'machine intelligence,' are constantly evolving" (`Directly stated`). It is the machinery that takes in [[Behavioral Surplus]] and outputs [[Prediction Products]] — "Machine intelligence processes behavioral surplus into prediction products designed to forecast what we will feel, think, and do: now, soon, and later" (`Directly stated`).

The defining trait Zuboff stresses is its appetite. Machine intelligence "feed[s] on behavioral surplus, and the more surplus they consume, the more accurate the prediction products that result." It is "the ultimate tapeworm," whose "intelligence depends upon how much data it eats" — and so it "reaches its full potential for quality only as it approximates totality" (`Directly stated`). This hunger is what links the *concept* to the *imperative*: machine intelligence is the technical engine that makes [[The Extraction Imperative]] economically rational.

## Context

The definition appears in Chapter 3, "The Discovery of Behavioral Surplus," at the moment Zuboff is reverse-engineering Varian's three-part scheme — Data, Extraction, Analysis. Having named "Data" as the raw material and "Extraction" as the social relations of supply, she fixes "Analysis" under her own umbrella phrase so that the reader keeps attention on the *objective* (prediction for profit) rather than the dazzle of any particular algorithm.

The rhetorical work of the phrase is anti-mystification. By refusing to settle on "AI" — "a moving target, as capabilities have evolved from primitive programs that can play tic-tac-toe to systems that can operate whole fleets of driverless cars" — Zuboff prevents the technology from becoming an alibi. Machine intelligence is presented not as autonomous progress but as a **specifically constructed human choice** harnessed to a logic of accumulation; its "inscrutability and exclusivity" are "the moat that surrounds the castle" (`Strongly implied` / `Directly stated`). The term thus functions inside her larger argument that surveillance capitalism is a *market form*, not a *technology*: the machinery is real and formidable, but it is the servant of objectives, not their author.

This definition note is the disambiguating companion to the concept note [[Machine Intelligence]], which sets the term in its full conceptual cluster (surplus → machine intelligence → prediction products → behavioral futures markets).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus "artificial intelligence" as commonly used.** Where most authors in the vault treat "AI" or "machine intelligence" as a *capability* of the machine, Zuboff treats it as an *economic function*. Her phrase is chosen precisely to "decenter from technology to its objectives." It is closer to a name for a means of production than to a claim about cognition.
- **Versus [[Strong AI]] (Kurzweil) and the consciousness debate.** Ray Kurzweil's "machine intelligence" is teleological — intelligence on an exponential path toward human-level and superhuman cognition, and ultimately a waking universe. Zuboff brackets that question entirely; for her the only thing that matters about machine intelligence is its accuracy as a predictor of human behavior. She is indifferent to whether it "understands," asking only how much surplus it eats and how close to "totality" it can approximate (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **Versus Harari's [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] / [[Dataism]].** Harari's Dataism universalizes algorithmic processing as the substrate of all life and posits intelligence decoupling from consciousness. Zuboff narrows the lens to a *commercial* machine intelligence whose purpose is the manufacture of prediction products sold in [[Behavioral Futures Markets]]; the metaphysics of algorithms is not her concern, the political economy of them is (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **Versus Han's [[Big Data]] / [[Dataism]] and [[Smart Power]].** Han diagnoses big data and dataism as a psychopolitical regime that reads and steers the soul. Zuboff supplies the missing *economic mechanism*: machine intelligence is what converts the surplus that big-data extraction yields into salable predictions. The two accounts are complementary — Han names the form of power, Zuboff names the manufacturing process beneath it (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **Versus [[Surveillance]] and the [[Quantified Self]].** Machine intelligence is the analytic layer that makes surveillance economically productive: it is what the captured data of the quantified, surveilled subject is *for*. Surveillance supplies; machine intelligence analyzes; prediction products are sold (`Strongly implied`).
