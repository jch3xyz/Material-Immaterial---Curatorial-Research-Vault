# Radical Indifference — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Radical indifference is the distinctive **way of knowing** cultivated by [[Instrumentarian Power]]: a regime of observation that reduces human experience to measurable, observable behavior while remaining steadfastly indifferent to the *meaning* of that experience. Zuboff names it directly and verbatim:

> Instrumentarian power cultivates an unusual “way of knowing” that combines the “formal indifference” of the neoliberal worldview with the observational perspective of radical behaviorism (see Figure 4 here). Thanks to Big Other’s capabilities, instrumentarian power reduces human experience to measurable observable behavior while remaining steadfastly indifferent to the meaning of that experience. I call this new way of knowing radical indifference. It is a form of observation without witness...

(`Directly stated` — this is the explicit coinage and naming passage.)

The term fuses two existing strands into something new: (1) the **"formal indifference" of the neoliberal worldview** and (2) the **observational perspective of radical behaviorism** (Zuboff's B. F. Skinner lineage). The result is "a form of observation without witness" — surveillance that watches but bears no relation to, and takes no responsibility for, the one observed.

## Context

Radical indifference appears in Chapter 13, where Zuboff names the surveillance-capitalist apparatus [[Big Other]] and the new species of power it produces, [[Instrumentarian Power]]. Radical indifference is the *epistemology* of that power — its characteristic stance toward the human. It is "operationalized in Big Other's dehumanized methods of evaluation that produce equivalence without equality," reducing individuals "to the lowest common denominator of sameness—an organism among organisms." From this vantage the human is not a subject but an **"Other-One": an organism that behaves**, a source of [[Behavioral Surplus]] to be observed, rendered, datafied, and instrumentalized. (`Directly stated`.)

Crucially, radical indifference is *not* malice. Zuboff contrasts it explicitly with the "intimate violent political religion" of totalitarianism: instead of the domination of the soul through terror, Big Other "does not care what we think, feel, or do as long as" it can harvest the behavioral surplus generated by connection. The havoc it produces is therefore of "an utterly different signature": "the remote and abstracted contempt of impenetrably complex systems," a "robotized veil of abstraction" across which social relations and economic exchange now occur. (`Directly stated`.)

This indifference is what lets surveillance capitalism evade moral and political resistance: because it does not claim our bodies "for some grotesque regime of pain and murder, we are prone to undervalue its effects and lower our guard." Radical indifference is thus both an account of *how* the system knows and an account of *why* it remains so hard to see and oppose. It is the observational complement to [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — the system observes without witnessing precisely so that it can predict and shape behavior toward guaranteed outcomes. (`Strongly implied`.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the Panopticon / [[Digital Panopticon]] (Bentham, Foucault, Han).** The disciplinary panopticon, and Han's digital panopticon, still depend on the *internalization of the gaze* — the watched subject is meant to feel watched and to conform. Radical indifference is the obverse: Big Other does *not* require conformity, internalization, or even awareness ("There is no need for mass submission to social norms"). It is "observation without witness" — the watcher is indifferent to whether the watched knows or cares. Where Han's panopticon runs on *voluntary self-exposure* before a felt gaze, Zuboff's radical indifference runs on a gaze that withholds recognition altogether. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
- **vs. Surveillance as moral relation.** The everyday and earlier-modern senses of surveillance imply a watcher who is *interested* in the watched — a guard, a state, a jealous god. Zuboff's coinage names a surveillance stripped of that relational interest: the watcher is indifferent to meaning, recognizing only behavior. (`Directly stated`.)
- **vs. behaviorism alone (Skinner).** Radical behaviorism supplies the *observational perspective* (the human as organism that behaves), but Zuboff insists radical indifference is more than behaviorism: it adds the **neoliberal "formal indifference"** that converts observed behavior into surplus for markets. It is the *combination* — behaviorist observation operationalized for accumulation through [[Big Other]] — that is new. (`Directly stated`.)
- **vs. Dataism (Harari) / [[Big Data]] (Han).** Dataism and Big Data describe the reduction of experience to data and the worship of information flows; radical indifference names the specific *moral-epistemic stance* that makes such reduction possible without remorse — observation that abandons the meaning lodged "in our bodies, our brains, and our beating hearts," leaving "the abandoned carcass." (`Interpretive synthesis`.)
