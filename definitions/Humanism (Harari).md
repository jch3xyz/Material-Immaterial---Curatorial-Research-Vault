# Humanism — Yuval Noah Harari

## Author's Definition

For Harari, **humanism is the worship of humankind** — a modern creed and a *religion* in which the human being takes the place that God once held. He names it directly in chapter 1: "humanism – the worship of humankind" (`Directly stated`). In chapter 9 he gives the operative core of the definition: humanism is the doctrine in which **human experience is the supreme source of authority and meaning**:

> All humanist sects believe that human experience is the supreme source of authority and meaning, yet they interpret human experience in different ways.

Where traditional religions located meaning and authority in a great cosmic plan or a superhuman source, humanism *reverses* the relationship: humans must draw meaning out of their own inner experiences and project it onto an otherwise meaningless cosmos (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Harari introduces humanism as the answer to a specific modern problem. Under what he calls the modern deal, humans renounce belief in a great cosmic plan in exchange for power — but order cannot survive without meaning. Humanism is "the antidote to a meaningless and lawless existence," "a revolutionary new creed that conquered the world during the last few centuries" (`Directly stated`). It "worships humanity, and expects humanity to play the part that God played in Christianity and Islam, and that the laws of nature played in Buddhism and Daoism."

The definition is therefore structural, not sentimental: humanism is the source-relocation of authority "from the sky to human feelings." Harari illustrates the shift across five domains — politics ("the voter knows best"), economics ("the customer is always right"), aesthetics ("beauty is in the eye of the beholder"), ethics ("if it feels good – do it"), and education ("think for yourself") (`Directly stated`).

This definition note is the crisp anchor for the broader concept note [[Humanism (Harari)]] and for its three sects. Harari is explicit that humanism is not monolithic: it "split into three main branches" — liberal humanism (the "orthodox" branch, locating authority in the individual's inner voice), socialist humanism, and evolutionary humanism (whose advocates included the Nazis) — all of which share the supreme premise that human experience is the source of authority and meaning while interpreting that experience differently (`Directly stated`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Against the everyday "humanitarian" sense.** Harari's "humanism" is not benevolence toward humans; it is a *religion of the human*. The load-bearing claim is theological in form (worship, creed, commandment) — "create meaning for a meaningless world" — not a moral disposition (`Directly stated` for the religious framing; `Interpretive synthesis` for the contrast with ordinary usage).

- **Hayles' "liberal humanist subject."** In the vault, [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] (via [[Posthumanism]], N. Katherine Hayles) names the autonomous, self-possessing, disembodied individual that the posthuman dissolves. Harari's "liberal humanism" is the *political-religious* sub-branch of his humanism — the orthodox sect that treats each person as "a singular ray of light" with a sovereign inner voice. The two converge on the sovereign individual but differ in object: Hayles critiques the subject's *disembodiment* and its erasure of the body; Harari diagnoses humanism's coming obsolescence under Dataism and biotechnology (`Interpretive synthesis`). They are complementary, not identical — link by intent.

- **Against transcendental religion.** Harari defines humanism precisely by its inversion of the traditional source of meaning: traditional religion has the cosmic plan give meaning to human life; humanism has human experience give meaning to the cosmos (`Directly stated`).
