# System — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

In the "Object and System" section that closes the Introduction to *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Burnham gives the system pole of his governing object/system axis an explicit, almost biological definition:

> A system is an aggregate of components; first, its parts are mutually dependent; and second, it may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life: self-organization, growth, internal or external mobility, irritability or sensitivity, input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death (humans are the most dramatic instances of systems which die).

`Directly stated`

A system is therefore not merely an assembly of parts but an assembly whose parts are *mutually dependent* and which, by virtue of that interdependence, can take on the attributes of a living thing. Earlier in the same section Burnham names the system more compactly as "an interacting assembly of varying complexity," contrasting it with "the object" as sculpture "in its traditional physical form." `Directly stated`

## Context

Burnham introduces the definition to ground the structural division of his book: "The two parts of this book divide modern sculpture respectively into object and system." The object — "palpable, finite in dimensions, varying in preciousness, a direct result of the human formative urge" — is the originary pole; the system is "the means by which sculpture gradually departs from its object state and assumes some measure of lifelike activity." `Directly stated` The full definitional sentence is set against the object's defining trait of place: "Above all the object occupies a specific space: it has place, remaining inert and stationary. A system is an aggregate of components..." `Directly stated`

The definition does argumentative work. Burnham frames the historical migration from object to system as the culmination of [[Reification]] — "the constant resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory" — and of the coercive force of technics. Because the system "may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life," it is the form modern sculpture must assume to fulfil what Burnham calls its underlying drive: "the simulation of biological life." `Strongly implied` The system's practical advantages over the object reinforce the point: "The advantages of a system over an object are flexibility of use, adaptability, and the capacity for measured reaction," whereas objects "may be more reliable and, because of their greater inherent physical stability, last longer than systems." `Directly stated`

The definition also doubles as a description of the surrounding world. For Burnham the modern object has itself been demoted into systems — "the object now is a replaceable component in an interlocking system of production and need fulfillment" — so that "the system becomes a logical extension of the object through intellectual domination of the environment." `Directly stated` The terminal stake is the substitution of information for matter: "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor," which ties this definition directly to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis and to [[Information]]. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the concept [[Object and System]]** — that note holds the full *axis* (the object pole and the system pole together, and their migration); this note isolates and pins the verbatim *definition* of the system pole alone. They are companion notes, not duplicates (same pattern as `Information` / `Information (Han)`).
- **vs. [[Systems Esthetics]]** — Burnham's founding thesis is the broader claim that sculpture migrates from object to life-simulating system under technics; "system" as defined here is the destination term that thesis names. The thesis is the argument; this is its key term. `Strongly implied`
- **vs. [[Systems Theory]] / general systems theory** — Burnham's "system" is an art-historical and ontological category for sculpture, defined by life-characteristics, not the formal interdisciplinary apparatus that Hayles tracks under [[Systems Theory]]. Burnham's later coinage "systems esthetics" (American spelling) is deliberately kept distinct from systems theory; his "system" leans on the *biological* register (self-organization, irritability, equilibrium, death) more than on cybernetic formalism, though the two converge in his cybernetic-art chapter. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Cybernetics]]** — the cybernetic organism is for Burnham the most fully realized instance of his system, but the definition is broader than any one technology: "a pail of sand, a spring-wound machine, a plant, and a human being represent systems of decreasing entropy." `Directly stated`
- **vs. Jacques Ellul's *technique*** — Burnham invokes Ellul's "technique" as the milieu in which a systems-oriented world operates ("A systems-oriented world is a milieu completely given over to what Jacques Ellul calls technique"), but Burnham's "system" is the unit of analysis, not Ellul's totalizing diagnosis of domination. `Directly stated`
