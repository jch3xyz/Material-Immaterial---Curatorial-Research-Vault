# Dataism — Yuval Noah Harari

## Author's Definition

Harari opens the chapter "The Data Religion" with a single-sentence creed that fixes the term:

> Dataism says that the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing.

(Directly stated — this is the chapter's defining sentence.)

The definition has two clauses that must be held together. The first is an **ontology**: the universe *is* data flows. The second is an **axiology**: value is reassigned, so that worth is no longer a property of an entity in itself but a measure of its contribution to processing data. Harari supplies a literal restatement of the axiological clause near the chapter's end:

> Dataism adopts a strictly functional approach to humanity, appraising the value of human experiences according to their function in data-processing mechanisms. If we develop an algorithm that fulfils the same function better, human experiences will lose their value.

(Directly stated.)

He also names the two scientific waves whose convergence produces the doctrine, and the move that follows from it:

> Dataism puts the two together, pointing out that exactly the same mathematical laws apply to both biochemical and electronic algorithms. Dataism thereby collapses the barrier between animals and machines, and expects electronic algorithms to eventually decipher and outperform biochemical algorithms.

(Directly stated.)

## Context

The term anchors the closing chapter of *Homo Deus*, where Harari treats Dataism not as a marginal idea but as an emerging religion — "the data religion" — that "has already conquered most of the scientific establishment." It is born from the confluence of two claims: that organisms are biochemical algorithms (the life sciences after Darwin) and that increasingly capable electronic algorithms can run the same mathematics (computer science after Turing). Its single supreme value is "information flow," and from that value it derives "practical commandments" — maximise data flow, "connect everything to the system," and treat the blocking of data flow (death) as "the greatest sin."

Two consequences carry the argument's weight. First, Dataism **inverts the traditional pyramid of learning** (data → information → knowledge → wisdom): because humans "can no longer cope with the immense flows of data," the work of processing should be "entrusted to electronic algorithms." Second, it completes a historical pivot Harari has tracked across the book — "in the eighteenth century, humanism sidelined God by shifting from a deo-centric to a homo-centric world view. In the twenty-first century, Dataism may sideline humans by shifting from a homo-centric to a data-centric view." The reassignment of value is therefore also a demotion of *Homo sapiens*: "Looking back, humanity will turn out to be just a ripple within the cosmic data flow."

Harari ends ambivalently, listing Dataism's "critics and heretics" — it is "doubtful whether life can really be reduced to data flows," and "we have no idea how or why data flows could produce consciousness and subjective experiences." The definition is thus offered as a diagnosis of a dominant scientific paradigm, not as the author's endorsement.

(The two-clause structure and the humanism-to-Dataism pivot are Directly stated; the framing as a diagnosis Harari does not endorse is Strongly implied by the chapter's closing critique.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Han (paired definition, [[Dataism (Han)]]).** Han and Harari converge on almost the same words — Han quotes David Brooks's "data-ism" and his own critical verdict; Harari coins the cosmological sentence. Both treat Dataism as a faith mutating out of a "neutral scientific theory" into a religion. But the emphasis differs. Han's center of gravity is the **verdict**: Dataism is "ideology," "data totalitarianism," "nihilism," "digital Dadaism" — a critique grounded in the loss of [[Narrativity]] ("counting is not recounting"). Harari's center of gravity is the **ontology and axiology**: he states the doctrine's content (universe = data flows; value = contribution to processing) and its trajectory (the eclipse of the human) more than he condemns it, reserving his objections for a final "Ripple in the Data Flow" section. Where Han diagnoses an ideology he opposes, Harari describes a paradigm he finds plausible enough to take seriously even while doubting it. The concept-level merge of the two lives in [[Dataism]].
- **Hayles / O'Gieblyn (adjacent ontologies).** The vault's other data/pattern definitions frame the problem differently. Hayles's [[The Posthuman (Hayles)]] and the concept [[The Body as Data]] treat the priority of pattern over presence as a *contingent* historical construction to be resisted, not a cosmic law. O'Gieblyn's [[Patternism]] and [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]] read the "organisms are algorithms" creed as a secularized theology. Harari's Dataism is broader than all of these: it is the explicit *value theory* that follows once the ontology is granted — the rule that converts "the universe is data" into "worth is contribution to data processing."

(The Han/Harari emphasis contrast and the cross-vault placements are Interpretive synthesis built on directly-stated passages in each source.)
