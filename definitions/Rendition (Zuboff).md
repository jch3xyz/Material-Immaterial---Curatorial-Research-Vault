# Rendition — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

[[Shoshana Zuboff]] coins **rendition** at the head of Chapter 8 ("Terms of Sur-render") as the name for the *operations* that close the gap between lived experience and behavioral data. Where the dispossession of human experience is named earlier as "the original sin of surveillance capitalism," rendition is the concrete machinery by which that sin is committed. The keystone definitional sentence (*Directly stated*, Ch.8):

> Rendition describes the concrete operational practices through which dispossession is accomplished, as human experience is claimed as raw material for datafication and all that follows, from manufacturing to sales.

She introduces the term by naming the operations directly (*Directly stated*, Ch.8): "the specific operations that target this gap on a mission to transform the one into the other. I call these operations rendition." So rendition is, exactly: **the concrete operational practices that transform human experience into behavioral data — the intermediate step in the chain of events that produces [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral surplus]], through which [[Digital Dispossession|dispossession]] is actually accomplished.** It is what makes the apparatus active rather than passive: "the apparatus of ubiquity is not a passive one-way mirror. Rather, it actively creates its own stores of knowledge through rendition" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8).

### The render / sur-render etymology (the two-sided equation)

Zuboff builds the term on the double meaning of the verb *render*, which she calls a "two-sided equation" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8):

> The noun rendition derives from the verb render, a most unusual word whose double meanings describe a two-sided equation that perfectly captures what happens in the gap between human experience and behavioral data.

**Side one — render as extraction/translation:** "the verb describes a process in which something is formed out of something else that is originally given. It designates the causal action of turning one thing into another, such as rendering oil from fat (extraction) or rendering an English text from the original Latin (translation)" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). This is the active, often invisible operation that surveillance capitalism's technologies perform on us.

**Side two — render as sur-render:** "render also describes the way in which the thing that is changed gives itself over to this process: it sur-renders" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). Zuboff traces the philology (*Directly stated*, Ch.8):

> The verb rendre first appears in tenth-century French, meaning “to give back, present, yield,” as in “rendering an account” or “the tree renders its fruit.” By the fourteenth century, the word also incorporated the idea of handing over, delivering, or acknowledging dependency or obligation, as in “Render unto Caesar.”

Surveillance capitalism "must work both sides of the equation": its technologies render our experience into data "as in rendering oil from fat," typically "outside of our awareness, let alone our consent," while "every time we encounter a digital interface we make our experience available to 'datafication,' thus 'rendering unto surveillance capitalism' its continuous tithe of raw-material supplies" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). The hyphenation **sur-render** is deliberate: it folds the language of yielding and tribute (*Render unto Caesar*) into the language of giving up / capitulation, so that the user's apparent participation and the firm's extraction name one and the same act.

## Context

Rendition is the answer to the question Zuboff says every debate about data ownership omits: "why is our experience rendered as behavioral data in the first place?" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). It is "Step One" of the [[The Dispossession Cycle|dispossession]] sequence — "the concrete operationalization of the 'original sin of simple robbery' that defined this market project from the start" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). Google "rendered the Earth, its streets, and its dwelling places, bypassing our consent"; Facebook "rendered the social network and its limitless details" for its behavioral futures markets (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). The chapter then catalogs the expanding frontier of rendition — from the "smart home" and the internet of things to the body, where "'smart' is a euphemism for rendition: intelligence that is designed to render some tiny corner of lived experience as behavioral data" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8).

Zuboff insists rendition is not optional and not symmetrical with consent: "under surveillance capitalism, rendition is typically unauthorized, unilateral, gluttonous, secret, and brazen," and "it is difficult to be where rendition is not" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). Individuals "do not render their experience out of choice or obligation but rather out of ignorance and the dictatorship of no alternatives" — "involuntary rendition has become an inescapable fact" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). The conceptual stakes are set by the chapter's logic: there can be rendition without surveillance capitalism (the abandoned "Aware Home" of Chapter 1, where the individual alone decides what experience becomes data), "However, the lesson of this chapter and the next is that there can be no surveillance capitalism without rendition" (*Directly stated*, Ch.8). Rendition is thus the indispensable supply operation: the input that feeds [[Machine Intelligence|machine intelligence]] and the [[The Extraction Imperative|extraction imperative]], producing the [[Behavioral Surplus]] from which prediction products are made.

This note isolates the explicit definitional passages; the cross-chapter and cross-author development of the idea lives in the concept note [[Rendition]], following the `Behavioral Surplus` / `Behavioral Surplus (Zuboff)` precedent.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Not the political/legal "extraordinary rendition."** Zuboff's term is etymological, not the post-9/11 sense of transferring a prisoner to another jurisdiction — though both senses share the root idea of an involuntary *handing over*. Her usage names the conversion of experience into data, with the moral charge of unconsented seizure carried by the *sur-render* pun rather than by the carceral connotation (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Distinct from rendering in computer graphics.** Zuboff explicitly acknowledges the digital-technology sense ("a 'rendering engine' converts the coded content of an HTML page for display") and uses it as one instance of side-one rendering, but her rendition is broader: not the display of already-coded content but the *prior* operation that turns un-coded human experience into code at all (*Directly stated* for the HTML example; *Interpretive synthesis* for the distinction).
- **Sharper than [[Big Data]] or [[Dataism]].** In the vault's Han/Harari sense, Big Data and Dataism name a quantifying epistemology or a quasi-religion of data flow. Rendition is a verb, not a worldview: it is the *specific operational practice* that performs datafication. Datafication is the outcome; rendition is the act that effects it (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Against Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[Quantified Self]].** Han names the *subjective* posture of voluntary self-exposure (the subject illuminates itself; auto-exploitation). Zuboff's two-sided equation absorbs that posture as only one side — sur-render — while insisting the other side (extraction "outside of our awareness, let alone our consent") is the firm's unilateral operation. The [[Quantified Self]] is, in her frame, one rendering *surface*, not the engine; rendition is mostly involuntary, "typically unauthorized, unilateral, gluttonous, secret, and brazen" (*Interpretive synthesis*; quoted phrase *Directly stated*).
- **The material/immaterial hinge.** Rendition is precisely the operation that converts the material substrate of lived experience — bodies, rooms, faces, movements — into immaterial behavioral data ([[The Body as Data]]). It is the named mechanism of one passage across the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` tension, on the surveillance-economic register rather than the art-historical (dematerialization) or posthumanist (disembodiment) one (*Interpretive synthesis*).
