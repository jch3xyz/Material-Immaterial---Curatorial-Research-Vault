# The Viral Metaphor — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

[[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] uses *the viral metaphor* in [[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch13 [Virality]|Chapter 13]] of *[[God Human Animal Machine]]* to name the now-naturalized figure by which information, ideas, and culture are said to spread like biological contagion — to reproduce *without the participation of conscious subjects* by latching onto hosts and hijacking their machinery. Her two anchor cases are the critic Douglas Rushkoff's “viral media” and Richard Dawkins's *meme*:

> The term “viral media” was coined in 1994 by the critic Douglas Rushkoff, who argued that the internet had become “an extension of a living organism” that spanned the globe and radically accelerated the way ideas and culture spread. The notion that the laws of the biosphere could apply to the datasphere was already by that point taken for granted, thanks to the theory of memes, a term Richard Dawkins devised to show that ideas and cultural phenomena spread across a population in much the same way genes do.

The defining move of the metaphor is that it explains cultural reproduction by *removing the human author from it*. Its benefit, O'Gieblyn writes, “was its ability to explain how artifacts and ideologies reproduce themselves without the participation of conscious subjects.” The candidate definition follows directly — the meme modeled exactly on viral infection:

> Just as viruses infect hosts without their knowledge or consent, so memes have a single “goal,” self-preservation and spread, which they achieve by latching on to a host and hijacking its reproductive machinery for their own ends.

The crucial cost — and the reason O'Gieblyn treats this as more than a harmless idiom — is what the figure does to *agency*. To make culture entirely passive, the metaphor has to smuggle the lost agency back in, but on the wrong side of the ledger, relocating it from the human host into the idea itself:

> That this entirely passive conception of human culture necessitates the awkward reassignment of agency to the ideas themselves—imagining that memes have “goals” and “ends”—is usually explained away as a figure of speech.

This *reassignment of agency to the ideas themselves* is the definitional crux. The metaphor's price is that it makes ideas the agents and humans the substrate — “the nodes of this enormous brain” — rather than “living agents with beliefs, preferences, and opinions.”

## Context

The chapter opens with the same thesis that drives O'Gieblyn's [[The Dead Metaphor (O'Gieblyn)|dead-metaphor]] argument — “The most successful metaphors become invisible through ubiquity” — and applies it to the language of contagion. The viral metaphor is, for her, a dead metaphor of exactly the second kind: a figure so common we no longer notice it is a figure, and so begin to take it literally. She tracks how “industry after industry, from entertainment to journalism to publishing, became preoccupied with how to harness the power of multiplication and spread,” until “we came to regard virality as something to be sought after.” That the very vocabulary was epidemiological she states outright:

> That we were using terminology inherited from epidemiology—the “curve,” the “inflection point”—was a truth that had long ago been lost on us.

And she notes, via Paul Elie, the etymological tell:

> As Elie noted, the term “influencers”—those masters of virality who capitalize on their social media presence—is an etymological outgrowth of “influenza.”

The metaphor belongs to a larger argument O'Gieblyn calls “an ontology of vacancy” — the [[The Computational Universe|computational]] worldview's “great emptying-out of qualities, content, and meaning,” in which “knowledge lies not in concepts themselves but in the relationships that constitute them.” Within that worldview, virality looks *democratic*: “A system that has no knowledge of a host's identity or status should, in theory, be radically democratic,” allowing “the most potent ideas to flourish, just as the most successful genes do under the indifferent gaze of nature.” But the blindness fails twice over. First, by 2019 Rushkoff had grown pessimistic: “The blind logic of the network was, it turned out, not as blind as it appeared—or rather, it could be manipulated by those who already had enormous resources” (his reversal in *Team Human*). Second, blindness guarantees no judiciousness: under purely quantitative metrics — “page views, clicks, shares” — “the potential for spread is often privileged over the virtue or validity of the content.” In fact, she writes:

> the most effective memes are those that bypass the gatekeeping rational mind and instead trigger “our most automatic impulses.”

The reassignment of agency is what makes the metaphor *dangerous* rather than merely loose. O'Gieblyn shows it metastasizing across the Covid-19 pandemic: viruses themselves become hard to “see ... as potentially dangerous, even lethal, biological phenomena” once “viral” is an aspiration; “armchair epidemiologists” treat infection as a modeling problem; the “growth hacker” Aaron Ginn argues “Data is data” by appealing to “that foundational creed of [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]]: that information is a universal metaphor whose structures and models remain identical across multiple disciplines” — to which O'Gieblyn answers, “Data is never merely data.” And the figure completes itself when the preferred host turns out not to be conscious at all: bots, “a virus in its own right, though in most cases they are merely the means of transmission.” “It should not have come as a surprise,” she writes, “that a system in which ideas are believed to multiply according to their own logic, by pursuing their own ends, would come to privilege hosts that are not conscious at all.” The endgame is an [[Emergence|emergent]] phantasm — “a phantasm arising mysteriously from the code, like Grendel awakening out of the swamp” — onto which culpability can never be assigned, because the agents are no longer human.

This is why the viral metaphor sits beside [[Conscious agency has never been in control]]: both descriptions strip conscious intention from the scene, but where the cybernetic tradition relocates control to distributed feedback, O'Gieblyn's worry is the *ethical* residue — that “it has become impossible to decipher between ideas that represent a legitimate political will and those that are being mindlessly propagated by machines.”

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus [[The Word as Virus]] (Hayles / Burroughs).** Both notes share the figure *idea = virus replicating through a host*, and they should be read together — but they pull in opposite directions and must **not** be merged. Hayles reads William Burroughs's *Snow Crash*-adjacent dictum “the word is now a virus” to demonstrate the **materiality** of informatics: there, taking the metaphor literally makes “the body materialize discourse,” forcing the corporeal medium back into view. O'Gieblyn's viral metaphor does the reverse work: taken literally, it **erases** the human host as agent, dissolving persons into “nodes” and reassigning agency to disembodied ideas. The Word as Virus is *Snow Crash*-specific and corporeal; the viral metaphor is general and de-corporealizing. Cross-link, do not collapse.

- **Versus Dawkins's and Rushkoff's own use.** For Dawkins the meme is an explanatory analogy for cultural transmission; for the early Rushkoff “viral media” named an emancipatory promise — he believed a networked world would benefit “people who lack traditional political power.” O'Gieblyn neither endorses nor simply debunks the science; she diagnoses what happens *rhetorically* once the analogy is naturalized — the quiet slide from spreading like a virus to acting as an agent with goals of its own, and the political consequences of that slide. (Rushkoff himself supplies her best evidence of the reversal by 2019.)

- **Versus the ordinary sense of *going viral*.** In everyday usage virality is a neutral metric of reach. O'Gieblyn's term is charged: it carries the **price** — the reassignment of agency — that the everyday usage conceals. This is the same structure as [[The Dead Metaphor (O'Gieblyn)|the dead metaphor]]: a figure rendered invisible by ubiquity and therefore read as literal description.

- **Versus [[Technology as Metaphor]].** That concept is O'Gieblyn's general method — each era reads itself through its dominant machine, and the metaphor runs in both directions. The viral metaphor is one *instance* of that method gone literal, the specific case where the biological figure for information feeds back to reshape how we understand ourselves (as hosts, as bots, as “the real virus”).
