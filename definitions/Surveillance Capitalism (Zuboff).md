# Surveillance Capitalism — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Zuboff fixes the title term twice. The book's primary anchor is a stipulative mock-dictionary headword in the short front-matter chapter **The Definition** — a lexicographic entry (`Sur-veil-lance Cap-i-tal-ism, n.`) of eight numbered senses that move from economics to politics to ontology. The opening sense:

> A new economic order that claims human experience as free raw material for hidden commercial practices of extraction, prediction, and sales

The full eight-sense entry, verbatim and in order (*Directly stated*):

1. > A new economic order that claims human experience as free raw material for hidden commercial practices of extraction, prediction, and sales
2. > A parasitic economic logic in which the production of goods and services is subordinated to a new global architecture of behavioral modification
3. > A rogue mutation of capitalism marked by concentrations of wealth, knowledge, and power unprecedented in human history
4. > The foundational framework of a surveillance economy
5. > As significant a threat to human nature in the twenty-first century as industrial capitalism was to the natural world in the nineteenth and twentieth
6. > The origin of a new instrumentarian power that asserts dominance over society and presents startling challenges to market democracy
7. > A movement that aims to impose a new collective order based on total certainty
8. > An expropriation of critical human rights that is best understood as a coup from above: an overthrow of the people’s sovereignty.

Chapter 1 ("Home or Exile in the Digital Future") supplies the operative one-sentence working form that the rest of the book unpacks (*Directly stated*):

> Surveillance capitalism unilaterally claims human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioral data.

## Context

Zuboff introduces the definition before the argument: the mock-dictionary entry stands as a front-matter chapter so that the title term arrives already stabilized, the way a coiner of a new economic category would register it. The eight senses are deliberately escalating — beginning as an "economic order" / "economic logic," sharpening into a "rogue mutation of capitalism," and ending as a political "coup from above" against "the people's sovereignty." The structure encodes the book's thesis in miniature: that what looks like a market practice is finally a question of knowledge, power, and freedom (*Strongly implied*).

Chapter 1 then operationalizes sense 1. The "free raw material" is human experience; its "translation into behavioral data" yields a proprietary [[Behavioral Surplus]] (the data beyond what is needed to improve a product or service); the surplus feeds "machine intelligence" to manufacture **prediction products**; and these are traded in [[Behavioral Futures Markets]] (*Directly stated*). As competition drives surveillance capitalists toward ever-more-predictive sources, the project shifts "from knowledge to power": the means of production are subordinated to [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]], the goal becoming "to automate us." This births what Zuboff names [[Instrumentarian Power]] — a power that "knows and shapes human behavior toward others' ends" (*Directly stated*). The definition note sits beside the concept [[Surveillance Capitalism]], following the `Information` / `Information (Han)` precedent: the concept note carries the cross-book and thematic development; this note isolates the explicit definitional passages.

## Differences from Other Uses

- Zuboff's term is not a synonym for "surveillance" in general. [[Surveillance]] in the vault names the broad condition of being watched/tracked; surveillance capitalism is a specific *economic logic* that monetizes that condition. Surveillance is the means; the capitalism is the end (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- It revises Marx. Zuboff explicitly "revives Karl Marx's old image of capitalism as a vampire that feeds on labor, but with an unexpected turn. Instead of labor, surveillance capitalism feeds on every aspect of every human's experience" (*Directly stated*, ch. 1). The expropriated substance is experience, not labor-power.
- Against Byung-Chul Han's [[Digital Panopticon]], the difference is locus of agency. Han diagnoses voluntary self-exposure and auto-exploitation — the subject illuminates *itself*. Zuboff insists the claim is **unilateral**: human experience is taken "as free raw material" without consent, "behind the scenes," a "coup from above." Han's is a phenomenology of complicity; Zuboff's is a political economy of expropriation (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- Against Harari's [[Dataism]] and Han's [[Big Data]], the difference is normativity and locus. Dataism is a *worldview/quasi-religion* in which data flow is the supreme value; Zuboff's surveillance capitalism is a concrete *market form* with named owners, products, and markets. Where Dataism dissolves the human into information, Zuboff keeps a human subject whose rights are "expropriated" (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- The [[Quantified Self]] — voluntary self-measurement — is, in Zuboff's frame, one of the rendering surfaces from which behavioral surplus is extracted, not the engine itself (*Strongly implied*).
