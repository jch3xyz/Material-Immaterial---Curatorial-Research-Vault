# Double Consciousness — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

Ascott self-glosses the term in two places, and the two formulations agree word-for-word at their core. In "Weaving the Shamantic Web" he writes: "By 'double consciousness,' I mean the state of being that gives access, at one and the same time, to two distinctly different fields of experience" (`Directly stated`). In the glossary "Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions" he names those two fields explicitly: "Double consciousness The state of being that gives access, at one and the same time, to two distinctly different fields of experience: psychic space and cyberspace, the material world and the virtual, in an artwork and outside of it" (`Directly stated`).

The state is therefore a *simultaneous* inhabitation of two orders of reality at once — not an oscillation in which one supplants the other, but co-presence in both. Ascott models it on two paradigm cases he treats as a single phenomenon: the shaman's trance, "in which the shaman is both in the everyday world and at the same time navigating the outermost limits of other worlds, psychic spaces," and the virtual-reality user's "ability, aided by computer technology, to move effortlessly through the infinities of cyberspace, while at the same time accommodating ourselves within the structures of the material world" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Ascott introduces double consciousness as the experiential ground of his late "moist" / technoetic aesthetic, where art is a means of navigating and constructing consciousness rather than producing objects. It is the perceptual condition underwriting [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]: the artwork is something one is *inside of and outside of at the same time*, a field of experience rather than a thing to be contemplated (`Strongly implied`).

He grounds the claim in first-person research, having "spent time immersed in the virtual reality of advanced computer systems and in the traditional reality of a native Indian tribe" — using [[Full-Immersion Virtual Reality]] laboratories on the one hand and the Amazonian plant brew ayahuasca on the other. Describing the ayahuasca state, he reports being "aware both of my own familiar sense of self and of a totally separate state of being," and "at one and the same time conscious of inhabiting two bodies" (`Directly stated`). The parallelism between "shamanic space and telematic space" is what he sets out to research, and double consciousness is named as "a part of both fields of experience."

Crucially, Ascott reads the condition as eroding the very categorical separation between the two fields. As "double technology develops," he argues, "the worlds of double consciousness... become less distinctly separate. The immaterial and material lose their categorical distinction" (`Directly stated`). Double consciousness is thus not merely a way of being in two worlds but a solvent of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] boundary itself — the artist who can "see in the mind's eye" can simultaneously "realize in the material world."

## Differences from Other Uses

- The phrase "double consciousness" is most familiar from W. E. B. Du Bois (the divided self-perception of the African American subject) and from nineteenth-century psychology (dissociation / alternating personalities). Ascott does not invoke either; his sense is neither a wound of social division nor a pathology but an *affirmative perceptual faculty* — closer to the Sufi "be in the world but not of the world," which he explicitly cites, than to a split or alienated self (`Directly stated` for the Sufi reference; `Interpretive synthesis` for the contrast with Du Bois).
- It is distinct from the vault's [[Embodiment]] discussions in Hayles and Haraway: where those center on the body as the irreducible substrate of cognition, Ascott's double consciousness describes a *second* body and a *second* world layered over the first ("a second body made up of a mass of multicoloured particles"), a doubling rather than a grounding (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- It differs from Kurzweil's [[Full-Immersion Virtual Reality]] and the broader vault concept of [[Virtuality]] in its aim: for Kurzweil immersion tends toward *replacement* of the biological substrate, whereas Ascott prizes the *simultaneity* of the virtual and the material — the value lies precisely in holding both fields at once, not in migrating fully to one (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- Within Ascott's own lexicon, double consciousness is paired with, but not identical to, the "double gaze" (the reciprocal seeing of cyberception): "The double gaze and double consciousness are related," he writes, the gaze being the perceptual mechanism and the consciousness the state of being it opens (`Directly stated`).
