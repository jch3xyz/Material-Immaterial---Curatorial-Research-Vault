# Hierarchy — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows defines hierarchy structurally, as the nesting of subsystems within larger subsystems. She builds the term up from an example before naming it:

> The world, or at least the parts of it humans think they understand, is organized in subsystems aggregated into larger subsystems, aggregated into still larger subsystems. A cell in your liver is a subsystem of an organ, which is a subsystem of you as an organism, and you are a subsystem of a family, an athletic team, a musical group, and so forth. These groups are subsystems of a town or city, and then a nation, and then the whole global socioeconomic system that dwells within the biosphere system. This arrangement of systems and subsystems is called a hierarchy.

Her glossary compresses the same sense into one line:

> Hierarchy: Systems organized in such a way as to create a larger system. Subsystems within systems.

`Directly stated`

## Context

Hierarchy is one of the three characteristics — alongside [[Resilience]] and [[Self-Organization]] — that Meadows uses to answer the chapter's title question, "Why Systems Work So Well." She introduces it not as a chart of command but as something a system makes out of itself: "one thing that a self-organizing system often generates is hierarchy." The definition therefore arrives as the product of [[Self-Organization]], the immediately preceding characteristic, and it is offered to explain how complex order gets built and held together at all (`Directly stated`).

Two features of Meadows's framing matter for the definition. First, hierarchy is near-inevitable for complexity, not merely common: via Herbert Simon's watchmaker fable of Hora and Tempus she generalizes that "Complex systems can evolve from simple systems only if there are stable intermediate forms. The resulting complex forms will naturally be hierarchic," and that "Among all possible complex forms, hierarchies are the only ones that have had the time to evolve." Second, the direction of purpose runs upward from the bottom, which inverts the ordinary connotation of the word: "Hierarchical systems evolve from the bottom up. The purpose of the upper layers of the hierarchy is to serve the purposes of the lower layers." A functional hierarchy grants stability and resilience while reducing the information any one part must track; it fails toward [[Suboptimization]] when a subsystem's goals dominate the whole, and toward overcontrol when the top starves subsystems of their autonomy (`Directly stated`). The fuller conceptual treatment lives in the concept note [[Hierarchy (Systems)]]; this note isolates Meadows's verbatim definition.

## Differences from Other Uses

Meadows's hierarchy is the vault's only formal, discipline-native definition of the word, and it deliberately holds apart from the everyday and political senses. The `(Meadows)` definition and its concept counterpart are titled **Hierarchy (Systems)** precisely to keep this structural meaning — subsystems nested inside larger subsystems — separate from hierarchy as a chain of command or a ranking of authority. Where those ordinary senses put purpose and power at the top, Meadows locates purpose at the bottom: the upper layers exist to serve the originating subsystems, so "top" and "bottom" name levels of aggregation, not superiority.

Within the vault's systems and cybernetics cluster the term is neighbor to, but distinct from, several others. It is the scaffold that [[Emergence]] climbs — the layered nesting is what lets higher-level properties arise from lower-level parts — but hierarchy names the structure, not the appearance of the new properties. It is a product of [[Self-Organization]], not a synonym for it: self-organization is the process of adding complexity, hierarchy one of the structures that process throws off. Its information economy — "relationships within each subsystem are denser and stronger than relationships between subsystems" — is what keeps [[Feedback Loops]] short and undelayed, but hierarchy is the arrangement, feedback the flow of information through it. No other author in the vault defines hierarchy in this structural, stability-granting sense; the resonant contrasts elsewhere (McLuhan's "center-margin structure," Han's flattening toward the "Hell of the Same," the surveillance cluster's coded command-control networks) invoke hierarchy in the coercive or organizational senses Meadows sets aside.
