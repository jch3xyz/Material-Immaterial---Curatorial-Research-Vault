# Cybernetics — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

In Chapter 8, "Robot and Cyborg Art," Burnham pauses his survey of the new control-machines to fix the meaning of the discipline that underwrites them. He gives two compact, deliberately definitional glosses. The first names its essence:

> Before going further, it should be made clear that cybernetics is preeminently the science of organization.

`Directly stated`

The second names its method, and routes it explicitly through Ludwig von Bertalanffy's biology:

> cybernetics is the analysis of linked and interacting systems-precisely Bertalanffy's view of biological activity.

`Directly stated`

For Burnham, then, cybernetics is not principally a theory of machines, of feedback, or of information narrowly construed, but the **science of organization** — and, operationally, the **analysis of linked and interacting systems**. Its subject is the pattern of interconnection that makes an aggregate behave as a unity, "both natural and man-created systems, if equipped with similar organizational properties," eliciting "related behavioral responses." `Directly stated`

## Context

Burnham introduces these definitions at the hinge of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*: the "cybernetic changeover," the moment sculpture ceases to be an inert object stimulating a one-way response and becomes a [[System (Burnham)|system]] capable of "a sustained two-way experience" with its observer. Cybernetics is the theoretical engine that licenses this migration — it is what allows Burnham to recast the relation of viewer and artwork "within a cybernetic context" as "a relationship between a complex, self-stabilizing, goal-seeking system (man) and an inert object." `Directly stated` The whole argument of [[Systems Esthetics]] — that modern sculpture departs its object-state and assumes "some measure of lifelike activity" — runs on this cybernetic substrate.

Burnham is careful about cybernetics' status as a discipline. He reports that it "never had a father, but rather a number of uncles," dating its scientific inception to "the publication in 1948 of Norbert Wiener's *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine*," and noting that it "never existed as a science in its own right, but became the theoretical springboard for a number of related activities" — "computer technology, information storage, systems analysis and development, learning theory... pattern recognition, artificial intelligence, and bionics." `Directly stated` It is from inside this branching field that he extracts the one feature that matters for art: organization.

The definition does specific work for Burnham's esthetics. Where 1920s–30s organicism was "a rather lovely if unrealized dream of architects and sculptors," cybernetics gives organicism real content: "The underlying role of cybernetics has been the implementation of organic relations through a profound understanding of the patterns of organization in evolving and living systems." `Directly stated` Cybernetics is thus the bridge by which the long sculptural "yearning... to furnish images capable of intelligent intercourse with their creators" becomes technically thinkable — and by which "the word *organic* ceases to be an unobtainable ideal held out to the artist." `Directly stated` Its most fully realized art-instance is [[The Cyborg|the cyborg]] ("cybernetic organism"), "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism." `Directly stated`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the concept [[Cybernetics]]** — that note is the shared, multi-author concept page (Hayles, Wiener, Haraway, McLuhan), reading cybernetics epistemologically, politically, and medially. This note isolates and pins Burnham's verbatim *art-world definition* — cybernetics as "the science of organization" recruited as the engine of a systems esthetics. They are companion notes, not duplicates (same pattern as `Information` / `Information (Han)`, `System` / `System (Burnham)`). `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s reading** — Hayles treats cybernetics as the seriated discipline (three waves) through which "information lost its body," and contests the abstraction of pattern from flesh. Burnham, writing in 1968 at the discipline's high tide, takes its organizational synthesis affirmatively and *prescriptively* — as the model sculpture should adopt to "rival the attributes of intelligent life." Where Hayles measures cybernetics against [[Embodiment|embodiment]], Burnham embraces precisely the move that demotes the material object: "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Donna Haraway]]'s reading** — Haraway politicizes the cybernetic recoding of organisms into "biotic components" as the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]]. Burnham invokes the same recoding optimistically as artistic possibility, though he registers its darker face elsewhere in the chapter (Radio-Hypnotic Intracerebral Control, "the possibility of efficient thought control"). His definition is esthetic and constructive, not a diagnosis of power. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Systems Theory]] / general systems theory** — Burnham explicitly fuses the two: "cybernetics is the analysis of linked and interacting systems-precisely Bertalanffy's view of biological activity." For Burnham, cybernetics (Wiener) and general systems theory (Bertalanffy) converge on the same object — organization — which is exactly what lets him build a *systems* esthetics on a *cybernetic* foundation. `Directly stated`
- **vs. Wiener's own emphasis** — Burnham distinguishes his organizational definition from "Wiener's emphasis on relationships between machines and animals," judging it "less dramatic" but holding "the implication is just as important." The Wiener register (control and communication between animal and machine) is the more famous one; Burnham deliberately foregrounds the quieter Bertalanffian register of pure organization. `Directly stated`
