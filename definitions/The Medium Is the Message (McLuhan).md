# The Medium Is the Message — Marshall McLuhan

## Author's Definition

McLuhan defines the thesis twice in the chapter. First, in terms of consequences and scale:

> This is merely to say that the personal and social consequences of any medium— that is, of any extension of ourselves — result from the new scale that is introduced into our affairs by each extension of ourselves, or by any new technology.

Then he restates it as the formal gloss on the word "message":

> For the "message" of any medium or technology is the change of scale or pace or pattern that it introduces into human affairs.

The "message" of a medium is therefore *not* its content but the structural transformation it works on human association and perception. `Directly stated`

## Context

McLuhan opens *Understanding Media* with this thesis as its governing principle, the lens through which every later case study (the railway, the airplane, the electric light, print, money, automation) is read. He introduces it against a culture "long accustomed to splitting and dividing all things as a means of control," for which it is "a bit of a shock to be reminded that, in operational and practical fact, the medium is the message." `Directly stated`

The chapter's pivotal move is to detach a medium's effect from its uses. The railway, he argues, "did not introduce movement or transportation or wheel or road into human society, but it accelerated and enlarged the scale of previous human functions, creating totally new kinds of cities and new kinds of work and leisure" — and this "is quite independent of the freight or content of the railway medium." `Directly stated` The electric light is his limiting case: "pure information," "a medium without a message, as it were, unless it is used to spell out some verbal ad or name," and so an "invaluable instance of how people fail to study media at all" precisely because it has no "content" to distract attention from its form. `Directly stated`

From this McLuhan derives the corollary that "the 'content' of any medium is always another medium" — the content of writing is speech, the written word is the content of print, and print is the content of the telegraph. `Directly stated` Content is thus "the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind": it "blinds us to the character of the medium," whose real effect is to "shape and control the scale and form of human association and action." `Directly stated`

The thesis is also a polemic against the commonplace that technologies are neutral instruments. McLuhan quotes General David Sarnoff — "The products of modern science are not in themselves good or bad; it is the way they are used that determines their value" — and calls it "the voice of the current somnambulism," "the numb stance of the technological idiot," because "it ignores the nature of the medium." `Directly stated`

This definition belongs *beside* the concept note [[The Medium Is the Message]], not in place of it: the definition note pins the verbatim formal gloss (the "change of scale or pace or pattern"), while the concept note carries the broader development, the case studies, and the cross-author links. McLuhan's sense of a "medium" is unusually wide: a medium is "any extension of ourselves," so that the electric light, the railway, money, automation, and speech are all media. This is broader than the communications-channel sense the slogan is often reduced to. `Directly stated` His claim that the medium's effects "do not occur at the level of opinions or concepts, but alter sense ratios or patterns of perception steadily and without any resistance" distinguishes his position from theories that locate a technology's significance in its symbolic content or in users' attitudes toward it. `Directly stated`

Within the vault, the thesis sits near but apart from O'Gieblyn's [[Technology as Metaphor]], which treats technology as a frame for thinking about mind and world; McLuhan's claim is the stronger, formal one that the medium *is itself* the operative message regardless of any metaphorical or representational content. The two are cross-linked, not merged. `Interpretive synthesis`
