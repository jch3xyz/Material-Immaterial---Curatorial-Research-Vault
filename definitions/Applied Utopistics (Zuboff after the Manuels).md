# Applied Utopistics — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Zuboff borrows the term **applied utopistics** from the historians of utopian thought Frank and Fritzie Manuel, who use it of Lenin: the practice of realizing a utopian vision *directly in operations and practice* rather than through articulated theory.

> Indeed, the Manuels describe Lenin as a specialist in "applied utopistics."

She turns it on the surveillance capitalists. Where the classic utopian (Marx) "grasped the world with his thickly articulated theory" but lacked the power to implement it, and where Lenin, Stalin, and Mao *applied* Marx's theory to real life only "long after the publication" of that theory, the surveillance capitalists collapse the gap entirely:

> In contrast, the surveillance capitalists seize the world in practice. Their theories are thin—at least this is true of the thinking that they share with the public. The opposite is true of their power, which is monumental and largely unimpeded.

Applied utopistics, in Zuboff's adaptation, names a power that needs no theory because it owns the means of execution. The utopia is not argued; it is built. `Directly stated` — Zuboff explicitly imports the Manuels' phrase, applies it to Lenin's lineage, and re-applies it to the surveillance capitalists as people who "seize the world in practice."

## Context

The term appears in section III ("Applied Utopistics") of Chapter 14, "A Utopia of Certainty." The chapter argues that surveillance capitalism's pursuit of certainty — the mandate of [[The Prediction Imperative]] — drives [[Instrumentarian Power]] toward totality, annexing "society," "social relations," and key societal processes as fresh terrain for rendition, calculation, modification, and prediction: "a utopia of certainty" enforced by [[Big Other]].

Zuboff first establishes that figures such as Nadella, Page, and Zuckerberg conform to five of the six elements with which the Manuels define "the classic profile of the most ambitious modern utopianists" (tunnel vision, an early grasp of a "new state of being," an obsessive idée fixe, belief in inevitability, and the drive for total reformation at species and world-system scale). The *sixth* Manuel characteristic is where the surveillance capitalists become "powerful exceptions": the classic utopian's "gadgets... rarely go beyond the mechanical potentialities of his age... he cannot make a world out of nothing." `Directly stated`

The Lenin analogy completes the argument: these are "sui generis utopianists" and "rare utopianists who can oversee the translation of their imaginations into fact without soldiers to pave the way in blood." `Directly stated` This is what makes "applied utopistics" load-bearing for Zuboff: it diagnoses a utopian *project executed as infrastructure*, not declared as doctrine. Methodologically, she notes, "the only way to grasp the theory advanced in their applied utopistics is to reverse engineer their operations and scrutinize their meaning" — the analytic posture of the whole book. `Directly stated`

The concept underwrites the chapter's distinction between [[Instrumentarian Power vs Totalitarianism]]: totalitarianism "seeks totality as a political condition and relies on violence," whereas instrumentarianism "seeks totality as a condition of market dominance" and relies on Big Other and its control over the division of learning. Applied utopistics is the *mode of realization* proper to the instrumentarian, non-violent species of power. `Strongly implied`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **The Manuels (origin) / Lenin.** For the Manuels, applied utopistics is the *application* of an already-articulated theory (Marxism) to lived political reality — theory precedes and authorizes practice. Zuboff's surveillance-capitalist version inverts this: practice precedes (and largely dispenses with public) theory. The world is seized first; the "thin" theory is reverse-engineered out of operations after the fact. `Directly stated`

- **Marx's theoretical utopianism.** Marx is Zuboff's foil for what applied utopistics is *not*: a thickly articulated vision without the power to implement it. The contrast sharpens the term — applied utopistics is defined by the possession of implementing power, not by the richness of the vision. `Directly stated`

- **Roy Ascott's "visionary pragmatism."** Ascott (*Telematic Embrace*) frames the artist as a builder who realizes a connective/technoetic vision in practice rather than in manifesto — a broadly *affirmative* fusion of vision and execution. Zuboff's applied utopistics names a structurally similar collapse of theory into practice, but recasts it as a vector of domination: the utopian rhetoric is "the minesweeper that precedes the foot soldiers," a strategic distraction that legitimates totalistic ambitions. The shared form (vision realized directly in operations) carries opposite valence. `Speculative connection`

- **Kurzweil / Harari techno-utopianism.** Kurzweil's Singularity and Harari's Dataism are *theoretical* utopias — elaborate, published worldviews. Zuboff's point is precisely that the surveillance capitalists are not primarily theorists of this kind; their utopia is enacted through [[Inevitabilism]] and infrastructure, not defended in argument. She warns against dismissing the "magical age" rhetoric as "mere 'techno utopianism'" — the rhetoric is operationally strategic, not merely speculative. `Interpretive synthesis`
