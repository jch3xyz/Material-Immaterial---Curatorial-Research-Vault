# Reinforcing feedback loop — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows gives the term a compact, box-set definition in *Thinking in Systems*:

> Reinforcing feedback loops are self-enhancing, leading to exponential growth or to runaway collapses over time. They are found whenever a stock has the capacity to reinforce or reproduce itself.

She unfolds it in the running text as the second of her two elementary feedback structures:

> The second kind of feedback loop is amplifying, reinforcing, self-multiplying, snowballing—a vicious or virtuous circle that can cause healthy growth or runaway destruction. It is called a reinforcing feedback loop, and will be noted with an R in the diagrams. It generates more input to a stock the more that is already there (and less input the less that is already there). A reinforcing feedback loop enhances whatever direction of change is imposed on it.

The defining operation, then, is *amplification of whatever change is imposed*: a reinforcing loop "generates more input to a stock the more that is already there," so more begets more (and less begets less) and the stock compounds on itself (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The definition appears in Chapter 1, "The Basics," in the section titled "Runaway Loops," immediately after Meadows has introduced the [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] as the goal-seeking, equilibrating structure. The reinforcing loop is presented as its exact counterpart and opposite — where the balancing loop "opposes whatever direction of change is imposed," the reinforcing loop "enhances" it — and Meadows marks it with an **R** in her loop diagrams against the balancing loop's **B**. Together these two primitives are the building blocks from which the rest of her systems vocabulary is assembled; she remarks that "by now you may be seeing how basic balancing and reinforcing feedback loops are to systems."

Meadows anchors the definition in the compound-interest example — "the more money you have in the bank, the more interest you earn, which is added to the money already in the bank, where it earns even more interest" — to make the crucial point that the resulting growth is *not linear*:

> This is not simple linear growth. It is not constant over time. The growth of the bank account at lower interest rates may look linear in the first few years. But, in fact, growth goes faster and faster. The more is there, the more is added. This kind of growth is called "exponential." It's either good news or bad news, depending on what is growing—money in the bank, people with HIV/AIDS, pests in a cornfield, a national economy, or weapons in an arms race.

Two features of the definition do heavy lifting in the larger argument. First, it is deliberately **value-neutral**: the same amplifying structure is "either good news or bad news, depending on what is growing," which is why Meadows can list interest, epidemics, pests, economies, and arms races in a single breath (`Directly stated`). Second, it is **structural rather than substantive** — the loop is "found whenever a stock has the capacity to reinforce or reproduce itself," so "those elements include populations and economies," and the identical skeleton describes rabbits breeding, capital reinvesting in output ("the central engine of growth in an economy"), and a hillside eroding (`Directly stated`). Meadows appends a practical corollary, the doubling-time rule of thumb: the time for an exponentially growing stock to double "equals approximately 70 divided by the growth rate (expressed as a percentage)."

## Differences from Other Uses

This is the vault's **native-discipline** definition of the mechanism — the value-neutral engine that other authors take up and load with a value sign. The distinction is a cross-link, not an identity:

- Against [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s [[Acceleration]]: Han treats runaway self-amplifying growth as a *pathology* — a temporal and social disorder to be diagnosed and condemned. Meadows describes the same compounding structure with no such charge; for her it is simply the loop that "enhances whatever direction of change is imposed on it," good or bad depending only on what grows (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- Against [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s [[The Law of Accelerating Returns]]: Kurzweil *celebrates* exponential technological growth as destiny. Meadows names the same exponential dynamic ("growth goes faster and faster… this kind of growth is called 'exponential'") without exaltation, and immediately balances it against the limiting counter-structure of the [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- Within the cybernetic tradition, the reinforcing loop corresponds to *positive feedback*. Meadows's contribution is not the discovery of the mechanism but its de-jargoned, diagram-driven presentation ("R" vs "B") and her insistence that in real systems these loops "rarely come singly" — "a single stock is likely to have several reinforcing and balancing loops of differing strengths pulling it in several directions" — so that no reinforcing loop should be read in isolation (`Directly stated`).
