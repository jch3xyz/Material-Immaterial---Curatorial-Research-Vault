# Objecthood — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

In the closing pages of his chapter on robot and cyborg art, Jack Burnham reports the critic Michael Fried's term **objecthood** as the name for what sculptural formalism has secretly been all along — the recognition that, once idealism is stripped away, sculpture is merely an object composed of inanimate material. Burnham presents the term as the long-delayed naming of a condition that had been visible for half a century:

> Yet the total awareness of what formalism implies has only recently been encapsulated into a single term, "objecthood" (Summer, 1967, pp. 12- 23), by the critic Michael Fried.

`Directly stated`

The content of the term — what that "total awareness" amounts to — Burnham states in the very next sentence as a completed unmasking:

> As the masks of idealism have dropped from sculpture, the process of inverse transubstantiation completes itself: sculpture is no longer sculpture, but mechanistically an object composed of inanimate material.

`Directly stated`

Two further moves complete Burnham's framing. First, he records Fried's own prescription — sculpture must hold the line against theatre to survive as art:

> Fried responds that sculpture must resist becoming theatre in order to remain an independent art.

Second, he inverts it: where Fried treats theatricality as the disease, Burnham reads it as the symptom of a coming cure — the static object's theatricality is a rehearsal for the systems view of art that will dissolve the object altogether.

> Yet it is more probable that the acknowledged theatricality of present modes of static sculpture are preparatory steps toward the acceptance of a systems perspective.

`Directly stated`

## Context

"Objecthood" enters *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) at the hinge between the book's two halves — the migration from [[Object and System|object to system]] (see [[Object (Burnham)]]) — and it is the term Burnham needs to close the argument of Part Two. Throughout the book Burnham has read modern [[Formalism (Burnham)|formalism]] not as decorative abstraction but as "incipient mechanization applied to art," a "preparation or pre-stage for the literal assembly of machine components and systems in art." Fried's coinage gives him the missing word: objecthood is "the total awareness of what formalism implies," the point at which the materialist properties of modern sculpture — "evident to the thoughtful observer for more than a half century" — finally name themselves. `Directly stated`

Burnham frames the recognition theologically, as an *inverse transubstantiation*. Where transubstantiation turns inert matter into spiritual substance, here the "masks of idealism" drop and the spiritual pretensions of sculpture collapse back into brute matter: "sculpture is no longer sculpture, but mechanistically an object composed of inanimate material." This is why objecthood matters to him — it is the de-idealizing endpoint of the object pole, the condition that [[Object Art]] (Minimal/literalist sculpture, where "physical presence is everything") deliberately embraces. `Directly stated`

The decisive contextual move is Burnham's reversal of Fried's value judgment. Fried, defending an independent art, holds that sculpture *must resist becoming theatre*; theatricality (the work's dependence on its situation, its phenomenal address to a beholder) is for him the failure of the medium. Burnham keeps the diagnosis but flips the prognosis: the theatricality of static sculpture is not a fall but a "preparatory step" toward "the acceptance of a systems perspective." It is theatrical, he adds, "not only in their implicit phenomenalism, but also in the sculptor's mock aloofness and objectivity toward the processes of fabrication" — postures Burnham reads as parodies of "the industrialist doing 'business.'" The same withdrawal of the maker's feeling that produces the inert object is what frees the object to "gain its independence from its human maker" and eventually "seek a life of its own through self-reproduction" — the system. `Directly stated` / `Strongly implied` Thus the term Fried offers as a *limit* on sculpture Burnham repurposes as the *threshold* of the [[Object vs System]] transition that structures the whole book.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Fried's normative objecthood vs. Burnham's developmental objecthood.** For Fried (whose 1967 essay Burnham cites by season and page) objecthood is something art should *escape*: the literalist object's theatricality compromises the autonomy of the medium, and sculpture earns its standing as "an independent art" only by resisting theatre. Burnham accepts the description but rejects the imperative — he treats objecthood as a real historical stage to be passed *through*, not a danger to be warded off. Where Fried wants to keep the object from sliding into theatre, Burnham wants the object to keep sliding: through theatricality into systems. `Directly stated`

- **Objecthood vs. Burnham's "object."** Objecthood is the *self-consciousness* of the [[Object (Burnham)|object]] pole — "the total awareness of what formalism implies," the moment the inert, place-bound, inanimate thing recognizes itself as such. The object is the entity ("it has place, remaining inert and stationary"); objecthood is the de-idealized condition of being only that entity, with the "masks of idealism" gone. `Strongly implied`

- **Objecthood (theatricality preceding systems) vs. Lippard's dematerialization (the object thinning into idea).** Both are diagnoses of the art object's eclipse, but they run in opposite directions. Lucy Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] tracks the object thinning toward concept, document, and information. Burnham's objecthood intensifies the object's *physicality* first — pure inanimate presence, "physical presence is everything" — and only then opens onto the [[System (Burnham)|system]], where "'information' is the key factor." For Burnham the road to the immaterial runs through a maximally materialized object, not past it. `Interpretive synthesis`

- **Theatre as failure (Fried) vs. theatre as rehearsal (Burnham).** The two readings of theatricality are the crux. Fried: theatre is what sculpture must *not* become. Burnham: the theatre of "9 evenings: theatre and engineering" and of the mock-objective fabricator is precisely the social form in which sculpture learns to become a "man-machine system" — "systems-oriented art... will deal less with artifacts contrived for their formal value, and increasingly with men enmeshed with and within purposeful responsive systems." `Directly stated`
