# Leverage Points — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

For Donella Meadows, *leverage points* are "places in the system where a small change could lead to a large shift in behavior." `Directly stated` She glosses them in three words a paragraph later: "Leverage points are points of power." `Directly stated`

The term is not her coinage. She credits it to MIT's Jay Forrester, and introduces the definition inside a report of his practice: "the average manager can define the current problem very cogently, identify the system structure that leads to the problem, and guess with great accuracy where to look for leverage points—places in the system where a small change could lead to a large shift in behavior." `Directly stated` The idea, she adds, "is not unique to systems analysis—it's embedded in legend: the silver bullet; the trimtab; the miracle cure; the secret passage; the magic password; the single hero who turns the tide of history; the nearly effortless way to cut through or leap over huge obstacles." `Directly stated`

Two qualifications are built into the definition as Meadows fixes it. First, leverage points are *counterintuitive*: "although people deeply involved in a system often know intuitively where to find leverage points, more often than not they push the change in the wrong direction." `Directly stated` Second, the noun names a *rankable* class, not a single place. The whole of Chapter 6 is Meadows's tentative, ordered list of twelve of them (see [[The Twelve Leverage Points]]) — "a list of places to intervene in a system" whose deep organizing principle is that leverage rises as the intervention point grows less material: "Now we're beginning to move from the physical part of the system to the information and control parts, where more leverage can be found." `Directly stated`

## Context

Meadows introduces the definition to answer the practical question that opens the chapter: "how do we change the structure of systems to produce more of what we want and less of that which is undesirable?" `Directly stated` The definition therefore does the work of naming *where* to push, while the chapter's ranked list does the work of saying *which* pushes pay off. She frames the whole exercise with humility — the list is "still a work in progress; it's not a recipe for finding leverage points. Rather, it's an invitation to think more broadly about system change" — and closes the chapter by displacing the metaphor of force entirely: "mastery has less to do with pushing leverage points than it does with strategically, profoundly, madly, letting go and dancing with the system." `Directly stated`

The definition's evidentiary spine is Forrester's modelling. His *World* model, built for the Club of Rome, located a single master leverage point behind poverty, hunger, environmental destruction and unemployment — *growth* — and found that "the world's leaders are correctly fixated on economic growth as the answer to virtually all problems, but they're pushing with all their might in the wrong direction." `Directly stated` His urban-dynamics study "demonstrated that subsidized low-income housing is a leverage point," but one where "the less of it there is, the better off the city is" — again a lever pushed backward. `Directly stated` "Counterintuitive—that's Forrester's word to describe complex systems." `Directly stated` The definition thus arrives bound to a warning: knowing that a point of power exists, and even where it is, does not tell you which way to lean on it.

The concept note develops leverage points across her framework (the full material→immaterial spectrum, the [[Stocks and Flows]] plumbing at the low end, the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension the ranking dramatizes) and their wider resonance in the vault. Same base name, disambiguated by the `(Meadows)` suffix and by folder, in the vault's established pattern (as with `Transparency` / `Transparency (Han)`).
- **vs. [[The Twelve Leverage Points]].** The definition names the *kind* of place ("a small change → a large shift"); that note enumerates the specific twelve, ranked from parameters (12) to transcending paradigms (1). Definition and enumeration are cross-linked but distinct.
- **vs. the vault's cybernetic vocabulary — [[Feedback Loops]], [[Homeostasis]], [[Self-Organization]].** Those name *mechanisms internal to* a system; a leverage point is a second-order notion — a *place to intervene on* those mechanisms from a modeller's standpoint. Several of Meadows's twelve are exactly such mechanisms (balancing and reinforcing loops at rungs 8–7, self-organization at rung 4), but the leverage-point concept ranks them by how much a change *there* moves the whole, rather than describing what each does. This grounds the systems/cybernetics cluster the vault otherwise carries secondhand through Hayles in its native discipline. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. everyday "leverage."** Meadows keeps the mechanical root — the trimtab, the silver bullet — but strips it of the promise of ease: "Magical leverage points are not easily accessible, even if we know where they are and which direction to push on them. There are no cheap tickets to mastery." `Directly stated` The higher the leverage, the *more* the system resists: "The higher the leverage point, the more the system will resist changing it." `Directly stated`
