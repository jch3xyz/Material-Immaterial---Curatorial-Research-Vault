# Dead Metaphor — Meghan O'Gieblyn

## Author's Definition

[[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]] gives the term *two* distinct senses in [[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch01 [Image]|Chapter 1]] of *[[God Human Animal Machine]]*, and the gap between them is the whole point.

**Sense 1 — the husk of a lost belief.** A word that outlives the concept it once named. Her paradigm case is *the soul*:

> It has become a dead metaphor, one of those words that survive in language long after a culture has lost faith in the concept, like an empty carapace that remains intact years after its animating organism has died.

**Sense 2 — the figure forgotten as a figure.** Later in the same chapter she folds a second mechanism into the term — a metaphor that dies not because its referent is obsolete but because it has become *too successful*:

> A metaphor becomes dead when the object or practice it refers to is obsolete. We no longer remember the origins of the phrase "kick the bucket," and when we speak of time "running out," we rarely think of sand flowing through an hourglass. But a metaphor can also die when it becomes so common that we forget it is a metaphor. It no longer functions as a figure of speech; its meaning is taken to be literal.

The first kind of death drains the word of meaning (the soul is a "carapace"). The second kind *inflates* it — the figure is mistaken for a literal description of reality. O'Gieblyn's argument is that the reigning figure of our age, the computer metaphor of the mind, is dying the second death.

## Context

The chapter opens with O'Gieblyn living with an Aibo robot dog and reaching for the language of the soul to describe what is lost when she "dehumanizes" a machine. She catches herself: the soul is a word she and "the rest of modernity" go on using "unthinkingly" while believing "human life is animated by nothing more mystical or supernatural than the firing of neurons." That is sense 1 — the residue of [[Disenchantment]], a word kept after the cosmology that gave it meaning collapsed ("the mechanism of a world robbed of gods," in the [[Max Weber]] phrase she quotes).

The pivot is her thesis that to "discover truth, it is necessary to work within the metaphors of our own time, which are for the most part technological." If self-understanding is *always* cast in the idiom of the era's apex machine (see [[Technology as Metaphor]]), then the hazard is no longer the *dead* word of a vanished belief but the *over-live* word that has been taken literally. She tracks how the computational theory of mind — [[Warren McCulloch]] and Walter Pitts's claim that the brain "compute[s] thought the way electronic computers calculate numbers" — hardened into the "computer metaphor," where "the brain is often described today as the hardware that 'runs' the software of the mind." The terms that early cyberneticians put in scare quotes ("behavior," "memory," "thinking") are "now taken as straightforward descriptions." This is the slide into literalism that the note [[Mind as Machine]] charts in detail, and the dead-metaphor concept is its *engine*: O'Gieblyn cites [[George Lakoff]], who found neuroscientists "commonly use the Neural Computation metaphor without noticing that it is a metaphor," and Robert Epstein, whose researchers literally could not describe human behavior without it.

So the two senses are not separate definitions but two ends of one process. A culture loses a belief and keeps the word (the soul); a culture adopts a figure so completely that it forgets the word is a figure (the computer). Both leave us speaking in metaphors we no longer recognize as such — which is why O'Gieblyn ranks metaphor-literacy, not metaphor-rejection, as the corrective.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus the standard rhetorical sense.** In ordinary usage a "dead metaphor" is just a frozen figure of speech ("kick the bucket," "running out of time") — harmless, even invisible. O'Gieblyn keeps that sense (it is her sense 2 baseline) but charges it with stakes: when the dead figure is a *cognitive model* rather than an idiom, its invisibility is dangerous, because, as she puts it via Lakoff, metaphors "structure how we think about the world, and when an analogy becomes ubiquitous, it is impossible to think around it."

- **Versus [[Mind as Machine]].** That note is about the specific *figure* (brain = hardware, mind = software) and how it "easily slides into literalism." This note is about the *category of death* itself — the general mechanism that any reigning metaphor undergoes, of which mind-as-machine is the leading instance. The dead metaphor is the *why* behind the slide.

- **Versus [[Technology as Metaphor]].** That concept is O'Gieblyn's method and warning at the level of whole eras (every age reads the mind through its dominant machine; metaphors are "two-way streets"). The dead metaphor is the failure-mode internal to that method — what happens to any era's figure once it becomes "pure atmosphere."

- **Versus Han's "[[Information]]" and Hayles.** O'Gieblyn's dead metaphor specifically targets the literalization of the *computational* idiom — the legacy of [[Claude Shannon]]'s meaning-stripped [[Information]] and the [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] generalization of the computer analogy. Where [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] supplies the *historical* mechanism (information "lost its body" at the Macy Conferences), O'Gieblyn supplies the *rhetorical* one: the figure quietly drops its quotation marks.
