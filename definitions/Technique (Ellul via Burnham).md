# Technique — Ellul via Burnham

## Author's Definition

In the Introduction to *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Jack Burnham glosses Jacques Ellul's **technique** as the autonomous ordering of the whole of life, of which science and technology are merely its most efficient instances:

> A systems-oriented world is a milieu completely given over to what Jacques Ellul calls technique. Science and technology for Ellul are simply the most efficient forms of technique.

Burnham distils technique's defining attributes from Ellul in a single line:

> It is enough to say about technique, as Ellul interprets it, that its flower is modern Western science; it has no psychic or ethical strictures; it is self-aggrandizing and, most alarming, irreversible.

So: technique is a *milieu* (not a tool); science and technology are its sharpest edge but not its whole; it is **without ethical or psychic strictures**, **self-aggrandizing**, and **irreversible**. `Directly stated`

## Context

Burnham reaches for Ellul at the hinge of his book — the passage where modern sculpture passes from **object** to **system** (the structure named in the concept [[Object and System]]). He needs a name for the condition that makes a *systems-oriented* world possible, and finds it in Ellul's technique: the force that turned "the patterns of unified social living" — art, magic, religion, hunting, shelter-building, medicine, once "merged into a single ritualistic program of survival" — into a partitioned, instrumentalized order. `Directly stated` Technique, "in Ellul's estimation has become the means by which the patterns of unified social living have progressively degenerated," its "pervasive invisibility of systems, systems which seem to operate more for their own sake than for the people they serve," replacing "earlier, direct, more sensual modes of expressing the phenomenon of being alive." `Directly stated`

Burnham treats Ellul's diagnosis as terminal but not, for himself, despairing. He reports Ellul's verdict that, though Ellul "disavows any signs of overt pessimism, he regards technique as a force which will in time destroy all traces of biological life; so technique remains a pernicious disease which Ellul, as a doctor diagnosing a terminal case, sees no hope of curing." `Directly stated` On art specifically, Burnham quotes Ellul directly: "Ecstasy is subject to the world of technique and is its servant." Technique, Burnham summarizes, "channels and diffuses those instincts in man which account for much of his artistic motivation." `Directly stated` Yet Burnham parts from Ellul's value system, which "views the fallibility of the human situation as the greatest good worth saving"; he argues instead for "at least the temporary survival of sculpture through transition from the object to the system" — Kinetic, Light, Environmental, and Cybernetic Art as "technique in the driver's seat." `Directly stated`

The sibling note [[Technics]] is Burnham's own coinage — science and technology welded "into a single word." **Technique** is the larger Ellulian milieu of which technics is the most efficient face; the two notes are deliberately paired, not merged.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Technique vs. [[Technics]] (Burnham).** Burnham's *technics* names the fused authority of *science and technology*; Ellul's *technique* is broader still — the total ordering of all of life, of which "science and technology... are simply the most efficient forms." Technics is technique's leading instance, not its whole. The relation is explicitly subset-to-set in Burnham's own framing. `Directly stated`

- **Technique vs. [[Technology as Metaphor]] (O'Gieblyn).** Kept DISTINCT. O'Gieblyn's concern is *figurative*: the way a technological model (computer, algorithm) becomes the literal idiom for mind, God, and cosmos. Ellul's technique is not a metaphor but an asserted, autonomous *force* — "self-aggrandizing," "irreversible," operating "for [its] own sake" regardless of human ends. The two diagnoses differ in register: captured language hardening into literalism versus a milieu that needs no metaphor to act. `Interpretive synthesis`

- **Technique vs. craft/skill.** Burnham's Ellulian technique is the opposite of the ordinary English sense of "technique" as an artist's manual skill. Here it is the impersonal logic that *dissolves* such skills into a systematized environment — the move "from craft methodology to a reflection of the modern production of goods" that Burnham lists among reification's trends. `Strongly implied`
