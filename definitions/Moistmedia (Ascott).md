# Moistmedia — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

Roy Ascott defines **moistmedia** as the substrate of twenty-first-century art that arises in the convergence of the *dry* (digital, computational) and the *wet* (organic, biological) — a third domain that is neither pure silicon nor pure biology but the fluid interspace where they fuse. The fullest definitional statement is in the *Moist Manifesto* ("The Future Will Be Moist!", 2000):

> Between the dry world of virtuality and the wet world of biology lies a moist domain, a new interspace of potentiality and promise. Moistmedia (comprising bits, atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination) will constitute the substrate of the art of our new century, a transformative art concerned with the construction of a fluid reality.

The manifesto's enumerated theses fix the term's components precisely: "MOIST MEDIA comprises bits, atoms, neurons, and genes"; "MOIST MEDIA is interactive and psychoactive"; "MOIST ART is digitally dry, biologically wet, and spiritually numinous"; "MOISTWARE erodes the boundary between hardware and wetware" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). Moistmedia is thus a *material* (bits + atoms + neurons + genes) that is at once digital, biological, and "spiritually numinous" — the third pole that ties the definition to Ascott's [[Technoetic (Ascott)|technoetic]] thesis ("MOIST MIND is technoetic multiconsciousness").

The future-tense declaration that seeds the term appears one address earlier, in the keynote that gives this definition note its assigned source chapter:

> I'd like to be a little outrageous here and tell you I have seen the future— and it's moist. It's where artificial life and artificial consciousness meet our own wet biology and our telematic society of mind.

(`Directly stated`, ch.29). Here Ascott names the convergence before he coins the noun: "moist" is where artificial life and artificial consciousness meet "our own wet biology and our telematic society of mind" — i.e. the [[Telematics (Ascott)|telematic]] network of distributed minds.

## Context

Ascott introduces moistmedia at the close of *Telematic Embrace* (the late 1990s–2000 essays) as the next step beyond the *dry* interactive and telematic art of the twentieth century. The opening of the same passage states the historical shift the term names: interactive media "replaced the cult of the objet d'art with a process-based culture," and "at the start of this century, we see a further artistic shift, as silicon and pixels merge with molecules and matter" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). Moistmedia is the substrate of that further shift.

The mechanism of the convergence is the rise of the artificial-life and nanotech sciences. In "The Mind of the Museum" Ascott projects: "just as the electronics revolution, which led from telecommunications to the computer, is now taking place in the human brain, so artificial life, engendered in silicon, may increasingly take on a moister reality, eventually integrating with the wet reality we call nature" (`Directly stated`, ch.29). The same chapter frames the *artistic* stakes — auto-constructed agents and artificial-life forms "might become artistic identities, whether in silicon or in the moist terrain of artificial life as it integrates with our own wet biology" (`Directly stated`, ch.29). Moistmedia is the term for that integrated terrain once it becomes the ground of practice.

Definitionally, the note sits **beside the concept** [[Moistmedia]]: the concept note carries the dry/wet/moist tripartition, the "100 Terms" glossary fix ("Moist reality emerges from the biotechnology of artificial life"), and the cross-book lineage; this note isolates Ascott's *explicit definitional act* — the manifesto's component list ("bits, atoms, neurons, and genes") and the "moist domain… interspace" formula (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Differences from Other Uses

No other author in the vault uses the term "moistmedia"; it is Ascott's coinage. But the definition deliberately sits *across* the vault's central opposition, so it must be distinguished from the poles it fuses:

- **Against pure dematerialization / immateriality.** Where Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] and Burnham's [[Object and System|object→system]] arc move art from the discrete material object toward the immaterial system, moistmedia is not an arrival at immateriality but a re-materialization that keeps the immaterial: the dry/informational is folded back into "molecules and matter" without forfeiting the bits, the network, or the numinous. It is the explicit form of Ascott's [[The Rematerialization of Art|re-materialization]] ("immaterial as well as re-materialized—I mean artificial life–based—art", ch.29). It therefore does not pick a side of the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension; it dissolves it (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **Against "virtuality."** Moistmedia is not [[Virtuality]] / VR. In Ascott's tripartition the dry "arid spaces of VR" are only one pole; moist reality is the *biotechnological* interspace that VR alone never reaches. "Dry pixels and wet molecules converge" in moist space — virtuality is the dry term being exceeded (`Directly stated`, ch.31).
- **Against a neutral "media" or "new media."** Unlike a delivery technology, moistmedia has *psychoactive*, living, self-organizing properties ("interactive and psychoactive"; "bottom-up, seeded, and emergent"). It is the substrate of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] taken into the biological register — the moist successor, not a synonym (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **Distinct from "moist" as mere wetness.** Ascott's "moist" is explicitly a *third* state between dry and wet, not a degree of the wet. The manifesto's "MOISTWARE erodes the boundary between hardware and wetware" marks moist as the boundary-eroding interspace itself, not either bounded side (`Directly stated`, ch.31).
