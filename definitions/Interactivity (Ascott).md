# Interactivity — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

For Ascott, **interactivity** is not a single property a work either has or lacks but a *graded distinction between two kinds of system* — one trivial, one nontrivial — turning on whether the work's data is closed and finite or open and able to grow. In his glossary "Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions for the Post-Biological Era," the entry is stated as a flat opposition (`Directly stated`):

> Interactivity The trivial form is a closed system with a finite data set. The nontrivial form has the open-ended capacity to accommodate new variables.

The same distinction is spelled out in the paired glossary entry **FDS and OES**, which adds the decisive question of *who or what is in control* (`Directly stated`):

> FDS are finite data sets: action within pre-designed limits, presented as either a unitary or a so-called interactive experience, with the artist in control. OES are open-ended systems: interaction within networked or evolutive systems that put the user or the environment in control.

So the two poles are:

- **The trivial form (FDS, finite data set):** a *closed system*. The viewer chooses among options the artist has already designed in; "action within pre-designed limits"; what Ascott pointedly calls "a *so-called* interactive experience." The **artist remains in control**, and the data set cannot grow. This is interactivity as menu, branch, or pre-authored response.
- **The nontrivial form (OES, open-ended system):** a *system able to accommodate new variables* — "networked or evolutive systems" — in which the **user or the environment is put in control**. The work is not a finished object presenting choices but an evolving, [[Emergence|emergent]] process whose content is co-produced and unbounded.

The distinction is, in effect, the difference between interaction *with* a predetermined work and interaction *within* an open, growing one. Genuine interactivity, for Ascott, is the nontrivial kind: the relinquishing of authorial control to the [[The Participator|participator]] and the environment.

## Context

The trivial/nontrivial cut is the load-bearing distinction of Ascott's entire program, and it long predates the 1990s glossary that states it most crisply. From his earliest "behaviourist art" of the 1960s onward, Ascott had insisted that art become a *system of interaction* rather than a closed *object of contemplation* — that the artwork move from a thing the viewer regards to a process the viewer enters and changes. The FDS/OES formulation is the mature, cybernetically precise name for that lifelong commitment, drawn from the language of open and closed systems.

What makes the distinction more than a technical taxonomy is its politics of authorship. The trivial form preserves the traditional hierarchy — the artist as author, the viewer as chooser-among-given-options — under a veneer of participation; this is why Ascott names it a "so-called interactive experience." The nontrivial form dissolves that hierarchy: when the user or environment is "in control" and the system can "accommodate new variables," authorship is dispersed into the network and the work becomes genuinely co-created. This is the conceptual engine behind [[Distributed Authorship]], the [[The Participator|participator]] (Ascott's replacement for the spectator), and his account of [[Telematic Art|telematic art]] as an art whose content is the *interaction itself*. The same essay collection warns that much art "that claims to have rejected the inert viewer in favour of the interactive user" still presupposes a "passive, voyeuristic, phantom audience" — precisely the failure of nontrivial interactivity that the FDS/OES distinction is built to expose.

The criterion thus underwrites Ascott's curatorial and pedagogical demand: a work, an interface, or an institution counts as genuinely interactive only if it is *open-ended and evolutive* — able to take in new variables it did not contain at the outset — not merely *responsive* within preset limits.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus Burnham's reciprocal communication.** Ascott's nontrivial interactivity is the direct successor to Jack Burnham's [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the cybernetic, two-way exchange between artwork and viewer that Burnham (in *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, 1968) named as the goal toward which kinetic and cybernetic sculpture was tending, captured in the vault as the [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] tension. Where Burnham described a *responsive object* that registers and answers the viewer's presence, Ascott radicalises the criterion: mere two-way response is not enough if the system stays closed and the artist stays in control (that is only the *trivial* form). The nontrivial form additionally requires that the data set be *open-ended* and that control pass to the user or environment — a step Burnham's responsive-object model anticipates but does not take. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

- **Versus the everyday/industry sense of "interactive."** In common usage (and in much "interactive media"), a work is "interactive" if the user can do anything at all — click, choose, navigate a branching menu. Ascott classes all of this as the *trivial* form and explicitly disparages it as "so-called interactive." His usage reserves the honorific "interactivity" for the open, evolutive, control-relinquishing case, making the term far more demanding than its ordinary meaning. (`Directly stated`)

- **Versus Han's reading of digital participation.** What Ascott celebrates as the participator's seizure of control, Han would read more warily: the dissolution of the authoring distance and the imperative to participate can themselves become a regime (the [[Digital Panopticon|digital panopticon]] of compulsory self-exposure and connection). Ascott's nontrivial interactivity is offered as emancipation — the user "in control"; the vault's surveillance strand reads the same handing-over of the closed work to the open network as a new mode of capture. (`Speculative connection`)

- **Trivial/nontrivial as a system distinction, not a degree of effort.** The cut does not track how *much* the viewer does, but whether the *system* is closed (finite) or open (able to absorb new variables). A demanding, elaborate branching piece with a huge but finite data set is still trivial; a sparse but genuinely open, networked, evolutive piece is nontrivial. The criterion is the openness of the data set and the locus of control, not the quantity of audience activity. (`Strongly implied`)
