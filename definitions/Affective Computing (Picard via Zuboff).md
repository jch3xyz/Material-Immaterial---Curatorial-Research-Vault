# Affective Computing — Rosalind Picard (via Zuboff)

## Author's Definition

"Affective computing" is the name Rosalind Picard, an MIT Media Lab professor, gave to a new field of computer science she founded with her 1997 book of that title. Zuboff reports the field's defining move as the rendering of emotion — conscious and unconscious alike — into machine-readable behavior:

> The key to affective computing, Picard argued, was to render both conscious and unconscious emotion as observable behavior for coding and calculation. A computer, she reasoned, would be able to render your emotions as behavioral information. Affect recognition, as she put it, is "a pattern recognition problem," and "affect expression" is pattern synthesis. The proposition was that "computers can be given the ability to recognize emotions as well as a third-person human observer."

The definition's two technical poles are **affect recognition** (a pattern-recognition problem — reading emotion off the body) and **affect expression** (its inverse, pattern synthesis — a machine performing emotion). The ambition, in Picard's own framing as Zuboff quotes it, is parity with the human onlooker: a computer that recognizes emotion "as well as a third-person human observer" [Directly stated].

What makes the field distinctive, and what makes it consequential for Zuboff's argument, is its reach past the conscious mind. Picard's founding insight was that some emotions are available to consciousness and can be reported "cognitively" ("I feel scared"), whereas others "may elude consciousness but nevertheless be expressed physically in beads of sweat, a widening of the eyes, or a nearly imperceptible tightening of the jaw." Affective computing's wager is that even these unconscious, unspoken signals can be measured "as behavior" — combining facial micro-expression (after Paul Ekman's facial-action coding) with vocal intonation and other physiological signals [Directly stated].

## Context

Zuboff places affective computing inside Chapter 9, "Rendition from the Depths" — the chapter where the extraction of [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral surplus]] migrates from what we do and where we go into the interior of the self, the emotional life. Affective computing is, for her, the scientific engine of that descent: it is what makes the inner life renderable. The chapter's heading-word, [[Rendition|rendition]], names the one-way operation; affective computing supplies the technique by which feeling is converted into observable behavior and then into data.

She is careful that the field "began innocently enough." Picard and her protégé Rana el Kaliouby founded a company, Affectiva, intending therapeutic and medical applications — training a system called MindReader to help autistic children read emotion. Zuboff narrates the company's drift as a parable of the whole undertaking: MIT Media Lab corporate sponsors and then ad agencies "overwhelmed the pair with queries," Picard was eventually "pushed out," and the firm turned "totally off the original mission" toward measuring whether consumers like an advertisement. The "do-good" science was drawn "into the competitive maelstrom for surveillance revenues" — recast as fresh supply for the [[The Prediction Imperative|prediction imperative]] [Directly stated]. The endpoint Kaliouby imagines is "emotion as a service" expanding "from observation to modification" — "happiness as a service," with reward points issued for the moods that keep customers "engaged" — which is why Zuboff treats affective computing not as neutral emotion-reading but as a frontier of behavioral control feeding [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance capitalism]] [Strongly implied].

The sibling concept note Affective Computing holds the broader conceptual treatment; this note records the explicit definitional passage as Zuboff transmits it from Picard.

## Differences from Other Uses

**vs. Byung-Chul Han's affect (see [[Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect (Han)]]).** Han, in *Psychopolitics*, draws a careful three-way distinction in which *affect* is the most fleeting, subjective, sub-narrative term — strictly subjective, resistant to recounting. For Han the analytic point of separating *affect* from *emotion* and *feeling* is to locate the exploitable unit of [[Emotional Capitalism|emotional capitalism]] (he names *emotion*, not affect, as what neoliberal power steers). Picard's "affect" runs the opposite way: it is precisely the unconscious, bodily, sub-cognitive register — the bead of sweat, the tightened jaw — that she insists *can* be objectified, measured, and computed "as observable behavior." Where Han treats affect as the term that escapes capture, affective computing is the engineering claim that nothing escapes capture, including the unspoken depths. The two are convergent in their object (capitalism's appetite for the emotional interior) but opposed in their premise about the renderability of the inner life [Interpretive synthesis].

**vs. the everyday and HCI senses.** In ordinary computer-science usage "affective computing" can mean any system that detects or simulates emotion (sentiment analysis, expressive interface agents). Zuboff narrows the term to its economic logic: affect rendered as behavioral information becomes raw material for prediction, so that emotion-recognition is never merely descriptive but always already extractive — "emotion as a service" sold into [[Behavioral Futures Markets|behavioral futures markets]] [Strongly implied].

**vs. the surveillance/self-tracking it serves.** Affective computing is one technique within the larger apparatus Zuboff anatomizes; it supplies the [[The Prediction Imperative|prediction imperative]] with a new and deeper class of [[The Body as Data|the body as data]]. It treats affect recognition as a [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]]-recognition problem — aligning the field with the broader cybernetic-informatic premise that the human can be read as pattern. It is distinct from, but feeds, the ambient instrumentation that fuses with biometrics from wearables to build "an emotional profile for you" [Directly stated].
