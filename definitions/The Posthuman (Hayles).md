# The Posthuman — N. Katherine Hayles

## Author's Definition

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], the posthuman is not a creature, a cyborg body, or a future species but a **point of view** — a configuration of assumptions about subjectivity. She introduces it with a question and a deliberately non-prescriptive list:

> What is the posthuman? Think of it as a point of view characterized by the following assumptions. (I do not mean this list to be exclusive or definitive. Rather, it names elements found at a variety of sites. It is meant to be suggestive rather than prescriptive. )

Four assumptions characterize the view. Each is stated verbatim below.

**1. Pattern over instantiation.**

> First, the posthuman view privileges informational pattern over material instantiation, so that embodiment in a biological substrate is seen as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life.

**2. Consciousness as epiphenomenon.**

> Second, the posthuman view considers consciousness, regarded as the seat of human identity in the Western tradition long before Descartes thought he was a mind thinking, as an epiphenomenon, as an evolutionary upstart trying to claim that it is the whole show when in actuality it is onlya minor sideshow.

**3. The body as the original prosthesis.**

> Third, the posthuman view thinks of the body as the original prosthesis we all learn to manipulate, so that extending or replacing the body with other prostheses becomes a continuation of a process that began before we were born.

**4. Seamless articulation with intelligent machines.**

> Fourth, and most important, by these and other means, the posthuman view configures human being so that it can be seamlessly articulated with intelligent machines. In the posthuman, there are no essential differences or absolute demarcations between bodily existence and computer simulation, cybernetic mechanism and biological organism, robot teleology and human goals.

## Context

Hayles offers this definition early in the chapter that frames the whole project of *How We Became Posthuman*, immediately after distinguishing her three intertwined stories — how information lost its body, how [[The Cyborg]] became a cultural icon, and how the human gave way to the posthuman. The four-assumption list is the book's compact definitional anchor; it specifies what *the posthuman* names before the historical narrative of [[Cybernetics]] elaborates how the view was constructed.

Crucially, the definition is a definition of a **viewpoint**, not of an entity. Hayles is explicit that the construction of the posthuman does not require a literal cyborg:

> Although these examples foreground the cybernetic aspect of the posthuman, it is important to recognize that the construction of the posthuman does not require the subject to be a literal cyborg. Whether or not interventions have been made on the body, new models of subjectivity emerging from such fields as cognitive science and artificial life imply that even a biologically unaltered Homo sapiens counts as posthuman. The defining characteristics involve the construction of subjectivity, not the presence of nonbiological components.

The first assumption — pattern over instantiation — is the load-bearing one for the rest of the book: it is the move that recasts [[Embodiment]] as contingent and renders the body a kind of data ([[The Body as Data]]). It sets up the central opposition Hayles tracks throughout, [[Embodiment vs Information]], and the broader vault axis of [[Material vs Immaterial]]. The fourth assumption ("seamlessly articulated with intelligent machines") is the one Hayles flags as "most important," and it is what positions the posthuman against the autonomous, self-possessing [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]].

This definition file is the verbatim anchor for the broader concept note [[Posthumanism]], which holds the historical development, the critique, and Hayles's own counter-dream of an embodied posthuman.

## Differences from Other Uses

Hayles's posthuman is markedly **not** the techno-utopian posthuman of Hans Moravec or Marvin Minsky — the disembodied consciousness uploaded to a machine, the "postbiological" future she explicitly treats with irony. That popular version takes the first assumption (pattern over instantiation) to its limit and concludes that the body can be discarded. Hayles instead uses the definition diagnostically: she names the assumptions in order to contest the erasure of embodiment they smuggle in. Her stated aim is "to put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased in contemporary discussions about cybernetic subjects."

Her posthuman also differs from a purely affirmative or liberatory reading. Hayles observes that the cybernetic posthuman *shares* with [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] an "emphasis on cognition rather than embodiment" — quoting Gibson's "data made flesh" — so that to the extent the posthuman constructs embodiment as the instantiation of thought, "it continues the liberal tradition rather than disrupts it." This is sharper and more ambivalent than the celebratory cyborg politics with which the term is often associated.

Within the vault, this is a Hayles-specific definition note and should not be collapsed into the everyday or science-fiction sense of "posthuman," nor into the concept note [[Posthumanism]], which carries the wider argument. The `(Hayles)` qualifier marks a real definition file.
