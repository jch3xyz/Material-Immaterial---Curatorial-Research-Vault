# Plasma — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

Plasma is the **fourth state of matter** — distinct from solid, liquid, and gas. It is the state certain gases reach when they are *ionized*, that is, given a negative electrical charge. In Burnham's account, the everyday technologies of fluorescent lighting, neon, and arc welding are already forms of **low-temperature plasma**, while high-temperature plasma remains, as of his writing, beyond reliable human control. Burnham frames the control of plasma phenomena as the future field of the Light artist.

> Plasma, itself, is the fourth state of matter: being neither solid, liquid, or gas, but the state that certain gases reach when they are "ionized" or given a negative electrical charge.

(`Directly stated`)

## Context

Burnham introduces plasma in the closing pages of "Light as Sculpture Medium," after surveying the plasma display panel of the PLATO computer-teaching system. Having traced the Light artist's progression away from the discrete, formed, solid object and toward illumination as such, he reaches for the most extreme dematerialized medium imaginable: a state of matter that is no stable body at all, but charged, mobile, and plastic. The definition is therefore not a neutral physics gloss; it is positioned as the horizon of [[Light as a Sculpture Medium|light-based sculpture]] — the artist's medium pushed to where matter itself becomes "the barely understood."

> The mysteries of controlling plasma phenomena could provide the ultimate field of exploration for the Light artist.

(`Directly stated`)

Burnham then speculates, at length and tentatively, that coherent bodies of plasma "charged with energy, highly mobile and plastic, and even possessing some form of intelligence" might underlie the U.F.O. phenomenon, and reports talk among computer designers "of fifth- or sixth-generation computers which build and rearrange their own circuits of plasma." These passages are flagged by Burnham himself as lacking "scientific substantiality," but they show why plasma attracts him: it names matter at its least object-like, at the threshold of the formless and the living. For the artist, he concludes, the value resides in what "remain[s] unknown and fantastic" (`Strongly implied` that plasma functions for Burnham as a limit-case of the dematerialized art medium rather than a settled technical proposal).

## Differences from Other Uses

This is the vault's first **physics-of-matter** definition. Where other authors invoke immateriality metaphorically or informationally, Burnham's plasma is literal: an actual fourth physical state.

- Against [[Dematerialization|Lippard's dematerialization]] (the art object thinning toward document, idea, and information): Burnham does not abandon the material substrate but seeks its most rarefied, least solid physical form. Plasma is dematerialized *matter*, not the replacement of matter by concept — the opposite vector from conceptual art's flight from the object.
- Against the **informatic** immateriality of Hayles, Harari, and Kurzweil (the body or world rendered as pattern/data): plasma is energy-charged ionized gas, not abstracted information. It sits on the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension as a physical pole that nonetheless behaves like the immaterial — luminous, fluid, without fixed boundary.
- Against everyday usage, where "plasma" most often names a blood component or, loosely, a glowing screen: Burnham fixes the strict physical sense (the ionized fourth state) and then extends it speculatively toward intelligence and self-organizing computation, an extension proper to his own argument and not to standard usage (`Interpretive synthesis`).
