# Surveillance as a Service (SVaaS) — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Zuboff coins the term as a corrective relabeling of an existing industry category. Surveillance — the capture and sale of [[Behavioral Surplus]] — has, she observes, "become a service in its own right," sold by firms that market themselves under the familiar software-business label "software-as-a-service" (SaaS). Her point is that the label misnames the product (*Directly stated*):

> In another trend, surveillance in the interest of behavioral surplus capture and sale has become a service in its own right. Such companies are often referred to as “software-as-a-service” or SaaS, but they are more accurately termed “surveillance as a service,” or “SVaaS.”

The definition is thus diagnostic: the commodity these firms actually sell is not software but *surveillance* — the rendering of human behavior into legible, tradable signal — and the SaaS framing functions as a euphemism that conceals the true product line (*Strongly implied*).

## Context

Surveillance-as-a-service appears in Chapter 5 ("The Elaboration of Surveillance Capitalism: Kidnap, Corner, Compete"), in the passage where Zuboff tracks how the surveillance-revenue logic spreads beyond the born-digital companies that pioneered it. She names two adjacent trends. First, "new and established companies from every sector — including retail, finance, fitness, insurance, automotive, travel, hospitality, health, and education — are joining the migratory path to surveillance revenues." Second — the SVaaS trend — surveillance itself is unbundled and "packaged" as a standalone product line that any client firm can buy off the shelf (*Directly stated*).

She gives three genres of SVaaS firm as evidence (all *Directly stated*):

- **App-based lending.** A lender "instantly establishes creditworthiness based on detailed mining of an individual's smartphone and other online behaviors," down to "the frequency with which you charge your phone battery" and "how many contacts you have listed in your phone." One CEO boasts that his firm "analyzes 10,000 signals per customer" and is "able to get in and really understand the daily life of these customers." Originally built for the "unbanked" in Africa, the technique migrated to "exploiting American families … economically hollowed out by the financial crisis."
- **Vetting of tenants and employees.** A firm "sells deep vetting of potential employees and tenants to employers and landlords," requiring a prospective tenant to "grant full access to all social media profiles," then "scrapes your site activity," runs it through natural-language processing, and reports "everything from your personality to your 'financial stress level,'" exposing protected-status information such as pregnancy and age, "with no opportunity for affected individuals to view or contest information."
- **Web-scraping prediction firms.** Firms like hiQ "scour the internet for behavioral surplus about individuals, either to sell it or to analyze and fabricate it into lucrative prediction products" — what legal scholar Frank Pasquale calls "the dark market for personal data." hiQ assigns "flight risk" scores so clients can "preemptively terminate someone who is predicted to be a 'flight risk.'"

Across all three, Zuboff stresses the asymmetry of the bargain: although individuals formally "opt in," "it is those who have less money and fewer options who are trapped in this Faustian bargain in which privacy is forfeit to social participation." SVaaS is therefore not a benign sub-market but the [[Behavioral Surplus]] supply chain sold as a turnkey commodity, fueling [[Behavioral Futures Markets]] and [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] for any buyer (*Interpretive synthesis*). It is the elaboration phase of the broader thesis: surveillance capitalism's "logic for translating investment into revenue is highly adaptive and exceptionally lucrative as long as raw-material supplies are free and law is kept at bay" (*Directly stated*).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **SVaaS vs. SaaS.** The whole force of the term is the one-letter substitution. "Software-as-a-service" frames the offering as a tool — neutral infrastructure rented by subscription. Zuboff insists the actual deliverable is surveillance: the product is the watching and the prediction it yields, not the code. The relabeling is a claim about misnaming, kin to her broader argument about [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]] and the euphemisms ("anonymization," "deidentification") that launder the practice (*Directly stated* / *Interpretive synthesis*).
- **SVaaS vs. surveillance capitalism in general.** [[Surveillance Capitalism]] names the whole economic order in which firms extract behavioral surplus for their *own* prediction products (Google, Facebook). SVaaS is a narrower and later specialization: surveillance offered *as a service to third parties* — the capture-and-sale capability itself sold as a product line, so that any retailer, landlord, or HR department can rent the instrument without building it (*Strongly implied*).
- **SVaaS vs. the [[Quantified Self]].** The quantified self is voluntary self-measurement — the rendering surface. SVaaS is the commercial machinery that captures such behavioral exhaust (often without the subject's meaningful knowledge) and resells it. The first supplies raw material; the second monetizes it (*Strongly implied*).
- **SVaaS vs. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and Harari's [[Dataism]].** Han's digital panopticon turns on voluntary self-exposure (the subject illuminates itself) and Harari's [[Dataism]] is a worldview that sacralizes data flow. Zuboff's SVaaS is neither a phenomenology of complicity nor a quasi-religion but a concrete *market category* with named firms, products, and buyers — surveillance dispensed by subscription, with the "opt-in" exposing the very poverty of consent (*Interpretive synthesis*).
