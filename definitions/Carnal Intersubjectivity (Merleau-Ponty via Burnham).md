# Carnal Intersubjectivity (Merleau-Ponty via Burnham)

## Author's Definition

"Carnal intersubjectivity" is the term Jack Burnham borrows from Maurice Merleau-Ponty to name the *relational perceptual field that arises between the embodied viewer and the object(s) among which he stands and is in turn perceived*. Burnham introduces it at the climax of his account of [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)|phenomenalism]], describing the encounter with several pieces of [[Object Art]] at once: "What arises from the first glance is what Merleau-Ponty called 'carnal intersubjectivity.'" The phrase names not a property of the object and not a state of the subject, but the field that opens up in the act of looking — the perceptual relation itself, generated "from the first glance," before any analytical reconstruction of the object as a stable geometric ideal (`Directly stated`).

The "carnal" qualifier locates this intersubjectivity in the body: it is the fleshly, situated act of perceiving while moving among things, not a meeting of disembodied minds. The "intersubjective" qualifier marks that the relation runs in both directions — the locus of Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology, in Burnham's gloss, is "the act of perception and bodily gesture while standing among objects-and in turn being perceived by other beings" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Burnham deploys the term at the hinge of *Beyond Modern Sculpture* — Chapter 4, "Form Exhaustion and the Rise of [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)|Phenomenalism]]" — where he argues that twentieth-century sculpture, having exhausted both vitalism and formal-reductivism, relocates the meaning of the work from the made form into "a series of perceptual experiences" staged in the viewer. The philosophical engine for this shift is phenomenology, and especially [[Maurice Merleau-Ponty]], from whom Burnham takes perception as "essentially an existential act by the observer." Carnal intersubjectivity is the name for *what that existential act produces*.

The immediate scene is gallery ambience. Burnham contrasts the old "inspection tour with an ideal vantage point" — viewers going "from sculpture to sculpture like shoppers at a sale" — with the experiential environments of Judd, Morris, and Flavin, in which "two or three pieces positioned so as to engage him at the same time" deny any single ideal viewpoint. "Each view has its contradictions and illuminations taken as they come"; the object does not present a finished image but "'arose' through the viewer's perceptual ordering." What "arises from the first glance," in this thicket of partial views, is carnal intersubjectivity (`Directly stated`). Through it "the viewer becomes aware of a set of latent and primordial truths concerning his habits of perception through the nature of the objects involved" — the concept's payoff: the relation reveals the perceiver to himself (`Directly stated`).

Burnham grounds the term in Merleau-Ponty's broader claim that perception delivers more than analysis can — that one "cannot *see* a cube as its geometrical definition presents it" but "can only *think* it" — so that the lived perceptual field is irreducible to a "list structure" of an object's properties, and meaning lies instead in "the concretion of a temporal Gestalt, an aggregate of sequential views that distort each other like the inconsistencies in a Cezanne painting." Carnal intersubjectivity is the experiential correlate of this irreducibility: the body's relation to the object, not a catalogue of the object's attributes (`Strongly implied`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Against the "list structure" / computational object.** Burnham explicitly sets carnal intersubjectivity against the computational model of perception: "list structures might adequately define an 'object' for a computer," but "perception allows us to know far more about things than we realize, or than we are given by inductive analysis." The term thus marks the same divide the vault tracks between lived [[Embodiment]] and informatic abstraction — perception as a carnal relation versus perception as a data record. It is the art-historical near-opposite of [[The Body as Data]] and of treating perception as the manipulation of a [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] of attributes (`Strongly implied`, `Interpretive synthesis`).
- **Beside [[Embodiment]] (Hayles) and [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]].** Where Hayles's [[Embodiment]] is a general claim that information is always instantiated in a body, carnal intersubjectivity is the specific *perceptual relation* that embodied seeing produces in front of an artwork — the primary-text root, in the vault's art corpus, of perception as a bodily, situated, reciprocal act. It is the relational field that [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)|phenomenalism]] stages; phenomenalism is the aesthetic program, carnal intersubjectivity is its phenomenological mechanism.
- **Against [[Idealism]] / the "ideal vantage point."** The term is defined precisely by *not* being the detached, single-point, geometric view of the object as immutable ideal. It is plural, partial, mobile, and embodied — what "arises" rather than what is "known."
- **A borrowing, not a coinage.** Unlike [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]], which is Burnham's own art-historical category, "carnal intersubjectivity" is attributed by Burnham to Merleau-Ponty ("what Merleau-Ponty called"). The "(Merleau-Ponty via Burnham)" qualifier records this provenance: it is Merleau-Ponty's phenomenological term as Burnham imports and applies it to the perception of Object sculpture. [[Maurice Merleau-Ponty]] remains a forward-stub author in the vault (named substantively but with no book yet in `raw/`); his own development of the term awaits the ingest of his work.

## Related Concepts

- [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] — Burnham's aesthetic program for which carnal intersubjectivity supplies the phenomenological mechanism: the work stages a perceptual situation, and what "arises from the first glance" within it is this carnal-intersubjective field.
- [[Embodiment]] — the body as the ground of perception; carnal intersubjectivity is the *relation* embodied seeing produces, the art-corpus correlate of Hayles's claim that perception is never disembodied.
- [[Object Art]] — the de-idealized, multi-viewpoint Minimal sculpture (Judd, Morris, Flavin) whose ambient arrangement denies an ideal vantage point and so elicits carnal intersubjectivity from "the first glance."
- [[The Observer]] — the perceiving, situated viewer who "must reconstruct the sculpture for himself" and is "in turn being perceived"; carnal intersubjectivity names the reciprocal relation in which the observer becomes aware of his own "habits of perception."
- [[Maurice Merleau-Ponty]] — the phenomenologist from whom Burnham borrows the term; forward-stub author, realized as a wiki-link target here because his perception-as-existential-act is the philosophical engine of the chapter.
