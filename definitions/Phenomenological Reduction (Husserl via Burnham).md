# Phenomenological Reduction (Husserl via Burnham)

## Author's Definition

Burnham reports Edmund Husserl's *phenomenological reduction* as the method by which "pure" conscious experience is distilled — incrementally, on its own terms — "unhindered by all previous experiences, abstractions, or presuppositions." It is the operation that yields Husserl's "absolute consciousness," a state of mind reached by suspending the habitual, second-hand modes of knowing and inspecting consciousness "increment by increment" for its own sake. The reduction is what makes Husserl's antidote to the "crisis in European science" possible: a way back to the modes of consciousness themselves, beneath the abstract picture of reality that formal science builds for itself (`Directly stated`).

Verbatim:

> As an antidote, Husserl formulated his absolute consciousness, a state of mind whereupon "pure" conscious experience could be distilled unhindered by all previous experiences, abstractions, or presuppositions. This process Husserl called phenomenological reduction.

The reduction's central move, in Burnham's gloss, is a refusal of the everyday stance toward perception:

> In part, this involves a rejection of the natural attitude of cognition, which serves, according to the phenomenologist, to obscure the perceiver from the true nature of his perceptions.

## Context

The definition appears in Chapter 4 of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, "Form Exhaustion and the Rise of Phenomenalism," where Burnham imports phenomenology as the philosophical engine of his titular coinage [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]]. Burnham reconstructs the lineage: Maurice Merleau-Ponty's relevance to art "postdates an awareness that had its start at the beginning of the present century with Edmund Husserl's *Logical Investigations*." Husserl, facing a "crisis in European science," "saw the need for studying the mechanisms of consciousness by which objectivity was reached" — so that "the raw materials of phenomenology became the modes of consciousness itself" (`Directly stated`).

What the reduction *does* in Burnham's larger argument is supply the method by which sculpture's concern can shift from the form of the object to the *experience of perceiving it*. Husserl observed — "as we have in relation to formalism," Burnham notes, drawing the parallel explicitly — that science "tended to produce an increasingly abstract, if not artificial, picture of reality for itself, derived less and less from sensory data and more by systems of formal logic." The phenomenological reduction is the corrective: it returns attention to the perceptual act itself. Burnham extends this from "empirical psychology" to "transcendental phenomenology," "an analytical method that, on its own terms, could be used to investigate dreams, memories, obsessions, sensual impressions of all kinds — in fact, anything that registered intentionally in human consciousness," consciousness being "defined by intention" (`Directly stated`).

Burnham is candid that the reduction is an arduous, specialist discipline — "a bewildering process for anyone but a philosopher trained in the phenomenological method" — and it is the *intent* of the method, not the technique, that he carries into art history: the relocation of meaning from the made form to the perceiving subject. This is why phenomenological reduction is, for the vault, the philosophical root beneath Burnham's account of [[Object Art|Object / Minimal sculpture]] as a perceptual apparatus and beneath the reading of seeing "as essentially an existential act by the observer" that he takes from Merleau-Ponty (`Strongly implied`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **A reported, not original, definition.** This is Husserl's term as Burnham *reports* it. The note is filed under Burnham because the verbatim wording ("distilled unhindered by all previous experiences, abstractions, or presuppositions"; "absolute consciousness"; the "rejection of the natural attitude of cognition") and the art-historical use are Burnham's. [[Edmund Husserl]] (forward-stub) is the source figure; this note is the specific method-term. Same relation as `Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)` (Bergson's coinage, Burnham's wording) to its source figure.
- **Distinct from [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] the concept.** Phenomenalism is Burnham's *art-historical* category — the post-formalist aesthetic in which the work's subject is the experience of perception. The phenomenological reduction is the *philosophical method* Burnham borrows to underwrite it: the technique of distilling pure conscious experience. The concept names the aesthetic; this note names its imported engine. The two are cross-linked, not equated.
- **Not the empiricist "phenomenalism" of sense-data.** Burnham's reduction descends from Husserl's transcendental phenomenology, the disciplined inspection of "the modes of consciousness itself," not from the empiricist doctrine that reduces objects to actual and possible sense impressions. The reduction *brackets* the "natural attitude," whereas sense-data phenomenalism takes sensory appearances as the basement of reality.
- **A method, not a metaphysics of life.** Unlike `Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)` — a non-material *force* posited within matter — the phenomenological reduction posits nothing; it is a procedure of suspension and inspection. Its product, "absolute consciousness," is a *state of mind* reached by subtraction (of presupposition, abstraction, prior experience), not an essence added to the world.
- **The body enters only with Merleau-Ponty.** Husserl's reduction as Burnham states it is a discipline of *consciousness* and intention; the turn to perception as an *existential bodily act* — and to "carnal intersubjectivity" — is Merleau-Ponty's development of Husserl, which is where the link to [[Embodiment]] is forged. The reduction is the precondition; the carnal, situated perceiving body is the later inflection (`Strongly implied`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] — the art-historical aesthetic the reduction underwrites; Burnham imports phenomenological reduction to explain how meaning relocates from form to the perceptual act.
- [[Embodiment]] — Husserl's reduction is the method that opens onto Merleau-Ponty's "existential act" of seeing; perception, returned to "its own sake," becomes grounded in the perceiving body. The phenomenological correlate of the body-as-ground-of-perception that Hayles theorizes.
- [[Idealism]] — Burnham frames the larger shift as "transcendent idealism" giving way to "transcendent phenomenalism"; the reduction is the operation by which attention turns from the immutable ideal (the icon) to the staged experience of seeing.
- [[Form Exhaustion]] — the reduction belongs to the successor program that arises once formalism and vitalism run out of new forms; phenomenology supplies the new program its method.
- [[Object Art]] — the de-idealized Object / Minimal sculpture whose meaning Burnham reads, via the reduction, as "a series of perceptual experiences" staged in the viewer.
- [[Edmund Husserl]] — source figure (*Logical Investigations*; the "crisis in European science"; "absolute consciousness"); forward-stub. Burnham's point of origin for twentieth-century phenomenology.
