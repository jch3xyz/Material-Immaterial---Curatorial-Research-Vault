# Supervenience — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

Ascott gives **supervenience** a single compressed formulation in his glossary "Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions for the Postbiological Era":

> Supervenience Mind is supervenient on the activity of its interfaces, whether organic or telematic, and although mind is ubiquitous and always present, it requires matter to become manifest.

`Directly stated`. The entry borrows the philosophy-of-mind term *supervenience* — the relation in which a higher-level property cannot vary without some change in its lower-level base — and bends it to his own purposes. For Ascott, mind supervenes not on the brain as such but on the **activity of its interfaces**, and he is explicit that those interfaces may be **organic or telematic** (biological bodies or networked technological systems alike). The definition holds two claims in tension: mind is "ubiquitous and always present" (a panpsychist-leaning, field-like ubiquity), yet it "requires matter to become manifest" (no appearance without a material substrate to carry it).

## Context

The term appears in chapter 32, Ascott's alphabetical lexicon of coinages and repurposed terms for what he calls the "postbiological era." Read against the surrounding entries, supervenience is the keystone metaphysical wager beneath his whole project: it licenses the claim that telematic networks are not merely conduits for already-formed minds but are themselves interfaces *on which* mind can supervene `Strongly implied`. This is the philosophical permission slip for `[[Telematic Art]]`, `[[The Telematic Embrace]]`, and the larger `[[Technoetics]]` programme — if mind is what supervenes on interface activity, then building new interfaces is, quite literally, building new conditions for mind.

The formulation sits directly on the vault's keystone `[[Material vs Immaterial]]` tension and is its single most concentrated statement on the Ascott side. Mind is treated as immaterial in its ubiquity ("always present") but strictly dependent on matter for manifestation — neither a pure idealism nor a reductive materialism, but a relation of dependence that runs *through* the interface `Interpretive synthesis`. It pairs the ubiquity half with his panpsychist commitments (`[[Panpsychism]]`, the "World-mind" / "consciousness-at-large" entries elsewhere in the same glossary) and the manifestation half with his insistence, voiced across the book, that "we are not junking the body" — that embodiment is redistributed across interfaces rather than discarded (`[[Embodiment]]`, `[[The Interface as Doorway]]`) `Strongly implied`.

## Differences from Other Uses

"Supervenience" is a standard term of analytic philosophy of mind, but no other author in the vault uses the word; Ascott's appropriation is idiosyncratic `Directly stated`. The contrasts with the vault's neighbouring positions on mind are sharp:

- In its canonical philosophical use, supervenience is most often invoked by **physicalists** to say the mental supervenes on the *physical/neural* base (no mental difference without a physical difference) — a thesis that tends to *contain* mind within matter. Ascott inverts the emphasis: he relocates the supervenience base from brain to **interface** (organic *or* telematic) and couples it to a ubiquity claim, so that the doctrine ends up *distributing* mind across networks rather than localising it in skulls `Interpretive synthesis`.
- Against the Hayles/Moravec/Kurzweil line on `[[Disembodied Information as the Soul]]` and substrate-independent `[[Mind Uploading]]`, Ascott's "requires matter to become manifest" is a refusal of pure disembodiment: mind always needs *some* material interface to appear, even if that interface is telematic rather than fleshly. This places him closer to Hayles's insistence on `[[Embodiment]]` than to the patternist dream of mind without matter `Strongly implied`.
- It overlaps with O'Gieblyn's `[[Panpsychism]]` and `[[Integrated Information Theory]]` and with `[[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]]` in granting mind a fundamental, ubiquitous status, but Ascott declines the metaphysical or theological framing those discussions carry, routing the question instead through art, interface, and `[[Distributed Cognition]]` `Strongly implied`.
- It is not the dualism of `[[The Mind-Body Problem]]` in the Cartesian sense, nor a flat idealism: matter is not derivative of mind here, it is the *condition of mind's manifestation*. The relation is one of dependence-for-appearance, not of substance `Interpretive synthesis`.
