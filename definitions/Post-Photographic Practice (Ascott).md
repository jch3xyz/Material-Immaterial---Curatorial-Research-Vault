# Post-Photographic Practice ‚Äî Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

For Roy Ascott, **post-photographic practice** is the condition photography enters once the photograph ceases to be a stable, finished material object and becomes an unstable, layered, viewer-transformable datum within [[Hypermedia]]. The definitional sentence is given negatively, as the giving-way of one regime to another: "photography as a stable medium is giving way to a practice that celebrates instability, uncertainty, incompleteness, and transformation." `Directly stated`

He fixes the historical marker explicitly ‚Äî "We are at the beginning of the era of post-photographic practice" ‚Äî and defines the practice through its technology, which operates in three distinct registers: it "captures images ('seen' images from still, video, and other cameras), constructs images ('unseen' data from remote sensors and data banks), and generates images (from raw numbers)." Capture is the residue of optical-chemical photography; construction and generation are new, producing images that no scene ever stood before a lens to make. `Directly stated`

The decisive clause of the definition is relational rather than technical: "the processes of transformation I have described are now in the hands of the viewer as much as the artist." Post-photographic practice is therefore not a new style of image-making but a new ontology of the image ‚Äî the photograph as a permeable, recomposable node in "a media flow that is ‚Äî in every serious sense ‚Äî unending and ubiquitous," whose final form lies with the viewing subject. `Directly stated`

## Context

The definition appears in "Photography at the Interface" (1992), an exhibition essay collected in *Telematic Embrace*. Ascott introduces the term to name a change of *state*, not of degree: the move from "the old economy of the image," in which a finished photograph framed a "composite moment" for a viewer to absorb, to a practice in which the artist's interest migrates from "things seen" toward "what cannot be seen at the surface level of reality" ‚Äî "human relationships, systems, forces, and fields" ‚Äî and toward "how frameworks are constructed from which image worlds can emerge, an open-ended process." `Directly stated`

Within Ascott's larger project the term is one regional instance of his thesis that art has become an experiential, telematic, reciprocally constructed system. Post-photographic practice is the test case for the object‚Üísystem spine that organizes the [[New Media Art]] / [[Systems Esthetics]] cluster: the photograph stops being a discrete material thing and becomes a datum in flow, which makes it a form of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] and of [[Reciprocal Communication]] rather than a medium of authored, finished statements. The fuller conceptual unpacking ‚Äî the verisimilitude‚Üímodeling shift, the absorption‚Üíimmersion shift, the aquatic metaphor ‚Äî lives in the companion concept note [[Post-Photographic Practice]]; this note isolates Ascott's own definitional language. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. [[Dematerialization]] ([[Lucy Lippard]])** ‚Äî Lippard's term names the conceptual-art object's recession into idea, process, and documentation. Ascott's term continues that trajectory but applies it specifically to the *photographic* image, and grounds the dissolution in a named technology (digital capture/construction/generation, sensors, data banks) rather than in a curatorial-conceptual program. Ascott does not cite Lippard; the relation is parallel extension, not derivation. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. the everyday "digital photography"** ‚Äî Ascott explicitly refuses the reductive framing. To the question of what distinguishes digital from regular photography, his answer is not the material-vs-immaterial substrate ("the material image on paper" vs "the immaterial apparition at the computer interface") but that the post-photographic image's "object lies in a virtual space ... layered, relational, and constantly shifting in content and context, depending on the behaviour and consciousness of the viewer." The defining difference is viewer-transformability, not the swap of silver for pixels. `Directly stated`
