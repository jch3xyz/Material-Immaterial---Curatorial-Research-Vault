# Archetypes — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows names the term in a single sentence, after separating merely *surprising* systems from *perverse* ones:

> "We call the system structures that produce such common patterns of problematic behavior archetypes." (*Directly stated*)

An archetype, in Meadows's precise usage, is therefore not a character, a myth, or an image, but a **recurring system structure** — a configuration of stocks, `Feedback Loops`, and goals that keeps generating the same troublesome behavior wherever it appears. The archetype is defined by what it *produces* (a "common pattern of problematic behavior") and by *where the trouble lives* (in the structure, not in the actors). She lists the behaviors these structures throw off:

> "Some of the behaviors these archetypes manifest are addiction, drift to low performance, and escalation. These are so prevalent" (*Directly stated*)

The same structures, once *diagnosed*, carry two further names that are the whole point of the term. Because their damage "is actually a consequence of system structure" and so cannot be fixed by "Blaming, disciplining, firing, twisting policy levers harder... tinkering at the margins," Meadows calls them **traps**:

> "That is why I call these archetypes 'traps.'" (*Directly stated*)

And because the same structural diagnosis opens an exit — "by reformulating goals, by weakening, strengthening, or altering feedback loops, by adding new feedback loops" — she calls them, in the same breath, **opportunities**:

> "That is why I call these archetypes not just traps, but opportunities." (*Directly stated*)

So the archetype is one object under three names: a *structure* that produces a pattern, a *trap* because the pattern resists blame and force, and an *opportunity* because it yields to redesign. This note fixes the term "archetypes"; the fuller working-through of the eight cases — their traps and their ways out — lives in the concept [[System Traps]], of which this is the definitional companion (*Interpretive synthesis*).

## Context

The definition opens Chapter 5, "System Traps... and Opportunities," and does the chapter's foundational sorting. Meadows first insists that most of what confounds us about systems is not pathological but native: "Delays, nonlinearities, lack of firm boundaries, and other properties of systems that surprise us" are "not properties that can or should be changed," and being less surprised "is mainly a matter of learning to expect, appreciate, and use the world's complexity" (*Directly stated*). Against that baseline she draws the line the term depends on:

> "But some systems are more than surprising. They are perverse. These are the systems that are structured in ways that produce truly problematic behavior; they cause us great trouble." (*Directly stated*)

Archetypes are the recurring structures of that perverse minority. Naming them is not, for Meadows, a diagnostic end in itself — "Understanding archetypal problem-generating structures is not enough. Putting up with them is impossible. They need to be changed" (*Directly stated*). The term is thus load-bearing for the book's method: it converts a scattering of famous troubles (the drug war, the arms race, the tragedy of the commons, addiction, GNP-chasing) into a small catalogue of *structures*, so that the response can shift from punishing actors to rewiring loops and goals. Every case in the chapter is then presented under a "THE TRAP / THE WAY OUT" schema, which is the archetype seen first as trap and then as opportunity.

The move rests on Meadows's deeper premise, [[Structure Determines Behavior]]: if behavior follows structure, then a recurring behavior implies a recurring structure, and the recurring structure — not the changing cast of actors blamed for it — is the real object of study and repair. This is also why the archetypes point forward to [[Leverage Points]]: each "way out" (re-goal a loop, add or strengthen a feedback) sits high on the later leverage ranking, while the "standard responses" that fail (forcing the same lever, tinkering at the margins) sit at the low-leverage end.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Not the Jungian / literary archetype.** In psychology and literary criticism an "archetype" is a primordial image, character, or motif (the hero, the shadow, the mother). Meadows's archetype is *structural and behavioral*: it is a feedback configuration that generates a behavior over time, with no reference to a collective unconscious or a symbolic repertoire. The vault's `symbols/` notes handle archetypal *images*; this note handles Meadows's *structures*. The two senses share only the word.

- **A narrower coinage than the systems-thinking "system archetypes" literature.** The term belongs to a broader systems-dynamics tradition (Senge and others catalogue "system archetypes" such as "fixes that fail," "shifting the burden," "limits to growth"). Meadows adopts that tradition's vocabulary but presents a specific working subset in Chapter 5, each fused to a concrete "way out," and foregrounds the *trap/opportunity* duality as the reason the label matters. Where the wider literature often stops at pattern-recognition, Meadows treats the archetype as actionable only once its escape is specified.

- **Structure, not instance.** An archetype is the *type*, not any one occurrence. "The drug war" and "the arms race" are instances; "policy resistance" and "escalation" are the archetypes they instance. The definition's word "common" is doing the work — it is the recurrence *across* domains (economies, ecosystems, families, firms) that makes a structure an archetype rather than a one-off "unique" trouble.

- **Definition note vs. concept note.** This note pins Meadows's term and its verbatim wording. The eight archetypes worked through as traps-and-opportunities — [[Policy Resistance]], the [[The Tragedy of the Commons|Tragedy of the Commons]], [[Drift to Low Performance]], [[Escalation]], [[Success to the Successful]], [[Shifting the Burden to the Intervenor]], [[Rule Beating]], [[Seeking the Wrong Goal]] — are catalogued in the concept [[System Traps]]. The two are cross-linked; this note is the definitional anchor.
