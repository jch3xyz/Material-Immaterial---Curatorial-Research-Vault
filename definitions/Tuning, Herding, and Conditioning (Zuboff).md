# Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Zuboff names three techniques by which surveillance capitalism's [[Economies of Action]] achieve behavior modification at scale. They are the operational methods of *actuation* — the moment when the sensors of ubiquitous computing become not only instruments of measurement but instruments of intervention.

> The scientists and engineers whom I interviewed identified three key approaches to economies of action, each one aimed at achieving behavior modification. The first two I call “tuning” and “herding.” The third is already familiar as what behavioral psychologists refer to as “conditioning.” Strategies that produce economies of action vary according to the methods with which these approaches are combined and the salience of each.

(`Directly stated`)

**Tuning.** The subtle, sub-awareness shaping of the flow of behavior:

> “Tuning” occurs in a variety of ways. It may involve subliminal cues designed to subtly shape the flow of behavior at the precise time and place for maximally efficient influence. Another kind of tuning involves what behavioral economists Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein call the “nudge,” which they define as “any aspect of a choice architecture that alters people’s behavior in a predictable way.”

Zuboff's twist on the behavioral economists' nudge is decisive: where Thaler and Sunstein imagine nudges that "shepherd individual choice making toward outcomes that align with their interests," the surveillance-capitalist nudge is "intended to encourage choices that accrue to the architect, not to the individual" (`Directly stated`).

**Herding.** The control of the immediate context so that alternatives are foreclosed:

> “Herding” is a second approach that relies on controlling key elements in a person’s immediate context. The uncontract is an example of a herding technique. Shutting down a car’s engine irreversibly changes the driver’s immediate context, herding her out the car door. Herding enables remote orchestration of the human situation, foreclosing action alternatives and thus moving behavior along a path of heightened probability that approximates certainty.

Herding is the technique behind the chapter's governing metaphor — "We are learning how to write the music, and then we let the music make them dance." It is also the mechanism of the [[The Uncontract]] (`Directly stated`).

**Conditioning.** Skinner's operant reinforcement, applied across whole populations:

> “Conditioning” is a well-known approach to inducing behavior change, primarily associated with the famous Harvard behaviorist B. F. Skinner. He argued that behavior modification should mimic the evolutionary process… Skinner called the application of reinforcements to shape specific behaviors “operant conditioning.”… Skinner imagined a pervasive “technology of behavior” that would enable the application of such methods across entire human populations.

In Zuboff's account a Silicon Valley education company's chief data scientist makes the contemporary realization explicit: "Conditioning at scale is essential to the new science of massively engineered human behavior," with reinforcements delivered as "treatments" or "data pellets" that "select good behaviors" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The three techniques appear in Chapter 10, "Make Them Dance," which completes Zuboff's account of the [[The Prediction Imperative]]. The imperative drives surveillance capitalism first to *scale* (more users), then to *scope* (more varied data), and finally to *action* — intervening directly in behavior to push it toward guaranteed outcomes, because "the most predictive source of all is behavior that has already been modified" (`Directly stated`).

Tuning, herding, and conditioning are the concrete tradecraft of that final phase. They turn the [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] from an abstraction into a working operational system: subliminal cues and nudges (tuning), context engineering that locks out alternatives (herding), and reinforcement schedules run continuously through smartphones, wearables, and networked devices (conditioning). Zuboff illustrates the techniques with two extended cases — Facebook's 61-million-person voter-mobilization and contagion experiments, and the augmented-reality game Pokémon Go — in which the three approaches are combined "in startling new ways."

The deeper claim is that these techniques "bypass human awareness, individual decision rights, and the entire complex of self-regulatory processes that we summarize with terms such as autonomy and self-determination" (`Directly stated`). They are therefore the executive arm of [[Instrumentarian Power]] and the operational logic of [[Big Other]]: power that does not coerce or discipline the body but tunes, herds, and conditions behavior from a distance, for profit.

## Differences from Other Uses

**Vs. the behavioral economists (Thaler and Sunstein).** Zuboff borrows the *nudge* and *choice architecture* directly from Thaler and Sunstein, but inverts their normative frame. Their "libertarian paternalism" aims nudges at the individual's own good as judged by experts; Zuboff documents the appropriation of the same instruments toward "a unilateral commercial program of behavior modification" whose beneficiary is the architect (`Directly stated`).

**Vs. Skinner's behaviorism.** Conditioning is Zuboff's most explicit inheritance — she names Skinner, operant conditioning, schedules of reinforcement, the "technology of behavior." What is new is automation and scale: Skinner's laboratory of mice and pigeons becomes "conditioning at scale" delivered through always-on networked devices, "massively engineered human behavior."

**Vs. Foucault's disciplinary power.** The vault's [[Surveillance]] cluster (Han's [[Digital Panopticon]], Harari's [[Dataism]]) shares the diagnosis of pervasive monitoring, but Zuboff's techniques mark a categorical shift. Disciplinary power and biopower work *on* the body and the population to normalize them; tuning, herding, and conditioning do not require internalized norms or visible threat at all. They reshape behavior directly and remotely, often below awareness — which is why Zuboff insists [[Instrumentarian Power]] is *not* a digital update of the Panopticon but a distinct species of power. (`Interpretive synthesis` for the comparison to the vault's other surveillance authors; the instrumentarian-vs-disciplinary distinction is `Directly stated` across Zuboff's chapters.)
