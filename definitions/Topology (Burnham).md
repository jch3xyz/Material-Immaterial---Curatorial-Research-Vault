# Topology — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

Burnham defines topology as the branch of mathematics concerned with the most innate and unchanging properties of objects in space — those that survive any deformation short of cutting or fusing the object:

> Topology is the study of the innate properties of objects in space. These properties remain unaffected by changes in magnitude, position, or proportion and are only altered by the tearing or joining of an object's parts.

(`Directly stated`)

These topological properties are the *most innate and unchanging* features of a three-dimensional object — its edges, faces, and vertices, its insideness and outsideness, its connectivity — as opposed to the *metric* properties (straightness, the measurement of angles and edges, cross-sectional areas) that the other geometries govern. An object "can be twisted and distorted beyond recognition without losing their topological characteristics," whereas metric properties "do not afford this type of plasticity." (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Burnham introduces topology in Chapter 3, "Formalism: The Weary Vocabulary," within his account of how nonobjective sculptors sought to ground their work in the inexhaustibility of formal relationships. Faced with sculptors who "became less concerned with proportional metric relationships and more interested in the universal properties of planes, edges, penetrations, and spaces," he names topology as the mathematics underwriting that shift: "This, then, became the basis of new sculptural formalism." (`Directly stated`) The definition is thus the technical hinge of the concept [[Topology as the New Formalism]] — topology supplies the rationale for treating "insideness and outsideness, connectivity, complex symmetry, and ambiguity" as "the most important plastic considerations" for Naum Gabo and other formalists.

The definition does double duty for Burnham's larger thesis about [[Formalism (Burnham)]]: because topological properties persist through radical distortion, an artist can "use the same mathematical-structural properties repeatedly" while making them "unrecognizable as the original configuration." Burnham reads Max Bill's Möbius-band sculptures — works that are "topologically the same piece" despite differing in material, position, and plastic effect — as demonstrating "both a topological truth and an artistic principle: there are no 'perfect forms,' only the ability of the artist to reveal meaningful aspects of the same reality." (`Directly stated`) Yet the chapter's title signals the limit: borrowed mathematical models, topology included, eventually exhaust themselves; "most three-space topological figures are not only more complicated than the Mobius band, they are ugly," and "the use of topological exploration has not been carried further by sculptors." (`Directly stated`) Topology is therefore both an exemplar of the new formalism and an instance of the weary vocabulary it could not finally renew. (`Strongly implied`)
