# Nonlinear relationship — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows defines the term by contrast with the linear relationship, in the "Linear Minds in a Nonlinear World" section of Chapter 4, "Why Systems Surprise Us":

> A nonlinear relationship is one in which the cause does not produce a proportional effect. The relationship between cause and effect can only be drawn with curves or wiggles, not with a straight line.

The defining features are two: the response is **not proportional** to the cause, and consequently the relationship is **not drawable as a straight line** — only as curves or wiggles (`Directly stated`).

## Context

Meadows introduces the definition against its foil. A *linear* relationship "can be drawn on a graph with a straight line. It's a relationship with constant proportions" — ten pounds of fertilizer add two bushels, twenty add four, thirty add six. The nonlinear case breaks that constancy, and Meadows drives the point home by extending the same fertilizer example past its threshold: "if I put 100 pounds of fertilizer on, my yield will go up by 10 bushels; if I put on 200, my yield will not go up at all; if I put on 300, my yield will go down. Why? I've damaged my soil with 'too much of a good thing'" (`Directly stated`).

The definition is doing specific work in the chapter's larger argument that systems surprise us. Because "the world is full of nonlinearities," it "often surprises our linear-thinking minds": we assume that "if we've learned that a small push produces a small response … twice as big a push will produce twice as big a response. But in a nonlinear system, twice the push could produce one-sixth the response, or the response squared, or no response at all" (`Directly stated`). So the definition is not offered for its own sake but as a diagnosis of a cognitive failure mode — the linear default we bring to a nonlinear world.

Meadows then escalates from cognition to systemic consequence, which is the definition's real payload. Nonlinearities "are important not only because they confound our expectations about the relationship between action and response. They are even more important because they change the relative strengths of feedback loops. They can flip a system from one mode of behavior to another" (`Directly stated`). A nonlinear response means a loop's gain is state-dependent, not fixed — so as a stock crosses a threshold, a negligible loop can suddenly dominate. This is why Meadows names nonlinearity "the chief cause of the shifting dominance that characterizes several of the systems in the zoo — the sudden swing between exponential growth caused by a dominant reinforcing loop, say, and then decline caused by a suddenly dominant balancing loop" (`Directly stated`). The definition of a *nonlinear relationship*, then, is the local building block whose aggregate effect is [[Shifting Dominance]].

## Differences from Other Uses

This is the vault's **native-discipline** definition of nonlinearity as a property of a cause–effect *relationship* and of loop dominance — kept distinct from the growth-rate framing that dominates the vault's other "the world is not linear" note:

- Against [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s [[Exponential vs Linear]] contrast: Kurzweil's concern is *growth rates* — linear intuition's failure to anticipate accelerating (exponential) returns. Meadows' concern is *proportionality and loop-dominance* — a response can be sub-linear, super-linear, saturating, sign-reversing, or threshold-triggered, and its significance is that it re-weights [[Feedback Loops]] rather than that it names any single accelerating trend. An exponential curve is one nonlinear shape, so the two overlap, but the analytic work each does is different; they are cross-linked, not merged (`Interpretive synthesis`).
