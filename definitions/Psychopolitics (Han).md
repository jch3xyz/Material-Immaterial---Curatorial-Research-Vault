# Psychopolitics — Byung-Chul Han

This note gathers Han's own recurring **term-definitions** of *psychopolitics*. It is the definitional companion to the concept note [[Psychopolitics]], which carries the broad argument and its lineage; per the vault's definition/concept split, this file keeps only the verbatim glosses by which Han fixes the term, kept distinct from the wider exposition.

## Author's Definition

Han does not state a single canonical sentence but pins the term down through three converging glosses across the book.

**1. As a passage in the technique of power — from watching to steering.** Han first names *psychopolitics* as a periodizing move beyond [[Society of Control|surveillance]]:

> Today, we are entering the age of digital psychopolitics. It means passing from passive surveillance to active steering.

(`Directly stated`.) The defining shift is from *passive surveillance* to *active steering* — power no longer merely records behaviour but intervenes to direct it. Han immediately specifies the means: "Big Data is a highly efficient psychopolitical instrument," and its knowledge "facilitates intervention in the psyche and enables influence to take place on a pre-reflexive level." (`Directly stated`.)

**2. As the 'form of government' of the neoliberal soul.** Reading Deleuze's mole and snake, Han opposes psychopolitics to the body-oriented biopolitics of disciplinary society:

> The neoliberal regime, in contrast, seems like a 'soul'. As such, psychopolitics is its form of government: it is 'constantly introducing an inexorable rivalry presented as healthy competition . . . wonderful motivation'.

(`Directly stated`.) He then enumerates the term's operative content:

> Motivation, projects, competition, optimization and initiative represent features of the psychopolitical technology of domination that constitutes the neoliberal regime.

(`Directly stated`.) Here psychopolitics is defined by what it mobilizes — not confinement and command, but motivation, optimization and self-initiated competition.

**3. As a technology of domination that stabilizes the system through psychic programming.** The book's most compressed definition appears in the closing chapter:

> Neoliberal psychopolitics is a technology of domination that stabilizes and perpetuates the prevailing system by means of psychological programming and steering.

(`Directly stated`.) This is the genus-and-differentia form of the definition: psychopolitics is a *technology of domination* (genus) whose *differentia* are (a) its end — to *stabilize and perpetuate the prevailing system* — and (b) its means — *psychological programming and steering*.

Taken together: **psychopolitics is the neoliberal technology of domination that, by means of digital data and psychological programming, passes from passively surveilling the soul to actively steering it, so as to stabilize and perpetuate the existing order.**

## Context

Han introduces the term to mark a mutation *within* capitalism rather than a break from it. In [[The Mole and the Snake|"The Mole and the Snake"]] the passage "from subject to project . . . represents a mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same" (`Directly stated`). Disciplinary [[Society of Control|biopolitics]] disciplined the *body*; psychopolitics governs the *soul* (psyche), and does so not against the will of its subjects but through their freedom. This ties the definition to Han's larger thesis of the [[Exploitation of Freedom]]: psychopolitics is efficient precisely because, as he writes elsewhere in chapter 1, "Only when freedom is exploited are returns maximized" (`Directly stated`). The [[Digital Panopticon]] supplies its sensory apparatus — voluntary self-exposure — while Big Data supplies its predictive reach, so that "the future is becoming calculable and controllable" and the [[Positivity|negativity]] of free decision is converted "into the positivity of factual states" (`Directly stated`).

The term is therefore polemical as well as descriptive: in [[Beyond the Subject|"Beyond the Subject"]] Han defines psychopolitics in order to name its antidote — "de-psychologization," "the art of killing psychology" — as "the praxis of freedom" that "serves to disarm psychopolitics, which is a means of effecting submission" (`Directly stated`). The definition's purpose is to make the technique nameable, hence resistible.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Within Han's own corpus**, psychopolitics is the active, steering successor to the largely passive optics of [[The Transparency Society]]. There the [[Digital Panopticon]] *watches* and exposes; psychopolitics adds the second move — *steering* — that the transparency apparatus only made possible. The definition's keyword "active steering" marks exactly this advance over surveillance. (`Strongly implied`.)
- **Against Foucault's biopolitics.** Han defines psychopolitics by explicit contrast: disciplinary power "organizes itself as a 'body'" and "is a biopolitical regime," whereas "the neoliberal regime . . . seems like a 'soul'" governed by psychopolitics (`Directly stated`). Where Foucault's biopower administers populations and bodies, Han's psychopolitics programs the individual psyche. The vault holds no Foucault book in `raw/`, so [[Michel Foucault]] is a forward-stub here.
- **Against Deleuze's control society.** Han adopts Deleuze's diagnosis of the [[Society of Control|control society]] (the snake replacing the mole) but renames its governing technique *psychopolitics*, foregrounding the psychic and motivational register that Deleuze's spatial figures leave implicit. (`Strongly implied`.)
- **Not surveillance studies' "soft" or "data" power in general.** Han's term is narrower: it is specifically *neoliberal*, defined by motivation, optimization, projects and competition, and by the conversion of the free subject into a self-exploiting [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] who steers itself. (`Strongly implied`.)
