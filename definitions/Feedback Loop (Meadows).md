# Feedback Loop — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

For [[Donella Meadows]], the feedback loop is the mechanism that makes a system a *system* rather than a mere chain of stocks and flows: it is what happens when a [[Stocks and Flows|stock]] "reaches around" to govern the very flows that fill or drain it. She states it twice, first minimally and then in full.

The minimal statement:

> A feedback loop is formed when changes in a stock affect the flows into or out of that same stock. (`Directly stated`)

The full definition:

> A feedback loop is a closed chain of causal connections from a stock, through a set of decisions or rules or physical laws or actions that are dependent on the level of the stock, and back again through a flow to change the stock. (`Directly stated`)

The word *closed* is doing the definitional work: unlike a "simple open-ended chain of stocks and flows," where "the levels of the chain's stocks don't affect its flows," a feedback loop bends the causal chain back on itself so the stock ends up controlling its own future. Meadows glosses the resulting behavior plainly: "The stock level feeds back through a chain of signals and actions to control itself" (`Directly stated`).

## Context

The definition appears in Chapter 1 of *Thinking in Systems* ("The Basics"), immediately after Meadows has established the vocabulary of [[Stocks and Flows|stocks and flows]] and before she introduces her two loop archetypes. Her diagnostic heuristic motivates it: "if you see a behavior that persists over time, there is likely a mechanism creating that consistent behavior. That mechanism operates through a feedback loop." The loop is thus offered as the hidden structure behind visible, persistent patterns of behavior — the reason a system "behaves" at all.

Meadows immediately partitions the concept into two kinds, which the vault treats as sub-forms of the concept note [[Feedback Loops]]:

- **Balancing (negative) feedback** — "goal-seeking or stability-seeking," it "opposes whatever direction of change is imposed on the system," keeping a stock at or near a value (her examples: the interest-adjusted checking account, the cup of cooling coffee, body temperature). This is the equilibrating loop, and it is the mechanism she names as the source of [[Homeostasis]]: balancing loops, she writes, are "both sources of stability and sources of resistance to change."
- **Reinforcing (positive) feedback** — "self-enhancing, leading to exponential growth or to runaway collapses over time," a loop that "generates more input to a stock the more that is already there." Her exemplar is compound interest: "This reinforcing feedback loop is the central engine of growth in an economy."

She closes the section with a caution against over-reading: not every decision sits inside a loop ("Not all systems have feedback loops"), and "if you see feedback loops everywhere, you're... " prone to the opposite error of the person who sees none.

## Differences from Other Uses

This is the vault's **native-discipline, primary-source** definition of the loop, and it should be read as the ground the corpus's other uses of "feedback" stand on. The concept note [[Feedback Loops]] already gathers four registers — the cybernetic-connective ([[N. Katherine Hayles]], [[Norbert Wiener]]), the physiological-homeostatic ([[Marshall McLuhan]]), the art-systems ([[Jack Burnham]]), and the participatory ([[Roy Ascott]]) — plus the autopoietic dissent. Meadows differs from all of them in aim and scope:

- **Against Hayles / Wiener (the connective register).** Where Hayles reads the informational feedback loop *diagnostically* — as the mechanism that puts "the boundaries of the autonomous subject... up for grabs" and deconstructs [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] — Meadows uses it *operationally*, as a modeling primitive for understanding and managing real systems (ecosystems, economies, thermostats). She has no stake in the subject at all; the loop is a tool of [[Systems Thinking]], not a theory of the human. Her loop is also stock-and-flow specific in a way Wiener's control-theoretic loop is not: it is always anchored to a measurable accumulation.
- **Against McLuhan (the homeostatic register).** McLuhan's "strategies of equilibrium for the central nervous system" and Meadows's "balancing feedback loops are equilibrating or goal-seeking" describe the *same* stabilizing dynamic, but McLuhan reaches it by physiological analogy while Meadows reaches it by explicit definition. Her balancing loop is the disciplinary source-form of the equilibrium McLuhan invokes and of [[Homeostasis]] generally.
- **Against Burnham (the art-systems register).** Burnham's "negative feedback... coupled systems... designed to maintain a relatively constant input-output relationship" is nearly a paraphrase of Meadows's balancing loop, and his positive-feedback "runaway" is her reinforcing loop; the difference is only that Burnham imports the concept to define an art *system* (the cyborg as "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism"), whereas Meadows defines it for systems as such.
- **Against Ascott (the participatory register).** Ascott's loop is a *relation* between persons (the artist/artwork/observer triad); Meadows's loop is a *causal circuit* within a single system's own accounting. They share the word and the closed-circuit form but not the referent.
- **Against the autopoietic dissent.** Second-order cybernetics (Maturana and Varela, as Hayles reports) turns the loop "inside out" so that "no information crosses the boundary." Meadows's loop is emphatically first-order and connective: the stock's level *is* the information, and it circulates by definition. She never entertains informational closure; for her the loop's whole point is that the stock feeds back to control itself.

In short: Meadows supplies the concept in its home discipline — quantitative, operational, ethically neutral — from which the corpus's more speculative uses (subjectivity, media, art, the psychic) are downstream extensions.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — the multi-author concept note this definition grounds in its native discipline; balancing and reinforcing feedback are treated there as its two sub-forms.
- [[Stocks and Flows]] — the loop is defined *in terms of* a stock and the flows into or out of it; no stock, no loop.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the loop is the elementary building block that distinguishes a system from an open-ended chain.
- [[Homeostasis]] — the behavior produced by a balancing (negative) feedback loop; Meadows's balancing loop is the disciplinary source-form.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the field whose control-theoretic feedback concept Meadows's systems-dynamics loop parallels and re-grounds operationally.
- [[Self-Organization]] — the capacity of complex systems to elaborate their own structure, which coupled feedback loops make possible (kept distinct from [[Emergence]] and cross-linked, not collapsed).
