# Nonrenewable and Renewable Resources — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

Meadows defines the two resource types by the structural feature that limits them — a **stock** versus a **flow**:

> Nonrenewable resources are stock-limited. The entire stock is available at once, and can be extracted at any rate (limited mainly by extraction capital). But since the stock is not renewed, the faster the extraction rate, the shorter the lifetime of the resource.

A **renewable** resource, by contrast, is *flow-limited*: its stock is continually fed by a regeneration flow, so it can support harvest indefinitely — but only at a rate no greater than that flow. Past that point the resource is driven toward collapse:

> If they are extracted faster than they regenerate, they may eventually be driven below a critical threshold and become, for all practical purposes, nonrenewable.

(Directly stated.) The decisive contrast is therefore not the physical substance but *what governs its limit*: for a nonrenewable resource the extraction rate sets only the **lifetime** of a fixed stock; for a renewable resource the regeneration rate sets a sustainable **ceiling** on the flow, above which the stock itself can be destroyed.

## Context

Meadows introduces this pair near the end of Chapter 2, "A Brief Visit to the Systems Zoo," after walking through two structurally similar model economies: a firm extracting a nonrenewable resource (an oil company) and a firm harvesting a renewable one (a fishery). Both are the *same* archetype — a reinforcing capital-growth loop, where profits are reinvested to buy more extraction capacity, constrained by a resource. Yet they behave in dynamically different ways, and Meadows uses the stock/flow distinction to explain why.

For the nonrenewable case, because "the stock feeding the extraction flow does not have an input," exponential growth of extraction guarantees a peak-and-decline: even doubling or quadrupling the discovered reserve buys only a little more time before the resource runs down. For the renewable case, the regeneration flow can in principle sustain harvest forever — but only if harvest stays at or below that flow. When it does not, a renewable resource can be converted into a functionally nonrenewable one; Meadows's fishery can be "turned, for all practical purposes, into a nonrenewable resource," while her New England logging example — "now in its third cycle of growth, overcutting, collapse, and eventual regeneration" — shows a flow-limited resource surviving repeated overharvest. Whether a real renewable system survives turns on the location of the critical threshold and on how quickly the balancing [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] slows capital growth as the resource depletes. (Strongly implied by the surrounding model discussion; the threshold clause is directly stated.)

The larger point is that the classification is about *system dynamics*, not a static taxonomy of substances: "Neither renewable nor nonrenewable limits to growth allow a physical stock to grow forever, but the constraints they impose are dynamically quite different." (Directly stated.)
