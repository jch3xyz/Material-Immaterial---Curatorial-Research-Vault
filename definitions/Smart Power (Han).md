# Smart Power — Byung-Chul Han

## Author's Definition

For Han, *smart power* (also *smart and friendly power*) names the form power takes under the neoliberal regime: power that sheds coercion, prohibition, and visibility, instead operating through permission, seduction, and positive emotion. Its defining gesture is to present itself *as* freedom rather than as a limit on freedom.

> "Today, power is assuming increasingly permissive forms. In its permissivity - indeed, in its friendliness - power is shedding its negativity and presenting itself as freedom." (`Directly stated`)

Its mechanism is not negation but fulfilment:

> "It proves so effective because it does not operate by means of forbidding and depriving, but by pleasing and fulfilling. Instead of making people compliant, it seeks to make them dependent." (`Directly stated`)

### Sub-definition — Free choice (*Wahl*) → free selection (*Auswahl*)

Han glosses the cost of smart power's "exploitation" rather than repression of freedom as the substitution of selection for choice:

> "Today's crisis of freedom stems from the fact that the operative technology of power does not negate or repress freedom so much as exploit it. Free choice {Wahl) is eliminated to make way for a free selection (Auswahl) from among the items on offer." (`Directly stated`)

The freedom that remains is the freedom to pick among pre-given options, not the freedom to will otherwise — a freedom internal to, and productive for, the regime of power.

## Context

Han introduces *smart power* in the second chapter of *Psychopolitics* to distinguish neoliberal power from Foucauldian disciplinary power. Disciplinary power "is still commanded by negativity": it is inhibitive, forbids, and forces people "into the straightjacket of commandments and prohibitions," which makes it inefficient and visible. Smart power is the more efficient successor — it "makes sure that people subordinate themselves to power relations on their own," activating, motivating, and optimizing rather than repressing (`Directly stated`).

Because it "escapes all visibility," the subjugated subject "is not even aware of its own subjugation" and "thinks itself free" (`Directly stated`). Smart power "cosies up to the psyche" and constantly calls on us "to confide, share and participate," to tell all about our lives. Han fixes its emblem in the social-media gesture: "Its signal and seal is the Like button... Neoliberalism is the capitalism of 'Like'" (`Directly stated`). The definition does the framing work for the book's larger thesis that neoliberal power operates as psychopolitics — through the psyche and through freedom itself — rather than as biopolitical discipline of the body (`Strongly implied`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Versus disciplinary power (Han via Foucault).** Han defines smart power explicitly against disciplinary power: the latter is negative, inhibitive, prohibitive, and visible; smart power is positive, permissive, seductive, and invisible. Where discipline says "no," smart power "says 'yes' more often than 'no'" (`Directly stated`). This is the same negative/positive distinction Han develops across his work — see [[Negativity]] and [[Positivity]].
- **Versus power as violence/coercion.** Han is careful that smart power is "not based on force." Power "that relies on violence does not represent power of the highest order"; the highest power "works quietly" and "has no need to draw attention to itself" (`Directly stated`). Smart power is thus the *more* powerful form precisely because it is friendly and unseen.
- **Versus a simple "soft power."** Although it resembles the political-science sense of soft power (attraction over coercion), Han's term is sharper: smart power does not merely attract but reads and appraises "our conscious and unconscious thoughts" and places its stock in "voluntary self-organization and self-optimization" (`Directly stated`). It is power that has become psychological and data-driven — the hinge to Han's [[Auto-Exploitation]] and to the self-optimizing subject of the [[Achievement Society]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).
