# Dataism — Byung-Chul Han

## Author's Definition

Han does not coin *dataism*; he quotes it. The term is **David Brooks's** name for "the rising philosophy of the day," which Han reproduces verbatim as the creed of a "second Enlightenment":

> If you asked me to describe the rising philosophy of the day, I'd say it is data-ism. We now have the ability to gather huge amounts of data. This ability seems to carry with it certain cultural assumptions - that everything that can be measured should be measured; that data is a transparent and reliable lens that allows us to filter out emotionalism and ideology; that data will help us do remarkable things - like foretell the future . . . The data revolution is giving us wonderful ways to understand the present and the past.

(Directly stated — Han introduces this Brooks passage as "the name of the new faith.")

Han's own contribution is not the definition but the **verdict**. He turns the creed against itself: the worldview that markets itself as post-ideological is, for Han, precisely an ideology.

> data totalitarianism, or data fetishism. Although it announces that it is taking leave of all ideology, dataism itself is an ideology.

(Directly stated.)

He sharpens the verdict twice more. Dataism is *nihilism* because it abandons meaning, and *Dadaism* because it abandons sense:

> Dataism is nihilism. It gives up on any and all meaning. Data and numbers are not narrative; they are additive.

> Dataism, it turns out, is amounting to digital Dadaism.

(Directly stated.)

So the definition has two layers that must be kept apart: the **creed** (Brooks's "data-ism" — everything measurable should be measured; data as a transparent, ideology-filtering lens that can foretell the future) and **Han's reframing** of that creed as ideology, totalitarianism, fetishism, and nihilism.

## Context

The term appears in the chapter "Big Data," where Han stages the present as a "second Enlightenment." The first Enlightenment opposed numerical-statistical objectivity to mythological narration; the second installs *transparency* as its buzzword and "data and information" as its imperative — "everything must become data and information."

Han borrows the prophetic register from two named sources. The term itself is Brooks's (his *New York Times* column "The Philosophy of Data"). The accompanying thesis — that with enough data, theory becomes superfluous — Han credits to **Chris Anderson's** "famous article 'The End of Theory'," which he quotes directly ("With enough data, the numbers speak for themselves"). Dataism, in Han's framing, *rides on* Anderson's end-of-theory: it is the faith that data-driven correlation can replace causal and theoretical understanding.

Against this, Han mobilizes a counter-argument: numbers are *additive*, not *narrative*. "Counting is not recounting." Because meaning rests on narration, a purely additive data-knowledge "simply fills up the senseless void" — hence nihilism. Read through Adorno and Horkheimer's *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, dataism's "transparency" becomes "another name for myth," a "false clarity" whose dialectic threatens to "switch over into barbarism" — "the barbarism of data." The chapter's later movements (Quantified Self, the digital unconscious, Hegel's Spirit) extend the verdict but the *term* is fixed here.

(Interpretive synthesis — the two-layer structure and the "rides on Anderson" reading are the system's organization of Han's directly-stated claims.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Brooks (origin, descriptive/affirmative).** For Brooks, "data-ism" names an ascendant philosophy in broadly hopeful terms — a method for filtering out "emotionalism and ideology." Han keeps the word but inverts the valuation: what Brooks calls the *escape* from ideology, Han calls ideology as such.
- **Anderson ("the end of theory").** Anderson supplies the epistemic claim dataism depends on — that sufficient data renders theory, taxonomy, ontology, and psychology obsolete. Han treats this not as a separate doctrine but as the engine of dataism, and rejects it: for him the "end of theory" means "taking leave of Spirit."
- **Within the vault.** Dataism is the *creed* whose medium is [[Information]] and whose atmosphere is [[Transparency]]; its failure, for Han, is the loss of [[Narrativity]] (the [[Narration vs Addition]] tension). It is the ideological self-description of the same apparatus that the [[Digital Panopticon]] describes structurally and that [[Auto-Exploitation]] describes subjectively. Where the vault's definition notes from *How We Became Posthuman* and *God Human Animal Machine* treat data/pattern as an ontological or epistemological problem (the disembodiment of information, patternism), Han's dataism is specifically a *critique of an ideology* — the term names a faith he is diagnosing, not a position he holds.

(Distinctions among Brooks, Anderson, and Han are Directly stated in the chapter; cross-vault placements are Interpretive synthesis.)
