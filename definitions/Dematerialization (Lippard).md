# Dematerialization — Lucy R. Lippard

## Author's Definition

The term [[Lucy R. Lippard|Lippard]] coined (with [[John Chandler]], in their 1968 *Art International* article "The Dematerialization of Art") and operationalized as the through-line of *[[Six Years]]*. Her own working definition, given in the Preface, deliberately does **not** claim literal immateriality:

> But for lack of a better term | have continued to refer to a process of dematerialization, or a deemphasis on material aspects (uniqueness, permanence, decorative [attractiveness]).

Dematerialization is therefore a *process* and a *de-emphasis*, not a disappearance: a downgrading of the material attributes that traditionally underwrote the art object — its **uniqueness**, its **permanence**, and its **decorative attractiveness** — rather than the literal vanishing of matter. The companion formulation in "Escape Attempts" pins the same idea to the genre it names, [[Conceptual Art]]:

> Conceptual art, for me, means work in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or "dematerialized."

Here "dematerialized" sits in a list of material attenuations — *secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious* — confirming that the term names a relative demotion of the material form beneath the idea, not its abolition. (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Lippard introduces the definition defensively, as a concession wrapped around a term she has decided to keep. By the Preface she has been writing about "dematerialization" since 1967, and she opens the paragraph by granting the standing objection: "it has often been pointed out to me that dematerialization is an inaccurate term, that a piece of paper or a photograph is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead. Granted." The definition that follows ("But for lack of a better term...") is thus an *operationalized* term-of-art, retained for utility despite a known inaccuracy, not a metaphysical claim about art shedding its body. This self-aware, qualified status is the whole point of pinning it as a definition note. (`Directly stated`)

In the larger argument of *[[Six Years]]*, the term does load-bearing work. It is the title concept ("The Dematerialization of the Art Object from 1966 to 1972") and the name for the historical tendency the book documents: a "gradual deemphasis of sculptural concerns" and a turn toward "textual and photographic work," toward ephemeral materials, "time itself, space, nonvisual systems, situations, unrecorded experience, unspoken ideas." It is also yoked to Lippard's political hope — that an art de-emphasizing the saleable object might escape "commodity status," "getting the power structure out of New York." The Postface records the failure of that hope ("art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries"), but the *definitional* content — de-emphasis, not disappearance — is stable across the book. (`Directly stated` for the textual turn and the commodity hope; `Strongly implied` for the term's structural centrality.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the concept [[Dematerialization]].** This note records Lippard's *explicit, self-qualified definition* of the word — the verbatim "process... or a deemphasis on material aspects" formulation and her concession that the term is inaccurate. The concept note [[Dematerialization]] is the place for the broader cross-author phenomenon and its art-historical reach; this note is the anchored definitional source, in the same way `[[Information (Hayles)]]` anchors the Shannon definition beneath the concept `[[Information]]`. Do not collapse the two.
- **vs. literal immateriality / "the overthrow of matter."** Lippard's term is routinely misread as a claim that art becomes immaterial. Her own gloss forecloses that reading: a photograph or a sheet of paper "is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead." Dematerialization (Lippard) is a *de-emphasis of selected material attributes* (uniqueness, permanence, decorative attractiveness), not a passage out of materiality. This is precisely the point at which her usage collides with [[The Materiality of Informatics]]: where an ideology of dematerialization imagines the idea can shed its body, Lippard concedes in advance that it cannot — the matter is renamed and demoted, not escaped. The opposition is internal to her own term. (`Interpretive synthesis`)
- **vs. "Ephemeralization" (Buckminster Fuller).** Lippard explicitly distinguishes her term from a near neighbor she cites in the Preface: "'Ephemeralization' is the term Buckminster Fuller uses for 'the design science strategy of doing even more with even less per unit of energy, space, and time.'" Fuller's word is an engineering-efficiency concept (more performance per unit of matter); Lippard's is an *aesthetic-priority* concept (idea over object, attribute de-emphasis). They rhyme but are not synonyms. (`Directly stated`)
- **vs. Sol LeWitt's small-c / capital-C distinction.** [[Sol LeWitt]] separated conceptual art "with a small c" (his own work, where conventional material forms are generated by a paramount idea) from Conceptual art "with a capital C." Lippard adopts the distinction; her "dematerialization" is the *process* characteristic of the capital-C scene, where the material form is most thoroughly demoted. The term names the de-emphasis; LeWitt's letters name the genre's range. (`Directly stated`)
