# Instrumentarian Power — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Zuboff coins **instrumentarianism** to name a new species of power born of surveillance capitalism — a power that does not seize bodies or souls but instruments and instrumentalizes behavior in order to predict and control it. She introduces the term at two registers. In Chapter 1 she gives the compact functional definition (`Directly stated`):

> Instrumentarian power knows and shapes human behavior toward others’ ends. Instead of armaments and armies, it works its will through the automated medium of an increasingly ubiquitous computational architecture of “smart” networked devices, things, and spaces.

In Chapter 12 she gives the formal naming, splitting the term into its two component operations (`Directly stated`):

> As to this species of power, I name it instrumentarianism, defined as the instrumentation and instrumentalization of behavior for the purposes of modification, prediction, monetization, and control. In this formulation, “instrumentation” refers to the puppet: the ubiquitous connected material architecture of sensate computation that renders, interprets, and actuates human experience. “Instrumentalization” denotes the social relations that orient the puppet masters to human experience as surveillance capital wields the machines to transform us into means to others’ market ends.

So *instrumentation* names the material apparatus (the "puppet" — what Chapter 13 christens [[Big Other]]) and *instrumentalization* names the social relation of domination (the "puppet masters") that bends that apparatus to others' commercial ends.

## Context

Instrumentarian power is the second of Zuboff's two unprecedented diagnoses. Having named **surveillance capitalism** as the economic logic (the unilateral claiming of human experience as free raw material for behavioral data), she argues that this economics births a corresponding **political** form — the power "that sustains and enlarges the surveillance capitalist project" (`Directly stated`). The two chapters titled "Two Species of Power" and "Big Other and the Rise of Instrumentarian Power" exist to keep this new power from being misread.

Her central polemical move is the contrast with **totalitarianism**. Critics reach reflexively for Orwell's Big Brother and "digital totalitarianism," but Zuboff insists this misnames the threat and so disarms resistance: "the equation of instrumentarian power with totalitarianism impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist" (`Directly stated`). Totalitarianism "operated through the means of violence, but instrumentarian power operates through the means of behavioral modification"; it "has no interest in our souls," is "profoundly and infinitely indifferent to our meanings and motives," and "only cares that whatever we do is accessible to its ever-evolving operations of rendition, calculation, modification, monetization, and control" (`Directly stated`). Where totalitarianism was "a transformation of the state into a project of total possession," instrumentarianism is "the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty" (`Directly stated`).

Zuboff traces the *origin* of this power not to political theory but to **radical behaviorism** — B. F. Skinner's "technology of behavior" and his utopia *Walden Two* — which imagined "the forfeit of freedom for knowledge" but lacked the instruments to realize it. Surveillance capitalism's "ubiquitous digital apparatus" finally supplies those instruments, so that instrumentarian power "knows our behavior but also shape[s] our behavior at scale" — the shift "from knowledge to power" whose endgame "is no longer enough to automate information flows about us; the goal now is to automate us" (`Directly stated`). The asymmetry is the sting: "The knowledge that now displaces our freedom is proprietary. The knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us" (`Directly stated`).

## Differences from Other Uses

This is the vault's first named *species of power* defined as **behavioral modification through a material computational architecture** rather than through discipline, biopower, or ideology. It belongs to the same surveillance-and-data cluster as Han's and Harari's work but is distinct in its source of leverage (`Interpretive synthesis`):

- **Han's [[Digital Panopticon]]** runs on *voluntary self-exposure*: subjects expose themselves and exploit their own freedom, and the power is psycho-political, working on the psyche from within. Zuboff's instrumentarian power is by contrast *indifferent to interiority* — it does not need belief, conformity, or self-exposure; it needs only that behavior be observable and actuable. As she puts it, "Our conformity is irrelevant to instrumentarianism's success" (`Directly stated`). The two converge on ubiquitous networked surveillance but split on whether the subject's *inner* life is the target.
- **The Deleuzean Society of Control** (Han via Deleuze) modulates rather than encloses; instrumentarian power resonates with this continuous modulation but is grounded specifically in a *market* imperative and a *behaviorist* epistemology, not merely a shift in the form of confinement.
- **Foucault's biopolitics** administers life at the level of populations; instrumentarian power is, on Zuboff's account, narrower and stranger — it administers *behavior as such*, treating the human as an "Other-One," "an organism among organisms," via what Chapter 13 names **radical indifference** (`Strongly implied`).
- **Dataism** (Han / Harari) names a *worldview* that worships data flow; instrumentarianism names the *power relation* that monetizes the behavioral data such a worldview supplies. They are complementary, not synonymous.
