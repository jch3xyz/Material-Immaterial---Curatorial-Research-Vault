# Hot and Cool Media — Marshall McLuhan

## Author's Definition

[[Marshall McLuhan]] states the formal definition at the head of the chapter "Media Cold and Hot" in [[Understanding Media]], announcing it as a "basic principle that distinguishes a hot medium like radio from a cool one like the telephone, or a hot medium like the movie from a cool one like TV":

> A hot medium is one that extends one single sense in "high definition." High definition is the state of being well filled with data.

The definition is built on the paired sub-term **high definition**, which he glosses immediately by example: "A photograph is, visually, 'high definition.' A cartoon is 'low definition,' simply because very little visual information is provided." A cool medium is therefore the inverse — low in definition, supplying "a meager amount of information." The "Telephone is a cool medium. or one of low definition, because the ear is given a meager amount of information. And speech is a cool medium of low definition, because so little is given and so much has to be filled in by the listener." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

The second half of the definition turns on **participation**. Because a low-definition medium leaves gaps, the audience must fill them in; a high-definition medium does not. McLuhan states the consequence directly:

> Hot media are, therefore, low in participation, and cool media are high in participation or completion by the audience.

He restates the whole principle as a proportion in the television chapter, where the definition is given its most compressed form: "A cool medium, whether the spoken word or the manuscript or TV, leaves much more for the listener or user to do than a hot medium. If the medium is of high definition, participation is low. If the medium is of low intensity, the participation is high." The principle is also given as a maxim — "the hot form excludes, and the cool one includes" — and tied to involvement: "Any hot medium allows of less participation than a cool one, as a lecture makes for less participation than a seminar, and a book for less than dialogue." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

## Context

This definition is the analytic engine of [[Understanding Media]]'s Part II case studies. McLuhan introduces it in Chapter 2 — directly after the book's keystone thesis that "the medium is the message" — precisely so that he can sort the individual media of the rest of the book onto a single temperature scale and predict their differing "effects on the user." The point is not the *content* a medium carries but the *sensory ratio* it imposes: a hot medium intensifies one sense to "dominant command of a situation," fragmenting and specializing perception, while a cool medium recruits "casual participation of the senses." McLuhan closes the chapter with the physiological wager that this implies: "the hotting-up of one sense tends to effect hypnosis, and the cooling of all senses tends to result in hallucination." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

The definition is also **relational and reversible**, not a fixed property of a device. McLuhan insists "no medium has its … meaning or existence alone, but only in constant interplay with other media," and the temperature of a medium can flip with use, culture, or period: "in terms of the reversal of procedures and values in the electric age, the past mechanical time was hot, and we of the TV age are cool." Whether an effect follows also depends on the surrounding culture — "it makes all the difference whether a hot medium is used in a hot or a cool culture" — so that "backward countries are cool, and we are hot." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

The case-study chapters apply and refine the definition. In "The Telephone" McLuhan grounds the *completion* mechanism in cross-sensory compensation: a low-definition auditory image forces the other senses to fill the gap — "Since the telephone offers a very poor auditory image, we strengthen and complete it by the use of all the other senses. When the auditory image is of high definition, as with radio, we visualize the experience or complete it with the sense of sight. When the visual image is of high definition or intensity, we complete it by providing sound." Hence "The telephone demands complete participation, unlike the written and printed page." In "Television: The Timid Giant" the same definition explains TV's political behavior: "Because the low definition of TV insures a high degree of audience involvement," the medium "is unsuited to hot issues and sharply defined controversal topics," whereas "The movie is a hot, high-definition medium." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

> **Spelling note (cool vs. cold).** The chapter is titled "Media Cold and Hot," and McLuhan uses "cold media" in a few places (e.g. "hot and cold media uses"). His running body text, however, overwhelmingly prefers **cool** ("a cool one like the telephone," "a cool medium like TV," "cool media are high in participation"). This note adopts **cool** as the canonical pole of the definition, matching McLuhan's dominant usage; the companion concept [[Media Hot and Cold]] and any tension note built on the same opposition should be unified to the same spelling so that "hot/cool" reads consistently across the vault. (Confidence: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Definition vs. companion concept.** This note is the explicit, verbatim *definition* of the terms; the broader idea — the temperature scale, its reversals, its application across dance, money, print, radio, the telephone, and TV — lives in the concept [[Media Hot and Cold]]. The pattern follows the vault's existing definition/concept pairings (e.g. the definition `Information (Han)` beside the concept `Information`). Reference the definition for McLuhan's own formula; reference the concept for the worked-out idea. (Confidence: `Interpretive synthesis`.)

- **"High definition" is McLuhan's, not the broadcast term.** McLuhan's "high definition" means *well filled with data for a single sense*, a measure of sensory completeness; it predates and is not identical to the later television-resolution sense of "high definition," even though TV is one of his examples. Within his system the term is one pole of an information/participation ratio, not a pixel count. (Confidence: `Strongly implied`.)

- **Counter-intuitive against everyday "hot/cool."** McLuhan's usage cuts across the colloquial valence of the words. A medium can be "hot" while feeling cold or formal (print, the lecture) and "cool" while feeling intimate or warm (the telephone, the seminar). The temperature names *information density and participation*, not emotional warmth — which is why he can call the famously reserved Calvin Coolidge "real cool" in "the very sense of a cool medium," because his low-data public image "compelled the participation of the press in filling in an image of him." (Confidence: `Directly stated`.)

- **Information here is sensory load, not Shannon's bit.** McLuhan's "data" / "information" is the amount supplied to a sense, gauged by how much completion it leaves for the perceiver — not the formal, content-neutral quantity of [[Information]] theory. He explicitly faults the Shannon–Weaver and Game-Theory approaches for studying "only the content aspect" and ignoring "the function of the form as form." His hot/cool scale is thus a different axis from the engineering definition of information that anchors the Hayles/Han/Harari cluster in the vault. (Confidence: `Strongly implied`.)
