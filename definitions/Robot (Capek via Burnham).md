# Robot — Karel Capek (reported by Jack Burnham)

## Author's Definition

The word *robot* was coined by the Czech playwright Karel Capek for his 1921 play *R.U.R.* ("Rossum's Universal Robots"). Jack Burnham reports its etymology and its built-in connotation verbatim:

> The term robot, first coined by Capek, comes from the Czech word meaning "forced labor," and implies a lack of autonomy and free will.

Burnham immediately records the crucial — and now largely forgotten — fact about Capek's *original* coinage: the robot was not a metal man at all.

> In Capek's play (Lewis, 1963, p. 13) his robots were not metallic imitations of men but androids, machines constructed to appear human. In a factory these were shaped from materials which could simulate the consistency of the body's organs.

So the term names, in its source, a *synthetic-flesh android* mass-produced as bonded labor — not the clanking steel figure the word now summons. The etymology ("forced labor") is the load-bearing element of the definition: it embeds servitude, the absence of autonomy, and the lack of free will into the very name of the thing. (`Directly stated`)

## Context

Burnham introduces the term in the closing movement of ch. 5, "Sculpture and Automata," where he traces the long history of [[Automata]] — self-propelled images of men and animals — down to its modern, debased terminus. He places Capek's coinage in a specific historical moment: the aftermath of "a 'war of machines'" (World War I), when "plays and stories about 'mechanical men' began to appear with some regularity." *R.U.R.* (1921) is for Burnham "the most enduring example of the new vision of automata," turning on "the old scientist Rossum's discovery of how to fashion artificial beings in human form" and animated by "a kind of Faustian fatalism which precludes the exchange of robots for men on this planet." (`Directly stated`)

The definition does critical work because the word's meaning did not stay still. Burnham charts a debasement of Capek's original conception:

> Gradually Capek's original conception of the word robot has undergone debasement until it implies either a fearsome, cartoon-style, clanking mechanical man, or a person who insensitively follows orders with rigid efficiency regardless of their implications.

The first debased sense (the steel-folklore monster) and the second (the human drained into mechanical obedience, "the totalitarian mentality") are both, Burnham notes, "hostile to some of the most valuable human qualities." This definition note holds the *term and its etymology*; the form and sociology of the figure are carried by the coexisting concept and symbol notes [[The Robot]]. (`Directly stated`)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the android (Capek's own original sense).** The defining irony Burnham preserves: Capek's robots "were not metallic imitations of men but androids, machines constructed to appear human," made from "materials which could simulate the consistency of the body's organs." The robot, at its coinage, is *flesh-like*, not metallic — the opposite of the figure the word later names. (`Directly stated`)

- **vs. the modern robot (Burnham's "ultra-functional machine").** Burnham's own working sense of the robot — distinct from Capek's — is the *non-anthropomorphic* mechanical man: "Its difference lies in its non-human characteristics, that is a lack of skin coloring, hair, clothing, and natural movements. Rather, the robot is an attempt to produce a mechanical servant in the spirit of the modern, ultra-functional machine." The concept [[The Robot]] carries this form. (`Directly stated`)

- **vs. the cybernetic pseudo-organism.** Burnham distinguishes the robot — "a Luddite fear of the conscious machine without ever really being conscious" — from the postwar [[Cybernetics]] "pseudo-organisms" (Grey Walter's tortoises, Ross Ashby's homeostat, the maze-running "mice" of [[Claude Shannon]]) that are "tenuously linked with life itself." The robot is the caricature; the cybernetic organism is the genuine successor. (`Directly stated`)

- **vs. McLuhan's "robotism."** Where Capek's term encodes *forced labor* and lost autonomy, [[Marshall McLuhan]]'s account of mechanization frames the human-as-machine through fragmentation and [[Narcosis|narcosis]] rather than servitude. The shared figure — the human reduced to mechanical obedience — surfaces in Burnham's second debased sense (the "cheerful robot," "a person who insensitively follows orders with rigid efficiency") and resonates with the vault's later diagnoses of internalized mechanization: the [[Achievement Society]] subject and [[Auto-Exploitation]]. (`Speculative connection`)
