# Autoamputation — Marshall McLuhan

## Author's Definition

For McLuhan, a technology or media extension is a defensive *self-amputation*: when the body's perceptual power can neither locate nor avoid an irritant, the central nervous system protects itself by isolating and offloading the over-stressed organ, sense, or function. He adopts the word verbatim from the stress physiology of Hans Selye and Adolphe Jonas, for whom every extension of the body is an attempt to maintain equilibrium:

> Any extension of ourselves they regard as "autoamputation," and they find that the autoamputative power or strategy is resorted to by the body when the perceptual power cannot locate or avoid the cause of irritation.

The defining mechanism — what does the amputating, and why — is stated immediately after, naming the central nervous system as the agent and "superstimulation" as the trigger:

> In the physical stress of superstimulation of various kinds, the central nervous system acts to protect itself by a strategy of amputation or isolation of the offending organ, sense, or function. Thus, the stimulus to new invention is the stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load.

McLuhan then generalizes the term from a specific bodily response into a law of all invention: "Any invention or technology is an extension or self-amputation of our physical bodies." Every extension is therefore at once a prolongation *outward* and a *severing* of a function from the bodily whole. `Directly stated`

## Context

McLuhan introduces autoamputation in Chapter 4 ("The Gadget Lover: Narcissus as Narcosis") to answer a question his media theory otherwise leaves open — *why* humans extend themselves at all. The answer is physiological, not progressive: extension is relief of strain on the central nervous system under "acceleration of pace and increase of load." He is explicit that Selye and Jonas had no such application in mind — "it was no part of the intention of Jonas and Selye to provide an explanation of human invention and technology" — and that his own move is to read technology as continuous with this self-protective reflex of the diseased or over-stressed body. `Directly stated`

The definition is inseparable from its twin, the numbness it produces: the amplified extension "is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception. This is the sense of the Narcissus myth." The severed extension returns as an image we fail to own — "Self-amputation forbids self-recognition" — which is why the gadget lover becomes the servomechanism of his own outered organ. This is the hinge between this definition and `[[Narcosis]]`. `Directly stated`

Chapter 7 ("Challenge and Collapse: The Nemesis of Creativity") supplies the **social-scale gloss**. The introduction of a new technology is "huge collective surgery carried out on the social body with complete disregard for antiseptics," and crucially the cut itself cannot be felt: "it is not the incised area that is most affected. The area of impact and incision is numb. It is the entire system that is changed." McLuhan names the resulting blindness the *Narcissus attitude* — "regarding the extensions of our own bodies as really out there and really independent of us" — which guarantees that we "meet all technological challenges with the same sort of banana-skin pirouette and collapse," since the amputating counter-irritant "usually proves a greater plague than the initial irritant, like a drug habit." `Directly stated`

## Differences from Other Uses

- This is the **definition** note. It sits beside the **concept** note `[[Autoamputation]]`, which traces the term across the book's Part II case studies (wheel/foot, print/eye, phonograph/voice, photograph and movie/eye); the two should not be collapsed — the same pattern as `Information` / `[[Information (Han)]]`. `Interpretive synthesis`
- For Selye and Jonas the term is a theory of *disease and irritation* — autoamputation as the body's attempt "to maintain equilibrium." McLuhan's difference is to transplant a pathology into a general theory of *technology*: invention is the body's autoamputative reflex carried outward into media. `Directly stated`
- The definition is the negative underside of `[[Media as Extensions of Man]]`: where that equation names the prolongation outward, autoamputation insists every extension is also a *subtraction*, a loss of the offloaded function and a numbing of it (`[[Narcosis]]`). It thereby builds a `[[Negativity]]`-like cost into every extension, distinguishing McLuhan from any purely additive or celebratory account of technological enhancement. `Strongly implied`
