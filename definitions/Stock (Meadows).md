# Stock — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

For Meadows, a **stock** is the foundational element of any system — a quantity that exists at a given instant, as distinct from the flows that change it over time. She names it plainly: stocks "are the elements of the system that you can see, feel, count, or measure at any given time," and "A system stock is just what it sounds like: a store, a quantity, an accumulation." Dynamically, its defining property is temporal: "A stock is the memory of the history of changing flows within the system," or, restated in the main text, "A stock, then, is the present memory of the history of changing flows within the system." (Directly stated.)

A stock is measured as a level, not a rate: the water in a bathtub, a population, the books in a bookstore, the wood in a tree, the money in a bank. Meadows is explicit that it "does not have to be physical" — one's reserve of good will toward others, or supply of hope that the world can be better, are stocks no less than water or money. (Directly stated.)

## Context

The definition opens Chapter 1 ("The Basics"), under the chapter epigraph that "A stock is the foundation of any system." Meadows introduces the stock as the first primitive of system dynamics, immediately before its complement, the flow, so that the pair can be diagrammed together: in her stock-and-flow diagrams a stock is drawn as a box and each flow as an arrow-headed "pipe" with a faucet. The reason she anchors the whole book on the stock is that reading the level of a stock is reading the integrated record of every inflow and outflow that has ever acted on it — hence "the present memory of the history of changing flows." This sets up the bathtub model and its rules (inflows exceeding outflows raise the level, the reverse lowers it, equality holds it constant), the counterintuitive lesson that a stock can be raised by throttling its outflow as well as by opening its inflow, and the systemic consequence that because "A stock takes time to change," stocks act as the delays, buffers, and momentum that "set the pace of the dynamics of systems." (Strongly implied.) The stock is therefore not a static curiosity but the term through which the whole apparatus of [[Feedback Loops|feedback]], [[Delays|delay]], and [[Dynamic Equilibrium|dynamic equilibrium]] is later defined.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Native disciplinary grounding, not metaphor.** Where the vault's systems/cybernetics cluster arrives through media theory, posthuman theory, and art (Hayles, McLuhan, Burnham, Ascott), Meadows supplies the term in its home discipline of system dynamics, inherited from Jay Forrester. Her stock is a precise modelling primitive — a level integrated from flows — not a loose figure for "accumulation."
- **Immaterial by design.** Meadows's insistence that a stock "does not have to be physical" makes the concept directly relevant to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension: good will, hope, and self-confidence are stocks on exactly the same footing as water in a reservoir. This is a native systems-theory warrant for treating the immaterial as measurable and dynamical, distinct from (though resonant with) the dematerialization and disembodiment arguments elsewhere in the vault.
