# Cybernetics — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

For Ascott, cybernetics is not principally a science of machines but a coordinating, integrative discipline that the artist should claim as native ground. Where Norbert Wiener subtitled his founding 1948 book *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine*, Ascott takes that subtitle and reassigns it. In "The Construction of Change" he glosses cybernetics' integrative character and then turns the famous phrase directly toward art:

> Science as a whole works on many fronts—too many highly specialised fields, in fact, for the artist to consult them all, except casually. Cybernetics, however, is essentially integrative, drawing many disparate sciences together.

`Directly stated`

> It is a coordinator of science, as art is the coordinator of experience.

`Directly stated`

> "Control and communication in animal and machine" is a proper study for the artist.

`Directly stated`

The phrase in quotation marks is Wiener's subtitle, but in Ascott's prose Wiener is **not named** at this point — the words are presented as a found definition to be requisitioned for art, not as a citation. `Directly stated`

In "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" Ascott supplies the second, value-laden half of the definition — cybernetics as oriented toward the betterment of self-organising systems and, beyond them, of man:

> Fundamentally, cybernetics concerns the idea of the perfectibility of systems; it is concerned in practice with the procurement of effective action by means of self-organising systems. It recognises the idea of the perfectibility of man, of the possibility of further evolution in the biological and social sphere.

`Directly stated`

So for Ascott cybernetics is, in essence, the **coordinator of science** (paralleling art as the coordinator of experience) and, in practice, the study of the **perfectibility of self-organising systems**. By "The Psibernetic Arch" he can present it as the very form of a contemporary worldview: "Our worldview is holistic and integrative. Our vision is cybernetic." `Directly stated`

## Context

Ascott reaches for cybernetics to give his art a theory. His "behaviourist art" abandons the finished, contemplated art object in favour of an open, participatory feedback relation between artwork and observer — what the vault tracks as [[Reciprocal Communication]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]. Cybernetics, as the science of organisation, communication and control across "animal and machine," is the discipline that makes such interactive, process-based art conceptually thinkable; it underwrites his contribution to what the vault names [[New Media Art]]. The definition is therefore programmatic, not merely descriptive: by declaring "control and communication in animal and machine" "a proper study for the artist," Ascott annexes the discipline for art and licenses an art of systems, behaviour and transaction rather than objects. `Strongly implied`

Ascott also takes care to historicise the word, and in doing so distinguishes the Wiener lineage from an older one. In "The Psibernetic Arch" he reports the Soviet reclamation of cybernetics "as the science of government," and traces that meaning back past Wiener:

> they have effectively redefined cybernetics as the science of government, which was the meaning the French physicist A.-M. Ampère intended when he coined the word, many years before Norbert Wiener.

`Directly stated`

This is the one place where Ascott names Wiener — and he names him to relativise him, recovering Ampère's prior coinage. The effect is to loosen cybernetics from any single proprietary definition so that Ascott can install his own art-and-experience inflection. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. the concept [[Cybernetics]]** — that note is the shared, multi-author concept page (Wiener's founding formulation, plus Hayles, Haraway and McLuhan's later readings). This note isolates and pins Ascott's verbatim definition: cybernetics requisitioned *for art* as the "coordinator of science" and the science of "the perfectibility of self-organising systems." They are companion notes, not duplicates (the `Information` / `Information (Han)` pattern). `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Cybernetics (Burnham)]]** — Burnham (1968) defines cybernetics as "the science of organization" and "the analysis of linked and interacting systems," recruiting it to explain how the sculptural **object** migrates into a system. His orientation stays inside the artwork-as-system. Ascott orients cybernetics outward, toward **art and experience**: it is the *coordinator of experience* matched to art, and a programme for the perfectibility of man, not an account of how the autonomous object becomes lifelike. Burnham's cybernetics services the object's dissolution into system; Ascott's services participation and the betterment of self-organising minds. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s reading** — Hayles narrates cybernetics critically, as the seriated discipline (three waves) through which "information lost its body," contesting the abstraction of pattern from flesh. Ascott, writing from inside the movement, takes cybernetics affirmatively and *prescriptively* — a vision to be adopted, a perfectibility to be pursued — rather than a regime to be measured against [[Material vs Immaterial|embodiment]]. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. [[Donna Haraway]]'s reading** — Haraway politicises the cybernetic recoding of organisms as the informatics of domination. Ascott's cybernetic vision is utopian and integrative ("holistic and integrative... Our vision is cybernetic"), trained on coordination and human evolution rather than on control as domination — even as both writers locate the human within cybernetic systems. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. Wiener's own subtitle** — the phrase Ascott quotes, "control and communication in animal and machine," is Wiener's subtitle, but Ascott deploys it unattributed in "The Construction of Change," converting a science's self-description into "a proper study for the artist." When he does name Wiener (in "The Psibernetic Arch"), it is to recover Ampère's earlier "science of government" coinage and so relativise Wiener's claim on the word. `Directly stated`
