# Object — Jack Burnham

## Author's Definition

For Jack Burnham the **object** is the originary pole of sculpture: that which occupies a specific space and has place, remaining inert and stationary. It is palpable, finite in dimensions, varying in preciousness, and a direct result of the human formative urge. Burnham gives two complementary statements. The first names its essential attribute — fixity in space:

> Above all the object occupies a specific space: it has place, remaining inert and stationary.

The second names its origin and historic function — the formative urge that subdivides the world into portable things:

> the object-palpable, finite in dimensions, varying in preciousness, a direct result of the human formative urge-has been the means by which environment has been subdivided into transportable units.

`Directly stated`

## Context

Burnham introduces the object as one half of the structural axis that organizes the whole of *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968): "The two parts of this book divide modern sculpture respectively into object and system" (see [[Object and System]]). The object is the older, traditional pole — "sculpture in its traditional physical form" — defined against the [[System (Burnham)|system]], "an interacting assembly of varying complexity" by which sculpture "gradually departs from its object state and assumes some measure of lifelike activity." `Directly stated`

The definition appears in the Introduction's "Object and System" section, where Burnham asks "What then are those psychological and socioeconomic properties which give objects their importance?" His answer is durability and place. The object "is real and lasting, and the sculpted object in particular has provided a large and unreplaceable record of all cultural history"; its "Durability, compactness, and changing concern for the meaning of human anatomy have given sculpture a position as an intimate, psychical chronicle unmatched by other arts or artifacts." `Directly stated`

The definition is load-bearing because Burnham's larger thesis is the object's *eclipse*. Under the "industrial trend toward a systematized environment," the once self-contained object "now is a replaceable component in an interlocking system of production and need fulfillment," so that "the object has lost its independent status in technological society." The inert, place-bound object is thus the baseline against which the book measures sculpture's drift toward life-simulating systems — the sculptural correlate of the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension and Burnham's own process of [[Reification]] ("thingification"). `Strongly implied`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Burnham's object vs. Lippard's dematerialized art object.** Where Lucy Lippard's [[Dematerialization (Lippard)|dematerialization]] tracks the conceptual-art object thinning toward idea, document, and information (the [[Dematerialization]] thesis), Burnham keeps the *physical* object firmly in view: his object is "palpable, finite," durable, place-bound — the thing whose loss of *independent status* (not its disappearance) he diagnoses. For Burnham sculpture is "by definition, it remains slavishly attached to the idea of physical mass." His displacement of the object is into the [[System (Burnham)|system]] (kinetic, light, cybernetic art), not into Lippard's disembodied concept. `Interpretive synthesis`

- **Object as inert place-bound thing vs. system as life-bearing process.** The defining contrast is internal to Burnham: the object "occupies a specific space... inert and stationary," whereas a system "may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life: self-organization, growth... input and output... and eventual death." The object is defined by stasis and place; the system by mutual dependence and lifelike activity. This pairs with [[Object Art]] (the late, "de-idealized" attempt to preserve pure physical presence) where "physical presence is everything." `Directly stated`

- **Object vs. Marx's reified "thing."** Burnham frames the object's loss of independence through Karl Marx's "thingification" (Verdinglichung) — but redirects it: "Reification will be described from another point of view," as "the constant resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory" rather than as commodity alienation alone (see [[Reification]]). The Burnham object is therefore both a product of the formative urge and a casualty of technics. `Directly stated`
