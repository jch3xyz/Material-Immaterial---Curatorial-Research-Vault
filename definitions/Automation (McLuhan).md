# Automation — Marshall McLuhan

## Author's Definition

For McLuhan, automation (which he treats as synonymous with "cybernation") is defined not by its mechanism but by its **identity with information**. The keystone definitional statement is flat and total:

> Automation is information and it not only ends jobs in the world of work, it ends subjects in the world of learning. It does not end the world of learning.

(`Directly stated`)

He defines it again by negation and by what it actually is — an invasion rather than a continuation:

> Automation is not an extension of the mechanical principles of fragmentation and separation of operations. It is rather the invasion of the mechanical world by the instantaneous character of electricity.

(`Directly stated`)

Automation is therefore the penetration of the mechanical order by **electric instantaneity** — an "instant inclusive embrace" that handles every unit of the industrial and marketing process the way a broadcast medium fuses its audience into a single interprocess:

> Automation or cybernation deals with all the units and components of the industrial and marketing process exactly as radio or TV combine the individuals in the audience into new interprocess.

(`Directly stated`)

This is the **concept+definition pattern** that sits beside the concept note [[Automation as Learning a Living]] — as `Media Hot and Cold` sits beside the definition [[Hot and Cool Media (McLuhan)]]. The definition note fixes McLuhan's terminological move; the concept note carries the broader argument about work, leisure, and "earning a living."

## Context

McLuhan introduces automation in the final chapter of *Understanding Media* as the limit case of his general thesis that electric technology reverses the mechanical age of fragmentation. Where mechanization proceeds by breaking processes "into homogenized but unrelated bits," electricity "unifies these fragments once more because its speed of operation requires a high degree of interdependence among all phases of any operation" (`Directly stated`).

The definitional equation `automation = information` does specific work in his argument:

- It dissolves the figure of the **job** and the **subject** (the school subject, the specialist slot) at once, because both were artifacts of mechanical fragmentation. "The future of work consists of earning a living in the automation age" (`Directly stated`).
- It rests on a prior identity McLuhan asserts for electricity itself: "Light is a nonspecial-ist kind of energy or power that is identical with information and knowledge. Such is also the relation of electricity to automation, since both energy and information can be applied in a great variety of ways" (`Directly stated`). Because the source of energy is separated from the work operation — as in any servomechanism — power can be "applied indifferently and quickly to many kinds of tasks," and what is moved is "not corporeal substance... but perception and information" (`Directly stated`). This is McLuhan's material/immaterial pivot: production fuses with information and learning.
- It depends structurally on **feedback**. "Perfecting the individual machine by making it automatic involves 'feedback.' That means introducing an information loop or circuit, where before there had been merely a one way flow or mechanical sequence" (`Directly stated`). Automation "certainly assumes the servomechanism and the computer... it assumes electricity as store and expediter of information" (`Directly stated`). This is the direct hinge to [[Cybernetics]] and [[Feedback Loops]].

The "instant inclusive embrace" phrasing matters: McLuhan defines automation's "real 'mass production'" as a matter of simultaneity, "not in terms of size, but of an instant inclusive embrace" (`Directly stated`) — the same structure he assigns to mass media, where "everybody becomes involved in them at the same time."

## Differences from Other Uses

Automation in the vault's other authors is treated as a *mechanism or an economic process*; for McLuhan it is an *ontological identity* (automation IS information). This distinguishes his use sharply:

- **Vs. labour/economics (Han, Lippard, Harari).** Where [[Immaterial Labour]] and [[Dataism]] frame the move into information as a regime of power, exploitation, or worldview, McLuhan's definition is medium-theoretic and largely affirmative about the *form*: automation ends the "fragmented tasks and specialist slots for 'workers'" and reopens "complex roles." His worry is not domination but readiness — that a "uniformly trained and homogenized citizenry" will be "unable to understand the cybernated world in which they live" (`Directly stated`). The convergence with Han/Harari on `automation = information` is real but the valence differs (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **Vs. [[Cybernetics]] (Hayles, Haraway).** McLuhan uses "cybernation" as a flat synonym for automation and leans on feedback and the servomechanism, but he does not develop cybernetics' technical apparatus (homeostasis, the observer, recursion); he absorbs it into a thesis about electric speed extending the "central nervous system" globally (`Strongly implied`).
- **Vs. the computational-mind tradition (Kurzweil, O'Gieblyn, Harari).** McLuhan explicitly declines the "computers seem to 'think'" claim: machines "are highly specialized at present, and quite lacking in the full process of interrelation that makes for consciousness," and a conscious computer "would still be one that was an extension of our consciousness, as a telescope is an extension of our eyes" (`Directly stated`). Automation extends faculties; it does not replace or upload them — a contrast with [[Patternism]] and [[Mind Uploading]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).
