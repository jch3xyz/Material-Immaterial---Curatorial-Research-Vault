# Cyberspace — Edward A. Shanken (introducing Roy Ascott)

> **Attribution note.** This note is anchored in chapter 04, *"From Cybernetics to Telematics,"* which is **Edward A. Shanken's editor's introduction** to *Telematic Embrace* — a scholar writing ABOUT Roy Ascott, not Ascott's own prose. Every verbatim quote below is Shanken's. The term *cyberspace* itself is not Shanken's coinage: he attributes it to **William Gibson** (in *Neuromancer*, 1984). Shanken's contribution here is the analytic DISTINCTION he draws between cyberspace (the individual pole) and [[Telematic Art|telematics]] (the collective pole) of the same networked condition.

## Author's Definition

In framing Ascott's work, Shanken defines cyberspace as the *virtual location* of an individual's state of mind inside networked telecommunications (`Directly stated`):

> Cyberspace applies a virtual location to the state of mind an individual experiences in telematic networks.

He fixes the term's origin in the same passage (`Directly stated`):

> Drawing a parallel between cybernetics and computer telecommunications, William Gibson coined the term "cyberspace" in his 1984 novel Neuromancer.

And he marks its ambivalence — cyberspace as a doubling of the physical world that both heightens and de-realizes it (`Directly stated`):

> Cyberspace is a double-edged sword that reproduces the physical world, simultaneously intensifying and dematerializing it.

## Context

Shanken introduces cyberspace not for its own sake but as one half of a deliberate **polarity** he uses to position Ascott's project. He sets it directly against telematics: cyberspace names the *phenomenology of the single mind* in the network, while [[Telematic Art|telematics]] names the *emergence of a shared mind* across the network (`Directly stated`):

> Telematics implies the potential exchange of information among all nodes in the network, proposing what might amount to a decentralized yet collective state of mind. Whereas cyberspace emphasizes the phenomenology of individual experience, telematics emphasizes the emergence of a collective consciousness.

This distinction does real work in the argument. Ascott's wager — the [[The Telematic Embrace|telematic embrace]], [[Network Consciousness|network consciousness]] "greater than the sum of its parts" — lives entirely on the COLLECTIVE side. Cyberspace is the term Shanken reserves for the INDIVIDUAL's experiential interior of that same networked space: a "virtual location" the single subject inhabits as a state of mind. The "double-edged sword" line then supplies the critical counterweight — cyberspace can intensify the world, but it also *dematerializes* it, and the surrounding sentence enumerates its pathologies (online violence, surveillance, viruses) as "part of the economy and structure of the global village" (`Strongly implied`: cyberspace is the ambivalent individual underside of Ascott's utopian collective telematics). The note thus sits at the hinge between Ascott's optimism and Shanken's editorial caution.

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. [[Telematic Art|Telematics]] (Ascott via Shanken).** This is the constitutive distinction of the source passage. Cyberspace = the INDIVIDUAL's state of mind / virtual location; telematics = the COLLECTIVE, decentralized, emergent consciousness of the whole network. They describe the same infrastructure from opposite poles — the one phenomenological and singular, the other social and emergent (`Directly stated`).

- **vs. [[Virtuality]] (Hayles).** Both names a relation between the material body and an informatic, non-located "where." But they are NOT the same concept and are kept distinct. For [[Virtuality]], Hayles defines a cultural *perception* — that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns — staged through the body/embodiment problematic of the posthuman. Shanken's cyberspace is narrower and more spatial: a *virtual location* for the experiencing individual mind inside telematic networks, inherited from Gibson's fiction rather than from cybernetic theory. Shanken's "intensifying and dematerializing" doubling resonates with Hayles's pattern/presence concerns, but the two are cross-linked, not merged (`Interpretive synthesis`).

- **vs. [[The Global Village]] (McLuhan).** Shanken explicitly folds cyberspace's harms back into "the economy and structure of the global village," so the two are adjacent framings of the networked planet. McLuhan's global village is the macro, social-sensorium figure of electric interconnection; cyberspace is the micro, first-person interior of inhabiting that same network (`Strongly implied`).
