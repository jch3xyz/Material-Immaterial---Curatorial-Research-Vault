# Individualization — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

Zuboff defines *individualization* negatively first — by ruling out two terms it is routinely confused with — and only then positively, as a historical process (*Directly stated*):

> First let’s establish that the concept of “individualization” should not be confused with the neoliberal ideology of “individualism” that shifts all responsibility for success or failure to a mythical, atomized, isolated individual, doomed to a life of perpetual competition and disconnected from relationships, community, and society. Neither does it refer to the psychological process of “individuation” that is associated with the lifelong exploration of self-development. Instead, individualization is a consequence of long-term processes of modernization.

She names it elsewhere in the chapter as the master process of the era (*Directly stated*):

> In fact, each is the fruit of distinct phases of a centuries-long process known as “individualization” that is the human signature of the modern era.

## Context

The definition appears in Chapter 2, "Setting the Stage for Surveillance Capitalism," where Zuboff establishes the historical conditions her whole argument rests on. Surveillance capitalism, she argues, "owes its success to the destructive collision of two opposing historical forces": one vector is "the longer history of modernization and the centuries-long societal shift from the mass to the individual"; the other is "the decades-long elaboration and implementation of the neoliberal economic paradigm" whose aim is "to reverse, subdue, impede, and even destroy the individual urge toward psychological self-determination and moral agency." *Individualization* is Zuboff's name for the first vector (*Directly stated*).

The precision of the definition is load-bearing. Zuboff needs *individualization* to denote a structural-historical condition — "the advent of the individual as the locus of moral agency and choice" — so that she can hold it apart from the neoliberal *ideology* it is then weaponized against. To collapse individualization into "individualism" would be to side with the very paradigm she is diagnosing, which blames atomized individuals for systemic failures. To collapse it into "individuation" would psychologize a process that is for her sociological and epochal. The distinction lets her argue that the unmet, structurally produced hunger for self-determination is precisely what surveillance capitalism feeds on (*Strongly implied*).

Individualization unfolds in phases. The "first modernity" is the moment when "life became 'individualized' for great numbers of people as they separated from traditional norms, meanings, and rules" — Ford's mass consumers, escaping "a still-feudal world." The [[Second Modernity]] is its bloom: "a new society of people born to a sense of psychological individuality, with its double-edged birthright of liberation and necessity." The definition note isolates the explicit definitional passage; the concept's historical development and its function as the staging ground for surveillance capitalism are carried in the [[Second Modernity]] concept note, on the `Surveillance Capitalism` / `Surveillance Capitalism (Zuboff)` precedent (*Directly stated*).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Vs. "individualism" (Zuboff's own contrast).** Individualization is a process of modernization that produces the individual as a locus of moral agency; individualism is a "neoliberal ideology" that recasts that individual as "a mythical, atomized, isolated" competitor and offloads all responsibility onto them. For Zuboff the two are opponents, not synonyms: individualism is an ideology aimed at reversing individualization (*Directly stated*).
- **Vs. "individuation" (Zuboff's own contrast).** Individuation is "the psychological process … associated with the lifelong exploration of self-development." Individualization is not a developmental task internal to a person but a "consequence of long-term processes of modernization" — a condition into which whole populations are born (*Directly stated*).
- **Vs. Han's [[Achievement Society]].** Where Zuboff treats the "requirement to choose our own lives" as a historically produced birthright that is then thwarted, Byung-Chul Han reads the same compulsory freedom as the engine of self-exploitation — "freedom itself is producing coercion." Zuboff's individualization names the emancipatory promise; Han's achievement-subject names what becomes of it under [[Self-Optimization]]. The two accounts converge on the "life sentence to uncertainty, anxiety, and stress" that Zuboff concedes the second modernity can be (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- **Vs. the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]].** Zuboff's self-determining individual is a sociological cousin of the autonomous liberal subject of Hayles and the vault's other authors; individualization is the historical process by which that subject's claims become felt mass experience rather than philosophical premise (*Interpretive synthesis*).
