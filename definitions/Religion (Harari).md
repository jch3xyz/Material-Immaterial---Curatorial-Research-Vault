# Religion — Yuval Noah Harari

## Author's Definition

Harari gives a deliberately **functional** definition of religion, one keyed to social effect rather than to the content of belief. The load-bearing sentence:

> Religion is anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures. It legitimises human norms and values by arguing that they reflect superhuman laws.

The decisive move is the clause that precedes it: religion "is created by humans rather than by gods, and it is defined by its social function rather than by the existence of deities" (`Directly stated`). Religion is therefore not a kind of *content* (gods, the supernatural, superstition) but a kind of *function* — the operation by which a human-made social order is made to appear non-negotiable, grounded in laws "that we did not invent and that we cannot change" (`Directly stated`).

This is why Harari's definition is radically inclusive. By it, "Buddhism and Daoism … Nazism, communism and liberalism" all count as religions: each "argue[s] that the superhuman laws are natural laws," each tells "its members that they must obey some superhuman moral law, and that breaking this law will result in catastrophe" (`Directly stated`). The presence or absence of a god is irrelevant; what matters is the conferral of *superhuman legitimacy*.

## Context

Harari introduces the definition in chapter 5 ("The Odd Couple") to clear away what he calls "faulty definitions of religion." He explicitly rejects three popular equations — religion is **not** superstition ("most people are unlikely to call their cherished beliefs 'superstitions'"), **not** belief in supernatural powers (for those who believe in demons, "demons aren't supernatural"), and **not** belief in gods ("Defining religion as 'belief in gods' is also problematic") (`Directly stated`). The functional redefinition is what survives this clearing operation.

The definition does specific work in the larger argument of *Homo Deus*. It lets Harari treat modern secular ideologies — above all [[Humanism (Harari)]] — as religions in the strict sense, setting up the book's claim that modernity is not the replacement of religion by science but "a deal between science and one particular religion – namely, humanism" (`Directly stated`). Read against Harari's account of how fictions bind strangers together, the definition is the hinge between religion and [[Large-Scale Flexible Cooperation]]: religion is the instrument that makes a shared imagined order feel like cosmic law, so that "Human cooperation requires firm answers rather than just questions" (`Strongly implied` — the cooperative function is stated, the identification with the cooperation thesis is interpretive).

Harari also distinguishes religion sharply from **spirituality**. Where religion cements the social order, spirituality tries to escape it:

> Religion is a deal, whereas spirituality is a journey.

Religion "gives a complete description of the world, and offers us a well-defined contract with predetermined goals," while spiritual journeys "take people in mysterious ways towards unknown destinations" (`Directly stated`). This opposition is developed in the paired tension [[Religion vs Spirituality]]; the functional ordering work is developed in the paired concept [[Religion as Social Order]].

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Against the everyday sense (religion = belief in God / the supernatural).** This is the usage Harari builds his definition *against*. His religion is a social mechanism, not a metaphysics; "a fervent communist" is religious not despite having no gods but because communism confers superhuman legitimacy on a social order (`Directly stated`).

- **Convergence with [[Humanism (Harari)]].** The definition is what licenses Harari to call humanism "the humanist religion" — a creed that "worships humanity." Humanism is the special case of this general definition in which the superhuman law is relocated into human experience itself. The two notes are mutually defining: religion is the genus, humanism a species (`Directly stated` for humanism-as-religion; `Interpretive synthesis` for the genus/species framing).

- **Against Han's [[Capital as Religion]].** In the vault, Byung-Chul Han (via Benjamin) treats capitalism as a *cultic* religion of pure debt/guilt with no possibility of atonement — religion as a structure of permanent indebtedness. Harari's religion is broader and more neutral: any conferral of superhuman legitimacy on a social order, with no necessary debt-structure. They overlap in treating economic ideology as religion but diverge on mechanism — Han's is cult and guilt, Harari's is legitimation and obedience (`Interpretive synthesis`; link by intent).

- **Against transcendence-based or "spiritual" senses.** Harari pointedly severs religion from spirituality: a "spiritual path" challenges and tries to escape exactly the worldly order that religion exists to cement, so that, in his reading, "the gap between religion and spirituality is much bigger" than commonly assumed (`Directly stated`).
