# Bounded Rationality — Herbert Simon (via Donella Meadows)

## Author's Definition

Under the heading "Bounded Rationality" in the chapter on why systems surprise us, Meadows names the concept, attributes it to its originator, and states it in one sentence:

> "Nobel Prize-winning economist Herbert Simon calls bounded rationality. Bounded rationality means that people make quite reasonable decisions based on the information they have. But they don't have perfect information, especially about more distant parts of the system." (*Directly stated*)

The definition has two halves that must be held together. First, a defense of the actor: decisions are "quite reasonable" — the actor is not stupid, irrational, or malicious. Second, a limit on the actor: the reasonableness runs only as far as the actor's information, which is never perfect and is thinnest precisely about "the more distant parts of the system." What bounds rationality, then, is not the mind but the *reach of information* from a particular position in a structure.

Meadows immediately supplies the positive counter-model Simon offered against neoclassical economics — the human as *satisficer* rather than optimizer:

> "We are not omniscient, rational optimizers, says Simon. Rather, we are blundering 'satisficers,' attempting to meet (satisfy) our needs well enough (sufficiently) before moving on to the next decision." (*Directly stated*)

The satisficer "do[es] our best to further our own nearby interests in a rational way, but we can take into account only what we know" — we rarely see the full range of possibilities and "often don't foresee (or choose to ignore) the impacts of our actions on the whole system," so "instead of finding a long-term optimum, we discover within our limited purview a choice we can live with for now, and we stick to it." (*Directly stated*)

## Context

The definition sits inside `Bounded Rationality` as one of Meadows's named reasons that systems produce surprises. It arrives as the answer to a question she poses against Adam Smith's "invisible hand": if rational self-interest were supposed to aggregate into "the good of the whole," why does "the world present us with multiple examples of people acting rationally in their short-term best interests and producing aggregate results that no one likes" — tourists ruining the resorts they flock to, fishermen overfishing their own livelihood, corporations collectively producing business-cycle downturns? Meadows's answer is the World Bank economist Herman Daly's "invisible foot" and, decisively, "what Nobel Prize-winning economist Herbert Simon calls bounded rationality." (*Directly stated*)

Two things about the *carrying* matter for this note's suffix. The concept is **Simon's** — Meadows is explicit that "the theory of bounded rationality challenged two hundred years of economics based on the teachings of political economist Adam Smith," dismantling the twin assumptions "that homo economicus acts with perfect optimality on complete information" and that many such actors together produce "the best possible outcome for everybody." But the *use* to which the term is put here is **Meadows's**: she recasts Simon's cognitive-economic thesis as a *systems* explanation. Bounded rationality becomes a property of an actor's *position* — "the information flows, the incentives and disincentives, the goals and discrepancies, the pressures — the bounded rationality — that goes with that position." (*Directly stated*) Swap the person and the bounded rationality stays, because it belongs to the seat, not the sitter: "If you become a manager, you probably will stop seeing labor as a deserving partner in production, and start seeing it as a cost to be minimized… If you are now a fisherman with a mortgage on your boat, a family to support, and imperfect knowledge of the state of the fish population, you will overfish." (*Directly stated*)

This is why the definition is anti-heroic in Meadows's hands. Because the constraint is structural, the intervention is structural: not replacing the actor but "redesigning the system to improve the information, incentives, disincentives, goals, stresses, and constraints that have an effect on specific actors." The suffix `(Simon via Meadows)` marks exactly this transmission — Simon's origin, Meadows's carrying voice, which routes the concept toward `Feedback Loops`, `No Separate Systems`, and the whole systems-thinking toolkit rather than toward decision theory. (*Interpretive synthesis*)

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Simon's original vs. Meadows's systems reading.** For Simon the boundedness is chiefly *cognitive-informational* — the mind is a satisficer that cannot compute the global optimum. Meadows keeps that sense verbatim ("blundering 'satisficers'") but *relocates the bound into the structure*: what an actor can know is set by the "information flows… incentives and disincentives… pressures" of a position in a system. The unit of analysis shifts from the individual chooser to the actor-in-a-structure, which is why her remedy is redesign of the structure rather than better individual reasoning.

- **Not a claim of irrationality.** Unlike everyday uses that treat bad collective outcomes as evidence of stupidity, greed, or bad faith, bounded rationality insists the decisions are "quite reasonable." Meadows draws the anti-blame corollary explicitly — "Blaming the individual rarely helps create a more desirable outcome" — a move a moralizing account of the same behaviors would not make.

- **Against homo economicus.** The definition is framed *against* the vault-relevant model of the omniscient optimizer acting "with perfect optimality on complete information." Where classical economics reads aggregated rational choice as social welfare, bounded rationality reads separately-rational choices aggregating into results "that no one likes" — the tension the vault names `Individual Rationality vs System Welfare`.

- **Distinct from the concept note it defines.** This is the verbatim *definition* note (Simon's sentence and satisficer model, fixed to the text). The broader concept — its role in `Policy Resistance`, the tragedy of the commons, and Meadows's leverage-oriented cure — is carried by `Bounded Rationality`. Same pattern as `Information` / `Information (Han)`; do not collapse the two.
