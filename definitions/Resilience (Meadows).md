# Resilience — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

For Donella Meadows, *resilience* is the property that lets a system take a beating and recover — it is "a measure of a system's ability to survive and persist within a variable environment," and "the opposite of resilience is brittleness or rigidity." `Directly stated`

She grounds the term first in ordinary language before generalizing it. Rather than pick among the competing technical senses ("many definitions, depending on the branch of engineering, ecology, or system science doing the defining"), she takes "the normal dictionary meaning": "the ability to bounce or spring back into shape, position, etc., after being pressed or stretched. Elasticity. The ability to recover strength, spirits, good humor, or any other aspect quickly." `Directly stated`

Structurally, resilience is not a substance a system has but a *capacity produced by its wiring*. It "arises from a rich structure of many [[Feedback Loops|feedback loops]] that can work in different ways to restore a system even after a large perturbation." A single [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing loop]] brings a stock back to its desired state; resilience "is provided by several such loops, operating through different mechanisms, at different time scales, and with redundancy—one kicking in if another one fails." At the limit, "a set of feedback loops that can restore or rebuild feedback loops is resilience at a still higher level—meta-resilience," and loops that "can learn, create, design, and evolve ever more complex restorative structures" are what Meadows calls [[Self-Organization|self-organizing]]. `Directly stated`

## Context

Resilience is the first of the three sources of good system behavior Meadows treats in Chapter 3, "Why Systems Work So Well" (the others being [[Self-Organization]] and [[Hierarchy (Systems)|hierarchy]]). Her aim in the chapter is to explain why real systems are so often robust and self-correcting, and she locates that robustness in the redundancy and layering of their [[Feedback Loops]]. The definition therefore does double duty: it names a desirable system property and it points immediately at the mechanism (many overlapping restorative loops) that supplies it.

Two of Meadows's emphases sharpen the definition. First, resilience is *not stability*: "Resilience is not the same thing as being static or constant over time. Resilient systems can be very dynamic," and "conversely, systems that are constant over time can be unresilient." A resilient system may oscillate, cycle, or pass through "succession, climax, and collapse" — resilience is what "acts to restore" it, not what holds it still. Second, resilience is *hard to see*: it "may be very hard to see, unless you exceed its limits, overwhelm and damage the balancing loops, and the system structure breaks down." Because it is invisible without "a whole-system view, people often sacrifice resilience for stability, or for productivity, or for some other more immediately recognizable system property" — the bovine-growth-hormone cow, the just-in-time supply chain, the single-species plantation. Meadows's practical upshot is a managerial imperative: "Systems need to be managed not only for productivity or stability, they also need to be managed for resilience— the ability to recover from perturbation, the ability to restore or repair themselves." `Directly stated`

Her governing image is spatial: "I think of resilience as a plateau upon which the system can play, performing its normal functions in safety. A resilient system has a big plateau, a lot of space over which it can wander, with gentle, elastic walls." As a system loses resilience "its plateau shrinks, and its protective walls become lower and more rigid, until the system is operating on a knifeedge" — so that "loss of resilience can come as a surprise," a system doing "something it has done a hundred times before" and then crashing. `Directly stated`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. [[Homeostasis]] (the cybernetic lineage the vault otherwise carries).** In the tradition the vault inherits from Cannon, Wiener and Hayles, homeostasis is a single goal-seeking loop holding one variable near a *set point*. Meadows's resilience is the more capacious property: not one balancing loop defending one target, but "a rich structure of many feedback loops" — layered, redundant, operating "at different time scales" — that can "restore or rebuild feedback loops" even after "a large perturbation." Homeostasis is thus closer to the *single* balancing loop that resilience is built out of many of; resilience is the system-level robustness that survives the failure of any one such loop. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. stability / static constancy (a distinction Meadows draws inside the text).** This is the sharpest boundary she herself insists on: "This distinction between static stability and resilience is important." Static stability is visible and short-horizon — "measured by variation in the condition of a system week by week or year by year" — while resilience concerns the system's capacity to bounce back over the long run and is invisible until stressed. A system can be stable yet brittle, or dynamic yet resilient. `Directly stated`
- **vs. [[Self-Organization]] (the next chapter-3 characteristic).** Meadows explicitly nests the two: resilience escalates into self-organization when the restorative feedback loops themselves "can learn, create, design, and evolve ever more complex restorative structures." Self-organization is thus resilience's higher gear, not a synonym for it — the two are cross-linked but kept distinct. `Directly stated`
- **vs. the concept note [[Resilience]].** This is the definitional entry — the verbatim term as Meadows fixes it in *Thinking in Systems*. The concept note develops resilience's role across her framework and its wider resonances in the vault's systems/cybernetics cluster. They share a base name, disambiguated by the `(Meadows)` suffix and by folder, in the vault's established pattern (as with `Transparency` / `Transparency (Han)`).
