# Telematics — Roy Ascott

## Author's Definition

For Ascott, **telematics** names the convergence of computers and telecommunications — but his working definition (as glossed by his editor, Edward A. Shanken) extends the term past the machinery to the relations it makes possible: "computer-mediated communications networking between geographically dispersed individuals and institutions . . . and between the human mind and artificial systems of intelligence and perception" (`Directly stated`, quoted by Shanken from Ascott — Source 1).

Ascott is careful to separate the medium from the activity it supports: "I prefer to use the terms 'networking,' for the activity, and 'telematics,' for the medium as a whole" (`Directly stated` — Source 2). And he insists the word does double duty — it both designates a technology and qualifies a condition of mind and culture: "The rubric 'telematics' not only refers to the convergence of computers and telecommunications systems but qualifies a whole class of consciousness and culture in which new modalities of knowledge and the means of their distribution are being tested and extended" (`Directly stated` — Source 3).

The term itself is not Ascott's coinage. As he records, "It was Simon Nora who coined the term telematics to describe the new electronic technology derived from the convergence of computers and telecommunications systems" (`Directly stated` — Source 4) — the reference is to the French *télématique*, introduced in the 1978 Nora–Minc report *L'Informatisation de la société* and later anglicized as "telematics." Ascott's contribution is not the word but its expansion: he takes a piece of state-commissioned policy vocabulary and turns it into an aesthetic and a theory of consciousness.

## Context

Ascott adopts "telematics" at the hinge of his career, where the 1960s **[[Cybernetics|cybernetic]]** / behaviourist art of his Change Paintings and the cybernetic art matrix gives way to the 1980s telematic art of networked, distributed projects (most famously *La Plissure du Texte*, 1983). The definition is doing load-bearing work in that turn: by defining the medium as networking "between geographically dispersed individuals and institutions . . . and between the human mind and artificial systems of intelligence and perception," Ascott liberates art from the unique physical object in a unique location and relocates it in process, exchange, and a "decentralized yet collective state of mind" (`Strongly implied` — the definition underwrites his whole programme).

The two-part structure of the gloss is deliberate. The first half (mind-to-mind networking) grounds **[[Network Consciousness]]** — the emergent, planetary field of mind Ascott takes telematic exchange to produce. The second half (mind to "artificial systems of intelligence and perception") keeps the cybernetic and AI lineage in view, so that telematics is never merely human telecommunication but a coupling of human and machine cognition. This is the definitional seed of Ascott's later technoetic and "moist" cosmology, and it is why he treats telematics as a "class of consciousness and culture," not just a set of cables and protocols (`Interpretive synthesis`).

In the vault this definition realizes and extends, rather than founds, the art-and-network cluster. The committed concepts **[[New Media Art]]** and **[[Art as Experiential Technology]]** — first realized as Jack Burnham–anchored notes in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* — receive their networked form here: telematic art is new media art carried to the planetary network, and the artwork-as-system the participator completes is experiential technology par excellence. Ascott's "no senders and receivers . . . only users" rejection of the information-theory dyad is the telematic flowering of Burnham's **[[Reciprocal Communication]]** (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Telematics vs the term's policy origin (Nora & Minc).** For Nora and Minc, *télématique* was an administrative-economic category — the infrastructural marriage of computing and telecommunications and its consequences for French society. Ascott keeps that technical sense but subordinates it: telematics for him "qualifies a whole class of consciousness and culture," an aesthetic and a psychic condition, not a policy domain (`Directly stated`).

- **Telematics vs [[Cybernetics]].** Cybernetics (Wiener: "control and communication in the animal and the machine") names the science of feedback and regulation that Ascott read in 1961 and that grounds his behaviourist art. Telematics is its networked, planetary descendant — the medium in which the cybernetic feedback loop is distributed across geographically dispersed minds and machines (`Strongly implied`).

- **Telematics vs cyberspace.** Shanken's editorial distinction (which frames Ascott's usage): cyberspace applies a virtual location to the *individual's* phenomenology inside the network, whereas telematics emphasizes the *emergence of a collective* consciousness. Ascott's telematics is irreducibly plural and relational where cyberspace can be solitary (`Directly stated`, by Shanken — Source 1 context).

- **Telematics vs [[Information]] (Shannon's model).** Ascott explicitly rejects "the old models of information theory" with their senders and receivers. Where the Shannon strand in the vault's `Information` note treats communication as the transmission of a signal across a channel, Ascott's telematics dissolves that dyad into "users" who co-create negotiated meaning — meaning "not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined" (`Directly stated`).

- **Telematics vs the broader [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension.** Ascott's definition supplies the art-side, networked pole of the vault's eight-author `Material vs Immaterial` tension: the telematic work is dematerialized into "virtual immateriality," a flux of relations rather than a discrete object (`Interpretive synthesis`).
