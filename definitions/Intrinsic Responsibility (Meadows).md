# Intrinsic Responsibility — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

For Donella Meadows, *intrinsic responsibility* is a property of a system's **design**, not a virtue of the people inside it: "'Intrinsic responsibility' means that the system is designed to send feedback about the consequences of decision making directly and quickly and compellingly to the decision makers." `Directly stated`

Three adverbs carry the whole definition — the feedback must reach the decision maker **directly** (to the right person, not a proxy), **quickly** (before the consequences compound), and **compellingly** (so it cannot be ignored). Her governing image is the cockpit: "Because the pilot of a plane rides in the front of the plane, that pilot is intrinsically responsible. He or she will experience directly the consequences of his or her decisions." `Directly stated` Responsibility here is engineered into the seating chart, not appealed to as conscience.

The definition is the design half of a two-part guideline whose analysis half Meadows states as a heading, "Locate Responsibility in the System" — a rule "both for analysis and design. In analysis, it means looking for the ways the system creates its own behavior." `Directly stated` The point is to stop blaming outside triggers and instead redraw the [[Feedback Loops]] so that whoever decides also bears the result.

## Context

Intrinsic responsibility is one of the practical "wisdoms" Meadows offers in the closing chapter, [[Living in a World of Systems]], for acting inside systems too complex to control. She introduces it against the reflex to blame the outside: "sometimes blaming or trying to control the outside influence blinds one to the easier task of increasing responsibility within the system." `Directly stated` The redesign she describes is a matter of routing consequence-carrying feedback back onto the decision maker.

Her worked examples are all about closing that loop. Dartmouth College *reduced* intrinsic responsibility "when it took thermostats out of individual offices and classrooms and put temperature-control decisions under the guidance of a central computer" — severing the fast local loop and producing "greater oscillations in room temperature." `Directly stated` One repair, she notes, would be "to let professors keep control of their own thermostats and charge them directly for the amount of energy they use." `Directly stated` Designing *for* intrinsic responsibility, she continues, "could mean, for example, requiring all towns or companies that emit wastewater into a stream to place their intake pipes downstream from their outflow pipe" `Directly stated` — the polluter forced to drink what it dumps. The negative cases sharpen the same principle: "A great deal of responsibility was lost when rulers who declared war were no longer expected to lead the troops into battle. Warfare became even more irresponsible when it became possible to push a button and cause tremendous damage at such a distance that the person pushing the button never even sees the damage." `Directly stated` Distance from consequence *is* the loss of intrinsic responsibility.

The design principle is thus a leverage move on feedback structure: it belongs to the "information and control" tier of Meadows's [[Leverage Points]], where you change not a physical stock but the wiring that tells actors what their actions cost.

- **vs. the companion concept [[Intrinsic Responsibility]].** This is the definitional entry — the verbatim sentence as Meadows fixes it in Chapter 7, with its cockpit and thermostat cases. The concept note develops the idea across her framework and the vault (its place among the systems "wisdoms," its resonance with feedback-loop design and with accountability at a distance). Same base name, disambiguated by the `(Meadows)` suffix and by folder, in the vault's established pattern (as with `Transparency` / `Transparency (Han)`).
- **vs. moral or legal "responsibility."** Meadows deliberately does *not* mean blame, duty, or after-the-fact accountability. Intrinsic responsibility is a structural property — "the system is designed to send feedback" — so that the right feedback reaches the right actor automatically, independent of anyone's good intentions. The reform is to the loop, not to the person. `Strongly implied`
- **vs. the vault's cybernetic vocabulary — [[Feedback Loops]], [[Homeostasis]], [[Self-Organization]].** Those name mechanisms *internal to* a system as it runs. Intrinsic responsibility is a second-order, *design* criterion applied to one of those mechanisms: it asks whether a system's feedback loops are routed so that consequences land on the deciders. It grounds, in its native discipline, the loop-design intuition the vault otherwise carries secondhand through Hayles. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and surveillance-as-feedback.** Meadows prizes feedback that returns consequences *to the decision maker* — a self-correcting, self-organizing move. This is the structural inverse of the surveillance regimes elsewhere in the vault, where feedback about the many is routed *away* from them and up to a central controller (the Dartmouth central computer is her miniature of exactly that severing). Same raw material — loops carrying consequence — turned to opposite ends: accountability versus asymmetric control. `Speculative connection`
