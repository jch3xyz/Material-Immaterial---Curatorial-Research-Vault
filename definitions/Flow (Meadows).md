# Flow — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

For Meadows, a **flow** is the *rate* that fills or drains a [[Stocks and Flows|stock]] — the changing action that alters an accumulation over time. She names flows ostensively, by the paired verbs of increase and decrease: "Flows are filling and draining, births and deaths, purchases and sales, growth and decay, deposits and withdrawals, successes and failures" (`Directly stated`). Where a stock is a level one can "see, feel, count, or measure at any given time," a flow is a rate per unit of time — an inflow or an outflow — and it is only through flows that "Stocks change over time" (`Directly stated`).

The definition is completed by its complement: "A stock, then, is the present memory of the history of changing flows within the system" (`Directly stated`). A flow is therefore the active term of the stock-flow pair — the movement whose accumulated record *is* the stock.

## Context

Meadows introduces flows in the opening pages of "The Basics," immediately after defining the stock as "the foundation of any system." The two are the elementary primitives of system dynamics (a vocabulary she inherits from Jay Forrester), and she teaches them through the bathtub: the water in the tub is a stock; the faucet and the drain are flows. In her stock-and-flow diagrams the stock is drawn as a box and each flow as an arrow-headed "pipe" fitted with a faucet that can be turned up or down.

From this picture Meadows derives the governing principles of accumulation: "As long as the sum of all inflows exceeds the sum of all outflows, the level of the stock will rise... if the sum of all outflows equals the sum of all inflows, the stock level will not change; it will be held in dynamic equilibrium" (`Directly stated`). Flows are what a manager can adjust abruptly — "You can adjust the drain or faucet of a bathtub—the flows—abruptly, but it is much more difficult to change the level of water—the stock—quickly" — yet their effect on the stock is never instantaneous, because "A stock takes time to change, because flows take time to flow" (`Directly stated`). This gap between fast-adjustable flows and slow-responding stocks is what gives systems their [[Delays|delays]], buffers, and momentum.

Meadows presses a cognitive correction that hinges on flows: "The human mind seems to focus more easily on stocks than on flows. On top of that, when we do focus on flows, we tend to focus on inflows more easily than on outflows" (`Directly stated`). The consequence is her recurring lesson — "A stock can be increased by decreasing its outflow rate as well as by increasing its inflow rate. There's more than one way to fill a bathtub!" A gain in energy efficiency (a reduced outflow) raises the stock of available oil exactly as a new oilfield (an increased inflow) does; a firm can enlarge its workforce by slowing quitting and firing rather than by hiring (`Strongly implied`). Flows come in matched inflow/outflow pairs, and both are levers.

Finally, flows are the site where control enters a system: "A feedback loop is formed when changes in a stock affect the flows into or out of that same stock." A [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] closes precisely when a stock's level loops back to adjust the flows that fill or drain it — so the flow is the point at which structure becomes self-regulating (`Directly stated`).

## Differences from Other Uses

- **Flow vs. stock (within Meadows).** The pair must not be collapsed. A stock is a *level* (a quantity at an instant); a flow is a *rate* (a change per unit of time). The stock is the memory; the flow is the writing and erasing of that memory. This note is the companion definition beside the concept [[Stocks and Flows]] and beside a stock's definitional role there.

- **"Flow of information" (Meadows, elsewhere).** In the same chapter Meadows also speaks of information flowing — "interconnections in systems operate through the flow of information." That is a looser, general sense of movement-through-a-network. The technical flow defined here is narrower: the specific inflow/outflow rate acting on a named stock in a stock-and-flow diagram. The two senses are related but not identical.

- **Distinct from Han's "flow"-adjacent vocabulary.** Meadows's flow is a neutral, quantitative rate of accumulation, native to systems science. It should not be conflated with the affective or temporal registers other vault authors work in — e.g. Byung-Chul Han's diagnoses of [[Acceleration]] and the smooth, frictionless circulation of the achievement society, which carry a critical and phenomenological charge Meadows's term does not (`Interpretive synthesis`). Meadows's flow is a modeling primitive, not a cultural symptom.

## Related Concepts

- [[Stocks and Flows]] — the parent concept; a flow is one half of the pair, the rate that raises or lowers the stock ("Stocks change over time through the actions of a flow").
- [[Feedback Loops]] — a loop closes when "changes in a stock affect the flows into or out of that same stock"; flows are the point at which a system controls itself.
- [[Delays]] — the temporal consequence of the flow-stock gap: because "flows take time to flow," a stock responds to a change in its flows only gradually.
- [[Systems Theory]] — stocks and flows are the elementary units in which system-dynamics models are built.
- [[Homeostasis]] — the limiting case in which inflow equals outflow and the stock holds at a constant level in dynamic equilibrium while flows continue.
