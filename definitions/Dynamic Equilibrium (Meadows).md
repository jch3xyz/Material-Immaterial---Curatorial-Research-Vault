# Dynamic Equilibrium — Donella Meadows

## Author's Definition

For Donella Meadows, *dynamic equilibrium* is the state of a stock whose level does not change because its total inflow exactly equals its total outflow — a constancy produced not by stillness but by a running balance of continuous flow. Her canonical statement comes from the bathtub: when the inflow faucet is set to pour water in at precisely the rate the drain lets it out, "the amount of water in the tub stays constant at whatever level it had reached when the inflow became equal to the outflow. It is in a state of dynamic equilibrium—its level does not change, although water is continuously flowing through it." `Directly stated`

Generalized to any [[Stocks and Flows|stock]], the definition becomes one of the "important principles" she draws from the bathtub: "If the sum of all outflows equals the sum of all inflows, the stock level will not change; it will be held in dynamic equilibrium at whatever level it happened to be when the two sets of flows became equal." `Directly stated`

## Context

Meadows introduces the term in Chapter 1 ("The Basics") while teaching the reader to read a stock-and-flow diagram. The bathtub is her deliberately minimal model — "one stock, one inflow, and one outflow" — chosen so that "all the dynamic possibilities" can be deduced by hand. Dynamic equilibrium is the third of three cases she lists (inflows exceed outflows → the stock rises; outflows exceed inflows → it falls; the two are equal → the stock holds). The word *dynamic* is load-bearing: it marks the state as an ongoing process rather than an inert one, since quantity keeps flowing through the stock even though the standing level is frozen.

In Chapter 2 ("A Brief Visit to the Systems Zoo") she restates the same balance at the level of [[Feedback Loops]]. In the population model, a reinforcing growth loop (births) competes with a balancing loop (deaths); as fertility falls to meet mortality the reinforcing loop "is gradually weakened" until it "exactly equals the strength of the balancing loop," at which point "neither loop dominates, and we have dynamic equilibrium," with "births exactly balancing deaths." She offers a second instance from capital economics: "When depreciation just balances investment, the economy is in dynamic equilibrium." Births balancing deaths, investment balancing depreciation, water in balancing water out — in each case the definition is the same: equal offsetting flows holding a stock steady. `Directly stated`

## Differences from Other Uses

- **vs. [[Homeostasis]] (the concept as the vault otherwise carries it).** Homeostasis, in the cybernetic lineage the vault inherits from Cannon, Wiener and Hayles, names a goal-seeking regulatory *mechanism* — a balancing loop that acts to hold a variable near a *set point*. Meadows's dynamic equilibrium is not a mechanism but a *state of the stock*: the balanced condition a system can be in. Two differences follow. First, the equilibrium level is *contingent* — it is "at whatever level it happened to be when the two sets of flows became equal," not a target the system steers toward. Second, it needs no controller: a stock can sit in dynamic equilibrium by mere coincidence of flows (the passive bathtub with no thermostat), whereas homeostasis by definition supplies an active target-seeking loop. Homeostasis, when it works, is one way of *producing and defending* a dynamic equilibrium; dynamic equilibrium is the state, not the servo. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **vs. the concept note [[Dynamic Equilibrium]].** This note is the definitional entry (the verbatim term as Meadows fixes it); the concept note develops the idea's role across her stock-and-flow framework and its wider resonances. They share a base name and are disambiguated by the `(Meadows)` suffix and by folder, in the vault's established pattern (as with `Transparency` / `Transparency (Han)`).
- **vs. a static or thermodynamic equilibrium.** Meadows's equilibrium is explicitly *not* the cessation of flow or the running-down to a dead state; it is a maintained balance of live throughput. The tub in equilibrium is one through which "water is continuously flowing." `Directly stated`
