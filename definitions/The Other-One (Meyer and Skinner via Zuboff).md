# The Other-One — Shoshana Zuboff

## Author's Definition

"The Other-One" is the **viewpoint of observation** of radical behaviorism, which Zuboff recovers from the psychologist Max Meyer's 1921 textbook *Psychology of the Other-One* and traces forward through B. F. Skinner. It is the methodological decision to study the human being only as an external *other* — an observable, measurable organism — and to exclude inner experience ("soul," "self," "mind," "consciousness") from science altogether. The phrase that named the perspective was the "Other-One" (`Directly stated`):

> The phrase that captured the new scientific perspective was the “Other-One.” Human behavior would yield to scientific research only if psychologists learned to view humans as others. This “viewpoint of observation” was an absolute requirement for an “objective science of human behavior” that ceased to confuse inner experience with external action.

Central to the viewpoint is the reduction of the person to *organism*. As Zuboff paraphrases and quotes Meyer (`Directly stated`):

> Central to this new viewpoint was his notion of the human being as organism. The human being is recast as an “it,” an “other,” a “they” of organisms: an “organism among organisms,” distinguishable from a lettuce, a moose, or an inchworm only in degree of complexity. A scientific psychology would restrict its interests to the social and therefore visible behaviors of this “organism as an organism.” It would be “the study of the life of the Other-One—but of his life in so far as it is of social significance rather than as it is of significance for himself.… We are studying the Other-One in preference to Our-Selves.”

The inner life is not denied so much as exiled from inquiry — declared "your own business," an "irreducible mystery" of no scientific value because it cannot be observed and measured (`Directly stated`):

> We do not deny the soul, but we do not devote our time to it. We find enough, and more than enough, to do studying the body.

## Context

Zuboff introduces the Other-One in Chapter 12 ("Two Species of Power") to locate the **intellectual origin** of [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the new species of power born of surveillance capitalism. She argues that instrumentarianism's "specific 'viewpoint of observation' was forged in the controversial intellectual domain known as 'radical behaviorism' and its antecedents in turn-of-the-century theoretical physics" (`Directly stated`). The lineage runs Planck → Meyer → Skinner: Meyer studied under Max Planck in Berlin, carried Planck's conviction that the outside world is "something absolute" governed by discoverable laws into the study of human behavior, and Skinner later credited Meyer's obscure 1921 textbook with establishing "the epistemological and methodological foundations of modern behaviorism." Skinner's own formula — "consider only those facts which can be objectively observed in the behavior of one person in its relation to his prior environmental history" — is the Other-One operationalized (`Directly stated`).

The point of the genealogy is polemical. The Other-One supplies the *epistemology* that lets instrumentarian power treat the human as raw behavioral material with no remainder. Because inner life is bracketed out as scientifically void, freedom and will become mere artifacts of ignorance — "freedom of action in the animal world signifies the same that is meant by accidents in the world of physics," accidents being "simply phenomena for which there are insufficient information and understanding" (`Directly stated`). Knowledge and freedom are made adversaries: as measurement and computation grow, "freedom" shrinks. Meyer even cast this otherization as **political liberation** — a road past the historic domination of the human soul, toward "why the trend of history is in the direction of democracy," a "scientifically induced global harmony of organisms in which freedom is necessarily forfeit to knowledge" (`Directly stated`). Skinner pursued the same dream of technical control in his utopia *Walden Two*, whose community is "founded on the physicist's ideal of the Other-One."

Zuboff's claim is that surveillance capitalism finally builds the instruments the behaviorists lacked. The Other-One is no longer a laboratory posture but is "pervasively institutionaliz[ed]" through ubiquitous digital apparatus that observes, computes, and "automatically reinforce[s] behavior." The asymmetry is the sting: "This new species of power follows the logic of Planck, Meyer, and Skinner in the forfeit of freedom for knowledge... The knowledge that now displaces our freedom is proprietary. The knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us" (`Directly stated`).

## Differences from Other Uses

The Other-One names a specific *stance of observation*, not a metaphysics of the self — and that distinguishes it from neighboring vault notes (`Interpretive synthesis`):

- It is the methodological correlate of Zuboff's [[Radical Indifference]]: where radical indifference describes the *attitude* of surveillance capitalism toward what it observes (it does not care what we mean, only that our behavior is accessible to its operations), the Other-One names the older *behaviorist epistemology* that first authorized treating the human as a bare, meaning-evacuated organism. The Other-One is the historical seedbed; radical indifference is its market-age flowering.
- It contrasts sharply with [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] (Hayles) and the will-bearing self of [[The Will to Will (Arendt via Zuboff)]], both of which the Other-One is designed to dissolve. Where the liberal subject is an autonomous interior agent, the Other-One is an exterior "it," "an organism among organisms."
- It converges with — but is not identical to — Harari's [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] and his [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]]. Harari arrives at the dissolution of the inner self through twenty-first-century life science and Dataism; Meyer and Skinner arrive there through early-twentieth-century behaviorism and Planckian physics. Both end at the forfeit of freedom to knowledge, but the Other-One is the *behaviorist viewpoint of observation* specifically — a decision about what science may look at, not (in the first instance) a claim about the brain's algorithmic substrate.
- Distinguish from Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and Foucault's biopolitics: those still address an *interior* (the psyche to be governed, the soul to be made docile). The Other-One's defining move is to declare the interior off-limits and study only "his life in so far as it is of social significance rather than as it is of significance for himself" — observable behavior, never lived experience.
