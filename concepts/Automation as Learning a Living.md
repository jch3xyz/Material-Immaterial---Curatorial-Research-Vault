# Automation as Learning a Living

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's thesis that because "automation is information," electric speed dissolves the dichotomies of work/leisure, production/consumption, and labor/learning into a single process — so that "paid learning" becomes the dominant employment and source of new wealth, and the worker is recast as a self-employed, artist-like "nomadic gatherer of knowledge."

## Longer Explanation

This is the one durably new idea of *Understanding Media*'s final chapter, the payoff that goes beyond the Part I toolkit ([[Media as Extensions of Man]], [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]]). McLuhan's pivot is the identification of automation with information rather than with mechanization: "Automation is information and it not only ends jobs in the world of work, it ends subjects in the world of learning. It does not end the world of learning." Because the same instantaneous, all-at-once character of electricity governs both industry and pedagogy, the historical separation between *doing* and *knowing* — fixed in place by writing and print, which "were supremely specialist in separating the roles of knowledge from the roles of action" — collapses. The future of work, he writes, "consists of earning a living in the automation age," and that living is increasingly *learning* a living (`Directly stated`).

The mechanism is the separation of energy from operation. In an automatic machine the "generation and transmission of power is quite separate from the work operation that uses the power," exactly as in any [[Feedback Loops|servo-mechanist structure]] involving feedback. McLuhan reads this through his recurring light analogy: "Just as light is at once energy and information, so electric automation unites production, consumption, and learning in an inextricable process." Energy and production "fuse with information and learning"; "marketing and consumption tend to become one with learning, enlightenment, and the intake of information." This is why the dichotomies fall: automation "ends the old dichotomies between culture and technology, between art and commerce, and between work and leisure" (`Directly stated`).

The consequences McLuhan draws are economic and anthropological. Economically, "paid learning is already becoming both the dominant employment and the source of new wealth in our society," teachers having become the largest employee group; the "older mechanistic idea of 'jobs,' or fragmented tasks and specialist slots for 'workers,' becomes meaningless under automation." The audience-position is abolished: "the consumer becomes producer in the automation circuit, quite as much as the reader of the mosaic telegraph press makes his own news, or just is his own news" — a prosumer claim made decades early (`Directly stated`). Anthropologically, automation completes a return: the sedentary, specialized worker of the print age reverts to the mobile, generalist condition that preceded it. "Men are suddenly nomadic gatherers of knowledge, nomadic as never before, informed as never before, free from fragmentary specialism as never before." This is the [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] reversal in human form — the worker freed from the [[Mechanical vs Electric|mechanical]] assembly line and projected, like the horse displaced by the motorcar, "onto the plane of entertainment." The "social and educational patterns latent in automation are those of self-employment and artistic autonomy" (`Directly stated`).

McLuhan frames this liberation ambivalently rather than utopically. The release "taxes our inner resources of self-employment and imaginative participation in society," a "fate that calls men to the role of artist in society"; he also speaks of "the specter of joblessness and propertylessness in the electric age" and of senior executives "among the hardest pressed and most persistently harassed groups in human history." But the redemptive reading dominates: leisure inverts into intense involvement ("the reverse is true in the electric age"), and "the very toil of man now becomes a kind of enlightenment," figured against the unfallen Adam appointed "the task of the contemplation and naming of creatures" (`Strongly implied` for the ambivalence; the dominant valence is `Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — the originating thesis. Automation, identified with information and feedback ([[Cybernetics]]), fuses production, consumption, and learning; paid learning becomes the new employment and wealth; the worker becomes a self-employed, artist-like "nomadic gatherer of knowledge." The valence is broadly affirmative and continuous with [[Decentralization]] and [[The Global Village]] — automation "creates patterns of decentralism and diversity in the work to be done."
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** *(opposing variant)* — Harari inherits the premise that algorithms displace human labor but inverts the outcome: where McLuhan turns the displaced worker into a learner-artist, Harari forecasts [[The Useless Class]] — people rendered economically superfluous with no new role to absorb them. See the disagreement below.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** *(opposing variant)* — Han accepts the collapse of the work/leisure boundary but reads "free" self-directed productivity as [[Auto-Exploitation]], not artistic autonomy: the subject who is "most at leisure when most intensely involved" is, for Han, the self-exploiting achievement-subject. See the disagreement below.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Useless Class]] — Harari's forecast that automation produces not artist-learners but the economically superfluous; the optimistic and pessimistic readings of the same displacement.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — Han's diagnosis that the collapse of work/leisure into "intense involvement" is self-exploitation, not liberation or artistic autonomy.

## Questions It Raises

- Does the identification "automation is information" actually entail that displaced workers become learners, or only that the *content* of the new economy is informational — leaving open whether there is any work (paid or otherwise) for the displaced?
- McLuhan and Harari share the premise but split on the conclusion. What does the optimistic reading assume about access, retraining, and the distribution of the "new wealth" that Harari's [[The Useless Class]] denies?
- If "we are most at leisure when we are most intensely involved," is the resulting subject McLuhan's liberated artist or Han's self-exploiting achievement-subject ([[Auto-Exploitation]])? Can the same phenomenology bear both readings?
- Is "paid learning" a genuine new employment category or a euphemism for the perpetual retraining that "harasses" the senior executive — i.e., a burden disguised as enlightenment?
