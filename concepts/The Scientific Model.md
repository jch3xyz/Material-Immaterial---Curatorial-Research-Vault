# The Scientific Model

## Brief Definition

The scientist's model — the formal (mathematical) or substantive (physical-analogy and spatial-theory) construct, and later the computer simulation, by which science grasps an invisible system — which the modern sculptor seized as an "exotic reservoir" of pure form representing "the creation of form purely through mental activity with no reference to visible reality."

## Longer Explanation

In the "The Scientific Model" section of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*'s third chapter, Jack Burnham distinguishes the model's scientific function from its sculptural appropriation. He first defines the model in its own right: "The model serves as an analogy of a proposed system or set of relationships, though not necessarily as a physical object." He then divides it: "Models can be divided into two categories, the formal and the substantive. Theformal model consists of mathematical relationships, those instances when a set of equations for a theory may have terms in common with the verified equations of another physical system. The category of model which has caught the imagination of the sculptor is the substantive, but only one type of that model." The type that seized sculptors is not the physical-analogy model (Fourier's heat conduction, the kinetic theory of gases) but "the construction by the mathematician or physicist of a model describing a theory in space" (`Directly stated`).

For Burnham the model is one of the great exotic stores of form available to an art that had exhausted figuration and was learning to invent by discovery: "The sculptor learned to invent by discovering the world about him, and, aside from visible nature, the theoretical constructs of science constituted one of the most exotic reservoirs of forms. In many instances these constructs resembled nothing existent; thus their attraction was obvious." Its appeal was precisely that it owed nothing to the eye: "The sculptor, on the other hand, was fascinated by the model because it seemed to represent the creation of form purely through mental activity with no reference to visible reality." The ball-and-stick atom, the Rutherford-Bohr "open" solar model, the stringed plaster figures of the German mathematics departments — these became, in Burnham's account, the prototype for open, dematerialized Constructivist sculpture (`Directly stated`).

The conceptual force of the note is a historical irony Burnham makes explicit. Science generates visual models only at a particular, transitional stage, then abandons them: "Visual models are constructed only when a science has reached a stage where they are possible and visual conceptualization is helpful; then, as the science develops, visual models are forsaken in favor of mathematical analysis." Sculptors latched onto the atomic and mathematical model just as physics was outgrowing it — Bertrand Russell already doubted "the ultimate reality of the solar model" in 1926, and quantum theory and Heisenberg's uncertainty principle soon confirmed that "wave and probability characteristics cannot be adduced from ball and stick models." So: "It is ironic that some sculptors of the 1920's felt influenced by scientific models precisely at the time when models were beginning to lose their importance for the mathematician and physicist" (`Directly stated`).

That irony is the engine of [[Dematerialization]]. Because the sculptor was visibly representing relationships that were themselves becoming invisible — and because the referent model was itself obsolescing toward "field models (extremely difficult to represent spatially) and pure mathematics" — sculpture thinned out as its source dematerialized: "With time, it became increasingly clear to scul ptors that their works attempted visible representation of the invisible relationships of physical reality. This accounts in part for the steady dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's. The model has been simply one way for the sculptor to cling to a tenuous reality." Burnham closes the section by tracing the model's own evolution past static object-models to biomolecular mock-ups and then to "computer-generated 'simulation' models of systems," predicting that "In time we may expect artists to devise their own computer simulations with visual effects through computer display panels" (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — the scientific model is the formal/substantive scientific construct (later the computer simulation) that the sculptor adopted as an "exotic reservoir" of pure form "with no reference to visible reality"; its obsolescence, tracking science's flight from visual conceptualization into mathematics, drove the 1910–1950s dematerialization of sculpture.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Scientific Model as Icon]] — the symbol-form of the same object: where this concept is the working scientific construct the sculptor plundered, the symbol is the model elevated into "the contemporary icon par excellence," the truth-image a scientific culture orients belief toward.
- [[Scientific Idealism]] — Burnham's name for the impulse to view essential reality through provisional scientific theories and models; the scientific model is the concrete construct that impulse converts into "ideational verities."
- [[Dematerialization]] — the model concept supplies the causal mechanism: as the referent model dematerialized into mathematics and field theory, so did the sculpture that tried to picture it.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the model dramatizes the tension at the heart of the vault: the sculptor "cannot rid themselves of the presence of objects," yet feeds on a model that "resembled nothing existent" and refers to no "visible reality," so material form is bound to an immaterial, vanishing referent.

## Questions It Raises

- If sculptors adopted the visual model exactly when science was abandoning it, is the "exotic reservoir" of scientific form always already obsolete — an art built on cast-off epistemology?
- Burnham predicts artists devising their own computer simulations "with visual effects through computer display panels": does the migration from object-model to simulation continue the dematerialization, or does the screen re-materialize the model as a new kind of object?
- If "the model itself is occasionally mistaken for the theory" in science, what is the corresponding category error in sculpture — taking the picture of reality for reality?
