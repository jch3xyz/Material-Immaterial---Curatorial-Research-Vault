# The Rematerialization of Art

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's forecast that the electronic/virtual trajectory of art will converge with a biological/molecular trajectory (nanotechnology, artificial life) — virtualisation followed by a re-materialisation, "a marriage of the immaterial and the material" — making him the first vault author to argue for *reconciling*, not merely dematerializing, material and immaterial.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, the dematerialization of the art object that ran through the twentieth century (Constructivism, Conceptual Art, the systems esthetics of [[Jack Burnham|Burnham]], the [[Dematerialization|dematerialization]] of [[Lucy Lippard|Lippard]]) is not the endpoint but the first half of a longer arc. The immateriality of the information society — virtualisation, telematic communication, computerised perception — is, on his account, a phase that *logically produces its own reversal* into a new materiality. "The logical outcome of the immateriality of the information society is the reinstatement in the twenty-first century of a natural materiality" (`Directly stated`). This is not a return to the old organic nature but the emergence of a constructed, second-order [[Nature II]] whose "engines of creation" embrace [[Artificial Life]].

The rematerialization comes about, Ascott argues, "as a convergence of the two apparently divergent trajectories of contemporary technology": one electronic (virtualisation, telematic communication, computerised intelligence), the other biological (nanotechnology, molecular engineering, the organisation of matter and emergent behaviour) (`Directly stated`). Their meeting is "a marriage of the immaterial and the material leading to a transcendence over the gross materiality of 'natural,' unmediated life." The product is "an interactive art of intelligent systems set within intelligent environments" — a holistic environment "of mind and matter, as much spiritual as material." This convergence is the direct conceptual precursor to his coinage [[Moistmedia]]: the fusion of the dry computational and the wet biological into a single substrate for art.

This is Ascott's MAJOR cross-book contribution to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis. Where the prior eight authors on that tension trace a dematerialization vector — object → system → information, body → pattern, presence → signal — Ascott is the first to argue that the immaterial does not *replace* the material but *re-binds* with it. He inverts the direction of the dominant story without denying it: virtualisation is real and necessary, but it is a passage, not a destination (`Strongly implied`). The same reversal appears in his account of the museum: "As the museum dematerialises, we might then see new metaphors of what it is to be human materialize all around us" — dematerialization of the institution becomes the condition for a re-materialization of culture into the lived environment (`Directly stated`).

The rematerialization thesis underwrites Ascott's parallel claim about the body — that "[[Networking re-materialises the body it does not dematerialise it|networking re-materialises the body, it does not dematerialise it]]" — and it is the conceptual hinge that turns his telematic/[[Cyberception|cyberceptive]] art away from pure disembodiment toward an [[Art as Experiential Technology|experiential]], embodied, environmental [[New Media Art|new-media practice]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the rematerialization of art is the forecast convergence of the electronic-virtual and the biological-molecular trajectories: virtualisation followed by materialisation, "a marriage of the immaterial and the material." It yields [[Nature II]] and [[Moistmedia]]. Ascott is the first vault author to argue for *reconciling* material and immaterial rather than dematerializing.
- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** (precursor, by contrast) — Lippard's [[Dematerialization|dematerialization]] of the art object is the vector Ascott extends and reverses: he accepts dematerialization as a real historical phase but denies it is terminal.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (precursor, by contrast) — Burnham's object → system spine and the "scribble in the air" reading of Constructivist dematerialization are the systems-esthetics ground Ascott builds on; Ascott adds the *return* swing (system → re-materialized living system via artificial life).

## Related Concepts

- [[Cyberception]] — the technologically amplified perception by which the rematerialized, intelligent environment is engaged.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — rematerialization is the deliberate counter-vector: Ascott accepts dematerialization as a phase but argues the immaterial re-binds with the material rather than dissolving it. He is the first vault author to oppose the dematerialization vector with a reconciling one.

## Questions It Raises

- Is rematerialization a genuine reversal of dematerialization, or a higher synthesis that preserves both (an "immaterial materiality")?
- If the immaterial "logically" produces a return to materiality, is the dematerialization tradition (Lippard, Burnham, the Conceptualists) a misreading of a phase as an endpoint — or did Ascott need that tradition to complete it?
- Does the "marriage of the immaterial and the material" actually transcend "gross materiality," or does it re-import the body and matter that the information-society dream tried to leave behind?
- How does Ascott's reconciling move reposition the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension — does it resolve the axis, or open a new pole (reconciliation) beside dematerialization?
