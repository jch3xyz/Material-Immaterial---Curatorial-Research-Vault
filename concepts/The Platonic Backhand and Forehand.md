# The Platonic Backhand and Forehand

## Brief Definition

Hayles's paired coinage for two rhetorical moves — one ancient, one computational — that reify a contingent abstraction into "the Real," so that embodied, material reality comes to appear as a derivative, messy instantiation of clean logical form rather than the other way around.

## Longer Explanation

In *How We Became Posthuman*, Hayles names the two rhetorical operations she holds responsible for "constructing the information/materiality hierarchy," adding "Irreverently, I think of them as the Platonic backhand and forehand." The figure is a tennis metaphor: two strokes that "make their play in opposite directions" yet "share a common ideology." Crucially, the pair is not the form/matter dichotomy itself but the rhetorical *enforcement* of it — the move by which a separation that is merely assumed gets invested with coercive, lawlike authority. This distinguishes the concept from [[Material vs Immaterial]], which names the hierarchy as a standing assumption; the backhand and forehand are how that hierarchy is rhetorically produced and naturalized.

The **backhand** is the older move, "dating back to the Greeks." It "works by inferring from the world's noisy multiplicity a simplified abstraction." Hayles concedes this is legitimate: "this is what theorizing should do." The illegitimate twist comes when "the move circles around to constitute the abstraction as the originary form from which the world's multiplicity derives." At that point the inference is forgotten and reversed: "complexity appears as a 'fuzzing up' of an essential reality rather than as a manifestation of the world's holistic nature." The forgetting is the whole trick — the abstraction was drawn *out of* embodied experience, but is then redescribed as the source *from which* embodied experience falls away. The **forehand** is the recent, computer-dependent counterpart. It "starts from simplified abstractions and, using simulation techniques such as genetic algorithms, evolves a multiplicity sufficiently complex that it can be seen as a world of its own" — generating the world *from* the abstraction rather than inferring the abstraction *from* the world. Together "they lay the groundwork for a new variation on an ancient game, in which disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form," and Hayles draws out the stakes in a single barbed question: "If we can capture the Form of ones and zeros in a nonbiological medium—say, on a computer disk—why do we need the body's superfluous flesh?"

The Macy Conferences chapter supplies Hayles's case study of the backhand in action. The McCulloch-Pitts neuron was a deliberately "stripped-down neural model," yet researchers used it metaphorically to bridge to "the complexities of embodied experience," and "in the process, the disembodied logical form of the circuit was rhetorically transformed from being an effect of the model to a cause of the model's efficacy. This move, familiar to us as the Platonic backhand, made embodied reality into a blurred and messy instantiation of the clean abstractions of logical forms." Hayles tracks the move down to its rhetorical mechanics in McCulloch's correspondence: although universality "is achieved by bracketing or 'black-boxing' the specific mechanisms," McCulloch "presents the theory as though it preexisted specific mechanisms and then was later imperfectly instantiated in them." This reversal is precisely what gives the abstraction its grip: "This backhanded swing invests the theory with a coercive power that cannot be ignored, for it expresses 'a law so general' that 'every circuit built by God or man must exemplify it in some form.'" Hayles's counter is that the law is "proved only in relation to the simplified model" and can be coercive for embodied actuality "only if the model is as complex and noisy as reality itself" — which would defeat the purpose of modeling, as the 1:1 map of Carroll and Borges shows.

The wider purpose of the concept is to make the leap to "bodiless information" *visible* and therefore contestable. Hayles's method is "to complicate the leap from embodied reality to abstract information by pointing to moments when the assumptions involved in this move were contested," because she wants "to make clear how much had to be erased to arrive at such abstractions." She is explicit that abstraction as such is indispensable — "no theory can account for the infinite multiplicity of our interactions with the real" — so the target is not abstraction but the forgetting and inversion that turns a contingent abstraction into the Real. Naming the moves as *rhetorical* strokes, rather than as discoveries, denaturalizes the hierarchy that lets [[The Body as Data]] and the broader project of [[Posthumanism]] treat flesh as superfluous to [[Information (Hayles)|information]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — coins the paired term to diagnose how the information/materiality hierarchy is rhetorically enforced; the backhand infers an idealized order from noisy experience and then forgets the inference, the forehand generates a world from the abstraction, and both "privileg[e] the abstract as the Real."
- **Andrea Nye** (as cited by Hayles) — Hayles links McCulloch's stroke to Nye's feminist history of logic, which "traces similar Platonic backhands that were made to develop a logic coercive in its lawlike power," made "in specific political and historical contexts" whose social implications "are masked by being presenting as preexisting laws of nature."
- **Warren McCulloch** (as analyzed by Hayles) — the worked example of the backhand: he treats the McCulloch-Pitts model as a "law so general" that every circuit "must exemplify it," yet, unlike the logicians Nye critiques, he "never relinquished his commitment to embodiment," which is why Hayles reads his work as a site where the move becomes visible rather than seamless.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the term the two strokes erase; Hayles wields embodiment ("a manifestation of the world's holistic nature," not a "fuzzing up" of an essential reality) precisely to reverse the backhand and recover what the inversion forgot. The pairing names the live stakes of [[Embodiment vs Information]].

## Questions It Raises

- If abstraction is indispensable to all theorizing, where exactly is the line between a legitimate inference from noisy experience and the backhand's illegitimate "circling around" that makes the abstraction originary — and who is authorized to draw it?
- How does forgetting the embodied origin of an abstraction convert a contingent model into a "coercive," lawlike Real, and what work does that forgetting do for projects that want to treat [[The Body as Data|the body as data]]?
- Does the forehand — generating worlds from abstractions in simulation — represent a genuinely new move, or merely the backhand's reversal given computational means; and does artificial life thereby *prove* the abstraction or only stage it?
- What politics ride on presenting these strokes "as preexisting laws of nature" rather than as situated rhetorical choices, as Nye's feminist history of logic suggests?
- Can the information/materiality hierarchy be contested without rejecting information theory itself — that is, can one keep the abstraction while refusing to let it become the Real that demotes [[Embodiment|embodiment]]?
