# Success to the Successful

## Brief Definition

A system archetype in which the winners of a competition receive, as part of the reward, the means to compete even more effectively in the future — a reinforcing feedback loop that rapidly divides a system into permanent winners and permanent losers.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Donella Meadows]], "success to the successful" is one of the recurring **system traps** — a structural configuration that produces problematic behavior regardless of the intentions of the actors caught inside it. Its engine is a [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]]: *"This system trap is found whenever the winners of a competition receive, as part of the reward, the means to compete even more effectively in the future. That's a reinforcing feedback loop, which rapidly divides a system into winners who go on winning, and losers who go on losing."* The winner's accumulated wealth, privilege, access, or information becomes the input that generates still more wealth, privilege, access, and information — the rich-get-richer dynamic Meadows glosses biblically as *"To him that hath shall be given."* (Directly stated.)

Meadows grounds the archetype in its native discipline, ecology, where it appears as the **competitive exclusion principle**: *"Success to the successful is a well-known concept in the field of ecology, where it is called 'the competitive exclusion principle.'"* Two species cannot occupy exactly the same niche competing for exactly the same resources; the marginally more efficient reproducer wins a larger share, which lets it multiply and keep winning, until it drives the losing competitor to extinction — *"not by direct confrontation usually, but by appropriating all the resource, leaving none for the weaker competitor."* The same loop runs in markets: two firms competing in a niche behave like two species, and *"Its reinforcing feedback loop of capital accumulation will be able to turn faster than that of the other firm."* (Directly stated.)

The trap is therefore, for Meadows, a structural vindication of Marx — she reads the fall of the Soviet Union as no refutation of the analysis that matters here: *"this particular analysis of his — that market competition systematically eliminates market competition — is demonstrated wherever there is, or used to be, a competitive market."* Competition, left uninhibited, consumes itself: many U.S. automobile companies were reduced to three (not one, only because of antitrust law); most major cities are left with a single newspaper; farms decline in number as they grow in size. Its "greatest damage" lies in the many mutually reinforcing ways it *"works to make the rich richer and the poor poorer"* — worse schools, no access to credit, tenancy rather than ownership — a mesh of feedbacks perpetuating inequitable distribution of income, assets, education, and opportunity. (Directly stated / Strongly implied for the aggregation of examples.)

Because the trap is structural, Meadows insists the exits are structural too. She names four leverage points against it. **Diversification** — a species evolving to exploit a new resource, a company creating a non-competing product — lets a loser "get out of that game and start another one," though it *"doesn't work as a strategy for the poor"* and can be crushed or bought up by the monopolist. **Antitrust-style limits** install balancing loops that keep any competitor from taking over entirely (with the caveat that a large enough winner can win the power to weaken antitrust enforcement itself). **Leveling the playing field** periodically resets advantage — Monopoly starts over with everyone equal, sports grant handicaps, tax and inheritance law and universal education redistribute. And **potlatch-style redistribution**: *"Gift-giving cultures redistribute wealth through potlatches and other ceremonies that increase the social standing of the gift giver."* The final motive Meadows offers is prudential as much as moral — losers with no hope of winning *"could get frustrated enough to destroy the playing field."* (Directly stated.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*) — coins the archetype's name for the systems-thinking canon; identifies its structure as a reinforcing loop, its ecological form as the competitive exclusion principle, its economic form as Marx's self-eliminating competition, and its exits as diversification, antitrust, leveling the playing field, and potlatch redistribution.

## Related Concepts

- [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] — the structural engine of the archetype; success-to-the-successful is a named instance of a self-amplifying loop.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the exits (antitrust, leveling) work by installing balancing loops to check the reinforcing one.
- [[The Limits-to-Growth Archetype]] — a companion system trap from the same chapter; both are reinforcing-loop pathologies Meadows diagnoses structurally.
- [[Shifting Dominance]] — the mechanism by which one loop (here the winner's accumulation loop) comes to govern system behavior.
- [[Dictatorship of Capital]] — Han's diagnosis of capital's self-augmenting accumulation as a form of domination parallels the market form of this trap.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — the corrective structure; the archetype's exits all amount to introducing a balancing loop to hold the reinforcing one in check.

## Questions It Raises

- If competition systematically eliminates competition, is a stable competitive market a contradiction that can only be sustained by continuous external intervention?
- Meadows notes that a large enough winner can win the very power to weaken antitrust enforcement — does this make the "way out" of the trap itself subject to the trap?
- When the competing "resource" is attention, data, or information rather than capital or an ecological niche, does the archetype describe the concentration dynamics of digital platforms — and are its classical exits (diversification, antitrust, potlatch) still available?
