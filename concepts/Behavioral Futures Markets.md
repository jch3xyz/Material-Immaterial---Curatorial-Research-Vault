# Behavioral Futures Markets

## Brief Definition

Shoshana Zuboff's name for the new kind of marketplace that trades exclusively in predictions of future human behavior — where surveillance capitalism's actual *customers* (the firms eager to bet on what we will do "now, soon, and later") buy the prediction products manufactured from our experience, while we ourselves are the raw-material source rather than the customer.

## Longer Explanation

Behavioral futures markets sit at the end of surveillance capitalism's supply chain. In Zuboff's account the chain runs: human experience is unilaterally claimed as free raw material and translated into behavioral data; the data exceeding what is needed for service improvement is declared a proprietary *behavioral surplus*; this surplus is fed into "machine intelligence" and fabricated into *prediction products* that anticipate what a person will do; and finally those prediction products "are traded in a new kind of marketplace for behavioral predictions that I call behavioral futures markets." This is where the money is made — Zuboff states that "surveillance capitalism's profits derive primarily from these behavioral futures markets" (`Directly stated`).

The decisive structural point is the inversion of who counts as the customer. The buyers in these markets are not the people whose futures are being told and sold; they are "many companies … eager to lay bets on our future behavior." Advertisers were the dominant early players, but Zuboff insists the markets are not essentially about ads — "any actor with an interest in purchasing probabilistic information about our behavior and/or influencing future behavior can pay to play in markets where the behavioral fortunes of individuals, groups, bodies, and things are told and sold." The same foundational mechanisms migrate from online advertising into insurance, retail, and finance. This is what grounds the wider argument that in surveillance capitalism the population is the source of raw material, not the clientele (`Directly stated`).

The book traces how these markets push out of the screen and into physical space. "Shopping from the steering wheel" means "hosting behavioral futures markets in your front seat"; the dashboard, the smart-home device, and "behavioral underwriting" extend the marketplace into the offline world, so that, as Zuboff puts it, "we now pay for our own domination" (`Directly stated`). The clearest case study is Niantic's *Pokémon Go*: its "sponsored locations" are "a euphemism for Niantic's behavioral futures markets," and the game's "actual customers" are "the entities who pay to play on the real-world game board, lured by the promise of guaranteed outcomes." Here the market does not merely *predict* behavior but is fed by *economies of action* that herd players toward real-world commercial checkpoints — the point at which prediction tips over into modification (`Strongly implied` for the prediction→modification escalation; `Directly stated` for the Niantic mechanics).

This note is a **forward-anchor**: the term is named at the outset (Chapter 1, the book's definition) and developed cumulatively through later chapters (rendition from the depths, Make Them Dance) where its mechanics, customers, and expansion into the offline world are filled in.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins the term to name the marketplace in prediction products. It is the profit center of [[Surveillance Capitalism]], the destination of [[Behavioral Surplus]], and the venue where [[Prediction Products]] are sold to firms that bet on our behavior. Its existence is what makes the *population* the raw-material source and the *enterprises* the customers.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic logic whose profits "derive primarily from these behavioral futures markets"; this marketplace is its institutional core.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the proprietary raw material extracted from human experience that is refined into the goods sold in these markets.
- [[Prediction Products]] — the actual commodity traded; behavioral futures markets are defined as the market *for* prediction products.
- [[Big Data]] — the data accumulation that feeds the "machine intelligence" fabricating the prediction products. (Zuboff anchors the surveillance critique that Han and Harari approach through `Big Data` / `Dataism`.)
- [[Dataism]] — Harari's and Han's name for the ideology that valorizes total data flow; behavioral futures markets are the concrete commercial form such flows assume.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's diagnosis foregrounds *visibility and self-exposure* as the mechanism of control; Zuboff's behavioral futures markets locate the engine of domination not in being seen but in the *trade in our predicted futures* by parties unknown to us. The two are complementary diagnoses of the same regime that nonetheless disagree about where power actually accrues — for Zuboff, "they predict our futures for the sake of others' gain, not ours."

## Questions It Raises

- If the population is the raw-material source and enterprises are the customers, what political or legal category names the people whose futures are "told and sold" without their consent?
- Once behavioral futures markets reward not only accurate prediction but guaranteed outcomes (economies of action), where is the line between predicting behavior and manufacturing it?
- Zuboff says the markets are "only incidentally about ads." If insurance, finance, and the connected car are equally available "domains," what limits, if any, are intrinsic to the marketplace itself?
