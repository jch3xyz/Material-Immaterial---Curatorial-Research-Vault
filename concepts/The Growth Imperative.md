# The Growth Imperative

## Brief Definition

Harari's name for modernity's "fundamental dogma" that economic growth is "not only possible but is absolutely essential" — a near-religious obligation that overrides ethics, demands that we "fear equilibrium as if it were the Devil," and turns avarice from a vice into "a force for good."

## Longer Explanation

The Growth Imperative is the economic engine of what Harari calls the Modern Covenant (the deal in which "humans agree to give up meaning in exchange for power"). Where premodern cultures "see the world as a static pie" — a fixed quantity in which "if somebody gets a bigger slice of the pie, somebody else inevitably gets a smaller slice" — modernity is "based on the firm belief that economic growth is not only possible but is absolutely essential." The dogma reduces to a single operational rule: "‘If you have a problem, you probably need more stuff, and in order to have more stuff, you must produce more of it.’" Famine, plague and war, on this view, "can only be solved through growth," not through redistribution or prayer. *(Directly stated)*

What raises growth from an economic policy to an imperative is its acquisition of "almost religious status." Harari argues that "it may not be wrong to call the belief in economic growth a religion, because it now purports to solve many if not most of our ethical dilemmas." Because growth is "allegedly the source of all good things," it "encourages people to bury their ethical disagreements and adopt whichever course of action maximises long-term growth"; it becomes "the crucial juncture where almost all modern religions, ideologies and movements meet," uniting Soviet planners and American robber barons, Hindu revivalists and Chinese communists in a shared faith that "wrangled only about the exact method." From "its belief in the supreme value of growth, capitalism deduces its number one commandment: thou shalt invest thy profits in increasing growth." This places the Growth Imperative alongside Han's [[Capital as Religion]] — for both, capital is not merely an economic order but a transcendent cult before which the subject is reconstituted. *(Directly stated; the cross-author link is an Interpretive synthesis)*

The imperative is also a moral inversion. "For millennia, societies strove to curb individual desires and bring them into some kind of balance"; when "the pie was of a fixed size, social harmony depended on restraint. Avarice was bad." Modernity "turned the world upside down": it "convinced human collectives that equilibrium is far more frightening than chaos, and because avarice fuels growth, it is a force for good," and so "dismantled the age-old disciplines that curbed greed." Equilibrium — the natural resting state of "most natural systems" — becomes the enemy. To keep individuals and institutions from retiring from the race, "modernity upholds growth as a supreme value for whose sake we should make every sacrifice and risk every danger," and organisations are taught "to measure their success in terms of growth, and to fear equilibrium as if it were the Devil." Even confronted with ecological collapse, "the creed of growth firmly objects" to deceleration: "Instead, it suggests we should run even faster." *(Directly stated)*

Crucially, the Growth Imperative is the hidden engine driving the three projects of [[The New Human Agenda]]. Harari foreshadows this in the opening chapter: "the modern economy needs constant and indefinite growth in order to survive. If growth ever stops, the economy won't settle down to some cosy equilibrium; it will fall to pieces. That's why capitalism encourages us to seek immortality, happiness and divinity." Because "there's a limit to how many shoes we can wear," consumer demand alone cannot sustain perpetual growth; "an economy built on everlasting growth needs endless projects — just like the quests for immortality, bliss and divinity." The quests that produce [[Homo Deus]] are thus not only the logical conclusion of humanism but an economic necessity: the upgrade is what growth eats when ordinary consumption is exhausted. *(Directly stated)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — frames the Growth Imperative as modernity's "fundamental dogma," elevated to "almost religious status." It is the economic half of the Modern Covenant: in exchange for the promise of indefinite growth, humans surrender the premodern belief in a fixed pie and a cosmic equilibrium. Harari grounds its possibility in the "alliance between scientific progress and economic growth" and in the discovery that knowledge — unlike raw materials and energy — "is a growing resource," but the imperative itself is moral and religious, not merely technical: it makes "‘more stuff’ . . . a panacea," sanctifies avarice, and ultimately fuels the quests for immortality, happiness and divinity.
- **[[Karl Marx]]** — the formula "thou shalt invest thy profits in increasing growth" restates capital's compulsion to accumulate (M–C–M′) as a religious commandment; Harari's account converges with the Marxian diagnosis of capital as self-expanding value, though Harari treats it as a faith rather than a contradiction. *(Interpretive synthesis — forward-stub)*
- **[[Walter Benjamin]]** — via Han, the reading of capitalism as a cult ("Capitalism as Religion," 1921) parallels Harari's claim that growth has "almost religious status"; both treat the economy as a structure of belief, not only of production. *(Interpretive synthesis — forward-stub)*

## Related Concepts

- [[The New Human Agenda]] — the Growth Imperative supplies the economic motive for the agenda's triad: an economy that "needs constant and indefinite growth" requires "endless projects," which is why "capitalism encourages us to seek immortality, happiness and divinity."
- [[Homo Deus]] — the upgraded human is, among other things, the next frontier of growth once ordinary consumer demand is saturated; the [[Three Paths to the Upgrade]] are open-ended projects of the kind everlasting growth requires.
- [[Capital as Religion]] — Han's parallel (via Benjamin): capital as a new transcendence and cult. Harari's "almost religious status" of growth and "number one commandment" describe the same elevation of the economy into a faith.
- [[Acceleration]] — the experiential face of the imperative: because the imperative forbids equilibrium and tells us to "run even faster," the rat race "just keeps accelerating" and "the margins for error keep narrowing."
- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — growth is "upheld as a supreme value"; the sanctification of human life, happiness and power that humanism performs is underwritten economically by the imperative to grow.
- [[The Anthropocene]] — the imperative's "real nemesis . . . is ecological collapse," yet "when the moment comes to choose between economic growth and ecological stability, politicians, CEOs and voters almost always prefer growth."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the imperative dissolves the figure of a self-restrained, balanced subject: where premodern societies "strove to curb individual desires and bring them into some kind of balance," modernity "dismantled the age-old disciplines that curbed greed," reconstituting the subject as one who must "constantly increase [their] income and standard of living."
- *Equilibrium* — Harari's explicit antonym: the static, zero-sum "pie of a fixed size" and the "cosy equilibrium" of "most natural systems," which the imperative teaches us "to fear . . . as if it were the Devil." *(rendered as plain text — no canonical note)*

## Questions It Raises

- If growth has acquired "almost religious status" and "now purports to solve . . . our ethical dilemmas," is there any standpoint left from which to weigh growth against the goods (ecological stability, family bonds, social equality) it asks us to discount?
- Harari grounds perpetual growth in knowledge as "a growing resource." Does that make the imperative genuinely open-ended, or does it merely relocate the limit from raw materials and energy to "the brittle biosphere" in which all growth occurs?
- If "an economy built on everlasting growth needs endless projects," does the pursuit of immortality, happiness and divinity follow from a *value* (the worth of human life) or from a *structural* demand of capital — and can the two be separated?
- The imperative "convinced human collectives that equilibrium is far more frightening than chaos." What would it take, individually or collectively, to "retire from the race" — and is such retirement even conceivable from inside the covenant?
