# Media as Translators

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's claim, and the title of Chapter 6 of *Understanding Media*, that all media are active metaphors whose defining work is to translate experience into new forms — so that "mechanization" is the translation of nature, and of our own natures, into amplified and specialized forms.

## Longer Explanation

For Marshall McLuhan a medium does not merely carry experience; it *translates* it, "spelling-out" one mode of knowing in another. He grounds the chapter in the etymology of "applied knowledge": "'Applied' means translated or carried across from one kind of material form into another." On this reading the long Western project of turning nature into art is a single sustained act of translation, and mechanization is its industrial case — "What we call 'mechanization ' is a translation of nature, and of our own natures, into amplified and specialized forms." Translation is therefore not one function media happen to have; it is the operation that makes something a medium at all. `Directly stated`

The distinctive move of the chapter is to fuse the extension thesis (`Media as Extensions of Man`) with the mechanics of metaphor. "All media are active metaphors in their power to translate experience into new forms," and "just as a metaphor transforms and transmits experience, so do the media." A metaphor, for McLuhan, is not a decorative likeness but a structural device of proportion: it sets up "complex ratios among four parts" so that we apprehend one set of relations through another. ("A man's reach / must exceed his grasp or what's a metaphor.") Money is his exemplary case of this stored proportion: "It is in this way that by seeing one set of relations through another set that we store and amplify experience in such forms as money. For money is also a metaphor." A medium translates by holding experience in a ratio that lets it be carried across, stored, and amplified. `Directly stated`

Translation also happens *between* the senses and *between* media, because the extensions repeat in technology what the body already does in perception. In Chapter 11 (Number) McLuhan generalizes: "Since all media are extensions of our own bodies and senses, and since we habitually translate one sense into another in our own experience, it need not surprise us that our extended senses or technologies should repeat the process of translation and assimilation of one form into another." Number translates touch; print translates the medieval positional zero into Renaissance infinity; one medium translates itself into the next. This is the same dynamic the vault tracks as `The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium` — content is always another medium because each medium translates a prior one — here stated as the deeper claim that translation is the basic gesture of mediation. `Directly stated`

In the electric phase the translation turns inward and total. "By putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems, by means of electric media," McLuhan writes, "all such extensions of our bodies, including cities—will be translated into information systems"; "we see ourselves being translated more and more into the form of information, moving toward the technological extension of consciousness." The chapter thus runs the translation operation to its limit — the rendering of the body, the city, and finally consciousness itself into information — which is why it is a direct precursor to `The Technological Extension of Consciousness` and, downstream, to the vault's `The Body as Data` and `Dataism` cluster. The case studies confirm the breadth of the claim: in Chapter 24 (Games) McLuhan extends it to art and play — "Art, like games, is a translator of experience." `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — the originating use. Media translate experience into new forms; the medium is an "active metaphor" working by four-part ratio; mechanization is the translation of nature into amplified, specialized forms; in the electric age the body and consciousness are themselves translated into information. The thesis spans the title chapter (Ch. 6) and the Part II case studies (number translating touch in Ch. 11; art and games as translators of experience in Ch. 24).
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** (cross-link, not merged) — McLuhan's "all media are active metaphors" is a structural ancestor of O'Gieblyn's `Technology as Metaphor`: where McLuhan says a medium translates experience by holding it in a metaphoric ratio, O'Gieblyn traces how metaphors borrowed from technology (mind as machine, the universe as computer) harden into literal descriptions. McLuhan keeps the metaphoricity explicit and generative; O'Gieblyn warns of its forgetting. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** (cross-link, not merged) — McLuhan's electric-age translation of "all such extensions of our bodies, including cities" into "information systems" anticipates, in a non-cybernetic key, the informatic rendering of the body Hayles documents under `The Body as Data` and `Informatics`. McLuhan treats the translation into information as continuous with bodily perception; Hayles tracks the tradition that uses it to make embodiment incidental. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Related Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the thesis this concept fuses with metaphor: a medium translates *because* it is an extension that re-proportions the senses; "since all media are extensions of our own bodies and senses ... our extended senses or technologies should repeat the process of translation."
- [[The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium]] — the corollary: content is always a prior medium because every medium translates one form into another.
- [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]] — the limit case of translation: the body, the city, and finally consciousness "translated into information systems."
- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — O'Gieblyn's later concept; McLuhan's "active metaphor" is its forerunner, with the metaphoricity foregrounded rather than forgotten.

## Questions It Raises

- If every medium translates experience, is anything lost in translation — and is McLuhan's "active metaphor" a faithful carrying-across, or does each medium impose the ratio it can hold? (Cf. the Freudian "repression" of what we fail to translate, which McLuhan invokes in Ch. 6.)
- McLuhan reads money as "also a metaphor" — a stored, amplifiable ratio of experience. Does treating money, number, and information all as translations level real differences among them, or expose a shared logic?
- When the electric age translates "our own natures ... into information systems," does the metaphor of translation still hold, or does total translation into information cut the tie to the originating body that `Media as Extensions of Man` insists on?
- Is the four-part metaphoric ratio McLuhan invokes a literal mechanism of how media work, or itself a metaphor for it — the same slippage `Technology as Metaphor` warns about?
