# The Document (Conceptual Art)

## Brief Definition

The material residue — the xerox, photograph, map, drawing, certificate, project description, recorded or spoken statement, or catalogue — that stands in for, or constitutes, an ephemeral or never-realized conceptual work whose existence lies "beyond direct perceptual experience."

## Longer Explanation

In Lippard's account of dematerialized art, the work itself frequently cannot be seen: it is an event already past, a condition too dispersed to perceive at once, a proposal never built, or words spoken but never recorded. What remains, and what circulates, is the document. Douglas Huebler's catalogue statement gives the canonical formulation: because the work is beyond direct perceptual experience, "awareness of the [work] depends on a system of documentation," and that documentation "takes the form of photographs, maps, drawings and descriptive language." The document is not an illustration of the artwork sitting elsewhere — for much of this work it is the only place the work is instantiated. `Directly stated`

This makes the document the most vault-generative idea in conceptual art for the material/immaterial problem: it is a *material trace of an immaterial work*. The structure is the exact art-historical analogue of Hayles's claim, in [[The Materiality of Informatics]], that information must always be instantiated in some medium — that there is no disembodied pattern, only different embodiments. Lippard's dematerialized object never escapes matter; it relocates from the made object to the documentary medium. Jan Dibbets says of his bird-territory piece that the "sculpture-drawing can[not] be seen in its entirety; only through its documentation can the viewer reconstr[uct] [its] form in his mind." The work lives as a mental reconstruction cued by a material support — a near-perfect figure for the tension catalogued under [[Material vs Immaterial]]. `Directly stated`

The document is also the seam at which conceptual art reorganizes itself around [[Information]] and [[Systems Theory]] rather than around composition and physical presence. Lippard notes that "information and systems replaced traditional formal concerns of composition, color, technique, and physical presence"; lists, diagrams, measurements, and "neutral descriptions" became the common vehicles. The drive toward neutral, non-distorting documentation — Ian Burn opting "for the most neutral format, one that doesn't interfere with or distort the information" — treats the document as a transparent channel, even as the work's whole reality depends on the channel's persistence. `Directly stated`

The decisive irony is recuperation. The dematerialized document was supposed to be the thing the market could not sell. Lippard recalls the 1969 expectation that "no one, not even a public greedy for novelty, would actually pay money, or much of it, for a xerox sheet referring to an event past or never directly perceived." Three years later "the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums," shown in "the world's most prestigious galleries." The trace meant to evade commodity status became the commodity. The document was the thing the market later resold. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** — frames the document as the characteristic residue of dematerialization: "a xerox sheet referring to an event past or never directly perceived, a group of photographs documenting an ephemeral situation or condition, a project for work never to be completed, words spoken but not recorded." She treats it both as the medium of a hoped-for de-commodified, decentralized art and as the site of its eventual recapture by the market.
- **Douglas Huebler** (quoted by Lippard) — supplies the load-bearing thesis that "awareness of the [work] depends on a system of documentation," fixing documentation as constitutive, not supplementary.
- **Jan Dibbets** (quoted by Lippard) — gives the perceptual version: the work cannot be seen whole, so the viewer reconstructs its form "only through its documentation."

## Related Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — the document is what dematerialization leaves behind; the concept is unintelligible without it.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — Hayles's "information must be instantiated in a medium" is the theoretical twin of the document's role; the document is dematerialization's confession that the immaterial still needs a body.
- [[Information]] — the document is the medium through which the work becomes information rather than object.
- [[Systems Theory]] — "the work concerns itself with things whose interrelationship[s are] beyond direct perceptual experience"; documentation is how a system, not a thing, is made knowable.
- [[Virtuality]] — the work exists virtually, as a reconstruction "in his mind," with the document as its material anchor.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the document does not resolve this tension; it embodies it, holding a material support and an immaterial, never-directly-perceived work in the same artifact. The opposition is internal to the document itself.

## Questions It Raises

- If the document is a material trace of an immaterial work, does dematerialization ever achieve immateriality, or only displace materiality from object to support — the [[Material vs Immaterial]] problem restated for art?
- When "awareness of the work depends on a system of documentation," is the artwork the experience the document induces in a viewer's mind, rather than any object at all — i.e., is conceptual art an early case of [[Art as Experiential Technology]]?
- The conceptual document anticipates the recorded statement, the map, the project file, and the screen-borne trace of [[New Media Art]]: is the gallery wall-label, the video, and the database entry the same problem of instantiation under a new medium?
- If the body that performs or perceives the work is absent and only its documentation remains, what happens to the role of the body in art — does documentation amount to the disappearance of the body that Hayles diagnoses elsewhere, or its relocation into the photograph and the record?
- Why did the trace meant to escape commodity status become the most resold commodity — is documentary "immateriality" structurally recuperable by the market it was meant to evade?
