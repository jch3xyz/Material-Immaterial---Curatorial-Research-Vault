# Policy Resistance

## Brief Definition

Policy resistance is the system trap — the "fixes that fail" archetype — in which many actors pull a single system [[Stocks and Flows|stock]] toward incompatible goals, so any effective new policy provokes counter-effort and settles the system into a standoff no one wants yet everyone maintains at great expense.

## Longer Explanation

In [[Donella Meadows]]'s *Thinking in Systems*, policy resistance is one of the recurring **system traps** (or archetypes) of Chapter Five, and it belongs structurally to the family of [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing-loop]] behaviors introduced in the systems zoo: "the primary symptom of a balancing feedback loop structure is that not much changes, despite outside forces pushing the system." When that stabilizing structure is turned against a well-meaning intervention, the result is a system that "seems to be intractably stuck, producing the same behavior every year" — Meadows names it "the systemic trap of 'fixes that fail' or 'policy resistance.'" Her examples are the familiar frustrations of policy: wars on drugs after which "drugs are as prevalent as ever," farm programs that never end the gluts, decades of "job creation" that never keep unemployment permanently low — domains where "energetic efforts consistently produce non-results."

The mechanism is not stubbornness or bad implementation but the structure of [[Bounded Rationality|bounded rationality]]. "Policy resistance comes from the bounded rationalities of the actors in a system, each with his or her (or 'its' in the case of an institution) own goals." Each actor monitors one important variable, compares it to a private goal, and acts to close the gap — usually more emphatically the larger the discrepancy. Because "goals of subsystems are different from and inconsistent with each other," these corrective efforts point in incompatible directions. Meadows's picture is a single stock — drug supply on the city streets — with addicts wanting it high, enforcement agencies wanting it low, and pushers wanting it in the middle. When one actor gains ground, "the others double their efforts to pull it back." The countermoves cancel: "the stock is not much different from before, and that is not what anybody wants." The steady state is expensive stasis — "everyone has to put great effort into keeping the system where no one wants it to be" — and it can run in a **ratchet mode**: "Intensification of anyone's effort leads to intensification of everyone else's," and de-escalating is hard because "it takes a lot of mutual trust to say, OK, why don't we all just back off for a while?"

The trap's danger is that the intuitive response makes it worse. One way to deal with policy resistance "is to try to overpower it," which can work "at the cost of monumental resentment and the possibility of explosive consequences if the power is ever let up." Meadows's grim case is Romania's 1967 abortion ban under Ceauşescu, whose attempt to overpower the population's resistance produced tripled maternal mortality, orphanages full of abandoned children, and — when the regime fell — his own execution, the ban being the first law the new government repealed. The counterintuitive way out is the opposite of force. "Let go. Give up ineffective policies." Because "much of the action you were trying to correct was in response to your own action," relaxing the push lets the counter-push relax too — as when the end of Prohibition in 1933 let "the alcohol-driven chaos also largely end." Letting go then buys the chance to look at the [[Feedback Loops|feedbacks]] and the [[Bounded Rationality|bounded rationality]] behind them.

But the most powerful remedy is not merely to stop pushing; it is to change what everyone is pushing toward. "The most effective way of dealing with policy resistance is to find a way of aligning the various goals of the subsystems, usually by providing an overarching goal that allows all actors to break out of their bounded rationality." When all the loops serve one goal, "the results can be amazing" — wartime economic mobilizations, or Sweden's population policy, which abandoned any target for family *size* and aligned government and citizens instead on the *quality* of every child's life, so that the birth rate has since "gone up and down several times without causing panic in either direction, because the nation is focused on a far more important goal than the number of Swedes." Meadows's boxed summary states the trap and its exit flatly: any effective new policy "just pulls the stock farther from the goals of other actors and produces additional resistance, with a result that no one likes, but that everyone expends considerable effort in maintaining," and THE WAY OUT is to "Bring in all the actors and use the energy formerly expended on resistance to seek out mutually satisfactory ways for all goals to be realized." Because the trap is rooted in incompatible goals rather than in the [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop|reinforcing engine]] of runaway growth, it is a distinct archetype from [[The Limits-to-Growth Archetype]], though both belong to the same systems-zoo repertoire.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — policy resistance is a balancing-loop **system trap**, the "fixes that fail" archetype: many actors, each acting inside its own [[Bounded Rationality|bounded rationality]] toward an inconsistent goal, pull one system [[Stocks and Flows|stock]] in different directions, so any effective policy provokes proportional counter-effort and the system freezes "where no one wants it to be," sometimes in a self-intensifying ratchet. Overpowering it breeds resentment and blowup; the counterintuitive fix is to let go, and the most effective fix is to align all the subsystems' goals under an overarching goal.

## Related Concepts

- [[Bounded Rationality]] — the root cause: each actor rationally serves its own local goal on its own local information, and the incompatibility of those goals is what produces the resistance.
- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — policy resistance is a pathological use of the balancing structure whose "primary symptom" is that "not much changes, despite outside forces pushing the system."
- [[Stocks and Flows]] — the trap forms around a single shared stock that the competing actors each try to pull toward a different level.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the standoff is a web of opposed corrective loops; "calming down" is what lets you finally see the feedbacks and redesign toward a shared goal.
- [[Systems Theory]] — policy resistance is one of the discipline's recurring archetypes ("frequently found system structures that produce familiar behavior patterns").
- [[The Limits-to-Growth Archetype]] — a sibling system trap from the same repertoire; limits-to-growth turns on a growth loop meeting an environmental limit, whereas policy resistance turns on incompatible actor goals.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] — the runaway-growth engine of the other traps; policy resistance is instead a *balancing*-loop trap of enforced stasis, so its dynamic (locked equilibrium, not amplification) runs opposite to the reinforcing archetype — even though its ratchet mode borrows reinforcing amplification among the actors' competing efforts.

## Questions It Raises

- If policy resistance is rooted in [[Bounded Rationality]] rather than in bad actors, is "overpowering" it always a mistake, or only when the power cannot be sustained indefinitely?
- Meadows says the way out is an "overarching goal that allows all actors to break out of their bounded rationality" — but who has the standing to define that goal without simply becoming one more actor pulling the stock, and does the wartime/disaster example imply that only external threat can supply it?
- When a policy-resistant system runs "in a ratchet mode," a *balancing*-loop trap starts amplifying like a [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] — so is the ratchet a separate reinforcing structure riding on top of the standoff, and does that make de-escalation a coordination problem rather than a policy problem?
- Is "letting go" genuinely available to a government or agency whose legitimacy is staked on the very goal it would have to abandon, or is the counterintuitive fix mostly unavailable precisely where the resistance is fiercest?
