# Art as a Form of Behaviour

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's keystone reformulation of art from the arrangement of forms into a participatory, behavioural system — "art as the behaviour of forms has become art as a form of behaviour" — in which the artist no longer supplies content but builds contexts and systems, and the viewer manipulates her own universe of experience.

## Longer Explanation

In "The Planetary Collegium" (1994) Ascott names *interaction* as one of the five defining features of telematic art, glossing it in a single compressed clause: "interaction: as the very form of art, such that art as the behaviour of forms has become art as a form of behaviour" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/26 The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-]]). The phrasing is deliberately chiastic. In the old dispensation, forms behaved — they composed, balanced, resolved on a surface, and the work was the well-arranged result the spectator received. In Ascott's account the relation inverts: behaviour itself becomes the form of the work. The locus of art shifts from the object's internal formal order to the participant's conduct inside a system the artist has set up. *Directly stated.*

This entails a redefinition of the artist's role. "Instead of providing content and meaning, directing passion and pre-empting feeling, the artist now creates contexts and systems, within which the viewer is instrumental in manipulating his own universe of experience and insight" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/26 The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-]]). The artist withdraws from the position of message-sender — the one who directs passion and pre-empts feeling — and becomes a designer of conditions. The work is no longer something delivered to a "passive observer" but something the participant generates; the viewing subject "now negotiates and navigates what is largely a world of her own making" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/26 The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-]]). This is the mature 1994 restatement of the [[Behaviourist Art]] position Ascott first set out in the 1960s ("Art is now a form of behaviour. Message ends.") — now generalized from the spectator-altered construction to the distributed, networked, telematic system. *Directly stated.*

Within the vault's art/systems cluster, "art as a form of behaviour" is the **participatory pole** opposite [[Systems Esthetics|Jack Burnham's]] object-to-system [[Systems Esthetics]]. Both 1960s cybernetic positions put process before object, but they reach it from opposite sides: Burnham (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*, 1968) traces the autonomous sculptural object migrating inward from [[Object and System|object to system]]; Ascott locates art from the start in the behavioural loop between artist, work, and participant. Where Burnham's systems esthetics asks what the object becomes when it ceases to be an object, Ascott's behavioural art asks what the *viewer* does when she ceases to be a spectator. The keystone restatement here also directly extends the committed-stub concepts Burnham realized — art as something the participant *does* (a [[Art as Experiential Technology|behaviour, not a contemplated object]]), and the participatory genealogy of [[New Media Art]]. *Interpretive synthesis.*

The formulation belongs to a longer genealogy in the same essay: Ascott describes "the metaphor of emergence" displacing "the aesthetic of the material object," so that art's defining quality becomes "the perpetual coming into being of meaning, matter, and mind" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/26 The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-]]). Art as a form of behaviour is the participatory face of this material→immaterial spine: the work is not a thing that exists but a behaviour that happens — open, unresolved, completed differently by each [[The Participator|participator]]. *Strongly implied.*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coiner and sole theorist. "Art as a form of behaviour" is the 1994 telematic restatement of his 1960s [[Behaviourist Art]]: the work is a context/system, not a content, and the participant manipulates "his own universe of experience and insight." The artist becomes [[The Artist as Designer of Context|a designer of contexts]] rather than a sender of messages; the criterion of the new art is [[Reciprocal Communication|two-way, mind-to-mind interaction]] rather than one-way transmission to a passive receptor.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (the opposing pole, not a variant) — Burnham's contemporaneous [[Systems Esthetics]] reaches a process-over-object conclusion from the side of the sculptural object's internal migration. Ascott supplies the participatory/behavioural counter-pole: where Burnham systematizes the object, Ascott behaviourizes the viewer. See [[Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham]] for the two poles of 1960s–90s cybernetic art.

## Related Concepts

- [[Behaviourist Art]] — the 1960s parent term this 1994 formulation restates and generalizes from spectator-altered constructions to distributed telematic systems.
- [[The Participator]] — the agent the reformulation requires: the viewer who, no longer passive, "manipulates his own universe of experience and insight."
- [[The Artist as Designer of Context]] — the corollary role-shift: "the artist now creates contexts and systems" instead of providing content and meaning.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the interactional criterion: "interaction: as the very form of art" is the two-way loop that makes behaviour, not form, the work.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — art reconceived as a feedback-driven experience the participant completes rather than an object contemplated; extended by this restatement.
- [[New Media Art]] — the participatory/telematic genealogy this concept descends into, extended by Ascott.
- [[Emergence]] — the fifth defining feature alongside interaction; behaviour-as-form is the participatory face of "the perpetual coming into being of meaning, matter, and mind."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's object→system pole. Both put process before object, but systems esthetics systematizes the autonomous artwork while art-as-behaviour relocates art into the participant's conduct. The contrast is complementary, not contradictory: the two poles of one cybernetic moment.
- [[Object vs System]] — the underlying opposition. Art as a form of behaviour falls decisively on the system/behaviour side: the work "resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system."

## Questions It Raises

- If the artist supplies only "contexts and systems" and the viewer "manipulates his own universe of experience," in what sense is there still an *authored work* — and how is its quality to be judged when each participant's behaviour produces a different outcome?
- Ascott frames the participant's freedom celebratorily ("a world of her own making"). But the artist still designs the system within which she acts. How free is behaviour that unfolds inside a context another has built — and how does that bear on the surveillance/control reading of feedback systems in the vault's later Han/Harari cluster?
- Burnham's systems esthetics and Ascott's art-as-behaviour reach the same process-over-object conclusion from opposite ends. Is "object vs system" really one tension, or do the object-side and viewer-side versions name two different aesthetics that only look alike?
