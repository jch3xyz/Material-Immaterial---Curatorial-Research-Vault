# Specialism as Counter-Irritant

## Brief Definition

The thesis that specialism and fragmentation are not signs of mastery but a defensive adaptation — a "counter-irritant" by which the handicapped, the conquered, and finally technological Western man make themselves indispensable under pressure, at the cost of a fatal over-adjustment in which "innovation is for them not novelty but annihilation."

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 7, McLuhan reads specialism through Arnold Toynbee's *A Study of History* and Émile Durkheim. Toynbee's account of how "the lame and the crippled respond to their handicaps in a society of active warriors" supplies the founding figure: the disabled man cannot fight, so "they become specialists like Vulcan, the smith and armorer." The same strategy, McLuhan argues, scales from the individual to the collective — conquered peoples "specialize and become indispensable to their masters." Specialism is therefore the compensation of the handicapped and the enslaved: a way of converting weakness into a niche that the strong cannot do without. From this long history McLuhan derives "the stigma of servitude and pusillanimity on the figure of the specialist, even in modern times." (Directly stated)

The decisive turn is that Western man's own fragmentation under specialist technology repeats this servile pattern — but *voluntarily*. The "capitulation of Western man to his technology, with its crescendo of specialized demands," has looked to many observers "like a kind of enslavement," yet "the resulting fragmentation has been voluntary and enthusiastic, unlike the conscious strategy of specialism on the part of the captives of military conquest." The literate, specialized subject (see [[Typographic Man]]) has chosen his own fragmentation; the chosenness does not redeem it. Specialism is a [[Autoamputation|self-amputation]] of the integral person into a single channeled function. (Directly stated for the voluntary/enthusiastic framing; Interpretive synthesis for the link to autoamputation)

McLuhan then names the danger built into this adaptation. "Perfect adaptation to any environment is achieved by a total channeling of energies and vital force that amounts to a kind of static terminus for a creature." The perfectly adapted specialist has spent all his resources on fit, so that "even slight changes in the environment of the very well adjusted find them without any resource to meet new challenge." This is the plight of "conventional wisdom": "Their entire stake of security and status is in a single form of acquired knowledge, so that innovation is for them not novelty but annihilation." The over-specialized organism cannot survive a shift in conditions because it has nothing left over with which to respond. (Directly stated)

The word *counter-irritant* is given its full sense in Chapter 24, where games and technologies are paired as the two great relief mechanisms for specialized life: "Both games and technologies are counter-irritants or ways of adjusting to the stress of the specialized actions that occur in any social group." Games are McLuhan's antidote to the very fragmentation specialism imposes, because, unlike a job, they restore the whole person — "in fun and play we recover the integral person, who in the workaday world or in professional life can use only a small sector of his being," and "games as popular art forms offer to all an immediate means of participation in the full life of a society, such as no single role or job can offer to any man." The counter-irritant is double-edged: McLuhan warns that "the counter-irritant usually proves a greater plague than the initial irritant, like a drug habit," so the specialism that relieves collective stress can itself become the deeper affliction. (Directly stated)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — Specialism/fragmentation is a "counter-irritant," a defensive over-adaptation traced (via Toynbee and Durkheim) from the lame smith and the enslaved community to the voluntary fragmentation of technological Western man. Perfect adaptation is a "static terminus," and for the over-specialized "innovation is for them not novelty but annihilation." Games are the counter-counter-irritant that recover the integral person the specialist job dismembers.

## Questions It Raises

- If Western fragmentation is "voluntary and enthusiastic," is it less of an enslavement than the conquered specialist's, or more — because it is not even recognized as servitude?
- When does a counter-irritant tip into being "a greater plague than the initial irritant," and can a culture detect the tipping point before its "static terminus"?
- If games and popular art are the only forms in which the modern specialist "recovers the integral person," what happens when those too are professionalized into "merely specialist" jobs?
- Does the diagnosis that "innovation is for them not novelty but annihilation" apply to whole institutions of "conventional wisdom" (universities, bureaucracies) as much as to individuals?
