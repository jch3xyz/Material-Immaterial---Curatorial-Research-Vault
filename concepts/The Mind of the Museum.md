# The Mind of the Museum

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's 1996 reconception of the museum as a brain that thinks for itself — "the museum as a brain, richly embodying the associative thought of its own, very tangible cortex, what properly should be called its hypercortex" — a fluid, intelligent organism synaptic with the artificial neural networks of the planet rather than a static repository of objects.

## Longer Explanation

In the keynote "The Mind of the Museum" (delivered at the Art Institute of Chicago, October 1996) Ascott argues that the digital museum cannot survive by retrofitting its old building — "smart interfaces, responsive interiors, or interactive fixtures and fittings" — but must itself "become intelligent, must get smart." The proposal is literal, not metaphorical: "We are no longer in the realm of the dream and the analogy of great visionaries such as, say, Pierre Teilhard de Chardin and Gregory Bateson. The field of consciousness engendered by the intensive hyperconnectivity of minds on the Internet is now palpably a part of our reality." The museum's model must become the model of the brain, sharing "the need to serve the functions of memory, thought, reality building, non-linear narrative, consciousness, and dreaming." (*Directly stated*.)

The concept's pivot is a renunciation of curatorial control. "We have to stop thinking for the museum and allow it to think for itself," Ascott writes; "we need to be less concerned with what it should be doing, what art it might contain, what policies it should embody, even what architectural form it should take, and concern ourselves with what art might emerge, what ideas might come into being, what forms might grow." The museum's mind is built from "the hypercortex, the cognitive nexus of all those ideas, forms, structures, and strategies that are generated in interspace, the associative cognition, the hyperlinks of a profound connectivity, that constitute the field of becoming between the virtual and the real that is our global domain." It is, in Ascott's framing, "as much emotional as instrumental, as intuitive as ordered" — a brain whose synapses are the hyperlinks of [[Hypermedia]] and whose substrate is the Net, the basis of an emergent [[Network Consciousness]]. (*Directly stated*.)

The mind of the museum is the **carrier concept** that holds three of the chapter's signature figures together. It is realized as the [[The Museum of the Third Kind|museum of the third kind]] — "where the close encounter of minds with minds, cortex to hypercortex, virtual with the actual, leads to interactivity at the level of the spirit" — superseding both the first kind (where "those with the leisure to do so could hang out with the muse") and the second (where "the materiality of art, in all its glorious and discrete objecthood, could be transposed into its virtual other"). It must be "riddled with wormholes" — a [[The Wormhole|wormhole]] is "a kind of cognitive tunnelling through what might be called 'data foam' on the Web, from one hyperlinked layer to another." And it dissolves the building into [[Gardens of Hypotheses|gardens of hypotheses]]: "It is time to release the metaphors from their cages, to let loose upon the world these tight concentrations of hypotheses." (*Directly stated*.)

Crucially, Ascott reframes the museum's historic function not as the control of information but the control of *meaning*: "I take issue with those who assert that the museum's function is simply to control information, since I believe that in truth it serves and has always served to control meaning, actually to assist in the dissemination of ideology, to shape consciousness." The intelligent museum is therefore not neutral plumbing but a participant in the construction of reality — a thesis Ascott grounds in his enthusiastic immaterialism: "Ours is increasingly an immaterial world," and as "the museum dematerialises, we might then see new metaphors of what it is to be human materialize all around us." (*Directly stated*; the immaterialism is the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis at its most affirmative.)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — the mind of the museum is the institutional consequence of the object→system drift; it opposes the museum as a repository of "material artifacts (concrete instances)."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott resolves the axis toward the immaterial ("Ours is increasingly an immaterial world"), against the museum's attachment of "meaning with materiality, and thus museums with ownership."
- [[Dematerialization]] — the concept enacts dematerialization at the scale of the institution: "As the museum dematerialises . . ."

## Questions It Raises

- If the museum is allowed to "think for itself," what becomes of curatorial responsibility, judgment, and the ethics of selection — does "curatorial obsolescence" abolish accountability or merely redistribute it?
- Ascott insists the brain-museum is "pragmatism rather than . . . metaphor." Where is the line between an operational claim about networked cognition and a poetic figure that "may quickly die"?
- If the museum has always controlled meaning and "disseminat[ed] ideology," does making it an intelligent, self-organizing system escape that power — or deepen it, as [[Roy Ascott and Byung-Chul Han|Han]] would predict of any pervasive networked apparatus?
- Can collective memory survive a museum that privileges "becoming" over "collections of artifacts of what has passed," or does the loss of the material trace foreclose the past entirely?
