# The Right to Be Forgotten

## Brief Definition

The legal right — won by ninety ordinary Spaniards against Google before the Court of Justice of the European Union in May 2014 — to have personal information de-linked from search results; for Shoshana Zuboff it is the concrete instance of the [[The Right to the Future Tense|right to the future tense]] in motion, democracy clawing back the digital future and declaring surveillance capitalism contingent rather than inevitable.

## Longer Explanation

The right to be forgotten is the abstraction of the future tense made into a courtroom struggle. Zuboff uses the Spanish case as the "stage-setting" episode of the entire book: on August 9, 2011, "Spanish citizens asserted their rights to a human future when they challenged Google by demanding 'the right to be forgotten'" (Directly stated). The Spanish Data Protection Agency championed ninety citizens — among them attorney Mario Costeja González, whose name still returned a long-resolved home foreclosure notice — by ordering Google to stop indexing the contested links. Costeja's case was referred up to the Court of Justice of the European Union, which in May 2014 "announced its decision to assert the right to be forgotten as a fundamental principle of EU law" (Directly stated).

For Zuboff the technical question of de-linking is the least of it. The deeper claim is about authorship of the future. Google had "unilaterally undertaken to change the rules of the information life cycle when it decided to crawl, index, and make accessible personal details across the world wide web without asking anyone's permission," so that "information that would normally age and be forgotten now remains forever young, highlighted in the foreground of each person's digital identity." The right to be forgotten reasserts that "not all information is worthy of immortality" — that "some information should be forgotten because that is only human" (Directly stated). In this it directly counters the data-extractive logic of [[Surveillance Capitalism]]: the case "pitted the complexity of human existence, with its thousand million shades of gray, against surveillance capitalism's economic imperatives that produced the relentless drive to extract and retain information" (Directly stated).

The pivotal move, for Zuboff, is the case's refusal of [[Inevitabilism]]. The opposition "asserted that even Google's capitalism was made by humans to be unmade and remade by democratic processes, not commercial decree" (Directly stated). This is the book's clearest evidence that surveillance capitalism is contingent: "It was there, in Spain, that the right to the future tense was on the move, insisting that the operations of surveillance capitalism and its digital architecture are not, never were, and never would be inevitable" (Directly stated). The Court's decision was therefore "a key inflection point at which democracy began to claw back rights to the future tense from the powerful forces of a new surveillance capitalism determined to claim unilateral authority over the digital future," recognizing that "search results are the contingent products of the specific economic interests that drive the action from within the belly of the machine" (Directly stated).

The case is thus the legal-political face of the [[The Right to the Future Tense|right to the future tense]]: a refusal of the claim that a person's data-rendered past must determine their available future. Zuboff frames the victory as a contest of weights — "technological inevitability is as light as democracy is heavy" — and reads it as "a stirring early chapter in the longer story of our fight for a third modern that is first and foremost a human future" (Directly stated). The contrasting reactions of Google's leaders, who "sneered at the decision" (Sergey Brin: "I wish we could just forget the ruling"; Larry Page suggesting "people should trust Google more than democratic institutions"), supply Zuboff her antagonist: a private power "indignant at being challenged" and "determined to retain privileged control over the future" (Directly stated). Against the "smart money" that said such a ruling could never happen in the US, she notes that in a national poll the following year "88 percent supported a law similar to the right to be forgotten" (Directly stated).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — treats the Spanish right-to-be-forgotten case not as a privacy or deletion technicality but as the concrete instantiation of the right to the future tense and as the book's proof that surveillance capitalism "was made by humans to be unmade and remade by democratic processes." The case demonstrates "the unbearable lightness of the inevitable" and establishes "the primacy of democratic institutions in shaping a healthy and just digital future."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Inevitabilism]] — the case is the book's central evidence *against* technological inevitability: surveillance capitalism "was made by humans to be unmade and remade by democratic processes," and its architecture is "not, never were, and never would be inevitable."

## Questions It Raises

- Is de-linking from search a sufficient remedy for a structural harm, given that the Spanish agency itself "rejected the idea of requiring newspapers and other originating sites to remove legitimate information"?
- Zuboff calls the case "a stirring early chapter" — does a right asserted case-by-case scale to a power that "operates at population scale and behind the screen"?
- If the right to be forgotten balances "a right to be forgotten and a right to know" (Eric Schmidt's framing), who arbitrates the balance, and on whose terms?
- Does grounding the victory in "individual sovereignty" reassert a liberal-humanist sovereign self that other authors in the vault (Hayles, Haraway) have placed under question?
