# Information Flows

## Brief Definition

In Donella Meadows's ranked list of places to intervene in a system, *information flows* is leverage point number six — "the structure of who does and does not have access to information" — and its central lesson is that missing or misrouted information is one of the most common causes of system malfunction, so that adding or restoring a feedback signal is among the cheapest, highest-leverage interventions available.

## Longer Explanation

Meadows names leverage point #6 exactly: "Information Flows—The structure of who does and does not have access to information" (`Directly stated`). It sits just above the two feedback-loop rungs (#8 balancing, #7 reinforcing) and just below rules (#5) — on the "information and control" side of the list, past the crossover where Meadows says "we're beginning to move from the physical part of the system to the information and control parts, where more leverage can be found." What distinguishes information flows from the loop-strength rungs beneath it is that it is not about tuning a loop that already exists. As she puts it, recalling the Dutch electric-meter story, "It's not a parameter adjustment, not a strengthening or weakening of an existing feedback loop. It's a new loop, delivering feedback to a place where it wasn't going before" (`Directly stated`). Information Flows is thus the *structural/relational* leverage property — a question of loop topology, of who is wired to see what — rather than a substance. It is deliberately kept distinct from the concept [[Information]] (information-as-such); here the operative variable is the geometry of access, not the content of the signal (`Interpretive synthesis`).

The thesis Meadows attaches to this rung is one of her most quoted: "Missing information flows is one of the most common causes of system malfunction. Adding or restoring information can be a powerful intervention, usually much easier and cheaper than rebuilding physical infrastructure" (`Directly stated`). The canonical illustration — cross-linked here as its own symbol — is [[The Electric Meter]]: identical Dutch houses in which "electricity consumption was 30 percent lower in the houses where the meter was in the highly visible location in the front hall" (`Directly stated`). Nothing material was added; a feedback signal already being generated was simply routed to a place a person could see it. That is why information flows outrank the physical rungs below (numbers, buffers, stock-and-flow structures, delays): supplying a signal costs a fraction of rebuilding the plumbing, yet reshapes behavior more.

Meadows insists the restored feedback must go "to the right place and in compelling form." Her fisheries example shows a *missing* flow driving collapse: "there is little feedback from the state of the fish population to the decision to invest in fishing vessels … It is not price information but population information that is needed" — indeed price is a *perverse* feedback, "a reinforcing loop that leads to collapse" as scarcity raises the value of the last fish (this is the [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] / [[Success to the Successful]] pattern in another guise). Her positive examples are all about *accountability by design*: the taxpayer specifying where taxes go, the town forced to site its water intake "immediately downstream from its own wastewater outflow pipe," the official who must store nuclear waste "on his or her lawn," the politicians who declare war being "required to spend that war in the front lines." Each closes a loop between a decision and its consequence.

This is where the concept gains its political charge in the vault. Meadows diagnoses "a systematic tendency on the part of human beings to avoid accountability for their own decisions. That's why there are so many missing feedback loops—and why this kind of leverage point is so often popular with the masses, unpopular with the powers that be" (`Directly stated`). Information flows are contested precisely because they are cheap and effective: she notes that market and democratic feedbacks alike are eroded when "the distributors of information" become "self-interested partners," and that the world-trade system's "rules exclude almost any feedback from any other sector of society. Most of its meetings are closed even to the press (no information flow, no feedback)." Restoring a flow redistributes visibility, and therefore power — which is why it is resisted from above even as it is one of the most reversible, low-cost fixes a system offers (`Interpretive synthesis`). This connects information-flow suppression to the vault's broader surveillance-and-legibility thread, though Meadows's own frame is engineering, not domination.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*, 2008) — the concept's sole and primary anchor. Information Flows is leverage point #6, "the structure of who does and does not have access to information": the addition of a *new* loop rather than the adjustment of an existing one; the fix for the single most common cause of system malfunction; "usually much easier and cheaper than rebuilding physical infrastructure." Meadows frames it as an accountability structure — feedback restored "to the right place and in compelling form" so that decision-makers feel the consequences of their decisions — and notes that it is systematically resisted by "the powers that be" because opening a flow redistributes power. Kept distinct from her broader use of [[Information]] and from the [[Feedback Loops]] rungs beneath it.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Central Control vs Autonomy]] — the tension the concept dramatizes: closed, centralized systems malfunction by *withholding* feedback (the world-trade meetings "closed even to the press," central planning "necessarily functions poorly"), whereas opening information flows distributes visibility outward. Meadows's leverage point pushes toward the autonomy pole; "the powers that be" resist it toward control.

## Questions It Raises

- If restoring a feedback loop is "usually much easier and cheaper than rebuilding physical infrastructure," why do so many systems remain broken? Meadows answers with "a systematic tendency … to avoid accountability" — is the barrier therefore never technical but always political?
- The meter case works because the signal is *made visible*. When does the same logic invert into surveillance — feedback restored not to empower the actor but to render them legible to power (the vault's Han/Zuboff thread)? Meadows treats visibility as remedy; does the concept have a dark twin?
- Meadows warns the feedback must reach "the right place and in compelling form." Who decides which place is right, and what makes a signal "compelling" rather than merely available (the aquifer users told the water is dropping still race to the bottom)?
- If price can be a *perverse* feedback that accelerates collapse, is more information ever the wrong intervention — and how do you tell a corrective flow from a destructive one before it runs?
