# A Field Theory of Art

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's 1980 paradigm proposing to replace the formalist modernist aesthetic: art resides neither in the artwork nor in the artist's activity alone but in a probabilistic, highly entropic field of psychic interplay between two sets of behaviours — the artist and the observer — where the viewer interrogates and interacts with the system "artwork" to generate meaning, and "the final responsibility for meaning lies with the viewer."

## Longer Explanation

In "Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art" (paper delivered at the College Art Association, 1978; published in *Leonardo*, 1980), Ascott sets out "the attributes of a paradigm for art, a field theory that would replace the formalist modernist aesthetic." Its decisive move is one of focus: the new paradigm "takes as its focus not form but behaviour." Where formalist modernism treated the work as a fixed, finished object transmitting "simple, clear, and direct messages," the field theory treats it as a *system* — "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer)." Meaning is no longer decoded from a one-way message but generated in the interaction between two psychic systems (`Directly stated`).

The "field" is irreducibly probabilistic. Ascott writes that "Art does not reside in the artwork alone, nor in the activity of the artist alone, but is understood as a field of psychic probability, highly entropic, in which the viewer is actively involved." The viewer's involvement is not "an act of closure in the sense of completing a discrete message from the artist (a passive process) but" a matter of "interrogating and interacting with the system 'artwork' to generate meaning." The viewer is thereby redefined as a [[The Participator|participator]] who is "of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system" — the participatory hinge of Ascott's entire oeuvre, and the point at which his work meets Burnham's `[[Reciprocal Communication]]` and the existing tension `[[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]]` from the behavioural rather than the object side (`Directly stated`).

Ascott insists that the language of this paradigm cannot be borrowed from art itself: "The new understanding of art calls for new terms, a new language, a new set of metaphors. We shall not find these terms in formalist modernism; we shall not find them among 'art' terms at all." He grounds the theory in `[[Cybernetics]]` (Norbert Wiener's information as "transmission of alternatives"), the new physics (Heisenberg's indeterminacy and the quantum observer, whose "observational behaviour and consciousness are a part of what he is observing"), semiotics (Umberto Eco's "gambling on the possibility of semiosis"), psi research (Jacques Vallée), and Eastern spiritual models of consciousness (Fritjof Capra's *The Tao of Physics*). The artwork-as-matrix interlocks the participator's consciousness with the work much as the quantum observer is folded into the observed — a convergence the vault tracks under `[[The Observer]]` (`Strongly implied` on the equivalence framing; the cited physics/cybernetics claims are `Directly stated`).

The essay is the hinge of *Telematic Embrace* — the bridge from Ascott's 1960s behaviourist/cybernetic art to his 1980s telematic/network art. It already carries the seeds of his later work without naming them: a "connective paradigm, which in turn demands a connective criticism," and the methodological commitment that "Networking is the antithesis of reductionism, and to be inconclusive in this sense is the essence of the postmodernist condition" — the network/connectivity vocabulary that prefigures `[[The Global Village]]`-style network thinking. It also announces a "transition from materialist culture" toward "the spiritual in art," seeding the technoetic, consciousness-oriented turn that resonates with O'Gieblyn's `[[Re-enchantment]]` and adds a distinct *materialist-vs-spiritual* inflection to the eight-author tension `[[Material vs Immaterial]]` (`Directly stated` for the quoted phrases; `Interpretive synthesis` for the cross-author resonances).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coiner of the term in its art-theoretic sense; the field theory of art (more fully "a field theory of art and consciousness") is his proposed replacement for formalist modernism. Art is a probabilistic field of psychic interplay between artist and observer; the participator generates meaning; the model is drawn from cybernetics, the new physics, semiotics, and Eastern spiritual models.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — does not use the phrase, but his 1968 `[[Systems Esthetics]]` and `[[Object and System]]` thesis is the object→system counterpart to Ascott's behaviour→system field theory; the two are the complementary poles of 1960s–70s systems-oriented art (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott's "transition from materialist culture" toward "the spiritual in art" makes him the consciousness-inflected pole of the vault's central tension.
- Formalist modernism — Ascott's explicit antagonist: a "one-way street," "totally Newtonian," reducing art to fixed form and a one-way message. (Distinct from Burnham's sculpture-specific `[[Formalism (Burnham)]]`.)

## Questions It Raises

- If "the final responsibility for meaning lies with the viewer," what remains of authorship, and how is an artwork distinguished from any other interrogable system?
- Is the equation of the art participator with the quantum observer a rigorous structural claim or a generative metaphor — and does the field theory depend on which it is?
- Does grounding art in psi research and Eastern spiritual models extend the cybernetic frame or abandon its empirical discipline?
- Can a theory that declares itself necessarily "inconclusive" still function as criticism, or does inconclusiveness become an alibi?
- Where does Ascott's behaviour→system field theory converge with, and where does it diverge from, Burnham's object→system `[[Systems Esthetics]]`?
