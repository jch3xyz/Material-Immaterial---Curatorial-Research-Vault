# Self-Organization

## Brief Definition

The capacity of a system to make its own structure more complex — to learn, diversify, complexify, and evolve — and, remarkably, to do so from just a few simple organizing rules; in Meadows's systems vocabulary it is the source of both hierarchy and heterogeneity and the strongest form of a system's resilience.

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows, self-organization names the most astonishing thing systems do. It is "the ability of a single fertilized ovum to generate, out of itself, the incredible complexity of a mature frog, or chicken, or person," "the ability of nature to have diversified millions of fantastic species out of a puddle of organic chemicals," and the ability of a society to turn burning coal and specialized labor into "an automobile assembly plant, a city of skyscrapers, a worldwide network of communications." She gives it a one-line definition: "This capacity of a system to make its own structure more complex is called self-organization" (`Directly stated`). It is one of the three properties — beside resilience and hierarchy — that, in her account, make systems "work so well." Crucially, self-organization is not confined to biology: Meadows sees it "in a small, mechanistic way" in a snowflake or a garden of crystals, and "in a more profound way whenever a seed sprouts, or a baby learns to speak, or a neighborhood decides to come together to oppose a toxic waste dump."

Meadows's central and most counterintuitive claim is that this open-ended complexity does not require a correspondingly complex cause. "New discoveries," she writes, "suggest that just a few simple organizing principles can lead to wildly diverse self-organizing structures" (`Directly stated`) — the Koch snowflake, the fern generated by a handful of fractal rules, the four-letter genetic code "that is the basis of all biological evolution." A self-organizing system is, at bottom, "a matter of an evolutionary raw material — a highly variable stock of information from which to select possible patterns — and a means for experimentation." Because of this, the property that once looked "so complex … that it could never be understood" is now modeled by "hundreds of self-organizing computer models," and even a putative divine creator "just has to write marvelously clever rules for self-organization."

In *Thinking in Systems*'s hierarchy of leverage points, self-organization ranks very high — number four, "The power to add, change, or evolve system structure," surpassed only by a system's goals, its paradigm, and the power to transcend paradigms. There Meadows equates it across domains: "In biological systems that power is called evolution. In human economies it's called technical advance or social revolution. In systems lingo it's called self-organization" (`Directly stated`). And she ties it directly to survival: "The ability to self-organize is the strongest form of system resilience. A system that can evolve can survive almost any change, by changing itself" (`Directly stated`). Self-organization is what lets the immune system meet a novel insult and the brain "pop out completely new thoughts."

Because self-organization "produces heterogeneity and unpredictability," it has a political edge that gives this concept its charge in the vault. "Like resilience, self-organization is often sacrificed for purposes of short-term productivity and stability" (`Directly stated`) — the same excuses "for turning creative human beings into mechanical adjuncts to production processes," for narrowing the genetic variability of crops, for "establishing bureaucracies … that treat people as if they were only numbers." It "requires freedom and experimentation, and a certain amount of disorder," so "these conditions that encourage self-organization often can be scary for individuals and threatening to power structures" (`Directly stated`): education systems restrict children's creative powers, economies favor established enterprises over upstarts, and "many governments prefer their people not to be too self-organizing." This is the systems-theory root of a suspicion the vault otherwise meets in Han and Zuboff — that the drive toward control and legibility is a drive against a system's own generative capacity (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*, 2008) — the concept's primary anchor. Self-organization is the "most marvelous characteristic of some complex systems," their capacity "to learn, diversify, complexify, evolve"; the generator of hierarchy and heterogeneity; the fourth-strongest leverage point ("The power to add, change, or evolve system structure"); the "strongest form of system resilience"; and — her signature move — a property arising from "just a few simple organizing principles" rather than complex ones. She frames its suppression as a systems crime and a threat to long-term survival.

## Related Concepts

- [[Emergence]] — closely allied but kept **strictly distinct** (per the vault's ingest rules): emergence is the bottom-up *appearance of unanticipated higher-order pattern*; self-organization is the *system's own restructuring of itself into greater complexity*. In [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s reading of second- and third-wave [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]], self-organization is the "springboard to emergence" — the two are cross-linked, never merged.
- [[Autopoiesis]] — also kept **distinct**: autopoiesis is *self-production* (a system continually regenerating its own components to maintain its organization); Meadows's self-organization is the *complexification and evolution* of structure, which by definition changes rather than merely conserves organization. Cross-linked as neighbors in the systems/cybernetics cluster.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the underlying mechanism through which self-organizing systems add, change, or evolve their structure (Meadows: self-organization means "adding new balancing or reinforcing loops, or new rules").
- [[Systems Theory]] — the native discipline in which Meadows works; self-organization is one of the three properties by which systems "work so well."
- [[Homeostasis]] — the stability-preserving counterpart: homeostasis holds a system at a set point, whereas self-organization changes the set points and the structure itself.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the tradition (via Bertalanffy, whom Meadows epigraphs, and later Hayles) in which self-organization first became a technical term.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Self-Regulation]] — a **deliberately maintained distinction** ([[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s neoliberal *self-regulation*): a governance ideology in which markets and firms are left to police themselves, protecting incumbent power. Meadows's self-organization is nearly its opposite — the generative, disorderly, incumbent-*threatening* capacity that "power structures" and central-planning goals routinely twist or suppress. The two share a word and oppose in substance.

## Questions It Raises

- If self-organization "produces heterogeneity and unpredictability" and this is what power structures find threatening, is the recurring drive toward transparency, legibility, and control (Han, Zuboff) best read as a systemic war against a system's own generative capacity?
- Meadows ranks self-organization *below* a system's goal and paradigm as leverage points — the goal "is a leverage point superior to the self-organizing ability of a system." What does it mean that the power to evolve can itself "be twisted to conform" to a controlling goal?
- If "just a few simple organizing principles can lead to wildly diverse self-organizing structures," where does the complexity actually reside — in the rules, in the raw material, or in the history of selection? (Meadows notes science "does not yet know" whether all complexity reduces to simple rules.)
- Does the equivalence Meadows draws — evolution, technical advance, social revolution, all as "self-organization" — flatten meaningful differences between biological, technological, and political change, or reveal a genuine shared logic?
