# Delays

## Brief Definition

A delay is the lag between a change in one part of a system and the response it produces elsewhere; because it makes a feedback loop act on stale information, a delay is a structurally unavoidable and often decisive determinant of how a system behaves.

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows, delays are not incidental frictions to be engineered away but a native feature of any system built from [[Stocks and Flows|stocks]]. She states the point flatly: "Delays are pervasive in systems, and they are strong determinants of behavior" (Chapter 2). Their unavoidability is structural rather than accidental. Because a [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] can only sense the current state of a stock and can only act on the flows that will fill or drain it next, its correction always arrives late: "The information delivered by a feedback loop—even nonphysical feedback—can only affect future behavior; it can't deliver a signal fast enough to correct behavior that drove the current feedback." Delay is therefore baked into the logic of feedback itself. Sharpening this to its structural root, Meadows adds in Chapter 4 that "Delays are ubiquitous in systems. Every stock is a delay" — any accumulation that must be filled or drained over time *is* a lag between cause and effect. `Directly stated`

Meadows distinguishes three kinds of delay, working through the example of a car dealer managing inventory: a *perception delay* (the dealer averages sales over several days "to sort out real trends from temporary dips and spikes"), a *response delay* (she "makes up one-third of any shortfall with each order" rather than correcting all at once), and a *delivery delay* (the time it takes the factory "to receive an order, process it, and" ship the cars). Each of these lags sits at a different point in the loop — sensing, deciding, acting — and each contributes independently to how sluggishly or how nervously the system tracks its goal. `Directly stated`

The behavioral consequence of delay is [[Oscillation|oscillation]]. A delay inside a balancing loop means corrections chronically arrive too late, so the system repeatedly overshoots and undershoots its target: "Overshoots, oscillations, and collapses are always caused by delays" (Chapter 4). In the leverage-points hierarchy of Chapter 6 Meadows makes the same claim structurally — "Delays in feedback loops are critical determinants of system behavior. They are common causes of oscillations" — and ranks the length of delays as a genuine, if sometimes stiff, lever on behavior. `Directly stated`

Crucially, delays are themselves candidate [[Leverage Points|leverage points]]. "Delays are often sensitive leverage points for policy, if they can be made shorter or longer" (Chapter 4). But Meadows resists the reflex that shorter is always better. A delay is frequently a physical fact — factories, breeding populations, and capital stocks turn over only so fast — and cannot simply be wished away. Her counsel is to match the pace of the fast parts of a system to the slow ones rather than to abolish the lag: "There's more leverage in slowing the system down so technologies and prices can keep up with it, than there is in wishing the delays would go away" (Chapter 6). This is why, in her account, delays reward respect over impatience: they are levers precisely because they set the timescale on which everything else can react. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*) — Delays are pervasive, structurally unavoidable (every stock is a delay), and strong determinants of behavior. In a balancing loop a delay is the cause of overshoot, oscillation, and collapse. Meadows distinguishes perception, response, and delivery delays, and treats delay-length as a real but double-edged leverage point — often better lengthened or respected than abolished.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — a delay only matters *inside* a loop; because feedback can act on future behavior alone, delay is intrinsic to how loops correct.
- [[Stocks and Flows]] — "every stock is a delay": accumulation over time is the material substrate of every lag.
- [[Oscillation]] — the characteristic behavior a delayed balancing loop produces; delays are the common cause of oscillation.
- [[Leverage Points]] — delay-length is itself a leverage point, though a stiff and easily-misused one.
- [[Systems Theory]] — delays are one of the recurrent structural features by which systems generate counterintuitive behavior.

## Questions It Raises

- If delay is intrinsic to feedback (a loop can only affect *future* behavior), is any perfectly responsive control system impossible in principle?
- When is the right move to shorten a delay, and when is it to lengthen it or slow the rest of the system to match — and how would one tell in advance?
- How do the three kinds of delay (perception, response, delivery) interact, and can shortening one while leaving the others fixed make oscillation worse rather than better?
- Do the informational delays of digital/networked systems differ in kind from the physical delays of factories and populations, or only in length?
