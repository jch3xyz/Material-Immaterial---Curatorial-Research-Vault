# Apparition

## Brief Definition

For [[Roy Ascott]], **apparition** is the coming-into-being of a new identity — emergence, becoming, "things as they could be" — the inward and emergent processes of becoming that supplant **appearance** (surface, representation, the look of things) as the proper concern of art in the cybersphere.

## Longer Explanation

Apparition is the keystone concept of *[[Telematic Embrace]]* — the figure that gives the book's pivotal essay its title, "From Appearance to Apparition." Ascott stakes the entire claim of an art for the networked, systems-based culture on the substitution of one word for another. Where Western art has "always been" concerned with **appearance** — "the look of things, with surface reality" — the emerging cyberculture is concerned instead with "apparition, emergence, and transformation." The opposition is not a tension between two equal poles so much as a succession: appearance is "dying" because it is "no longer relevant to a culture that is progressively concerned with the complexity of relationships and subtlety of systems, with the invisible and immaterial." This makes apparition the affirmative term in a paradigm shift Ascott reads as decisive and irreversible *(Directly stated)*.

The content of the concept is ontological, not merely aesthetic. "Apparition is about the coming into being of a new identity, which is often, at first, unexpected, surprising, disturbing." Ascott sharpens the contrast with a chiasmus: "If appearance is claimed as the face of reality, of things as they are, apparition is the emergence of things as they could be." Appearance is the stance of "classical science" and "rigid determinism," seeing "man as little more than a material object"; apparition belongs to a constructivist universe in which observers create reality (he builds the essay from Schrödinger's Cat, Greg Egan, Heinz von Foerster, and Paul Watzlawick's radical constructivism). "Apparition implies action, just as appearance implies inertia." The shift is summarised as a move "from the outward and visible look of things to the inward and emergent processes of becoming" *(Directly stated)*.

Crucially, apparition reframes what an artwork *is*. In the cybersphere — Ascott's [[Data Space]] — art is no longer a finished object but a **context** from which apparition emerges: "Instead of creating, expressing, or transmitting content, [the artist] is now involved in designing context: contexts within which the observer or viewer can construct experience and meaning." This is the same object→process spine that runs through Ascott's [[Telematic Art]] and connects to [[The Interface as Doorway]] — "Art is no longer a window onto the world but a doorway through which the observer is invited to enter into a world of interaction and transformation." Apparition is therefore inseparable from interactivity, [[Emergence]], and the narratives and technologies of [[Artificial Life]]: "Cyberspace is the space of apparition, in which the virtual and real not only co-exist, but co-evolve in a cultural complexity" *(Directly stated)*.

Across the later essays apparition becomes Ascott's general name for what a networked, [[Posthumanism|post-biological]] art produces. In "The Planetary Collegium" the apparitions are "the constructions of distributed mind, the coming-into-being of new forms of human presence, half real, half-virtual." In "The Architecture of Cyberception" art "is concerned with apparition, the coming into being of what has never before been seen or heard or experienced." In the "Technoetic Aesthetics" glossary he names the whole programme "the aesthetics of apparition," and in "Weaving the Shamantic Web" he asks for "an art that favours the ontology of becoming, rather than the assertion of being" — the clearest statement that apparition is, finally, a wager on becoming over being *(Directly stated / Strongly implied)*.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — apparition is his coinage and the book's title figure: the coming-into-being of new identity, emergence, becoming, "things as they could be"; the affirmative successor to appearance/representation, realised in the cybersphere where art is a context rather than an object. He frames it both as an ontology ("the ontology of becoming") and as a programme ("the aesthetics of apparition").
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** *(precedent, not the same term)* — Burnham's 1968 object→system thesis ([[Systems Esthetics]], [[Object and System]]) reaches the same destination by a different road: "For Object Art presence is everything for Systems Art information is the key." Where Burnham dissolves the sculptural *object* into a *system of forces*, Ascott dissolves the artwork's *appearance* into a process of *apparition*. Ascott's [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] extend the cluster Burnham realized *(Interpretive synthesis)*.
- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** *(parallel)* — Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] of the art object is the conceptual-art precursor of the same shift from finite object to immaterial process, though her account is sceptical about escaping the market where Ascott's is celebratory *(Interpretive synthesis)*.

## Related Concepts

- [[Emergence]] — apparition *is* emergence given an aesthetic and ontological name; both name bottom-up coming-into-being rather than top-down composition.
- [[Telematic Art]] — the art form whose proper concern is apparition; "art in a state of endless becoming."
- [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]] — the epistemology underwriting apparition: observers construct reality, so art constructs reality ("to construct art is to construct reality").
- [[The Interface as Doorway]] — apparition's spatial figure: art as a doorway one enters, not a window one looks through.
- [[Data Space]] — "the space of apparition," where virtual and real co-evolve (Ascott's term for the network's data/virtual space).
- [[Artificial Life]] — apparition's exemplary subject matter: an art of "transformation, growth, and change."
- [[Dematerialization]] — the precursor shift from object to process that apparition completes.
- [[Posthumanism]] — apparition produces "new forms of human presence, half real, half-virtual"; a post-biological condition.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Appearance vs Apparition]] — the governing opposition: appearance (surface, representation, "things as they are," inertia, the mirror that "always lies") versus apparition (emergence, becoming, "things as they could be," action). Apparition coexists as both this **tension** and this **concept**, the same dual concept/tension pairing as [[Hell of the Same]].

## Questions It Raises

- If appearance "is a fraud" and "the mirror always lies," does Ascott leave any legitimate role for representation in art, or is the verdict total?
- Is apparition a genuine ontological claim (reality is constructed in consciousness) or an aesthetic preference dressed as one — and does the radical-constructivist framing it rests on bear that weight?
- If the artwork becomes a context from which apparition emerges, what remains of authorship, judgement, and the "skill" Ascott insists is "no less than" that of classical art?
- Does apparition's "endless becoming" describe a real condition of networked art, or does it risk making the work unjudgeable — perpetually deferred, never sufficiently stable to evaluate?
- How does Ascott's celebratory apparition relate to the same object→immaterial shift that [[Lucy Lippard]] reads as a *failure* to escape the market?
