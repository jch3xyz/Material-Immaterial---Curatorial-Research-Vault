# Machine Intelligence

## Brief Definition

Shoshana Zuboff's umbrella term for surveillance capitalism's computational "means of production" — machine learning, classical algorithmic production, predictive analytics, and "AI" — that feeds on [[Behavioral Surplus]] and processes it into [[Prediction Products]] forecasting what we will feel, think, and do.

## Longer Explanation

For Zuboff, "machine intelligence" names not a single technology but the whole twenty-first-century apparatus of computation that surveillance capitalism deploys as its productive engine. It is the "umbrella phrase" she adopts for the "Analysis" stage of Hal Varian's "data extraction and analysis" — "the complex of highly specialized computational systems that I will generally refer to in these chapters as 'machine intelligence'" — chosen because it "trains us on the forest rather than the trees, helping us decenter from technology to its objectives" (Chapter 3). Following Google's own usage, the term "includes machine learning as well as 'classical' algorithmic production, along with many computational operations that are often referred to with other terms such as 'predictive analytics' or 'artificial intelligence'" (`Directly stated`). It is what converts the raw material of [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the surplus behavioral data left over after service improvement — into the prediction products sold in [[Behavioral Futures Markets]]. Crucially, machine intelligence is *trained* by the surplus it consumes: Zuboff cites Kevin Kelly's suggestion that Google develops Search less to improve Search than as "a means of continuously training its evolving AI capabilities," and calls this "the essence of the machine intelligence project" (`Directly stated`).

The concept carries a distinctive economic logic. Where industrial capitalism faced "a tension between quantity and quality" in its raw materials, machine intelligence dissolves that tension. Its quality *is* a function of quantity: "As the ultimate tapeworm, the machine's intelligence depends upon how much data it eats" (`Directly stated`). Because more behavioral surplus yields more accurate predictions, the apparatus has no natural stopping point — "it reaches its full potential for quality only as it approximates totality." This drive toward totality is what couples machine intelligence to the *extraction imperative*: the system must render economies of scale in the supply of surplus "automatic and ubiquitous" (Chapter 3), pushing operations toward comprehensiveness, omnipresence, and eventually the saturation of everyday life. The "ultimate tapeworm" is therefore not merely hungry but structurally insatiable (`Strongly implied` — the totality claim and the extraction imperative are stated; their joint reading as a drive toward ubiquity is interpretive synthesis).

Zuboff places machine intelligence inside an explicit Marxian frame as the surveillance economy's *new means of production*. The convergence of "behavioral surplus, data science, material infrastructure, computational power, algorithmic systems, and automated platforms" amounts, she writes, to "an expensive, sophisticated, opaque, and exclusive twenty-first-century 'means of production'" (`Directly stated`). This places machine intelligence on the *immaterial* pole of the vault's central opposition: the productive apparatus is informational and computational rather than the mills, machines, and labor of industrial production — a re-siting of the means of production from matter to data that connects Zuboff to the object→system spine running through [[Material vs Immaterial]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).

Within Zuboff's larger architecture, machine intelligence is the *means*, [[Behavioral Surplus]] is the *raw material*, and [[Prediction Products]] are the *outputs*: "Machine intelligence processes behavioral surplus into prediction products designed to forecast what we will feel, think, and do: now, soon, and later." These methods are among the most closely guarded corporate secrets — a black box whose opacity is itself part of the asymmetry of knowledge and power that defines [[Surveillance Capitalism]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — *machine intelligence* is her umbrella term for the computational means of production proper to surveillance capitalism: machine learning, classical algorithmic production, predictive analytics, and "AI" that eat [[Behavioral Surplus]] and excrete [[Prediction Products]]. The "ultimate tapeworm" whose quality dissolves the industrial quantity/quality tension and so reaches full potential only as it approximates totality.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (resonance, not the same term) — Han's [[Smart Power]] and his account of [[Big Data]] / [[Dataism]] describe a kindred apparatus of data-driven prediction and psychopolitical steering; where Han stresses the seductive, freedom-exploiting *modality* of control, Zuboff names the *economic* engine that produces the predictions (`Speculative connection` — Han and Zuboff do not cite each other; the parallel is interpretive).

## Related Concepts

- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the raw material machine intelligence consumes; the "ultimate tapeworm's" food.
- [[Prediction Products]] — the manufactured output machine intelligence fabricates from surplus.
- [[Behavioral Futures Markets]] — where the prediction products are traded; the demand that drives surplus capture toward ubiquity.
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the downstream apparatus that uses predictions to actuate behavior; machine intelligence is its predictive core.
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic order for which machine intelligence is the means of production.
- [[Big Data]] — the data substrate; Han/Harari name the worldview, Zuboff names the productive use to which the data is put.
- [[Dataism]] — the ideology that all of reality is best processed as data; machine intelligence is its commercial instrument.
- [[Smart Power]] — Han's kindred mode of data-driven, predictive, non-coercive control.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the rendering of experience into the behavioral data that machine intelligence eats.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — machine intelligence re-sites the means of production from the *material* mills and machines of industrial capitalism to the *immaterial* apparatus of data, computation, and algorithm, while still depending on a vast material infrastructure; it sits on, and complicates, this tension.

## Questions It Raises

- If quality reaches its potential "only as it approximates totality," is there any internal limit to surveillance capitalism's drive toward ubiquity, or only external (legal, political, collective) ones?
- Does the "means of production" framing license a labor-theoretic critique of surveillance capitalism, or does the fact that we supply raw material rather than labor (Zuboff: "we are not the product... we are the objects from which raw materials are extracted") break the Marxian analogy?
- Is machine intelligence's dissolution of the quantity/quality tension a genuine economic novelty, or a restatement of older scale economies in informational dress?
- How does Zuboff's "machine intelligence" relate to the philosophical debates over [[Strong AI]] and machine consciousness — is it deliberately agnostic about intelligence in the cognitive sense, naming only a productive capability?
