# Liberal Humanism (Harari)

## Brief Definition

For [[Yuval Noah Harari]], liberal humanism is the orthodox branch of the humanist religion: the creed that each human being is a unique individual with a distinctive inner voice and a never-to-be-repeated string of experiences, and that the free will of that individual is the highest authority of all — the sect that won the twentieth-century "war of religion" because "the supermarket proved to be far stronger than the gulag."

## Longer Explanation

Liberal humanism is one of three sects into which [[Humanism (Harari)|Harari's humanism]] splits. Where humanism in general "worships humanity, and expects humanity to play the part that God played in Christianity and Islam," its *orthodox* branch reads "the human experience" as an irreducibly *individual* phenomenon. Harari states it directly: "The orthodox branch holds that each human being is a unique individual possessing a distinctive inner voice and a never-to-be-repeated string of experiences." Every person is "a singular ray of light, which illuminates the world from a different perspective" — so "individual free will should have far more weight than state interests or religious doctrines." "Due to this emphasis on liberty, the orthodox branch of humanism is known as 'liberal humanism' or simply as 'liberalism'." (`Directly stated`)

Harari renders the creed as a set of domain-specific slogans, all flowing from the same axiom that meaning and authority reside in the individual's inner experience: "It is liberal politics that believes the voter knows best. Liberal art holds that beauty is in the eye of the beholder. Liberal economics maintains that the customer is always right. Liberal ethics advises us that if it feels good, we should go ahead and do it. Liberal education teaches us to think for ourselves, because we will find all the answers within us." This is what marks liberal humanism off from its sibling sects: socialist humanism redirects attention from my feelings to *everyone's* ("the party knows best," "the trade union is always right"), and evolutionary humanism ranks experiences hierarchically ("some humans are simply superior to others"). All three agree "that human experience is the supreme source of authority and meaning"; they disagree on whether that experience is individual, collective, or unequal. (`Directly stated`)

The historical core of Harari's account is the "war of religion" framing. The three sects, once their differences "seemed rather frivolous," "eventually flared up into the deadliest war of religion in history." "From 1914 to 1989 a murderous war of religion raged between the three humanist sects, and liberalism at first sustained one defeat after the other" — fascism and communism took over numerous countries, and "the core liberal ideas were exposed as naïve at best." Then "everything changed. Liberal democracy crawled out of history's dustbin, cleaned itself up and conquered the world. The supermarket proved to be far stronger than the gulag." This compressed image — consumer abundance defeating the labour camp — is Harari's verdict on *why* liberalism won: not philosophical sophistication but its fit with the information age and the free market. "In the early twenty-first century, this is the only show in town." (`Directly stated`)

Harari's framing is deliberately *theological*: liberalism is a "creed," a "sect," an "orthodoxy," with "commandments" and a "founding myth." This is the register that distinguishes the `(Harari)` suffix here from [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] as Hayles uses it. For Harari, liberal humanism is a *religion of the self* whose god is the free, feeling individual; he frames it to set up its coming obsolescence — once [[Dataism]] and the new biotechnologies "reveal their full potential," "liberalism, democracy and free markets might become as obsolete as flint knives, tape cassettes, Islam and communism." The note that liberalism "still sanctifies individual liberties above all, and still has a firm belief in the voter and the customer" is, in Harari's argument, the very thing the data religion is poised to dissolve. (`Directly stated` for the theological register and the obsolescence claim; `Interpretive synthesis` for the framing of `(Harari)` vs Hayles.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (*Homo Deus*) — the orthodox/individualist branch of the humanist religion; the creed of the unique inner voice, maximal free will, and "the voter knows best / the customer is always right." Treated as a *religion* that won the 1914–1989 war of sects and now faces its own undoing. Distinct from, but resonant with, the Hayles/O'Gieblyn [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]].
- **Distinct from [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]]** — in [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] (and [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]) the "liberal humanist subject" names a *constructed subject-position* — the autonomous, rational, self-possessing individual grounded in Macpherson's possessive individualism, analysed for its erasure of [[Embodiment]]. Harari's "liberal humanism" names the *creed/religion* that sanctifies that individual's free will. The `(Harari)` suffix marks the religion register; the bare title marks the subject-position.

## Related Concepts

- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — the umbrella religion of which this is the orthodox branch; liberal humanism is one of three sects.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the constructed Enlightenment subject (Hayles/O'Gieblyn) whose free will liberal humanism worships; same figure, different register.
- [[Dataism]] — the emerging techno-religion that, in Harari's argument, threatens to render liberal humanism obsolete by recoding free choice as a calculable, designable product.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Socialist Humanism (Harari)]] — the sibling sect that subordinates individual feeling to the collective ("the party knows best," "the trade union is always right"); a rival in the same war of religion.
- [[Evolutionary Humanism (Harari)]] — the sibling sect that ranks human experiences hierarchically and applauds conflict; the other rival, "whose most famous advocates were the Nazis."

## Questions It Raises

- If liberal humanism is a *religion* rather than a neutral political philosophy, what would count as its "death," and is [[Dataism]] its successor faith rather than its refutation?
- Harari claims the supermarket beat the gulag because liberalism fit the information age — does the same logic predict that whatever fits the *next* technological order (algorithmic governance) will bury liberalism in turn?
- Does Harari's individualist creed (the orthodox branch) survive the critique that the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal subject]] was never autonomous to begin with — that its "free choices" were already, in Hayles's and O'Gieblyn's senses, conditioned and computable?
