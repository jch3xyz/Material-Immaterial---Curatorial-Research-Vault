# The Distributed Self

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's account of selfhood in the Net: the unitary, undivided personality dissolves into the multiple, distributed presences of many selves — *l'homme éclaté*, Paul Virilio's "exploded man" — such that the explosion of the one and the connectivity of the many become the single most important effect of the telematisation of culture.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, the arrival of telematic networks does not merely give the existing individual new tools; it transforms what an individual *is*. "The individual human presence of the individual human self, a unitary and undivided personality, has become the multiple, distributed presences of a set of many selves, of multileveled, complex, diverse personalities—l'homme éclaté, as Paul Virilio has called it." The bounded, hermetically sealed person of Western thought — "the single and solitary, whose boundary and isolation was hermetically complete" — gives way to a connective impulse. Ascott frames this as an epochal inversion: "It could be argued that the appearance of the zero in Western thought has been equalled in importance only by the disappearance of the one." The distributed self is therefore not a loss but, in his reading, an evolutionary advance toward "mind at large." `Directly stated`

The distributed self is constituted by *telepresence*. Through online living and remote meetings in cyberspace, presence is dispersed across many sites at once: "Telepresence means instant global interaction with a thousand communities, being in any one of them, or all of them, virtually at the same time." Ascott calls this "the disseminated self," replacing "the unitary mind and solitary consciousness of the old order." As presence is distributed, the supports of the old self give way too: "human presence becomes virtualised and the individual self is distributed; space becomes virtualised and place becomes distributed." The self is now "both present and absent, here and elsewhere, all at the same time." `Directly stated`

This distribution is bound up with Ascott's wider claim that the body and embodiment are being left behind as the measure of reality. "Through telematic networks, our presence is distributed... It seems that embodied representation is giving way to disembodied reconstruction of ourselves." In his lexicon of technoetic terms, the "Cyberself" is defined directly: "We are each made up of many selves: de-centred, distributed, and constructively schizophrenic. We are the embodiment of technoetic relativity." Identity becomes "Non-linear": "I connect, therefore I am multiple" — a deliberate rewriting of the Cartesian cogito in which connectivity, not solitary thought, founds the self. `Directly stated`

Ascott reads this as the end of the Freudian myth of psychic unity: "Freud is dead and the myth of the unified individual has been destroyed. We are each made up of many selves: de-centred, distributed, and tele-schizophrenic." Where psychology presupposed an integrated ego to be healed, Ascott embraces a "constructively schizophrenic" multiplicity as the native condition of the networked subject. The distributed self is thus both a diagnosis of what telematics does to personhood and a value Ascott affirms — the celebratory counterpart to the same disembodiment that other authors in the vault treat as domination or loss. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — The distributed self is the networked subject of the telematic age: a unitary personality exploded into many de-centred, connected presences via telepresence. Ascott welcomes it as evolutionary, naming the explosion of the one and the connectivity of the many "the single most important effect of the telematisation of our culture."
- **Paul Virilio** (forward-stub; no book in `raw/`) — supplies the phrase Ascott adopts, *l'homme éclaté*, the "exploded man." Ascott borrows the figure affirmatively, where Virilio's own usage carries a more critical charge about acceleration and dislocation.

## Related Concepts

- [[Fractured Identities]] — Haraway's cyborg refusal of a natural, unified self is the political-feminist precursor to Ascott's networked multiplicity; both reject the bounded liberal individual, though Ascott reframes the fracture as telematic evolution rather than coalition politics.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the distributed self is Ascott's posthuman subject: consciousness exceeding the bounded organism, "leaving behind embodiment as the primary measure and sign of reality."
- [[The Cyborg]] — Ascott's "cyborg living" and the "Cyberself" extend the human–machine hybrid into the question of how many selves one networked person can be.
- [[Virtuality]] — distribution operates through virtualised presence and place; the self is "here and not here," present as virtual presence in virtual spaces.
- [[Embodiment]] — the distributed self is defined against embodiment as the measure of reality; "embodied representation is giving way to disembodied reconstruction of ourselves."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, bounded, self-identical individual of Western humanism is exactly what Ascott declares dissolved; the distributed self is its undoing ("the disappearance of the one").

## Questions It Raises

- If the self is genuinely distributed and multiple, who or what is the locus of responsibility, memory, and continuity across its many presences?
- Ascott embraces the "constructively schizophrenic" self as liberation; is this an emancipation from the "chains of trauma," or a euphemism for the dispersal and capture of the subject that surveillance-critical authors diagnose?
- Can a self be distributed without first being disembodied, or is the distribution of presence inseparable from the abandonment of the body as "the primary measure and sign of reality"?
