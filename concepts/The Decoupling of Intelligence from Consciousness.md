# The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness

## Brief Definition

Harari's thesis that intelligence (the ability to solve problems) and consciousness (the capacity for subjective experience) — which throughout history always travelled together — are now coming apart, because computers and algorithms perform intelligence-demanding tasks with no inner experience whatsoever, leaving humans liable to lose their value once "intelligence is mandatory but consciousness is optional."

## Longer Explanation

The wedge is seeded in Chapter 3 (titled "The Human Spark"), where Harari uses the autonomous car to show that high-level, real-time problem-solving requires no felt experience at all: the car "successfully stops at red lights, bypasses obstacles and keeps a safe distance from other vehicles – without feeling any fear… The car does all that without any problem – but without any consciousness either" (`Directly stated`). The point is generalised — "Many other computer programs make allowances for their own actions, yet none of them has developed consciousness, and none feels or desires anything" — which dismantles the old hope that self-modelling or intelligence is the *cause* of consciousness, and prepares the reader for the great divergence to come.

That divergence becomes the spine of Chapter 9 ("The Great Decoupling"). Harari observes an asymmetry in the recent history of computing: "Over the last decades there has been an immense advance in computer intelligence, but there has been exactly zero advance in computer consciousness." From this he draws the book's pivotal claim: "Humans are in danger of losing their value, because intelligence is decoupling from consciousness" (`Directly stated`). Historically the two were welded together — "Until today, high intelligence always went hand in hand with a developed consciousness. Only conscious beings could perform tasks that required a lot of intelligence, such as playing chess, driving cars, diagnosing diseases or identifying terrorists." But "we are now developing new types of non-conscious intelligence that can perform such tasks far better than humans," which forces a question that "was just a pastime for philosophers" into the centre of politics and economics: "which of the two is really important, intelligence or consciousness?" Harari's blunt answer, "at least for armies and corporations," is the formula at the heart of this concept: "intelligence is mandatory but consciousness is optional" (`Directly stated`). The richness of a human taxi driver's inner life — enjoying music, awe at the stars, tears at a child's first step — is real but economically surplus; "the system doesn't need all that from a taxi driver" (`Directly stated`).

In Chapter 10 ("The Ocean of Consciousness") the decoupling is restated as the *motive* of techno-humanism. Because Homo deus must "hold its own even against the most sophisticated non-conscious algorithms," Harari writes, "Since intelligence is decoupling from consciousness, and since non-conscious intelligence is developing at breakneck speed, humans must actively upgrade their minds if they want to stay in the game" (`Directly stated`). The same proposition is finally elevated to one of the three grand processes overshadowing all others in Chapter 11 ("The Data Religion"), listed simply as: "Intelligence is decoupling from consciousness" (`Directly stated`). The decoupling is thus the connective tissue running from the critique of the "human spark," through the economic obsolescence argument, to the case for self-upgrade and the rise of [[Dataism]].

### Consciousness as Epiphenomenon / Mental Pollution

The decoupling rests on a prior, stronger move in Chapter 3: stripping consciousness of any demonstrable function. "The better we understand the brain, the more redundant the mind seems," Harari writes; "If a chain of electrochemical reactions leads all the way from the nerve cells in the eye to the movements of leg muscles, why add subjective experiences to this chain? What do they do?" (`Directly stated`). He notes that "99 per cent of bodily activities… take place without any need of conscious feelings," and that no known algorithm — mathematical or computational — requires subjective experience to run: "none of the data-processing systems we have created needs subjective experiences in order to function, and none feels pain, pleasure, anger or love." The most radical formulation he reports is that consciousness is a by-product with no causal role at all: "Consciousness is the biologically useless by-product of certain brain processes… consciousness may be a kind of mental pollution produced by the firing of complex neural networks. It doesn't do anything. It is just there" (`Directly stated`, as a theory Harari relays as "the best theory of consciousness that contemporary science has to offer"). If consciousness does no work, then intelligence never needed it — and its separation from intelligence is not a future surprise but the unmasking of a contingent historical pairing. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

A boundary matters here: Harari does *not* claim that consciousness is unreal or worthless. He insists the opposite about its reality — pain "is a direct and very tangible reality" — and grants that consciousness "may actually have great moral and political value." The decoupling is therefore a claim about *function and substitutability*, not about existence or value: intelligence can be detached from consciousness and outsourced to silicon, even though consciousness remains the thing on which "the whole edifice of modern politics and ethics is built." (`Strongly implied`.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — coins and carries the phrase across *Homo Deus*: consciousness and intelligence are conceptually distinct, were historically fused, and are now coming apart because non-conscious algorithms can out-perform conscious humans at pattern-recognition tasks. Its primary home is Chapter 9 ("The Great Decoupling"); it depends on the prior thesis that [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] (so intelligence is substrate-independent) and feeds directly into the value-collapse of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] and the rise of [[Dataism]].
- **Relation to the hard problem (Chalmers, via O'Gieblyn).** The decoupling presupposes that there is something — subjective experience — that intelligence can be *separated from*; that "something extra" is exactly what [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] names. Harari treats the hard problem as unsolved ("nobody has any idea how a congeries of biochemical reactions and electrical currents in the brain creates the subjective experience of pain, anger or love") and turns that very ignorance into the ground for setting consciousness aside as functionally optional. (`Interpretive synthesis` — the cross-author bridge; the hard-problem note is to be extended separately.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the substrate-independence premise that makes the decoupling possible: if intelligence is algorithmic, it can run on silicon without carbon's accompanying consciousness.
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — names the "extra" (subjective experience) from which intelligence is being decoupled; the decoupling is parasitic on the hard problem remaining unsolved.
- [[Dataism]] — the techno-religion the decoupling licenses: once consciousness is optional, value migrates from conscious beings to data-processing efficiency.
- [[Can It Suffer (The Reality Test)]] — the obverse criterion: where intelligence is the economically salient capacity, the *moral* test Harari reserves for consciousness is the capacity to suffer.
- [[Transhumanism]] / [[Evolutionary Humanism]] — the decoupling is restated as techno-humanism's motive: humans "must actively upgrade their minds if they want to stay in the game."
- [[Patternism]] — both share the assumption that mind reduces to information-processing patterns indifferent to their material carrier.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — directly threatened: liberalism ascribes ultimate authority to the conscious individual's inner experience, but if intelligence is decoupled from (and out-performs) consciousness, "the system" needs the intelligence and not the experiencing self, so "Humans are in danger of losing their value."

## Questions It Raises

- If intelligence can be fully realised without consciousness, what — if anything — is consciousness *for*? Harari's own answer ("we have no clue") leaves the function of subjective experience an open lacuna.
- "Which of the two is really important, intelligence or consciousness?" — once the question leaves philosophy for economics and politics, who decides, and on whose behalf?
- If "the system doesn't need" the inner life of the human worker, on what non-economic basis can the value of conscious experience be defended?
- Does treating consciousness as "mental pollution" — a useless by-product — entail that the suffering and joy of conscious beings carries no weight, or does Harari's insistence on its reality and moral value block that inference?
