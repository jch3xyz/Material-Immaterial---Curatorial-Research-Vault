# Pattern and Randomness

## Brief Definition

The master dialectic of *How We Became Posthuman*: the proposition that information is "a pattern rather than a presence," paired with the paradoxical recognition that information must be identified with both pattern *and* its apparent opposite, randomness — a coupling Hayles tracks as an epistemic shift displacing the Western dialectic of presence and absence.

## Longer Explanation

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], the pattern/randomness dialectic is the conceptual engine of the posthuman. Its premise is the information-theoretic definition of information as form rather than substance: information "is conceptually distinct from the markers that embody it," so that it "is a pattern rather than a presence, defined by the probability distribution of the coding elements composing the message." If information is pattern, common sense expects its negation — "noninformation" — to be the *absence* of pattern, "that is, randomness." But Hayles's whole point is that this common-sense opposition collapses. Within information theory, the average information of a source reaches a maximum "when there is no pattern or when the message is at the extreme of randomness"; following Warren Weaver's reading of Shannon, "information should be understood as depending on both predictability and unpredictability, pattern and randomness." Information is thus equated with both terms at once. This is the [[Information (Hayles)|informational]] paradox at the root of the concept.

The paradox is generative rather than merely logical. "Identifying information with both pattern and randomness proved to be a powerful paradox, leading to the realization that in some instances, an infusion of noise into a system can cause it to reorganize at a higher level of complexity." Within such a system, Hayles writes, "pattern and randomness are bound together in a complex dialectic that makes them not so much opposites as complements or supplements to one another. Each helps to define the other; each contributes to the flow of information through the system." Randomness is not the simple lack of pattern but the contrastive ground against which pattern becomes legible: "only against the background of nonpattern can pattern emerge." Hayles names the catastrophe internal to this dialectic *mutation* — "the bifurcation point at which the interplay between pattern and randomness causes the system to evolve" in a new direction — positioning it as the pattern/randomness analogue of castration within the older presence/absence tradition.

Hayles's larger claim is historical and epistemic. As information technologies saturate everyday life, "the interplay between pattern and randomness became a feature of everyday life," and "discursive formations based on pattern and randomness jostle and compete with formations based on presence and absence." She insists the surprise is "not that formations based on them continue to exist but that these formations are being displaced so rapidly across a wide range of cultural sites." The shift toward pattern/randomness is what she elsewhere calls the "contemporary pressure toward dematerialization" — but she is careful that the dialectic "does not erase the material world," since "information in fact derives its efficacy from the material infrastructures it appears to obscure." This guards the concept against being read as a celebration of disembodiment; it is the axis along which the [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy and the disagreement over [[Embodiment vs Information]] are fought.

In the book's mapping chapter, Hayles formalizes pattern/randomness as one of two axes of a semiotic square, treating its relation to presence/absence as "one of exclusion rather than opposition" and arguing that "pattern/randomness tells a part of the story that cannot be told through presence/absence and vice versa." The synthetic term emerging from "the interplay between pattern and randomness" is, simply, *information* itself. By the Conclusion, the dialectic's valence has shifted decisively: where pattern had "traditionally been the privileged term," randomness is reframed as "the creative ground from which pattern can emerge," even "invested with plenitude." In this final inflection, pattern/randomness underwrites a [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] in which "meaning is not front-loaded into the system," origins do not ground signification, and complex systems "evolve toward an open future marked by contingency and unpredictability." It is the conceptual frame, not the [[Flickering Signifier|semiotic instantiation]]; that figure is how the dialectic shows up in the signifier.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — pattern/randomness as the dialectic that information theory installs at the heart of communication; first as a paradox (information is both pattern and randomness), then as a complementary dialectic (noise drives reorganization at higher complexity), finally as an epistemic regime displacing presence/absence and, in the Conclusion, reframing randomness as the plenitude-bearing creative ground.
- **Claude Shannon and Warren Weaver** (as cited by Hayles) — the information-theoretic source: information defined by probability distribution, with average information maximized at the extreme of randomness; Weaver's gloss that information depends on "both predictability and unpredictability, pattern and randomness."
- **Self-organization and complexity theorists** (Chris Langton, Stuart Kauffman, Francisco Varela, Henri Atlan, as cited by Hayles) — the Conclusion's chorus who "agree in seeing randomness not simply as the lack of pattern but as the creative ground from which pattern can emerge."
- **Gregory Bateson** (epigraph and cited by Hayles) — communication as "the creation of redundancy or patterning"; randomness as "what exists outside the confines of the box in which a system is located."

## Questions It Raises

- If information is "a pattern rather than a presence," what becomes of the body once it is recoded as informational pattern — and does the pattern/randomness regime make [[The Body as Data]] thinkable in the first place? (Ties to the body as data.)
- Hayles insists the dialectic "does not erase the material world." Can pattern/randomness be embraced without the "systematic devaluation of materiality and embodiment" she warns against — i.e., can [[Embodiment vs Information]] be held as complementarity rather than antagonism?
- If randomness is reframed by the Conclusion as "the creative ground from which pattern can emerge," does this redeem the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] from the disembodiment fantasy, or does investing noise with "plenitude" simply relocate the metaphysics of presence into the realm of pattern?
- For a curatorial practice concerned with the [[Material vs Immaterial]] divide, what is the difference between an artifact valued as *presence* (the singular, located object) and one valued as *pattern* (the replicable, accessible code) — and what does the shift "from ownership to access" do to the status of the original?
- Is "mutation" — "the bifurcation point at which the interplay between pattern and randomness causes the system to evolve" in a new direction — a model of creativity that depends on embodiment, or one that bypasses it?
