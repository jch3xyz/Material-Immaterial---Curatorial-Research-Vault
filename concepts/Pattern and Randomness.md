# Pattern and Randomness

## Brief Definition

The master dialectic of *How We Became Posthuman*: the proposition that information is "a pattern rather than a presence," paired with the paradoxical recognition that information must be identified with both pattern *and* its apparent opposite, randomness — a coupling Hayles tracks as an epistemic shift displacing the Western dialectic of presence and absence.

## General Orientation

"Pattern" is shared vocabulary across this note, but its senses diverge sharply and should not be collapsed. They sort into distinct camps by what each makes of the relation between pattern and its apparent opposite — randomness, noise, the aleatory.

**Pattern/randomness as a critical dialectic.** For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], pattern is *one pole* of a paradoxical coupling in which information is identified with both pattern and its apparent opposite, randomness. The valence is critical: Hayles uses the dialectic to expose, and guard against, the fantasy that informational pattern can be cleanly extracted from material embodiment. This is the developed sense of the present note — the conceptual engine of the posthuman, traced from a paradox (information is both pattern and randomness) through a complementary dialectic (noise drives reorganization at higher complexity) to an epistemic regime that displaces presence/absence.

**Pattern as the warrant for immortality.** [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] inherits Hayles's word but bends it toward the opposite valence. Tracing [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s functionalist account of the self — the *[[Patternism]]* he names for himself, in which the self is a persisting, transferable computational pattern — she shows pattern recruited not to expose the dematerialization fantasy but to fulfill it. This celebratory, immortalist inflection has its own developed concept note ([[Patternism]], in kinship with [[Mind as Machine]] and [[The Computational Universe]]); it is cross-linked here, not merged. O'Gieblyn herself flags it, citing Hayles, as the ideology in which disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form — the very privileging of pattern over embodiment that Hayles warns against.

**Pattern as a bias of the perceiving mind.** O'Gieblyn's second inflection is cognitive rather than metaphysical: *apophenia*, the mind as a meaning-making organ that overproduces pattern, "imagining signal where there is mostly noise." This relocates *signal* and *noise* from the information channel to the perceiver, supplying a human-scale mirror of the dialectic — pattern is legible only against a ground of nonpattern, yet the mind is built to read pattern even where the ground is in fact random.

**Pattern/randomness at the level of the art object.** [[Jack Burnham]] arrives at the same axis from the side of sculpture rather than information theory — the opposition between programmed (determinate) and aleatory (random) motion in kinetic and light art, the [[Determinism vs Randomness]] tension. His light artists treat statistically-leveled randomness as the generative substrate of a perceptually stable pattern, an artifact-level rhyme with Hayles's reframing of randomness as the creative ground from which pattern emerges, reached without invoking information theory.

## Longer Explanation

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], the pattern/randomness dialectic is the conceptual engine of the posthuman. Its premise is the information-theoretic definition of information as form rather than substance: information "is conceptually distinct from the markers that embody it," so that it "is a pattern rather than a presence, defined by the probability distribution of the coding elements composing the message." If information is pattern, common sense expects its negation — "noninformation" — to be the *absence* of pattern, "that is, randomness." But Hayles's whole point is that this common-sense opposition collapses. Within information theory, the average information of a source reaches a maximum "when there is no pattern or when the message is at the extreme of randomness"; following Warren Weaver's reading of Shannon, "information should be understood as depending on both predictability and unpredictability, pattern and randomness." Information is thus equated with both terms at once. This is the [[Information (Hayles)|informational]] paradox at the root of the concept.

The paradox is generative rather than merely logical. "Identifying information with both pattern and randomness proved to be a powerful paradox, leading to the realization that in some instances, an infusion of noise into a system can cause it to reorganize at a higher level of complexity." Within such a system, Hayles writes, "pattern and randomness are bound together in a complex dialectic that makes them not so much opposites as complements or supplements to one another. Each helps to define the other; each contributes to the flow of information through the system." Randomness is not the simple lack of pattern but the contrastive ground against which pattern becomes legible: "only against the background of nonpattern can pattern emerge." Hayles names the catastrophe internal to this dialectic *mutation* — "the bifurcation point at which the interplay between pattern and randomness causes the system to evolve" in a new direction — positioning it as the pattern/randomness analogue of castration within the older presence/absence tradition.

Hayles's larger claim is historical and epistemic. As information technologies saturate everyday life, "the interplay between pattern and randomness became a feature of everyday life," and "discursive formations based on pattern and randomness jostle and compete with formations based on presence and absence." She insists the surprise is "not that formations based on them continue to exist but that these formations are being displaced so rapidly across a wide range of cultural sites." The shift toward pattern/randomness is what she elsewhere calls the "contemporary pressure toward dematerialization" — but she is careful that the dialectic "does not erase the material world," since "information in fact derives its efficacy from the material infrastructures it appears to obscure." This guards the concept against being read as a celebration of disembodiment; it is the axis along which the [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy and the disagreement over [[Embodiment vs Information]] are fought.

In the book's mapping chapter, Hayles formalizes pattern/randomness as one of two axes of a semiotic square, treating its relation to presence/absence as "one of exclusion rather than opposition" and arguing that "pattern/randomness tells a part of the story that cannot be told through presence/absence and vice versa." The synthetic term emerging from "the interplay between pattern and randomness" is, simply, *information* itself. By the Conclusion, the dialectic's valence has shifted decisively: where pattern had "traditionally been the privileged term," randomness is reframed as "the creative ground from which pattern can emerge," even "invested with plenitude." In this final inflection, pattern/randomness underwrites a [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] in which "meaning is not front-loaded into the system," origins do not ground signification, and complex systems "evolve toward an open future marked by contingency and unpredictability." It is the conceptual frame, not the [[Flickering Signifier|semiotic instantiation]]; that figure is how the dialectic shows up in the signifier.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — pattern/randomness as the dialectic that information theory installs at the heart of communication; first as a paradox (information is both pattern and randomness), then as a complementary dialectic (noise drives reorganization at higher complexity), finally as an epistemic regime displacing presence/absence and, in the Conclusion, reframing randomness as the plenitude-bearing creative ground. The critical, anti-disembodiment use of the axis, and the developed sense of this note.
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — two inflections that borrow Hayles's word but bend it. *Patternism*: [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s self-applied label for the self as a persisting computational pattern, where pattern is celebratory and immortalist — the inverse valence to Hayles's critical dialectic (developed in its own note, [[Patternism]], and cross-linked here). *Apophenia*: the mind as a pattern-detecting organ that imagines "signal where there is mostly noise," the cognitive counterpart of the information-theoretic pattern.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — the artifact-level realization of the axis in kinetic and light art: the determinate (programmed) and the aleatory (random) as rival solutions to generating time-based form, the [[Determinism vs Randomness]] tension. Schöffer's "continuous discontinuity" — statistically-leveled randomness as the generative ground of a perceptually stable pattern — rhymes with Hayles's reframing of randomness as the creative ground from which pattern emerges, reached through sculpture rather than Shannon-Weaver information theory.
- **Claude Shannon and Warren Weaver** (as cited by Hayles) — the information-theoretic source: information defined by probability distribution, with average information maximized at the extreme of randomness; Weaver's gloss that information depends on "both predictability and unpredictability, pattern and randomness."
- **Self-organization and complexity theorists** (Chris Langton, Stuart Kauffman, Francisco Varela, Henri Atlan, as cited by Hayles) — the Conclusion's chorus who "agree in seeing randomness not simply as the lack of pattern but as the creative ground from which pattern can emerge."
- **Gregory Bateson** (epigraph and cited by Hayles) — communication as "the creation of redundancy or patterning"; randomness as "what exists outside the confines of the box in which a system is located."

## Patternism and Apophenia (O'Gieblyn)

[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] inherits Hayles's word *pattern* but routes it through two distinct inflections that pull in opposite directions from the critical dialectic above. The first is celebratory and immortalist. Tracing [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s functionalist account of the self, O'Gieblyn notes that [[Ray Kurzweil|Kurzweil]] "calls himself a 'patternist,'" believing "consciousness is a pattern of information, a biological configuration of energy and matter that persists over time" — not the cells and neurons but "the computational patterns that make up our sensory systems, our attention system, and our memories, which together form the distinctive algorithm that we think of as our identity." The figure is the same fluid-pattern image [[Norbert Wiener]] used ("we are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves"), but the valence is inverted: where Wiener, "like Heraclitus, was emphasizing the transience of identity," O'Gieblyn observes that "for Kurzweil, patternism was precisely what made possible the most decisive form of permanence: immortality." This is the *opposite* of Hayles's guarded, anti-disembodiment use of pattern/randomness — pattern recruited not to expose the dematerialization fantasy but to fulfill it ("a pattern, after all, is essentially computational, which means it can, at least in theory, be transferred onto a computer"). Because this inflection has its own developed concept note, it is cross-linked here rather than merged: see [[Patternism]] (and its kinship with [[Mind as Machine]] and [[The Computational Universe]]). O'Gieblyn herself flags, citing Hayles directly, that this is "an ideology … in which 'disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form,'" the same critique Hayles levels at the privileging of pattern over [[Embodiment|embodiment]] and [[The Materiality of Informatics|material instantiation]].

The second inflection is cognitive rather than metaphysical: *apophenia*, the mind as a meaning-making organ that overproduces pattern. Reflecting on the run of coincidences and "doubling" she experienced, O'Gieblyn writes that "our brains have evolved to detect patterns and attribute significance to events that are entirely random, imagining signal where there is mostly noise." This is the psychological counterpart of the information-theoretic vocabulary — *signal* and *noise* relocated from the channel to the perceiving mind — and it supplies a human-scale mirror of Hayles's dialectic: pattern is only legible against a ground of nonpattern, but the mind is biased to read pattern even where the ground is in fact random. O'Gieblyn pushes the point toward pathology, citing John Nash's slide from "the conviction that the world was rational" into a state where "everything had meaning, everything had a reason, nothing was random or coincidental." The two O'Gieblyn inflections are linked to but distinct from the Hayles axis: patternism wagers that the self *is* a pattern stable enough to survive transfer; apophenia warns that the patterns we perceive may be artifacts of a perceiver built to find them — "imagining signal where there is mostly noise."

## Jack Burnham (Beyond Modern Sculpture)

[[Jack Burnham]] arrives at the pattern/randomness problem from the side of the art object rather than information theory, but his kinetic and light art turn on the same axis — here named as the opposition between programmed (determinate) and aleatory (random) motion, the [[Determinism vs Randomness]] tension. For Burnham, the central unsolved technical-aesthetic problem of the Kinetic revival is *programming*: a fully deterministic control is so demanding that artists default to chance. As he puts it, "any kind of detailed and precise programming, demands more engineering ability from artists than a decision to let randomness run its course." The deterministic, "closed kinematic chain" of the first age of machines was Kineticism's deep root and its limitation; the New Tendency's escape was to let statistical, field-distributed randomness carry the motion, which is why Burnham can describe the determinate program and the aleatory field as rival solutions to the same problem of generating time-based form. (`Directly stated`.)

The light artists push the same opposition toward a positive aesthetic of structured randomness. Burnham reports Nicolas Schöffer's preference for indeterminate light sequences that go on indefinitely, "never quite the same but always appearing the same" — a regime Schöffer names *continuous discontinuity* and which Burnham frames as "the statistical leveling-off of random phenomena," explicitly held to be "superior to a repetitive determinate program." (`Directly stated`.) This is a near-exact artifact-level rhyme with the Hayles dialectic above: where Hayles treats randomness not as the simple absence of pattern but as "the creative ground from which pattern can emerge," Burnham's light artists treat statistically-leveled randomness as the generative substrate of a perceptually stable pattern — the determinate program reframed not as the ideal but as the impoverished case. The convergence is a resonance across very different vocabularies (sculpture-as-system vs. information theory), not a citation; Burnham (1968) does not invoke Shannon-Weaver information theory in these passages. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Questions It Raises

- If information is "a pattern rather than a presence," what becomes of the body once it is recoded as informational pattern — and does the pattern/randomness regime make [[The Body as Data]] thinkable in the first place? (Ties to the body as data.)
- Hayles insists the dialectic "does not erase the material world." Can pattern/randomness be embraced without the "systematic devaluation of materiality and embodiment" she warns against — i.e., can [[Embodiment vs Information]] be held as complementarity rather than antagonism?
- If randomness is reframed by the Conclusion as "the creative ground from which pattern can emerge," does this redeem the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] from the disembodiment fantasy, or does investing noise with "plenitude" simply relocate the metaphysics of presence into the realm of pattern?
- For a curatorial practice concerned with the [[Material vs Immaterial]] divide, what is the difference between an artifact valued as *presence* (the singular, located object) and one valued as *pattern* (the replicable, accessible code) — and what does the shift "from ownership to access" do to the status of the original?
- Is "mutation" — "the bifurcation point at which the interplay between pattern and randomness causes the system to evolve" in a new direction — a model of creativity that depends on embodiment, or one that bypasses it?
