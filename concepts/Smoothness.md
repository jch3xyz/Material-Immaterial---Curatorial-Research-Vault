# Smoothness

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], smoothness is the surface property of [[Transparency]] — the leveled, frictionless, uninterrupted condition that matters, bodies, images, language, and space assume once all [[Negativity]] (otherness, secrecy, threshold, ambivalence) has been stripped away.

## General Orientation

Within Han's own corpus, "smooth" carries two related but distinct registers, and this note holds both. The first, developed in *The Transparency Society* (below, "Longer Explanation"), is **aesthetic and ontological**: smoothness as the leveled surface-texture of transparency, the condition things assume when negativity, the veil, and the threshold are dissolved. The second, developed in *Psychopolitics*, is **political**: smoothness as the texture of the neoliberal "technology of power" — power that has gone "subtle, supple and smart," that operates by pleasing rather than prohibiting, and that meets the subject halfway instead of erecting obstacles. The two senses converge: in both, the elimination of friction, resistance, and the no is what makes circulation (of capital, communication, and subjugation) frictionless. The political section makes explicit what the aesthetic section implies — that a smooth surface is not neutral but a mode of operation. See [[Smart Power]] for the dedicated treatment of this technology of power.

## Longer Explanation

In *The Transparency Society*, smoothness names not a quality of finish so much as an ontological state: things become transparent precisely insofar as they are "smoothed out and leveled," able to circulate without resistance in the "smooth streams of capital, communication, and information" (Ch1). Smoothness is therefore inseparable from the dismantling of negativity that Han identifies as the defining gesture of the [[Positivity|Society of Positivity]]. What resists, interrupts, or remains ambiguous obstructs the smooth — and is dissolved.

Across the book, smoothness recurs as a single operation taking different objects. In [[Communication]], it is a "smoothing and leveling effect" that yields synchronization, uniformity, and the elimination of [[The Other|Otherness]] (Preface). In the [[Pornographic Body]], it is the absence of interruption: "The pornographic body is smooth. Nothing interrupts it. Interruption produces ambivalence, ambiguity" (Ch4) — and ambivalence, for Han, is the very tissue of the erotic. In [[Transparent Space]], it is what acceleration requires: "Space becomes transparent when it is smoothed, leveled, and emptied out" (Ch5), so that the pilgrim's threshold-rich path collapses into the tourist's empty passage. Han also reads Barthes's "unary" photograph through this register: pornographic images "are smooth and transparent, and they reveal no breaks, no ambiguity" (Ch4), lacking the [[Punctum]] that would wound the gaze.

The political stakes of smoothness lie in its violence. Because smoothness eliminates the gap, the threshold, the veil — every site where meaning, eroticism, narrative tension, and the Other might still appear — it produces what Han calls an "inferno of the same" (Ch1). Smoothness is the texture by which transparency conducts its leveling: it is how Gleichschaltung looks at the level of surface. Han hints that this aesthetic register extends beyond this book; the connection between smoothness and the loss of beauty, eros, and singularity becomes a sustained theme in his later writing (see *Saving Beauty*, *Non-Things* — Speculative connection; not addressed in this volume).

## Psychopolitics (Han) — smart power is smooth and permissive

In *Psychopolitics*, smoothness migrates from the aesthetic surface of transparency to the operational texture of power itself. Han contrasts disciplinary power, which is "commanded by negativity" and whose "mode of articulation is inhibitive, not permissive," with the neoliberal regime, which "beams forth in positivity." The decisive passage gives smoothness a directly political function: "The neoliberal regime’s technology of power takes on subtle, supple and smart forms; thereby, it escapes all visibility." Smart power is smooth in exactly the sense the *Transparency* note describes — frictionless, resistance-free, invisible — but here the consequence is that "the subjugated subject is not even aware of its own subjugation," so that "the subject thinks itself free" (`Directly stated`). The smooth surface is no longer merely a leveled aesthetic; it is the medium by which domination disappears from view (cross-link [[Smart Power]], [[Positivity]]).

What makes this technology smooth is its permissivity. Power "is assuming increasingly permissive forms," and in its "friendliness - power is shedding its negativity and presenting itself as freedom." Where disciplinary power expends energy forcing people "into the straightjacket of commandments and prohibitions," smart power "makes sure that people subordinate themselves to power relations on their own," seeking "to activate, motivate and optimize - not to inhibit or repress." It is frictionless because it does not work frontally: "It says ‘yes’ more often than ‘no’; it operates seductively, not repressively." The chapter's most precise statement of the smooth texture of power is its mode of guidance: smart power "leads astray instead of erecting obstacles," and rather than standing opposed to the subject, "smart and friendly power meets the subject halfway" (`Directly stated`). This is the smooth as a political method — not the obstacle, the threshold, or the no, but the seductive surface that the will glides along without noticing.

The link to the *Transparency* register is structural, not merely verbal. There, smoothness was what let matters "not resist being integrated into smooth streams of capital, communication, and information"; here, the same absence of resistance ("As such, it has no need to overcome resistance. ... It simply happens") is what lets power operate without expenditure or visibility. The friction that disciplinary power had to overcome — the will that "manages to form and turn against the power-holder" — is precisely the negativity that smooth power has dissolved. Smoothness is thus the shared signature of transparency and smart power alike: in both, the elimination of the negative is what makes circulation effortless, and effortlessness is what makes it total. Han's emblem for this convergence is [[The Like Button]] — power's "signal and seal" — under which "people subjugate themselves to domination by consuming and communicating" while clicking Like all the while (`Strongly implied` that this is continuous with the aesthetic smoothness of *The Transparency Society*; the two books do not cross-reference the term explicitly).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Smoothness is the surface signature of transparency: leveled, uninterrupted, frictionless. It applies to communication, bodies, images, language, time, and space, and is always paired with the elimination of negativity, otherness, and the veil.
- **[[Guy Debord]]** — Speculative connection. Han closes Ch4 with "The society of pornography is a society of the spectacle," echoing Debord; whether Debord's spectacle is "smooth" in Han's specific sense would need to be argued from *The Society of the Spectacle*. Not asserted in this book.
- **[[Roland Barthes]]** — Han borrows Barthes's reading of the "unary" photograph (lacking [[Punctum]]) as paradigmatically smooth and transparent (Ch4). The concept of smoothness as Han uses it here is interpretive synthesis built on Barthes's vocabulary, not Barthes's own term.

## Related Concepts

- [[Transparency]] — smoothness is its surface phenomenology; transparency is what smoothness produces and presupposes.
- [[Positivity]] — smoothness is positivity made tactile; both name the dismantling of negativity.
- [[Leveling]] — Han pairs "smoothing" and "leveling" almost as a single operation (Preface, Ch1, Ch5).
- [[Acceleration]] — smoothness is the condition of possibility for the accelerated circulation of capital, information, and communication (Ch1, Ch5).
- [[Pornography (Han)]] — pornographic images and bodies are Han's paradigm of the smooth (Ch4).
- [[The Same]] — smoothness is how the "chain reaction of likeness" propagates without resistance (Ch1).
- [[Exhibition Value]] — smoothness is the texture of the wholly exhibited (Ch4).
- [[Unary Image|Unary Image (Barthes)]] — Han's appropriated term for the smooth, breakless, puncta-less photograph (Ch4).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — the negative (gap, withdrawal, refusal, interruption) is precisely what smoothness eliminates.
- [[The Other|Otherness]] — the Other is what disturbs the smooth communication of the Same (Ch1, Preface).
- [[The Veil]] — for Han via Benjamin, beauty requires veil and veiling; smoothness is the condition where "the secret vanishes in favor of total exhibition and bareness" (Ch4).
- [[Threshold]] — meanings arise "at thresholds and in transitions, indeed, through obstacles" (Ch5); smoothness levels the threshold.
- [[Punctum]] — the wound, the rift, the interruption of the studium; smooth images "reveal no breaks, no ambiguity" (Ch4).
- [[Ritual]] / [[Procession]] — narrative, scenographic, unaccelerable forms whose tact resists smoothing (Ch5).
- [[Eroticism]] — "There is no erotics of transparency" (Ch4); the erotic photo is "disturbed, fissured."

## Questions It Raises

- Is smoothness a metaphor, a phenomenology, or an ontology in Han? He uses tactile language ("smoothed out") but applies it to language, time, and space — where the tactile cannot strictly hold.
- How does smoothness relate to the digital interface specifically? Han hints at this in the Preface ("the human being glassy") but does not name the touchscreen here; the link is developed in his later work (Speculative connection).
- If smoothness is the surface condition of capital's frictionless circulation, what counter-aesthetics would resist it? Han gestures toward [[Distance]], [[Shame]], the [[The Veil|Veil]], and the [[Threshold]], but stops short of an affirmative program.
- Does Han's smoothness/roughness opposition reproduce a romantic aesthetic of friction, or does it propose something more specific? (Interpretive synthesis — not addressed directly in the book.)
