# Conquest by Declaration

## Brief Definition

Surveillance capitalism's founding strategy of self-asserted legitimacy: a cascade of six rights "pulled from thin air" — modeled on Columbus's three-phase "conquest pattern" and the Spanish *Requirimiento* — by which a new reality is imposed first and consent manufactured afterward, with terms-of-service agreements standing in for monarchical edicts as "faux-legitimation."

## Longer Explanation

Conquest by declaration is [[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s name for the way the facts of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] were brought into being: not negotiated, not licensed, but simply *declared*, and then defended until the declaration stood as fact. She reads it through John Searle's philosophy of language, in which a declaration is "a particular way of speaking and acting that establishes facts out of thin air, creating a new reality where there was nothing." A declaration fuses describing the world with changing it — "asserting a new reality by describing the world as if a desired change were already true" — so that, in Searle's formula, "We make something the case by representing it as being the case" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). Because Searle holds that "All of institutional reality, and therefore… all of human civilization is created by… declarations," declarations are "inherently invasive": they "impose new facts on the social world while their declarers devise ways to get others to agree to those facts," and so "rise or fall on the strength of others' acceptance of the new facts" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). The full Searlean machinery — the soup-bowl example, brute versus institutional fact, the declaration as a speech act — belongs to the companion definition note; here the concept is the *use* surveillance capital makes of that machinery.

Zuboff grounds the strategy in a historical template: the "conquest pattern" set in motion when Columbus made landfall in 1492, "a design that unfolds in three phases: the invention of legalistic measures to provide the invasion with a gloss of justification, a declaration of territorial claims, and the founding of a town to legitimate and institutionalize the conquest" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). The legalistic gloss had its purest instrument in the *Requirimiento*, the Monarchical Edict of 1513 that Spanish soldiers were tasked to read aloud to indigenous villagers before attacking them — a declaration that summoned "native people… in a language they could not fathom to surrender without resistance in recognition of authorities they could not conceive," after which "the way was clear for pillage and enslavement" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). The incomprehensible edict, mumbled into the conquistadors' beards in the dark, is Zuboff's prototype for the unread, incomprehensible click-through agreement.

The conceptual payload is the six-declaration cascade, which Zuboff says "should sound familiar because the facts of surveillance capitalism have been carried into the world on the strength of six critical declarations pulled from thin air when Google first asserted them" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). The six are a staircase of self-conferred rights, each standing on the one below it: (1) "We claim human experience as raw material free for the taking" — the founding move of [[Digital Dispossession]]; (2) the right "to take an individual's experience for translation into behavioral data" — the act of [[Behavioral Surplus|rendition]]; (3) the right "to own the behavioral data derived from human experience"; (4) "the right to know what the data disclose"; (5) "the right to decide how we use our knowledge" — the seizure of [[Decision Rights]]; and (6) the right "to the conditions that preserve our rights to take, to own, to know, and to decide" — the self-defense of the whole edifice (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). The architecture is load-bearing and brittle at once: "each declaration builds on the one before it. If one falls, they all fall" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6) — which is why Zuboff treats the declarations as a single integrated target for democratic challenge, not six separable claims.

The strategy's signature is that it does not seek permission. "These twenty-first-century invaders do not ask permission; they forge ahead, papering the scorched earth with faux-legitimation practices. Instead of cynically conveyed monarchical edicts, they offer cynically conveyed terms-of-service agreements whose stipulations are just as obscured and incomprehensible" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). Trust is rendered unnecessary: as Zuboff observes, "Eric Schmidt asked for trust, but Google's 'declarations' ensured that it did not require our trust to succeed" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). The final phase of the pattern — Columbus's "founding of a town" — recurs as the building of "towns in intricate ecosystems of commerce, politics, and culture that declare the legitimacy and inevitability of all that they have accomplished" (`Directly stated`, Ch.6). Conquest by declaration is thus the *mechanism* by which dispossession is normalized: declaration is how the unilateral taking acquires, retroactively, the appearance of law. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (Ch.6). The originating and only vault use. Zuboff builds the concept by layering John Searle's theory of the declarative speech act onto historian Matthew Restall's account of the colonial "conquest pattern" and the *Requirimiento*, then mapping both onto Google's six self-asserted rights. The concept is her explanation for *how* [[Digital Dispossession]] succeeds without consent — the rhetorical and legal-aesthetic surface ("faux-legitimation") over the underlying extraction.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — conquest by declaration is the strategy that inaugurates it; the six declarations "defined it as an age of conquest."
- [[Digital Dispossession]] — the substance the declarations legitimate; the first declaration ("human experience as raw material free for the taking") *is* the founding act of dispossession.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — produced by the second declaration's right "to take an individual's experience for translation into behavioral data."
- [[The Dispossession Cycle]] — the operational counterpart: incursion-habituation-adaptation-redirection is *how* a declaration is made to stick once asserted.
- [[The Extraction Imperative]] — the economic pressure that drives the serial issuing of new declarations and "the next incursion."
- [[Decision Rights]] — what the fifth declaration unilaterally seizes ("the right to decide how we use our knowledge").
- [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]] — a parallel legitimation tactic (post-9/11 license) operating alongside the declarations.

- [[Quantified Self]] — the rendition of experience into data that the second and third declarations claim as their property.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Right to Sanctuary]] — the counter-claim of an inviolable space the declarations refuse to recognize.
- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the human right to act on one's own behalf that the declarative seizure of decision rights overrides.

## Questions It Raises

- If institutional reality is, with Searle, made by declarations, what countervailing declaration could a democratic public issue to make the six fall?
- Does the colonial analogy illuminate or overstate — are "we" the native peoples whose self-determination has vanished, or does the metaphor obscure differences between territorial and experiential conquest?
- Are terms-of-service agreements genuinely "faux-legitimation," or does their legal enforceability make them something more than the mumbled *Requirimiento*?
- Since the declarations "rise or fall on the strength of others' acceptance," what counts as acceptance — clicking "I agree," or merely failing to resist?
