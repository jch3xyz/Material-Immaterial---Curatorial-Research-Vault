# Knowledge = Experiences × Sensitivity

## Brief Definition

Harari's formula for humanism's epistemology: the modern method for attaining ethical and aesthetic knowledge, in which we connect to our inner experiences and observe them with the utmost sensitivity — the third in a historical sequence after the medieval *Knowledge = Scriptures × Logic* and the scientific *Knowledge = Empirical Data × Mathematics*.

## Longer Explanation

In *Homo Deus*, Yuval Noah Harari presents three successive "formulas for knowledge," each tied to a source of authority. The medieval formula was `Knowledge = Scriptures × Logic`: to answer a question one read scriptures and used logic to interpret the text correctly. The Scientific Revolution proposed `Knowledge = Empirical Data × Mathematics`: gather relevant empirical data and analyse it mathematically. That scientific formula produced astounding breakthroughs but, Harari stresses, "had one huge drawback: it could not deal with questions of value and meaning." No amount of data can prove that it is wrong to murder, yet societies cannot survive without such value judgements. (Directly stated.)

Humanism supplies the missing third formula. As humans gained confidence in themselves, `Knowledge = Experiences × Sensitivity` appeared as the way to attain *ethical* knowledge: to answer any ethical question we connect to our inner experiences and observe them with the utmost sensitivity. Harari defines the two terms carefully. An "experience" is not empirical data but a subjective phenomenon with three ingredients — sensations, emotions and thoughts. "Sensitivity" means both paying attention to those sensations, emotions and thoughts, and allowing them to influence and change us. The two build one another in a never-ending cycle, illustrated by Harari's parable of learning to taste tea: sensitivity is a practical skill that ripens only through a long string of experiences. (Directly stated.)

This epistemology founds what Harari calls "the humanist view of life as a string of experiences," which "has become the founding myth of numerous modern industries, from tourism to art." Travel agents and chefs do not sell flight tickets or dinners but "novel experiences"; modern novels, films and poems revolve around feelings rather than external deeds. The highest aim of humanist life becomes to develop one's knowledge through "a large variety of intellectual, emotional and physical experiences" — Harari quotes Wilhelm von Humboldt that the aim of existence is "a distillation of the widest possible experience of life into wisdom." (Directly stated.) This relocation of meaning into experience parallels the relocation of value Han traces from cult to display: a culture that prizes lived experience above the deed underwrites an economy of [[Exhibition Value]]. (Interpretive synthesis.)

The formula also transforms heavy matters such as war. Harari argues it "has changed not just our popular culture, but even our perception of weighty issues like war." Where war narratives once asked God, scriptures, kings and priests whether a war was just — focusing on emperors, generals and heroes — the limelight has shifted over the last two centuries onto the common soldier and his experiences. The truth of war is relocated to the soldier's eyes: in Tom Lea's *That 2,000 Yard Stare* and Otto Dix's *Der Krieg*, meaning does not emanate from tactical movements or divine proclamations but from looking "straight into the eyes of the common soldiers." (Directly stated.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — coins the three-formula schema in *Homo Deus*. The formula is humanism's epistemology, paired with humanism's relocation of authority from a "great cosmic plan" to "the experiences of humans." It is explicitly an instrument for *ethical and aesthetic* knowledge (the humanist "yin"), complementary to the scientific formula (the "yang") rather than replacing it.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — does not use Harari's formula, but his distinction [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)]] names a fault line *inside* Harari's "experience." Where Harari's formula prizes the accumulation of experiences as such, Han's worry is that modern life multiplies *Erlebnis* (point-like, consumable experience) at the expense of *Erfahrung* (transformative experience that changes the one who undergoes it). Harari's own "sensitivity" — experiences "permit[ting] them to change my views, my behaviour and even my personality" — is closer to Han's *Erfahrung*. (Speculative connection — Han is not cited by Harari.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Exhibition Value]] — Harari's "life as a string of experiences" as the founding myth of tourism and art is the economic-aesthetic correlate of a culture organized around display and the experience economy.
- [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)]] — names the internal split within "experience" that Harari's formula leaves unmarked.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the self presupposed by the formula: an inner self with authentic feelings that is the ultimate source of meaning and authority.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the emptying of the exterior cosmos ("the exterior universe ... became empty space") that makes the inner world the sole reservoir of meaning.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Re-enchantment]] — the formula presupposes a disenchanted outer world and locates all meaning within human experience; projects of re-enchantment push back against precisely this inward relocation of the sacred.
- The medieval `Knowledge = Scriptures × Logic` and the scientific `Knowledge = Empirical Data × Mathematics` — the two prior formulas Harari sets this one against; the humanist formula is defined by its difference from both.

## Questions It Raises

- If ethical knowledge is `Experiences × Sensitivity`, what settles a conflict when one person's experience says "good" and another's says "bad"? (Harari notes humanism's three branches — liberal, socialist, evolutionary — diverge precisely here.)
- Is the "experience" the formula valorizes Han's transformative *Erfahrung* or merely accumulable *Erlebnis* — and does the experience economy systematically substitute the latter for the former?
- If the truth of war lives in the common soldier's eyes rather than in tactics or doctrine, what authority remains for collective or political judgements about whether a war is just?
- Does relocating all meaning into inner experience (the formula's premise) make [[Re-enchantment]] of the outer world impossible, or its inevitable compensatory reaction?
