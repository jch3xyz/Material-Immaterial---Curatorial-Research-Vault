# Idiotism

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], **idiotism** is the affirmative counter-figure that closes *Psychopolitics*: against total communication and total surveillance, the **idiot** — heretic, outsider, fool — is a practice of freedom, "unallied, un-networked, and uninformed," who alone "has access to the wholly Other."

## Longer Explanation

Idiotism is the final word of *Psychopolitics* and the book's only sustained affirmation. After diagnosing smart/friendly power, emotional capitalism, gamification, and the self-exploiting achievement-subject, Han turns to the figure that escapes psychopolitics altogether. He reaches it through Deleuze, who in his 1980 Spinoza course observed that "they play the fool [ils font les idiots] . . . Playing the fool, that's always been one function of philosophy." On this basis Han makes the history of philosophy "a history of idiotisms": Socrates ("knows only that he does not know") and Descartes (the *cogito* as "idiotic") are idiots because every thinker who brings forth a new idiom must break with the prevailing system. The reward of that break is access to what intelligence cannot reach: "Only the idiot has access to the wholly Other." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The *practice* and the *figure* are co-extensive — idiotism is what the idiot does, and the idiot is one who practises idiotism — which is why this note carries **The Idiot** as an alias. Han characterizes the idiot privatively, by everything he is not: "By nature, the idiot is unallied, un-networked, and uninformed." He "inhabits the immemorial outside, which escapes communication and networking altogether." He is *idiosyncratic*: idiosyncrasy is "an immunological defence against the [[The Other|Other]]" that "stands in the way of unbounded communicative exchange," and so the smooth, accelerated circulation of the Same requires its suppression. In this way idiotism is the precise inverse of the [[Hell of the Same]]: where communication "achieves maximum velocity when the Same reacts to the Same," the idiot's recalcitrant otherness deliberately troubles that velocity. (Label: `Directly stated` for the quotations; `Interpretive synthesis` for the framing as inverse of the Hell of the Same — Han states the mechanism but the synthesis joins the two notes.)

Idiotism is therefore explicitly political: "Idiotism stands opposed to the neoliberal power of domination: total communication and total surveillance." Its mode is not counter-speech but silence. The idiot "does not 'communicate'"; "as such, the idiot veils himself in silence," and idiotism "erects spaces for guarding silence (Freiräume des Schweigens), quiet, and solitude, where it is still possible to say what really deserves to be said." Han enlists Deleuze's 1995 "politics of silence" against psychopolitics precisely because "Repressive forces don't stop people from expressing themselves but rather force them to express themselves" — so the act of resistance is withdrawal, "the right to say nothing," not more disclosure. Against the horizontal plane of [[Hypercommunication]] and networking, the idiot gains "the vertical dimension inasmuch as he takes leave of the prevailing system." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The idiot is, finally, "a modern-day heretic." Han turns on the etymology: "heresy means 'choice'. Thus, the heretic is one who commands free choice, the courage to deviate from orthodoxy." This lets him separate freedom from intelligence: intelligence is "choosing-between (inter-legere)," but it is "system-immanent" and "can only select among the offerings the system affords," so it "has no access to what is wholly Other." Genuine free choice belongs to the heretic-idiot who quits the system. Beneath this lies the chapter's metaphysical ground in Deleuze's late [[Immanence and A Life|"A Life"]]: a plane of pure singularity that "can neither be psychologized nor subjectivized," upon which "No order of domination can be erected." The idiot is "not defined by individuality or subjectivity, but by singularity," "similar in nature to the child, who is not yet an individual, not yet a person." Idiotism is thus the affirmative face of *Psychopolitics*' [[Negativity]]: a de-subjectivation that wrests life out of the colonized psyche and back into immanence. (Label: `Directly stated` for quotations; `Interpretive synthesis` for reading idiotism as the affirmative face of negativity and the book's positive conclusion.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (*Psychopolitics*, Ch. 13) — idiotism as a *practice of freedom* and resistance against neoliberal psychopolitics; the idiot as heretic, outsider, silent, idiosyncratic, un-networked, with access to the wholly Other and the "immemorial outside." Han's own contribution is to politicize the figure against "total communication and total surveillance" and to read silence and idiosyncrasy as immunological resistance to acceleration.
- **Gilles Deleuze** (cited) — the source of Han's figure: "playing the fool" as a function of philosophy (1980 Spinoza course); the **old idiot vs. the new idiot** in *What Is Philosophy?* ("The old idiot wanted truth, but the new idiot wants to turn the absurd into the highest power of thought"); the **politics of silence** (*Negotiations*, "Mediators," 1995); and **"A LIFE" / pure immanence** (*Pure Immanence: Essays on a Life*). Han adopts and politicizes Deleuze rather than departing from him.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Other]] — the idiot alone "has access to the wholly Other"; idiosyncrasy is "an immunological defence against the Other." Idiotism is Han's name for keeping the Other available.
- [[Immanence and A Life]] — the Deleuzian plane of "A LIFE" on which "No order of domination can be erected"; the metaphysical ground of idiotism (singularity, the child, de-subjectivation).
- [[Negativity]] — the sphere of immanence is named "negativity, wresting the subject out of itself"; idiotism is the affirmative, practised form of Han's negativity.
- [[Society of Control]] — Deleuze's "politics of silence" is aimed at the same neoliberal/control-society lineage; idiotism is its counter-practice.
- [[Smoothness]] — the idiot's idiosyncrasy and otherness are the friction that "troubles and impedes the smooth communication of the Same."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Hypercommunication]] — idiotism "stands opposed to . . . total communication"; the idiot "does not 'communicate'" and withdraws into silence on the horizontal plane of networked exchange.
- [[Acceleration]] — communication and capital accelerate only through "immunosuppression" of idiosyncrasy; the idiot's recalcitrant otherness is what acceleration must suppress.
- [[Hell of the Same]] — "In the Inferno of the Same, communication attains its highest speed." The idiot, as bearer of otherness, is the antidote to the Same reacting to the Same.

## Questions It Raises

- Is idiotism a genuine politics or a privatized, monastic retreat? Han calls it "a practice of freedom," but its mode is silence, solitude, and withdrawal — can the "immemorial outside" become collective, or is it irreducibly singular?
- If "total communication and total surveillance" is the field of domination, is non-communication actually escapable, or does opting out (silence, going dark) itself become legible to the system?
- How does idiotism's appeal to "the wholly Other" relate to Han's earlier valorizations of negativity, the veil, and distance — is the idiot simply negativity made into a subject-position (or a *non*-subject-position)?
- Does grounding resistance in Deleuze's "A Life" / pure immanence make idiotism unpolitical by definition — a plane on which "no order of domination can be erected" because no order of anything can be?
