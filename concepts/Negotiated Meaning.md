# Negotiated Meaning

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's thesis that in telematic process meaning is never given or transmitted but is continually co-created in the interaction between participants — "endlessly reconstituted and redefined" — so that truth, "always relative," resides not in any fixed location but in the networking process itself.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, the defining trait of telematic art is not its technology but its ontology of meaning. Where the older communication model imagines a stable message dispatched from a sender to a receiver, telematics dissolves that dyad into "users" who together generate sense. Meaning, on this account, is not a content to be conveyed but an event of relation: it comes into being through ambiguity, uncertainty, and incompleteness, and it remains permanently open to revision. Ascott states this most directly in "Concerning Nets and Spurs" (1985): "meaning is not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined; truth, always relative, does not lie in an absolute location but is embedded in process" (`Directly stated`). This is the hinge that connects telematics to poststructuralism — meaning is deferred, intertextual, and never finally present — and it is what licenses Ascott's parallel claims about [[Distributed Authorship]] and the death of the single "Author God."

Negotiated meaning is best understood as the *how* and the ontology of meaning, distinct from [[Distributed Authorship]], which concerns the *who*. Dispersed authorship names the distribution of creative agency across all participants (or across "the system of interaction itself"); negotiated meaning names the mode in which sense is constituted within that distributed field — relationally, processually, never as transfer. In "Art and Education in the Telematic Culture" (1988) Ascott draws the distinction sharply by importing the biology-of-cognition critique of communication: against the "tube" or conduit picture, in which something is generated at one point, carried, and delivered intact, he insists that "meaning is created out of interaction between people, rather than being 'something' that is sent from one to another" (`Directly stated`). The boundary lines "between human, computer, and environment," he adds (following Bateson), are "purely artificial and fictitious . . . they are the lines across the pathways along which information passes and within which meaning is created" — not the walls of a container holding meaning in place.

This makes negotiated meaning a direct counter to the Shannon strand of [[Information]]. Ascott names the model he rejects: there are "no 'senders' and 'receivers' (as in the old models of information theory) but only 'users.'" He recruits Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela in support — on their structural-determinist account "communication depends on not what is transmitted, but what happens to the person who receives it. And this is a very different matter from 'transmitting information'" — converting the transmission/conduit model from a description of communication into a "basically false" metaphor (`Directly stated`). Where the [[Information]] note carries the Shannon sender/receiver/channel lineage, this concept marks the explicit refusal of that lineage as a theory of *meaning* (as opposed to a theory of signal): for Ascott, telematic networks do not move meanings around, they occasion their construction.

Negotiated meaning therefore extends the participatory-system lineage the vault already grounds in Burnham. Burnham's [[Reciprocal Communication]] established two-way exchange as the condition of the art-as-system; Ascott radicalises it by locating meaning *only* in the reciprocal act, "embedded in process" rather than encoded in any object or message. The artwork, accordingly, "does not reside in the object alone, but in a system, a negotiation between artist and spectator in the creation of meaning" — the through-line from Burnham's object→system spine into the networked, telematic register, and the experiential core of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the source of the concept. Meaning in telematic process is "not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined"; truth is relative and "embedded in process," "telematically inscribed in the networking that is human behaviour at its most liberated." It is "created out of interaction between people, rather than being 'something' that is sent from one to another." Ascott explicitly opposes this to the sender/receiver model of information theory and grounds it in poststructuralism (Barthes, Derrida) and the biology of cognition (Maturana and Varela).
- **Maturana and Varela (via Ascott)** — Ascott borrows their structural-determinist refusal of the conduit/tube metaphor: what matters is "not what is transmitted, but what happens to the person who receives it." This is the cited theoretical backstop for negotiated meaning's anti-transmission ontology. (Both remain forward-stub figures.)
- **Roland Barthes / Jacques Derrida (via Ascott)** — the poststructuralist correlate Ascott invokes: the text as "a tissue of quotations" with no single "theological" message, and meaning as deferred (différance). Negotiated meaning is the telematic enactment of this literary-theoretical insight (`Strongly implied`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Distributed Authorship]] — the companion concept: dispersed authorship is the *who* (creative agency distributed across all participants, or the system itself as "author"); negotiated meaning is the *how/ontology* (sense co-created in interaction, never transmitted). They are two faces of the same telematic dissolution of the singular author and the fixed message.
- [[Telematic Art]] — negotiated meaning is the defining trait of telematic art's process; the art "is all process, all system."
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — Burnham's two-way-communication thesis, which Ascott extends: meaning lives *only* in the reciprocal act, not in any transmitted content.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — the Bateson "human + computer + environment is a thinking system" frame within which meaning "is created"; negotiated meaning is its semantic dimension.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the planetary scale at which negotiated meaning operates ("richly layered fields of 'meaning' being created" when systems are activated globally).
- [[New Media Art]] · [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the artwork as a system in which the spectator co-creates meaning rather than receiving it.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Information]] — specifically its Shannon strand. Ascott explicitly rejects "the old models of information theory" with their "senders" and "receivers"; for him meaning is not transmitted signal but co-constituted relation. The transmission/conduit metaphor is "basically false" as an account of meaning (`Directly stated`).

## Questions It Raises

- If meaning is "always relative" and "endlessly reconstituted," what stabilises shared understanding enough for collaboration to occur at all — or is provisional, partial agreement precisely the point?
- Can the transmission model (Shannon) and the negotiation model (Ascott) be reconciled as accounts of *signal* versus *meaning*, or are they genuinely rival ontologies?
- If "the system of interaction itself" can be the author of meaning, where does responsibility for what is meant reside?
- Does negotiated meaning's celebration of flux underestimate the asymmetries of power that shape who gets to negotiate — the "monopolisation and colonisation of electronic space" Ascott elsewhere warns against?
