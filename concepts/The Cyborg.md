# The Cyborg

## Brief Definition

The cyborg is the figure produced when biological organism and cybernetic device are fused — at once a literal technological object and a discursive formation — whose existence violates the boundaries between human and machine, human and animal, and animate and inanimate. In *How We Became Posthuman* it is the second of the book's three interrelated stories.

## Longer Explanation

For Hayles the cyborg is not merely a science-fiction icon but a doubled entity that "manifest[s] itself as both technological object and discursive formation." This doubleness is constitutive, not incidental. Were the cyborg only discourse, it could be relegated to science fiction; were it only a technological practice, it could be confined to bionics and prosthetics. Because it is both, it "partakes of the power of the imagination as well as of the actuality of technology" — and so, Hayles insists, "Cyborgs actually exist." She borrows the concept directly from [[Donna Haraway]]'s "A Manifesto for Cyborgs," for whom cyborgs are "simultaneously entities and metaphors, living beings and narrative constructions." The cyborg is therefore the privileged site where the figural and the material meet, which is exactly why it carries the weight of Hayles's larger argument about [[Material vs Immaterial]] entanglement.

What makes the cyborg theoretically explosive is the set of distinctions it dissolves. Fusing cybernetic device and biological organism, "the cyborg violates the human/machine distinction"; replacing cognition with neural feedback, "it challenges the human-animal difference"; explaining thermostats and people alike through theories of feedback and control, "it erases the animate/inanimate distinction." These violations follow from the cybernetic premise — dramatized in Gregory Bateson's question about whether a blind man's cane is part of the man — that systems are constituted by flows of information rather than by epidermal surfaces. Once a prosthesis that *compensates* for a deficiency slides, "over the space of a comma," into a device that *enhances* normal functioning, the conceptual limits to becoming-cyborg collapse, and "body boundaries are up for grabs."

In the Prologue, Hayles locates the cyborg's birth at the inaugural scene of the computer age. The Turing test, she argues, splits the human into an enacted body "present in the flesh on one side of the computer screen" and a represented body "produced through the verbal and semiotic markers constituting it in an electronic environment." "This construction necessarily makes the subject into a cyborg, for the enacted and represented bodies are brought into conjunction through the technology that connects them." The cyborg is thus produced wherever a technological interface joins the body one *is* to the body one is *represented as* — which is why Hayles can write "You are the cyborg, and the cyborg is you." Crucially, the cyborg is distinct from [[Posthumanism]]: the posthuman is the *point of view* that follows from this splicing, while the cyborg is the *figure* in which the splice is embodied.

Hayles's cyborg is also a historical construction with a specific biography. In her reading of [[Norbert Wiener]], the cyborg is forged during "the foundational period of the late 1940s and 1950s" out of an uneasy alliance among cybernetics, liberal humanism, and possessive individualism. Wiener "labor[s] mightily to construct the mirror of the cyborg," urging us to see ourselves as control-communication devices — and then, glimpsing himself in that mirror, "makes a horrified withdrawal." The cyborg therefore carries both erotic fascination ("pleasurably tight coupling" between parts not supposed to touch) and acute anxiety, because the same fusion that promises to extend the autonomous liberal self also undermines its foundations. This ambivalence is what gives the cyborg its charge as "the stage on which are performed contestations about the body boundaries that have often marked class, ethnic, and cultural differences."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — In "A Manifesto for Cyborgs," the cyborg is a feminist figure of boundary transgression: "simultaneously entities and metaphors, living beings and narrative constructions," whose disruption of the dualisms organism/machine and nature/culture opens emancipatory political possibilities. Hayles takes her account of the cyborg's "pleasurably tight coupling" and its three violated distinctions directly from Haraway.
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — Adopts Haraway's cyborg but reframes it historically and epistemologically: as the second of the book's three stories, "created as a technological artifact and cultural icon in the years following World War II," and as the figure produced by the splicing of enacted and represented bodies through technology. For Hayles the cyborg is the hinge between the loss of information's body (the first story) and the emergence of the posthuman (the third).

## Related Concepts

- [[Posthumanism]] — the point of view the cyborg makes thinkable; the third of the three stories, distinct from the cyborg as figure
- [[Cybernetics]] — the science whose dissolution of boundaries (control, communication, information as one system) constructs the cyborg
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the mechanism by which "replacing cognition with neural feedback" the cyborg challenges the human-animal difference and joins subject to environment
- [[Embodiment]] — the enacted body that is spliced to the represented body in the cyborg conjunction
- [[The Body as Data]] — the cyborg recoded as informational pattern; the body imagined as "data made flesh"
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the body as "original prosthesis," extended at the cane/hearing-aid/firing-control splice that produces the cyborg

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the cyborg is the figure where this disagreement is staged: it can be read either as the triumph of substrate-independent information (the body as replaceable pattern) or, in Hayles's preferred reading, as proof that information "must always be instantiated in a medium." The opposition lies in which side of the splice is privileged.

## Questions It Raises

- If the cyborg is produced by joining the enacted and represented bodies through technology, does it confirm that [[Embodiment]] can be bypassed, or does its very dependence on a material interface refute the dream of disembodied information? (ties to embodiment / information vs materiality)
- When cybernetics constructs "bodies as immaterial flows of information," does the cyborg figure the [[The Body as Data|body as data]] — and what is lost when the body's plenitude is reduced to pattern? (ties to the body as data)
- Is the cyborg's erasure of the human/machine, human/animal, and animate/inanimate distinctions liberating or lethal — and does it matter whether the cyborg is "grafted onto a liberal humanist view of the self"? (ties to posthumanism)
- Because the cyborg is "both technological object and discursive formation," can its material and figural registers ever be separated — or does it permanently entangle the [[Material vs Immaterial]] divide? (ties to information vs materiality)
