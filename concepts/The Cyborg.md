# The Cyborg

## Brief Definition

The cyborg is the figure produced when biological organism and cybernetic device are fused — at once a literal technological object and a discursive formation — whose existence violates the boundaries between human and machine, human and animal, and animate and inanimate.

## General Orientation

The cyborg is the vault's name for the human-machine merger, and the authors gathered here disagree less about what it is than about what it is *for* — whether the merger is chosen or suffered, political resource or technological diagnosis, and whether anything of the prior "human" survives it. They sort into three stances.

The **political-mythic** stance is [[Donna Haraway]]'s, the originating account. In "A Cyborg Manifesto" (1985) the cyborg is an *ironic political myth* — "a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction" — built to ground a postgender, socialist-feminist politics committed to "partiality, irony, intimacy, and perversity" and "completely without innocence." Here the cyborg is normative and *chosen* ("I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess"); it is "our ontology" and "gives us our politics." [[N. Katherine Hayles]] inherits this figure directly ("As Donna Haraway has pointed out…") but reframes it as a postwar technological artifact and as the splice of enacted and represented bodies produced at the Turing test; her cyborg is descriptive-diagnostic where Haraway's is a deliberately blasphemous resource. The two share the same defining move — both use the cyborg precisely to *dissolve* the human/machine boundary, so that the cyborg "is us," with no prior, unextended self left over. (Confidence: Interpretive synthesis on the relation between the treatments; the individual quotes are Directly stated.)

The **technological-prosthetic** stance, the cyborg's prehistory, belongs to [[Marshall McLuhan]] in *Understanding Media* (1964). McLuhan never uses the word "cyborg," but his "extensions of man" — media and technologies as prostheses of the body, the senses, and finally the central nervous system — describe the same merger two decades before Haraway names it. The decisive difference is that McLuhan keeps an intact "Man" who is *being extended*: the body and its nervous system are projected outward into tools, but a recognizable human subject survives at the center of the extensions, and the merger is *suffered* through numbness and servility rather than seized as ironic affinity. He supplies the cyborg's conceptual materials while retaining the humanist subject the figure was later coined to retire. (Confidence: Interpretive synthesis on the precursor relation; McLuhan's individual quotations are Directly stated.)

The **systems-and-network** stance reads the cyborg as a circuit of exchange rather than a hybrid body, and reaches it from the art world. [[Jack Burnham]], writing in 1968, gives the earliest primary-text use of the term in the vault and the most technical definition — "a cyborg, then, is any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism" — and coins "Cyborg Art" ("the art of cybernetic organisms") as the terminal stage of sculpture's object-to-system trajectory, predating Haraway by seventeen years. [[Roy Ascott]] then pushes the figure past McLuhan's intact "Man" altogether: in his telematic writings the bounded individual dissolves into the planetary network itself — "We are all interface" — so that the cyborg becomes a networked, post-biological, transformable body. Across all five, the cyborg is the privileged site where the figural and the material meet, which is why it carries the weight of the vault's argument about [[Material vs Immaterial]] entanglement. (Confidence: Directly stated for the quoted definitions and coinages; Interpretive synthesis on the systems-to-network lineage.)

## Longer Explanation

What makes the cyborg theoretically explosive is the set of distinctions it dissolves. Fusing cybernetic device and biological organism, "the cyborg violates the human/machine distinction"; replacing cognition with neural feedback, "it challenges the human-animal difference"; explaining thermostats and people alike through theories of feedback and control, "it erases the animate/inanimate distinction." These violations follow from the cybernetic premise — dramatized in Gregory Bateson's question about whether a blind man's cane is part of the man — that systems are constituted by flows of information rather than by epidermal surfaces. Once a prosthesis that *compensates* for a deficiency slides, "over the space of a comma," into a device that *enhances* normal functioning, the conceptual limits to becoming-cyborg collapse, and "body boundaries are up for grabs."

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]] the cyborg is not merely a science-fiction icon but a doubled entity that "manifest[s] itself as both technological object and discursive formation." Were it only discourse, it could be relegated to science fiction; were it only a technological practice, it could be confined to bionics. Because it is both, it "partakes of the power of the imagination as well as of the actuality of technology" — and so, Hayles insists, "Cyborgs actually exist." She borrows the concept directly from [[Donna Haraway]], for whom cyborgs are "simultaneously entities and metaphors, living beings and narrative constructions." Hayles locates the cyborg's birth at the inaugural scene of the computer age: the Turing test splits the human into an enacted body "present in the flesh on one side of the computer screen" and a represented body "produced through the verbal and semiotic markers constituting it in an electronic environment." "This construction necessarily makes the subject into a cyborg, for the enacted and represented bodies are brought into conjunction through the technology that connects them" — which is why she can write "You are the cyborg, and the cyborg is you." The cyborg is thereby distinct from [[Posthumanism]]: the posthuman is the *point of view* that follows from the splicing, while the cyborg is the *figure* in which the splice is embodied. Hayles's cyborg is also a historical construction with a specific biography, forged in "the foundational period of the late 1940s and 1950s" out of an uneasy alliance among cybernetics, liberal humanism, and possessive individualism — carrying both erotic fascination ("pleasurably tight coupling") and acute anxiety, since the same fusion that promises to extend the autonomous liberal self also undermines its foundations.

For [[Donna Haraway]] the same hybridity is wielded as a *political* resource rather than diagnosed. "By the late twentieth century… we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism—in short, cyborgs. The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics. The cyborg is a condensed image of both imagination and material reality." The cyborg is defined by its refusals: it is postgender, has "no origin story in the Western sense," and is "resolutely committed to partiality, irony, intimacy, and perversity… completely without innocence" — the basis of [[The Refusal of Innocence]]. Because "the machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment," Haraway treats the cyborg as a tool *seized* rather than a fate suffered, enabling a politics of [[Affinity Not Identity|affinity rather than identity]] across [[The Breached Boundaries|three breached boundaries]] and aimed at "challenging the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]]."

The remaining sources approach the merger from outside this Hayles-Haraway axis. [[Marshall McLuhan]] supplies the cyborg's prosthetic materials: all [[Media as Extensions of Man|media are extensions of man]], and under electric technology the human becomes "an organism that now wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide." But his merger runs through servility — "by continuously embracing technologies, we relate ourselves to them as servo-mechanisms" — and his grammar always presupposes an intact "Man" who owns the extensions, the boundary Haraway and Hayles abolish. [[Jack Burnham]] grounds the figure in cybernetics rather than myth or epistemology: a cyborg is "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism," unified not as a hybrid body but as a *system of exchange* possessing "a capacity for reciprocal relationships with human beings" — the through-line from his [[Cyborg Art]] to Hayles's informatic cyborg, with the organism-machine parallel to Haraway developed in [[Jack Burnham and Donna Haraway]]. [[Roy Ascott]] takes the prosthetic and systems strands into the network: "The cybernet… is part of our sensory apparatus. It redefines our individual body, just as it connects all our bodies into a planetary whole," so that "the sense of the individual is giving way to the sense of the interface… We are all interface." His networked, "post-biological," transformable body is the heir of McLuhan's extended nervous system stripped of its prior unextended self, converging with [[Posthumanism]] and the transhumanist body (parallel to Haraway in [[Roy Ascott and Donna Haraway]]).

## Donna Haraway (A Cyborg Manifesto)

This is the originating account. The note above derives the cyborg secondhand, through Hayles's citation of Haraway; here it is restored from Haraway's own text. Haraway's definition is exact: "A cyborg is a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction." [[Cyborg (Haraway)|The definition]] is doubled by design — the cyborg lives in modern medicine and "modern war" as much as in "contemporary science fiction" — because "the boundary between science fiction and social reality is an optical illusion." From this Haraway draws the keystone theses that make the cyborg a *political* figure rather than a curiosity: "By the late twentieth century, our time, a mythic time, we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism—in short, cyborgs. The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics. The cyborg is a condensed image of both imagination and material reality, the two joined centers structuring any possibility of historical transformation." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

The manifesto is, in Haraway's own framing, "an effort to build an ironic political myth faithful to feminism, socialism, and materialism," and "perhaps more faithful as blasphemy is faithful, than as reverent worship and identification." The cyborg is accordingly defined by its refusals. It is postgender — "a creature in a postgender world; it has no truck with bisexuality, pre-oedipal symbiosis, unalienated labor, or other seductions to organic wholeness." It has no genealogy: "in a sense, the cyborg has no origin story in the Western sense," and "The cyborg skips the step of original unity, of identification with nature in the Western sense." And it is purged of moral purity: it "is resolutely committed to partiality, irony, intimacy, and perversity. It is oppositional, utopian, and completely without innocence." A cyborg body, as the closing myth puts it, "is not innocent; it was not born in a garden; it does not seek unitary identity and so generate antagonistic dualisms without end (or until the world ends); it takes irony for granted." This is the basis of [[The Refusal of Innocence]] — the cyborg cannot ground its politics in the "innocence of the merely violated," and so escapes the feminist and Marxist habit of building "a revolutionary subject from the perspective of a hierarchy of oppressions." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

What the cyborg myth makes possible is a politics of [[Affinity Not Identity|affinity rather than identity]] — relation "not by blood but by choice." Its enabling condition is [[The Breached Boundaries|three boundary breakdowns]]: between human and animal ("thoroughly breached"), between organism and machine ("Our machines are disturbingly lively, and we ourselves frighteningly inert"), and between physical and nonphysical ("Our best machines are made of sunshine… nothing but signals"). Because the cyborg's machine is not an external threat — "The machine is not an it to be animated, worshipped, and dominated. The machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment" — Haraway can treat the cyborg as a tool *seized* rather than a fate suffered. This is the work of [[Cyborg Writing]]: "the power to survive, not on the basis of original innocence, but on the basis of seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other." Politically the cyborg is born compromised — "the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism, not to mention state socialism" — but "illegitimate offspring are often exceedingly unfaithful to their origins. Their fathers, after all, are inessential." This unfaithfulness is its promise. The cyborg myth is finally aimed at "challenging the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]]" — Haraway's name for the "scary new networks" of a "polymorphous, information system" that has displaced the older "organic, industrial society" — closing with the manifesto's signature line: "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

## Marshall McLuhan (Understanding Media)

This is the figure's 1960s precursor, not a fourth sense of the cyborg (McLuhan never uses the word). McLuhan's central thesis — that all [[Media as Extensions of Man|media are extensions of man]] — frames every technology, "whether of skin, hand, or foot," as a prosthesis grafted onto the human body and psyche, so that "any extension … affects the whole psychic and social complex." Where the older mechanical technologies extended the body piecemeal in space, electric technology extends the nervous system itself: "During the mechanical ages we had extended our bodies in space. Today, after more than a century of electric technology, we have extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace, abolishing both space and time as far as our planet is concerned." The endpoint of this trajectory is the "technological extension of consciousness." This is the cyborg merger described in advance — the human spliced into its tools and circuits — and it culminates in a figure McLuhan draws almost as a literal cybernetic organism: under electromagnetic technology man becomes "an organism that now wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide" (cross-linked to [[Media as Translators]]). (Confidence: Directly stated for the quotations; Interpretive synthesis for reading these "extensions" as a cyborg precursor.)

For McLuhan the merger is not chosen, as it is for Haraway, but suffered: it runs through numbness and servility rather than ironic affinity. In the Narcissus-as-narcosis argument, "by continuously embracing technologies, we relate ourselves to them as servo-mechanisms," and "to use them at all, [we] serve these objects, these extensions of ourselves, as gods or minor religions" (the basis of the companion concept [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions]]). The extension is simultaneously a self-amputation that anesthetizes the user — "self-amputation forbids self-recognition" — so that the relation to one's own extended being is one of subliminal trance. McLuhan's strongest formulation of the resulting captivity is that media, through "subliminal and docile acceptance," "have made them prisons without walls for their human users." This is the surveillance-adjacent inflection that makes McLuhan legible to the vault's wider concern with the [[Digital Panopticon|digital panopticon]]: the human is enclosed not by an external apparatus but by its own prosthetic extensions, which it can no longer perceive as separate from itself. (Confidence: Directly stated for the quotations; Speculative connection for the panopticon link.)

The point at which McLuhan stops short of the cyborg is precisely the one the General Orientation flags. His grammar always presupposes an intact "Man" who *owns* the extensions — "extensions of *ourselves*," "extensions of *our* bodies," our nerves outside *our* hide. Even at the most radical, electric phase, "we wear all mankind as our skin": the self is enlarged, globalized, externalized, but never dissolved. There remains a someone who is doing the wearing. Haraway and Hayles take the same vocabulary of extension and prosthesis and push it past the boundary McLuhan holds — for them the prosthesis is constitutive, there is no prior unextended "Man" to be extended, and "the machine is us." McLuhan thus supplies the cyborg's conceptual materials (the body merged with its technologies, the nervous system worn on the outside) while retaining the humanist subject that the cyborg figure was later coined to retire. (Confidence: Interpretive synthesis.)

## Jack Burnham (Beyond Modern Sculpture)

This is the figure's earliest primary-text appearance in the vault, and an *art-world* deployment of the term that predates Haraway (1985) and Hayles by some seventeen years. Writing in 1968, the critic [[Jack Burnham]] names the terminal stage of his book's object-to-system trajectory "Cyborg Art," which he glosses directly as "the cybernetic organism as an art form" — "the most exotic, if distant, goal" of sculpture and "very gradually becoming the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture." In the Introduction he announces it as the herald of "the sculpture of the future": "Nothing more spectacular heralds the beginnings of the sculpture of the future than the slow emergence of what is called \"Cyborg Art\" (the art of cybernetic organisms)." For Burnham this is distinct enough to warrant its own heading; in the vault it is cross-linked as [[Cyborg Art]] — the art form proper, as opposed to the cyborg as cultural and ontological figure tracked in the Hayles, Haraway, and McLuhan sections above. (Confidence: Directly stated for Burnham's coinage and definition of Cyborg Art; Interpretive synthesis on its precedence over Haraway and Hayles.)

Burnham also supplies a compact, technical definition of the cyborg itself, grounded in cybernetics rather than in political myth (Haraway) or epistemology (Hayles): "A cyborg, then, is any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism." What unifies organism and machine in his account is [[Feedback Loops|reciprocal feedback]] — the same two-way coupling that, for Burnham, separates genuine cyborg art from all prior, one-way sculpture. His criterion for the art form is that it possess "a capacity for reciprocal relationships with human beings," so that the cyborg is for him less a hybrid body than a *system of exchange* joining the human to the responsive artwork. This cybernetic, systems-theoretic framing is the through-line connecting Burnham to Hayles's later informatic cyborg, and the conceptual parallel with Haraway's organism-machine hybrid is developed in [[Jack Burnham and Donna Haraway]]. (Confidence: Directly stated for Burnham's definition and the reciprocal-feedback criterion; Interpretive synthesis on the lineage to Hayles and the parallel with Haraway.)

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

[[Roy Ascott]] gives the cyborg a *telematic, networked, and ultimately moist* inflection that none of the prior authors anticipate: where Hayles splices the cyborg at the computer screen and McLuhan extends the nervous system through media, Ascott dissolves the bounded individual into the global network itself. In "The Architecture of Cyberception" (1994) the human sensorium is fused with the planetary net — "The cybernet, the sum of all the interactive computer-mediated systems and telematic networks in the world, is part of our sensory apparatus. It redefines our individual body, just as it connects all our bodies into a planetary whole" — so that "the sense of the individual is giving way to the sense of the interface. … We are all interface. We are computer-mediated and computer-enhanced." This is the cyborg pushed past McLuhan's intact "Man": Ascott's networked body is the heir of McLuhan's extended central nervous system, but it has no prior unextended self left over, and its new faculty of cyberception is named a "post-biological faculty." Cross-linked to [[Cyborg Art]] as the art-form correlate and to [[Roy Ascott and Donna Haraway]] for the organism-machine parallel. (Confidence: Directly stated for the quotations; Interpretive synthesis for reading the cybernet-fused interface as a telematic cyborg.)

In "Back to Nature II" (1995) Ascott states the cyborg thesis directly and folds it into Haraway's breached natural/artificial boundary: "The self is a complex system that interconnects with all other systems in a way that renders the line between natural and artificial systems meaningless. We are all becoming, to a greater or lesser extent, bionic." The merger is not merely physical but cognitive — "Our prostheses not only amplify and extend the body and its five senses but also augment cognition and memory and subtly transform the personality" — a prosthetic argument that takes McLuhan's [[Media as Extensions of Man|extensions of man]] into Haraway's and Hayles's territory where the prosthesis is constitutive of the self rather than owned by it. (Confidence: Directly stated.)

By the "Moist Manifesto" ("Art @ the Edge of the NET: the Future Will Be Moist!," 2000) the cyborg becomes explicitly *post-biological and transformable*: "Increasingly, the attitude of the mind towards the body is post-biological. Its view is cybernetic, seeking always the perfectibility of systems." Ascott's demand is for a body that is not just augmented but malleable at will — "our frustration with the limitations of our own bodies will demand prostheses and genetic intervention of a high order. We realise that the body, like our own identity, can be transformed, indeed must become transformable" — a register that converges with [[Posthumanism]] and the transhumanist body while retaining Ascott's distinctive spiritual-technoetic optimism. (Confidence: Directly stated.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — *Originating account (see the section below).* In "A Cyborg Manifesto" the cyborg is an ironic political myth: "a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction." It is "our ontology" and "a condensed image of both imagination and material reality"; it is postgender, "completely without innocence," has "no origin story in the Western sense," and is the "illegitimate offspring" of militarism and capitalism who is "exceedingly unfaithful" to its origins. Its politics are of [[Affinity Not Identity|affinity not identity]], enabled by [[The Breached Boundaries|three breached boundaries]] and aimed at "challenging the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]]." The cyborg is normative and chosen, not merely diagnostic: "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess."
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — Adopts Haraway's cyborg ("As Donna Haraway has pointed out…") but reframes it historically and epistemologically: as a technological artifact "created… in the years following World War II," and as the figure produced by splicing the enacted and represented bodies through technology — "both technological object and discursive formation," so that "Cyborgs actually exist." The cyborg is the hinge between the loss of information's body and the emergence of the posthuman.
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — *Precursor, not a definition of the cyborg (he never uses the word; see the section below).* In *Understanding Media* the human is merged with its tools through the [[Media as Extensions of Man|extensions of man]] — media as prostheses of body, sense, and nervous system, culminating in an organism that "wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide." By "continuously embracing technologies, we relate ourselves to them as servo-mechanisms" (see [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions]]), and media's "docile acceptance" makes them "prisons without walls." Decisively, McLuhan keeps an intact "Man" being extended where Haraway and Hayles use the cyborg to abolish that boundary — he supplies the merger's materials while retaining the humanist subject the figure was coined to retire.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — *Earliest primary-text use in the vault, and an art-world deployment (1968, predating Haraway by seventeen years; see the section below).* Supplies the most technical definition — "a cyborg, then, is any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism" — grounded in cybernetics rather than political myth or epistemology, and coins [[Cyborg Art]] ("the art of cybernetic organisms") as sculpture's terminal object-to-system stage. What unifies organism and machine is [[Feedback Loops|reciprocal feedback]]: the cyborg is less a hybrid body than a *system of exchange* with "a capacity for reciprocal relationships with human beings."
- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — *Telematic, networked, post-biological inflection (see the section below).* Dissolves the bounded individual into the global network: "The cybernet… is part of our sensory apparatus. It redefines our individual body, just as it connects all our bodies into a planetary whole," so that "We are all interface." Folds the merger into Haraway's breached natural/artificial boundary ("We are all becoming… bionic") and McLuhan's prosthetics ("Our prostheses… augment cognition and memory"), and demands a body that "must become transformable" — pushing the cyborg past McLuhan's intact "Man" toward [[Posthumanism]] and the transhumanist body, with the art-form correlate in [[Cyborg Art]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Posthumanism]] — the point of view the cyborg makes thinkable; the third of the three stories, distinct from the cyborg as figure
- [[Cybernetics]] — the science whose dissolution of boundaries (control, communication, information as one system) constructs the cyborg
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the mechanism by which "replacing cognition with neural feedback" the cyborg challenges the human-animal difference and joins subject to environment
- [[Embodiment]] — the enacted body that is spliced to the represented body in the cyborg conjunction
- [[The Body as Data]] — the cyborg recoded as informational pattern; the body imagined as "data made flesh"
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the body as "original prosthesis," extended at the cane/hearing-aid/firing-control splice that produces the cyborg

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the cyborg is the figure where this disagreement is staged: it can be read either as the triumph of substrate-independent information (the body as replaceable pattern) or, in Hayles's preferred reading, as proof that information "must always be instantiated in a medium." The opposition lies in which side of the splice is privileged.

## Questions It Raises

- If the cyborg is produced by joining the enacted and represented bodies through technology, does it confirm that [[Embodiment]] can be bypassed, or does its very dependence on a material interface refute the dream of disembodied information? (ties to embodiment / information vs materiality)
- When cybernetics constructs "bodies as immaterial flows of information," does the cyborg figure the [[The Body as Data|body as data]] — and what is lost when the body's plenitude is reduced to pattern? (ties to the body as data)
- Is the cyborg's erasure of the human/machine, human/animal, and animate/inanimate distinctions liberating or lethal — and does it matter whether the cyborg is "grafted onto a liberal humanist view of the self"? (ties to posthumanism)
- Because the cyborg is "both technological object and discursive formation," can its material and figural registers ever be separated — or does it permanently entangle the [[Material vs Immaterial]] divide? (ties to information vs materiality)
