# The Modern Covenant

## Brief Definition

For [[Yuval Noah Harari]], the modern covenant is the deal that constitutes modernity itself — a contract that "can be summarised in a single phrase: humans agree to give up meaning in exchange for power" — and, read at the level of institutions, "a deal between science and one particular religion — namely, humanism."

## Longer Explanation

The modern covenant is Harari's master frame for modernity. Modernity, he insists, "is a deal. All of us sign up to this deal on the day we are born, and it regulates our lives until the day we die." It looks impossibly complicated — like the legalese you scroll past to tick "I agree" — but "in fact modernity is a surprisingly simple deal. The entire contract can be summarised in a single phrase: humans agree to give up meaning in exchange for power." This trade of meaning for power is the load-bearing structure of the whole book; the chapter exists to name the terms and the next chapter ("The Humanist Revolution") to explain how humanity tried to evade paying the price. (`Directly stated`)

The covenant is legible only against what it replaced. Premodernity ran on the opposite settlement: "the cosmic plan gave meaning to human life, but also restricted human power." Harari renders this through a theatrical metaphor — "Humans were much like actors on a stage. The script gave meaning to their every word, tear and gesture — but placed strict limits on their performance. Hamlet cannot murder Claudius in Act I, or leave Denmark and go to an ashram in India. Shakespeare won't allow it." A script that confers meaning is simultaneously a script that confiscates power: you cannot live for ever, escape disease, or do as you please, because "It's not in the script." The premodern human is rich in meaning and poor in power. (`Directly stated`)

Modernity inverts both halves at once. It "rejects this belief in a great cosmic plan": "Life has no script, no playwright, no director, no producer — and no meaning," and "If modernity has a motto, it is 'shit happens'." But the void that abolishes meaning also abolishes the limit on power: "if shit just happens, without any binding script or purpose, then humans too are not limited to any predetermined role. We can do anything we want — provided we can find a way. We are constrained by nothing except our own ignorance." This is the hinge of the whole concept — the same purposelessness that strips meaning grants near-omnipotence, because the only remaining boundary is what we do not yet know rather than what some god forbids. The covenant therefore presents, in Harari's words, "an enormous temptation, coupled with a colossal threat": "Omnipotence is in front of us, almost within our reach, but below us yawns the abyss of complete nothingness." Modern life becomes "a constant pursuit of power within a universe devoid of meaning." (`Directly stated`)

There is a second, institutional reading of the covenant, set out in the preceding chapter ("The Odd Couple"). There Harari argues that the popular story of modernity as a war between science and religion is wrong: as collective institutions, "science and religion prefer order and power over truth," and so "they can therefore make good bedfellows." On this view, "It would accordingly be far more correct to view modern history as the process of formulating a deal between science and one particular religion — namely, humanism." Science supplies power but "there is no scientific method for determining how humans ought to behave"; humanism supplies the meaning and the values that science needs in order to act. The covenant thus has two faces that name the same deal: at the level of the individual contract it is *meaning traded for power*; at the level of civilisational institutions it is the alliance between science (power) and [[Humanism (Harari)|humanism]] (the new "revolutionary religion" that "salvaged" modern society and supplies the meaning the deal had promised to renounce). (`Directly stated`)

Crucially, Harari argues the price has not actually been paid. "Yes, we moderns have promised to renounce meaning in exchange for power; but there's nobody out there to hold us to our promise." Humanism is precisely the device by which "humankind has used its growing power to somehow sneak meaning back into the infinite emptiness of the cosmos" — letting us "enjoy the full benefits of the modern deal, without paying its price." The covenant is therefore unstable by design, and the book's third part argues that the science–humanism covenant "is disintegrating," poised to "give way to a very different kind of deal, between science and some new post-humanist religion" — the opening through which [[Dataism]] enters. (`Directly stated` for the unpaid price and the coming dissolution; `Interpretive synthesis` for reading the two formulations as one covenant under two descriptions.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (*Homo Deus*) — the master frame for modernity. Two equivalent formulations: (1) the *existential contract* — meaning surrendered for power, the premodern cosmic script (meaning, no power) replaced by the modern void ("shit happens": no meaning, near-omnipotence "constrained by nothing except our own ignorance"); (2) the *institutional deal* — "a deal between science and one particular religion — namely, humanism," where science delivers power and humanism delivers the values. Harari frames the deal as one whose price is never collected, and whose science–humanism form is now "disintegrating" toward a post-humanist successor.

## Related Concepts

- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — the "new revolutionary religion" that is the *other party* to the institutional covenant; the means by which moderns smuggle meaning back into a universe the deal had declared meaningless.
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — the orthodox, individualist sect of that humanism; the specific creed that, for now, holds up the meaning-side of the covenant.
- [[Capital as Religion]] — resonant: the covenant's engine of power is the "alliance between scientific progress and economic growth," and Harari calls the resulting "belief in economic growth a religion"; growth-as-faith is the economic face of the deal's pursuit of power.
- [[Dataism]] — the candidate "post-humanist religion" Harari names as the deal that may replace the science–humanism covenant once the latter crumbles.
- [[Homo Deus]] — the telos the covenant licenses: a humanity "constrained by nothing except our own ignorance," reaching for omnipotence and the upgrade to godhood.

## Opposing Concepts

- The **premodern cosmic plan** — the inverse settlement the covenant abolishes: meaning conferred by an unchangeable script in exchange for strictly limited power ("the cosmic plan gave meaning to human life, but also restricted human power"). Rendered here as plain text — Harari names it as a contrast, not a standing vault concept.
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — listed under `opposes` only in the structural sense that the covenant is a *renunciation* of meaning while liberal humanism is the device that *restores* it; the two are partners, not enemies, but they pull in opposite directions on the meaning question.

## Questions It Raises

- If the modern covenant's price (the loss of meaning) is never actually collected because humanism "sneaks meaning back," is the deal a genuine trade or a confidence trick we play on ourselves — and what happens when the bill finally comes due?
- The covenant says power is bounded only by ignorance. Where, then, does the *ecological* limit ("the real nemesis of the modern economy is ecological collapse") fit — is it a refutation of the deal, or just the next "technical difficulty" the growth-creed promises to engineer away?
- If, as "The Odd Couple" argues, science needs religion to supply values, can there be any "purely scientific" successor to the covenant — or must even [[Dataism]] count as the next *religion* rather than the deal's escape from religion?
- How does Harari's "meaning for power" map onto the vault's other accounts of the modern subject — is the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]]'s autonomy a beneficiary of the covenant's power, or another casualty of its evacuation of meaning?
