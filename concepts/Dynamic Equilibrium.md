# Dynamic Equilibrium

## Brief Definition

The steady state of a stock whose level does not change because its total inflow exactly equals its total outflow — a balance held not by stasis but by continuous, offsetting flow.

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows, dynamic equilibrium is a *state*, not a mechanism: the condition a stock arrives at when the things adding to it and the things draining from it come into exact balance. Her canonical illustration is the bathtub. Fill the tub, open the drain, and then, when it is about half empty, turn the faucet on so that water pours in at precisely the rate it pours out. The water level then freezes — "the amount of water in the tub stays constant at whatever level it had reached when the inflow became equal to the outflow." Crucially, nothing has stopped: "its level does not change, although water is continuously flowing through it." The equilibrium is *dynamic* because it is a running balance of motion, not the absence of motion. `Directly stated`

This is why dynamic equilibrium is a state of the stock rather than a rule imposed on it. Meadows derives it as one of a few "important principles that extend to more complicated systems": as long as inflows exceed outflows the stock rises, as long as outflows exceed inflows it falls, and "if the sum of all outflows equals the sum of all inflows, the stock level will not change; it will be held in dynamic equilibrium at whatever level it happened to be when the two sets of flows became equal." The equilibrium level is contingent — it is simply wherever the stock stood at the moment the flows equalized — which distinguishes it sharply from a set point that a system is actively steering toward. `Directly stated`

At the level of [[Feedback Loops]], Meadows restates the same balance as an equality of loops. In her population model, a reinforcing growth loop (births) and a balancing loop (deaths) compete; while fertility exceeds mortality the reinforcing loop dominates and growth is exponential, but as fertility falls the reinforcing loop "is gradually weakened" until it "exactly equals the strength of the balancing loop of mortality." At that point "neither loop dominates, and we have dynamic equilibrium" — "births exactly balancing deaths." She generalizes the pattern beyond biology: in a capital economy, "when depreciation just balances investment, the economy is in dynamic equilibrium." Births balancing deaths, investment balancing depreciation: in each case a quantity flows through continuously while the standing stock holds steady. `Directly stated`

Meadows treats this balanced-flow state as ordinary and pervasive rather than as an ideal — it is one behavior among the repertoire (growth, decline, oscillation) that stocks-and-flows can produce, and it emerges from "shifting dominance of feedback loops" rather than being engineered in. It should be kept distinct from [[Homeostasis]], which names the goal-seeking regulatory *mechanism* — the balancing loop with a target that acts to restore a set point. Dynamic equilibrium is the balanced *state* such a mechanism (or a mere coincidence of flows, as in the bathtub) may hold the stock in; a stock can sit in dynamic equilibrium with no thermostat-like controller at all. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*) — the balanced-flow steady state of a stock: inflow equals outflow (equivalently, a reinforcing and a balancing loop are exactly equal), so the stock's level holds constant even as quantity flows continuously through it. Illustrated by the half-full bathtub, by births balancing deaths in a stabilized population, and by depreciation balancing investment in a capital economy.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — dynamic equilibrium is what results when a reinforcing loop and a balancing loop reach exactly equal strength (shifting dominance resolving into a standoff).
- [[Homeostasis]] — closely related but distinct: homeostasis is the goal-seeking *mechanism* that holds a stock near a set point; dynamic equilibrium is the balanced *state* itself, which can also arise without any target-seeking controller.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the stock-and-flow framework within which dynamic equilibrium is one of the basic behaviors a system can settle into.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Acceleration]] — Han's diagnosis of a temporal order that cannot settle: where dynamic equilibrium is a running balance whose level holds steady, acceleration names an inflow that permanently outruns any offsetting outflow, so nothing stabilizes. `Speculative connection`

## Questions It Raises

- If dynamic equilibrium can arise by mere coincidence of flows (the bathtub) or by a goal-seeking mechanism (a thermostat), when is the distinction from [[Homeostasis]] doing real analytical work?
- The equilibrium level is contingent — "at whatever level it happened to be" when flows equalized. What determines *which* equilibrium a system falls into when several are possible?
- A stock in dynamic equilibrium is not inert but has quantity continuously flowing through it. How does this reframe seemingly static material forms (a body, an institution, a population) as ongoing processes rather than fixed things?
