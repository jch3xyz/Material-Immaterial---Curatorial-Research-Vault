# The God View

## Brief Definition

The God view is Shoshana Zuboff's name for the "distant detached gaze" — Alex Pentland's "God's eye view" — the elevated, computer-mediated angle of observation from which human beings are seen "like watching beavers from outer space" and from which "any sense of 'we' quickly dissolves into the viewpoint of the Other-One."

## Longer Explanation

The God view is the optic of [[Instrumentarian Power]]: the position of observation that makes [[Radical Indifference]] possible. Zuboff develops it in Chapter 15 through the figure of MIT's Alex Pentland, whose "social physics" she reads as this century's reanimation of B. F. Skinner. The view originates as a literal experience of altitude — the sensation of looking down on a city from an airplane window — and hardens into an epistemology. "The experience appears to have hooked Pentland on the distant detached gaze, which he would later embrace as the 'God's eye view'" (Directly stated, Ch. 15). From that height the social bond evaporates: "Up there, any sense of 'we' quickly dissolves into the viewpoint of the Other-One, and it is this angle of observation that founded Pentland's science." The God view is thus the *vantage* from which the human ceases to be a "we" and becomes one of the [[The Other-One|Other-Ones]] — an organism among organisms to be counted, measured, and tuned.

The defining quality of the God view is its reduction of meaning to behavior observed from a distance. Pentland's own analogy makes the indifference explicit: "If you think about people across the room talking, you can tell a lot.… It's like watching beavers from outer space, like Jane Goodall watching gorillas. You observe from a distance." Zuboff is careful to register the betrayal in the comparison — "This is a slur on Goodall, of course, whose seminal genius was her ability to understand the gorillas she studied not as 'other ones' but rather as 'one of us'" (Directly stated, Ch. 15). Where Goodall's distance produced kinship, the God view's distance produces only data. It is the optic in which observation is severed from witness, and in which the observer's interest extends only to "rules of behavior, reaction, and learning" derivable from the herd, the hive, the flock.

Crucially, the God view is not contemplative but operational. It is the precondition for control: "instrumentation makes it possible for those with the God view to modify others' behavior" — to calculate idea flow and "individual susceptibilities to new ideas," and so to achieve "the optimum performance of Skinner's superorganism" through what Pentland calls "tuning the network" (Directly stated, Ch. 15). To see from above is already to govern from above. The view persuades Pentland that "assessing human action really is comparable to counting beavers," that "we can observe humans in just the same way we observe apes or bees and derive rules of behavior, reaction, and learning." The God view is therefore the cognitive form of [[The Hive]]: the angle from which the parts dissolve into the whole and individuality becomes "merely vestigial."

In Chapter 16 Zuboff shows the God view migrating from Pentland's laboratory into the commercial machinery of surveillance capitalism, where it becomes the engine of Facebook's News Feed. "By 2016 the News Feed function depended upon one of the world's most secretive predictive algorithms, derived from a God view of more than 100,000 elements of behavioral surplus that are continuously computed to determine the 'personal relevancy' score of thousands of possible posts" (Directly stated, Ch. 16). The God view here is not metaphor but infrastructure: an aggregate, all-seeing computation over [[Behavioral Surplus]] that selects what each user sees. "Facebook has Pentland's prized God view on its side, an unparalleled resource that is drawn upon to remake this naturally longed-for fusion into a space of no escape" (Directly stated, Ch. 16). The God view drives the computations, the computations enable the tuning, and the tuning, Zuboff concludes, "replaces private governance and public politics, without which individuality is merely vestigial."

## Metaphors (the airplane and the beaver)

The God view is carried by two of Zuboff's most vivid borrowed images, which she draws directly from Pentland and reframes critically. Rather than mint separate metaphor notes, the imagery is captured here:

- **The airplane window.** "You may have experienced the sensation of the God view from the window seat of an airplane as it lifts you above the city, transforming all the joys and woes below into the mute bustle of an anthill" (Directly stated, Ch. 15). Altitude converts lived human experience — joy, woe — into the silent, legible swarm of an anthill. The image makes the God view a felt, almost seductive sensation before it is a method.
- **Watching beavers from outer space.** Pentland's own formula — "It's like watching beavers from outer space, like Jane Goodall watching gorillas. You observe from a distance" — is the metaphor's distilled form. To observe humans as one observes beavers from orbit is precisely to abolish the "we." Zuboff turns the analogy against itself, noting that Goodall's genius was the opposite: to see her subjects "not as 'other ones' but rather as 'one of us'" (Directly stated, Ch. 15). The metaphor thus names both the optic and its moral cost.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — uses "the God view" / "God's eye view" critically, as the angle of observation that founds instrumentarian science and underwrites [[Radical Indifference]]. She attributes the phrase to Alex Pentland (after B. F. Skinner's behaviorism) and tracks it from the airplane window, to Pentland's "social physics," to the predictive algorithm of Facebook's News Feed. For Zuboff the God view is never neutral: it is the optic that dissolves the "we" into the viewpoint of [[The Other-One]] and converts seeing into tuning.

## Related Concepts

- [[Radical Indifference]] — the God view is the angle of observation; radical indifference is the moral and epistemic stance it makes possible ("observation without witness"). The two are two faces of one optic.
- [[The Other-One]] — what the human becomes when seen from the God view; the standpoint into which "any sense of 'we' quickly dissolves."
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the form of power founded on the God view and operationalized through tuning.
- [[Big Other]] — the ubiquitous sensing apparatus that supplies the God view its 360-degree material.
- [[The Hive]] — the social form the God view produces: the superorganism in which the parts dissolve into the whole.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the raw material the God view computes (the News Feed's "more than 100,000 elements of behavioral surplus").
- [[Surveillance]] — the broader practice the God view perfects and automates from above.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, first-person "we"/"I" whose viewpoint the God view explicitly rejects; Skinner's "destructive fiction of individual autonomy" is exactly what the distant gaze is designed to dissolve.

## Questions It Raises

- If the God view abolishes the "we," is there any vantage of large-scale observation that does not? Goodall's "one of us" suggests distance need not mean indifference — what would a non-indifferent aggregate gaze look like?
- The God view is presented as both a felt sensation (the airplane window) and an industrial computation (100,000 elements of surplus). Does its seductiveness as experience help naturalize its operation as infrastructure?
- When observation becomes inseparable from tuning — when "those with the God view" can "modify others' behavior" — is there any meaningful difference left between seeing a society and governing it?
- The God view recasts humans as beavers, bees, apes. Is this dehumanization a contingent ideology of behaviorism, or an inevitable epistemic effect of computing behavior at scale?
