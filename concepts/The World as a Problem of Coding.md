# The World as a Problem of Coding

## Brief Definition

The common epistemic operation that, for Haraway, unites the communications sciences and the modern biologies: the translation of the world into a problem of coding — the search for a single common language in which all heterogeneity can be disassembled, reassembled, exchanged, and submitted to instrumental control.

## Longer Explanation

In *A Cyborg Manifesto*, Haraway argues that two seemingly distinct domains — the communications sciences and modern biology — are "constructed by a common move." That move is the recasting of every object of knowledge as a coding problem: a search for a "common language in which all resistance to instrumental control disappears and all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange." This is not a metaphor she endorses but the operating logic she diagnoses at the heart of post-WWII technoscience. Once the world is rendered as code, no architecture is given by nature; "any objects or persons can be reasonably thought of in terms of disassembly and reassembly; no 'natural' architectures constrain system design." `Directly stated`

In the communications sciences, the move appears as cybernetic systems theory applied to telephony, computer design, weapons deployment, and databases, where "the key operation is determining the rates, directions, and probabilities of flow of a quantity called information." Information becomes "that kind of quantifiable element (unit, basis of unity) that allows universal translation, and so unhindered instrumental power." In the modern biologies — molecular genetics, ecology, sociobiology, immunobiology — "the organism has been translated into problems of genetic coding and readout," such that "organisms have ceased to exist as objects of knowledge, giving way to biotic components, i.e., special kinds of information-processing devices." Haraway condenses the biological case in a single line: "Biology here is a kind of cryptography." `Directly stated`

The operation is what makes the cyborg world legible and governable. Because everything can be coded in a common tongue, "any component can be interfaced with any other if the proper standard, the proper code, can be constructed for processing signals in a common language" — an exchange that, Haraway notes, "transcends the universal translation effected by capitalist markets that Marx analyzed so well." Coding is therefore the condition of the regime, not merely its description: it produces the interchangeability of bodies, labor, sex, and mind ("labor → robotics," "sex → genetic engineering," "mind → artificial intelligence"). The corresponding pathology is no longer disease but "stress — communications breakdown"; the great threat to power is "interruption of communication." `Directly stated`

Read across the vault, this 1985 formulation anticipates by decades the body- and world-as-information theses developed elsewhere: Hayles's account of how `Pattern and Randomness` displaces presence/absence, and Harari's claim that `Organisms Are Algorithms`. Haraway names the *operation* — coding as the common move — that those later thinkers describe as an accomplished ontology. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — names coding as the *common move* shared by the communications sciences and modern biologies. For Haraway it is a diagnostic, not an endorsement: the translation into code is precisely what dissolves the "natural" object and enables the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]]. The operation is double-edged — the same blurring of organism and machine that enables instrumental control also grounds her ironic cyborg politics.
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** (variant emphasis) — develops the consequence as `Pattern and Randomness`: when the world is coded, materiality is treated as the carrier of information rather than its ground. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (variant emphasis) — extends the biological half into the flat claim that `Organisms Are Algorithms`, the keystone of `Dataism`. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the persistence of the lived, material body as more than a recodable substrate; coding treats heterogeneity and "depth, integrity" as obstacles to be reduced to "surface, boundary."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the coding move is the mechanism by which the immaterial (information, code) is granted priority over the material; the tension it produces.

## Questions It Raises

- If coding is an *operation* rather than a discovery, what is lost — politically and epistemically — when "all resistance to instrumental control disappears"? Who benefits from universal translatability?
- Haraway diagnoses coding critically yet builds an affirmative cyborg politics on the same blurred boundaries. Can one inhabit the coding move ironically without ratifying the informatics of domination it serves?
- Is there heterogeneity that *cannot* be "submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange," or does the move's premise foreclose that possibility in advance?
- How does Haraway's 1985 "common move" relate to the later, often celebratory theses (Harari's algorithms, Kurzweil's patternism) that take the coded world as simply given rather than constructed?
