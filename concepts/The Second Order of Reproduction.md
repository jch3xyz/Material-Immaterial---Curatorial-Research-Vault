# The Second Order of Reproduction

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's explicit periodization beyond Walter Benjamin: where mechanical reproduction (the *first* order) reproduced the image, digital co-production (the *second* order) transforms the image itself, repositioning the viewing subject as a co-author whose hands hold "the destiny of the image" and distributing authorship "across time."

## Longer Explanation

Ascott frames the move from chemical/photographic to digital/hypermedia image-making as a generational shift in the very nature of reproduction. He names Benjamin's "work of art in the age of mechanical reproduction" as the *first* order — the regime in which a technology multiplies copies of an existing image — and posits a *second* order that supersedes it: **the stage of co-production**, in which the technology does not merely reproduce but transforms the image, and does so in the hands of the viewer as much as the artist (`Directly stated`). The decisive break is not quantitative (more copies, faster, more ubiquitous) but ontological: the image becomes permeable, manipulable, recombinable — "endlessly manipulated, recombined with other elements, and transformed."

What changes "from the old economy of the image," for Ascott, is agency. In the second order the processes of transformation "are now in the hands of the viewer as much as the artist" (`Directly stated`). He distinguishes two layers of co-production: first, an older co-production "between the photographer and the viewing subject in the realm of *meaning*"; now, on top of that, "a technology of co-production and transformation of the *image itself*" (`Directly stated`). The reader/viewer is no longer the receiver of a finished, aura-bearing (or aura-stripped) object but a manipulator who can "distort, collage, dismember, fractionise, and in every way transform the image at will." This is Ascott's photographic-interface instance of his broader thesis that interactive/telematic media convert spectatorship into participation — the same logic that animates [[Reciprocal Communication]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]].

Crucially, Ascott situates the second order in time as well as authorship. Hypermedia "opens" the image and effects a "distribution, across time, of authorship at the public level" (`Directly stated`). Authorship is no longer a single originating act but an ongoing, distributed, public process — meaning is unfixed ("a world of fuzzy ambiguities, darting associations, shifting contexts, and semantic leaps") and the image's "destiny" lies with whoever manipulates it next. Ascott reads this as having "unprecedented effects both on the perceived legitimacy of the photograph and on the perceived authenticity and cultural status of the photographic artist" — that is, the second order corrodes precisely the categories (authenticity, the cultural status of the author) that Benjamin's first order had already begun to destabilize (`Strongly implied`).

The concept is also a substantive *engagement* with Benjamin rather than a passing reference. Benjamin is the named prior term — the named "first order" against which Ascott measures his "second" — and Ascott cites him directly ("[Benjamin 1969]") to mark the boundary he is crossing (`Directly stated`). Although Benjamin was trimmed from the working book corpus, he is a legitimate forward-stub figure here because *Telematic Embrace* engages his text by name; [[Walter Benjamin]] is the anchor against which the periodization is defined.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins "the second order of reproduction" in "Photography at the Interface" (1992) as a stage *beyond* Benjamin: the move from reproducing the image to **co-producing and transforming** it, with the viewer empowered as manipulator and authorship distributed across time at the public level.
- **[[Walter Benjamin]]** — supplies the *first* order, mechanical reproduction ("the work of art in the age of mechanical reproduction"), the named baseline Ascott periodizes past. Ascott cites him directly. (Benjamin has no book in `raw/`; he is a forward-stub figure, cited substantively in this chapter.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — kindred trajectory in the art object: where Lippard's conceptualists dematerialized the *object*, Ascott's second order dematerializes the *fixity* of the image, dissolving stable meaning into manipulable data ("stand-alone meanings and conceptual linearities disappear").
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the second order is reciprocity applied to the image: the viewer transforms what the artist provides, so the image becomes a two-way, co-produced site rather than a one-way transmission.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the broader Ascott/Burnham frame the second order extends: art as a participatory technological process the viewer enacts, not a finished object received.
- [[New Media Art]] — the field within which Ascott's hypermedia/digital-photography periodization sits; the second order is its claim about how digital media reconfigure authorship.
- [[Exhibition Value]] — Benjamin's other axis (cult value → exhibition value) is the same first-order lineage Ascott pushes past; the second order is a candidate *third* term beyond reproduction altogether.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the second order sits on the immaterial pole: the chemically fixed, material photographic print (silver grains) gives way to the manipulable, immaterial pixel "flowing in data space," and the stable material object yields to a process.

## Questions It Raises

- If authorship is "distributed across time" and "at the public level," what becomes of authenticity, originality, and the legal/economic category of the author — and does the second order finish the demolition Benjamin's first order began?
- Is the viewer's "empowerment as a manipulator" a genuine liberation of agency, or is it continuous with the surveillance/participation logics other vault authors (Han, Harari) read as voluntary self-exposure?
- Ascott anticipates "the artificial observer, the eye of the neural net" entering the observing system — does a *third* order arrive when the co-producer is no longer human?
- How does Ascott's "co-production of the image itself" differ from Lippard's dematerialization and Burnham's object→system shift — is it a distinct stage or the same arc seen from the image's side?
