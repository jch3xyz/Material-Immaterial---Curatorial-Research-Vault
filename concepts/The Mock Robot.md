# The Mock Robot

## Brief Definition

The mock robot is figurative sculpture that *looks* mechanical — robot-, doll-, or manikin-like — but possesses none of the goal-seeking, self-stabilizing responsiveness of a true cybernetic organism: the robot reduced to its outward functional definition and emptied of feedback, "indeed, a mock robot."

## Longer Explanation

In the "Mock Robots as Sculpture" section of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*'s Chapter 8, Jack Burnham draws a sharp line between two kinds of robot-imagery. On one side stand the real postwar machines — General Electric's pedipulator and Handyman, the IBM "1500" teaching machine, the Luna 9 and Surveyor moon probes, Disney's audio-animatronic Lincoln — which "work, they affect matter and involve man in their doings more every day." On the other stand the sculptors, who "instinctively realize that they lack the technical skills to create functional robots" and so "continue — as sculptors have with the human model for thousands of years — to produce obsessional figures which simulate machineness." The mock robot is that simulation: a static, traditional sculpture *of* a machine that has no machine inside it. *Directly stated*.

Burnham's keystone formulation comes in his account of Enrique Castro-Cid's early robots (his 1965 Richard Feigen Gallery show). Castro-Cid's *Anthropomorphic 1* (1964) is "technologically an unartful assemblage of synchron timing motors, a set of mechanical relays and a handful of light bulbs — all used to terrifying effect. But, reduced to its functional definition, this machine has none of the goalseeking, self-stabilizing ability of even such relatively simple animals as Grey Walter's *Machina speculatrix*; it is, indeed, a mock robot." This is the conceptual test: a thing that *appears* to be a robot fails the definition the moment one asks what it can do. It cannot seek a goal, cannot stabilize itself, cannot enter into [[Reciprocal Communication|two-way communication]] with its environment — the very capacities that, for Burnham, separate a true cybernetic organism (see [[The Tortoise (Machina Speculatrix)|Grey Walter's tortoise]]) from a dead image. *Directly stated*.

The mock robot is therefore the negative limit of [[Cyborg Art]]: it is what [[The Robot]] becomes when it enters the gallery as fine art rather than the laboratory as engineering. Burnham populates the category with a roster of artists — Eduardo Paolozzi's lost-wax "rusticated machine men," "metalized demons" welded into "torn, top-heavy figures with cog wheels insides"; Ernest Trova's "highly polished bronze manikins" with "spindly legs and distended stomachs," figures of "cheerful robots" anesthetized to "the pain of robot existence"; and, as the bridge to the cybernetic chapter that follows, Nam June Paik's *Robot-K456* (1965), "the robot 'stripped bare' of everything but her skeletal aluminum and components," who "responds listlessly to human intervention." K456 sits at the border — a "semi-operative robot" whose pathos "is that of Descartes's man-machine" — between the wholly inert mock robot and the genuinely responsive cybernetic sculpture. *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis*.

Burnham extends the category sideways into [[Subsculpture]]: "Aside from the pseudo-mechanical man, anthropomorphism in recent sculpture has usually been confined to varieties of subsculpture: the doll, the waxwork curiosity, and the painted, store-window manikin — all proto-robots of everyday life. Shells inhabited by human souls, these subsculptures live tenuously as imitations of human beings." George Segal's plaster people, Niki de Saint-Phalle's Nanas, Frank Gallo's epoxy "humanoids," Larry Rivers's fetishized manikins, Edward Kienholz's tableaus — all are mock robots in the wider sense: figures kept "still and silent by the knowledge of its makers: that intuition which tells the sculptor that to induce motion forsakes the protective security of sculpture." The mock robot marks the exact threshold sculpture has not yet crossed — the point past which it would have to stop *depicting* life and start *behaving*. *Directly stated*.

## The Robot as Cultural Figure

Burnham frames the mock robot against a longer cultural history of the robot as a *figure of dread* that has been gradually domesticated. "There is an inherent fear connected with the term robot. For the early mechanical age, the robot was the collective demon in human form, and to give its powers to actual subjugated machines, to realize its potential capabilities, is to undermine the image of the soulless, clanking monster." As real, autonomous, decision-making machines proliferate, the dread dissolves: cybernetics and electrical engineering "diminished the dread of robotry by making it a reality." *Directly stated*.

The result is a transformation of the figure itself. Where, "at the beginning of this century the robot represented a symbolic threat to human values," it now becomes ambivalent and intimate: "Today, moreover, the robot has become a semipersonal, fallible being — a pensive, twilight figure, an unknowing accomplice to the undermining of once intransigent Western values." And this softened, half-human robot reverses the ancient direction of mimesis: "For our generation much significant anthropomorphic sculpture does not imitate man, but imitates robots trying to become human." The mock robot is the sculptural deposit of this cultural shift — no longer the demon to be feared, but a melancholy, fallible double, the human condition "mirrored by the robot, the spineless doll, or the waxen image," which "has become an idee fixe" for the sculptor. *Directly stated*.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*, ch. 8 "Robot and Cyborg Art") — the mock robot is figurative sculpture that simulates "machineness" without function: it looks mechanical but, "reduced to its functional definition," has "none of the goalseeking, self-stabilizing ability of even such relatively simple animals as Grey Walter's *Machina speculatrix*." Burnham's examples run from Paolozzi's "metalized demons" and Trova's "cheerful robots" through the subsculptural doll/manikin/waxwork to Castro-Cid's early robots and Paik's listlessly responsive K456 — the last marking the border with genuine [[Cyborg Art]]. He frames it against the robot's cultural arc from "collective demon in human form" to "semipersonal, fallible being." *Directly stated*.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Robot]] — the parent figure; the mock robot is the robot rendered as static fine-art sculpture rather than functional machine — appearance without autonomy.
- [[Subsculpture]] — Burnham folds the doll, waxwork, and store-window manikin ("all proto-robots of everyday life") into the mock-robot category as its non-mechanical wing.
- [[Cyborg Art]] — the positive counterpart the mock robot fails to be; where cyborg art achieves [[Reciprocal Communication|two-way communication]], the mock robot offers only one-way stimulation.
- [[Automata]] — the long tradition the mock robot debases: the eighteenth-century android sought organic fidelity; the mock robot offers only the *look* of mechanism.
- [[The Tortoise (Machina Speculatrix)]] — Grey Walter's goal-seeking, self-stabilizing cybernetic tortoise, the very benchmark whose absence defines Castro-Cid's *Anthropomorphic 1* as a mock robot.
- [[The Cyborg]] — Burnham's responsive man-machine successor; the mock robot is the dead end the cyborg is meant to surpass. Bridges to [[Donna Haraway]] via [[Jack Burnham and Donna Haraway]].

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Cybernetics]] — the science of goal-seeking, self-stabilizing, feedback-governed systems; the mock robot is precisely what *lacks* cybernetic capability — "none of the goalseeking, self-stabilizing ability" of even a simple cybernetic organism. *Directly stated*.
- The true **cybernetic organism** / [[Cyborg Art]] — responsive, behaving, in two-way communication with its environment; the mock robot is its inert, merely-resembling antithesis.

## Questions It Raises

- If a robot is defined by *function* (feedback, goal-seeking, self-stabilization) rather than *form*, can any traditional sculpture ever be more than a mock robot — and does this mark the limit at which "sculpture seeks its own obliteration" into cybernetic life?
- Why does the sculptor keep the figure "still and silent," intuiting "that to induce motion forsakes the protective security of sculpture"? Is the mock robot a confession of sculpture's fear of its own dematerialization into [[Object and System|system]]?
- Does the robot's drift from "collective demon" to "semipersonal, fallible being" anticipate the vault's later companionable machines — O'Gieblyn's Aibo, the friendly AI — where the once-feared automaton becomes an intimate?
- "Anthropomorphic sculpture does not imitate man, but imitates robots trying to become human" — what does it mean for the human image when its model is no longer the human but the machine straining toward the human?
