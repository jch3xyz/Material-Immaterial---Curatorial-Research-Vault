# Trance and Automatism

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's altered-states artistic method: working "at what amounts to the level of trance" — an open, random, automatic process played against a deliberate grid — coupled with divinatory and occult apparatus (a "pictomancy" board, ouija boards, a wired planchette, a domed light-and-sound environment) used to drag visual configurations up "from a deep level of consciousness."

## Longer Explanation

In "The Psibernetic Arch" (1970), Ascott turns from the futurology of the essay's opening to his own painting practice and describes a procedure rooted in deliberate self-suspension. He finds it necessary "to operate at what amounts to the level of trance" — not a loss of intent but a heightened one: an "open, unconstrained but intense process of working that can be induced by random behaviour, open systems of play with marks, colours, and gestures interacting with a deliberate grid." The grid is the constant against which randomness becomes legible; trance is the means of generating, the grid the means of structuring. Ascott frames this "intimate level of operation" as the microcosm of a social macrocosm — the [[Psibernetics|psibernetic]] [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — and notes that "there is much to be learned from Tantric art" `Directly stated`.

The method shades from trance into outright **automatism** and divination. Ascott's working board "can become the arena for any kind of force, just as the ouija board seems to elicit information from a deep psychic level of the participants"; he names the goal "dragging up visual configurations from a deep level of consciousness." His coinage for the readable, divinatory artwork is **pictomancy**: he would like "the board to be read like the palm of the hand—'pictomancy'— a kind of non-figurative, all-at-once tarot." This is divination redesigned as abstract art — a tarot stripped of figures and read all at once rather than sequentially, "the crystal ball rolled out and transferred on to the flat surface of the board" as "an alternative future structured in terms of pure visual energy" `Directly stated`. The divinatory apparatus thus does double duty: it is both a generative technique and a vehicle for [[Futuribles|the art of futuribles]].

Ascott then scales the studio practice into built apparatus. With "a group of associates" he reports running "four or five ouija boards simultaneously," and plans to "feed instructions from a planchette on a wired table (on/off switches replace the circle of letters and numbers normally used) within a domed structure linked to an electronic system of light and sound" — which he names "a kind of system with a psychic brain." This is an early cybernetic-occult artwork: the psychic gesture of the planchette is digitised (on/off switches) and routed to an environmental light-and-sound system, so that the occult and the electronic close a single feedback loop. The phrase "a system with a psychic brain" anticipates Ascott's lifelong coupling of technology and consciousness `Directly stated`.

The essay's most far-reaching claim is the **clairvoyant-computer linkage**. Ascott sees "room for fascinating speculation on the possibilities of linking a sensitive clairvoyant to a computer so that it can acquire a psychic repertoire and in turn amplify the subject's messages and images." This is an early human-plus-machine-consciousness coupling: the computer is not a calculator but a partner that *learns a psychic repertoire from a person* and then *amplifies* that person's images back — a [[Distributed Cognition|distributed]] psychic cognition split across mind and machine. Read forward, it is a seed of Ascott's later [[Technoetics]] (his technology-and-consciousness program) and an early anchor for the book's later shamanism / altered-states cluster `Speculative connection`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — Trance and automatism are Ascott's personal artistic method (ch.9, 1970), explicitly continuous with his cybernetic and [[Psibernetics|psibernetic]] thought: the trance studio is the microcosm of the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]], "pictomancy" is the abstract-art form of divination, and the planchette-fed dome ("a system with a psychic brain") plus the speculative clairvoyant-computer link extend automatism into electronic and machine-coupled apparatus. The clairvoyant-computer linkage and "pictomancy" carry `Speculative connection` weight — they are Ascott's visionary register, not sober reportage.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Futuribles]] — not a true opposite but a corrective pairing: futuribles is the *deliberate, structured* generation of alternative futures, whereas trance and automatism is its *random, surrendered* counterpart; in Ascott they are yoked (the trance board "generate[s] anticipated future structures"), so the opposition is internal to a single method rather than between two camps.

## Questions It Raises

- If automatism surrenders authorial control to "random behaviour," in what sense is the resulting artwork still authored — and does the "deliberate grid" reinstate exactly the control the trance suspends?
- Is "a system with a psychic brain" a metaphor or a literal claim about the dome's apparatus? Where does Ascott's visionary register cross from method into mysticism?
- Does coupling a clairvoyant to a computer "so that it can acquire a psychic repertoire" attribute genuine cognition to the machine, or only treat it as an amplifier of human signal — and how does this early coupling relate to later [[Distributed Cognition|distributed-cognition]] accounts?
- Is "pictomancy" a serious epistemic claim (that an all-at-once non-figurative tarot can *read* a future) or an aesthetic conceit dressing abstraction in divinatory language?
