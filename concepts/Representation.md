# Representation

## Brief Definition

For [[Guy Debord]], representation is directly-lived life turned into image and appearance — the "separate pseudo-world that can only be looked at" (th.2) into which "everything that was directly lived has receded" (th.1), and whose independence continually regenerates the [[Spectacle]].

## Longer Explanation

Representation names the fundamental operation of the [[Spectacle]] as Debord diagnoses it in *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*. Under "modern conditions of production," life "is presented as an immense accumulation of spectacles," and "everything that was directly lived has receded into a representation" (th.1). Representation is thus not a neutral picture of the world but a *displacement* of lived experience into a domain of images that stands over against it. The register of the directly lived is emptied out, and what remains in its place is a world available only to be contemplated. (Directly stated.)

Crucially, Debord distinguishes representation from the [[Spectacle]] itself: the spectacle "is not a collection of images" but "a social relation between people that is mediated by images." Representation is the *form* — the image-appearance — through which that social relation is mediated and reproduced. Fragmented images "detached from every aspect of life" cannot be reassembled into a recoverable unity; instead "fragmented views of reality regroup themselves into a new unity as a separate pseudo-world that can only be looked at" (th.2). Representation is therefore always tied to [[Separation]]: it is what separated, autonomized fragments become when they congeal into a pseudo-world set apart from the life they were torn from. (Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis.)

The decisive claim about representation is that its *independence* is the engine of the spectacle. The spectacle "is the opposite of dialogue. Wherever representation becomes independent, the spectacle regenerates itself" (th.18). Where dialogue is reciprocal, corrigible activity between people, representation is "whatever escapes people's activity, whatever eludes their practical reconsideration and correction" (th.18). Once an image-appearance breaks free of the practice it should answer to, it becomes self-perpetuating; the spectacle is precisely this generalized independence of representation. This grounds representation as the near pole of the [[Reality vs Appearance]] tension: representation is the "social reign of appearances" that displaces reality as directly lived. (Directly stated.)

Debord extends representation from the register of images to the register of *political delegation* in the book's fourth chapter, "The Proletariat as Subject and Representation." When a revolutionary class is *represented* by a party or bureaucracy that stands in for it, the same logic of separation returns: "the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class" (th.100). The delegated representation autonomizes itself against those it claims to speak for, becoming "both the primary cause and the central result of the general falsification of society." Against this, a genuine revolutionary organization "cannot claim to represent the working class"; its task "is to embody a radical separation from the world of separation" (th.119) — a refusal of representation as such, and thus of the spectacle it feeds. (Directly stated.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — Representation is directly-lived life become image/appearance: the "separate pseudo-world that can only be looked at" (th.2) into which the lived has "receded" (th.1). It is "the opposite of dialogue," and "wherever representation becomes independent, the spectacle regenerates itself" (th.18). Debord also names its political form — delegated representation of a class — as "an enemy of the working class" (th.100), to be answered by "a radical separation from the world of separation" (th.119).

## Related Concepts

- [[Spectacle]] — the social relation mediated by images; representation is the image-form through which the spectacle is reproduced, and the spectacle "regenerates itself" wherever representation becomes independent (th.18).
- [[Separation]] — representation is what separated, autonomized fragments become when they "regroup themselves into a new unity as a separate pseudo-world" (th.2); the two concepts are paired.
- [[Reification]] — Debord's representation extends the Lukácsian/Marxian logic of lived relations congealing into an autonomous thing-like appearance that stands over against those who produced it.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Reality vs Appearance]] — representation is the appearance-pole: it is the "social reign of appearances" that displaces reality as directly lived, the pseudo-world set against "everything that was directly lived" (th.1–2).

## Questions It Raises

- If representation is "the opposite of dialogue" (th.18), what practices could restore reciprocity — and can any representation ever be non-independent, or is independence intrinsic to it?
- When representation of a class becomes "an enemy" of that class (th.100), does the critique of the spectacle imply that all political delegation is spectacular, or only its autonomized forms?
- How does Debord's "everything that was directly lived has receded into a representation" (th.1) relate to later diagnoses of image-saturated experience, and does the "directly lived" it presupposes remain a recoverable ground?
