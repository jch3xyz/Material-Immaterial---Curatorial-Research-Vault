# Immunological Age

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], the **Immunological Age** is the historical paradigm in which experience is structured by *immunological negativity* — a defence reaction against the Other, the Alien, the Enemy, organized on a Manichaean opposition of Good and Evil. Shock, as a violent intervention against an adversary, is its emblematic operation.

## General Orientation

"Immunological" has two distinct senses that this note keeps apart. The first is the *affirmative* immunitas tradition (Roberto Esposito), which reads contemporary society — epidemics, extradition fights, immigration barriers, computer viruses — as still organized by a protective immune response, immunization as the master category cutting across medicine, law, politics, and information technology. The second is **Han's epochal-shift sense**: the immunological is precisely the paradigm that has *passed*. Han names "the past century" an immunological age in order to argue that the twenty-first century is no longer one — that the defence against the Other is obsolete because Otherness itself is disappearing. This note treats Han's *The Burnout Society* (ch. 2, "Neuronal Power") as the term's fullest and primary exposition; Esposito appears only as the position Han refutes (captured inside the obsolescence material below), not as a co-author of the concept (`Directly stated` for Han's framing; `Interpretive synthesis` for the two-sense distinction).

## Longer Explanation

Han introduces the term in the chapter on **Shock**, reading Naomi Klein's *Shock Doctrine* through the figure of Dr Ewen Cameron ("Doctor Shock"), the Montreal psychiatrist whose CIA-funded electroshock experiments aimed to "de-brand" and "re-brand" the psyche. Cameron's anticommunism, his likening of patients to "communist prisoners of war," and the structure of his practice all rest, Han writes, on "the Manichaean opposition between Good and Evil": "Evil would be wiped away, eradicated, and replaced by Good." This is what makes Cameron exemplary of an *epoch*: "As Doctor Shock, Cameron represents a phenomenon of the Immunological Age. The immunological intervention of shock therapy takes aim at the Other, the Alien, the Enemy" (`Directly stated`).

The Immunological Age is thus defined by **[[Negativity]]** — the negativity of defence, threat, and annihilation. Shock therapy "owes its efficacy to paralyzing and annihilating the contents of the psyche"; "its essential trait is negativity." Han marks this as a *disciplinary* and biopolitical mode of power, distinct from the neoliberal regime that succeeds it. Where the immunological/disciplinary register works by negative threat against an Enemy, neoliberal psychopolitics "is dominated by [[Positivity]]. Instead of working with negative threats, it works with positive stimuli." The Immunological Age names, in other words, the era *before* the dissolution of immunological boundaries — the era of the recognizable adversary against whom a defence can still be mounted (`Strongly implied`).

Chapter 13, **Idiotism**, supplies the concept's inverse and clarifies why Han periodizes it as passing. Acceleration is incompatible with immunological reaction: "When communication is to be accelerated, idiosyncrasy poses an obstacle inasmuch as it amounts to an immunological defence against the Other." Because idiosyncrasy "stands in the way of unbounded communicative exchange," Han concludes that "immunosuppression is necessary for [[Acceleration]] to proceed." The accelerated, hyper-connected present therefore *suppresses* immune response so that "information will circulate faster and capital will accelerate." Its terminal condition is not defence against the Other but its abolition: "Communication achieves maximum velocity when the Same reacts to the Same" — the [[Hell of the Same]], where "the resistance and recalcitrance of otherness, of foreignness, troubles and impedes the smooth communication of the Same" (`Directly stated`).

Read across the two chapters, the Immunological Age is one term of a two-stage diagnosis: immunological negativity (defence, shock, the Enemy, disciplinary society) gives way to post-immunological positivity (smoothness, the Same, accelerated communication, neoliberal psychopolitics). The Other that the Immunological Age combated is, in the present, no longer expelled but dissolved — there is no Enemy left to immunize against (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## The Burnout Society (Han) — the immunological → neuronal epochal shift (PRIMARY source)

*The Burnout Society* opens with the term's fullest exposition. Han periodizes pathology: "a bacterial age existed; at the latest, it ended with the discovery of antibiotics," and "thanks to immunological technology, we have already left" the viral age behind. The present is neither bacterial nor viral but **neuronal** — depression, ADHD, BPD, and burnout, which "are not infections, but infarctions; they do not follow from the negativity of what is immunologically foreign, but from an excess of positivity." Against this, he defines the epoch that has just closed: "The past century was an immunological age. The epoch sought to distinguish clearly between inside and outside, friend and foe, self and other. The Cold War also followed an immunological pattern. Indeed, the immunological paradigm of the last century was commanded by the vocabulary of the Cold War, an altogether military dispositive. Attack and defense determine immunological action." The **immunological dispositive** has a "blind spot": "everything foreign is simply combated and warded off… it is eliminated on the basis of its Otherness." The structure is one of pure [[Negativity]]: "The dialectic of negativity is the fundamental trait of immunity. The immunologically Other is the negative that intrudes into the Own… The Own asserts itself in—and against—the Other by negating its negativity." Even prophylaxis obeys this logic — "Immunological prophylaxis, that is, inoculation, follows the dialectic of negativity… negation of negation occurs without the danger of death" (`Directly stated`).

Han's decisive move is to declare the immunological paradigm **obsolete**. "When a paradigm has come to provide an object of reflection, it often means that its demise is at hand"; a "paradigm shift has been underway," and "the Cold War ended precisely as this paradigm shift was taking place." Society now "escapes the immunological scheme of organization and defense altogether… It is marked by the disappearance of otherness and foreignness." Because "Otherness represents the fundamental category of immunology," its replacement by mere *difference* — which "in terms of immunology… represents the Same" — dissolves the very condition for an immune reaction. Crucially, the paradigm "proves incompatible with the process of globalization. Otherness provoking an immune reaction would work against the dissolution of boundaries." The figures who once anchored immunological reaction are deactivated: "Today, even the so-called immigrant is not an immunological Other, not a foreigner in the strong sense… Immigrants and refugees are more likely to be perceived as burdens than as threats," and "Foreignness itself is being deactivated into a formula of consumption. The alien is giving way to the exotic. The tourist travels through it. The tourist—that is, the consumer—is no longer an immunological subject." It is on these grounds that Han rejects Roberto Esposito's immunitas thesis: Esposito "makes a false assumption the basis of his theory of immunitas" because "his immunological analysis does not address contemporary problems, but only objects from the past" (Esposito rendered here as plain text — a position refuted, not a co-author of the concept) (`Directly stated`).

The closing pole of the periodization is **neuronal violence**, which "escapes all immunological optics, for it possesses no negativity. The violence of positivity does not deprive, it saturates; it does not exclude, it exhausts" — the "terror of immanence" set against the immunological horror that "emanates from the foreign" (Medusa as "the immunological Other in its extreme form"). Chapter 3 ("Beyond Disciplinary Society") then names the limit case that confirms the shift: "The self, in the strong sense of the word, still represents an immunological category. However, depression eludes all immunological schemes. It erupts at the moment when the achievement-subject is no longer able to be able." Burnout and depression thus belong not to the immunological epoch of the Other but to the [[Achievement Society]] of [[Positivity]], where "perpetrator and victim can no longer be distinguished" in [[Auto-Exploitation]] (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — *Psychopolitics*: the Immunological Age is the paradigm of immunological negativity, defence against the Other/Alien/Enemy on a Manichaean Good/Evil structure (ch. 7, Cameron as its emblem). Its inverse is the accelerated present, which requires the *immunosuppression* of idiosyncrasy and otherness so that "the Same reacts to the Same" (ch. 13). The concept belongs to a durable Han periodization that distinguishes a negative, immunological epoch from a positive, post-immunological one.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — *The Burnout Society* (**primary source**, ch. 2 "Neuronal Power"): the term's fullest exposition. A three-stage periodization of pathology (bacterial → viral/immunological → neuronal); the immunological *dispositive* as a "military" Cold-War vocabulary of friend/foe, inside/outside, self/other, with negation-of-negation (inoculation) as its logic and "the dialectic of negativity… the fundamental trait of immunity." The paradigm is declared **obsolete**: Otherness has disappeared / is incompatible with globalization, so the immigrant, the tourist, and the consumer are "no longer an immunological subject" and Esposito's immunitas thesis "address[es]… only objects from the past." Ch. 3 confirms the shift: "depression eludes all immunological schemes." The neuronal pole (excess positivity, saturation rather than exclusion) belongs to the [[Achievement Society]], not the immunological epoch.

## Related Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — the Immunological Age is the epoch *of* negativity (threat, defence, annihilation); shock's "essential trait is negativity."
- [[Hell of the Same]] — the post-immunological terminus, reached once immune defence against the Other is suppressed and "the Same reacts to the Same."
- [[Acceleration]] — incompatible with immunological reaction; "immunosuppression is necessary for acceleration to proceed."
- [[Smoothness]] — the smooth communication of the Same that immunological recalcitrance "troubles and impedes."
- [[Society of Control]] — neoliberal "total communication and total surveillance," the post-immunological power against which idiotism is set.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Positivity]] — the structural opposite epoch: neoliberal psychopolitics "is dominated by positivity… it works with positive stimuli," seducing rather than shocking the soul.
- *The Same* (post-immunological condition) — abolishes the Other/Enemy that the Immunological Age was organized to repel; rendered here as plain text pending a dedicated note.

## Questions It Raises

- If the present is post-immunological, does the disappearance of the Enemy also remove the very *negativity* through which resistance, distance, and otherness were possible?
- Is "immunosuppression for the sake of acceleration" a description of a power that no longer needs an outside — and what political form does opposition take once defence against the Other is itself the obstacle?
- How does the immunological model of the Other relate to Han's later, fuller periodizations (e.g. in *The Burnout Society*)? (Cross-book; flagged for consolidation.)
- Does Han's Manichaean reading of Cold War shock practice over-tidy the historical record, or is the Good/Evil structure doing diagnostic rather than merely descriptive work?
