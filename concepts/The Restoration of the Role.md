# The Restoration of the Role

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's name for the electric-age return of "the role" — a person reinvested with collective, nonvisual character — that appears in the place vacated when electric media dissolve the lineal, delegated, specialist power of the mechanical age.

## Longer Explanation

The concept is the positive counterpart to a destruction. In the Telephone chapter McLuhan argues that the telephone, by demanding "complete participation" and bypassing "all hierarchical arrangements," makes "delegated authority" unworkable: "It is not feasible to exercise delegated authority by telephone." The pyramid of "job-division and description and delegated powers cannot withstand the speed of the phone." What replaces the chain of command, on the telephone, is "the authority of knowledge" — "nonlineal, nonvisual, and inclusive." But that is only half the consequence. Alongside the inclusive authority of knowledge, McLuhan claims, something older returns: "in place of delegated power, there tends to appear again with electric media, the role. A person can now be reinvested with all kinds of nonvisual character." `Directly stated`

"The role" is McLuhan's term for an investiture that is collective rather than functional — the opposite pole of "the job," the specialist, fragmented, delegated task of mechanical-age literate man. Where the job assigns a private individual a narrow, visualizable function within a hierarchy, the role gathers up the collective and confers it on a person as "nonvisual character." His paradigm is monarchy: "King and emperor were legally endowed to act as the collective ego of all the private egos of their subjects." The king is not an official performing a delegated duty; he is the condensed embodiment of the community itself. McLuhan's claim is that electric media — by abolishing the center-margin order and demanding total, depth involvement — recreate the conditions in which such collective investiture again becomes possible. `Directly stated`

McLuhan is careful to date this as incipient and partial: "So far, Western man has encountered the restoration of the role only tentatively. He still manages to keep individuals in delegated jobs." His sharpest contemporary instance is the movie star, which he reads as an unwitting, half-conscious slide back into archaic investiture: "In the cult of the movie star, we have allowed ourselves somnambulistically to abandon our Western traditions, conferring on these jobless images a mystic role." The phrase "jobless images" is precise to his job/role opposition — the star performs no delegated function, holds no office, yet is loaded with collective meaning: stars "are collective embodiments of the multitudinous private lives of their subjects." The restoration of the role is thus not nostalgia but diagnosis: electric culture re-mythologizes persons, investing celebrities, leaders, and figures with the collective-ego weight that print culture had stripped away in favor of the private point of view. `Directly stated`

Within the vault the concept sits inside McLuhan's larger reversal of [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] and his account of electric [[Detribalization and Retribalization|retribalization]]: the role is the *recovered tribal investiture* of the print-individuated subject, the social-psychological face of the same implosion that produces [[The Global Village]]. It is paired with, and depends on, the dissolution of delegated authority described under [[The Authority of Knowledge]] — the role is what fills the space that "checks and balances" and "the separation of powers" once occupied, now that "the chain-of-command pyramids cannot obtain support from electric technology." A speculative line runs forward to the vault's surveillance and image thread: McLuhan's "jobless images" given "a mystic role," whose names and images "are bloated on ever widening screens" even as they grow "unapproachable in the flesh or phone," prefigure later accounts of the image-as-exhibition and the celebrity as a collective screen — though McLuhan's valence is more wonder than critique. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Job vs Role]] — The tension this concept inhabits. "The job" is the mechanical-age pole — delegated, specialist, visual, fragmented; "the role" is the electric-age pole — collective, nonvisual, participational, whole. The restoration of the role is the swing from job back toward role.

## Questions It Raises

- McLuhan presents the restored role neutrally, even admiringly, but his examples (king, emperor, the "mystic role" of the movie star) are forms of charismatic, anti-democratic investiture. Is the restoration of the role a recovery of communal meaning or the re-enchantment of authority that "checks and balances" were designed to restrain?
- If the role returns "somnambulistically" — without the West noticing it has "abandon[ed] our Western traditions" — who or what is invested? McLuhan names monarchs and stars; do electric leaders, brands, and platforms also become "collective embodiments of the multitudinous private lives of their subjects"?
- The role is "nonvisual character," yet McLuhan's movie star is precisely an *image* "bloated on ever widening screens." How does an investiture he calls nonvisual ride on the most intensely visual medium? Is the screen-image the role's vehicle or its contradiction?
- Does the restored role complete the recovered wholeness of the [[The Global Village|global village]], or does it concentrate collective psychic weight on single figures in a way that reintroduces a center the electric age was supposed to abolish?
