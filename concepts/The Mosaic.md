# The Mosaic

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's recurring form-concept for the mode of the corporate or collective image — a field of discontinuous, juxtaposed, simultaneous items carrying no point of view, unified only by a dateline — which commands deep, communal, inclusive participation, in direct contrast to the private, detached "point of view" of the book.

## Longer Explanation

For Marshall McLuhan in *Understanding Media* the mosaic is not a metaphor for one particular medium but a general *form* that recurs across the electric press, the photograph, the telegraph, the iconic ad, the phonograph's jazz, and above all the TV image. Its defining feature is the absence of a fixed vantage. Writing of the telegraph press, McLuhan contrasts "columns representing points of view, and a mosaic of unrelated scraps in a field unified by a dateline," and concludes flatly: "Whatever else there is, there can be no point of view in a mosaic of simultaneous items" (Chapter 25). The mosaic is a field of discontinuous items held together not by argument, narrative, or perspective but by sheer co-presence — "the dateline is the only organizing principle of the newspaper image of the community" (Chapter 21). (Directly stated.)

The mosaic's social signature is *participation*. "The mosaic is the mode of the corporate or collective image and commands deep participation. This participation is communal rather than private, inclusive rather than exclusive" (Chapter 21). Where the book is "a private confessional form that provides a 'point of view,'" the press is "a group confessional form that provides communal participation"; the mosaic form "means, not a detached 'point of view,' but participation in process" (Chapter 21). This places the mosaic squarely against [[Typographic Man]], the private, perspectival, detached reader produced by print; the mosaic *displaces* the "Gutenberg structural assumptions." McLuhan states it directly of advertising and television: "It is the new mosaic form of the TV image that has replaced the Gutenberg structural assumptions" (Chapter 23). (Directly stated.)

The mosaic is recursive and cross-medial. The press is "now not only a telephoto mosaic of the human community hour by hour, but its technology is also a mosaic of all the technologies of the community" (Chapter 21). The photograph dissolves national frontiers into "the total mosaic of the contemporary world" (Chapter 20); jazz "belongs in that family of mosaic structures that reappeared in the Western world with the wire services" (Chapter 28). The TV image is the purest instance: "The TV image is now a mosaic mesh of light and dark spots which a movie shot never is" (Chapter 31). Crucially, the mosaic is a low-definition form that demands completion by its receiver — "The TV image offers some three million dots per second to the receiver. From these he accepts only a few dozen each instant, from which to make an image" (Chapter 31) — which is why it links so tightly to McLuhan's [[Media Hot and Cold]] axis (the cool medium is the participational, mosaic one). (Directly stated.)

Finally, the mosaic is explicitly *not* a visual structure, and this is what distinguishes it from the lineal forms of print and the brick. "The repetitive, uniform brick, indispensable agent of road and wall, of cities and empires, is an extension, via letters, of the visual sense. The brick wall is not a mosaic form, and neither is the mosaic form a visual structure" (Chapter 31). The brick is uniform, continuous, repetitive, lineal — the logic of the visual sense and of [[Typographic Man]]; the mosaic is "discontinuous, skew, and nonlineal, like the tactual TV image" and "demands participation and involvement in depth of the whole being, as does the sense of touch" (Chapter 31). The mosaic is thus the formal hinge of McLuhan's larger opposition between lineal/visual/fragmented order and tactile/acoustic/inclusive order — an opposition the vault tracks as [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] and [[Detachment vs Participation]]. (Strongly implied — McLuhan does not use the phrase "acoustic space" in these six chapters, but the tactile/nonvisual character he assigns the mosaic is the same wholeness pole.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — coins and deploys "the mosaic" across the Part II case studies of *Understanding Media* (1964) as the form of the corporate/collective image: discontinuous juxtaposed items unified only by a dateline, carrying no point of view, commanding communal and inclusive participation. It is the form of the press, the telephoto news page, the iconic ad, the phonograph's jazz, and the low-definition TV image, and it is the form that displaces print's private "point of view" — [[Typographic Man]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Media Hot and Cold]] — the mosaic is the form of the *cool*, low-definition, high-participation medium; "from these he accepts only a few dozen" dots "from which to make an image" is the perceptual completion that defines coolness.
- [[Government by News Leak]] — the press chapter where McLuhan states the mosaic "commands deep participation… communal rather than private, inclusive rather than exclusive."
- [[The Brothel-without-Walls]] — the photograph as a corporate, collective image form ("Nobody can commit photography alone"); a mosaic-adjacent dissolution of the private viewpoint.
- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the mosaic sits on the participation pole: it demands "involvement in depth of the whole being."
- [[Fragmentation vs Wholeness]] — the mosaic's discontinuous items produce an inclusive, synesthetic wholeness rather than lineal fragmentation.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Typographic Man]] — the private, detached, perspectival reader created by print; the book is "a private confessional form that provides a 'point of view,'" and the mosaic of the TV image "replaced the Gutenberg structural assumptions" the typographic subject lives by. The mosaic is the form that displaces him.

## Questions It Raises

- If "there can be no point of view in a mosaic of simultaneous items," can the mosaic carry an argument or a politics at all, or only an inclusive *mood* of participation?
- McLuhan calls the mosaic communal, inclusive, and democratic ("the press is inseparable from the democratic process"), yet also notes Lenin, Stalin, and Khrushchev prized "the collective form of the press mosaic, with its magical power to impose its own assumptions." Is the mosaic's inclusiveness emancipatory or a tool of mass mobilization?
- Is the mosaic a property of the medium's *form* (the scanned dot-mesh, the dateline) or of the receiving culture's habits of perception, given that McLuhan ties it to tactility and the nonvisual sense-mix?
- Does the digital screen — high-definition yet endlessly juxtaposed and feed-organized — extend McLuhan's mosaic, or break the inverse law that the mosaic is necessarily *low* in data?
