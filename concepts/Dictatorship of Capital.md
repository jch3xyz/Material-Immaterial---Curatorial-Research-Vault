# Dictatorship of Capital

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], the **Dictatorship of Capital** is the name for what rules in place of Marx's "dictatorship of the proletariat": because classless [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploitation]] is now universal under [[Immaterial Labour|immaterial production]], there is no proletariat and no class antagonism, so a dictatorship *of the proletariat* is structurally impossible — and Capital instead "rules over everyone."

## Longer Explanation

The concept is Han's deliberate substitution for the central political category of classical Marxism. In "The Dictatorship of Capital" (the second section of *Psychopolitics*' opening chapter), Han accepts Marx's framing of a contradiction between forces and relations of production but denies its dialectical resolution: "communist revolution cannot resolve the contradiction... The contradiction admits no dialectical Aufhebung. Capitalism can always escape into the future precisely because it harbours permanent and inherent contradiction." Rather than turning into communism, "industrial capitalism has now mutated into neoliberalism and financial capitalism, which are implementing a post-industrial, immaterial mode of production." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The decisive premise is the abolition of the proletariat — not by revolution but by neoliberalism itself. Because production is immaterial, the worker becomes an entrepreneur: "no proletariat exists under the neoliberal regime at all. There is no working class being exploited by those who own the means of production. When production is immaterial, everyone already owns the means of production him- or herself. The neoliberal system is no longer a class system in the proper sense. It does not consist of classes that display mutual antagonism. This is what accounts for the system's stability." From this Han draws the conclusion that names the concept: "This means that a 'dictatorship of the proletariat' is structurally impossible. Today, the Dictatorship of Capital rules over everyone." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

What makes the Dictatorship of Capital *stable* rather than revolutionary is the form exploitation now takes. The neoliberal regime "transforms allo-exploitation into auto-exploitation; this process affects all 'classes'." This "classless self-exploitation — which was something utterly unknown to Marx — renders impossible any social revolution based on the difference between the exploiters... and the exploited," and "given the auto-exploiting achievement-subject's isolation, no political We is even possible that could rise up and undertake collective action." The exploited subject is "master and slave in one"; class struggle has "transformed into an inner struggle against oneself." There is no exploiting *other* to unite against, so solidarity has no object. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The system's intelligence lies precisely in this pre-emption of resistance. "People who fail in the neoliberal achievement-society see themselves as responsible for their lot and feel shame instead of questioning society or the system." Where classical allo-exploitation left the exploited "able to show solidarity and unite against those who exploit them" — the logic underwriting Marx's "dictatorship of the proletariat" — the neoliberal regime turns aggression inward: "people are turning their aggression against themselves. This auto-aggressivity means that the exploited are not inclined to revolution so much as depression." The Dictatorship of Capital therefore produces a clinical, not a political, outcome: depression and burnout in place of revolt. This is why, for Han, the system reproduces rather than collapses. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The concept routes two of Han's explicit disagreements. Against Marx (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Karl Marx]]), he denies the *Aufhebung* of the production-contradiction and the survival of the proletariat as a revolutionary subject. Against [[Antonio Negri]] (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Antonio Negri]]), he rejects the post-Marxist replacement of the proletariat by the "Multitude": "The cooperative 'Multitude' that Antonio Negri has exalted as the post-Marxist successor to the 'proletariat' does not describe the contemporary mode of production," whose conditions are "defined by the solitude of an entrepreneur who is isolated and self-combating and practises auto-exploitation voluntarily." For Han, "The Marxist scheme to which Negri adheres will prove to have been yet another illusion." (Label: `Directly stated`.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — coins the Dictatorship of Capital as the structural successor to (and refutation of) the "dictatorship of the proletariat." Because immaterial production makes everyone "an auto-exploiting labourer in his or her own enterprise," there is no class antagonism, no political *We*, and hence no revolutionary subject; Capital "rules over everyone," and the stability of this rule is what produces depression rather than revolt. The concept is bound up with his theological figure of [[Capital as Religion|Capital as a new transcendence]] — Capital as "a new master" before which politics "becomes the handmaiden of Capital." (Label: `Directly stated`.)
- **[[Karl Marx]]** — the foil. Han retains Marx's account of the forces/relations-of-production contradiction and his relational definition of freedom, but inverts the political conclusion: the contradiction is permanent and unsublatable, so capitalism mutates rather than resolving into communism, and the proletariat that would dictate has been dissolved. (Forward-stub — Marx's own texts are not yet ingested in `raw/`; cited by Han via *The German Ideology*, *Grundrisse*, and *Capital*.)
- **[[Antonio Negri]]** — the second foil. His "Multitude," advanced as the post-Marxist successor to the proletariat, is rejected by Han as failing to describe a mode of production defined by isolated, self-combating auto-exploitation. (Forward-stub — Negri's work is not yet ingested in `raw/`.)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the Dictatorship of Capital is the pole at which freedom (the auto-exploiting subject's voluntary self-exploitation) becomes the medium of total control; revolt is foreclosed because the controlling instance is internal.
- Dictatorship of the proletariat — `Directly stated`. The Marxist category the concept replaces: Han argues it is "structurally impossible" because the proletariat and the class antagonism it presupposes no longer exist.
- The Multitude (Negri) — `Directly stated`. The post-Marxist successor-subject Han rejects as not describing the contemporary, isolated, auto-exploiting mode of production.

## Questions It Raises

- If "no political We is even possible," is collective action structurally foreclosed under immaterial production, or only the specifically *class-based* form of it that Marx and Negri theorize? What other grounds of solidarity (if any) survive the dissolution of class?
- Han claims classlessness is "what accounts for the system's stability." Is stability here an empirical claim about neoliberalism's durability, or a definitional one (a system with no antagonistic outside cannot, by definition, be overthrown)?
- The concept converts a political outcome (revolution) into a clinical one (depression). Does this medicalize politics — treating the absence of revolt as pathology rather than as a contingent balance of forces?
- Han's premise that "everyone already owns the means of production him- or herself" under immaterial production is contestable: does the platform economy not concentrate ownership of infrastructure, data, and capital as sharply as industrial capitalism? Where does this leave the "no proletariat" claim?
- How does the Dictatorship of Capital relate to Han's later digital-psychopolitical argument that Big Data "has announced the end of the person who possesses free will" — is the abolition of the revolutionary subject completed politically (no We) and then again technologically (no free will)?
