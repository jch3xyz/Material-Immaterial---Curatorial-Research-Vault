# Singularitarian

## Brief Definition

A Singularitarian is Ray Kurzweil's self-applied identity: someone who *understands* (not believes in) the accelerating-technology trends of [[The Singularity]] and has reflected on their meaning for his or her own life — an understanding that "causes one to rethink everything," from health and wealth to death and self.

## Longer Explanation

The Singularitarian is the *believing, first-person pole* of Kurzweil's project — the chapter "Ich bin ein Singularitarian" is where he steps out of technological forecasting and states, in confessional register, what the [[The Singularity|Singularity]] means *for him*. He is emphatic that this is an epistemic stance, not a creed: "while being a Singularitarian is not a matter of faith but one of understanding, pondering the scientific trends I've discussed in this book inescapably engenders new perspectives on the issues that traditional religions have attempted to address: the nature of mortality and immortality, the purpose of our lives, and intelligence in the universe." Citing Max More, he insists "the last thing we need is another dogma, nor do we need another cult, so Singularitarianism is not a system of beliefs or unified viewpoints." What follows is offered as "my personal philosophy, not a proposal for a new doctrine." `Directly stated`

That personal philosophy is organized as a numbered creed. Its load-bearing points: that "we have the means right now to live long enough to live forever" by aggressively slowing aging until biotechnology and then nanotechnology arrive (the "bridge to a bridge to a bridge"); that "my body is temporary" because "only the pattern of my body and brain have continuity" — the link to [[Patternism]]; that "death is a tragedy," the loss of "a profound pattern (a form of knowledge)"; and that the purpose of life *and* of the universe is "to create and appreciate ever-greater knowledge, to move toward greater 'order'" and "greater intelligence." The creed culminates cosmologically: having reached a tipping point, "self-replicating nonbiological intelligence" will infuse the solar system and then the universe — the [[The Universe Wakes Up|universe waking up]] in Epoch Six. `Directly stated`

The Singularitarian's antagonist is traditional religion *as a death-management technology*. "A primary role of traditional religion is deathist rationalization—that is, rationalizing the tragedy of death as a good thing." Against Malcolm Muggeridge's "if it weren't for death, life would be unbearable," Kurzweil holds that the explosion of knowledge the Singularity brings "will make life truly meaningful." Yet the chapter is saturated with religious vocabulary — transcendence, spirit, soul, immortality, God, "we need a new religion" — and George Gilder, then Bill Gates, both read his optimism as "almost a religious faith." The Singularitarian thus simultaneously *denies* and *performs* a religious structure: it is the avowedly first-person voice that critics outside the program diagnose as techno-religion. `Strongly implied`

This note is kept **distinct from [[The Singularity]]** (the predicted *event*), mirroring the vault's book-vs-concept pattern (`Psychopolitics`, `Homo Deus`). Singularitarian names the *identity and personal philosophy of the person who has metabolized that event* — what Kurzweil calls "Singularitarianism." `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — the term's coiner and primary case. A Singularitarian "understands the Singularity and has reflected on its meaning for his or her own life"; the stance is "an understanding of basic technology trends" that is "simultaneously an insight that causes one to rethink everything." He distinguishes his own active position from "passive Singularitarianism" (which Max More warns can "engender a passivity in addressing today's issues"): because technology is "a double-edged sword," the Singularitarian must take a *proactive* stance, since "even small delays in implementing emerging technologies can condemn millions of people to continued suffering and death." `Directly stated`
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** (cross-reference, not in this raw file) — historicizes the Singularitarian self-statement from outside as secularized Christian eschatology; the "deathist rationalization" inversion and the immortality creed are exactly her material (see [[Transhumanism]], [[Meghan O'Gieblyn and Ray Kurzweil]]). `Interpretive synthesis`
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (cross-reference, not in this raw file) — frames the same believing pole as a [[Techno-Religion|techno-religion]] / evolutionary humanism, observed diagnostically rather than professed. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the Singularitarian holds the body to be "temporary" and the self to be its pattern; the embodiment thesis denies precisely this ranking of pattern over flesh.

## Questions It Raises

- Can a stance be "not a matter of faith but one of understanding" while using the full vocabulary of religion (soul, spirit, immortality, God, "a new religion")? Is the disavowal of dogma itself a rhetorical move?
- If the Singularitarian is defined by *understanding* trends rather than *believing* claims, what distinguishes it from the techno-religion its critics (O'Gieblyn, Harari) diagnose it as?
- The creed treats death as a "tragedy" to be defeated and the body as "temporary." What is lost — ethically, experientially — when [[Embodiment|embodiment]] is reframed as a maintenance problem?
- Kurzweil insists on a *proactive* Singularitarianism against passivity. Does anticipating that "only technology can provide the scale" to solve human problems crowd out non-technological politics?
