# Distributed Cognition

## Brief Definition

Cognition, judgment, and decision-making understood as residing not in an individual mind but across a system of human and nonhuman actors, devices, and environments coupled together — such that the system as a whole "thinks" more than any of its components.

## Longer Explanation

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], distributed cognition is one of the load-bearing moves by which the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist subject]] is reconstituted as the posthuman. The liberal subject locates thought, will, and identity inside a bounded, autonomous individual. Distributed cognition relocates the cognitive function outward, into the interactions between embodied humans and the "smarter environments" they build. Hayles draws her central case from Edwin Hutchins's study of oceangoing ship navigation, where "the cognitive system responsible for locating the ship in space and navigating it successfully resides not in humans alone but in the complex interactions within an environment that includes both human and nonhuman actors." The crucial inversion is that cognition is not internal and then externalized; rather, what looked like internalization is, in Hutchins's phrase that Hayles quotes, "a gradual propagation of organized functional properties across a set of malleable media."

The concept lets Hayles re-read John Searle's "Chinese room" against its author's intent. Searle imagined himself sealed in a room, shuffling Chinese characters by a rulebook without understanding a word, to argue that machines cannot really think. In Hutchins's "neat interpretation," which Hayles endorses, the argument backfires productively: "it is not Searle but the entire room that knows Chinese." The cognitive achievement belongs to the whole assemblage, "more than do any of its components, including Searle." This is the same structure that, in the Prologue, the Turing test silently installs: the test "puts you into a cybernetic circuit that splices your will, desire, and perception into a distributed cognitive system in which represented bodies are joined with enacted bodies through mutating and flexible machine interfaces." Distributed cognition is thus seeded at the book's threshold as the mechanism of becoming posthuman, long before it is named as such in the Conclusion.

In the Conclusion's catalogue of substitutions that define the benign — rather than apocalyptic — posthuman, "distributed cognition replaces autonomous will." This is the polemical heart of the concept. Against Joseph Weizenbaum's insistence that judgment is "a uniquely human function" that must never be ceded to machines, Hayles advances the claim that "conscious agency has never been 'in control.'" The "very illusion of control bespeaks a fundamental ignorance about the nature of the emergent processes through which consciousness, the organism, and the environment are constituted." Decision-making, like cognition, "is distributed between human and nonhuman agents" — Hutchins's failed steering system, charts, and pocket calculators solve their navigational problem at the level of the organization "before it was discovered by any of the participants."

Importantly for the user's foci, Hayles's distributed cognition does not dematerialize thought; it re-embodies it. Cognition is distributed across *material* media and enacted bodies, not lifted free of them. This is why she pairs the concept with embodiment in the same substitution list and uses it as "a potent antidote" to the fantasy of leaving the body behind for "the bodiless exultation of cyberspace." Where the dream of uploading consciousness treats mind as portable [[The Body as Data|information]], distributed cognition insists that "human functionality expands because the parameters of the cognitive system it inhabits expand" — through "electronic prosthesis," not against the body but as an extension of "embodied awareness in highly specific, local, and material ways."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — distributed cognition is the term that names how the posthuman subject is "distributed rather than located solely in consciousness." She deploys it normatively, as the substitute for "autonomous will" that makes a non-apocalyptic posthumanism thinkable, and she insists it is material and embodied, not a flight into pure information.
- **Edwin Hutchins** — Hayles's primary source for the concept; his ethnography of naval navigation supplies both the ship example and the gloss that thinking is "a gradual propagation of organized functional properties across a set of malleable media," with cognition and decision-making alike distributed across human and nonhuman actors.
- **Gregory Bateson** — supplies the framing that the emergent human subject "becomes a metaphor for" the distributed cognitive system as a whole, in which "'thinking' is done by both human and nonhuman actors."

## Related Concepts

- [[Posthumanism]] — distributed cognition is one of the constitutive substitutions ("distributed cognition replaces autonomous will") that define Hayles's preferred posthuman.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the cybernetic "circuit" / "splice" is the formal structure of a distributed cognitive system; the concept is born from the cybernetic reframing of self and environment.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the human and nonhuman actors are coupled through recursive loops; the Turing test alters "who can think" "in a reverse feedback loop."
- [[Embodiment]] — distributed cognition for Hayles is embodied and material, an extension rather than an abandonment of the body.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the spliced human-machine assemblage is the figure whose cognition is distributed.
- [[Systems Theory]] — cognition resides in "the system as a whole," whose capacities exceed those of its parts.
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the extension of cognition across prosthetic, material media.
- The Observer — distinct from distributed cognition: the single observer included in a reflexive system is one participant in, not the locus of, the distributed cognitive whole.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-bounded individual whose conscious will is "the essence of human identity"; distributed cognition is precisely the dissolution of that locus, the claim that "conscious agency has never been 'in control.'"
- [[The Body as Data]] — the fantasy that mind is portable information to be uploaded; distributed cognition opposes this by re-anchoring thought in material media and embodied enaction, even as both belong to the same posthuman seriation. (Relationship is dialectical rather than flatly oppositional — see [[Embodiment vs Information]].)

## Questions It Raises

- If "conscious agency has never been 'in control,'" what becomes of responsibility, judgment, and ethics — the very faculties Weizenbaum reserved for humans? Does distributed cognition dissolve accountability or merely relocate it to the system?
- Distributed cognition is invoked to keep the posthuman embodied and material — yet the substrate-independence of information (Shannon, Wiener) was what let mind seem uploadable in the first place. Does the concept resolve the tension of [[Embodiment vs Information]], or does it inherit it?
- If cognition is "distributed across a set of malleable media," is there a principled line between the [[The Body as Data|body-as-information]] and the body as one material actor among others — between the [[Material vs Immaterial|material and the immaterial]] in the cognitive circuit?
- For a curatorial/exhibition context: if "thinking" is done by "both human and nonhuman actors," what is the status of the apparatus, the interface, the installation — are they participants in cognition or merely its supports?
- How does the included [[Embodiment|embodied]] observer differ from the distributed system that observes through it? (The Observer vs Distributed Cognition distinction.)
