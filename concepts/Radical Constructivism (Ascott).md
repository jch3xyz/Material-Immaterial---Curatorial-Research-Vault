# Radical Constructivism (Ascott)

## Brief Definition

The epistemological position — drawn by Roy Ascott from Paul Watzlawick's "radical constructivism," Richard Rorty's pragmatism, and the observer-dependence of quantum physics — that "reality is built by us," so that the old documentary-truth-versus-faking debate is obsolete and computer networking becomes a means to construct and inhabit multiple coexisting realities.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, the question that haunted twentieth-century photography and representation — whether an image faithfully *represents* the real or merely *fakes* it — is the wrong question. We are, he writes, "sufficiently self-aware to know that reality is built by us, to fit our psychic and sensory specifications and to satisfy our conceptual longings" (ch.21). Once reality is understood as a construct rather than a given, the documentary anxiety dissolves: "In post-photographic practice, the lust for verisimilitude gives way to the love of modeling" (ch.21). The artist's role shifts from depicting a pre-existing world to *modeling* worlds and admitting the viewer "into the process of construction." Ascott names his source directly: "Just as, in his contributions to radical constructivism, Paul Watzlawick asks how we know what we believe we know, so photography has the potential to show how realities are constructed" — and he invokes Rorty's "Contingency, irony, and solidarity" as the pragmatist register in which this constructed, relativistic relation to truth operates (`Directly stated`, ch.21).

Ascott grounds the position not only in pragmatist philosophy but in the physics of information. In the *Gesamtdatenwerk* essay (ch.18) he proposes that "replace the term 'quanta' with 'data' and a physics of information is invoked. For it is in the quantum world that reality is the production of observation." Immersion in electronic global networks, he argues, drives "a re-evaluation of the status of reality; to an understanding of its provisional nature, as one of many co-existing realities, all of which are constructed." The observer-dependence is literalized in ch.24: "We are dealing here with observer-created reality. To look is to have the system jump from a both/and situation to an either/or outcome, the quantum jump producing what is known as the eigenstate." Reality, on this telling, is not measured but *precipitated* by the act of observation (`Directly stated`, ch.18, ch.24).

What distinguishes Ascott's constructivism from the relativism of his contemporaries is its affirmative, even utopian charge. Where postmodernism reads the loss of stable truth as cause for "negative pessimism," Ascott reads it as an opening: "It is through computer networking that we can deal creatively with relativism and with pluralism, providing a radically constructive repudiation of the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought" (ch.18). The network is the instrument that lets us *build* and *enter* alternative realities rather than mourn the loss of the one true one. This is why he can fold the position into a program for art: in "The Mind of the Museum" (ch.29) the constructivist premise — "we talk the world into being, that our descriptions are its becoming" — becomes a curatorial and poetic injunction: "Since the world is all metaphor, let us build metaphors of alternative realities in the midst of our material environment" (`Directly stated`, ch.29).

This makes radical constructivism the epistemological engine beneath Ascott's larger program of [[Telematic Art]], [[New Media Art]], and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]: if reality is constructed and multiple, then the networked, interactive, immersive artwork is not a *representation* of the world but an apparatus for the collaborative *production* of new ones (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — radical constructivism as an affirmative epistemology synthesized from Watzlawick (the term and the "how do we know what we believe we know" question), Rorty (contingency/irony/pragmatism), and quantum observer-dependence ("reality is the production of observation," "observer-created reality"). The networking artist constructs and enters multiple coexisting realities; the position is offered explicitly as "a radically constructive repudiation of the negative pessimism in so much postmodernist thought."

## Related Concepts

- [[Intersubjective Reality]] — strong resonance, NOT a merge: Harari's intersubjective reality (money, nations, gods) is a *shared fiction* sustained by collective belief and large-scale cooperation; Ascott's constructed reality is grounded instead in the *individual/quantum observer* and in network-mediated participation. Both deny a single mind-independent reality, but Ascott's is affirmative-poietic (build new worlds) where Harari's is diagnostic (these worlds are stories).
- [[Idealism]] — resonance, NOT a merge: O'Gieblyn's treatment of idealism (mind-first ontologies, the measurement problem read as consciousness-creating-reality) overlaps with Ascott's "reality is the production of observation," but Ascott routes the claim through art-making and pragmatist contingency rather than metaphysics of mind.
- [[The Measurement Problem]] — the quantum substrate Ascott leans on: the wave-function collapse / eigenstate that he reads as literal observer-created reality (he names Bell and Aspect; ch.24 places the collapse "right in the brain" via Greg Egan).
- [[Virtuality]] — Ascott's "co-existing realities, all of which are constructed—'virtual' in a sense" overlaps Hayles's analysis of the virtual.
- [[Telematic Art]] · [[New Media Art]] · [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the artistic programs this epistemology underwrites.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — radical constructivism leans hard to the immaterial/informational pole: reality as data, as observation, as metaphor ("the world is all metaphor"), with the material environment recast as a site to seed "metaphors of alternative realities." It opposes any documentary realism that grants a settled, mind-independent material ground (`Strongly implied`).

## Questions It Raises

- If reality is "built by us," what constrains construction — is any model as real as any other, or do Watzlawick's "internal coherence" and Rorty's "solidarity" smuggle in non-arbitrary criteria?
- Ascott offers networking as a "radically constructive repudiation" of postmodern pessimism — but is networked multiplicity genuinely affirmative, or does it merely automate the relativism it claims to overcome?
- How does observer-created reality at the quantum scale legitimately license a cultural epistemology — is the "physics of information" an argument or a metaphor (Ascott himself concedes the physicists' ideas are "scandalously metaphysical at heart," ch.29)?
- If "the world is all metaphor," what becomes of the curatorial/documentary claim to preserve evidence — does the museum conserve truth, or merely "metaphors that have outlived their usefulness" (ch.29)?
