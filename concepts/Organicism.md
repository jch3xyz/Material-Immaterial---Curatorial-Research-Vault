# Organicism

## Brief Definition

For Jack Burnham, organicism is a twin-sense creed: (1) the awareness of the interrelation between systems and their components within larger systems, so that the behavior of the whole ensemble can be understood and manipulated; and (2) any arrangement possessing the attributes of life.

## Longer Explanation

In *Beyond Modern Sculpture* Burnham treats organicism not as a fixed term but as "an evolving creed" — a mode of awareness whose meaning has migrated across architecture, sculpture, biology, and, decisively, postwar systems analysis. He offers two non-equivalent but overlapping definitions: organicism "as a conscious concern of the designer" is "the awareness of the interrelation between systems and their components within larger systems so that behavior of the whole ensemble can be understood and manipulated"; organicism "could also be defined as any arrangement possessing the attributes of life." The first definition "relates closely to machines, factories, and social systems"; the second "is obviously tied to biological life." Crucially, Burnham argues that "the nature of the second definition is often the working objective of the first" — the "bringing to life" of machines and social systems is, in his account, "the unconscious obsession of the twentieth-century technician," just as the "bringing to life" of wood and stone has always been the obsession of the sculptor. `Directly stated`

Burnham traces the genealogy of the term carefully. Citing the writer Lancelot Law Whyte, he dates the modern usage of organicism — "a harmonious arrangement of parts" — to between 1720 and 1750, but credits Horatio Greenough in America with first extolling organicism "as a virtue of right living and thinking," coining the phrase "organic beauty." Above this American lineage he places Lewis Mumford's *Technics and Civilization*, which "over thirty years ago" charted "an increasing gravitation toward what he termed 'an organic ideology.'" For Burnham, Mumford's key claim — against the displacement thesis that technology would eventually supplant living beings — was that "the real destiny of the machine was to merge itself with natural organisms." This makes organicism "the harmonious fusion of the natural and the man-made," an ideal Burnham says society "has yet to realize, except in isolated instances." `Directly stated`

The book's central historical claim is that organicism finds its fullest modern realization not in art or architecture but in postwar systems work. Since the Second World War, Burnham observes, "a number of scientific and technological disciplines have recognized the need for restructuring along organic organizational lines," and "the largest and most comprehensive attempt to use the analogues of biological organization has been systems analysis and development." Wartime man-machine units (bombers, gun stations, radar) "deemed too complex to be controlled by human reflexes" forced "studies in the systematic organization and control of such groups." Some experts, he notes, "regard the organization of men and machines into optimally functioning systems as tantamount to creating synthetic organisms." This is why organicism, for Burnham, points toward [[Systems Theory]] and the broader project of [[Cybernetics]]: the convergence of biology, machine theory, and sculpture "toward a single end result — the understanding of living matter through its creation." `Directly stated`

Burnham holds organicism in deliberate polar tension with [[Vitalism]]. "Vitalism is a preeminently poetic view of life, a celebration of the natural condition, while organicism is a beautiful view of the utilitarian evolution of systems, both biological and non biological." The two are not simply opposites: "As modes of awareness, organicism and vitalism have influenced each other; in some essential ways they remain in polar opposition." Sculptors, he notes, have "wrongly referred to their work as being 'organic'" — only a few, such as Henry Moore, "saw the inappropriateness of the word and supplied 'vitalistic' in its stead" — because sculpture's claim to the organic was merely its ability to "look plantlike, rocklike or animal-like," not the functional working of systems and subsystems that organicism properly names. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — organicism as a twin-sense evolving creed: the design-systems sense (interrelation of systems and components, so the whole ensemble can be understood and manipulated) and the biological sense (any arrangement possessing the attributes of life), the second often being the working objective of the first; realized most fully in postwar systems analysis; held in polar opposition to [[Vitalism]].
- **[[Lewis Mumford]]** — Burnham's primary source for the lineage: *Technics and Civilization* charts the gravitation toward "an organic ideology," dating its turning point to 1870, and holds that "the real destiny of the machine was to merge itself with natural organisms" (a forward-stub author; cited substantively by Burnham, no book yet in `raw/`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Systems Theory]] — the discipline in which Burnham says organicism is most fully realized; systems analysis as "the largest and most comprehensive attempt to use the analogues of biological organization."
- [[Cybernetics]] — named by Burnham (with information theory, electronic circuit theory, systems analysis) as the science that for the first time lets a culture "literally fuse organic activities with the linear-geometric precision of machines."
- [[Emergence]] — the systems-organicist intuition that the behavior of a whole ensemble exceeds, and can be understood and manipulated beyond, that of its components.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Vitalism]] — the poetic celebration of a life force versus organicism's utilitarian account of the evolution of systems; the two "have influenced each other" yet "remain in polar opposition." See the tension [[Vitalism vs Organicism]].

## Questions It Raises

- If "the nature of the second definition is often the working objective of the first," is organicism ultimately a program for *manufacturing* life — and does that collapse the very distinction between the organic and the machine?
- Burnham locates organicism's fullest realization in systems analysis (corporations, weapons, automation), not in art. Does the "moral imperative" he attributes to the term survive that migration, or does organicism become merely an instrument of control?
- If organicism and vitalism remain "in polar opposition" yet "have influenced each other," what does abstract vitalist sculpture inherit from the systems-organicist worldview it claims to refuse?
