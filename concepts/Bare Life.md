# Bare Life

## Brief Definition

Bare life is life stripped of all narrative, transcendent, and teleological value and reduced to "the immanency of vital functions and capacities, which are to be maximized by any and all means" — the condition Han argues late-modern achievement society has universalized, so that "all of us, without exception" become *homines sacri*, yet with the predicate inverted: this bare life is not absolutely killable but absolutely *un*-killable, "undead," holy rather than accursed, "preserved at any cost."

## Longer Explanation

Han borrows "bare life" and *homo sacer* from [[Giorgio Agamben]] but redirects both. In Agamben's account — which Han summarizes accurately before turning against it — *homo sacer* "refers to someone excluded from society because of a trespass: one may kill him without incurring punishment," and so "stands for absolutely expendable life," exemplified by "Jews in concentration camps, prisoners at Guantanamo, people without papers or asylum-seekers awaiting deportation in a lawless space, and patients attached to tubes and rotting away in intensive care." For Agamben this is a life produced by sovereignty at the margins, in a state of exception. Han's first move is to **universalize** it: "If late-modern achievement society has reduced us all to bare life, then it is not just people at the margins or in a state of exception—that is, the excluded—but all of us, without exception, who are *homines sacri*." (`Directly stated` — Ch. 5, "Vita Activa")

Han's decisive second move is to **invert the predicate**. Agamben's bare life is defined by being killable with impunity; Han's is defined by the opposite. "This bare life has the particularity of not being absolutely expendable [*tötbar*]; rather, it cannot be killed absolutely [*absolut untötbar (ist)*]. It is undead, so to speak. Here the word *sacer* does not mean 'accursed' but 'holy.' Now bare, sheer life itself is holy, and so it must be preserved at any cost." Where sovereignty produced a life exposed to death, achievement society produces a life condemned to survival — "too alive to die, and too dead to live." This is why Han can call it "even barer than the life of *homo sacer*": "Life today is even barer than the life of homo sacer." (`Directly stated` — Ch. 5, Ch. 9)

What makes it barer is the evacuation of meaning. Bare life arises, for Han, from "the general denarrativization of the world," the "modern loss of faith" that "involves reality itself and makes human life radically fleeting." With the collapse of "narrative thanatotechnics" — the religions that once removed the fear of death and conferred a "feeling of duration" — "the unconditional compulsion arises to keep bare life healthy." In the [[Achievement Society|achievement society]] this compulsion does not produce the brute *Gelassenheit* of the animal but its opposite: "hyperactivity, hysterical work, and production." Stripped of any "horizon of meaning extended beyond bare life," "the cult of health" achieves an absoluteness it could not otherwise attain — "after the death of God, health rose to divine status." (`Directly stated` — Ch. 5)

Chapter 9 gives the concept its sharpest formulation, the one this note takes as its definitional core: bare life "is bare because, stripped of all transcendent value, it has been reduced to the immanency of vital functions and capacities, which are to be maximized by any and all means." From this immanence Han derives the society's pathological telos: "The inner logic of achievement society dictates its evolution into a doping society. Life reduced to bare, vital functioning is life to be kept healthy unconditionally. Health is the new goddess. That is why bare life is holy." The cult of health and the resulting doping society are thus not side-effects but the direct corollary of bare life — a life with no "in-order-to [*jedes Umzu*]" left, whose health has become "self-referential" and "voids itself into purposiveness without purpose." (`Directly stated` — Ch. 9)

> *Awaits Agamben.* Han's bare life is, by his own framing, a borrowing-and-inversion of a concept he attributes to Agamben's *Homo Sacer*, which is not in the library. The Agamben-owned [[Homo Sacer]] forward-stub and the cross-author dispute note [[Byung-Chul Han and Giorgio Agamben]] are kept open for a future [[Giorgio Agamben]] author-section that will ground the source-side of this concept directly rather than through Han's quotation. ## Byung-Chul Han (The Agony of Eros)

Where *The Burnout Society* routed bare life through Agamben's *homo sacer* and the loss of narrative, *The Agony of Eros* (Ch. 4, "Bare Life") re-grounds the same concept in the **incapacity for death** — and, from there, in the incapacity for [[Eros]]. Han reads Hegel's master/slave dialectic as "the battle for life and death": the master "does not fear death," while "preferring servitude to the threat of death, the slave clings to bare life." What proves decisive is not physical superiority but "the 'ability to die,' or a capacity for death": "Those who do not have freedom unto death (*Freiheit zum Tod*) do not risk their life." The slave "does not venture as far as death, and therefore becomes a vassal who labors." Bare life is thus the correlate of a life that cannot die — the same disqualification that bars the labouring, self-preserving subject from the erotic, since "eros as excess and transgression denies both work and bare life." (`Directly stated`)

This chapter supplies the economic and theological register the *Burnout Society* account only gestured at. "Capitalism absolutizes bare life. Its telos is not the good life. Capitalism's compulsive accumulation and growth is specifically aimed against death, which counts as absolute loss." Han invokes Aristotle — accumulating capital "merits scorn because it has no concern for the good life—only for bare life" — to argue that production, "by doing away with the teleology of the good life," accelerates "by losing all sense of direction": "This is how capitalism becomes obscene." (See [[Capital as Religion]].) The *Burnout Society*'s "health as the new goddess" recurs here as the terminal form of this defense: "Today, the defense of bare life is intensifying into the absolutization and fetishization of health," so that "health per se represents an absolute value" and is "exalted and made the 'great goddess.'" "Where bare life is hallowed, theology gives way to therapy." (`Directly stated`)

The chapter's most concentrated figure is the **survivor as the undead**: "A survivor is like the undead: too dead to live, and too alive to die" — allegorized in Wagner's Flying Dutchman, the ship "manned by the undead," "flying like an arrow without aim." This survival is a mode of the [[Hell of the Same|inferno of the same]] and the pure expression of [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploitation]]'s enslaved master: "In a society where everyone is an entrepreneur of the self, the economy of survival reigns. It stands diametrically opposed to the non-economy of eros and death." And because a life that clings to survival cannot admit the [[Negativity|negativity]] of death, it forfeits vitality itself: "Whatever is merely positive is lifeless. Negativity is essential to vitality." Bare life's "vigor or fitness" is therefore *not* vitality — it is "a slave's life," survival "in the face of discontinuity." (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Giorgio Agamben]]** — origin of the concept. For Agamben, bare life (*nuda vita*) is the life of *homo sacer*: "absolutely expendable life," produced by sovereignty in the state of exception, that "can be destroyed at any time" because it "stands outside the legal order." "Sovereignty's originary achievement is to have produced the bare life of homo sacer." *Forward-stub:* Agamben has no book in the library; his usage here is mediated entirely through Han's quotation and paraphrase, and Han charges him with an "anachronism" — describing a "society of sovereignty" while standing "at the middle of the society of achievement," holding to "the scheme of negativity" and so unable to grasp "the violence of positivity." This note is kept open for a future Agamben author-section. (`Directly stated`, via Han's citations)
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — universalizes *homo sacer* ("all of us, without exception") and inverts its predicate. The bare life of the [[Achievement Society|achievement society]] is *absolut untötbar* — un-killable, "undead," "holy" not "accursed," "preserved at any cost," and "even barer than the life of homo sacer." Its injunction is not the sovereign's *Bann* but "the injunction to achieve [*Bann der Leistung*]": "The achievement-subject that considers itself free and fashions itself as *homo liber*... stands under the injunction to achieve; thereby, it makes itself into *homo sacer*. The sovereign of achievement society, then, is the *homo sacer* of his own self." Bare life is, finally, life "reduced to the immanency of vital functions," to be "maximized by any and all means" — the seed of the "doping society" in which "health is the new goddess." In *The Agony of Eros* Han re-grounds all of this in the **incapacity for death**: the slave "clings to bare life" because he lacks "freedom unto death"; capitalism "absolutizes bare life," its accumulation "specifically aimed against death"; the survivor is "the undead: too dead to live, and too alive to die"; and this same incapacity for death disqualifies the subject from [[Eros|eros]] ("eros as excess and transgression denies both work and bare life"). (`Directly stated`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Homo Sacer]] — the Agamben figure Han borrows, universalizes, and inverts; bare life is the life *homo sacer* possesses, which Han re-predicates as un-killable rather than killable. *(forward-stub — awaits Agamben)*
- [[Achievement Society]] — the formation that, on Han's account, reduces "us all to bare life," replacing the sovereign's external injunction with the internal "injunction to achieve."
- [[Healing as Killing]] — the cult-of-health corollary: when bare life "must be preserved at any cost" and "health is the new goddess," care turns coercive and self-preservation becomes a form of self-violence.
- [[Self-Optimization]] — the practical regime of a life "to be maximized by any and all means"; the doping society is self-optimization pushed to vital functioning with no purpose beyond itself.
- [[Biopolitics]] — adjacent but distinct: in *Psychopolitics* Han bounds biopolitics to the disciplinary body and population; here, in the earlier *Burnout Society*, bare life names the reduction of the subject to vitality from the side of meaning and narrativity rather than demographic administration.
- [[Eros]] — the erotic counter-force *The Agony of Eros* opposes to bare life: "eros as excess and transgression denies both work and bare life," and "the economy of survival" is "diametrically opposed to the non-economy of eros and death." The subject that "clings to bare life," lacking "freedom unto death," is thereby "incapable of erotic experience."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — bare life's social form in *The Agony of Eros*: "everyone is an entrepreneur of the self," master and slave at once, whose "economy of survival reigns."
- [[Capital as Religion]] — capitalism as "the absolutization of bare life," accumulation "specifically aimed against death," production that "becomes obscene" by abolishing the teleology of the good life.
- [[Negativity]] — the faculty bare life forecloses: "Negativity is essential to vitality"; "whatever is merely positive is lifeless"; the "vigor or fitness of bare life" is not vitality because it "lacks all negativity."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Narrativity]] — the direct opposite. Bare life is produced precisely by "the general denarrativization of the world"; narrative thanatotechnics once gave life "duration or substance [*Bestand*]," and their loss "makes life bare." A life that "lacks all narrative content" is reduced to "biological, vital processes."

## Questions It Raises

- Is Han's inversion (un-killable holy life) a *correction* of Agamben or a *different concept* wearing Agamben's name? He keeps the term "bare life" and "*homo sacer*" while reversing their defining predicate — does the borrowing survive the inversion?
- Han charges Agamben with "anachronism" for reading sovereignty's negativity into a society of positivity. But Agamben's own argument is that the state of exception has become the rule — is Han's universalization closer to Agamben than his polemic admits? (awaits the Agamben author-section to adjudicate)
- If bare life "cannot be killed at all" and is "preserved at any cost," does the language of *homo sacer* — historically the killable one — still illuminate the achievement-subject, or does it strain past breaking?
- Does the reduction of life "to the immanency of vital functions... to be maximized by any and all means" name a loss of meaning, or a *transfer* of meaning into health, fitness, and survival themselves? (the cult of health; the doping society; health as the new goddess)
- If bare life is the correlate of the *incapacity for death* (*The Agony of Eros*), is its cure a recovered "freedom unto death"? And is the erotic — "the non-economy of eros and death" — the only exit from the economy of survival, or merely its mirror-negation?
