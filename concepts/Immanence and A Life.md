# Immanence and A Life

## Brief Definition

The late-Deleuzian metaphysics of *pure immanence* — a plane that is "A LIFE, and nothing else," self-sufficient and subject to nothing — which Han adopts as the metaphysical ground of the idiot's de-subjectivation and sets against Capital figured as alienating transcendence.

## Longer Explanation

In the closing pages of the chapter on idiotism, Han borrows the formula of Gilles Deleuze's final work, *Pure Immanence: Essays on a Life*, to give the figure of the [[Idiotism|idiot]] a metaphysical foundation. Deleuze, in Han's citation, "elevates immanence to a formula for beatitude": pure immanence "is A LIFE, and nothing else." Crucially, this is "not immanence *to* life" — immanence is not a property *of* a pre-existing life or subject — but rather "the immanent that is in nothing is itself a life." A life is therefore "the immanence of immanence, absolute immanence," and as such "complete power, complete bliss" (`Directly stated`).

Han glosses the formula ontologically. Immanence "is in nothing" because "it does not occur in something else — it exists only in itself"; it is "subject to nothing" and so "self-sufficient." From this self-sufficiency Han draws the political consequence that matters most to his argument: "No order of domination can be erected on this level of life's immanence" (`Directly stated`). The plane of immanence is, by its very structure, a surface on which no hierarchy, no command, no relation of power can find purchase. This is what makes it the matrix for a politics of freedom rather than a mere mystical retreat.

Against this self-sufficient plane Han poses Capital. "In contrast, Capital manifests itself as transcendence: it estranges life from itself." Where immanence is in nothing and exists only in itself, Capital is the form that stands *over* and *outside* life, introducing the alienating split between life and itself. "Immanence as life suspends this alienating relationship" (`Directly stated`). The opposition immanence/transcendence is thus mapped directly onto life/Capital, with immanence the dissolving, de-alienating term (`Strongly implied`).

This is why immanence underwrites the idiot's de-subjectivation. "Pure immanence is the void, which can neither be psychologized nor subjectivized"; "the idiot is not defined by individuality or subjectivity, but by singularity." Han's exemplary case is the child — "not yet an individual, not yet a person" — who has no subjective qualities but only singularities (a smile, a gesture) and is "infused with an immanent life that is pure power and even bliss" (`Directly stated`). The idiot, like Deleuze's "homo tantum," sheds the subject not by lack but by entry into a plane that precedes and undoes subjectivation; immanence is the negative space (in Han's sense of [[Negativity]]) that "wrests the subject out of itself" (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Gilles Deleuze]]** — the source. The formula is taken verbatim from *Pure Immanence: Essays on a Life* (trans. Anne Boyman, Zone Books, 2001) and from *What Is Philosophy?*. For Deleuze "a life" is impersonal singularity, the immanence that is in nothing but itself; this note realizes the previously committed `Gilles Deleuze` author forward-stub by citation. (`Directly stated`)
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — the adopter. Han does not develop immanence as an independent metaphysics; he conscripts Deleuze's formula as the ground for [[Idiotism]] and as the antithesis of neoliberal domination, reading "no order of domination can be erected" on the plane of immanence as the metaphysical guarantee of the idiot's resistance. He aligns transcendence with Capital and immanence with the suspension of alienation. (`Strongly implied`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Idiotism]] — the practice this concept grounds; the idiot is the figure who gains admittance to the "sphere of immanence," "the matrix of de-subjectivation and de-psychologization."
- [[Negativity]] — Han identifies the plane of immanence with negativity that wrests the subject out of itself; immanence is the "void," the empty time, that de-subjectivizes.
- [[Society of Control]] — Capital-as-transcendence and "total communication and total surveillance" are what immanence-as-life suspends; the plane of immanence is the outside no control society can colonize.
- [[Acceleration]] — immanence withdraws from the horizontal plane of networked, accelerated communication into a vertical, self-sufficient stillness.

## Opposing Concepts

- Capital as transcendence — within the chapter itself, immanence's structural opposite: Capital "estranges life from itself," which "immanence as life suspends."
- [[Idealism]] — O'Gieblyn's idealism makes mind or consciousness the fundamental ground; Deleuze/Han's "A LIFE" is pre-subjective, impersonal singularity that "can neither be psychologized nor subjectivized" — immanence is precisely *not* a metaphysics of mind. (`Speculative connection`)
- [[Panpsychism]] — likewise distinct: panpsychism distributes consciousness or experience as a property of matter, whereas Deleuze's immanence "is in nothing" and is "not immanence *to* life" — it is not a property predicated of substances at all. (`Speculative connection`)

## Questions It Raises

- If "no order of domination can be erected" on the plane of immanence, is immanence a genuinely *political* resource, or only an apophatic escape — a "void" that resists power by refusing to be anything power can grasp?
- Han equates transcendence with Capital and immanence with liberation. Does this inversion of the usual theological valence (transcendence as the alienating term) hold, or does it smuggle a quietism — withdrawal into self-sufficiency — into what Han presents as resistance?
- How does an impersonal "A LIFE" without individuality or subjectivity relate to embodiment? The child is "infused with an immanent life," yet immanence "can neither be psychologized nor subjectivized" — what is the body of a singularity that is not a subject?
- Is Han's immanence compatible with the materialist concerns of the vault, or does its self-sufficiency ("it exists only in itself") risk a new immateriality — a plane abstracted from material relations of domination?
