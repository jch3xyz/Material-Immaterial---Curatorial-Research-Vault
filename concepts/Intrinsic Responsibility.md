# Intrinsic Responsibility

## Brief Definition

A property of a system's *design*, not of its actors' virtue: the arrangement of feedback so that the consequences of a decision return to the decision-makers "directly and quickly and compellingly."

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows, intrinsic responsibility is a structural — not a moral — reframing of accountability. It is one of her answers to the question of what to do inside a self-determining system, and it belongs to a family of design moves that work *with* a system's own feedback structure rather than against its outputs. Meadows introduces it as "the easier task of increasing responsibility within the system," set explicitly against the harder, often futile impulse to blame or "control the outside influence." A system has intrinsic responsibility when it is *built* so that whoever makes a decision is also the one who feels its result — the [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] loop from action to consequence is closed onto the actor themselves. (Directly stated.)

Her canonical image is the pilot: "Because the pilot of a plane rides in the front of the plane, that pilot is intrinsically responsible." The pilot cannot outsource or defer the outcome of a bad decision — position in the physical system guarantees that "he or she will experience directly the consequences of his or her decisions." The property is thus about *where the decision-maker sits relative to the feedback*, not about how conscientious they are. Her design prescription follows the same logic: requiring "all towns or companies that emit wastewater into a stream to place their intake pipes downstream from their outflow pipe," so that a polluter drinks its own effluent. Responsibility here is engineered into the plumbing. (Directly stated.)

Just as design can install intrinsic responsibility, it can strip it out. Meadows's counter-example is Dartmouth College, which "reduced intrinsic responsibility when it took thermostats out of individual offices and classrooms and put temperature-control decisions under the guidance of a central computer." The people who *feel* the room — cold or overheated — no longer *decide* about the room; the decision migrated to a distant central authority reachable only by a phone call "across campus," and the observable consequence was "greater oscillations in room temperature." This is the concept's link to the vault's control cluster: centralization that severs the sensing party from the deciding party is a removal of intrinsic responsibility, and it degrades the system's own [[Homeostasis|self-regulation]]. (Directly stated.)

Read across the vault, intrinsic responsibility is a quiet inversion of the surveillance and control logics catalogued elsewhere. Systems like the [[Digital Panopticon]] or the [[Society of Control]] concentrate sensing and decision *away* from those who bear the outcome — the watched are not the deciders. Meadows's design principle points the other way: bring the consequence back to the hand on the lever. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — coins/uses the term as a systems-design property: the system is engineered so feedback about consequences reaches decision-makers "directly and quickly and compellingly." Illustrated by the pilot in the cockpit, the intake pipe downstream of the outflow, and the negative example of Dartmouth's central-computer thermostat. It is one of her interventions in a world of systems, alongside working with feedback rather than fighting outputs.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — intrinsic responsibility just *is* a well-placed feedback loop: consequence routed back to actor. The concept is unintelligible without Meadows's feedback vocabulary.
- [[Systems Theory]] — a systems-lens reframing of accountability as a property of structure, not of individual will.
- [[Homeostasis]] — closing the consequence-to-decider loop is what lets a system self-correct; severing it (the Dartmouth thermostat) produces "greater oscillations."
- [[Cybernetics]] — the pilot/thermostat imagery is native cybernetic control; intrinsic responsibility is where cybernetic feedback meets governance.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Digital Panopticon]] — surveillance concentrates sensing and decision away from those who bear the consequences; intrinsic responsibility returns the consequence to the decision-maker.
- [[Society of Control]] — modulated, centralized control detaches the deciding party from the feeling party — the very move (Dartmouth's central computer) that Meadows names as *reducing* intrinsic responsibility.

## Questions It Raises

- If responsibility is a design property rather than a virtue, is "irresponsibility" ever a personal failing, or always a structural one?
- Meadows's own example privatizes a commons (charging professors for their heat) — when does engineering intrinsic responsibility slide into commodifying a shared good?
- Surveillance systems also "send feedback about consequences to decision-makers" — but to *watchers*, not to the watched. What distinguishes intrinsic responsibility from a panopticon that routes feedback the wrong way?
- Can a highly centralized or automated system (a central-computer thermostat, an algorithmic controller) ever preserve intrinsic responsibility, or does centralization structurally remove it?
