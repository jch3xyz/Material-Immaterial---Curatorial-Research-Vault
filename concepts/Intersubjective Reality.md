# Intersubjective Reality

## Brief Definition

Harari's third ontological level — between the objective and the subjective — where entities such as money, gods, nations, corporations and human rights exist not in matter and not in any single mind but in the communication among many humans, and where, so long as enough people keep believing the shared story, these fictions become the most powerful agents in history.

## Longer Explanation

For Harari the common intuition that reality comes in only two flavours — objective and subjective — is the source of most confusion about how human power actually works. He insists on a third register: "However, there is a third level of reality: the intersubjective level. Intersubjective entities depend on communication among many humans rather than on the beliefs and feelings of individual humans" (Ch. 4, *The Human Spark*). Money is his paradigm case: a dollar bill has no objective value and is not sustained by any one person's private faith; it works because billions transact as if it works, and it evaporates the moment they stop. Gods, empires, laws and corporations share this structure — "One moment they are busy shaping the world, and the next moment they no longer exist." The category is therefore not a softer synonym for "subjective" or "imaginary-and-thus-unreal"; intersubjective entities are, in Harari's account, "many of the most important agents in history." `Directly stated`

The intersubjective level is what large-scale cooperation runs on. Harari's master claim is that Sapiens dominate the planet not through superior individual intelligence or toolmaking but through a unique capacity to "cooperate flexibly in large numbers," and that flexible mass cooperation is impossible on the basis of personal acquaintance alone (Sapiens cap out around 150 intimate relationships). What bridges the gap is shared fiction: "All large-scale human cooperation is ultimately based on our belief in imagined orders. These are sets of rules that, despite existing only in our imagination, we believe to be as real and inviolable as gravity" (Ch. 4). The companion image he develops for this self-perpetuating fabric of belief is [[The Web of Meaning]] — "Sapiens rule the world because only they can weave an intersubjective web of meaning: a web of laws, forces, entities and places that exist purely in their common imagination." Keep the two notes distinct: *intersubjective reality* is the ontological category (the third level); *the web of meaning* is the metaphor Harari uses for how that level is spun, reinforced and eventually unravelled across history. `Directly stated`

A counter-intuitive corollary, developed in Ch. 7 (*The Odd Couple*), is that science does not dissolve the intersubjective realm but fortifies it: "Modern science certainly changed the rules of the game, but it did not simply replace myths with facts. Myths continue to dominate humankind. Science only makes these myths stronger. Instead of destroying the intersubjective reality, science will enable it to control the objective and subjective realities more completely than ever before." This is the "science-strengthens-myth" move: religion supplies order while science supplies power, and the two cooperate rather than war. The consequence Harari draws at the close of Ch. 4 is ontological escalation — "As human fictions are translated into genetic and electronic codes, the intersubjective reality will swallow up the objective reality and biology will merge with history." Here the concept bears directly on the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension and on [[Information]]: when fictions are written into DNA and silicon, the immaterial story begins to overwrite material substrate. `Directly stated`

The concept finally turns inward. In Ch. 11 (*The Time Bomb in the Laboratory*) Harari extends the logic of fiction to the self itself: the "narrating self" spins a continuous yarn that we mistake for a unified identity, but "as convincing and tempting as it may be, this story is a fiction. Medieval crusaders believed that God and heaven provided their lives with meaning. Modern liberals believe that individual free choices provide life with meaning. They are all equally delusional." The individual who supposedly authors meaning — the chooser at the heart of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — is itself, on Harari's account, a story; the same fiction-making capacity that builds money and nations builds the "I." This is the hinge to his later argument that [[Dataism]] and algorithmic prediction (see [[Algorithmic Determinism]]) can out-know and so depose that fictional self. `Strongly implied` (the explicit self-as-fiction claim is `Directly stated`; the link to Dataism is Harari's own trajectory across the book, here labelled as synthesis of the chapter sequence).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — coins the three-level ontology (objective / subjective / intersubjective) and makes intersubjective reality the load-bearing concept of *Homo Deus*: the ground of mass cooperation (Ch. 4), the engine of history reinforced rather than dispelled by science (Ch. 7), and ultimately the medium in which even the self is "a fiction" (Ch. 11). Synonymous and adjacent phrasings he uses include "imagined orders," "the web of meaning / web of stories," "fiction," and "intersubjective entities/networks."

## Related Concepts

- [[The Web of Meaning]] — the metaphor for how intersubjective reality is woven, reinforced through mutual confirmation, and unravelled over time; companion note, kept distinct.
- [[Information]] — Harari's claim that fictions "translated into genetic and electronic codes" let the intersubjective realm overtake the objective makes this a node in the vault's cross-author conversation on information and immateriality.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-knowing chooser, which Harari recasts as itself a product of the narrating self's fiction-making.
- [[Dataism]] — the emerging ideology (Han and O'Gieblyn-via-Harari already in the vault) that, on Harari's account, supersedes the liberal humanist version of intersubjective meaning by relocating authority to the algorithm.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the predictive apparatus that, once it "knows the customer better than she knows herself," dissolves the fictional self the intersubjective order had sustained.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the relevant axis of tension: intersubjective entities are non-material yet causally dominant, and Harari predicts the immaterial fiction "will swallow up the objective reality." The opposition is productive, not flat — the concept lives precisely on this fault line rather than on either pole.

## Questions It Raises

- If money, nations and the self are all intersubjective fictions, is "fiction" doing critical work (unmasking) or merely descriptive work (naming a third ontological kind)? Harari insists fiction "isn't bad. It is vital" — so what distinguishes a benign from a pathological intersubjective order?
- Does Harari's "science only makes these myths stronger" claim collapse the very objective/intersubjective distinction it depends on, once fictions are inscribed into genetic and electronic codes?
- How does Harari's intersubjective reality relate to Han's account of [[Information]] and the dissolution of narrative, or to Hayles's posthuman erasure of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — convergent diagnoses, or incompatible ontologies?
- If the self is the narrating self's fiction, what exactly is deposed when [[Algorithmic Determinism]] "out-knows" it — a person, or only a story?
