# Informatics

## General Orientation

"Informatics" carries two distinct senses in this vault, and they must be kept apart. The originating sense is [[Donna Haraway]]'s: in *A Cyborg Manifesto* (1985) she names "the scary new networks I have called the informatics of domination" — a specific historical *regime of power*, a column of post-WWII transitions (organism → biotic component, reproduction → replication, mind → artificial intelligence) in which the world is reconstrued as "a problem of coding." The broader, descendant sense is [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s: she takes the bare word "[f]ollowing Donna Haraway" and generalizes it into a *relational field* — the network of feedback among bodies, texts, and technologies. Hayles's is the field; Haraway's is the regime that first put the word in circulation.

This note holds the **Hayles relational-field sense** as its through-line. Haraway's **regime sense** gets its own dedicated treatment in [[The Informatics of Domination]] — the two are kept reciprocally distinct on the model of [[Information]] vs. [[Information (Han)]] in the vault; the section below records the borrowing and the primary text, but does not collapse the regime account into this note.

## Brief Definition

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], **informatics** is the whole network of relations binding information technologies together with the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes that "initiate, accompany, and complicate their development" — the name for the [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] among bodies, texts, and technologies, not for the technical concept of information itself.

## Longer Explanation

Informatics is Hayles's term, adopted from [[Donna Haraway]], for a relational *field* rather than a thing. In "Virtual Bodies and Flickering Signifiers" she introduces it precisely to name a network she has spent the chapter assembling: the affinity between human bodies and textual bodies, both of which "incorporate" their encodings in a durable material substrate, and the "complex feedback loops between contemporary literature, the technologies that produce it, and the embodied readers who produce and are produced by books and technologies." Informatics is what she calls this network of relations. It is therefore distinct from [[Information (Hayles)|information]] in the technical, Shannon sense — the pattern conceptually distinct from its markers — even though information theory is one of its constituents. Informatics is the larger weave; information is one thread within it.

The defining feature of informatics is its recursivity, which is why it belongs to the same conceptual family as [[Feedback Loops]], [[Cybernetics]], and [[Systems Theory]]. Hayles is emphatic that the relations are not one-directional. Each category in the chain — "production, signification, consumption, bodily experience, and representation" — "is in constant feedback and feedforward loops with the others." Technologies of inscription change models of signification; changed signification reshapes consumption; shifted consumption "initiate[s] new experiences of embodiment"; and embodied experience loops back to generate new textual worlds. Informatics names this entire circuit of mutual constitution, in which the body is never simply prior to or separable from the technologies it interfaces with — a point she condenses elsewhere in the chapter as "[t]he computer molds the human even as the human builds the computer."

Because informatics is the field of these reciprocal pressures, it is also the medium through which Hayles's larger thesis travels. The "contemporary pressure toward dematerialization" — the epistemic shift "toward pattern/randomness and away from presence/absence" — "affects human and textual bodies on two levels at once, as a change in the body (the material substrate) and as a change in the message (the codes of representation)." Informatics is the name for the "massively parallel and highly interdigitated" connectivity between those two levels. It is also, she notes, a cultural theme in its own right: "cyberpunk, for example, takes informatics as its central theme," and even narratives without that focus "can hardly avoid the rippling effects of informatics."

It matters that informatics is a *network* and not an essence, because this framing is what lets Hayles resist the disembodiment fantasy she calls [[The Body as Data|the body as data]]. If informatics were simply information-as-pattern, the body would be an optional substrate. Because informatics includes "the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes" that accompany the technologies, the material world is built into the term from the start. This is the seam where informatics opens onto its counter-thesis, [[The Materiality of Informatics]]: the insistence that informatics is itself materially produced, that "information in fact derives its efficacy from the material infrastructures it appears to obscure," and that "[t]he pattern/randomness dialectic does not erase the material world." Informatics is thus a deliberately doubled concept — it names both the powerful illusion of dematerialization and the material relations that the illusion depends upon and conceals.

## ## Donna Haraway (the originating sense: informatics of domination)

The word "informatics" enters this vault's vocabulary through Donna Haraway, who coins **the informatics of domination** in *A Cyborg Manifesto* (the chapter of that name). It is not, in Haraway, a neutral relational field but a specific historical *regime of power*. She charts a column of transitions "from the comfortable old hierarchical dominations to the scary new networks I have called the informatics of domination" — representation → simulation, organism → biotic component, reproduction → replication, mind → artificial intelligence, "white capitalist patriarchy → informatics of domination." The left column is the "organic, industrial society"; the right is the "polymorphous, information system." Crucially, the dichotomies are "[s]imultaneously material and ideological," so the regime is not a story of pure immateriality — a point that aligns it with [[The Materiality of Informatics]] rather than against it.

What unifies the regime is a single epistemic operation. "Furthermore, communications sciences and modern biologies are constructed by a common move—the translation of the world into a problem of coding, a search for a common language in which all resistance to instrumental control disappears and all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange." Under this move the organism "ceased to exist as objects of knowledge, giving way to biotic components, i.e., special kinds of information-processing devices," and the governing logic becomes that of [[Information (Hayles)|information]] as "that kind of quantifiable element... that allows universal translation, and so unhindered instrumental power." Haraway condenses the whole technology into the military's metaphor "C3I, command-control-communication-intelligence." This is the source from which [[N. Katherine Hayles]] borrows: where Haraway names a regime of domination, Hayles generalizes the bare word into a field of feedback among bodies, texts, and technologies (`Directly stated` that Hayles follows Haraway; `Interpretive synthesis` that the field-sense softens the regime-sense).

The two senses are deliberately **not collapsed**. Haraway's regime-sense — the polemical, power-laden account with its full chart of transitions, its [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] subject "not subject to Foucault's biopolitics," and its "women in the integrated circuit" — lives in the dedicated note [[The Informatics of Domination]]. This note keeps Hayles's broader relational-field sense as its through-line. The split mirrors [[Information]] / [[Information (Han)]] in the vault: one canonical word, two source-anchored senses, linked reciprocally rather than merged (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Information (Hayles)]] — the technical concept (pattern distinct from its markers) that is one constituent of informatics; informatics is the broader network, information one element within it.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the structural principle of informatics: production, signification, consumption, embodiment, and representation stand in "constant feedback and feedforward loops with the others."
- [[Cybernetics]] — the disciplinary matrix that first constituted "humans, animals, and machines as information-processing devices"; informatics generalizes its relational logic to bodies, texts, and culture.
- [[Systems Theory]] — informatics is a systems concept: a circuit of mutually constituting parts in which noise can reorganize the whole at a higher level of complexity.
- [[Embodiment]] — informatics is "formed by technology at the same time that it creates technology"; embodiment is the loop through which informatics reaches and is reached by the lived body.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the disembodiment fantasy that a thin reading of informatics (as pure pattern) makes thinkable; Hayles's relational definition is designed to block it.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — the counter-thesis: informatics is materially produced; "information in fact derives its efficacy from the material infrastructures it appears to obscure."
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — informatics is the site where the dematerialization hierarchy operates, and where Hayles argues it must be contested rather than presupposed.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — informatics is the field on which this axis plays out; the term is built to hold both poles in feedback rather than to let information float free of the body. Read against the grain (information as pure pattern), informatics would collapse into the very opposition Hayles resists.

## Questions It Raises

- If informatics is by definition a *network* that includes "biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes," can the body ever be the disposable substrate that [[The Body as Data|the body as data]] imagines — or does the term smuggle materiality back in at the ground floor?
- How does informatics differ from [[Information (Hayles)|information]] in practice? If the technical concept of information is medium-independent by construction, what work does the relational frame of informatics do to re-embed it?
- Informatics is organized by [[Feedback Loops|feedback and feedforward loops]] in which "[t]he computer molds the human even as the human builds the computer." Where, on a [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis, does agency sit when both poles are continuously reconstituting each other?
- If "cyberpunk... takes informatics as its central theme," is informatics primarily an analytic category, a historical condition, or a cultural imaginary — and can a curatorial practice treat artworks as nodes in this network rather than as objects?
- Does naming the network "informatics" risk granting the technologies of information a centering role that the [[The Materiality of Informatics|materiality counter-thesis]] then has to fight to displace?
