# Free Speech Fundamentalism

## Brief Definition

Legal scholar Frank Pasquale's term, taken up by Shoshana Zuboff, for the "cyberlibertarian" ideology through which surveillance capitalists weaponize First Amendment free-expression rights to shield platform content and the "algorithmic orderings of information" produced by their machine operations from any form of oversight — recasting corporate extractive action as constitutionally protected speech.

## Longer Explanation

In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, free speech fundamentalism is one of the political-cultural strategies by which the new market form is protected from "fatal challenge." Zuboff places it inside her third domain of analysis — the "political and cultural circumstances and strategies that advanced surveillance capitalism's claims and protected them from fatal challenge" — alongside the firm's insistence on a "right to lawless space," the broader shelter of [[Neoliberalism]], and the deliberate construction of "fortifications" in politics and culture. Free speech fundamentalism is the constitutional plank of that shelter: the conversion of First Amendment doctrine into a moat (`Directly stated`; the chapter is titled "The Moat Around the Castle").

The legal substrate, as Zuboff reports it, is that "First Amendment jurisprudence, especially over the last two decades, has reflected a 'conservative-libertarian' interpretation of First Amendment rights" (quoting constitutional scholar Steven Heyman). This orientation "asserts a close connection between free speech and property rights," and "the logic that links ownership to an absolute entitlement to freedom of expression has led to a privileging of corporate action as 'speech' deserving of constitutional protection" — which some scholars regard as "a dangerous reversion to the feudal doctrines from which corporate law evolved in the seventeenth century." It is in this context, Zuboff writes, that "surveillance capitalists vigorously developed a 'cyberlibertarian' ideology that Frank Pasquale describes as 'free speech fundamentalism.'" Their legal teams "aggressively assert First Amendment principles to fend off any form of oversight or externally imposed constraints that either limit the content on their platforms or the 'algorithmic orderings of information' produced by their machine operations" (`Directly stated`). The move is candid: an attorney representing leading surveillance capitalists tells Zuboff that "all of these companies talk about their businesses in the language of free speech."

The crucial pivot in Zuboff's argument is a double vision. The internet's "expansion of opportunities for free expression… has been an emancipatory force in many vital respects, but this fact must not blind us to another condition: free speech fundamentalism has deflected careful scrutiny of the unprecedented operations that constitute the new market form and account for its spectacular success" (`Directly stated`). The same constitutional instrument that genuinely emancipates speech is, under surveillance capitalism, "exploited to shelter a range of novel practices that are antidemocratic in their aims and consequences and fundamentally destructive of the enduring First Amendment values intended to protect the individual from abusive power." This is the *Free Speech as Emancipation vs Free Speech as Shelter* opposition that runs through the chapter (not yet a standalone tension note in this vault; rendered here as plain text): free expression cuts both ways, and fundamentalism trades on its emancipatory reputation to camouflage extraction.

Zuboff is careful that this is "one respect in which the surveillance capitalists are not unprecedented" — citing historian Adam Winkler, she notes that "throughout American history the nation's most powerful corporations have persistently mobilized to use the Constitution to fight off unwanted government regulations." Free speech fundamentalism therefore sits inside a longer lineage of corporate constitutional self-defense, but it acquires new stakes when the "speech" being shielded is the algorithmic processing of [[Behavioral Surplus]]. In the chapter's architecture it pairs with two further "shelters": the statutory shield of Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (which treats platforms as non-publishing "intermediaries," a stance that "perfectly converged with the reigning ideology and practice of 'self-regulation'") and the post-9/11 emergence of [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]]. Together these form the neoliberal/juridical habitat that let the surveillance mutation "root and flourish" — and that the [[Right to Sanctuary]] and [[The Right to the Future Tense]] are invoked, elsewhere in the book, to contest (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — adopts Pasquale's term to name the constitutional plank of surveillance capitalism's shelter: a conservative-libertarian First Amendment jurisprudence that privileges corporate action as "speech," letting platforms' content and "algorithmic orderings of information" escape oversight. Distinguishes the internet's genuine emancipatory expansion of expression from its weaponization, which "deflects careful scrutiny" of extractive operations.
- **Frank Pasquale** (legal scholar, via Zuboff) — coiner of the phrase "free speech fundamentalism" to characterize the surveillance capitalists' cyberlibertarian ideology. Cited as a one-off source in this chapter (also for the "notice and consent" / privacy-as-competitive-good critique of self-regulation); rendered as plain text per the vault's one-off-figure policy.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — free speech fundamentalism is one of the fortifications that shelter this market form from scrutiny.
- [[Neoliberalism]] — the larger enabling habitat; free speech fundamentalism is its constitutional/juridical expression, converging with the "self-regulation" doctrine and the demolition of countervailing institutions.
- [[Surveillance Exceptionalism]] — the parallel post-9/11 shelter analyzed in the same chapter; both deflect regulation, one via the Constitution, the other via national-security demand.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — what the "algorithmic orderings of information" actually process; reframing this extraction as "speech" is the sleight of hand.
- [[The Right to Sanctuary]] — the counter-claim to a protected, unobserved space, set against the firms' insistence on a "right to lawless space."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the elemental claim to self-determination that the shelter of free speech fundamentalism is, in effect, designed to evade; democratic action reasserts it against the moat.

## Questions It Raises

- If free expression is genuinely emancipatory online, how can oversight of "algorithmic orderings of information" be designed without eroding the First Amendment values it claims to protect?
- Is the equation of corporate algorithmic processing with protected "speech" a category error, or the logical endpoint of linking ownership to absolute expressive entitlement?
- Zuboff calls this "not unprecedented" — corporations have always used the Constitution against regulation. What is genuinely new once the shielded "speech" is the rendition of [[Behavioral Surplus]]?
- How do the three shelters — free speech fundamentalism, Section 230's "intermediary" doctrine, and self-regulation — reinforce one another, and which is most load-bearing?
