# Connective Criticism

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's coinage (ch.11, 1977) for an interdisciplinary metacritique that interconnects the methodologies of science, psychology, linguistics, social and economic theory, metaphysics and philosophy into a unified field — shifting critical attention from form to function, and from the *what* and *how* of art to the *why* — and which, because art is an open system, must itself be open-ended, speculative, and a formative part of the art-making.

## Longer Explanation

Connective criticism arises for Ascott from a diagnosis of crisis: at a time when "all the resources of the human mind must be brought into play," he asks how criticism can "ignore the paradigms of science, the researches of psychology, the structures of language, social and economic theory, the intimations of metaphysics, or the propositions of philosophy?" The remedy is to combine these separate inquiries "into a unified field" — an intersection that "would constitute a field of connective criticism," one that not only holds "the ambiguities, polarities, and contradictions of these separate approaches" but "would identify correspondences, similarities, and new relationships." Criticism, on this model, is not a verdict delivered from outside the work but a connective tissue spanning disciplines. (Directly stated.)

The decisive move is structural. For Ascott, "Art is a system and finds its form in the interaction of many parts. The role of criticism in this system, like the role of the viewer or participant, is formative." This makes the critic the structural equivalent of [[The Participator]]: just as the participating spectator completes the work by acting within it, "Critical inquiry is a part of the making of art." Criticism is therefore generative rather than terminal — and it follows that "A definitive critique is a contradiction in terms," because "Art is a starting point; by its very nature, it is open-ended. Criticism must be as well." (Directly stated.) This open-endedness is the same logic Ascott applies to the artwork in his account of art as an open system, and it is why he reorients the whole enterprise away from "the questions of style and form" toward "a more comprehensive insight": "there is a shift of interest from form to function." (Directly stated.)

Beyond elucidation, connective criticism has a forward, world-making function: "Speculative criticism acts as a springboard to further dimensions of creative work, triggering new behaviors and attitudes of mind." (Directly stated.) Ascott frames this as the necessary contemporary form of what Northrop Frye called "reforg[ing] the broken links between creation and knowledge, art and science, myth and concept" — Frye's exhortation supplies the cultural mandate, but the connective field is Ascott's own construction. (Directly stated; Frye is an in-text citation, not a vault reference note.)

The concept is not confined to its founding essay. In ch.12 ("[[Network as Artwork]]") Ascott projects that "the currently emerging mode of participation in art will develop further, leading to a connective, network paradigm for art and art theory, calling for a connective criticism," explicitly tying connective criticism to the network paradigm of distributed, telematic art. In ch.13 ("Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art") the same coupling recurs at the level of period: "Postmodernist art, by encompassing these qualities, presents a connective paradigm, which in turn demands a connective criticism." Across the three essays the term names a stable correspondence — a connective (networked, participatory) art demands a criticism of the same shape. (Directly stated; the synthesis that the three usages name one stable concept is interpretive.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins the term in ch.11 (1977) as an interdisciplinary metacritique and a formative, open-ended, speculative practice structurally equivalent to the participating spectator; redeploys it in ch.12 as the criticism the network paradigm "calls for," and in ch.13 as the criticism the postmodernist "connective paradigm" "demands."

## Related Concepts

- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's object→system thesis is the broader art-theoretical ground; connective criticism is the critical counterpart to art conceived as a system, where the role of criticism, "like the role of the viewer or participant, is formative."
- [[The Participator]] — the keystone relation: criticism in Ascott's open system occupies the same formative role as the participating spectator; "Critical inquiry is a part of the making of art."
- [[Network as Artwork]] — the ch.12 setting in which the "connective, network paradigm" is said to "call[] for a connective criticism."

## Opposing Concepts

- The notion of a *definitive critique* — for Ascott "a contradiction in terms" — is the internal foil the concept is defined against, rather than a separate vault note.

## Questions It Raises

- If "a definitive critique is a contradiction in terms," what criteria distinguish a generative connective criticism from mere assimilation or relativism?
- When the critic is "formative" and "a part of the making of art," is there any position left from which to evaluate the work — or only positions from which to extend it?
- Does the shift "from form to function" (and to the *why* of art) presuppose that form is critically exhausted, or merely that it is no longer where the interesting questions lie?
- Can an interdisciplinary "unified field" actually integrate science, psychology, linguistics, economics, metaphysics and philosophy, or does it inevitably privilege one discipline's paradigm as the connector?
