# Reciprocal Communication

## Brief Definition

The criterion that separates [[Cyborg Art]] from all prior sculpture — and even from [[Kinetic Art]]: the move from one-way stimulation of the viewer to a sustained two-way exchange, which requires the artwork itself to possess some degree of intelligence (i.e., [[Feedback Loops|negative feedback]]).

## Longer Explanation

For Jack Burnham, the entire Western sculptural tradition — from the paleolithic carving to the Calder mobile to the Tinguely machine — shares one "inherent defect": its objects "do not respond to man in any intelligent fashion" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]). Drawing on Norbert Wiener's image of the music-box automata that receive only a "one-way" message and remain "blind, deaf, and dumb," Burnham argues that even the most charged aesthetic encounter — "the painterly handling of a Rubens nude, the texture of a Henry Moore bronze, the noisy dissonance of a Tinguely exhibition" — is finally a one-way message. Placed in a cybernetic frame, the historical art relationship was always between "a complex, self-stabilizing, goal-seeking system (man) and an inert object," so that "the result in every case was not communication but one-way stimulation for the human party involved." *Directly stated.*

Reciprocal communication is Burnham's name for what would replace that asymmetry: making "communication between the work of art and the observer a sustained two-way experience," the aim he attributes to New Tendency aesthetics and develops as the horizon of [[Cyborg Art]]. The decisive move is not more motion (Kinetic Art already has motion) but intelligence in the work. Burnham's sharpest formulation is his critique of Julio Le Parc, whose call for "active participation" of the artwork "never touches the consideration that art, to engage in a series of interactions with a human observer, must itself possess some degree of intelligence." Mechanical kineticism only appears to "act"; it "no more 'acts' than does a ball of yarn when it is batted and chased by a kitten." Genuine two-way exchange requires the work to model the [[Feedback Loops|negative-feedback]] loops Wiener identified as "the prime means by which intelligent beings communicate with one another." *Directly stated.*

The concept therefore supplies the strongest conceptual ground in the chapter for treating art as [[Art as Experiential Technology|experiential technology]] rather than as a contemplated object: an artwork capable of reciprocal communication is a [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] system coupled to the viewer through [[Feedback Loops|feedback]], not a stimulus delivered to a passive recipient. Burnham orders the possibilities by the degree of two-way coupling: as we are "able to steer or program the actions of a machine, and the machine reacts in sundry ways to our guidance, we attain higher levels of communication." Its distant limit case is the [[Cyborg|cyborg]] relationship — "the kind of interaction of two men talking" — which Burnham calls "the most exotic, if distant, goal of Cyborg Art" and "the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture." *Directly stated / Strongly implied for the experiential-technology framing.*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — reciprocal communication is the dividing criterion that distinguishes Cyborg (post-kinetic) art from the whole prior history of sculpture. Its precondition is intelligence in the work (negative feedback); without it, even participatory and kinetic art delivers only "one-way stimulation."
- **Norbert Wiener** (cited by Burnham) — supplies the underlying claim that "the reciprocated message is the prime means by which intelligent beings communicate with one another," against the one-way message of the insensate music-box automata. (Wiener is a forward-stub author; *Cybernetics*, 1948, is not in `raw/`.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — the mechanism: reciprocal communication is operationalized as negative feedback between viewer and work; "art, to engage in a series of interactions with a human observer, must itself possess some degree of intelligence."
- [[Cyborg Art]] — the art category defined by this criterion; reciprocal communication is what cyborg/post-kinetic art achieves that kinetic art does not.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the framework; reciprocal communication is "communication" in the cyberneticist's sense (interaction between coupled systems).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — reciprocal communication is the strongest argument that the artwork is an interactive system the viewer participates in, not an object contemplated.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's later program; the object→system migration of which reciprocal communication is the endpoint.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — the tension this concept names: prior art (including Kinetic Art) is one-way stimulation; reciprocal communication is the two-way pole.
- [[Kinetic Art]] — opposed as the limit of the old paradigm: motion without intelligence is still one-way; it only appears to "act."

## Questions It Raises

- If reciprocal communication requires the work to "possess some degree of intelligence," at what threshold does an artwork stop being a stimulus and become a genuine interlocutor — and is that threshold aesthetic or technical?
- Burnham locates the criterion in negative feedback. Does a contemporary interactive or AI-driven artwork satisfy his test, or does it still deliver "one-way stimulation" dressed as response?
- Is reciprocity between viewer and intelligent artwork continuous with the [[Cyborg|cyborg]] coupling ("the kind of interaction of two men talking"), or does the latter dissolve the viewer/work distinction reciprocal communication presupposes?
