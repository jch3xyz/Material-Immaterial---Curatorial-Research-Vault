# Exhibition as Medium

## Brief Definition

The reinvention, in Conceptual art, of the exhibition and its catalogue as the artwork's *primary* site of existence rather than a secondary record of objects shown elsewhere — so that the publication is "art rather than merely about art."

## Longer Explanation

For Lippard, the most consequential structural invention of the 1966–72 period was not a new kind of object but a new kind of *container*. When a work no longer "depend[s] upon its physical presence," its appearance in print stops being a degraded copy and becomes the thing itself. This is the engine of [[Dematerialization]] read from the distribution side: if the idea is paramount and the material form "secondary, lightweight, ephemeral," then the magazine, the catalogue, and the mailed card can carry the work undistorted, at the speed of mail and telephone rather than the speed of crated paintings. The exhibition becomes a medium, not a venue.

The load-bearing figure is [[Seth Siegelaub]], whom Lippard credits with bypassing the art world "with exhibitions that took place outside of galleries and/or New York and/or were united in publications that were art rather than merely about art" (`Directly stated`). His January 1969 show announced the inversion bluntly — "The exhibition consists of (the ideas communicated in) the catalog; the physical presence (of the work) is supplementary to the catalog" — and his *One Month* (March 1969) was, in Lippard's chronicle, the "First exhibition to exist in catalogue form alone; distributed free worldwide" (`Directly stated`). The catalogue had stopped documenting a show and *become* the show.

The hinge of the whole model is the primary-vs-secondary-information distinction, stated by Siegelaub in his interview with Ursula Meyer: a secondhand reproduction of a physical painting is "bastardized and distorted," but dematerialized work, "when it has become an abstraction," is "not distorted and altered by its representation in books and catalogues. It becomes primary information" (`Directly stated`). Print is not a lossy channel for such work; it is the work's native register. This is what licenses the gallery itself to be reconceived as "not [an] exhibition hall but as bank of ideas" (Pierre Restany's Bonino show, 1970) and the periodical to be reconceived as an exhibition space — as in the 1970 *Studio International* issue Siegelaub edited into "a kind of magazine exhibition" with six critic-curators each given eight pages (`Directly stated`). Lippard's own contribution there was a "round robin" in which each artist set a "situation" for the next, the magazine page operating as both site and score.

Lippard extends the principle to her own curatorial practice and to the book in your hands. Her "557,087"/"955,000" exhibitions used "randomly arranged packs of index cards" as catalogues, her own aphorisms "mixed in, unsequentially, with the artists' cards," so that the catalogue was a deck the reader could reshuffle and cull (`Directly stated`). And *Six Years* is itself built as an exhibition-medium: a three-register chronicle in which, per the Author's Note, "All factual information . . . is in bold type; all anthological (excerpts, statements, art works, symposia) is in roman type; and all commentary by the editor is in italics" (`Directly stated`). The book does not describe the dematerialized exhibition from outside; it performs one.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Lucy R. Lippard]]** — treats the exhibition/catalogue/magazine as the artwork's primary carrier and aligns it with her politics of de-commodification; her index-card catalogues and the typographic apparatus of *Six Years* make the chronicle itself a medium, not a record.
- **[[Seth Siegelaub]]** — the operative theorist of the model: the catalogue-only *One Month*, the January 1969 inversion of catalogue and physical show, the magazine-exhibition, and the primary/secondary information distinction that holds the whole apparatus together. ## Related Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — the precondition: only work that has "become an abstraction" survives print "undistorted," so dematerialization and the print-as-site move are two faces of one shift.
- [[Information]] — Siegelaub's "primary information" reframes the exhibition as an information channel; the book's own bold/roman/italic schema is an information design.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the art-historical instance of the larger material/immaterial problem: the catalogue is a material print object carrying an immaterial work whose "physical presence . . . is supplementary."
- [[Seth Siegelaub]] — the load-bearing figure; see Authors and Variants.
- [[New Media Art]] — the magazine-exhibition, mail art, and "bank of ideas" gallery prefigure distribution-native, network-native art practices.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the exhibition reconceived as an apparatus for delivering an experience (or "primary information") rather than housing precious objects.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Exhibition Value]] (Han via Benjamin) — link-only resonance: where Lippard/Siegelaub wield publication and circulation to *escape* commodity status, Han describes circulation and exhibition as the very mechanism by which everything is exposed, displayed, and recuperated as value. The 1970s "escape was temporary"; the recuperation Han names is the longer-run condition.

## Questions It Raises

- If dematerialized work becomes "primary information" in print, is the exhibition-as-medium a defeat of [[Material vs Immaterial]] or merely its displacement — trading the gallery object for the equally material print object and network?
- Does treating the catalogue/magazine as the artwork make the exhibition a kind of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — an apparatus engineered to deliver an experience — and how does that square with Conceptual art's refusal of the "experiential"?
- The circulation Siegelaub mobilized against commodification is, for Han, exactly how [[Exhibition Value]] operates. Did the exhibition-as-medium build the distribution infrastructure that later recuperated it?
- Print, mail, telephone, telex: the model is bound to its period's media. How does it translate to [[New Media Art]] and networked distribution, where the "publication" has no edition and no undistorted "original" to be primary about?
- Where does *the body* go when the exhibition is a magazine page or an index card — is the dematerialized exhibition a removal of the spectator's body from the encounter, or its relocation to the reader's act of shuffling, culling, and reading?
