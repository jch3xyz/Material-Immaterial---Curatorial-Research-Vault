# The Telematic Embrace

## Brief Definition

The title figure of Roy Ascott's collected writings: a planetary "embrace" of minds united through computer-mediated telecommunications networks — a coming-together "greater than the sum of its parts" that carries Ascott's utopian theory of love, or "passionate attraction," as the organizing principle of networked culture.

## Longer Explanation

"The Telematic Embrace" is at once a concept, a guiding metaphor, and the contested, utopian heart of the book. It names the condition in which the converging technologies of computing and telecommunications draw human minds into a single distributed field of exchange. In editor Edward A. Shanken's framing, the embrace is meant to "unite minds around the world in a global telematic embrace that is greater than the sum of its parts" — a phrasing that ties the figure directly to [[Network Consciousness]] and to the [[Emergence|emergent]] collective mind Ascott sees networked art producing. The "embrace" is thus not a metaphor of two bodies but of many minds: a [[The Global Village|global village]] of [[Telematic Art|telematic]] participators clasped in mutual, asynchronous, distributed connection. *(Interpretive synthesis — the figure as both concept and metaphor.)*

The figure receives its fullest development in Ascott's own 1990 essay, "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" (chapter 20), where he poses the book's titular question. There the "embrace" is the field of operations produced when "the two powerful technologies of computing and telecommunications converge into one field of operations, which has drawn into its embrace other electronic media." The polemical question Ascott raises — "Is there love in the telematic embrace?" — is, he writes, the deep fear beneath many critiques of computer-based art: the fear "of the machine coming to dominate the human will and of a technological formalism erasing human content and values." His answer is cautiously affirmative: networking provides "the very infrastructure for spiritual interchange that could lead to the harmonisation and creative development of the whole planet," so that "the metaphor of love in the telematic embrace may not be entirely misplaced." *(Directly stated — Ascott, ch. 20.)*

The embrace carries Ascott's theory of **love** as a force, drawn (as Shanken documents) from Charles Fourier's principle of "passionate attraction" — a field that, "like gravity, draws together human beings and bonds them." Ascott extends this attraction beyond physical bodies to the merging of human and technological consciousness, hoping telematic networks will activate a unifying love drawing minds toward "universal unity." This is what makes the embrace the *utopian* heart of the book: it stakes the value of networked art not on information transfer but on connection, communion, and an enlarged planetary mind. *(Strongly implied — Shanken's exegesis of Fourier/Ascott, ch. 04.)*

The figure is deliberately double-edged, and the book does not let its utopianism stand unguarded. The "embrace" of an arm is also a grip; Shanken's caution that "one must remain vigilant that its hugs do not squeeze too tightly" registers the danger that connection collapses into control or surveillance — the network as embrace turning into the network as cage. This internal tension (connection vs. narcissism; embrace vs. confinement; harmonization vs. domination) keeps the concept contested rather than triumphant, and is why the embrace sits in productive opposition to the surveillance reading of the network in Han's [[Digital Panopticon]]. *(Interpretive synthesis — the embrace/grip duality, ch. 04.)*

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

A later chapter, "Telenoia" (ch. 23), gives the embrace its most lyrical and its most theological statement. Ascott folds the figure into the question of human presence at the close of the millennium: "To be in the telepresence of another, of others who in turn feel your own telepresence close to themselves, is to define community in a quite radically different way." Out of this redefined, telepresent community he restates the love-figure with a pun on the [[New Media Art|multimedia]] that carry it: "Love in the telematic embrace is a many-splendoured thing, or in the light of the multimedia involved, a multisplendoured thing!" The 1990 essay's cautious "the metaphor of love... may not be entirely misplaced" (ch. 20) here becomes an unguarded affirmation — love as a quality of *telepresence*, extended (Ascott goes on) to "cybersex and virtual affection... we are only beginning to define." *(Directly stated — Ascott, ch. 23.)*

The chapter also supplies the embrace's cosmological backstory through Ascott's coinage **[[Telenoia]]** — "from the Greek roots *tele*, 'far off,' and *nous*, 'mind'... networked consciousness, interactive awareness, mind at large." Ascott stages this as a deliberate **inversion of the opening of the Gospel of John** ("In the beginning was the Word"): "But if, in the beginning, the Word was Telenoia; it is evident now that we lost it, perhaps in inverse ratio to our gain in verbal intelligence, or at any rate in the cultural prioritisation of linear, mechanical thinking." The Fall here is not from grace into language but from a primordial *networked* mind into linear, left-hemisphere, "deterministic industrialisation" — so that the telematic embrace becomes a *recovery* of a lost original [[Network Consciousness|networked consciousness]], not a novelty. This re-enchanting, prelapsarian framing deepens the embrace's spiritual register (cf. [[Re-enchantment]]) and ties the figure to the [[Emergence|emergent]] "shared consciousness" Ascott dates to the origin of the human itself. *(Directly stated — the coinage and the John 1:1 inversion, ch. 23. Interpretive synthesis — reading the inversion as making the embrace a recovery rather than an invention.)*

> Orientation note: this concept was **oriented** from Shanken's introduction (ch. 04) and **anchored** in Ascott's love/embrace essay ("Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?", ch. 20). The "Telenoia" chapter (ch. 23) now **deepens** the figure with its multisplendoured-love image and its John 1:1 inversion, and is the bridge to the dedicated [[Telenoia]] concept.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** (*Telematic Embrace*, ch. 20, "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?", 1990) — Ascott's own essay frames the embrace as the convergence of computing and telecommunications "into one field of operations," then asks whether love can survive within it. His answer is the affirmative-but-vigilant utopianism that defines the book: networking as an "infrastructure for spiritual interchange" and the "metaphor of love" as "not... entirely misplaced." This is the primary-text anchor.
- **[[Roy Ascott]]** (*Telematic Embrace*, ch. 23, "Telenoia") — restates the love-figure as a quality of *telepresence* and community ("Love in the telematic embrace is a many-splendoured thing... a multisplendoured thing!"), and supplies the embrace's mythic backstory via [[Telenoia]] and the inversion of John 1:1 ("if, in the beginning, the Word was Telenoia"), recasting the embrace as the recovery of a lost original networked consciousness.
- **Edward A. Shanken** (editor; *Telematic Embrace*, ch. 04, "From Cybernetics to Telematics," editor's introduction) — Shanken supplies the book's title figure and its synthesizing gloss: the embrace as a planetary union of minds "greater than the sum of its parts," grounded in Fourier's "passionate attraction," and carrying the built-in caution that its "hugs" must not "squeeze too tightly." Chapter 04 is Shanken writing *about* Ascott, not Ascott; its framing is attributed to the editor.

## Related Concepts

- [[Network Consciousness]] — the emergent, distributed field of mind the embrace produces; the embrace is its affective/figural name.
- [[Telenoia]] — Ascott's coinage (ch. 23) for "networked consciousness, interactive awareness, mind at large"; the embrace is the affective figure for the telenoiac mind, framed as a recovery of a primordial networked consciousness.
- [[Telematic Art]] — the art practice whose medium (networked telecommunications) makes the embrace possible.
- [[Emergence]] — the "greater than the sum of its parts" logic the embrace depends on.
- [[The Global Village]] — McLuhan's electric-age figure of networked social interdependence, which Shanken names as Ascott's explicit prophetic frame for the embrace.
- [[New Media Art]] — the broader category the telematic embrace inhabits (Burnham-anchored; extended by Ascott).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the embrace as experiential, participatory technology par excellence.
- [[Re-enchantment]] — the spiritual/harmonizing register of the embrace ("spiritual interchange," planetary harmonization) resonates with O'Gieblyn's re-enchantment of technology.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's surveillance reading of the network: where Ascott sees the network as a loving embrace, the panopticon reading sees the same converged communications-and-surveillance technologies as a regime of voluntary self-exposure and control. The embrace's own caution ("hugs do not squeeze too tightly") concedes this danger. *(Interpretive synthesis — Ascott does not cite Han; the opposition is vault-internal.)*

## Questions It Raises

- Can "love" or "passionate attraction" be a genuine property of a technical network, or is it a category mistake — projecting an intimate human relation onto an infrastructure?
- How wide are the arms of the embrace? Shanken notes that at the end of the millennium only a fraction of the world had even a telephone — does the embrace presuppose an exclusion it cannot acknowledge?
- When does an embrace become a grip? What distinguishes the harmonizing union Ascott hopes for from the squeezing control the same converged technologies enable?
- Is the embrace truly emergent ("greater than the sum of its parts"), or does the rhetoric of collective consciousness paper over the persistence of individual narcissism within the network?
