# Fantasy

## Brief Definition

Fantasy is the erotic faculty of imagination that inhabits undefined, vague, and absent space and, through the negativity of withdrawal, constructs the [[The Other|Other]] — the exact opposite of information, which as positivity dismantles that negativity and so stifles rather than liberates the imaginative.

## Longer Explanation

In the sixth chapter of *The Agony of Eros*, Byung-Chul Han makes fantasy the pivot of his defense of [[Eros]] against a culture of total visibility. Fantasy, for Han, is not the free play of images that more information would enrich; it is a faculty defined by *negativity*. "Fantasy inhabits space that is undefined," and so "information and fantasy are opposing forces" (`Directly stated`). Where information is [[Positivity|positivity]] — the accretion of attributes, the filling-in of vagueness and ambiguity — fantasy needs the emptiness that information abolishes. This is why Han insists that "only the negativity of withdrawal brings forth the [[The Other|Other]] in its atopic otherness": the beloved is not idealized because information is scarce (Illouz's "information-thin imagination"), but *constructed* through absence, privation, and the retreat of the object of desire (`Directly stated`).

From this Han draws his sharpest media claim: the high-definition, image-saturated apparatus of "new communications media do not give flight to fantasy… it stifles fantasy." He gives this the name **hypervisibility** — a condition where visual information is maximized and nothing is left withdrawn. "Hypervisibility is not conducive to imagination. As such, pornography — which maximizes visual information, as it were — destroys erotic fantasy" (`Directly stated`). Hypervisibility is thus the erotic corollary of [[Transparency]]: "the telos of the society of transparency," achieved by smoothing space and dismantling thresholds. Because "thresholds and transitions are zones of mystery and riddle — here, the atopic [[The Other|Other]] begins," the levelling of those thresholds levels fantasy itself. Han's diagnosis — "the disappearance of the Other. This is the agony of eros" — makes fantasy the faculty whose death he is tracking (`Directly stated`).

Han reads three literary scenes to show fantasy operating through *visual privation* rather than visual plenitude. First, Flaubert: in a "seeming paradox, the most erotic scene in *Madame Bovary* offers practically nothing to see" — the closed carriage wanders the named streets of Rouen while the lovers, behind the curtains, are never depicted (`Directly stated`). Second, Ballard's "The Gioconda of the Twilight Noon," whose protagonist Maitland destroys his own eyes not to ascend to a Platonic daylight of Ideas (as Slavoj Žižek reads it) but for the reverse: "to withdraw from the world of truth and hypervisibility, to retreat into the cave — a twilight space of dreams and desire." Han corrects Žižek's reading, calling Maitland's gesture *anti-Platonic* — a retreat *into* the cave, not out of it (`Directly stated`). Third, Barthes-via-Kafka: "'We photograph things in order to drive them out of our minds. My stories are a way of shutting my eyes.'" For Han, "shutting your eyes is negativity, which does not pair well with the positivity and hyperactivity of contemporary [[Acceleration|acceleration]] society" — the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]]'s compulsive hypervigilance can no longer close its eyes (`Directly stated`).

A crucial distinction underwrites the chapter: fantasy is *not* expectation. Han faults Eva Illouz for collapsing the two in her sociology of disappointment — "it is not heightened fantasy, but — if anything — higher expectations that are responsible for the mounting disappointment experienced in contemporary society" (`Directly stated`). Expectation belongs to the calculus of choice and the "rationalization" of desire; fantasy belongs to the negativity of the withdrawn Other. Where desire is "always desire for the Other" nourished by "the negativity of privation and absence," the ego of unlimited choice — endlessly enunciating criteria in mate-selection — "does not desire." So the crisis of fantasy is continuous with Han's larger thesis: a positivized, hypervisible world of the same ([[Hell of the Same]]) can produce wants and needs but not desire, images but not the Other.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (*The Agony of Eros*, ch. 6) — fantasy as the erotic faculty of the negative: it inhabits undefined space, constructs the [[The Other|Other]] through withdrawal, and is destroyed, not fed, by information, high-definition imagery, pornography, and hypervisibility. Han-anchored; this note is open to other authors (e.g. a psychoanalytic account of fantasy, or Illouz's contrasting "information-thin imagination," which Han cites to reject).

## Related Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — fantasy is a *form of negativity*; it lives on privation, absence, and the withdrawal of the object. Its opposite, [[Positivity|positivity]], is what dismantles it.
- [[The Other]] — fantasy's product and correlate; "only the negativity of withdrawal brings forth the Other in its atopic otherness."
- [[Eros]] — fantasy is the faculty through which eros reaches the withdrawn Other; the "crisis of fantasy" *is* "the agony of eros."
- [[Negative Potency]] — the wider Han figure of a productive not-to (here: the not-to-see of shutting one's eyes, the not-depicting of the carriage scene) as a positive power, against the merely additive positivity of information.
- [[Smoothness]] — hypervisibility "smooths out and levels" space; the smoothing that erases thresholds is what erases fantasies of the Other.
- [[Achievement Society]] / [[Acceleration]] — the hypervigilant, hyperactive achievement-subject of acceleration society "can no longer shut [its] eyes," and so cannot enter the negativity fantasy requires.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Information]] — "Information and fantasy are opposing forces." Information as positivity (accretion of attributes) fills in the undefined space fantasy needs; there is "no such thing as an 'information-thick' imagination."
- [[Positivity]] — fantasy belongs to the negative; information/hypervisibility is "positivity that leads to a dismantling of the Other's negativity."
- [[Transparency]] — hypervisibility is "the telos of the society of transparency"; the transparent, thresholdless, smoothed-out space is precisely where fantasy withers.
- [[Pornography (Han)]] — the limit case: by "maximiz[ing] visual information," pornography "destroys erotic fantasy" — the antithesis of Flaubert's carriage that shows nothing.
- [[Dataism]] — the broader ideology of maximal information and total visibility that Han opposes across his books; fantasy names what that ideology cannot house.

## Questions It Raises

- If fantasy requires the negativity of absence, can any high-definition, information-dense medium ever be erotic — or is eros structurally at odds with visual plenitude?
- Han sharply separates fantasy from expectation. Is disappointment in contemporary intimacy really a failure of expectation (choice/criteria) rather than of imagination, and does the distinction hold outside romantic love?
- Is Han's "hypervisibility" a distinct condition or simply transparency applied to the visual field? What, if anything, does the erotic case add to his general theory of transparency?
- Does the anti-Platonic reading of Ballard's Maitland — self-blinding as a *retreat into* the cave — generalize into an ethics of not-seeing, and how does that square with Han's valorization of contemplative "lingering"?
- If capital's new walls "no longer generate the Other" but only "extend through the inferno of the same," is the crisis of fantasy ultimately economic as much as perceptual?
