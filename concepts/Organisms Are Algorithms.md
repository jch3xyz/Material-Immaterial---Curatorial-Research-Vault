# Organisms Are Algorithms

## Brief Definition

Harari's keystone biological thesis that every animal — Homo sapiens included — is an assemblage of organic (biochemical, electrochemical) algorithms shaped by natural selection, such that sensations, emotions and decisions are themselves algorithms, and the validity of an algorithm is independent of the substrate (carbon or silicon) in which it runs.

## Longer Explanation

"Organisms are algorithms" is the three-word formula Harari treats as "current scientific dogma" — the premise on which most of *Homo Deus*'s argument about the human future rests. He first builds it up in **The Anthropocene** (Ch. 2 in the file numbering, "The Anthropocene"), where he reframes emotions not as a "mysterious spiritual phenomenon" but as "biochemical algorithms that are vital for the survival and reproduction of all mammals." Having defined an algorithm as "a methodical set of steps that can be used to make calculations, resolve problems and reach decisions," he extends the vending-machine model to the human being: "the man pressing the buttons and drinking the tea is also an algorithm. A much more complicated algorithm than the vending machine, no doubt, but still an algorithm." The crucial move is that the algorithms run *through feeling*: "What we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms." `Directly stated`

The thesis carries an explicit **substrate-independence** claim, which is what makes it the engine of the later chapters rather than a mere metaphor. In **The Great Decoupling** Harari compresses it into three principles: (1) "Organisms are algorithms. Every animal – including Homo sapiens – is an assemblage of organic algorithms shaped by natural selection over millions of years of evolution"; (2) "Algorithmic calculations are not affected by the materials from which you build the calculator"; (3) "As long as the calculations remain valid, what does it matter whether the algorithms are manifested in carbon or silicon?" The carbon/silicon indifference is the hinge: if organic and electronic algorithms obey the same mathematical laws, then non-organic algorithms can in principle "replicate or surpass" anything organic algorithms do. `Directly stated`

In **The Human Spark** the thesis is restated in its sharpest mathematical form — "Present-day dogma holds that organisms are algorithms, and that algorithms can be represented in mathematical formulas" — and is also stress-tested against its own limit. Harari notes that "none of the data-processing systems we have created needs subjective experiences in order to function," so consciousness remains a residue the organism-as-algorithm picture cannot yet explain (a tension this vault tracks under [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]]). He likewise flags that human "signatures of consciousness" derived from "carbon-based human neural networks" may not transfer to "silicon-based computers." So Harari holds the thesis as reigning dogma while marking consciousness as the open wound in it. `Directly stated` / `Strongly implied`

**The Time Bomb in the Laboratory** supplies the corollary for agency: once the "Sapiens black box" is opened, scientists find "neither soul, nor free will, nor 'self' – but only genes, hormones and neurons," and "the free individual is just a fictional tale concocted by an assembly of biochemical algorithms." Finally, **The Data Religion** makes the thesis the first premise of [[Dataism]]: "In the 150 years since Charles Darwin published *On the Origin of Species*, the life sciences have come to see organisms as biochemical algorithms," and "the Dataist idea that 'organisms are algorithms' is significant due to its day-to-day practical consequences." Harari is careful that the responsibility "lies with the biologists," not the computer scientists — it was biology that "dismantled the wall between the organic and inorganic." `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — Coins and repeats the exact phrase "organisms are algorithms" across five chapters. His variant is specifically *biological and substrate-independent*: emotions/sensations are organic algorithms shaped by natural selection (the evolutionary genealogy matters), and their validity does not depend on carbon vs silicon. He presents it not as his own claim but as reigning "scientific dogma," then uses it as the load-bearing premise for [[Dataism]] and for the obsolescence of the liberal individual.

## Related Concepts

- [[Dataism]] — the worldview "organisms are algorithms" underwrites; Harari names it the first of Dataism's two founding scientific waves (Darwin + Turing).
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the broader ontology (the universe as data flows / computation) of which this thesis is the biological special case.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the somatic correlate: if the organism is an algorithm, the living body is rendered as data-processing rather than felt flesh.
- [[Patternism]] — the transhumanist claim that the self is a pattern/substrate-independent; "carbon or silicon" indifference is the shared structural move, here grounded in biology rather than personal identity.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the governance/epistemic extension: if humans are biochemical algorithms, external algorithms can know and steer them.
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — the residue the thesis cannot dissolve: no known algorithm requires subjective experience, so consciousness resists reduction to algorithm.
- [[The Mind-Body Problem]] — the older question the thesis tries to liquidate by collapsing mind into electrochemical algorithm.
- [[Eliminativism]] — kindred reductive move (soul, self, free will "ditched" as the eliminativist would ditch folk-psychological entities).
- [[Mind Uploading]] · [[Transhumanism]] — downstream possibilities licensed by substrate-independence.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the indivisible, free, self-authoring "individual" that the thesis explicitly dissolves into "an assembly of biochemical algorithms"; Harari frames the two as incompatible.
- [[Embodiment]] — to the extent embodiment insists that lived, felt flesh is not reducible to data-processing, it resists the algorithmic reduction (though Harari's own emphasis on evolved *organic* algorithms complicates a clean opposition).

## Questions It Raises

- If sensations and emotions *are* algorithms, why does any organism need subjective experience at all — what does feeling add that a non-conscious algorithm lacks? (Harari concedes science has no answer.)
- Does the carbon/silicon indifference actually follow, or does it smuggle in the unproven assumption that consciousness is irrelevant to the algorithm?
- Is "organisms are algorithms" a discovery or a period metaphor — the computer succeeding the steam engine as the era's model of the psyche?
- If the thesis is true, on what non-circular ground can human life retain special value over a more capable non-organic algorithm?
