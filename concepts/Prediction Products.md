# Prediction Products

## Brief Definition

For Shoshana Zuboff, prediction products are the manufactured outputs of machine intelligence — forecasts of human behavior fabricated from [[Behavioral Surplus]] and sold to surveillance capitalism's actual customers in [[Behavioral Futures Markets]], anticipating what we will do "now, soon, and later."

## Longer Explanation

Prediction products sit at the center of surveillance capitalism's value chain. Human experience is unilaterally claimed as free raw material and translated into behavioral data; the portion not returned as service improvement is declared a proprietary [[Behavioral Surplus]] and "fed into advanced manufacturing processes known as 'machine intelligence,' and fabricated into prediction products that anticipate what you will do now, soon, and later" (`Directly stated`). The products are not improvements to the user but bets *about* the user, manufactured for someone else.

This reframes who the customer is and what is being sold. Zuboff distinguishes three moments: the raw material (surplus), the manufacturing engine ([[Machine Intelligence]]), and "the products," which "process behavioral surplus into prediction products designed to forecast what we will feel, think, and do: now, soon, and later" (`Directly stated`). Because the firm sells *predictions* rather than raw data, it can claim privacy purity while monetizing a "world-historic private hoard of behavioral surplus." The decisive discovery is that "we are less valuable than others' bets on our future behavior" — a reversal that, Zuboff writes, "changed everything" (`Directly stated`): the human is demoted from customer to the source of the commodity, while the real demand comes from those eager to lay bets on our future.

The quality of a prediction product depends on the volume and granularity of surplus, which is why the logic drives ever-deeper extraction. In the "rendition" phase, "online and offline behavioral surplus — your e-mail content, where you went this afternoon, what you said, what you did, how you felt — are combined into prediction products that can serve an emerging marketplace in which every aspect of your daily reality is up for bid" (`Directly stated`). Prediction quality thus becomes the engine that pushes [[Rendition]] from the surfaces of online life into the depths of feeling, body, and personality. The drive toward certainty — predictions that approach observed fact — links the prediction-product to later developments in the book: behavioral modification, "make them dance," and instrumentarian power (`Strongly implied` — this note is a forward-anchor; the certainty/modification arc is developed in later chapters).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — the coiner of the term within her account of surveillance capitalism. Prediction products are the saleable output of the "behavioral value reinvestment cycle" turned extractive: machine intelligence converts behavioral surplus into forecasts of human action, sold not to the people predicted but to business customers in behavioral futures markets. Their pursuit of predictive certainty motivates ever-deeper rendition and, ultimately, behavioral modification.
- Resonant variants in the vault: Zuboff's prediction product is the commercial instantiation of what Han names the ambition of [[Big Data]] and what Harari frames as [[Dataism]] — the claim that aggregated behavioral data can know and pre-empt us. Zuboff differs in locating the value not in knowledge as such but in the *sale of bets* about future behavior (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the raw material; prediction products are what surplus is manufactured into.
- [[Machine Intelligence]] — the manufacturing process that fabricates predictions from surplus.
- [[Behavioral Futures Markets]] — the marketplace where prediction products are traded; the demand side that makes surplus extraction profitable.
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic logic for which prediction products are the core commodity.
- [[Rendition]] — the extraction operations driven ever deeper by the demand for better predictions.
- [[Big Data]] — Han's and Harari's framing of the predictive ambition that prediction products commercialize.
- [[Dataism]] — the worldview in which behavioral data is held to know us better than we know ourselves.

## Opposing Concepts

- The image of the digital service as built *for the user*: prediction products invert this, making the user the source of a commodity sold to others (`Strongly implied`).

## Questions It Raises

- If the product is a *bet on* our future rather than a service *to* us, what does "the customer" mean — and who, if anyone, represents the people being predicted?
- Does the demand for predictive certainty necessarily escalate from forecasting behavior to modifying it? (Forward-anchor: developed in later chapters on behavioral modification and instrumentarian power.)
- Can a prediction product be "accurate" without foreclosing the very future tense it forecasts?
- How does selling *predictions* rather than *data* let surveillance capitalists claim privacy compliance while monetizing the deepest behavioral surplus?
