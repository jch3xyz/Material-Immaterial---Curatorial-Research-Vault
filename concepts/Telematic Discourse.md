# Telematic Discourse

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for the new order of communication generated by telematic networking — neither speech nor writing, but an "out-of-body, asynchronous, dispersed, interactive, and semantically layered" form of discourse that, read through Foucault's *L'Ordre du discours*, promises to escape the institutional control of discourse and constitute a planetary "fellowship of discourse."

## Longer Explanation

In "Art and Telematics: towards a Network Consciousness" (1984) Ascott argues that interwoven, shared telematic text is "a form of discourse for which we have no name. It is not speech, nor is it writing as we know it in the book." It is a genuinely new *order* of discourse — the same category Foucault analyzes — but one whose form is unlike its predecessors: distributed in authorship, negotiated in meaning, dispersed across the planet, and "invisible in a sense." Ascott takes Foucault's *L'Ordre du discours* explicitly as his guide while noting that "in no way does he refer to the telematic process or to distributed authorship." The move is to apply Foucault's apparatus — the "will to truth," the procedures by which "the production of discourse is at once controlled, selected, organised, and redistributed," the closed "fellowships of discourse" — to a medium Foucault never anticipated [`Directly stated`].

The crux is an *inversion* of Foucault's diagnosis. Where Foucault describes how every order of discourse is policed and contained, Ascott proposes "perhaps naively" that telematic discourse can *escape* that containment: its planetary reach, its invisibility, its asynchronous and out-of-body qualities make it "less amenable to control" and "less vulnerable to cultural constraint than earlier modes of expression." From this he projects "a much more inclusive, indeed, planetary, 'fellowship of discourse'... lying outside and circumnavigating the institutional management of discourse as it now exists in book production, conventional telecommunications, and entertainment media structures." The closed fellowships of art (sender/receiver rules, the "separateness of the writer") are to be dissolved into an open, interchangeable, distributed field. Ascott is candid that this hope runs against "society's relentless determination always to institutionalise and contain creativity by any means," and he registers the counter-pressure of "both regional and international regulatory bodies" [`Directly stated` for the hope; `Strongly implied` for its hedged status].

Ascott names the political stakes precisely: "To engage in telematic communication is to be at once everywhere and nowhere. In this, it is subversive." The everywhere/nowhere placelessness that makes the network ungovernable is the same condition that lets it subvert authorship, individual ownership, and "the bricks and mortar of institutions of culture and learning," replacing them with "an invisible college and a floating museum" [`Directly stated`].

In "Concerning Nets and Spurs: Meaning, Mind, and Telematic Diffusion" (1985) Ascott deepens the discourse thread by setting telematics within the poststructuralist project. "Telematics brings a new order of discourse into being that embodies and amplifies Barthes's earlier insight" — the text as "a tissue of quotations" with no original "Author God." Here discourse is carried by "telematics... the system of systems," which moves images and texts "in a flux of meanings in which there are no 'senders' and 'receivers' (as in the old models of information theory) but only 'users.'" Telematic process "involves ambiguity, uncertainty, and incompleteness; meaning is not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined." Telematic discourse is thus the *medium of meaning* in the network: not a channel for messages but a field in which meaning is "telematically inscribed in the networking that is human behaviour at its most liberated" [`Directly stated`].

This concept holds the discourse/Foucault thread specifically. It is distinct from [[Distributed Authorship]] (the authorship pole — the death of the single author, the dispersal of creation) and from [[Reciprocal Communication]] (the sender/receiver→user pole — the collapse of the information-theory dyad). Telematic discourse is what those two transformations *produce*: a new order of language with no settled name, characterized through Foucault's order-of-discourse [`Interpretive synthesis`].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and theorizes the concept across chapters 15–16. Telematic networking "generates discourse—discourse of an order unlike speech or writing." Read through Foucault's *L'Ordre du discours*, it is the order of discourse that escapes institutional control to form a planetary "fellowship of discourse"; via Barthes and the "system of systems," it is the flux of negotiated meaning carried among "users" rather than senders and receivers.
- **[[Michel Foucault]]** (Ascott's source, used *against* its original grain) — *L'Ordre du discours* supplies the framework: discourse "controlled, selected, organised, and redistributed," the "will to truth," the closed "fellowships of discourse." Ascott deploys it to argue that telematic discourse can evade this control. **Vault-internal inversion:** [[Byung-Chul Han]] reads the same Foucault for *intensified* control — the [[Digital Panopticon]] — where Ascott reads him for *escape* from control. See [[Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault]] for the control-side reading [`Interpretive synthesis`].

## Related Concepts

- [[Telematic Art]] — the medium and art form whose communicative life *is* telematic discourse; "telematics... the system of systems."
- [[Distributed Authorship]] — the authorship pole; telematic discourse is the discourse such distributed authorship generates ("Authorship is distributed, meaning is negotiated").
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the sender/receiver pole; in telematic discourse "there are no 'senders' and 'receivers'... but only 'users.'"
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the planetary scale of the discourse; the "fellowship of discourse" spreading "over the whole planet."
- [[Information]] — Ascott rejects "the old models of information theory" (Shannon's sender/receiver dyad) that telematic discourse supersedes.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's reading of the networked society as a regime of control and self-exposure; the same Foucauldian apparatus and the same global network that Ascott hopes will *evade* control, Han argues *intensifies* it. The opposition is the vault's sharpest Foucault inversion.

## Questions It Raises

- Was Ascott's hope "naive," as he half-conceded? Has telematic discourse escaped institutional management, or has it been recaptured by precisely the regulatory bodies, multinationals, and platform structures he flagged — and which Han later theorizes as the [[Digital Panopticon]]?
- If telematic discourse is "neither speech nor writing," what is its grammar of authority — how do power and the "will to truth" operate where roles of speaking and listening are interchangeable and authorship is dispersed?
- Can a "planetary 'fellowship of discourse'" be genuinely open, or does every order of discourse, by Foucault's own logic, reconstitute a closed fellowship with its own rules of entry?
- How does the everywhere/nowhere placelessness that makes telematic discourse "subversive" also make it ungovernable in ways that cut against, not only for, emancipation?
