# Elements Interconnections and Purpose

## Brief Definition

The three constituents of any system — elements (its visible, often tangible parts), interconnections (the relationships that bind them, largely flows of information), and a function or purpose (the least obvious constituent, yet the most crucial determinant of the system's behavior).

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows, a system "isn't just any old collection of things"; it is "an interconnected set of elements that is coherently organized in a way that achieves something," and so "must consist of three kinds of things: elements, interconnections, and a function or purpose" (Ch. 1). The triad is Meadows's opening anatomy of what a system *is* — the frame on which the whole of *Thinking in Systems* is built — and the way she ranks the three parts is what makes the concept load-bearing for this vault.

The ranking runs against intuition. Elements are "often the easiest parts to notice, because many of them are visible, tangible things," yet they are "often (not always) least important in defining the unique characteristics of the system." A tree changes its cells constantly and its leaves every year; a body replaces most of its cells every few weeks; a university turns over its students and staff — and each "goes on being itself... even with complete substitutions of its elements—as long as its interconnections and purposes remain intact." The parts we can hold in our hands turn out to be the least defining. `Directly stated`.

The interconnections — the relationships "that hold the elements together" — matter more, and Meadows locates their power in a register that is not material at all: "Many of the interconnections in systems operate through the flow of information. Information holds systems together and plays a great role in determining how they operate." Change the interconnections and "the system may be greatly altered... even though the same players are on the team" (change football's rules to basketball's, and "you've got... a whole new ball game"). `Directly stated`.

The decisive constituent is the least perceptible one. "If information-based relationships are hard to see, functions or purposes are even harder," because a purpose "is not necessarily spoken, written, or expressed explicitly, except through the operation of the system" — it must be "deduced from behavior, not from rhetoric or stated goals." Meadows's summary judgment is the ranking that makes this concept a spine for the vault: "The least obvious part of the system, its function or purpose, is often the most crucial determinant of the system's behavior." The intangible register — information and purpose — governs the tangible one, and it does so precisely because it is the harder to see. This is the native-discipline grounding of the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` line: not a claim about art or media but a general systems principle that the immaterial ordering of a thing outranks its matter. It also seeds the book's later leverage-points hierarchy, where the highest-leverage places to intervene (goals, paradigms, the power to transcend paradigms) sit in the intangible register of purpose and information, and the lowest (numbers, physical stocks) sit in the material one. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — introduces the triad as the definition of a system and ranks the three parts by their effect on behavior when "imagined changed one by one": elements least, interconnections more, function or purpose most. She stresses that "to ask whether elements, interconnections, or purposes are most important in a system is to ask an unsystemic question. All are essential. All interact. All have their roles" — the ranking concerns which part, when altered, most changes behavior, not which part is dispensable.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — this concept does not so much oppose the tension as *adjudicate* it: Meadows ranks the immaterial constituents (information, purpose) above the material ones (elements), giving the vault's material/immaterial axis a systems-theoretic verdict rather than an aesthetic one.

## Questions It Raises

- If purpose is "deduced from behavior, not from rhetoric or stated goals," how do we read the actual (as opposed to professed) purpose of a technological or economic system whose designers claim one aim and whose behavior enacts another?
- When Meadows says information "holds systems together," is she using "information" in the same sense as Shannon, Han, or Hayles — or does the systems sense (a signal that reaches a decision point) sit athwart the informatic one?
- If the intangible constituents most determine behavior, does the vault's interest in dematerialization and the immaterial art object find, in general systems theory, a claim that the immaterial was always the governing register — or is that an over-reading of a claim scoped to system *behavior*?
