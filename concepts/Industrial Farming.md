# Industrial Farming

## Brief Definition

Modern industrialised animal agriculture as Harari treats it: the meat and dairy industries' systematic confinement, manipulation and exploitation of billions of domesticated animals, made possible by science and justified by humanism — the central moral test case for whether human exceptionalism can survive its own consequences.

## Longer Explanation

For Harari, industrial farming is not merely an economic practice but the proving ground of an entire worldview. He frames it through a historical sequence of human–animal relations: animists saw "no essential gap separating humans from other animals"; the Agricultural Revolution "silenced animals and plants" and justified their subjugation through theist religions; and the Scientific Revolution then "silenced the gods too," leaving "a one-man show" in which "humankind now does with them as it pleases." Industrial farming is the endpoint of that sequence — humanity acting as god over other animals, but "not been particularly just or merciful gods." (Directly stated)

The decisive move is Harari's parallel between justifications: "Whereas theism justified traditional agriculture in the name of God, humanism has justified modern industrial farming in the name of Man." Crucially, modern science does not merely inherit ancient cruelty; it intensifies it. Because "modern science and technology give humans powers that far exceed those of the ancient gods," industrial farming can "subjugate cows, pigs and chickens to more extreme conditions than those prevailing in traditional agricultural societies." Where a pre-modern peasant confining thousands of animals would have triggered a "deadly epidemic" that "no priest, shaman or god could have prevented," vaccinations, hormones, antibiotics and climate control now make the crowded coop "feasible." The powers exceeding the gods are precisely what enable a crueller exploitation than any pre-modern society managed. (Directly stated)

The cruelty is specific, not rhetorical. Drawing on evolutionary psychology, Harari argues that domesticated animals retain "physical, emotional and social needs that are redundant on human farms" — needs that "continue to mould the subjective experiences of the animal" even after the selection pressures that shaped them vanish. The sow in a gestation crate "still feels very strong urges" to explore, socialise and bond, "and if these urges are not fulfilled she suffers greatly," displaying "acute frustration alternating with extreme despair." Above all, industrial agriculture attacks the one emotion "apparently shared by all mammals: the mother–infant bond" — "the most fundamental emotional bond in the mammal kingdom." This grounds Harari's most pointed analogy, recruiting Harry Harlow's cloth-mother experiments: "What Harry Harlow did to a few hundred monkeys, the meat and dairy industries are doing to billions of animals every year." (Directly stated)

Finally, Harari turns industrial farming into a mirror held up to the human future. Having argued that "organisms are algorithms" — that human and porcine emotions are "biochemical algorithms" differing in complexity but not in kind — he asks whether a future superintelligence might treat humans as humans treat pigs: "Would it be okay, for example, for an artificial intelligence to exploit humans and even kill them to further its own needs and desires? If it should never be allowed to do that, despite its superior intelligence and power, why is it ethical for humans to exploit and kill pigs?" Industrial farming thus becomes a preview of how a more intelligent and powerful entity might treat a lesser one — the unflattering image humanism would rather not see. (Directly stated)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — In *[[Homo Deus]]* (Ch. 2, "The Anthropocene"), industrial farming is the empirical and moral hinge of the book's critique of humanism. It demonstrates that "industrial farming sanctifies human needs, whims and wishes, while disregarding everything else," that science grants powers "exceeding those of the ancient gods," and that the "magical spark" humans claim to possess — and the AI may not — is the same fiction that licenses the slaughterhouse. The practice is condemned not on sentiment but on Harari's own functionalist premise that organisms are algorithms: if there is no spark distinguishing pigs from humans, there may be none distinguishing humans from a superior machine.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality]] — Industrial farming operationalises one pole of this tension. It is built on human exceptionalism (the "sacred essence" of *Homo sapiens*, the soul "only" humans were given) and is precisely what collapses once Harari's "organisms are algorithms" thesis pulls ontological equality back into view; the AI thought-experiment threatens to reposition humans on the exploited side of the same hierarchy.

## Questions It Raises

- If the only difference between human and porcine suffering is the complexity of the algorithm, on what non-arbitrary ground can the exploitation of one be condemned and the other licensed?
- Is the "magical spark" Harari interrogates a real moral property, a humanist religious residue, or a self-serving fiction — and does the answer change once a superintelligence can claim the same superiority of "intelligence and power" over us?
- Does the fact that modern science *intensifies* rather than merely inherits ancient cruelty implicate scientific progress itself in the moral indictment, or only the humanist frame that directs it?
- If industrial farming is a "preview" of how a superintelligence might treat humanity, does the ethical force of the analogy depend on our being wrong about human exceptionalism — or on its being true and simply applied against us?
