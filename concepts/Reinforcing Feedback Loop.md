# Reinforcing Feedback Loop

## Brief Definition

A reinforcing feedback loop is the amplifying, self-multiplying kind of feedback loop — the "vicious or virtuous circle" — in which a stock's growth feeds back to accelerate its own further growth (or its decline feeds its own further decline), producing exponential growth or runaway collapse.

## Longer Explanation

In [[Donella Meadows]]'s *Thinking in Systems*, the reinforcing feedback loop is one of the two elementary building blocks of all system dynamics, the counterpart and opposite of the [[Balancing Feedback Loop]]. Where a balancing loop is goal-seeking and equilibrating — it "opposes whatever direction of change is imposed on the system" — a reinforcing loop does the reverse: it "enhances whatever direction of change is imposed on it." It "generates more input to a stock the more that is already there (and less input the less that is already there)," so the loop compounds on itself. Meadows notes it with an **R** inside her loop diagrams (against the **B** of the balancing loop). This is the [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] mechanism responsible for anything in a system that snowballs, explodes, or spirals down.

The canonical example is compound bank interest: "The more money you have in the bank, the more interest you earn, which is added to the money already in the bank, where it earns even more interest." Crucially, Meadows insists this is "not simple linear growth" — "growth goes faster and faster. The more is there, the more is added. This kind of growth is called 'exponential.'" The loop is structurally identical whether the growing stock is money, rabbits ("the more rabbits there are, the more rabbit parents there are to make baby rabbits"), an eroding hillside, capital reinvesting in output, or "weapons in an arms race." She supplies a rule of thumb — the doubling time of an exponentially growing stock is roughly 70 divided by the growth rate expressed as a percentage.

The concept is deliberately **structural and value-neutral**. The same amplifying structure is "either good news or bad news, depending on what is growing." Meadows lists money in the bank, people with HIV/AIDS, pests in a cornfield, a national economy, and an arms race in one breath, because the loop is indifferent to what it multiplies. Reinforcing loops "are found whenever a stock has the capacity to reinforce or reproduce itself" — populations and economies being the paradigm cases. This native-discipline definition is the parent structure that other authors in the vault take up and load with a value sign: it is the value-neutral engine underneath [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s pathologized [[Acceleration]] and [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s celebrated [[The Law of Accelerating Returns]]. Those are cross-links, not equivalences — Meadows describes the mechanism; Han condemns and Kurzweil exalts a particular instance of it.

Reinforcing loops rarely act alone. Meadows stresses that in real systems "feedback loops rarely come singly" — "a single stock is likely to have several reinforcing and balancing loops of differing strengths pulling it in several directions." The interplay of a reinforcing loop against a balancing loop — where control passes from one to the other as conditions change — is the engine of [[Shifting Dominance]], and a reinforcing growth loop constrained by a balancing limit is the skeleton of [[The Limits-to-Growth Archetype]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — the reinforcing loop is one of her two elementary feedback structures (with the [[Balancing Feedback Loop]]): "amplifying, reinforcing, self-multiplying, snowballing — a vicious or virtuous circle." Self-enhancing, it drives exponential growth or runaway collapse, and is "found whenever a stock has the capacity to reinforce or reproduce itself." Structural and value-neutral: good or bad news depending only on what is growing.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — the parent category; the reinforcing loop is one of its two elementary kinds.
- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — its structural opposite; the two co-occur and tug against each other in every real system.
- [[Shifting Dominance]] — the pattern produced when a reinforcing loop and a balancing loop trade control of a stock over time.
- [[The Limits-to-Growth Archetype]] — a reinforcing growth loop eventually checked by a balancing loop as it approaches a carrying capacity or constraint.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the reinforcing loop is a foundational primitive of the discipline.
- [[Acceleration]] — [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s pathologized register of runaway self-amplifying growth; the reinforcing loop is its value-neutral parent structure (cross-link, not identity).
- [[The Law of Accelerating Returns]] — [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s celebrated register of the same exponential dynamic in technology (cross-link, not identity).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — a balancing loop is goal-seeking and equilibrating, opposing any imposed change; a reinforcing loop enhances any imposed change. They are structural opposites.

## Questions It Raises

- If the reinforcing loop is value-neutral, is the distinction between a "virtuous" and a "vicious" circle purely a matter of what one values in the growing stock — or does the runaway structure itself carry a normative charge?
- Because reinforcing loops "rarely come singly," when does a growth loop stay dominant and when does a balancing loop take over? (See [[Shifting Dominance]] and [[The Limits-to-Growth Archetype]].)
- Do [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s [[Acceleration]] and [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s [[The Law of Accelerating Returns]] describe the *same* underlying reinforcing dynamic under opposite value signs — and does naming the shared structure dissolve or sharpen their disagreement?
