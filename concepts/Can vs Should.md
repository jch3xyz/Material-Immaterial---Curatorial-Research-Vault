# Can vs Should

## General Orientation

This note tracks a single Han pairing across two of his books. *The Burnout Society* (2010) is the **origin** of the *Should→Can* modal shift: there Han first formulates that the modal verb determining [[Achievement Society|achievement society]] is not the disciplinary *Should* but *Can*. *Psychopolitics* (2014) then redeploys the pair in its sharper, more formal "freedom-as-crisis" register (the *Should* has a limit, the *Can* has none, so the *Can* is the more total coercion). The original note below was anchored secondhand in *Psychopolitics*; the section added here restores the primary source and folds in the *Burnout Society* material on the exhaustion of the *Can* — the "no-longer-being-able-to-be-able" that the limitless *Can* produces. The two accounts are continuous, not rival: the *Psychopolitics* asymmetry (limit vs. no-limit) is the worked-out consequence of the *Burnout Society* claim that *Can* simply outperforms *Should* at maximizing production. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], **Can vs Should** names the formal opposition at the core of his freedom-as-crisis thesis: the disciplinary *Should* (Sollen) governs through commandments and prohibitions and therefore has a limit, whereas the neoliberal *Can* (Können) has no limit — and so the compulsion it generates is unbounded and more coercive than disciplinary prohibition ever was.

## Longer Explanation

In the opening pages of *Psychopolitics*, Han locates the present "crisis of freedom" in a shift from one grammar of power to another. The disciplinary regime operated in the mode of the *Should*: it issued positive commandments and negative prohibitions, drawing a line the subject was not to cross. Because a prohibition draws a boundary, it also marks where its demand stops. "Should has a limit." The neoliberal regime, by contrast, operates in the mode of the *Can*: it does not forbid but enables, motivates, optimizes — "yes we can." And precisely because possibility has no ceiling, the imperative to actualize it never closes. "In contrast, Can has none. Thus, the compulsion entailed by Can is unlimited." `Directly stated`.

The pair is the formal engine of Han's central paradox. Freedom is, by definition, the opposite of coercion — "Being free means being free from constraint" — and yet, Han argues, "now freedom itself, which is supposed to be the opposite of constraint, is producing coercion." The *Can* is what makes this inversion possible. Where the disciplinary *Should* confronted the subject as an external master setting limits, the neoliberal *Can* is internalized as a self-issued project of "compulsive achievement and optimization." There is no external authority to satisfy and therefore no point of completion; the subject is freed from the master only to subjugate itself without limit. This is why Han insists the *Can* is the more total form of coercion: a prohibition can be obeyed, but a possibility can always be exceeded. `Directly stated`.

The *Can* thus supplies the formal underpinning for the rest of Han's diagnosis in this chapter. It is the mechanism by which the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] becomes "master and slave in one," practising [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploitation]] voluntarily; it is the structural reason the [[Exploitation of Freedom|exploitation of freedom]] yields higher returns than coercion against one's will ("Only when freedom is exploited are returns maximized"). The psychic symptoms Han names — depression and burnout — are read as the pathologies of a freedom with no limit: the exhaustion of a *Can* that can never have done enough. `Strongly implied`.

## The Burnout Society (Han) — the Should→Can modal shift (PRIMARY source)

*The Burnout Society* is where Han first stages the *Should→Can* shift, and it does so as a **change in the governing modal verb** of a whole social formation rather than as a formal asymmetry. [[Disciplinary Society|Disciplinary society]] is "a society of negativity," "defined by the negativity of prohibition," whose negative modal verb is *May Not* and whose compulsion adheres to *Should*. [[Achievement Society|Achievement society]], by contrast, "is in the process of discarding negativity": "Unlimited Can is the positive modal verb of achievement society. Its plural form—the affirmation, 'Yes, we can'—epitomizes achievement society's positive orientation." Prohibitions, commandments, and the law give way to "projects, initiatives, and motivation." The most compressed statement comes in the title chapter: "The modal verb that determines achievement society is not the Freudian Should, but Can." `Directly stated`.

Crucially, Han grounds the switch in **efficiency, not emancipation**. There is an underlying continuity — "the drive to maximize production inhabits the social unconscious" — and the disciplinary *Should* simply hits a productivity ceiling: "after a certain level of productivity obtains, the negativity of prohibition impedes further expansion." So the move to *Can* is a productivity upgrade: "The positivity of Can is much more efficient than the negativity of Should. Therefore, the social unconscious switches from Should to Can. The achievement-subject is faster and more productive than the obedience-subject." Han is careful to add the **continuity caveat** that this note's *Psychopolitics* section can obscure: "However, the Can does not revoke the Should. The obedience-subject remains disciplined. It has now completed the disciplinary stage. . . . Where increasing productivity is concerned, no break exists between Should and Can; continuity prevails." The *Can* does not abolish discipline; it absorbs and surpasses it. `Directly stated`.

**No-longer-being-able-to-be-able (the exhaustion of the *Can*).** The same chapter names the pathology that the limitless *Can* secretes. Depression "erupts at the moment when the achievement-subject is no longer able to be able [nicht mehr können kann]"; it is "first and foremost . . . creative fatigue and exhausted ability." Han's signature formulation makes the *Can* the very condition of its own collapse: "The complaint of the depressive individual, 'Nothing is possible,' can only occur in a society that thinks, 'Nothing is impossible.'" Because possibility is declared unlimited ("Nothing is impossible"), its felt failure ("Nothing is possible") is total and self-directed: "No-longer-being-able-to-be-able leads to destructive self-reproach and auto-aggression. The achievement-subject finds itself fighting with itself." This is the hinge between *Can* and [[Burnout]] — the *Can* runs without limit until the capacity to be able is itself exhausted, and the wound it leaves is turned inward. `Directly stated`. (The "no-longer-being-able-to-be-able" material is folded here rather than given a standalone note: it is the limit-case of the *Can*, not a separate concept.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — *Can* (Können) and *Should* (Sollen) are Han's own pairing, marking the passage from Foucault's disciplinary society to the neoliberal [[Achievement Society|achievement society]]. The *Should* prohibits and so has a limit; the *Can* enables and so has none. The distinctive Han move is that the *limitless* term is the more coercive one — freedom, not prohibition, becomes the medium of unbounded compulsion. *The Burnout Society* is the **primary source** of the shift (the modal verb of achievement society is *Can*, not *Should*; the switch is driven by *Can*'s greater efficiency at maximizing production; the *Can* does not revoke the *Should*); *Psychopolitics* sharpens it into the limit/no-limit asymmetry. The exhaustion of the *Can* — "no-longer-being-able-to-be-able," "Nothing is possible" in a society that thinks "Nothing is impossible" — names the burnout that the limitless *Can* produces.

## Related Concepts

- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — the *Can* is the formal reason exploiting freedom outperforms external coercion; "only when freedom is exploited are returns maximized."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the limitless *Can* is internalized as self-generated constraint; the achievement-subject exploits itself without a master.
- [[Achievement Society]] — the social formation governed by the *Can*; its subject is "master and slave in one." Han's primary statement of the modal shift: its positive modal verb is "unlimited Can," not the disciplinary *Should*.
- [[Burnout]] — the pathology of the exhausted *Can*: "no-longer-being-able-to-be-able," the moment the achievement-subject can no longer be able. "Nothing is possible" arising only where "Nothing is impossible" is the depressive/burnout flipside of the limitless *Can*.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Disciplinary Society]] — `Directly stated` (as the contrast term). The regime of the *Should*: commandments and prohibitions that have a limit. Han positions the *Can* against it not as more freedom but as more coercion.

## Questions It Raises

- If the *Should* is limited and the *Can* unlimited, is there any mode of freedom in Han's framework that is not already a form of compulsion — or has "Can" foreclosed the very possibility of an emancipatory freedom?
- Han treats the limit of the *Should* as a kind of shelter (a prohibition can be satisfied). Does this risk romanticizing disciplinary power, or is it a strictly formal point about boundedness?
- Where, concretely, is the line between an enabling possibility and a coercive imperative? The *Can* is coercive only once it is felt as something that *must* be actualized — what converts possibility into obligation?
- Is the limitlessness of the *Can* the same limitlessness Han attributes elsewhere to capital and to information, or are these distinct unbounded logics that happen to coincide?
