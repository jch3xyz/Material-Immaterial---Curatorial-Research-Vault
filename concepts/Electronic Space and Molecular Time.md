# Electronic Space and Molecular Time

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's paired diagnosis of the twenty-first-century condition for art: telematic networks make space "electronic" (dispersed, non-local, virtualised), while molecular biology, nanotechnology, and quantum physics make time "molecular" — a "bundle" of "variable rates, jumps, delays, phases, and reversals" — so that "the art of the next century is set in electronic space and molecular time."

## Longer Explanation

Ascott offers the couplet as a single compressed claim about where art now lives: "Just as space becomes 'electronic,' so time is becoming 'molecular.'" (Directly stated.) The two halves are not symmetrical metaphors but name two distinct technological trajectories whose convergence Ascott tracks across the essay. *Electronic space* is the space of [[Telematic Art]] and [[Connectivism]] — the non-local, asynchronous [[Data Space]] of "computer-mediated networks of data transfer, interactive videoconferencing, remote sensing, and telerobotics," where "human presence becomes virtualised and the individual self is distributed; space becomes virtualised and place becomes distributed." It is the spatial side of [[Network Consciousness]] and [[Distributed Cognition]], the field in which the telematic artist's "presence [is] distributed across the connection matrix."

*Molecular time* is Ascott's coinage for the temporality emerging from the biological and physical sciences. He defines it explicitly: "I use the term 'molecular time' to denote that bundle of meanings and interpretations of time provided in different but connected ways by cognitive science, neurobiology, computer science, and quantum physics, each of which recognises the variable rates, jumps, delays, phases, and reversals of time that exist both within our consciousness, in our interactions with artificial memory and intelligence, and in the world of quantum events." (Directly stated.) Time, on this account, ceases to be the uniform, linear flow of classical physics and clock culture and becomes granular, discontinuous, and reversible — a "molecular" texture borrowed from the time-scales of the nanosecond, the synapse, and the quantum event. Ascott routes the lineage through Gilles Deleuze's return to [[Henri Bergson]] on "time and duration" via "the new lines, openings, traces, leaps, dynamisms, discovered by a molecular biology of the brain." (Directly stated — Ascott cites Deleuze 1988.)

The couplet does its real work as a forecast for art. "This question of the transformation of the self within electronic space and molecular time — that is to say, in the context of advanced telematics and nanotechnology — will dominate the art of the next century." (Directly stated.) Electronic space corresponds to the "essentially electronic" trajectory — "computerised perception and intelligence, virtualisation, telematic communication"; molecular time corresponds to the "biological" trajectory — "nanotechnology, molecular engineering, the organisation of matter and emergent behaviour." Their meeting is what Ascott calls "Nature II": "This convergence set in electronic space and molecular time may well point us to a path leading back to nature, the sequel to nature . . . Nature II." (Directly stated.) Crucially, the convergence reverses Ascott's earlier emphasis on immateriality: it is a "rematerialization of art," "a marriage of the immaterial and the material," in which the "virtualisation" of electronic space is joined by "new processes of materialisation." This places the couplet squarely on the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension and reframes Ascott's own [[Dematerialization]]-adjacent telematics as a passage *through* the immaterial toward a "natural materiality" populated by [[Artificial Life]] and [[Emergence]]. (Strongly implied — Ascott names "rematerialization" and "the reinstatement … of a natural materiality" as the "logical outcome of the immateriality of the information society.")

The molecular-time half of the couplet is closely related to, but should be cross-linked rather than merged with, Ascott's [[Telematic Time]] (the "schizo-time" of chapter 19). Both name a non-linear, non-classical temporality of the network era; [[Telematic Time]] derives it from the *dispersal of the perceiving system* across the telematic network, whereas *molecular time* derives it from the *time-scales of the sciences* (neurobiology, computer science, quantum physics, nanotechnology). They are two descriptions of the same departure from linear time approached from the network side and the matter side. (Interpretive synthesis — the two essays use different vocabularies and grounds; Ascott does not equate the terms.) Both stand opposed to [[Acceleration]] in Han's and McLuhan's sense: molecular time is not linear time sped up but linear time made discontinuous and reversible, an expansion to be inhabited rather than a pathology of speed.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the couplet "electronic space and molecular time": space made "electronic" by telematic networks (dispersed, non-local, virtual) and time made "molecular" by cognitive science, neurobiology, computer science, and quantum physics ("variable rates, jumps, delays, phases, and reversals"). Their convergence is the "rematerialization" of art and the return to "Nature II." Read through [[Marshall McLuhan]]'s "rear-view mirror" (the content of a new medium is the medium that preceded it) and Deleuze's molecular reading of Bergson.

## Related Concepts

- [[Telematic Time]] — the closest cousin; the network-side "schizo-time." Cross-linked, not merged: molecular time is grounded in scientific time-scales (neuro/quantum/nano), telematic time in the dispersal of the perceiving system.
- [[Data Space]] / [[Network Consciousness]] / [[Distributed Cognition]] — the substance of "electronic space": the non-local field and dispersed intelligence in which telematic art is set.
- [[Connectivism]] — Ascott's name for telematic art practice; "everything is connected," the paradigm within which the couplet operates.
- [[Telematic Art]] / [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the practice and the affirmative networked encounter that inhabit electronic space.
- [[Artificial Life]] / [[Emergence]] — the molecular/biological pole: "life as a property of the organisation of matter," "bottom-up, distributed, local determination," which Ascott maps directly onto telematic art ("For 'life,' read 'art'!").
- [[Virtuality]] — "here and not here"; the virtualisation of presence that characterises electronic space.
- [[Dematerialization]] — the immaterial moment the couplet passes through; Ascott's "rematerialization" is its dialectical sequel ("a marriage of the immaterial and the material").

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Acceleration]] — Han's (and McLuhan's) pathology of linear speed and lost duration. Molecular time is not linear time made faster but linear time made *discontinuous and reversible* — "jumps, delays, phases, and reversals" — an affirmative, generative temporality rather than a temporal disorder. (Interpretive synthesis — the contrast is drawn across distinct vault sources; Ascott does not name acceleration.)

## Questions It Raises

- Is "molecular time" a literal claim about how time is structured at quantum and neural scales, or a metaphor that aestheticizes the time-scales of new instruments (the nanosecond, the synapse-scan, the clock-pulse)?
- If molecular time and [[Telematic Time]] both name the same departure from linear time from different directions, is there one new temporality of the network era or several, and does the difference matter for art practice?
- Ascott frames the couplet's convergence as a return to nature ("Nature II"). Is "rematerialization" a genuine reversal of dematerialization, or does grounding the "natural" in nanotechnology and artificial life simply rename the artificial as natural?
- Does binding art's temporality to a particular reading of quantum physics (variable rates, reversals, "the world of quantum events") commit the work to a contested physics — and what survives if that reading is rejected?
