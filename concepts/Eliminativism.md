# Eliminativism

## Brief Definition

The philosophical position that consciousness or interior experience does not exist — that subjectivity is an illusion produced by the brain rather than a real feature of the world.

## Longer Explanation

In *God, Human, Animal, Machine*, Meghan O'Gieblyn arrives at eliminativism through the [[Mind as Machine]] metaphor itself. She observes that metaphors are normally abandoned once they prove insufficient, but that in some cases "they become only more entrenched: the limits of the comparison come to redefine the concepts themselves." Eliminativism is that move taken to its limit: "Just as computers can operate convincingly without any internal life, so can we." If the brain is a computer running unconscious computation all the way down, then the felt interiority that the computer cannot model is not an unsolved residue — it is simply declared nonexistent. (`Directly stated`)

For O'Gieblyn the doctrine is defined chiefly through Daniel Dennett, whom she names "chief among the deniers." Dennett ridicules belief in interior experience as the "Cartesian theater" — the delusion that a homunculus sits in the brain watching its representations of the world on a screen, a picture that collapses into infinite regress. On his account "the mind is just the brain and the brain is nothing but computation, unconscious all the way down," and introspection is "merely an illusion, a made-up story." The contrast O'Gieblyn foregrounds is with the philosophical zombie: "The idea that, in addition to all of those, there is this extra special something—subjectivity—that distinguishes us from the zombie," Dennett writes, "that's an illusion." (`Directly stated`)

Eliminativism is the explicit denial of [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]]: O'Gieblyn writes that for these thinkers "there is no 'hard problem' because that which the problem is trying to explain—interior experience—is not real." The position is therefore paired with the Hard Problem as its mirror image — the Hard Problem insists subjective experience is the thing left unexplained; eliminativism dissolves the problem by abolishing its object. (`Directly stated`)

O'Gieblyn's distinctive intervention is to read eliminativism as itself crypto-Cartesian. Although [[René Descartes]] is the figure the deniers most want to escape — Dennett's "Cartesian theater" is named as a target — O'Gieblyn argues the denial reproduces his metaphysics: "To say that consciousness is an illusion is to place it outside the material world, deeming it something—much like Descartes's soul—that does not exist within time or space." The eliminativist, in trying to banish the ghost, makes consciousness even more ghostly, relocating it to a non-spatial, non-temporal nowhere. She frames this as the long aftermath of the scientific bracketing of mind: "Science put a bracket around consciousness because it was too difficult to study objectively, but this methodological avoidance eventually led to metaphysical denial." (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — treats eliminativism not as a settled science but as the terminal symptom of the computational metaphor and a covert return of [[René Descartes]]'s dualism; reads it against her own loss of faith in the "thinking mind."
- **[[Daniel Dennett]]** — the position's foremost exponent in O'Gieblyn's account: the mind is the brain, the brain is computation "unconscious all the way down," and subjectivity that would distinguish us from the zombie "is an illusion."
- **[[Galen Strawson]]** — its sharpest critic: he dubs the theory "the Great Denial" and calls it "the most absurd conclusion ever to have entered into philosophical thought."

## Related Concepts

- [[Mind as Machine]] — eliminativism is the metaphor pushed past its breaking point, where the machine's lack of inner life is taken as proof we have none either.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — supplies the premise (brain as nothing but computation) that licenses the denial of consciousness.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the same informational reduction that recasts the body as pattern recasts mind as substrate-independent computation.
- [[Posthumanism]] — eliminativism radicalizes the posthuman erasure of interiority that O'Gieblyn inherits from the [[Mind as Machine]] tradition.
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — Dennett's brain-as-computation reduces mind to information processing rather than felt presence.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — the direct opposite: the Hard Problem treats subjective experience as the central unexplained fact; eliminativism denies that there is any such fact to explain.

## Questions It Raises

- If subjectivity is an illusion, what becomes of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] subject — is the body-as-data project completed, or rendered incoherent, when there is no one for whom the data is experienced?
- O'Gieblyn notes that "if I have no 'privileged access' to the workings of my brain," she cannot claim authority over her own motives. Can the denial of consciousness be asserted by a being who, on its own theory, has no reliable interior access to assert anything?
- If consciousness is placed "outside the material world... much like Descartes's soul," has eliminativism actually defeated dualism, or smuggled it back as a non-spatial residue?
- When technology and perception are modeled computationally, why are the "sensory perceptive tasks" we share with animals — seeing, feeling the world — the hardest for machines, and what does that say about where meaning and experience actually live?
- Does declaring meaning and interior life unreal solve "our most pressing existential problems," or merely transfer them, as O'Gieblyn says of the metaphor, "to a new substrate"?
