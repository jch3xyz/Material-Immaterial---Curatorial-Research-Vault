# Information Civilization

## Brief Definition

Shoshana Zuboff's name for the widest possible frame within which the oldest political questions must now be posed: the civilization-scale stage of human society — successor to "information society" — that is being shaped by surveillance capitalism and its instrumentarian power, and which threatens to "thrive at the expense of human nature."

## Longer Explanation

*Information civilization* is Zuboff's deliberate enlargement of scale. Where earlier critics worried about an "information workplace" or an "information society," Zuboff argues that the stakes of the digital have outgrown those frames: "the oldest questions must be addressed to the widest possible frame, which is best defined as 'civilization' or, more specifically, information civilization." The move is rhetorical and analytical at once — it lifts the discussion from the firm, the market, or the polity to the level of a whole order of human life, and it reframes the central question as a question of dwelling: "Will this emerging civilization be a place that we can call home?" `Directly stated`

The concept is structurally a successor term. It names the same long arc that "information society" once named, but at the moment that arc is captured by a specific economic logic. The note [[Information]] (the cross-author hub running from Shannon and Wiener through Han, Hayles, Harari, McLuhan and others) is the substance this civilization is built from; *information civilization* is the civilizational *stage* that the production and capture of information now organizes. Zuboff is precise that the stage is not neutral: "we cannot evaluate the current trajectory of information civilization without a clear appreciation that technology is not and never can be a thing in itself, isolated from economics and society." Technological inevitability, on her account, does not exist — which is exactly why the civilization can be contested. `Directly stated`

The frame carries Zuboff's central analogy. Just as industrial civilization "flourished at the expense of nature and now threatens to cost us the Earth, an information civilization shaped by surveillance capitalism and its new instrumentarian power will thrive at the expense of human nature and will threaten to cost us our humanity." Climate chaos is the legacy of the first; the threat to human nature — to "the human expectation of sovereignty over one's own life and authorship of one's own experience" — is the legacy the second is in the process of producing. In the book's final chapter Zuboff calls this a new "arc": "Right now we are at the beginning of a new arc that I have called information civilization, and it repeats the same dangerous arrogance. The aim now is not to dominate nature but rather human nature." The shift is from machines that overcome the limits of bodies to machines that modify behavior — the operational signature of [[Instrumentarian Power]]. `Directly stated`

What is finally at stake in the information civilization, for Zuboff, is "the dominant principle of social ordering" and the right of individuals and societies to answer three questions — "Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?" Surveillance capitalism's usurpation of these rights is what makes the civilization a "battleground" rather than a settled condition. The book ends on the wager that the frame is still open: "the crucial friction that reasserts the primacy of a flourishing human future as the foundation of our information civilization. If the digital future is to be our home, then it is we who must make it so." The concept is thus load-bearing and kept distinct from [[Surveillance Capitalism]] (the economic logic) and [[Instrumentarian Power]] (its form of power): it is the *civilizational frame* those two forces are contesting. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins and owns the term as the widest frame for the book's argument. Information civilization is the successor to "information society," the stage now being shaped by [[Surveillance Capitalism]] and [[Instrumentarian Power]]; it is historically conditioned, not technologically inevitable, and its defining peril is that it will "thrive at the expense of human nature."
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** (resonance, not citation) — McLuhan's [[The Global Village]] is an earlier civilization-scale frame for the electric extension of the nervous system; Zuboff supplies the *economic* and *political* content (extraction, instrumentarian power) that McLuhan's media-form analysis brackets. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (resonance, not citation) — [[Dataism]] names the civilization-scale *worldview* in which information flow becomes the supreme value; Zuboff names the *market form and power* that worldview underwrites. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — information civilization intensifies the immaterial-information pole at civilizational scale: lived, material human experience is rendered into behavioral data, so the frame sits on the vault's object→system / material→immaterial spine. The opposition is between a civilization ordered around material human life and one ordered around its data-image.

## Questions It Raises

- If "technology is not and never can be a thing in itself," what is the necessary condition of the information civilization's current trajectory — its digital infrastructure or the economic logic that captures it?
- Zuboff frames the civilization as a "place" we might "call home" — is dwelling (home or exile) the right register for a political-economic argument, and what does it add beyond rights and regulation?
- How does Zuboff's economically-driven information civilization relate to McLuhan's [[The Global Village]] and Harari's [[Dataism]], which name the same civilization-scale shift without naming the extraction logic?
- If the aim of the new arc is to dominate "human nature" rather than nature, what would a successful defense of "human nature" even consist in — and who decides what it is?
