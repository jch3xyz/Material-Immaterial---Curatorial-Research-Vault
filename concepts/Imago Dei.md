# Imago Dei

## Brief Definition

*Imago dei* — the image-of-God doctrine from Genesis (Hebrew *tselem*, denoting in the rabbinical tradition a functional rather than physical likeness) — is, for [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]], "the original anthropological metaphor," the answer to the question "What is a human like?" that for centuries was answered "Like a god."

## Longer Explanation

O'Gieblyn opens her account of the imago dei as a *metaphor* and then immediately reverses its arrow. The doctrine "comes from the creation narrative in the first chapter of Genesis, when God declares his intent to 'make man in Our image, according to Our likeness.'" The Hebrew word for "image," *tselem*, "means 'shadow' or 'outline,' though in the rabbinical tradition the phrase denotes less a physical resemblance than a functional one" — God "had conferred some essential part of himself to man as a special honor." That essential part was historically identified with mind: Maimonides located the image of God in consciousness, "the ability to conceive of oneself as a self," while for the Christian fathers "imago dei has long been synonymous with reason," what Tertullian called "the rational element." This is the doctrine's first axis — a claim about human exceptionalism grounded in interiority.

The note's center is O'Gieblyn's **two-way reading of the metaphor**. Tracing Stewart Guthrie's argument that anthropomorphism is an evolutionary perceptual strategy — natural selection rewards those who, confronting an uncertain object, "bet high" that it is alive and human — she concludes that "this habit of seeing our image everywhere in the natural world is what gave birth to the idea of God." The decisive inference is that "metaphors are two-way streets": "It is not so easy to distinguish the source domain from the target, to remember which object is the original and which is modeled after its likeness." Hence her epigram — "For centuries we said we were made in God's image, when in truth we made him in ours." The imago dei is thus simultaneously a theology of human dignity and a diagnosis of [[Anthropomorphism]]: the projection of the human onto "the blank vault of heaven."

O'Gieblyn then extends the doctrine forward into AI. In the Network chapter, the human capacity to *create life by manipulating symbols* is read as the imago dei reasserting itself in the laboratory. Drawing on the theologian Anne Foerst and the Kabbalistic golem tradition — in which "humans can manipulate these numbers and letters in order to animate the inanimate" because "they are made in the image of God and endowed with the same creative capacities" — she records the computer scientist Mitchell Marcus's claim that "creating and animating by the manipulation of symbols, which is what computer scientists both do and study, is but one aspect of our being created in God's image." AI becomes "a repetition of God's act of creating us." The engineers stand "as though they were gods making creatures in their image"; Stewart Brand's slogan, "We are gods and might as well get good at it," is the secularized form. This is continuous with [[Transhumanism]], where the imago-as-divinization reading (the *theosis* O'Gieblyn was warned against at Bible school — "to be made in the image of God was not the same as to be gods ourselves") returns as mind-uploading.

The doctrine has a darker double. In the Network chapter O'Gieblyn quotes Annie Dillard's suspicion that "the very notion of imago dei — that humans had some special distinction, a solitary romance with God — that caused us to believe we were distinct from the rest of nature and brought about our alienation." Here human exceptionalism is the *fall*, the source of "dominion" and ecological degradation. Its sharpest inversion appears in the Virality chapter as [[The Human as Error]]: the human recoded as virus, glitch, and "biological bootloader." Against precisely this economic-computational reduction, O'Gieblyn finds that "the most cogent and unqualified argument" for valuing human life over economic logic during the pandemic was Russell Moore's imago dei — that to weigh the economy against public health is to "turn human lives into checkmarks on a page rather than the sacred mystery they are." The image-of-God doctrine is, in O'Gieblyn's secular survey, the one framework that defends human life as priceless against [[The Human as Error]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — treats *imago dei* as "the original anthropological metaphor" and performs its two-way reversal (we made God in our image), then extends it into AI/Kabbalah (creation by symbol-manipulation as the imago extended) and sets it against the secular reduction of the human to error and data. A former fundamentalist, she reads the doctrine both as the lost guarantor of human worth and as the root of human alienation from nature.

## Related Concepts

- [[Anthropomorphism]] — the imago dei's two-way underside: the doctrine read as humanity projecting its own image onto the divine ("we made him in ours").
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the disenchanted heir of the imago's identification of the human essence with reason/consciousness; the computational theory of mind inherits the question "What is a human like?" and answers "Like a computer."
- [[Theodicy]] — the companion theological problem (divine justice, the Ivan/Alyosha debate) O'Gieblyn carries forward into the disenchanted, networked present.
- [[Transhumanism]] — the imago read as *theosis* (divinization), returning as mind-uploading and the engineer "as though they were gods."
- [[Disenchantment]] — the modern condition against which imago dei stands as the abandoned doctrine of intrinsic human worth.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the secular descendant of the imago's exceptional, rational, interior self.
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — the modern form of the interiority (consciousness, "the rational mind") in which the imago was once located, now "impossible to prove."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Human as Error]] — the secular inverse: where imago dei makes human life a "sacred mystery," the computational-economic view recodes the human as virus, glitch, and "biological bootloader." O'Gieblyn frames Moore's imago dei as the strongest defense against exactly this reduction.

## Questions It Raises

- If "metaphors are two-way streets," is the imago dei a doctrine of human dignity or a record of [[Anthropomorphism]] — and can the two be separated once we admit "we made him in ours"? (ties to meaning)
- The body as data: when the human essence is relocated from a divinely conferred soul to a transferable *pattern* of information, what survives of the imago — and does mind-uploading complete the divinization the doctrine promised or void it? (ties to the body as data; posthumanism)
- Technology and perception: if engineers animate machines by "getting strings of symbols just right," are they exercising the imago dei or merely confirming that creation-by-symbol is a [[Mind as Machine]] illusion? (ties to technology and perception)
- Is imago dei the last secure ground for treating human life as priceless against economic and computational logic — Moore's "sacred mystery" versus the $10-million statistical life — and can any wholly secular framework replace it? (ties to meaning)
- Was the imago dei the source of human worth or the source of human alienation from nature, as Dillard suggests — and does its loss free us into ecological humility or abandon us to [[The Human as Error]]? (ties to meaning; posthumanism)
