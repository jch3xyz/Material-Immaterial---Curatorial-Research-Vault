# The Right to Sanctuary

## Brief Definition

The second elemental right of individual sovereignty in Shoshana Zuboff's account: the ancient human need for an inviolable space of refuge — a place where a self can turn inward and be "birthed and nurtured" — now under attack as surveillance capital builds a world of "no exit."

## Longer Explanation

Zuboff introduces the right to sanctuary in Chapter 1 as the companion to [[The Right to the Future Tense]], both of them "elemental rights that bear on individual sovereignty." Where the right to the future tense names a person's authorship of tomorrow, the right to sanctuary names a person's claim to a *space* set apart — "a space of inviolable refuge" whose need "has persisted in civilized societies from ancient times." Its endangerment, on Zuboff's account, is not incidental intrusion but a structural consequence of surveillance capitalism's drive to render all of life as behavioral data: the regime "creates a world of 'no exit,'" closing the door to the very inwardness on which a self depends (Directly stated, Ch.1).

The historical depth of the concept is its argumentative weight. In Chapter 17 — where the right is fully developed (forward-anchor below) — Zuboff traces it back to "the principle of sanctuary," which "has stood as an antidote to power since the beginning of the human story." Even under tyranny "there was an exit from totalizing power, and that exit was the entrance to a sanctuary in the form of a city, a community, or a temple." She grounds the word itself: "The Greek word *asylon* means 'unplunderable' and founds the notion of a sanctuary as an inviolable space." English common law later "translated that to the broader notion of 'home,' a sanctuary free from arbitrary intrusion: unplunderable" (Directly stated, Ch.17). The right to sanctuary is thus the long thread joining the ancient temple of asylum to the modern home and the constitutional "privacies of life."

The threat surveillance capitalism poses is, in Zuboff's framing, an assault on this most ancient citadel: "In the march of institutional interests intent on implementing Big Other, the very first citadel to fall is the most ancient: the principle of sanctuary." The mechanism is partly rhetorical — the disarming demand "What have you got to hide?" — which Zuboff answers with a developmental and psychological claim: the "self-other balance cannot be negotiated adequately without the sanctity of 'disconnected' time and space for the ripening of inward awareness," so that "the real psychological truth is this: If you've got nothing to hide, you are nothing" (Directly stated, Ch.17). Sanctuary is not a hiding place for wrongdoing but the precondition of selfhood; to surrender it is to surrender the inwardness that makes a self possible.

The concept is bound to Zuboff's figure of the hive. Chapter 16 ("Of Life in the Hive") sets up the right by redefining exit as entrance: "Inside the hive, it is easy to forget that every exit is an entrance.… To exit means to enter the place where a self can be birthed and nurtured. History has a name for that kind of place: sanctuary." The hive — the "human simulation of machine learning systems," the tuned social confluence of instrumentarian power — is precisely the world of "no exit" that the right to sanctuary contests. This places the concept on a [[Home vs Exile]] axis: home as sanctuary is the inhabitable refuge, and its loss is a kind of exile from oneself, the condition Zuboff names when she writes that surveillance capitalism "effectively exiles us from our own behavior" (Interpretive synthesis). It also extends the vault's surveillance cluster — Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] of voluntary self-exposure — by insisting on what total visibility destroys: not merely privacy as a transactable good but sanctuary as "the well from which we draw the capacities to promise and to love."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins and develops the right to sanctuary as the second of two elemental rights bearing on individual sovereignty (the first being [[The Right to the Future Tense]]). For Zuboff sanctuary is at once historical (the *asylon*, the temple, the home as "unplunderable" castle), legal (the constitutional "privacies of life"), and psychological (the "disconnected" time and space required for "the ripening of inward awareness"). Its forfeit "for the sake of [surveillance capitalism's] wealth and power" is among the gravest harms the book names.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the paired elemental right; Zuboff names the two together as the rights "that bear on individual sovereignty." Sanctuary is the spatial/inward right; the future tense is the temporal/authorial right.
- [[Surveillance]] — the watching that, taken to the limit of "no exit," eliminates the refuge sanctuary protects; this book realizes the long-committed surveillance forward-stub.
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic order whose imperatives drive the conquest of human nature and the demand for "the forfeit of sanctuary."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Home vs Exile]] — the tension that frames the concept: sanctuary is home as inhabitable refuge, and the world of "no exit" is a paradoxical exile *into* total connection, an exile from the self (forward-stub, realized in later chunks).

## Questions It Raises

- If sanctuary is "unplunderable," what could restore inviolability once the home has been instrumented with always-on, data-extracting devices?
- Does grounding sanctuary in "inward awareness" and a bounded "self-other balance" reassert a liberal-humanist interior self that other authors in the vault (Hayles, Haraway) have placed under question?
- Is Zuboff's "If you've got nothing to hide, you are nothing" a defense of privacy, or a stronger ontological claim that selfhood itself requires concealment?
- How does the hive's redefinition of "every exit is an entrance" change what political resistance to surveillance capitalism would look like — flight to sanctuary rather than reform of the platform?
