# Quantified Self

> [!note] Realized forward-stub
> `Quantified Self` was a long-committed research-focus forward-stub in this vault (a declared placeholder for the surveillance/embodiment cluster). It is realized here, Zuboff-anchored, by the body-rendition material of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*. The note remains **open to other authors** — the "quantified self" as a self-improvement movement (wearables, self-tracking, lifelogging) and its critics span many writers; Zuboff supplies the dispossession-side account, and the optimization-side valence (resonant with Han's `Self-Optimization`) can be extended by later ingests.

## Brief Definition

The rendering of the living body and its behavior — location, biometrics, sleep, gait, voice, emotion — into continuous streams of behavioral data; in Zuboff's account, not an empowering practice of self-knowledge but the extraction of the self as raw material for prediction.

## Longer Explanation

The "quantified self" names the conversion of lived embodiment into measurable, indexable data. In its popular sense it is a movement of voluntary self-tracking — fitness bands, sleep monitors, diet and heart-rate apps — undertaken in the name of self-improvement. Shoshana Zuboff approaches the same phenomenon from the opposite end of the power relation: in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* the quantified self is the substrate of what she calls **rendition**, the concrete operational practices by which human experience is "claimed as raw material for datafication." Where the optimization narrative casts self-tracking as a gift to oneself, Zuboff shows the data flowing outward — to firms whose business is the manufacture of `Behavioral Surplus` into prediction products sold on behavioral futures markets.

Zuboff's treatment is most explicit in the **body-rendition** section of Chapter 8. "Your body is reimagined as a behaving object to be tracked and calculated for indexing and search." Rendition of the body "begins quite simply with your phone" — the device that knows "exactly where you are all the time" — and extends into ever more intimate territories: "organs, blood, eyes, brain waves, faces, gait, posture." Wearables, m-health, and fitness/diabetes apps datafy heartbeat, breathing, and movement; in Chapter 7's telematics the body becomes "simply a set of coordinates in time and space where sensation and action are translated as data," and the quantified body is folded into insurance "behavioral underwriting" where compliance is monitored and enforced in real time. The self-tracking that the optimization culture frames as personal mastery is, in Zuboff's reading, the same operation seen from the standpoint of dispossession.

In Chapter 9 ("Rendition from the Depths") this quantification descends past the surface of the body into the interior: voice, personality, and emotion. **Affective computing** seeks "to render both conscious and unconscious emotion as observable behavior for coding and calculation," so that even "your unconscious — where feelings form before there are words to express them — must be recast as simply one more source of raw-material supply." The quantified self, pursued to its limit, reaches "the dark data continent of your inner life." The deepest layer of the quantified self is therefore not a sport-watch metric but the will and the feeling that precede speech.

What distinguishes Zuboff's account from the self-optimization frame is the asymmetry of knowledge and power. The data are accumulated, in her phrase, "from us, but not for us": "Surveillance capitalists know everything about us, whereas their operations are designed to be unknowable to us." The quantification of the self thus becomes, for Zuboff, a coup against the "right to sanctuary" — the inviolable inward space of meaning-making. It is the body and the self rendered legible to others' ends, and only secondarily, if at all, to one's own.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — the quantified self as **body rendition**: the living body and ultimately the inner self translated into behavioral data for prediction and profit; a practice of dispossession ("from us, but not for us"), not self-empowerment. The phone, wearables, m-health apps, telematics, and affective computing are its instruments; insurance "behavioral underwriting" is its disciplinary edge.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (cross-link, not yet authored here) — resonant via `Self-Optimization` and `Auto-Exploitation`: the achievement-subject who tracks and improves the self voluntarily, exploiting its own freedom. Han stresses the seductive interiority of self-quantification; Zuboff reveals the third-party extraction behind it.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance]] — the quantified self is the body brought within surveillance capitalism's extraction operations; self-tracking is corporate surveillance reframed as self-care.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the broader, cross-author concept of the body rendered as information; the quantified self is its self-directed, everyday instance.
- [[Dataism]] — the worldview that experience can and should be rendered as data; the quantified self is its lived, embodied practice.
- [[Self-Optimization]] (Han) — the self-improvement valence of self-tracking; Zuboff supplies the extraction underside.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] (Han) — the ubiquitous apparatus within which the quantified body is rendered; Zuboff stresses secret, unilateral extraction where Han stresses voluntary self-exposure.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] (Zuboff, forward concept) — the raw material that body and self rendition produce.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Right to Sanctuary]] (Zuboff) — the inviolable inward refuge that total quantification of the self attacks; the quantified self, pushed to the depths, leaves "no exit."

## Questions It Raises

- When does self-tracking for self-knowledge become rendition for others' profit — and is the line ever visible to the person being quantified?
- Can the body be quantified "for us" rather than "from us," or does the prediction imperative always pull self-tracking data outward?
- If even the unconscious — feeling before words — can be rendered as behavioral data, what remains of an inward self that is not quantified?
- Is the quantified self a practice of freedom (Han's self-optimizing subject) or of dispossession (Zuboff's rendered body), and can it be both at once?
