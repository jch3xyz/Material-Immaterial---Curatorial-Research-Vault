# The Sculpture Base

## Brief Definition

The base (with its sub-elements the pedestal and plinth) is the sculptor's convention for rooting an artwork to surrounding reality while letting it stand apart — a physical-and-psychic "twilight zone" that confers presence, an aura of distance and dignity, and transcendence, and that simultaneously serves as a biological/orientational device reaffirming mammalian anatomy and man's gravity-bound mobility.

## Longer Explanation

In the keystone first chapter of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Jack Burnham treats the base not as a neglected craft detail but as the load-bearing convention of the whole representational tradition — the device that makes a worked object legible as sculpture. Its function is double. Physically it supports top-heavy standing figures and provides a perch to minimize damage. But "beyond that, the base helps to create an aura of distance and dignity around the favored object." The base "is the sculptor's convention for rooting his art to surrounding reality while permitting it to stand apart"; in so doing it "creates a twilight zone both physically and psychically. It says, in effect, that this sculpted object has a life, a 'presence' of its own." The base is thus the mechanism of what Burnham later in the chapter calls the "transcendent qualities of sculpture" and the "aesthetic distance which necessarily remained between the viewer and art object." (`Directly stated`)

Burnham fixes the vocabulary precisely. The substructure's "Nomenclature ... is ill-defined," but he supplies working definitions: "the base, the greatest mass upon which a sculpture rests, it refers to the support as a whole; the pedestal, a shaftlike form which elevates the sculpture; the plinth, a flat, planar support which separates the sculpture from the ground or from a pedestal." Conventionally taught to be unobtrusive, bases "tended to be strong geometric shapes with an architectonic simplicity separating them from the organic activity above" — Burnham notes that, historically, "architecture was considered the mother of sculpture." (`Directly stated`)

The chapter's most original move is to read the base biologically. Beyond circumscribing a "fixed situation" — "Traditional sculpture is virtually life that cannot move" — the base, Burnham writes, "becomes an orientational device; it is a tacit acknowledgment that all mammals have a top and bottom or head and feet, plus a ventral and dorsal side. The base is a biological reaffirmation that man is constructed to walk the ground, to gain his mobility through successive contacts with the earth." The base is therefore an anthropomorphic claim: it grounds the object in the same gravity-bound, oriented anatomy as its human viewer, which is precisely why a non-idealistic, mobility-seeking modern sculpture would find "the base had lost its reason for being." (`Directly stated`)

This concept is the positive term whose negation Burnham tracks across the rest of the chapter. Because the base is what confers presence, distance, and orientation, its "destruction or withering away ... is very much a part of the 'secularization' of sculpture": as the modern object sheds "all the old reverberations and implications of the 'art object,'" the base is either absorbed into the sculpture (floor-bound work) or dissolved entirely (gravity-defying, air-borne, and ultimately the magnetically levitated piece where "the base has vanished altogether!"). See the companion note `The Vanishing Base` for that countermovement. (`Strongly implied`)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Vanishing Base]] — direct opposition by negation: the base is the convention that the modern "secularization" of sculpture progressively renders meaningless, until in Burnham's closing example "the base has vanished altogether!"
- [[Dematerialization]] — Lippard's later dematerialization of the art object can be read as a downstream consequence of removing the very support (base/presence/aura) that marked an object as fine art; the withering of the base loosens the object from its protocol. (`Speculative connection`)

## Questions It Raises

- If the base is what confers presence and "an aura of distance and dignity," does sculpture without a base lose its aura, or merely relocate it (to the floor, the wall, the room, the field of force)?
- Burnham roots the base in mammalian anatomy and gravity. What happens to that biological reaffirmation when sculpture becomes "gravity defying," air-borne, or magnetically levitated — does the object cease to be anthropomorphic?
- Is the base a means of "rooting art to surrounding reality," or a means of cordoning art off from it? Burnham insists it is both at once — how stable is that "twilight zone"?
- If a base can become sculpture (Brancusi) and a sculpture can become as inert as a base (the Minimalists), is "base" a property of objects or only of relations between them?
