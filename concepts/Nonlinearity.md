# Nonlinearity

## Brief Definition

A relationship in which the cause does not produce a proportional effect — one that can be drawn only with curves, not a straight line — and which, because it can change the relative strengths of a system's [[Feedback Loops]], can flip the whole system from one mode of behavior to another.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Donella Meadows]], nonlinearity is defined against the linear relationship: a *linear* relationship "can be drawn on a graph with a straight line. It's a relationship with constant proportions" — ten pounds of fertilizer add two bushels, twenty add four, thirty add six. A *nonlinear* relationship breaks that constancy: "the cause does not produce a proportional effect. The relationship between cause and effect can only be drawn with curves or wiggles, not with a straight line" (Directly stated). Because our minds are "linear-thinking," we assume that if a small push produces a small response, twice the push produces twice the response — but "in a nonlinear system, twice the push could produce one-sixth the response, or the response squared, or no response at all" (Directly stated). This is why nonlinearities are, for Meadows, one of the central reasons [[Systems Theory|systems surprise us]].

Meadows' key move is to insist that nonlinearity is *more than* a violation of proportional intuition. Her chapter frames it first as something that "confound[s] our expectations about the relationship between action and response," and then escalates: "They are even more important because they change the relative strengths of feedback loops. They can flip a system from one mode of behavior to another" (Directly stated). A nonlinear response means the gain on a loop is not fixed; as a stock crosses some threshold, a loop that was negligible can suddenly become powerful, or a dominant loop can weaken. The relationship between action and response is itself the lever that re-weights the system's internal control structure.

This makes nonlinearity the *mechanism* beneath [[Shifting Dominance]]. Meadows names it directly as "the chief cause of the shifting dominance that characterizes several of the systems in the zoo — the sudden swing between exponential growth caused by a dominant reinforcing loop, say, and then decline caused by a suddenly dominant balancing loop" (Directly stated). A reinforcing loop drives runaway growth until a nonlinearity (a resource limit, a saturation, a damaged soil) strengthens a balancing loop past the point where it takes over and reverses the trajectory. The system does not fail gracefully in proportion to the push; it holds, then tips. Her worked examples — highway traffic where speed is barely affected across a wide range of density and then "drops to zero," soil erosion that has "much affect on crop yield" only "until the topsoil is worn down to the depth of the root zone," and advertising that "awaken[s] interest" in small doses but "cause[s] disgust" in large ones — are all cases where the same action reverses sign or collapses once a threshold is passed (Directly stated).

Nonlinearity in Meadows' sense should be kept **distinct** from the [[Exponential vs Linear]] tension that runs through [[Ray Kurzweil]]. Kurzweil's contrast is about *growth rates* — the failure of linear intuition to grasp accelerating (exponential) returns. Meadows' concern is *proportionality and loop-dominance* — a relationship's response can be sub-linear, super-linear, saturating, sign-reversing, or threshold-triggered, and its significance lies in how it re-weights feedback rather than in any single accelerating trend. (An exponential curve is itself one nonlinear shape, so the two overlap; but the analytic work each does is different, so they are cross-linked, not merged.) (Interpretive synthesis.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*[[Thinking in Systems]]*) — nonlinearity is a relationship in which cause and effect are not proportional, drawable only with curves. Its systemic importance is that it changes the relative strengths of [[Feedback Loops]] and so is the chief cause of [[Shifting Dominance]] — the mechanism by which a system flips between behavior modes.
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — treats nonlinearity primarily through the [[Exponential vs Linear]] lens (the Law of Accelerating Returns): linear intuition fails to anticipate *exponential growth*. Related but distinct emphasis — accelerating-returns growth rather than loop-dominance and proportionality. Cross-linked, not merged.

## Related Concepts

- [[Shifting Dominance]] — the behavior nonlinearity produces: because nonlinear relationships re-weight loops, a formerly negligible loop can take over and reverse a system's trajectory. Nonlinearity is the mechanism; shifting dominance is the effect.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — nonlinearity's deepest work is on loops: it "change[s] the relative strengths of feedback loops," making loop gains state-dependent rather than fixed.
- [[Systems Theory]] — nonlinearity is one of Meadows' named reasons that systems surprise our linear-thinking minds.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Exponential vs Linear]] — a Kurzweil tension about *growth rates* (accelerating returns vs constant-slope intuition), kept distinct from Meadows' concern with *proportionality and loop-dominance*. Not an opposition so much as a neighboring, non-identical framing of "the world is not linear" — cross-linked to prevent conflation.

## Questions It Raises

- If nonlinearity is what re-weights feedback loops, is *every* case of shifting dominance ultimately traceable to a nonlinear relationship, or can dominance shift for other reasons (e.g., a change in an external inflow)?
- Where in a system are the nonlinearities located — in physical limits, in perceptions and decisions, or in the loop structure itself — and does that location change how one intervenes?
- Meadows treats linear intuition as a cognitive default. Is nonlinearity better understood as a property of the world, a property of our models, or an artifact of the boundaries we draw around a system?
