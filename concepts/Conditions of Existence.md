# Conditions of Existence

## Brief Definition

Zuboff's Durkheimian causal category: the perennial human quest to live effectively in our "conditions of existence" is the invisible power that summons the division of labor, technology, work organization, capitalism, and ultimately civilization — so capitalism is called forth by the needs of people in a time and place, not by the drive to produce more.

## Longer Explanation

"Conditions of existence" is the methodological keystone of Zuboff's opening account of *how* market forms arise, and it functions throughout *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* as a touchstone for judging whether a given capitalism is healthy or aberrant. She borrows the phrase from Émile Durkheim, who argued that the great upheavals of industrialization — factories, specialization, the complex division of labor — could be *described* by economists but not *grasped*, because economists are systematically blind to the social facts that cause them. For Durkheim the division of labor exists not "to produce more" but "to enable us to live in the new conditions of existence that have been made for us." Zuboff elevates this into a general law of capitalist development: market forms are summoned by the changing needs of people struggling to live effective lives, and "the rationality of capitalism reflects this alignment, however imperfect, with the needs that people experience." `Directly stated`

The payoff is a normative criterion. If capitalism is *summoned* by people's conditions of existence, then "new market forms are most productive when they are shaped by an allegiance to the actual demands and mentalities of people." `Directly stated` This is why a market form that serves its populations — Ford's mass consumers, Sloan's General Motors — develops "institutionalized reciprocities" with the social order: durable employment, oversight, rights for workers and consumers. The criterion is reversible: a market form that ceases to depend on, and answer to, the people whose needs called it forth is, by Zuboff's measure, a pathological mutation rather than a fulfillment.

Zuboff anchors the concept in two phases of modern individualization. Ford's mass consumers were the "first modernity"; the new "conditions of existence" of the **second modernity** produced a different kind of individual — self-determining, demanding "my life, my way, at a price I can afford" — for whom the digital innovations of the [[The Apple Inversion|Apple inversion]] became essential. The same conditions that summoned Apple, she notes in "an unexpected twist," also "helped to enable the surveillance capitalism that would follow." `Directly stated` The second modernity is "a hard place to live": its instability is partly the inevitable cost of self-creation and partly the deliberate work of the neoliberal paradigm, which thwarts the very claims to self-determination it released. This is the "existential contradiction" — "we want to exercise control over our own lives, but everywhere that control is thwarted." `Directly stated`

The concept is the standard by which Zuboff judges the Apple inversion **a transition failure**. The inversion "implied trustworthy relationships of advocacy and reciprocity embedded in an alignment of commercial operations with consumers' genuine interests" — an intimation of a "third-modernity capitalism summoned by the self-determining aspirations of individuals." But "the potential significance of Apple's tacit new logic was never fully grasped, even by the company itself," and Apple "never did seize the reins on developing the consistent, comprehensive social and institutional processes that would have elevated the iPod's promise to an explicit market form, as Henry Ford and Alfred Sloan had once done." `Directly stated` She poses the diagnostic question directly: "Was the Apple inversion a powerful new economic mutation running the gauntlet of trial and error on its way to fulfilling the needs of a new age, or was it a case of transition failure?" `Directly stated` Because Apple failed to institutionalize reciprocity with the conditions of existence that summoned it, the digital sphere was left open to the older forces of capital — and surveillance capitalism filled the vacuum. By this lens, "Mutations fundamentally change the nature of capitalism by shifting it in the direction of those it is supposed to serve"; surveillance capitalism, which does not, is the failed transition's bitter fruit. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Related Concepts

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Growth Imperative]] — the economist's premise (greater production) that Durkheim and Zuboff reject as the *consequence*, not the cause, of capitalist development; conditions of existence is the explicit refusal of "we specialize... to produce more"

## Questions It Raises

- If a market form must be held in "allegiance to the actual demands and mentalities of people," what institutional mechanisms — beyond Apple's failed example — could bind digital capitalism back to the conditions of existence that summon it?
- Is surveillance capitalism a failed transition that can still be redirected toward a "third modernity," or a stable mutation that has decoupled itself from human conditions of existence entirely?
- Durkheim's causal claim is normatively loaded: does identifying human need as capitalism's "invisible causal power" risk explaining away the violence and inequality the same market forms produce?
- How does Zuboff's "conditions of existence" relate to the disciplinary/control diagnoses of [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[Dataism]] — is her quarrel with surveillance capitalism's *economic logic* compatible with their quarrel with its *form of power*?
