# The Artist as Designer of Context

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's redefinition of the artistic role: rather than creating, expressing, or transmitting *content*, the artist designs *context* — the framework, system, or environment (the goal a "hypercontext") within which an observer constructs experience and meaning for themselves.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, the durable creative act in a telematic, interactive culture is the design of context, not content. If reality and meaning are constructed by the participant — Ascott's constructivist premise, drawn from observer-created reality and radical constructivism — then a finished, content-bearing object can no longer be the locus of art. What the artist makes instead is a context: an open-ended, cybernetic system "responsive in important ways to the behaviour of its user," which the observer enters and completes. The artist's skill, "insight, sensibility, feeling, and intelligence" is now spent designing such contexts, a demand Ascott insists is "no less than that demanded of the artist in classical, orthodox art" — but the outcome is radically different (Directly stated).

This relocates the observer from periphery to centre: "The observer of art is now at the centre of the creative process, not at the periphery looking in." The corresponding figure is the shift from window to doorway — see [[The Window vs the Doorway]] — art "no longer a window onto the world but a doorway through which the observer is invited to enter into a world of interaction and transformation" (Directly stated). The context is something one inhabits and acts within, not something one looks at; the interface itself becomes the medium of that entry (see [[The Interface as Doorway]]).

Ascott pushes the principle to a near-identity of context with the work: "Interface is all context, just as content is all interactivity between the viewer and the interface-as-art." On this account the only "content" left is the live interactivity between viewer and interface, so what the artist authors is precisely the conditions of that interactivity — the context. He coins the term **hypercontext** for the goal, and folds it into a wider cosmology: "Just as the artist is concerned no longer with the creation of content but with the construction of a context (the goal is a hypercontext), so, too, the brain seeks its hypercontext in the hyperbody" (Directly stated). The same rule governs his vision of education-as-interface: "the telematic rule that context must supersede content applies."

Because the artwork has become an open-ended system whose meaning emerges through participation, the artist-as-context-designer is the participatory pole of cybernetic art (see [[Reciprocal Communication]], [[Art as Experiential Technology]], [[New Media Art]]). It is the constructive correlate of Ascott's "watchwords" — connectivity, interaction, and emergence — and the practical job description of the [[Telematic Art|telematic]] artist (Interpretive synthesis).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the radically new artistic role in the cyberculture: the artist designs contexts (a "hypercontext") within which the [[The Observer|observer]] constructs experience and meaning, since meaning and reality are constructed by the participant. The figure is the doorway, not the window; the rule is that "context must supersede content."
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — resonant precursor (not Ascott's term). McLuhan's medium/content split — that the message is the form of the medium, not its content, and that fixation on content is "the numb stance of the technological idiot" (see [[The Content Obsession]]) — anticipates Ascott's elevation of context over content; both relocate art's significance away from transmitted content toward the structuring frame (Interpretive synthesis).

## Related Concepts

- [[The Window vs the Doorway]] — the governing metaphor: appearance-art is a window looked through from outside; the context-as-doorway is entered and completed by the observer.
- [[The Interface as Doorway]] — the interface as the threshold the observer crosses into the work; "interface is all context."
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the context is built to be experienced and entered, not viewed; the observer constructs experience inside it.
- [[New Media Art]] — interactive, networked, systems-based art is precisely the practice of designing contexts rather than objects.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the two-way, behaviour-coupled responsiveness that a designed context must afford.
- [[The Observer]] — moved to the centre of the creative process; the constructor of meaning the context is designed to enable.
- [[Telematic Art]] — the medium in which Ascott's context-design operates.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Content Obsession]] (McLuhan) — the fixation on content that blinds us to the medium/form; the inverse of Ascott's principle that "context must supersede content."

## Questions It Raises

- If meaning is constructed by the participant, in what sense does authorship remain with the artist — does designing the context still constitute an authorial act, or only a delegation of one?
- Does "context must supersede content" abolish content, or merely demote it to the interactivity the context enables?
- How is a designed context evaluated or curated, if its "content" is whatever observers construct inside it?
- Where does the context end — at the interface, the network, the [[Telematic Art|telematic]] system, or (in Ascott's "hypercontext"/"hyperbody") the body and brain themselves?
