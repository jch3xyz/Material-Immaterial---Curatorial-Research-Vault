# The Interface as Doorway

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's reconception of the computer interface: not a membrane that seals off the computer as a discrete object from the self, but a *doorway* into data space — a "synaptic interval" in a human-computer symbiosis — and, in his mature account, the very site that displaces the *objet d'art* as the locus of the aesthetic.

## Longer Explanation

The "interface as doorway" is Ascott's polemic against thinking of the computer as a *thing*. In "Gesamtdatenwerk" (chapter 18) he insists that "the computer is not primarily a thing, but a set of behaviours," an "agent of the data field, a construct of data space," whose purpose is "not only computation but transformation; not only storage but dissemination." On this view the interface cannot be a boundary between two objects — user here, machine there — because there is no machine-object to be bounded. Ascott's governing instruction follows directly: "Rather than seeing the computer interface as a membrane separating out the computer as a discrete object from ourselves, we should see it and design it as a doorway into data space, a synaptic interval in a human-computer symbiosis." The figure does double duty: a *doorway* one passes through (not a *screen* one looks at), and a *synaptic interval* — the gap across which two parts of one nervous system communicate, making interface a junction *within* a coupled human-computer organism rather than a wall between separate parties. *(Directly stated — Ascott, ch. 18.)*

The doorway is set against a recurring antagonist: the **window**. Ascott rejects the interface as "screen presenting the pages of an illuminated book, or as an internally lit painting" — the "posture of a clerk," an "art of the 'bottom line,'" of "finalities, of completion." The window frames a composed, finished scene to be received; the doorway opens onto an unfinished field to be entered and altered. This is why the interface, properly designed, must preserve "the essential qualities of open-endedness, nonlinearity, and fecund incompleteness." Crucially the interface is also *plural and continuous*: "The computer interface is, in each individual case, an aspect of a holographic unity. To be in or at any one interface is to be with all the interfaces throughout the network of which it is a part." Each doorway is the whole network in miniature, which folds this concept into [[Network Consciousness]] and the [[The Telematic Embrace|telematic embrace]]. *(Directly stated — Ascott, ch. 18.)*

In "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" (chapter 20) the figure makes its most radical move: the interface does not merely *mediate* the artwork — it *replaces* it. "The culturally dominant objet d'art as the sole focus (the uncommon carrier of uncommon content) is replaced by the interface." And: "Instead of the artwork as a window onto a composed, resolved, and ordered reality, we have at the interface a doorway to undecidability, a data space of semantic and material potentiality." The locus of the aesthetic shifts "from the observed object to participating subject," from "observed systems" to the "(second-order) cybernetics of observing systems" — "the canon of the immaterial and participatory." At this interface "content is created rather than received," then "disposed of... by reinserting it, transformed... back into the network." This is the conceptual hinge by which Ascott's interface absorbs and supersedes the art object — the [[New Media Art|new-media]] continuation of Burnham's [[Object vs System|object→system]] turn and Lippard's [[Dematerialization|dematerialization]]. *(Directly stated — Ascott, ch. 20.)*

The same window-vs-doorway figure recurs in "Photography at the Interface" (chapter 21), now aimed at the analogue/digital divide: "The window onto a world of analogue actualities gives way to the doorway into a world of digital potentialities." Photography's classical claim — a transparent window onto what *was* — yields to an interface onto what *could be*, emphasizing "the behaviour of the viewing subject, her inter-action with the apparatus and the image." Across all three chapters the doorway names the passage from *actuality* (fixed, resolved, received, material) to *potentiality* (open, undecidable, participatory, immaterial). This makes "The Interface as Doorway" the default conceptual target for the interface material in this vault; any generic "interface" reference in the Ascott corpus should resolve here and be qualified as Ascott's. *(Directly stated — Ascott, ch. 21; the cross-chapter synthesis is `Interpretive synthesis`.)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** (*Telematic Embrace*, chs. 18, 20, 21) — the interface is a doorway / synaptic interval into data space, not a membrane around a computer-object (ch. 18); it *replaces* the objet d'art as the locus of the aesthetic, opening "a doorway to undecidability, a data space of semantic and material potentiality" (ch. 20); and it converts photography's "window onto... analogue actualities" into "the doorway into a world of digital potentialities" (ch. 21). The window-vs-doorway antithesis is Ascott's signature figure for the passage from received object to participatory system. This is the primary-text anchor.

## Opposing Concepts

- **The window** (Ascott's own antagonist figure) — the interface as screen/painting/illuminated book framing a "composed, resolved, and ordered reality" to be received; "the posture of a clerk," "an art of finalities." The doorway is its negation. *(Directly stated — Ascott, chs. 18, 20, 21; not given a standalone note here.)*
- [[The Readymade]] — the unique, framed art *object* (the "uncommon carrier of uncommon content") whose privileged status the interface explicitly replaces. *(Interpretive synthesis — Ascott names the displaced "objet d'art," not the readymade specifically.)*

## Questions It Raises

- If the interface *replaces* the art object, what is left to collect, conserve, or contemplate — and does an aesthetics with no object still need an interface, or has the doorway itself become the new fetish?
- Is "doorway vs. window" a real ontological difference or a rhetorical one? Every screen is also a frame; does Ascott's design imperative actually escape the window, or merely redecorate it?
- What does it mean for an interface to be a "synaptic interval" — does the human-computer symbiosis require that the self genuinely extend across the threshold, and at what point does the doorway become indistinguishable from McLuhan's extension or Han's [[Digital Panopticon|panoptic exposure]]?
- If "to be in or at any one interface is to be with all the interfaces," does the doorway preserve the singular subject at all, or dissolve it into the holographic network?
- When content is "created rather than received" at the interface, who authors the work — and can "undecidability" be a stable basis for value?
