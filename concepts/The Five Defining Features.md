# The Five Defining Features

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's named cluster of five features — **connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, emergence** — that he claims are intrinsic to networked/post-biological art and "so conspicuously differentiate it from the art of earlier eras"; always presented as a set, never singly.

## Longer Explanation

In his 1994 essay "The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-Biological Era," Ascott pauses to "remind you of the five defining features of our art." He has just argued that telematic art is "bottom-up, distributed, local determination of behavior" — the same phrase he borrows from Chris Langton's definition of [[Artificial Life]] — and the five features are presented as the qualities "intrinsic to this bottom-up process." They are not five independent attributes so much as five aspects of one condition: an art that has migrated from the made object to the participatory, emergent system. Ascott glosses each in a single telegraphic line: **connectivity** ("of part to part, person to person, mind to mind"), **immersion** ("into the whole, and the dissolution thereby of subject and ground"), **interaction** ("as the very form of art"), **transformation** ("perpetual flux of image, surface, and identity"), and **emergence** ("the perpetual coming into being of meaning, matter, and mind").

The cluster's load-bearing term is **interaction**, because it carries Ascott's keystone reformulation. Interaction is "the very form of art, such that art as the behaviour of forms has become art as a form of behaviour" — the inversion that moves the locus of the artwork from the formal arrangement of an object to the participant's conduct within a system. The viewer no longer reads a finished content; "the artist now creates contexts and systems, within which the viewer is instrumental in manipulating his own universe of experience and insight." **Connectivity** locates this behaviour in a network — the "mind-to-mind collaboration through computer-mediated telecommunications systems" Ascott calls telematic art. **Immersion** describes the participant's absorption into that whole and the consequent "dissolution… of subject and ground" — the collapse of the spectator-distance that representational art depended on. **Transformation** names the resulting instability of the work (and of identity), and **emergence** names the upshot: meaning, matter, and mind continually coming into being rather than being delivered ready-made (`Directly stated`).

The fifth feature, **emergence**, already carries its own cross-author note in the vault — it is the keyword of Hayles's third wave of cybernetics, O'Gieblyn's "uncomfortably like magic," Kurzweil's bounded engineering, and Burnham's object-to-system migration (see [[Emergence]]). Ascott's use of it is continuous with that cluster but bent toward art and consciousness: where Hayles locates emergence in [[Artificial Life]] software, Ascott transplants Langton's bottom-up principle wholesale onto networked art, declaring that "the defining metaphor in art practice of electronic flow—which in recent years has replaced the aesthetic of the material object—is now giving way to the metaphor of emergence." This note holds the *cluster*; the standalone [[Emergence]] note holds the fifth feature in its full cross-author treatment, and the two are cross-linked rather than merged (`Interpretive synthesis`).

The five features are best read as Ascott's participatory, behaviourist counter-pole to Jack Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]]. Both writers narrate the same historical movement away from the discrete art object and toward the system (see [[Object and System]], and the [[Material vs Immaterial]] spine that Ascott extends from "the aesthetic of the material object" through "electronic flow" to "the metaphor of emergence"). But where Burnham's systems esthetics still describes the *work* as a system of forces and information, Ascott's five features describe the *participant's behaviour* within a connective network — "art as a form of behaviour." The cluster is therefore the concrete content of Ascott's [[Art as Experiential Technology]] and the maturation of [[New Media Art]] into telematic, network-distributed practice (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and always deploys the five features as a single set (connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, emergence), the differentiating signature of telematic/post-biological art; the interaction feature carries his "art as a form of behaviour" reformulation, and the emergence feature ties the cluster to [[Artificial Life]]'s bottom-up principle.

## Related Concepts

- [[Emergence]] — the fifth feature; already a cross-author note (Hayles, O'Gieblyn, Kurzweil, Burnham, and now Ascott). This cluster holds the set; the standalone note holds the fully developed fifth term. Cross-linked, not merged.

- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — connectivity and interaction realize "mind-to-mind collaboration"; two-way communication as the basis of the work.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — connectivity and emergence presuppose "the artist or author is a complex and often widely distributed system," human and artificial cognition combined.

- [[The Global Village]] — connectivity "person to person, mind to mind" extends McLuhan's global village into a planetary network consciousness.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's object-to-system thesis is the systems pole; Ascott's five features are the behavioural/participatory counter-pole. "Art as the behaviour of forms" (Burnham's system as a configuration of forces/information) versus "art as a form of behaviour" (Ascott's participant conduct within a connective network).
- [[Object and System]] — the five features describe what art becomes once it has left the object behind; they oppose the object pole of that distinction.

## Questions It Raises

- Are the five features genuinely irreducible to one another, or is "interaction as the very form of art" the master term from which connectivity, immersion, transformation, and emergence all follow?
- "Immersion… the dissolution thereby of subject and ground" promises liberation from spectator-distance — but is the dissolved, networked subject Ascott celebrates the same subject Han diagnoses as exposed and exploited in the digital network? (cf. [[Distributed Cognition]], and Han's [[Digital Panopticon]].)
- If emergence means meaning is "perpetually coming into being" rather than authored, what remains of the artist's intention, and how does one evaluate a work whose content is never finished? (The same authorship problem the [[Emergence]] note raises across authors.)
- Does the cluster describe a real rupture from "the art of earlier eras," or does it re-describe long-standing participatory and process traditions in the vocabulary of cybernetics and [[Artificial Life]]?
