# Embodiment

## Brief Definition

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], embodiment is the "specific instantiation generated from the noise of difference" — the local, particular, performative enactment of human and informational existence that is always "instantiated, local, and specific," and that the disembodiment of information erases.

## Longer Explanation

Embodiment is the affirmative center of *How We Became Posthuman*, and Hayles is precise about how it differs from "the body." The body, in her usage, is a normative abstraction: "an idealized form that gestures toward a Platonic reality." Embodiment, by contrast, is what actually exists — "contextual, enmeshed within the specifics of place, time, physiology, and culture, which together compose enactment." Where the body is normalized and singular, embodiment is plural, excessive, and deficient at once: "Relative to the body, embodiment is other and elsewhere, at once excessive and deficient in its infinite variations, particularities, and abnormalities." She aligns her usage with Elizabeth Grosz's claim that "there is no body as such; there are only bodies." The move from the body to embodiment is thus the move from the Platonic to the instantiated — the same move, in reverse, that the disembodiment of information performs and that Hayles wants to resist. This is the positive term against which [[The Body as Data]] is the rejected reduction, and is distinct from [[Embodiment vs Information]], which names the axis of tension rather than the affirmed pole.

Crucially, for Hayles embodiment is not opposed to thought but is its ground. "Embodiment makes clear that thought is a much broader cognitive function depending for its specificities on the embodied form enacting it." Against the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] fantasy that consciousness is a pattern portable across substrates, she insists that "human being is first of all embodied being, and the complexities of this embodiment mean that human awareness unfolds in ways very different from those of intelligence embodied in cybernetic machines." Embodiment is where thought happens, in a way that is irreducibly specific to the form enacting it; "incorporated knowledge" gained through habitual bodily performance "has the power to define the boundaries within which conscious thought takes place." This is why she treats embodiment, not disembodied information, as the foundation that the liberal humanist and cybernetic traditions alike work to erase.

To analyze embodiment Hayles builds a framework of two interacting polarities: the body versus embodiment, paired with inscription versus incorporation. Inscription, "like the body," is "normalized and abstract" — a system of signs that travels unchanged across media. Incorporation cannot be abstracted from its enactment: "An incorporating practice such as a good-bye wave cannot be separated from its embodied medium, for it exists as such only when it is instantiated in a particular hand making a particular kind of gesture." The homology she draws — "as the body is to embodiment, so inscription is to incorporation" — links the materiality of the body to the materiality of the sign, and lets embodiment mediate between technology and discourse. Embodiment is "formed by technology at the same time that it creates technology," sitting at the [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] "between materiality and discourse."

This grounding carries the book's ethical weight. Because embodiment is a specific material form rather than a transferable pattern, it has the property information's champions deny it: irreversibility. "Information, like humanity, cannot exist apart from the embodiment that brings it into being as a material entity in the world; and embodiment is always instantiated, local, and specific. Embodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated." Once "the specific form constituting it is gone, no amount of massaging data will bring it back" — a claim Hayles makes "as true of the planet as it is of an individual life-form." Embodiment is finite and singular; this is precisely what the [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy, which makes information "more essential, more important, and more fundamental than materiality," obscures.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — embodiment as the instantiated, local, specific, performative particular, distinguished from "the body" as a normalized Platonic abstraction; the ground of thought and the irreplaceable material form that the disembodiment of information erases. The affirmative term in her critique of [[Posthumanism]].
- **Elizabeth Grosz** — Hayles aligns her account with Grosz's *Volatile Bodies* and the maxim "there is no body as such; there are only bodies," and adapts (while criticizing the limits of) Grosz's Möbius-strip model of body and embodiment.
- **Maurice Merleau-Ponty and Paul Connerton** — the source of Hayles's distinction between inscribing and incorporating practices, and of the idea that habit is "a knowledge and a remembering in the hands and in the body."

## Related Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the axis of tension; embodiment is the affirmed pole on it, not the axis itself.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the broader hierarchy embodiment resists; embodiment is the material instantiation that the ideology of immateriality purges.
- [[Posthumanism]] — embodiment is the ground Hayles reasserts against the posthuman privileging of "informational pattern over material instantiation."
- [[Feedback Loops]] — embodiment mediates between technology and discourse within feedback loops "between materiality and discourse."
- [[Cybernetics]] — the tradition that, having decided "information had lost its body," "systematically downplayed or erased" embodiment.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Body as Data]] — Gibson's "data made flesh," the reduction of the body to portable informational pattern; the rejected reduction to which embodiment is the answer.
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the body imagined as "the original prosthesis," extensible and replaceable; embodiment's specificity and irreplaceability oppose this.
- **The body** (as normative Platonic abstraction) — within Hayles's own usage, "the body" is the idealized form embodiment is set against; "embodiment never coincides exactly with 'the body.'"

## Questions It Raises

- If embodiment "can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated," what does it mean to treat the body as data, as in [[The Body as Data]]? Does the reduction silently presuppose the very replicability that embodiment denies?
- If thought "depend[s] for its specificities on the embodied form enacting it," can intelligence "embodied in cybernetic machines" think in the same sense humans do — and what does this do to the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] dream of substrate independence?
- Where is the line between the body (normalized, Platonic) and embodiment (instantiated, specific) drawn, and who draws it? Is the move from one to the other — as in PET imaging that converts "embodiment into a body" — itself a technology of power?
- If embodiment is the pole Hayles affirms, is [[Embodiment vs Information]] a genuine opposition or a false binary that her notion of "embodied virtuality" already begins to dissolve?
- How does embodiment, as the ground of finitude, reframe the stakes of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] hierarchy for a curatorial practice concerned with what survives, what is copied, and what cannot be?
