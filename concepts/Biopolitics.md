# Biopolitics

## Brief Definition

Biopolitics is the governmental technology of disciplinary power — "a politics of the body in the fullest sense" that administers populations through demography, health, and the somatic — which Han bounds strictly to disciplinary society and declares "altogether unsuited to the neoliberal regime, which exploits the psyche above all."

## Longer Explanation

Biopolitics enters *Psychopolitics* not on its own terms but as the negative foil against which Han defines his title concept. Originating with [[Michel Foucault]], the term names the historical mutation by which power, "since the seventeenth century," ceased to operate as the sovereign's "might of the sword" — the "'privilege to seize hold of life in order to suppress it'" — and became instead a "power over life" whose function is "no longer to kill but to 'invest life through and through'." Biopolitics is the moment when disciplinary power "discovered 'population' as a productive and reproductive mass to be administrated carefully": its objects of regulation are "reproductive cycles, birth and death rates, levels of general health, and life expectancy." Han states the equation flatly: "Biopolitics is the governmental technology of disciplinary power." (`Directly stated` — Ch.4)

Crucially, biopolitics is a politics of *bodies*, not psyches. Following Foucault, Han ties it to the disciplinary fitting of the body to industrial production — the "careful 'administration of bodies'," the "'concerted orthopaedy'" that fashions "'shapeless dough'" into a "'machine'." Quoting Foucault's "The Birth of Social Medicine," Han fixes its remit: "biopolitics fundamentally concerns the biological and the physical. All in all, it constitutes a politics of the body in the fullest sense." This corporeal restriction is exactly its limit. The "orthopaedic technology of disciplinary power is too crude to penetrate into the deeper layers of the soul"; "demography is not the same thing as psych-ography," and "statistics and Big Data lie worlds apart." Biopolitics "makes use of population statistics" and so "has no access to the psychic realm" — it "can deliver no material for drawing up a psychogram of the population." (`Directly stated` — Ch.4, Ch.5)

Han routes the concept through Deleuze's bestiary to mark the epochal break. "According to Deleuze, the disciplinary regime organizes itself as a 'body'. It is a biopolitical regime"; the neoliberal regime, "in contrast, seems like a 'soul'." The animal of biopolitical, disciplinary society is the mole — "a labourer," "a subjugated subject" — superseded by the snake, "the animal of the neoliberal regime." Because neoliberalism turns to "immaterial and non-physical forms of production" in which "the body no longer represents a central force of production," biopolitics is rendered obsolete: it "proves altogether unsuited to the neoliberal regime, which exploits the psyche above all." This is the hinge of Han's whole argument — biopolitics is the foil that defines [[Psychopolitics]] by everything it cannot do. (`Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis` — Ch.3, Ch.4)

This framing also drives Han's critique of Foucault himself. Foucault, turning to neoliberal government in the late 1970s, "stuck to the concepts of population and biopolitics" and so "failed to do what the circumstances actually called for: to make the turn to psychopolitics." Against Agamben's speculation that "Foucault's death kept him from showing how he would have developed the concept and study of biopolitics," Han argues the opposite — early death "deprived him of the chance to reevaluate his idea of biopolitics — and abandon it." Citing Stiegler, Han adds that "Foucault's biopower... is no longer the force behind our age." (`Directly stated` — Ch.5)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Michel Foucault]]** — origin of the concept (the "biopolitics of the population"), as the regime of "power over life" that "invests life through and through" and administers the body and population through discipline, demography, and the "calculated management of life." *Forward-stub:* Foucault has no book in the library yet; his usage here is mediated entirely through Han's quotation and paraphrase. This note is kept open for a future Foucault author-section. (`Directly stated`, via Han's citations)
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — adopts biopolitics only to bound it to disciplinary society and disqualify it for the present. For Han it is "a politics of the body in the fullest sense," tied to "the biological, the somatic, the corporal," and therefore structurally barred from the psyche — the negative foil that makes psychopolitics necessary. Han pointedly rejects re-housing psychopolitics *inside* biopolitics (criticizing Alexandra Rau and Thomas Lemke for treating it "as a biopolitical form of government"): for Han biopolitics is a superseded predecessor, not a genus of which psychopolitics is a species. (`Directly stated`)
- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — does not use the word "biopolitics," but Chapter 13's instrumentarian power is constructed as biopolitics' precise inversion. Where biopolitics is "a politics of the body in the fullest sense" that "invests life through and through" and administers the somatic, instrumentarian power leaves the body untouched and severs it from power entirely: "Instead of death, torture, reeducation, or conversion, instrumentarianism effectively exiles us from our own behavior. It severs our insides from our outsides, our subjectivity and interiority from our observable actions." The instrumentarian price "is not with our bodies but with our freedom." (`Interpretive synthesis` / `Directly stated`)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Psychopolitics]] — the defining opposition of the book. Biopolitics governs the body, the somatic, the population, through demography and discipline; psychopolitics governs the psyche, the "soul," through optimization and active steering. Body vs. soul, demography vs. psychogram, "passive surveillance" vs. "active steering" — biopolitics is precisely the power that *cannot* do what psychopolitics does.

## Questions It Raises

- If Han bounds biopolitics so tightly to the disciplinary body, does he underread Foucault's own later "technologies of the self" — the very hinge Foucault used to move beyond the docile body — and thereby overstate the cleanness of the body/soul break?
- Does the body genuinely drop out under "immaterial production," or does Han's wager that biopolitics is obsolete obscure how psychic optimization (fitness, "sexiness," neuro-enhancement, cosmetic surgery) re-inscribes a governed, marketed body? (the body as data; embodiment; material vs immaterial)
- Is the demography/psychography distinction stable, or does Big Data dissolve it — making the "psychogram of the population" a continuation of biopolitical population-management by other means rather than its supersession? (systems/feedback; the body as data)
- Han insists psychopolitics is a *successor* to biopolitics, not a *form* of it (contra Rau and Lemke). What hangs on that distinction, and is the body really external to neoliberal power, or only displaced?
- Han and Zuboff agree the body falls away — Han to the exploited psyche, Zuboff to the engineering of behavior indifferent to interiority. But if biopolitics "invests life through and through" while instrumentarianism "exiles us from our own behavior," is the somatic really released by both regimes, or does the rendition of the body as data (Fitbit, pacemaker, biometric ID) smuggle a renewed biopolitics back in under cover of behaviorism?
