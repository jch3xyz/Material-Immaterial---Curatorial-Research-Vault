# The Sociometric Badge

## Brief Definition

The sociometric badge is the wearable sensor — combining a microphone, accelerometer, Bluetooth connection, analytic software, and machine learning — that Alex "Sandy" Pentland and his MIT students developed to render "the structure and dynamic relationships" in human groups, the concrete instrument by which `[[Rendition]]` is extended from individual to *social* experience, and which was commercialized as Humanyze's "smart employee badge."

## Longer Explanation

For Shoshana Zuboff the sociometric badge is the workplace register of social rendition — the device that drags the previously "elusive domain" of the social into the light of behavioral measurement. Where earlier chapters trace how surveillance capitalism renders virtual and then actual experience into `[[Behavioral Surplus]]`, Chapter 15's section "When Big Other Eats Society: The Rendition of Social Relations" describes the apparatus that completes the sequence: the rendering of *social* behavior itself. The badge began life in 2002 as the "sociometer," introduced in answer to the problem that, as Pentland and doctoral student Tanzeem Choudhury wrote, "there are currently no available methods to automatically model face-to-face interactions." *Directly stated* (Ch.15): "The researchers' response was to introduce the 'sociometer,' a wearable sensor that combines a microphone, accelerometer, Bluetooth connection, analytic software, and machine learning techniques designed to infer 'the structure and dynamic relationships' in human groups."

The badge is the material embodiment of `[[Instrumentarian Power]]` at the scale of the office. It "instruments" the worker — in a 2009 deployment, twenty-two office employees were fitted with the badge for a month to "automatically measure individual and collective patterns of behavior, predict human behavior from unconscious social signals, identify social affinity among individuals working in the same team, and enhance social interactions by providing feedback to the users of our system." Its avowed goal was machines that "monitor social communication and provide real-time intervention" — surveillance fused with `[[The Means of Behavioral Modification]]`. The badge thus folds together rendition (capturing voice tone, body language, who interacts with whom) and modification (feedback, intervention, "tuning") in a single worn object, making the employee at once a sensor and a target. *Directly stated / Strongly implied* (Ch.15).

The instrument's commercial trajectory is, for Zuboff, the proof of its meaning. Pentland and his coauthors founded Sociometric Solutions in 2010 to bring the badge "to the marketplace … to captive populations of office workers"; in 2015 the firm "opted for euphemism in a rebranding effort, changing its name to Humanyze." *Directly stated* (Ch.15): "Its technology is described as a platform that uses a 'smart employee badge to collect employee behavioral data, which it links to specific metrics with the goal of improving business performance.'" The rebrand from "Sociometric" to "Humanyze," and the relabeling of the device as a *smart* badge, exemplify the pattern Zuboff names in `[[Smart as a Euphemism for Rendition]]`: the word *smart* operating as a "Trojan horse" that smuggles continuous behavioral capture into the workplace under the sign of optimization. The forty-measure "business metric dashboard" — deployed, per one account, across 10,000 Bank of America customer-service workers and in partnership with Deloitte — turns the worn body into a continuous, tradable data stream. *Directly stated* (Ch.15).

Crucially, the badge depends on the disappearance of awareness. The researchers acknowledged that "the continuous pervasive collection of human behavioral data could succeed only when conducted outside the boundaries of human awareness, thus eliminating possible resistance," anticipating that the "slight sense of surveillance" the badge conveys "is likely reduced as sensors get smaller and smaller." For Zuboff this is the device through which `[[Surveillance]]` colonizes the social itself, and a concrete answer to B. F. Skinner's lament about the absence of "instruments and methods" for studying human behavior — Pentland, she argues, "completes Skinner" by building exactly the `[[Radical Behaviorism]]` apparatus Skinner could only imagine. *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis* (Ch.15).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — treats the sociometric badge as the literal hardware of social rendition: the wearable that converts the body's social signals (voice tone, movement, proximity, "lean in") into behavioral data, fuses surveillance with behavioral modification ("real-time intervention," "tuning the network"), and is then commercialized by Humanyze as a "smart employee badge." It is the workplace instance of `[[Rendition]]` extended from individual to social experience, and a worn object that doubles as a measure of the `[[Quantified Self]]` enforced from outside rather than chosen from within.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Right to Sanctuary]] — the badge's premise of pervasive, awareness-evading measurement of social behavior negates any inviolable inner or social space exempt from rendition.

## Questions It Raises

- If the social bond itself can be rendered as data and "tuned," what is left of solidarity, empathy, or collective action that escapes the instrument?
- Does worker consent mean anything when, by the researchers' own admission, the device succeeds only by operating "outside the boundaries of human awareness"?
- Is the sociometric badge a tool of measurement or of control — and can the two be separated once "real-time intervention" is built into the instrument?
- When the quantified self is imposed by the employer rather than adopted by the subject, does the rhetoric of self-optimization conceal a transfer of power over the body's social life?
