# The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self

## Brief Definition

Harari's two-selves model (adapted from Daniel Kahneman): the **experiencing self** is moment-to-moment consciousness, which remembers nothing and is seldom consulted, while the **narrating self** retrieves memories, tells stories, applies the peak-end rule and neglects duration, and is the entity we mean when we say "I."

## Longer Explanation

Harari draws the distinction out of Kahneman's cold-water experiment, in which 80 per cent of volunteers chose to repeat a *longer* episode of discomfort because it ended on a slightly warmer note. The experiment, Harari writes, "exposes the existence of at least two different selves within us: the experiencing self and the narrating self. The experiencing self is our moment-to-moment consciousness." For the experiencing self the longer ordeal was plainly worse — more total seconds of cold — yet that self does not get the casting vote. `Directly stated`

The asymmetry is the whole point. "The experiencing self remembers nothing. It tells no stories, and is seldom consulted when it comes to big decisions. Retrieving memories, telling stories and making big decisions are all the monopoly of a very different entity inside us: the narrating self." The narrating self works like a journalist or politician: it "doesn't narrate everything, and usually weaves the story only from peak moments and end results," then evaluates the whole "by averaging peaks with ends." Two systematic distortions follow. First, the **peak-end rule** — it "remembers only the peak moment and the end moment, and evaluates the whole experience according to their average." Second, **duration-neglect** (Harari: the narrating self "is duration-blind") — the length of an experience drops out entirely, which is why the colonoscopy patient whose test lasted three times as long, but tapered to a mild end, remembered *less* pain. `Directly stated`

Crucially, the narrating self is what we identify *as*. "Nevertheless, most people identify with their narrating self. When they say 'I', they mean the story in their head, not the stream of experiences they undergo." This is the move that makes the model load-bearing for *Homo Deus*: if the "I" is a story-spinning module rather than a unified soul, then the self "too is an imaginary story, just like nations, gods and money" — an [[Intersubjective Reality]] running inside one skull — and the liberal premise of a single authentic inner voice collapses. The two selves are "closely intertwined" (the stories shape what the experiencing self feels, and the experiencing self can sabotage the narrating self's plans), but they have genuinely different interests, which is why "the patient has at least two different selves, and they have different interests." `Directly stated`

The model is also the hinge of the political argument in **The Great Decoupling**: because the narrating self decides "on the basis of cooked-up stories" and is "misled by cognitive short cuts and the peak-end rule," an external algorithm that actually remembered every moment could represent a person's interests better than the narrating self does — "Liberalism sanctifies the narrating self... Yet once we have a system that really does know me better, it will be foolhardy to leave authority in the hands of the narrating self." Here the two-selves model becomes the psychological warrant for [[Algorithmic Determinism]] and [[Dataism]]. `Directly stated`

### The Left-Brain Interpreter and Confabulation

Harari explicitly anchors the narrating self in neuroscience: "The narrating self is akin to Gazzaniga's left-brain interpreter. It is forever busy spinning yarns about the past and making plans for the future." In the split-brain experiments of Roger Sperry and Michael Gazzaniga, the left hemisphere — which controls speech — would confabulate reasons for actions actually driven by data only the mute right hemisphere had seen. Shown a chicken claw (left brain) and a snow scene (right brain), patient PS pointed to a chicken and a shovel and, asked why, answered: "the chicken claw goes with the chicken, and you need a shovel to clean out the chicken shed." From repeated trials Gazzaniga "concluded that the left hemisphere of the brain is the seat not only of our verbal abilities, but also of an internal interpreter that constantly tries to make sense of our life, using partial clues in order to concoct plausible stories." `Directly stated`

Harari generalises the interpreter beyond the clinic with his CIA / State Department image: "Again and again my own private CIA does things without the approval or knowledge of my State Department, and then my State Department cooks up a story that presents me in the best possible light. Often enough, the State Department itself becomes convinced of the pure fantasies it has invented." The narrating self, in other words, is a permanent **confabulation engine** — not a faithful reporter of an inner truth but a press office retro-fitting plausible reasons onto decisions already made by sub-personal processes (the "tug of war between different and often conflicting inner entities"). This is why the model belongs to Harari's wider thesis that humans are not individuals but "dividuals" and that [[Organisms Are Algorithms]]. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — Imports the pair directly from Kahneman ("the experiencing self and the narrating self") and fuses it with Gazzaniga's "left-brain interpreter," treating the narrating self as both a story-spinner governed by the peak-end rule and duration-neglect *and* a confabulation engine. His distinctive use is political and ontological: the narrating self is the "I" that liberalism sanctifies and the polling booth consults, so demonstrating its systematic unreliability is a step toward dethroning the liberal individual in favour of monitoring algorithms.
- **Daniel Kahneman** (named in the text; no book in `raw/`) — Originates the experiencing-self / remembering-self distinction and the peak-end rule via the cold-water and colonoscopy studies Harari recounts. Rendered as plain text here pending a dedicated source.
- **Michael S. Gazzaniga / Roger Wolcott Sperry** (named in the text; no book in `raw/`) — Source of the "left-brain interpreter" and the split-brain confabulation evidence Harari equates with the narrating self.

## Related Concepts

- [[Narrativity]] — Han's account of narration as a binding, time-giving practice. It *resonates* with the narrating self (both concern the self constituted through story) but is distinct: for Han narration is a positive capacity whose *loss* is the crisis; for Harari the narrating self is the very illusion to be exposed. Cross-link, do not merge.
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the biological substrate: both selves are biochemical processing, and the "I" of the narrating self is "the collection of biochemical algorithms known as 'I'."
- [[Intersubjective Reality]] — the self as story stands alongside nations, gods and money as a fiction with real effects.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the narrating self's unreliability (peak-end rule, duration-neglect, confabulation) is Harari's argument that external algorithms can know us better than we know ourselves.
- [[Dataism]] — the worldview that inherits authority from the discredited narrating self.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — the right/left-hemisphere data on which the interpreter model rests treats cognition as distributed across sub-systems rather than seated in one self.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the single, indivisible, self-authoring "individual" the two-selves model dissolves into a story-spinning module plus a mute stream of experience.
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — the ideology that "sanctifies the narrating self" and trusts its inner voice in the polling station, supermarket and marriage market.

## Related Tensions

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the narrating self's confabulation ("I don't choose my desires. I only feel them, and act accordingly") is Harari's evidence against the free, self-transparent will.
- [[Narration vs Addition]] — Han's tension over whether the self narrates time or merely accumulates points; sits adjacent to Harari's pair but is a separate note (Han-anchored), cross-linked not merged.

## Questions It Raises

- If the narrating self is a confabulator that "cooks up a story," on what ground can any self-report — including this one — be trusted? Does the model undercut its own narration?
- Is the experiencing self even *consultable*, given that it "remembers nothing"? If it cannot speak, can its interests ever genuinely count in a decision?
- Harari uses the model to argue an algorithm could represent me better than my narrating self. But would such an algorithm serve the experiencing self, the narrating self, or some third construction of its own?
- Does equating Kahneman's remembering self with Gazzaniga's left-brain interpreter conflate two different findings (a memory-evaluation bias vs a hemispheric confabulation effect), or are they the same mechanism seen twice?
- If "I" is the narrating self, is meditation's attempt to inhabit the experiencing self a route out of the illusion, or just a different story the narrating self tells?
