# The Computer as Matrix

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's image of the computer not as a stand-alone image-generator but as the universal "matrix" through which the abundance of data in all its modes passes and is integrated across diverse media — digitisation as a "lingua franca," a universal machine that holds and expresses only as much creative thought as is put into it.

## Longer Explanation

In "Art and Education in the Telematic Culture" (1988) Ascott argues that the computer's significance for art lies not in its capacity to produce pictures but in its function as a *matrix*: a single passage through which "the abundance of data in all its modes can pass, from remote sensors, scanners, metering devices, and difference machines of all kinds." Because all of these heterogeneous inputs are digitised, they become commensurable — they can be combined, transformed, and worked between. Ascott names this commensurability a *lingua franca*: digitisation is "the lingua franca of an enormous range of visual and notational systems reporting on, recording, and analysing the world," and simultaneously a medium through which "our dreams, fantasies, speculations, and assertions can find expression." The matrix is thus both an instrument of measurement and an instrument of imagination [Directly stated].

The decisive move is the demotion of the computer-as-output-device in favour of the computer-as-connective-medium. "As a matrix," Ascott writes, "it is much more than a stand-alone generator of images, for it extends enormously the capability of the artist to integrate and work between diverse media." The matrix is what lets film, video, photography, graphics, paint, print, and text become a single workable field — a between-the-media position rather than any one medium [Directly stated]. This is why the concept sits inside, not beside, Ascott's larger telematic thesis that "art is all process, all system": the matrix is the technical condition under which the artwork can be a process distributed across media and participants rather than a discrete material object.

Ascott is careful to deny the matrix any creativity of its own. He calls the computer "simply a universal machine," and invokes the programmer's maxim to fix its moral status: "'Garbage in, garbage out' is, I suppose, the phrase to invoke here. That is to say, the universal machine—which the computer is—can contain as much creative thought and express as much emotion as we put into it." The matrix is therefore a mirror of its use: deployed "merely as tools of production," telematic networks and computer systems "will certainly and very effectively promote sterility and alienation"; deployed in the spirit of Socratic enquiry rather than Catonic productivity, the same machine becomes a medium of communication "of desire and of anxiety" [Directly stated]. The universality of the machine is value-neutral; what it amounts to depends entirely on the thought it is given.

The matrix-image is durable across the book and resonates with Ascott's earlier `Cybernetic Art Matrix` (the CAM proposal), though the two are not identical: the CAM names an institutional/social system for cybernetic art, while "the computer as matrix" names the digital substrate that makes all media commensurable. Read against Marshall McLuhan, the matrix is the limit case of `Media as Translators` — McLuhan held that every medium translates experience into a new form; Ascott's universal machine translates *all* media into one another through the common form of the digit [Interpretive synthesis].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the computer is "the matrix through which the abundance of data in all its modes can pass"; digitisation is a "lingua franca"; the "universal machine" expresses only "as much creative thought . . . as we put into it." The matrix is a connective, between-the-media substrate, not an image-generator; its value is wholly a function of its use ("garbage in, garbage out").
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — anticipates the matrix obliquely through [[Media as Translators]]: each medium translates one mode of experience into another. Ascott's digital matrix radicalises this into a single medium that translates *every* mode into every other. (Cross-author resonance, not a term McLuhan used. [Interpretive synthesis])

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the matrix dissolves the material specificity of each medium into a common immaterial (digital) substrate; it is an engine of dematerialisation, pulling against the material art object.

## Questions It Raises

- If the computer is "simply a universal machine" with no creativity of its own, where does the agency in telematic art reside — in the artist, the participants, or the system of interaction?
- Does the "lingua franca" of digitisation enable a genuine multiplicity of media, or does reducing all media to a common digital form homogenise them — the very flattening Ascott elsewhere warns against?
- Is "garbage in, garbage out" still adequate once the matrix becomes generative (machine learning, autonomous image synthesis), or has the universal machine begun to contain more than is "put into it"?
- How does the computer-as-matrix differ from the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] as a social institution — is one the substrate of the other?
