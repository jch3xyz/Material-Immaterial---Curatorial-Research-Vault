# The Datapool

## Brief Definition

Ascott's master metaphor for the electronic-arts institution reconceived as a dynamic living organism — a pool into which and within which data in all their modes flow, are endlessly transformed through human interaction, and emerge as "art-in-flux," flowing on into the wider data sea.

## Longer Explanation

The datapool is the identity Ascott proposed for the Ars Electronica Center (AEC) in Linz, a term he coined while serving as consultant on the project. It is the figure through which he replaces the static museum with a "dynamic organism rather than a fixed and static institution" — what he calls "a museum of the twenty-first century." The institution is no longer an archive that imposes perspectives on the history of art but "a pool of possibilities," "not reactive but anticipatory," working "as an agent of cultural change, as a cause of art practice rather than simply a cultural effect." Connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence are the five qualities the datapool exists to support — the same "fivefold path" Ascott elsewhere names the "Tao of telematics" *(Directly stated)*.

The metaphor is governed by an image-set of water and swimming. The work of art is "not as finite object but as process and system, a fluid, moving stream of data configurations." The change it demands is above all a change in the spectator's body and stance: "no longer to observe, stand back, look from a distance, and judge, but to plunge into the datapool, immerse themselves in its fluid changeability, share in its swirling transformations, navigate its knowledge bases, dive to its depths of meaning." Ascott extends the image with deliberate humour — "Splashing, paddling, plunging, wading, diving, fast crawl and dog paddle all have their place" — as descriptions of "the variable degrees of physical, intellectual, and emotional engagement each visitor may wish to invest." The contemplative distance of the classical museum gives way to total bodily immersion in a fluid medium; the viewer becomes a "free-floating cybernaut" *(Directly stated)*.

The datapool is also a biological-evolutionary figure, and this is where it carries its deepest claim. "The datapool metaphor reminds us of the parallel between the emergence of proto-organisms from primeval rock pools and the emergence of the constituents of a post-biological culture from the pools of data forming on the planet today." The pool of data is the new primeval soup; new life forms — Ascott's "animats," "progranimals," and other artificial-life entities — are imagined as growing within it. The governing frame is therefore "not that of the machine" but of "the new biology, artificial life, neural networks, cognitive science, and radical constructivism." Art's role shifts "from imitating life" to "emulating it," from representation to "creating worlds, of inventing alternative realities." The datapool is the institutional vessel for entering "the post-biological era" *(Directly stated)*.

In Ascott's compressed dictionary entry the metaphor is fixed in a single sentence and made portable across his later writing: "Datapool A metaphor for the digital museum into which data flows, endlessly transformed through human interaction, and from which it flows on out into the data sea. Post-biological life forms grow in these datapools just as proto organisms emerged from primeval rock pools." The image migrates from a specific architectural proposal (AEC) into a general figure for any "cultural organism," and is recruited again in "Telenoia" as the model for telematic, networked existence — life "by the datapool" *(Directly stated / Strongly implied)*.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins "datapool" as consultant for the Ars Electronica Center, Linz (1993). The datapool is the institutional embodiment of his telematic program: it converts the museum from showcase into "operations centre" and "collaboratory," realizes [[Network Consciousness]] in built form, and stages [[Art as Experiential Technology]] as immersive process rather than displayed object. The primeval-rock-pool image binds it to his post-biological vision of [[Artificial Life]] and [[Emergence]] as the new ground of art.

## Related Concepts

- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the datapool is the architecture that houses experiential, immersive, participatory art; "art not as finite object but as process and system." It EXTENDS this Burnham-anchored concept from the apparatus to the institution.
- [[New Media Art]] — the datapool is Ascott's proposed institutional home for the electronic/telematic arts; it names the museum form that "older cultural institutions cannot adapt" to provide.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the datapool is one node ("a major, generative node") in the planetary web; its "telematic level" is "the alias of all other levels," realizing networked consciousness as building and behaviour.
- [[Emergence]] — emergence ("of new data, images, forms, structures, and systems") is one of the five qualities the datapool exists to support; the pool is a "seedbed" from which the new art "evolves."
- [[Artificial Life]] — the rock-pool parallel makes the datapool the medium in which "post-biological life forms grow"; the top floor of AEC is literally "a zoo set in artificial nature."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — the datapool stages art's passage from object to system at the scale of the institution: "art not as finite object but as process and system." It is the museological correlate of the object→system spine, opposing the finite, contemplated, base-bound art object with a fluid, navigable, participatory data-system.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the datapool is explicitly poised on this tension. Ascott lists among AEC's energising contradictions that "its output is fluid and immaterial, but its engineering and construction are precise," and that "its processes are digital, but its attitudes are profoundly human" — the immaterial data-flux housed in a precise material structure.

## Questions It Raises

- If the museum becomes a fluid organism with "its own memory," who curates a pool — and can a curator stand outside a medium they are asked to swim in?
- Does immersion-as-imperative ("plunge," "dive," "no longer to observe... and judge") abolish critical distance, or relocate it? Ascott names a residual "phantom audience" haunting even the art that claims to have dissolved the spectator.
- The datapool's "data drain" carries "waste data" out of the system: what is the entropy, the loss, the discard of a culture that models itself as endless transformation?
- If "post-biological life forms grow in these datapools," is the metaphor a description of an institution or a wager about the next medium of life itself?
- Ascott insists AEC "must be transparent in its structures, its goals, and its systems" — how does the datapool's promised transparency sit beside its "smart card" access control and "technology of power"?
