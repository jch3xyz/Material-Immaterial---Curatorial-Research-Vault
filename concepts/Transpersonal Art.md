# Transpersonal Art

## Brief Definition

For Roy Ascott, **transpersonal art** is the art whose defining behaviour is [[Cyberception|cyberception]] — an art made through *transpersonal technology* (the technology of communicating, sharing, collaborating, and transcending bodily limits) that gives insight into the interconnectedness of all things and the instability of every boundary.

## Longer Explanation

Ascott introduces transpersonal art in "The Architecture of Cyberception" (1994) as the corollary of his central 1990s coinage: "Cyberception heightens transpersonal experience and is the defining behaviour of a transpersonal art." (`Directly stated`) Where [[Cyberception]] names the new, post-biological *faculty* — a fusion of perception and conception carried by telematic connectivity — transpersonal art names the *practice* that that faculty makes possible. The art is "transpersonal" because it does not stay within, or address itself to, the bounded individual; it operates across the connected, [[The Distributed Self|distributed self]] of networked culture.

The medium of this art is what Ascott calls **transpersonal technology**: "the technology of communicating, sharing, and collaborating, the technology that enables us to transform our selves, transfer our thoughts, and transcend the limitations of our bodies." (`Directly stated`) He immediately fixes its concrete referent — "Transpersonal technology is the technology of networks, hypermedia, and cyberspace" — so the term is not a vague spiritualism but a name for the [[The Cybernet|cybernet]] grasped as an instrument of self-transcendence rather than mere data exchange. It is the same telematic substrate that produces [[Network Consciousness|networked consciousness]], named now from the side of what it does to the self.

What the transpersonal experience *yields* is a particular content of awareness: "Transpersonal experience gives us insight into the interconnectedness of all things, the permeability and instability of boundaries, the lack of distinction between part and whole, foreground and background, context and content." (`Directly stated`) This is the conceptual hinge between cyberception as a perceptual claim and Ascott's later [[Technoetics|technoetic]] register: a transpersonal art is one whose business is the dissolution of the part/whole, inside/outside, material/immaterial distinctions that organised the perception of "the age of reason."

In the same chapter Ascott pushes the term toward outright [[Re-enchantment|re-enchantment]]. The transpersonal technologies "may be stimulating and re-activating parts of the apparatus of a consciousness long forgotten and made obsolete by a mechanistic worldview of cogs and wheels," so that "Cyberception may mean an awakening of our latent psychic powers, our capacity to be out of body or in mind-to-mind symbiosis with others." (`Strongly implied` that transpersonal art is the bridge — the claim is made *of cyberception*, which Ascott has just named as transpersonal art's defining behaviour.) This is where the concept stops being only a media-art category and becomes a precursor to the moist, technoetic, shamanic vocabulary of Ascott's later essays: transpersonal art is the form in which a re-enchanted, post-mechanistic consciousness could be practised.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins *transpersonal art* and *transpersonal technology* together in "The Architecture of Cyberception." Transpersonal art is "the defining behaviour" expression of [[Cyberception]]; transpersonal technology is "the technology of networks, hypermedia, and cyberspace" used to transcend the limits of the body and the bounded self. The concept is the bridge from his perceptual claim (cyberception) to his consciousness-and-re-enchantment claim ([[Technoetics]], "the future will be moist").

## Related Concepts

- [[Cyberception]] — the defining behaviour of transpersonal art; the faculty of which this art is the practice.
- [[The Cybernet]] — the planetary network that *is* the transpersonal technology ("networks, hypermedia, and cyberspace").
- [[The Distributed Self]] — the self that transpersonal art addresses and produces; "out of body or in mind-to-mind symbiosis with others."
- [[Network Consciousness]] — transpersonal experience named from the side of the connected collective mind.

## Opposing Concepts

- The bounded, "personal" art of the unitary self — implied opposite. Transpersonal art is set against the "mechanistic worldview of cogs and wheels" and the perception "of the old order of Western thought" that Ascott elsewhere in the chapter calls "the age of reason." (`Interpretive synthesis` — Ascott names no single opposing art movement.)

## Questions It Raises

- Is "transpersonal" a genuinely post-individual category, or does it still presuppose persons who then transcend their limits? Ascott's phrasing ("transcend the limitations of our bodies") keeps the body as the thing transcended.
- How far is the claim empirical (networks enable shared, distributed making) versus mystical (an "awakening of our latent psychic powers")? Ascott elides the two; the note keeps them as separate confidence claims.
- Does transpersonal art's "interconnectedness of all things" describe an artwork, an audience, or a worldview — and can a single category carry all three?
- Where does transpersonal art sit relative to Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]] and the [[Object vs System|object→system]] shift? Ascott's "transpersonal technologies that are shaping our global culture" extend the systems move from the object into consciousness and the body. (`Speculative connection`)
