# Hypermedia

## Brief Definition

Ascott's term — after Ted Nelson's hypertext and Roland Barthes's intertext — for the layered, nonsequential virtual data space in which the digital image floats, a site of interactivity and connectivity where the viewing subject immerses, navigates, and co-transforms what was once a fixed picture.

## Longer Explanation

In "Photography at the Interface," Ascott reads the digital photograph not as a thing on paper but as data adrift in a relational sea. Hypermedia is the medium of that sea: it "provides for a nonsequential inputting and navigation of multimedia data (photo, graphics, text, video, audio)," dissolving the single linear frame into a manifold of layers, routes, and passages. The image no longer sits still to be looked at; it is "brought up from the database into our realm of seeing" and is, once "flowing in data space," subject to "endless" recombination and transformation. This is what Ascott calls the "second order of reproduction" — beyond Walter Benjamin's age of mechanical reproduction — "the stage of co-production," in which the technology itself produces and transforms the image alongside artist and viewer (`Directly stated`).

The term is explicitly genealogical. Ascott nests it in two prior moves: Ted Nelson's *hypertext* and Roland Barthes's *intertext*. His keystone formulation is the aquatic figure: "data images exist always and at all times in a sea of interdata, just as the hypertext exists within the boundless domain of the intertext, where Ted Nelson meets Roland Barthes." Just as no text stands alone but floats in the boundless intertext, no data image is a discrete object — it is permanently relational, "layered, relational, and constantly shifting in content and context, depending on the behaviour and consciousness of the viewer" (`Directly stated`).

Crucially, hypermedia is defined by what it does to the *viewer*, not only to the image. It is "a site of interactivity and connectivity in which the viewer can play an active part in the transformation or affirmation of the images the photographer provides." Ascott's metaphor for entry is "emphatically aquatic": the hypermedia systems "call for sensory immersion and conceptual navigation on the part of the viewing subject" — immersion in the images surfaced from the database, navigation through the layers and "multiplicity of (HyperCard) routes and (SuperCard) passages." This repositions the viewer "as a manipulator of that image, as the one in whose hands the destiny of the image may lie" — the same empowerment Ascott elsewhere figures as `The Interface as Doorway`, where "the window onto a world of analogue actualities gives way to the doorway into a world of digital potentialities" (`Directly stated`).

Hypermedia thus operationalizes Ascott's larger wager that art's substance has shifted from the material object to immaterial, connective, transformable `Information`. The digital photograph's difference from "regular photography," he insists, "cannot be simply" the difference "between the material image on paper and the immaterial apparition at the computer interface"; it "lies finally in the opportunity for its further transformation by the viewing subject" — a relational potential that only the hypermedia environment affords (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — Hypermedia is the layered, nonsequential `Data Space` in which the digital photograph floats; a site of interactivity and connectivity that demands immersion and navigation and turns reception into co-production. Ascott derives the term by analogy from Nelson's hypertext and Barthes's intertext (the image in a "sea of interdata").
- **Ted Nelson** (named by Ascott) — coiner of *hypertext*, "the inventor of Hypertext and of 'Xanadu,' the vision of a globally connected knowledge network"; Ascott's structural precursor for nonsequential, linked media.
- **Roland Barthes** (named by Ascott) — the *intertext* as "boundless domain"; Ascott transposes Barthes's relational theory of the text onto the data image.

## Related Concepts

- [[Data Space]] — hypermedia is the layered, "infinitely layered" data space in which images flow; the two are nearly co-extensive in Ascott's usage.
- [[Information]] — hypermedia is the connective, transformable medium through which art becomes immaterial information rather than fixed object.
- [[The Interface as Doorway]] — the interface to hypermedia is the doorway "into a world of digital potentialities" through which the empowered viewer enters.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — hypermedia stages the passage from "the material image on paper" to "the immaterial apparition at the computer interface," though Ascott insists the real difference is relational transformability, not the material/immaterial divide alone.

## Questions It Raises

- If the image "exists always and at all times in a sea of interdata," can a digital work ever be said to be finished, authored, or owned?
- Does the viewer's "empowerment as a manipulator" of the image democratize meaning, or merely relocate authorship into the affordances of the apparatus?
- What survives of photography's evidentiary or "veridical" claim once the image is "endlessly manipulated, recombined... and transformed"?
- Is Ascott's "second order of reproduction" a genuine break from Benjamin's mechanical reproduction, or an intensification of it?
