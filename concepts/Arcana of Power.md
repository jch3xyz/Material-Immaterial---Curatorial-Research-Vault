# Arcana of Power

## Brief Definition

The *arcanum* names the constitutive sphere of secrecy that, in Han's reading of [[Carl Schmitt]], every serious politics requires in order to remain politics rather than collapse into mere staging.

## Longer Explanation

For Han, the arcanum is not a contingent vice of political life — a place where corruption hides — but a structural condition of political action as such. Because "[p]olitics is strategic action," it "inhabits a realm of secrecy" (Ch. 1). Strategy presupposes withheld intentions, reserve, and the possibility of decision; total illumination strips action of the cover under which it can be planned and executed. The arcanum is therefore the negative space within which political will can take shape. Han imports this thesis directly from Schmitt, who insists that "the 'postulate of openness' . . . finds its specific opponent in the idea that arcana belong to every kind of politics" (Ch. 1, quoted in Han). The Schmittian provenance of the concept — and the questions it raises about Han's own borrowing — are treated separately in [[Han and Schmitt on the Arcanum]].

When the arcanum is dismantled, what remains is not a purer politics but its theatrical residue. Han endorses Schmitt's diagnosis that "[o]nly politics amounting to [[Theatocracy|theatocracy]] can do without secrets" and that, before "an audience of Papagenos," "political action gives way to mere staging" (Ch. 1). The conclusion he draws is stark: "the end of secrecy would be the end of politics" (Ch. 1, *Directly stated*). On this logic, the contemporary demand for transparency is not a deepening of democratic life but a slide into the [[Postpolitics|Postpolitical]] — administration, opinion-management, and referendum without political form.

In the later chapter on control, Han generalizes the point beyond high politics to power as such. "Power likes to cloak itself in secrecy. The praxis of arcana is one technique that power employs. Transparency dismantles the arcane sphere of power" (Ch. 9). The arcanum here names a *technique* — a praxis — by which power maintains the asymmetry between knowing and not-knowing that makes its operations possible. Han ties this to [[Trust]]: trust "is only possible in a state between knowing and not knowing" (Ch. 9), so that the arcane reserve of power is also what allows a ruler "a measure of freedom to act without constant auditing, monitoring, and oversight" (Ch. 9). The [[Digital Panopticon]], by contrast, abolishes this reserve through mutual illumination, which Han reads not as democratization but as the conversion of power into surveillance and of politics into [[Gleichschaltung]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Han uses *arcanum* in a tightly Schmittian sense to argue that [[Transparency]] is anti-political: the dismantling of secrecy is the dismantling of politics itself, leaving only theatocracy, opinion, and self-exhibiting [[Auto-Exploitation]].
- **[[Carl Schmitt]]** — The source of the term as Han deploys it; for Schmitt, arcana, hierarchy, and "secret diplomacy" belong to the aristocratic concept of politics, and their loss is the loss of politics as such (quoted in Ch. 1).
- **[[Guy Debord]]** — *Speculative connection*: Debord's analysis of [[The Society of the Spectacle|the spectacle]] as the moment when social life is wholly captured by representation may parallel Han's diagnosis of theatocracy, though Debord locates the loss in commodified appearance rather than the destruction of arcana.
- **[[Herbert Marcuse]]** — *Speculative connection*: Marcuse's account of a society that absorbs all negativity ([[One-Dimensional Society]]) resonates with Han's claim that compulsive transparency is "inherently positive" and "blind to what lies outside the system" (Ch. 1).

## Related Concepts

- [[Secrecy]] — the broader category of which the political *arcanum* is a specific, constitutive instance.
- [[Politics as Strategic Action]] — Han's reason why politics needs the arcanum: "Politics is strategic action. For this reason alone, it inhabits a realm of secrecy" (Ch. 1).
- [[Trust]] — like the arcanum, structurally requires the gap between knowing and not-knowing that transparency abolishes.
- [[Negativity]] — the arcanum is one figure of the productive negativity Han defends against the [[Positivity|Society of Positivity]].
- [[Theatocracy]] — the political form that survives the destruction of arcana.
- [[Han and Schmitt on the Arcanum]] — dedicated note on the direct citation relationship.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Transparency]] — the demand whose generalization explicitly "dismantles the arcane sphere of power" (Ch. 9).
- [[Postpolitics]] — the condition that supervenes when arcana, ideology, and political will give way to opinion and administration.
- [[Compulsive Transparency]] — Han's term for the systemic form transparency takes when it becomes a "systemic compulsion" (Ch. 1); it is precisely this compulsion that destroys arcana.
- [[Pirate Party]] — Han's contemporary example of the anti-arcane, "colorless" party of opinion (Ch. 1).

## Questions It Raises

- If the arcanum is constitutive of politics, is Han committed to defending forms of political secrecy — including those of states — against democratic demands for accountability? How does he distinguish necessary arcana from corruption?
- Han imports Schmitt's argument without flagging its authoritarian provenance. To what extent does adopting Schmitt's definition of politics commit Han to Schmitt's broader anti-liberalism? (See [[Han and Schmitt on the Arcanum]].)
- The Ch. 9 reformulation generalizes the arcanum from politics to "power" as such. Is this a friendly extension of Schmitt's claim or a category shift — from sovereignty to a more diffuse, possibly Foucauldian, conception of power?
- If transparency replaces arcana with mutual illumination in the [[Digital Panopticon]], does any reserve remain in which political will could re-form? Han's text leaves this open.
- Han contrasts ideology (with "cogent negativity") to mere opinion. Does the defense of arcana implicitly require a defense of ideology? (Cf. the [[Pirate Party]] passage in Ch. 1.)
