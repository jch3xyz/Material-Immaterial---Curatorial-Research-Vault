# Hyperactivity as Hyperpassivity

## Brief Definition

Byung-Chul Han's diagnostic inversion: when positive potency is absolutized, hyperactivity switches into hyperpassivity — an obedience to every impulse without resistance — so that the maximally active subject is in fact maximally passive, barred from free action.

## Longer Explanation

The concept names a paradox at the heart of *The Burnout Society*. Common sense, and the philosophical tradition that valorizes the active life, treats activity as the vehicle of freedom: the more one does, the freer one is. Han argues the opposite. In "The Pedagogy of Seeing," he tracks what happens when activity is intensified without limit — when "positive potency," the sheer power *to do*, is "made absolute to the exclusion of all else." At that limit, activity does not deliver more freedom; it abruptly reverses into its opposite. The hyperactive subject, having lost the negative capacity to inhibit, to "set a no in opposition," now "obeys every impulse or stimulus without resistance." This is hyperpassivity: a doing so total that it is no longer free doing at all. `Directly stated`

The key claim is that hyperactivity is "an extremely passive form of doing, which bars the possibility of free action." Free action, for Han, requires the negativity of refusal — the power *not to do* (see [[Negative Potency]]). Stripped of that power, action does not stop; it "scatters into restless, hyperactive reaction and abreaction." Activity in this pure, positive state "only prolongs what is already available"; it cannot turn to the Other, cannot pause, cannot begin anything genuinely new. The subject is delivered over to the object, "at the mercy of rushing, intrusive stimuli and impulses" — which is to say, made passive precisely in and through its frenzied activity. `Directly stated`

This is why Han presents the contemplative life not as passivity but as the *more* active mode: as "a mode of saying no, sovereign action [Tun]" that "proves more active than any and all hyperactivity, which represents a symptom of mental exhaustion." Hyperactivity, by contrast, is "a symptom of exhaustion" — the achievement subject's overheated doing that, lacking the inhibiting "excluding instincts" Nietzsche praises, collapses into compulsion. `Directly stated`

Han mounts the argument explicitly against Hannah Arendt's rehabilitation of the *vita activa*. "What eludes Arendt in the dialectic of being-active [Aktivsein] is that hyperactive intensification leads to an abrupt switch into hyperpassivity." The gain in activity does not free the subject; "instead of freedom, it produces new constraints." The closing verdict is direct: "It is an illusion to believe that being more active means being freer." This is the affirmative-diagnostic counterpart to the [[Achievement Society]]'s pathology — the same structure by which the freedom of the achievement subject becomes [[Auto-Exploitation]], here seen from the side of action rather than exploitation. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (*The Burnout Society*) — hyperactivity as hyperpassivity is Han's coinage. It rests on his distinction between [[Positivity|positive]] and negative potency: hyperpassivity is what positive potency produces once it is absolutized and the power-not-to is foreclosed. The concept is set against [[Hannah Arendt]]'s "dialectic of being-active" and forms the polemical core of his defense of the *vita contemplativa* (see [[Vita Activa vs Vita Contemplativa]]). It carries the argument [[Hyperactivity is a form of hyperpassivity not freedom]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Negative Potency]] — the cure and the conceptual opposite: the power *not to do*, whose absence is exactly what turns hyperactivity into hyperpassivity.
- [[Achievement Society]] — the social form whose excess of positivity and "Can" drives the hyperactivity diagnosed here; *The Burnout Society* is its primary source.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the parallel inversion on the side of labor and freedom: the achievement subject's freedom that is itself coercion.
- [[Acceleration]] — the temporal correlate: acceleration "is abolishing all intervals," leaving no pause within which free action could be measured.
- [[Positivity]] — hyperactivity is positivity made absolute; the surplus of the positive, not the negative, is the disorder.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Negative Potency]] — the power to say no, distinct from impotence; sovereign refusal is "more active than any and all hyperactivity," whereas hyperactivity is "an extremely passive form of doing."

## Questions It Raises

- If freedom requires the power *not to* act, what political or aesthetic practices could restore negative potency under conditions of generalized acceleration?
- Han reads hyperactivity as exhaustion rather than agency; how does this revalue the cultural ideals of productivity, multitasking, and "being active"?
- Where exactly is the threshold at which intensified activity "abruptly" switches into hyperpassivity — is it a quantitative limit, or a structural foreclosure of negativity from the start?
- Does Han's critique of Arendt's "dialectic of being-active" do justice to her account of action as beginning, or does it conflate her *vita activa* with mere laboring?
