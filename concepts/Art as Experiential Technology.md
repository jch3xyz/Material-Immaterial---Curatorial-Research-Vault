# Art as Experiential Technology

> **Status note (forward-stub now realized).** This was a long-deferred, *committed* research-focus forward-stub in the vault (18 inbound files), declared in the Lippard ingest and deferred "until Ascott/Burnham." It is **realized here, Jack Burnham–anchored**, from *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (Part Two: "Sculpture as System," chapters 6–8). It is scoped to be **extended when Ascott is ingested** — Ascott's cybernetic/telematic art is the other half of the committed cluster, and his author-section should be added to this note rather than minting a competing one.

## Brief Definition

Art conceived not as an object to be looked at but as a responsive technological *system* — kinetic, light-based, or cybernetic — that the participant enters, acts upon, and lives through in real time, ideally engaging the human being in a sustained two-way (reciprocal) exchange rather than a one-way message.

## Longer Explanation

Across Part Two of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Jack Burnham tracks the migration of sculpture from the discrete OBJECT to the interacting SYSTEM, and with it a parallel shift in the spectator's role: from the detached viewer of an inert artifact to a participant whose own perception and movement realize the work in time. **Art as experiential technology** is the name for the terminus of that arc — art that *behaves*, that is built from technological means (motors, light, electronics, feedback), and whose value lies in the experience of interacting with it rather than in the contemplation of its surface. *(Interpretive synthesis — the term is the vault's canonical name for the cluster; Burnham's text supplies the substance across kinetic, light, and cybernetic art.)*

In the kinetic chapter (ch. 6) the experiential turn first appears as the dissolution of "the entire ritual of seeing which presupposes a codified viewer-object relationship," and as a new mode in which the spectator "lives" the work in real time: "the viewer sees the work in a real time. The particular movement of his perception solidifies a measure of time in which the work realizes itself for him." *(Directly stated.)* In the light chapter (ch. 7) the experiential turn becomes environmental and participatory: emitted light tends "to eliminate the specific art object and to transform the environment into a light-modulating system sensitive to responses from organisms which invade its presence," and groups like G.R.A.V. aim not at spectacle "but to provoke spectators into acts of participation" — viewer-controlled rheostats, sensing photo-cells, the spectator who carries the imprint of laser light on the body. *(Directly stated.)*

The cybernetic chapter (ch. 8) supplies the most demanding version of the idea. Burnham argues that all prior sculpture — and even Kinetic Art — delivers "one-way messages," and that the next aim is "to make communication between the work of art and the observer a sustained two-way experience." *(Directly stated.)* The most distant goal is **Cyborg Art**, "the cybernetic organism as an art form," "the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture," in which the artwork possesses enough intelligence (via feedback) to interact with a human being as two people talking interact, not as a bow interacts with a violinist. *(Directly stated.)* This is where art-as-experiential-technology converges with [[The Cyborg]], [[Cybernetics]], and [[Feedback Loops]]: the experiential system becomes a reciprocal, responsive, quasi-living partner.

Two through-lines bind this concept to the vault's titular axis. First, the experiential system is increasingly *immaterial* — Burnham frames the object-to-system shift as one in which "for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor," and the whole light/kinetic program as a yearning toward immateriality — making this concept the pre-Lippard art-theoretical root of [[Dematerialization]] and a load-bearing case for [[Material vs Immaterial]]. *(Directly stated for Burnham's language; Interpretive synthesis for the dematerialization-precursor bridge.)* Second, the system requires an artist–engineer collaboration (E.A.T., "9 evenings"), aligning experiential art with technology not as metaphor but as literal medium and infrastructure. *(Strongly implied.)*

## Questions It Raises

- If the artwork must "possess some degree of intelligence" to interact reciprocally, where is the line between experiential art and an autonomous responsive system — and does the human remain the point, or become a component?
- Does art-as-experiential-technology dissolve the artwork into information and infrastructure (E.A.T., the artist–engineer symbiosis) at the cost of the autonomous object — and is that loss or liberation?
- Burnham warns of a "technology gap" between subsidized and unsubsidized artists; does experiential technology make art more participatory but less egalitarian to *make*?
- How does the participant's "living" of the work in real time relate to later regimes of interactivity and self-exposure (the vault's [[Digital Panopticon]] / participation-as-data cluster)?

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

Roy Ascott is the committed second anchor of this cluster, and chronologically its precursor: his "cybernetic vision" essays (*Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision*, 1966–67) predate Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968). Where Burnham reads sculpture's history as a drift from the object toward the responsive system, Ascott stakes out the **participatory / behavioural** pole — art as feedback technology whose value lies entirely in the experience the spectator has when *behaving* with it. His functional definition of the behaviourist artifact is the precise statement of art-as-experiential-technology: "the artifact functions as both matrix and catalyst. As matrix, it is the substance between two sets of behaviours; it exists neither for itself nor by itself. As a catalyst, it triggers changes in the spectator's total behaviour." *(Directly stated.)* The work is not an object to contemplate but a device for triggering and structuring lived behaviour — completed only by the participant, via the [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] triad of artist/artwork/observer. His own *Change-Painting* and the *Groundcourse* ("The Construction of Change") were exactly such experiential devices: interchangeable transparent panels the spectator manipulates, so that "the act of changing becomes a vital part of the total aesthetic experience." *(Directly stated.)*

Ascott also supplies the digital-age version of the experiential apparatus. The computer, for him, is not an extension of physical power like welding gear but "a tool for the mind, an instrument for the magnification of thought, potentially an 'intelligence amplifier,' to use H. Ross Ashby's term" *(Directly stated; via Shanken's edition)* — the experiential system relocated into thought itself. By his 1993 telematic essays the interactive artwork has become "inherently cybernetic," an open-ended system "structurally coupled" to its user, such that "Art is no longer a window onto the world but a doorway through which the observer is invited to enter into a world of interaction and transformation." *(Directly stated.)* This is Burnham's "sustained two-way experience" radicalized into a thesis about reality construction: the experiential technology does not merely respond to the participant, it positions the observer "at the centre of the creative process" so that, in the cyberculture, "to construct art is to construct reality." *(Strongly implied — the experiential pole pushed to its constructivist limit.)* Ascott thus extends this concept from Burnham's kinetic/light/cybernetic sculpture toward the **participatory, interactive, immersive, networked** pole — the same object-to-system spine, read from the side of the behaving, completing spectator. *(Interpretive synthesis for the Ascott↔Burnham complementarity; the two do not cite each other and Ascott is the earlier source.)*
