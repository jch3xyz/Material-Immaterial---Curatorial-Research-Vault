# The Division of Learning in Society

## Brief Definition

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], the **division of learning in society** is the new axial principle of social order in the twenty-first century — the way an information civilization distributes the answers to three questions, *Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?* — which surveillance capitalism has captured and privatized through its unprecedented asymmetries of knowledge and power.

## Longer Explanation

Zuboff frames the division of learning as the successor to Émile Durkheim's *division of labor*. Where the division of labor was the organizing principle of industrial society — what bound diverse members into "a larger prospect of solidarity" — the division of learning is its analogue for "the second modernity," the [[Information|information]] civilization in which "learning, information, and knowledge" have become primary. She states the parallel directly: "The division of learning is to us, members of the second modernity, what the division of labor was to our grandparents and great-grandparents, pioneers of the first modernity" (Ch. 6). This is not a metaphor but a claim about the deep structure of social order: the division of learning is "the axial principle of social order in an information civilization" (Ch. 6) and "the critical axis of social order in the twenty-first century" (Ch. 1). `Directly stated`.

The concept resolves into **three essential questions** that recur as a refrain across Part I and Part II of the book. "The first question is 'Who knows?' This is a question about the distribution of knowledge and whether one is included or excluded from the opportunity to learn. The second question is 'Who decides?' This is a question about authority… The third question is 'Who decides who decides?' This is a question about power" (Ch. 6). The three questions map onto three things at stake — knowledge, authority, and power — and the answers to them, taken together, constitute the division of learning at any given moment. The questions are restated almost verbatim at successive points in the argument: every "smart" object "repeats our essential questions: What does a smart product know, and whom does it tell? Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?" (Ch. 8), and the synthesis chapter "return[s] to the essential questions that define the division of learning: Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?" (Ch. 10). `Directly stated`.

Zuboff's diagnosis is that surveillance capitalism has supplied all three answers in its own favor. "As things currently stand, it is the surveillance capitalist corporations that know. It is the market form that decides. It is the competitive struggle among surveillance capitalists that decides who decides" (Ch. 6). This concentration is what she names the **privatization** of the division of learning: "On the strength of its unprecedented concentrations of knowledge and power, surveillance capitalism achieves dominance over the division of learning in society" (Ch. 6), an "unprecedented concentration of knowledge [that] produces an equally unprecedented concentration of power: asymmetries that must be understood as the unauthorized privatization of the division of learning in society" (Ch. 6). The grounding asymmetry is stated in Chapter 1: "Surveillance capitalists know everything about us, whereas their operations are designed to be unknowable to us. They accumulate vast domains of new knowledge from us, but not for us. They predict our futures for the sake of others' gain, not ours" (Ch. 1). Knowledge *from* us but not *for* us is the asymmetry that hijacks the division of learning. `Directly stated`.

Following Durkheim, Zuboff distinguishes a healthy from a **pathological** division of learning. Durkheim warned that an "abnormal" (sometimes translated "pathological") division of labor could produce "social distance, injustice, and discord in place of reciprocity," its most dangerous form being "extreme asymmetries of power that make 'conflict itself impossible'" (Ch. 6, quoting Durkheim). Zuboff applies the warning to her own moment: "today our societies are threatened as the division of learning drifts into pathology and injustice at the hands of the unprecedented asymmetries of knowledge and power that surveillance capitalism has achieved" (Ch. 6). By the closing argument the pathology is named as a new caste order: "A pathological division of learning forged by unprecedented asymmetries of knowledge and power fixes us in a new inequality marked by the tuners and the tuned, the herders and herded, the raw material and its miners, the experimenters and their unwitting subjects, those who will the future and those who are shunted toward others' guaranteed outcomes" (Ch. 11). The book's project of "naming and taming" is offered precisely as a way to "challenge the pathological division of learning" (Ch. 11). `Directly stated`.

> Forward-anchor: the concept is *named* and given its Durkheimian framing here; it is the spine along which the surveillance-capitalism argument unfolds across Part I and Part II, so it recurs (and is deepened with the mechanisms of [[Surveillance|surveillance]], rendition, and behavioral prediction) in later chunks of this ingest. This note records the concept's definition and its three-question structure; the mechanisms that effect the capture are developed in the notes for [[Surveillance]] and the rendition chapters.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — the division of learning is the "axial principle of social order in an information civilization" (Ch. 6), structured by the three questions *Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?* (knowledge / authority / power). Surveillance capitalism has effected its "unauthorized privatization" (Ch. 6) through asymmetries of knowledge that are "from us, but not for us" (Ch. 1), producing a "pathological division of learning" of "the tuners and the tuned" (Ch. 11). `Directly stated`.
- **Émile Durkheim** — the explicit precursor. Zuboff updates Durkheim's *The Division of Labor in Society* for the information age; the division of learning "is to us… what the division of labor was to our grandparents" (Ch. 6), and the threat she names — "the unauthorized privatization of the division of learning" — is the analogue of "the subversion of the division of labor by the powerful forces of industrial capital a century ago" (Ch. 6). Durkheim is named substantively but has no book in `raw/`; rendered as plain text. `Directly stated`.

## Related Concepts

- [[Information]] — the division of learning is the *distributional* question that the vault's [[Information]] note ("who knows / who decides") raises at the level of social order; for Zuboff the world is "pervasively rendered as information" and the contest is over who commands that information (Ch. 6).
- [[Surveillance]] — surveillance is the *mechanism* by which surveillance capitalism captures the division of learning; the asymmetries of knowledge are produced by "unilateral surveillance and the self-authorized extraction of human experience" (Ch. 1).
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's voluntary-self-exposure panopticon and Zuboff's "know everything about us… unknowable to us" describe the same knowledge asymmetry from different traditions; cross-link, not identity.
- [[Dataism]] — the division of learning is hijacked under the same data-positivist creed that Han and Harari diagnose as [[Dataism|dataism]]: "the machine knows" (Ch. 6).
- [[Big Data]] — the "titanic agglomerations of knowledge about our behavior" (Ch. 10) are the substance over which the division of learning is fought.
- [[Quantified Self]] — the rendering of lived experience into behavioral data ("smart" as "a euphemism for rendition," Ch. 8) is the raw material whose ownership the division of learning distributes.

## Opposing Concepts

- Knowledge vs Power — Zuboff's whole argument turns on the *coupling* of the two ("unprecedented concentration of knowledge… produces an equally unprecedented concentration of power," Ch. 6); the pathology is their fusion in private hands. The healthy division of learning is the *decoupling* — a democratic distribution of who knows from who decides.

## Questions It Raises

- If the division of learning is the new axial principle of social order, what democratic institution could play the role for it that collective bargaining and public education played for the division of labor?
- Durkheim held that a healthy division of labor "renders [members] solidary." Can a division of *learning* generate solidarity, or does knowledge asymmetry inherently corrode it?
- Is the privatization of the division of learning reversible, or has the "innately illegible character" of surveillance capitalism's action placed it beyond contest "without means of combat" (Ch. 6)?
- How does Zuboff's "who knows / who decides" differ from the older critiques of the knowledge–power nexus (Foucault), and does naming it a *division of learning* add analytic purchase beyond *power/knowledge*?
