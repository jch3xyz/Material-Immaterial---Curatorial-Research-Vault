# Light as a Sculpture Medium

## Brief Definition

The thesis, pivotal to *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, that "light-not form-determines our perception of sculpture," so that the sculptor's work becomes the construction of objects "solely designed as modulators of light" — a relational optical system of reflective and emitting surfaces rather than a coherent inner mass.

## Longer Explanation

Burnham's argument arrives at the close of Chapter 3 ("Optical Properties of Sculpture") as the hinge that turns Part I's account of sculpture-as-object toward Part II's sculpture-as-system. Across the chapter he has tracked the exhaustion of the formalist vocabulary — proportion, scale, structure, mass — and here he names what has quietly replaced it: not a new repertoire of forms but a different *perceptual* basis altogether. "In the past decade the tacit conviction has arisen that light-not form-determines our perception of sculpture" (`Directly stated`). Writing about the "optical properties" of sculpture, he says, would ten years earlier "have seemed a very curious, if not oblique, approach to an art based primarily on the ordering of physical mass." It no longer does: the greatest recent sculptors, he claims, "saw their works quite analytically, in fact dispassionately, for their optical effects created purely as light-reflecting sources."

The mechanism is a reversal of priority. "As the need to control light (for we perceive forms in terms of their light-emitting properties) gained priority, the tendency grew to construct works solely designed as modulators of light" (`Directly stated`). The sculptor no longer derives reflection from a unified body of metal but fragments the work into a distributed optical array: "Instead of deriving reflection from a continuous, coherent mass of metal, the sculptor conceived of a fragmented sculpture through semi-random, reflective surfaces positioned in spatial configurations." Burnham gathers this fragmented-reflective tendency under the term Herbert Read gave it — the **"glitter principle"** (`Directly stated`) — naming Lippold, Lassaw, and Bertoia, whose open wire and sheet-metal constructions scatter reflection across many small, oddly pitched surfaces (Bertoia's M.I.T. chapel altar of "many small steel squares covered with brazing brass, pitched at odd angles" is his exemplar). Reflectivity, "beginning with the plight of Narcissus," is for Burnham one of the "most primitive and hypnotic transmitters of light" — a consciousness-expanding phenomenon, but one that locates the work's force on its skin rather than in its depths.

This is, "in the broadest sense," a polemic against `Vitalism`. The optical conviction is "a reaction against the vitalist myth that the psyche of sculpture is buried within the object itself. Furthermore, it suggests that what is important about sculpture lies on its surface for optical examination" (`Directly stated`). The metaphysical payoff is Burnham's most dematerializing sentence: as the cult of optical deception grows, "form and matter lose their substantiality. Form, in effect, is what we make it appear to be." Perception, not the object, becomes the locus of the work — which is precisely why this concept hinges into the system thinking of Part II (`Strongly implied`).

Chapter 7 ("Light as Sculpture Medium") realizes the thesis as a genre. Where Chapter 3 concerns *reflected* light playing over surfaces, Chapter 7 turns to *emitted* light — light "directly discharged from the art object or system," whose defining quality is "the relative quality of luminosity" (`Directly stated`). Here the modulation of light ceases to need a sculptural object at all: "the trend of Light Art is to eliminate the specific art object and to transform the environment into a light-modulating system sensitive to responses from organisms which invade its presence" (`Directly stated`). Light demonstrates "one of the primary qualities of systems: the tendency to fuse art object and environment into a perceptual whole." Burnham situates the whole development within the larger movement he is tracking: "light-along with linear open sculpture, transparent materials, and attempts to set sculpture free of the ground plane-must be viewed as a further step in the 'ephemeralization' of the art object" (`Directly stated`). He closes the chapter recalling that Marshall McLuhan, lecturing, pointed to a glowing bulb and said it radiated pure information; "Increasingly pure energy and information seem to be the essences of art; all else is being dropped methodically by the wayside" (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — The primary theorist of the concept in this vault. In Chapter 3 it is the *optical/reflective* claim (light, controlled by color, transparency, or reflection, supersedes form; the "glitter principle" of fragmented reflective surfaces; the refutation of the vitalist interior psyche). In Chapter 7 it becomes the *emitted-light* genre of Light Art, whose tendency is to dissolve the discrete object into a responsive light-modulating environment — Burnham's own neon and electroluminescent-tape work among the American examples. The two chapters are continuous: both make perception, not mass, the carrier of the work.
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — Cited by Burnham at the end of Chapter 7 (the lightbulb that "radiated pure information") as the touchstone for reading light as pure energy/information; see [[The Electric Light]] and [[Jack Burnham and Marshall McLuhan]]. McLuhan's "the medium is the message" — the contentless electric light as the clearest case — corroborates Burnham's claim that light is significant as a relational system, not as a vehicle for depicted form (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Vitalism]] — the concept it explicitly refutes: the "vitalist myth that the psyche of sculpture is buried within the object itself." Light relocates significance to the surface; vitalism locates an inner life-force.
- [[Form vs Light]] — the tension this concept inaugurates: Burnham's claim that light, not form, determines our perception of sculpture sets the two in direct opposition ("Form, in effect, is what we make it appear to be").

## Questions It Raises

- If "form, in effect, is what we make it appear to be," does optical sculpture dissolve the object entirely into the perceiver's act — and is that a liberation or a "cult devoted to optical deception"?
- Where is the line between reflected light playing over a fragmented surface (the "glitter principle," still an object) and emitted light that "eliminates the specific art object"? Is it a difference of degree or of kind?
- Burnham reads light as "pure energy and information" with McLuhan; does this make Light Art an early instance of the material/immaterial drift the vault tracks elsewhere ([[Material vs Immaterial]])?
- If light sculpture's value lies on the surface "for optical examination," how does it differ from spectacle or commercial display (the neon sign, the pylon, advertising) that Burnham repeatedly invokes as its kin?
