# The Informatics of Domination

## Brief Definition

[[Donna Haraway]]'s coinage for the "scary new networks" of coded command-control-communication that have displaced "the comfortable old hierarchical dominations" — the regime of power belonging to the movement "from an organic, industrial society to a polymorphous, information system."

## Longer Explanation

Haraway names the informatics of domination at the head of the third section of *A Cyborg Manifesto*, where she sets out to chart a historical mutation in the very form of power. She argues that worldwide social relations have been so "rearrang[ed]" by science and technology that we are living through "a movement from an organic, industrial society to a polymorphous, information system—from all work to all play, a deadly game." The phrase itself labels one column of a two-column chart: on the left, the *organics* of domination (white capitalist patriarchy, the bourgeois novel, the organism, depth and integrity, reproduction, biology as clinical practice); on the right, the *informatics* of domination (simulation, science fiction, the biotic component, surface and boundary, replication, biology as inscription). The dichotomies, she insists, are "simultaneously material and ideological" — this is not merely a change of metaphor but a reorganization of how power operates on bodies and populations.

The hinge of the concept is *coding*. Haraway argues that "communications sciences and modern biologies are constructed by a common move—the translation of the world into a problem of coding, a search for a common language in which all resistance to instrumental control disappears and all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange." Under this regime the organism "has been translated into problems of genetic coding and readout"; organisms "have ceased to exist as objects of knowledge, giving way to biotic components, i.e., special kinds of information-processing devices." The "key operation" is "determining the rates, directions, and probabilities of flow of a quantity called information," because information "is just that kind of quantifiable element... that allows universal translation, and so unhindered instrumental power (called effective communication)." Control therefore migrates from "the integrity of natural objects" to "boundary conditions and interfaces, on rates of flow across boundaries." The technical and military emblem of the whole apparatus is C3I — "command-control-communication-intelligence" — which Haraway also invokes in the opening section, where "modern war is a cyborg orgy, coded by C3I... an $84 billion item in 1984's U.S. defense budget."

What makes the informatics of domination a *domination* rather than a neutral description is its lived cost. Returning to the figure in "Women in the Integrated Circuit," Haraway delivers the summary verdict: "The only way to characterize the informatics of domination is as a massive intensification of insecurity and cultural impoverishment, with common failure of subsistence networks for the most vulnerable." The actual situation of women, she writes, is "their integration/exploitation into a world system of production/reproduction and communication called the informatics of domination" — a system in which "the home, workplace, market, public arena, the body itself—all can be dispersed and interfaced in nearly infinite, polymorphous ways." The point is double-edged and ironic, not nostalgic: Haraway refuses to mourn the "comfortable old hierarchical dominations," because what they offered was often "virulent forms of oppression, nostalgically naturalized in the face of current violation." The new networks are not a fall from an organic grace; they are the terrain on which an "oppositional" cyborg politics must now be fought.

This note is kept deliberately distinct from [[Informatics]], mirroring the [[Information]] / [[Information (Han)|Information (Han)]] pattern in the vault. [[N. Katherine Hayles]] borrows the bare word "informatics" from Haraway — "Following Donna Haraway, I take informatics to mean the technologies of information as well as the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes that initiate, accompany, and complicate their development" — and repurposes it as the name for a *relational field* of feedback among bodies, texts, and technologies. Haraway's *informatics of domination* is the narrower, polemical source: a specific historical *regime of power* (a column of dominations), not Hayles's generalized network of relations. Hayles's note explicitly records the debt; this note holds Haraway's own usage. The two should be read reciprocally: Hayles's [[Informatics]] is the field; Haraway's informatics of domination is the regime that first put the word in circulation.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — the informatics of domination is the regime of "scary new networks" that has displaced the "comfortable old hierarchical dominations" of white capitalist patriarchy; power now works through coding, communication, and command-control (C3I) rather than through the integrity of natural objects, intensifying insecurity for the most vulnerable. The source of the term that Hayles later generalizes.
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — borrows the bare word "informatics" "[f]ollowing Donna Haraway" and broadens it into a relational network of bodies, texts, and technologies bound by feedback loops. Her sense is the field; Haraway's is the regime of power. See [[Informatics]]. (Strongly implied by Hayles's own attribution; the conceptual relationship is interpretive synthesis.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Informatics]] — Hayles's broader, relational descendant of this term; she takes the word from Haraway. Read reciprocally: field (Hayles) vs. regime of power (Haraway), on the model of [[Information]] vs. [[Information (Han)]].
- [[The Cyborg]] — the figure native to this regime; the cyborg is "the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism" and "is not subject to Foucault's biopolitics; the cyborg simulates politics, a much more potent field of operations."
- [[Information]] — the "quantifiable element... that allows universal translation, and so unhindered instrumental power"; the currency in which the informatics of domination conducts its exchanges.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the body reconstrued as a "biotic component," an "information-processing device" whose boundaries are "differentially permeable to information"; the corporeal form the regime takes.
- [[Dataism]] — the later worldview that makes the translation-of-the-world-into-coding into an explicit creed; Haraway's regime is its surveillance-era ancestor.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the informatics of domination is the historical engine of dematerialization that this axis tracks: it dissolves "the integrity of natural objects" into "rates of flow," boundaries, and code. Yet Haraway holds it "simultaneously material and ideological," refusing to let the immaterial float free of the electronics, labor, and bodies that sustain it ("Microelectronics is the technical basis of simulacra").
- The "organics of domination" — the left-hand column of Haraway's chart (organism, depth/integrity, reproduction, white capitalist patriarchy); the prior regime the informatics displaces. Rendered as plain text, not a link, as it is one pole of Haraway's internal contrast rather than a durable vault concept.

## Questions It Raises

- If "all resistance to instrumental control disappears" once "all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange," where does Haraway locate the leverage for an *oppositional* cyborg politics within the very regime she names?
- Haraway insists the dichotomies are "simultaneously material and ideological." How does this guard against collapsing the informatics of domination into a story of pure immateriality — and how does it relate to Hayles's [[The Materiality of Informatics]]?
- The cyborg "is not subject to Foucault's biopolitics; the cyborg simulates politics." What is gained and lost in reading domination as *simulation* and *command-control* rather than as the disciplinary administration of life?
- Haraway refuses to mourn the "comfortable old hierarchical dominations" because what is lost is often "virulent forms of oppression, nostalgically naturalized." Does this ironic stance disarm critique of the new networks, or sharpen it?
- For curatorial practice: if microelectronic machines are "everywhere and... invisible," "as hard to see politically as materially," how does one exhibit or make legible a domination that operates as "copies without originals"?
