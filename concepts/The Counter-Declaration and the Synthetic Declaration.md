# The Counter-Declaration and the Synthetic Declaration

## Brief Definition

[[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s paired distinction between the two forms that withdrawal of agreement to [[Surveillance Capitalism]] can take: the *counter-declaration*, a defensive measure (encryption, opt-out, "data ownership") that leaves the offending facts intact and thereby paradoxically legitimates them — "checkmate" to the declaration's "check" — versus the *synthetic declaration*, a game-changing assertion of an alternative framework that transforms the opposing facts, as in the Berlin Wall's "We will."

## Longer Explanation

The distinction completes Zuboff's account of [[Conquest by Declaration]]. If surveillance capitalism's facts were instituted by self-asserted declarations "pulled from thin air," then resistance, too, is a question of declarations — of how social agreement is withdrawn. "The withdrawal of agreement takes two broad forms, a distinction that will be useful as we move into Part III," she writes, and she names them precisely (`Directly stated`, Ch.11). The chess metaphor carries the whole logic: "If the declaration is 'check,' the counter-declaration is 'checkmate,' and the synthetic declaration changes the game. It asserts an alternative framework that transforms the opposing facts" (`Directly stated`, Ch.11).

The **counter-declaration** is defensive and individual. "These are defensive measures such as encryption and other privacy tools, or arguments for 'data ownership.' Such measures may be effective in discrete situations, but they leave the opposing facts intact, acknowledging their persistence and thus paradoxically contributing to their legitimacy" (`Directly stated`, Ch.11). Its structural weakness is that it concedes the field while merely seeking shelter on it: "if I 'opt out' of tracking, I opt out for me, but my action does not challenge or alter the offending practice" (`Directly stated`, Ch.11). The counter-declaration "checkmates" a single position without ending the game; it can make life "more tolerable" but cannot transform the conditions that make hiding necessary. By acknowledging the offending facts as the thing to be evaded, it certifies their persistence.

The **synthetic declaration** is constructive and collective. It does not negotiate within the imposed framework but supplies a new one — "an alternative framework that transforms the opposing facts" — so that "only a synthetic alternative vision will transform raw surveillance capitalism in favor of a digital future that we can call home" (`Directly stated`, Ch.11). Zuboff illustrates the pair through the history of the Berlin Wall: the seventy-one tunnels dug beneath the city were "testament to the necessity of counter-declarations, but they did not bring down the wall or the power that sustained it" (`Directly stated`, Ch.11). The synthetic declaration arrived instead as a transformation of certainty itself — the moment the border officials, asked "Will you let us pass?," answered "We will." The change was not an evasion of the wall but the dissolution of the authority that held it in place. "It's the wall, not the tunnels, that requires our attention" (`Directly stated`, Ch.17).

In Chapter 17 ("The Right to Sanctuary") the distinction returns as a diagnosis of the present. Absent synthetic declarations, the counter-declaration metastasizes into a self-defeating spiral: "In the absence of synthetic declarations that secure the road to a human future, the intolerability of glass life turns us toward a societal arms race of counter-declarations in which we search for and embrace increasingly complex ways to hide in our own lives" (`Directly stated`, Ch.17). Zuboff reads the "art and science of hiding" — anti-facial-recognition visors, signal-blocking garments, the 3-D-printed prosthetic masks of artists like Leo Selvaggio — as precisely such counter-declarations: brilliant, poignant, and ultimately an adaptation where outrage is needed. "Like every counter-declaration, hiding risks becomes an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage… Tunnels under this wall are not enough. This wall must come down" (`Directly stated`, Ch.17). The constructive alternative is institutional, not individual: "We need synthetic declarations that are institutionalized in new centers of democratic power, expertise, and contest that challenge today's asymmetries of knowledge and power" (`Directly stated`, Ch.17).

The pair is therefore the strategic mirror of [[Conquest by Declaration]]: declarations made surveillance capitalism's facts, and only counter-vailing declarations can unmake them — but the form of the response decides its scope. The counter-declaration operates inside the declared reality and so cannot escape it; the synthetic declaration changes the rules of the game and so can. The aesthetic stakes are notable for a curatorial reading: Zuboff treats anti-surveillance art as a *counter*-declaration whose nobility is also its limit, "a message in a bottle cast from the front lines of mourning and revulsion" rather than the systemic transformation it points toward. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (Ch.11, Ch.17). The originating and only vault use. Zuboff coins the pair ("what I call the counter-declaration… what I call the synthetic declaration") as the action-side complement to [[Conquest by Declaration]], extending John Searle's theory of the declarative speech act from how surveillance capitalism's facts are *made* to how they may be *withdrawn*. The terms are normative: the counter-declaration is the form she warns against settling into; the synthetic declaration is the collective, institutionalized response she advocates.

## Opposing Concepts

- The counter-declaration and the synthetic declaration **oppose each other** as defensive evasion versus transformative reframing — the central internal opposition of the note.
- [[Conquest by Declaration]] — the pair is the mirror-opposite move: where conquest *imposes* facts by declaration, these *withdraw agreement* to them.

## Questions It Raises

- Where is the line between a counter-declaration and a synthetic one — can a defensive practice (encryption, a privacy law) ever scale into a transformation of the framework, or does it always "leave the opposing facts intact"?
- If anti-surveillance art is, in Zuboff's reading, a counter-declaration that "risks becomes an adaptation," what would a *synthetic* artistic or curatorial intervention look like — one that changes the game rather than hides from it?
- What concretely are the "new centers of democratic power, expertise, and contest" that could institutionalize a synthetic declaration, and who issues "We will" in a network where authority is distributed?
- Does the Berlin Wall analogy hold when the "wall" is not a state border but the economic imperatives of private firms — can a synthetic declaration topple a market the way it toppled a regime?
