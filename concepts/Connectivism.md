# Connectivism

## Brief Definition

Connectivism is Roy Ascott's name for both a worldview — the "connectivist paradigm," which holds that everything is connected and everything interacts with and affects everything else — and his proposed replacement for the word "art" when speaking of telematic art practice, such that all artists working with computer-mediated systems are, by definition, connectivists.

## Longer Explanation

Connectivism is the most general name Ascott gives to the metaphysics underwriting his entire telematic project. In "Back to Nature II" (ch. 28) he states it as a principle shared across science and art: "Second-order cybernetics and second order nature share in what can be called the connectivist paradigm, which holds that everything is connected, everything interacts with and affects everything else." He glosses it as "this connectedness, this undivided wholeness, unmediated action-at-a-distance, capable of transcending the laws of space and time with non-local interaction" — an idea he calls "as old, as traditional as oriental religions and as new as the quantum physics of David Bohm." The connectivist paradigm is reflected, for Ascott, in the telematic environment of computer-mediated networks: communication that is itself "non-local" and asynchronous. [Directly stated]

Crucially, Ascott keeps connectivism (in art) DISTINCT from connectionism (in science), even as he derives both from the one paradigm. Connectionism is, in his words, the work on "the microstructure of cognition, known as connectionism, parallel distributed processing, or neural networks," which "developed out of a re-evaluation of some of the earliest ideas of cybernetics, of neural nets and the Perceptron, rejecting the linear, symbolic systems of the old AI." His summary formula is exact: "The connectivist paradigm thus embraces both connectionism in science and what it is appropriate to call connectivism in art." Connectionism is the scientific modeling of mind; connectivism is its artistic counterpart. The two are siblings under one paradigm, not synonyms — connectivism is not a theory of neural nets. [Directly stated]

The second, narrower sense of connectivism is terminological and polemical: it is Ascott's proposed substitute for the word "art." In Note 1 he explains the motive — "The term 'art' is too heavy with the meanings given to it by both romanticism and classicism, as well as, of course, by modernism, and too deeply embedded in the notion of the individual creator and the reactive rather than interactive viewer" — and concludes that "it may be useful for a while to avoid using the term 'art' as far as possible in these circumstances, and to use the term 'connectivism' when we are referring to telematic art practice. All art practice involving computer-mediated systems and electronic media of whatever complexion is by definition telematic, thus all artists engaged in the field are connectivists." Connectivism thus folds directly into [[Telematic Art]]: to be telematic and to be a connectivist are the same. Ascott is careful, in the same note, to deny that connectivism is "yet another 'ism,' another art movement for the history books" — it names a whole new field of creative endeavour, not a style. [Directly stated]

As a programme, connectivism prizes interactivity over reception and reciprocity over transmission: it "calls for interactivity, reciprocity, and negotiation in the act of communication," giving rise to what Ascott calls "implicate meaning" after Bohm's implicate order, where "each quantum of meaning is many-layered, carrying within it a multiplicity of possible semantic trajectories." Much computer art fails this test for Ascott — "this work is not connectivist in its attitudes, and it is not telematic in its use of media," because "the computer is still too much contained, finitely located, isolated as a black box." Connectivity, for Ascott, is "a principle of life," and the connectivists' horizon is a "rematerialization of art" that marries the immaterial (electronic/telematic) and the material (nanotechnology, molecular engineering, [[Artificial Life]]) into "an holistic environment of mind and matter, as much spiritual as material" — the [[Network Consciousness]] and re-enchanted nature his telematics points toward. [Strongly implied]

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and uses "connectivism" in two linked registers: (1) the *connectivist paradigm*, the worldview that everything is connected and interacts with everything else, shared by second-order cybernetics and "second-order nature"; and (2) connectivism as the replacement-term for "art" in telematic practice, so that all artists of computer-mediated systems are connectivists. He explicitly distinguishes connectivism (in art) from connectionism (neural nets / PDP, in science), holding both to be expressions of the one paradigm.

## Questions It Raises

- If connectivism is meant to *replace* the word "art," does relabeling resolve Ascott's complaint about the individual-creator / passive-viewer baggage of "art," or merely relocate it onto a new term?
- Is the "connectivist paradigm" ("everything is connected, everything interacts with and affects everything else") an empirical claim, a metaphysics, or — given its kinship with Bohm, Sheldrake's morphic resonance, and Lovelock's Gaia — a re-enchantment that "second-order cybernetics" lends a scientific veneer?
- Ascott yokes connectivism (art) to connectionism (neural nets) under one paradigm; does the analogy survive scrutiny, or does it borrow the legitimacy of cognitive science for an artistic worldview?
- If "all art practice involving computer-mediated systems... is by definition telematic," does connectivism include — or does it indict, as a "black box" replay — the very digital art Ascott says "is not connectivist in its attitudes"?
