# Algorithmic Determinism

## Brief Definition

Algorithmic determinism — a term [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] takes from Google researcher Vyacheslav Polonski — is the condition in which predictive systems, built from past behavior, "trap" people "within the mirror of their digital reflection," so that the forecast is not a neutral reading of a fixed future but a behavior-modifying loop that helps bring about the very outcome it predicts.

## Longer Explanation

O'Gieblyn introduces the term at the point where prediction stops being a description and becomes a force. Because predictive models "rely on past behavior and decisions—not just of the individual but of others who share the same demographics," she writes, "people become trapped within the mirror of their digital reflection, a process that Google researcher Vyacheslav Polonski calls 'algorithmic determinism.'" The crucial step before this is the reduction of the person to data: the "metadata—the shell of human experience—becomes part of a feedback loop that then actively modifies real behavior." The self is first recoded as [[The Body as Data|external, measurable states]] — "your zip code," "the tone of your voice," "whom you're emailing and how often" — on the cybernetic premise "that a person can be described purely in terms of pattern and probabilities, without any concern for interiority." Stripped of interiority, the digital reflection is fed back as a constraint on the living subject.

Her canonical example is predictive policing. Systems like PredPol "designate in red boxes particular neighborhoods where crime is likely to occur" and "gather their predictions from historical crime data, which means that they often send officers to precisely the same poor neighborhoods they patrolled when they were guided by their intuition alone." The prediction then manufactures the data that confirms it: officers entering a red box expect to find crime, so they do, and "the person is a suspect because the algorithm identified the area as one where suspects might be located." O'Gieblyn (citing Jackie Wang's *Carceral Capitalism*) stresses that the difference from ordinary intuition is the borrowed authority of "empirical evidence," which "engender[s] confirmation bias in a way that intuition does not." The forecast launders its own circularity as objectivity.

The deepest stakes of the term are not technical but metaphysical, and O'Gieblyn frames them through her abiding obsession with free will. Her signature formulation distills the whole problem into a single question: "To what extent does the act of prediction enact the very fate it foresees?" She places this in a long lineage of determinisms — Laplace's intellect that could "predict any future event," Calvin's God who "not only foresees but controls" the future — but locates its distinctly modern form in the predictive analytics that "actively modif[y] real behavior." The accuracy of prediction comes to seem proof "that human behavior is deterministic," even as that accuracy is partly an artifact of the prediction's own feedback. This is the free-will-versus-determinism problem returning in a social key — not in the soul or the brain but in the loop between a profiled population and the institutions acting on its profile.

Algorithmic determinism is therefore best read as the *social-political* expression of the same disenchanted picture that drives the rest of O'Gieblyn's book. Once the self is rendered as [[Information]] and [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern and probability]], the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|autonomous self who "knows what is best for herself"]] is undermined from the outside: not by an argument about consciousness but by systems that "predetermine[]" fate. The Philadelphia probationer Darnell Gates names the dread exactly — "How do you win against a computer that is built to stop you? How do you stop something that predetermines your fate?" — recognizing, as O'Gieblyn puts it, "the shadowy line between prediction and determination."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — adopts Polonski's term and gives it her signature formulation ("To what extent does the act of prediction enact the very fate it foresees?"), reading it as the contemporary, secular reincarnation of the predestination problem (Calvin, Weber) and as the political face of disenchantment. For her the danger is not chiefly inaccuracy but the loop: prediction "actively modifies real behavior."
- **Vyacheslav Polonski** — the Google researcher credited with coining "algorithmic determinism" to name the process by which people "become trapped within the mirror of their digital reflection." (Forward-reference; O'Gieblyn cites him rather than developing his account at length.)
- *Adjacent framings O'Gieblyn marshals:* Shoshana Zuboff's "behavioral futures" and surveillance capitalism (prediction as a market in "raw material" extracted from behavior); Yuval Noah Harari's "Dataism" ("Listen to the algorithms! They know how you feel"), which O'Gieblyn reads as the explicit announcement of the end of liberal humanism. These are cross-author forward-references, not yet vault notes.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — the underlying cybernetic mechanism (Hayles's sense). Algorithmic determinism is what that loop *does socially* when one of its arms is a profiled population and the other is an institution acting on the profile. Cross-linked, not merged: the loop is the device; algorithmic determinism is the political-existential predicament it produces.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the precondition: the person must first be reduced to "the shell of human experience," external states without interiority, before the digital reflection can be fed back as a constraint.
- [[Information]] — the form to which the predicted subject is reduced; the content-free, interiority-free description that prediction operates on.
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — the cybernetic ontology ("pattern and probabilities") that licenses describing and forecasting a person without reference to inner states.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the broader metaphysics; algorithmic determinism is its social-political symptom, the Archimedean view that "cannot account for human agency."
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the reflexive endpoint: to be predetermined and opaque to oneself "is to become a machine," a "black box."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the directly threatened figure. Algorithmic determinism dissolves the autonomous chooser who "knows what is best for herself"; prediction "predetermines [her] fate" from outside. O'Gieblyn (via Harari) reads this as marking "the end of liberal humanism."

## Questions It Raises

- **Meaning / agency:** if "the act of prediction enact[s] the very fate it foresees," is the predicted person still an agent, or merely a "member of a data set"? Where does the loop leave the difference between a choice and a forecast fulfilling itself?
- **The body as data:** prediction works precisely because it forgoes interiority — it estimates "the internal states of the human" only indirectly, through "external states that can be measured." What is lost, ethically and ontologically, when the self acted upon is only its data-shell? (See [[The Body as Data]].)
- **Technology and perception:** does the red box change what the officer *perceives* — manufacturing the crime it predicts through confirmation bias — and if so, can any prediction built on past behavior ever be a neutral "forecast" rather than a self-confirming intervention?
- **Posthumanism:** if "Dataism" can "know the customer better than she knows herself," and we "defer to machine wisdom" on whom to marry or vote for, is the resulting subject still human in the liberal sense — or a node whose preferences are read off the clickstream? (See [[Posthumanism]], [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]].)
- **The free-will problem, socialized:** O'Gieblyn ties this to predestination (Calvin, Weber). Is algorithmic determinism a genuinely new condition, or the predestination anxiety "repeating a historical pattern" — fate relocated from God's decree to the proprietary model?
