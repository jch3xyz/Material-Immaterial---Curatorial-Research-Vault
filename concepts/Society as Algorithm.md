# Society as Algorithm

## Brief Definition

Harari's thesis that writing let humans organise entire societies in an algorithmic fashion — each person becoming only a small step in a huge algorithm, so that it is "the algorithm as a whole," i.e. "the system" and not any flesh-and-blood individual, that makes the important decisions; this, for Harari, is the essence of bureaucracy.

## Longer Explanation

In **The Storytellers**, Harari turns the word *algorithm* — which he had earlier used for emotions and brains — onto the *social* scale. The hinge is the invention of writing and money in Sumer about 5,000 years ago, which "broke the data-processing limitations of the human brain." Once stories, taxes and laws could be stored "on tablets and papyri rather than in human heads," cooperation no longer had to fit inside any single skull. Harari states the consequence flatly: "Writing has thus enabled humans to organise entire societies in an algorithmic fashion." Reusing his own definition of an algorithm as "a methodical set of steps that can be used to make calculations, resolve problems and reach decisions," he draws the contrast between two kinds of society: "In illiterate societies people make all calculations and decisions in their heads. In literate societies people are organised into networks, so that each person is only a small step in a huge algorithm, and it is the algorithm as a whole that makes the important decisions. This is the essence of bureaucracy." `Directly stated`

The decisive move is that decision-making migrates *out of the person* and *into the network*. Harari's worked example is the modern hospital: the receptionist hands you a standard form, the nurse compares your answers with regulations, the doctor "follows a strict protocol," specialists analyse results "according to well-known statistical databases" — a relay in which no one node decides and the protocol decides. The point of the structure is precisely that the human is interchangeable: "This algorithmic structure ensures that it doesn't really matter who is the receptionist, nurse or doctor on duty. Their personality type, their political opinions and their momentary moods are irrelevant." From this Harari extracts what he calls the algorithmic ideal: "According to the algorithmic ideal, your fate is in the hands of 'the system', and not in the hands of the flesh-and-blood mortals who happen to man this or that post." `Directly stated`

Harari is explicit that this is not modern: "What's true of hospitals is also true of armies, prisons, schools, corporations – and ancient kingdoms." Ancient Egypt is his deep case. Pharaoh "hardly lifted a finger"; the biological pharaoh "was of little importance," and "the practical work of administering Egypt was left to thousands of literate officials." There, too, "most decisions were made not by a single wise person, but by a network of officials linked together through papyri and stone inscriptions," and it was that network — not any wise ruler — that "restructured human society and reshaped the natural world," digging the canals that made Lake Fayum and raising the pyramids. The technological gap between a Bronze Age kingdom and a modern hospital is vast, but for Harari "the algorithmic principle was the same." `Directly stated`

This is the **social** precursor to the biological thesis [[Organisms Are Algorithms]], and the two must be kept distinct. There, the *individual organism* is itself an assemblage of biochemical algorithms; here, the *individual person stays whole* but is enrolled as a single replaceable step inside a larger, supra-personal algorithm carried by writing. The first is a claim about what a person *is* (substrate, biology, the inside of the body); the second is a claim about how persons are *organised* (bureaucracy, the network, the inscription that outlives the brain). They rhyme — both relocate decision away from the sovereign individual — but Harari reaches the social version through the history of writing and money, not through evolutionary biology. The bridge between them is interpretive: the same author who dissolves the inner self into algorithms also dissolves the outer society into one. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — Applies *algorithm* to whole literate societies: writing turns a population into "a huge algorithm" in which each person is "only a small step," so that "the system" decides and the post-holder is interchangeable. His variant is historical-bureaucratic and substrate-agnostic about *people* (any nurse will do), anchored in concrete institutions — the hospital, the army, the school, the corporation, and ancient Egypt's papyrus bureaucracy. He calls this "the essence of bureaucracy" and treats it as old (Sumer, the Nile Valley) rather than a modern computing phenomenon.
- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — Gives the thesis its for-profit, computational, twenty-first-century form. Where Harari's bureaucratic algorithm ran on human steps linked by papyri, Zuboff's instrumentarian society runs on the machine hive: "Machine learning is rendered here as a collective mind—a hive mind—in which each element learns and operates in concert with every other element," and "the machine hive becomes the role model for a new human hive." The algorithmic principle is now explicitly normative — "we should be more like machines" — and the supra-personal "system" that decides is [[Big Other]], directing human and machine action toward "guaranteed outcomes." Harari's interchangeable, irrelevant post-holder becomes Zuboff's "individual freedom… forfeit to collective knowledge and action."

## Related Concepts

- [[Distributed Cognition]] — the structural cousin: cognition/decision spread across a network of agents and inscriptions rather than housed in one mind. Harari's "huge algorithm" of clerks linked by papyri is a historical instance of cognition distributed across people and written media; writing is the medium that makes the distribution possible.
- [[Narrativity]] — the precondition: the algorithmic society runs on shared fictions (gods, money, nations, corporations) that Harari calls "stories." The network can only cohere because its members believe the same intersubjective story; the algorithm processes and enforces a narrative.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the modern intensification: where Harari's bureaucratic algorithm still ran on human steps, the contemporary version hands the steps to computers. "Society as algorithm" is the analogue ancestor of governance by electronic algorithm.
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the *biological* counterpart in the same book. Distinct in scope (the self vs. the social order) but structurally parallel: both relocate decision-making away from the free individual. Relate, do not merge.
- [[Biopolitics]] — adjacent register: the administration of populations through documents, statistics and protocols. Harari's papyrus bureaucracy and the school's "precise marks" are mechanisms by which a system measures and manages its members.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the self-authoring, sovereign individual whose decisions count. The algorithmic-society ideal explicitly empties this figure: it "doesn't really matter who" mans the post, and your fate rests with "the system," not with the "flesh-and-blood mortals." The individual decider is precisely what the algorithm makes irrelevant.

## Questions It Raises

- If "the system" decides and every post-holder is interchangeable, where does responsibility lodge — can an algorithmic society be held accountable, or does accountability dissolve along with the individual decider? (Harari's Sousa Mendes case — one consul disobeying the protocol — sits on exactly this fault line.)
- Is bureaucratic interchangeability a gain (your fate no longer hostage to one official's mood or prejudice) or a loss (no one is answerable, and "what's written on your form" outranks the truth)?
- Where is the line between the *social* algorithm (people as steps) and the *biological* one (the person as an algorithm)? Does treating society as an algorithm make it easier to then treat its members as algorithms — and is that slide a discovery or a metaphor running ahead of itself?
- If decisions migrate from heads into networks of inscription, what happens to embodiment and lived judgement — does the "algorithmic ideal" require the body and the person to be subtracted from decision?
- When the algorithm's steps become machines rather than clerks, and the "system" becomes a sensate computational apparatus owned by a firm, does Harari's substrate-agnostic bureaucracy turn into Zuboff's instrumentarian power — and is the for-profit machine hive the same algorithmic society Harari describes, or a different and more total one?
