# Religion as Social Order

## Brief Definition

Harari's functional, god-agnostic redefinition of religion: anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures by claiming that human norms and values reflect superhuman laws — a definition by *social function* rather than by belief in deities.

## Longer Explanation

In "The Odd Couple" Harari sets out to clear away what he calls "faulty definitions of religion." Religion, he argues, is not superstition, not spirituality, not belief in supernatural powers, and not even belief in gods. Each of these conventional definitions fails: people never call their own cherished beliefs superstitions; demons, for those who believe in them, are part of nature rather than apart from it; and gods are inessential, since "religion is created by humans rather than by gods, and it is defined by its social function rather than by the existence of deities" (Directly stated). What remains, once the gods are subtracted, is a function: "Religion is anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures. It legitimises human norms and values by arguing that they reflect superhuman laws" (Directly stated).

The decisive move in this redefinition is to detach "superhuman" from "supernatural." A religion need not appeal to a god; it need only assert that humans are bound by a moral order they did not invent and cannot revise at will. Theistic traditions locate that order in divine command (the Jewish father invoking God's law against pork); non-theistic ones locate it in *natural* law. This is what lets Harari enroll a striking range of modern ideologies as religions: by his functional claim, "from Buddhism and Daoism to Nazism, communism and liberalism," all "argue that the superhuman laws are natural laws, and not the creation of this or that god" (Directly stated). The SS officer explaining racial extermination as obedience to "the laws of nature," and the liberal MP explaining universal human rights as flowing from a shared human "nature," are — on Harari's analysis — performing the same legitimating gesture as the devout believer. The enrollment of Nazism, communism and liberalism as religions is **Harari's functional claim, advanced to expose a shared structure of legitimation; it is not the vault's position** (Directly stated, as Harari's claim).

Harari sharpens the definition by contrasting religion with spirituality: "Religion is a deal, whereas spirituality is a journey" (Directly stated). Religion cements the worldly order with a "well-defined contract" of predetermined goals; spirituality challenges that order and walks toward an unknown destination — which is why, "for religions, spirituality is a dangerous threat." This is also why the concept is properly named *social order*: "Religion is interested above all in order. It aims to create and maintain the social structure" (Directly stated), in contrast to science, which "is interested above all in power."

The redefinition does load-bearing work in Harari's larger argument. Because science "always needs religious assistance in order to create viable human institutions" — there being "no scientific method for determining how humans ought to behave" — modern history is best read "as the process of formulating a deal between science and one particular religion — namely, humanism" (Directly stated). Humanism thus becomes the covenant partner of science: the religion whose dogmas science is enlisted to implement rather than to question. This is the hinge by which the functional definition of religion opens onto the book's account of [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] and its eventual successor, a "new post-humanist religion."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (*Homo Deus*) — Defines religion functionally and god-agnostically: any system that grants *superhuman legitimacy* to social structures by claiming they reflect superhuman (divine *or* natural) laws. On this definition Nazism, communism and liberalism count as religions, and humanism is the religion that strikes a covenant with modern science.
- **Max Weber** *(forward-stub)* — Resonant precursor: Harari's instrumental, order-and-power reading of religion as the legitimation of social structures echoes the Weberian sociology of religion and its concern with the legitimation of domination and the rationalization of value-spheres. The connection is a thematic resonance noted by the vault, not a citation Harari makes in this chapter (Speculative connection). To be realized if Weber is ingested.

## Related Concepts

Cross-author resonance on religion-as-social-form.
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — The specific "religion" Harari says modernity wed to science; the chief application of this functional definition.
- [[Evolutionary Humanism]] — A second humanist creed Harari analyzes; on this definition its appeal to "the laws of nature" (Harari's examples include Nazi natural-selection rhetoric) is itself a religious legitimation.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Transhumanism]] / post-humanist religion — Harari frames the modern science–humanism covenant as disintegrating, to be replaced by "some new post-humanist religion." The post-humanist deal stands as the successor that breaks the humanist social order this concept describes.

## Questions It Raises

- If "superhuman" is decoupled from "supernatural," does the functional definition become so broad that *any* shared normative order counts as a religion — and does that broadness illuminate or dissolve the category?
- Harari attributes the enrollment of Nazism, communism and liberalism as religions to a shared structure of legitimation. Is the shared structure a genuine equivalence, or does flattening these into "religions" obscure morally decisive differences the chapter itself dramatizes?
- If religion supplies the "ought" that science cannot, and humanism is the religion science serves, what happens to social order when the humanist covenant "crumbles"?
- How far does the Weberian resonance extend — is Harari's "order vs. power" split between religion and science a restatement of Weber's value-spheres, or a different settlement?
