# The Planetary Collegium

## Brief Definition

The Planetary Collegium is Roy Ascott's name for the emergent, non-hierarchical, non-linear, intrinsically interactive global learning organism arising bottom-up within the Internet — a community of equal access to knowledge concerned with art and the advancement of learning, which Ascott offers as "a paradigm of the twenty-first-century interversity."

## Longer Explanation

In the 1994 essay "The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-Biological Era," Ascott argues that traditional art education — organized around the material object, the canvas-on-its-easel, and the atelier — is in terminal decline because the very nature of art has changed: art is now invisible, distributed, interactive, and emergent, "art as a form of behaviour" rather than the arrangement of forms. The pedagogy must therefore be rebuilt to match. Borrowing the Artificial Life program's principle of bottom-up, distributed, local determination of behaviour, Ascott names the new model the **Planetary Collegium**: a learning organism that is, "by definition, non-hierarchical, non-linear, and intrinsically interactive" — *Directly stated*. Each member holds "more or less equal power and authority both in access to knowledge and in the means of its reconfiguration and distribution" — a flat, distributed economy of knowledge rather than the centralized authority of the book, the academy, or the ivory tower.

The decisive claim is one of cultivation versus engineering. The Collegium "cannot be planned and implemented top-down," because "it is already emerging, bottom-up from the infinity of interactions within the Internet" — *Directly stated*. The proper response is horticultural rather than architectural: to "cultivate this new organism as it emerges, to feed it, support it, nourish it." This grounds the cross-cutting [[Bottom-up vs Top-down]] tension that runs through Ascott's whole post-biological program: the emergent, cultivated organism stands against the planned, imposed institution, exactly as the bottom-up logic of [[Emergence]] stands against "the classical top-down imposition of ideology or aesthetic." The Collegium is the institutional embodiment of the [[Network Consciousness]] Ascott theorizes elsewhere — the "constructions of distributed mind" given a place to learn ([[Distributed Cognition]]).

Ascott frames the Collegium through a recurring historical periodization of the seat of learning. In the "moist" essay he writes that "just as historically the church as the centre of learning had to give way to the secular university, so now the university has to give way to new forms of learning set within the domain of cyberspace and moistmedia"; in the technoetic-terms glossary the same succession is compressed — "just as the monastery gave way to the university in mediaeval Europe, so now the university will give way to the Planetary Collegium" — *Directly stated*. The Collegium is thus positioned as the next epochal institution of knowledge, not a mere upgrade of the existing one. It is also where Ascott's market anxiety sharpens: if artists do not seize the digitalization of education, "the politicians, merchants, and entrepreneurs will," a fear that grounds the [[Art vs Commerce]] tension — utopian network optimism shadowed by the threat of corporate enclosure.

In Ascott's later vocabulary the Collegium becomes "inherently moist, a paradigmatic embodiment of the nascent moist culture" — "an example of visionary pragmatism that seeks to develop advanced research in the interspace between artistic, technological, scientific, and spiritual disciplines" — *Directly stated*. (The real-world realization is Ascott's CAiiA / European Cyberspace Collegium, later institutionalized as the Planetary Collegium research network.) The Collegium thereby joins Ascott's [[The Post-Biological Era|post-biological]] turn toward consciousness and connectivity, and its supporting fabric is the [[Cyber-estate]] — "the interfaces, networks, circuits and sensors of telematic educational structures."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the originator and sole author of the concept. He uses it across three registers: (1) the 1994 manifesto, where it is the emergent bottom-up learning organism and "a paradigm of the twenty-first-century interversity"; (2) the "moist" essay, where it becomes the institutional embodiment of moist culture, succeeding the university as the university succeeded the church; (3) the technoetic glossary, where it is defined tersely as that which "promotes web wisdom and online learning." Across all three the constant is the cultivated-not-planned, distributed-equal-access organism.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Bottom-up vs Top-down]] — the Collegium is the bottom-up, cultivated, emergent pole against the top-down, planned, imposed institution; this opposition is constitutive of the concept.
- [[Art vs Commerce]] — the artists' collaborative, originality-driven learning models versus the "cynical manipulations of the market" and the corporate takeover of online education.

## Questions It Raises

- Can an emergent, bottom-up learning organism be "cultivated" by deliberate institutional founding (CAiiA, the actual Planetary Collegium) without becoming the top-down structure Ascott rejects? Is cultivation itself a covert form of planning?
- Ascott's egalitarian "more or less equal power and authority" presumes equal access to the Internet — how does the concept survive the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]] and the deep inequalities of network access?
- If the Collegium succeeds the university as the university succeeded the church, what authority and what loss attend each succession — does the "fixed and frozen" authority of the book really yield to something more democratic, or merely to a different distribution of control?
- Ascott embraces the very planetary connectivity that Han diagnoses as the [[Hell of the Same]] and the [[Digital Panopticon]]; is the Collegium's optimism about distributed mind defensible against the surveillance and conformity critiques?
