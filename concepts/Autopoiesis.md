# Autopoiesis

## Brief Definition

Autopoiesis is Maturana and Varela's name for a living system understood as a circularly organized network that continuously produces the very components that produce it — a system whose only product is its own organization, and for which, in Hayles's reading, "information without a body does not exist."

## Longer Explanation

In *How We Became Posthuman*, Hayles treats autopoiesis as the conceptual center of gravity of the **second wave of cybernetics** (roughly 1960–1985), the mature theory that the Chilean neurophysiologist [[Humberto Maturana]] and his collaborator [[Francisco Varela]] built out of the [[Reflexivity|reflexive]] insight that "the observer, like the frog, does not so much discern preexisting systems as create them through the very act of observation." A living system is not defined by what it does, nor by reproduction or genetic code, but by a self-referential loop of production: "The living organization is a circular organization which secures the production or maintenance of the components that specify it in such a manner that the product of their functioning is the very same organization that produces them." Because this closure is what makes a system alive, "life and autopoiesis are coextensive with one another" — an amoeba and a human share the same organization (the conservation of autopoiesis) even as their *structure* differs from moment to moment.

The decisive move for Hayles's larger argument is that autopoietic systems are **informationally closed**. Information, coding, messages, feedback, teleology, even time and causality are not intrinsic to the living system; they are "inferences drawn by an observer" who stands in a "descriptive domain" outside the system and projects them onto its circular operations. This is why autopoiesis matters to the book's project: it "swerves from the trajectory traced in chapters 3 and 4 with regard to information, insisting that information without a body does not exist other than as an inference drawn by an observer." Where first-wave [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] worked to divest information of its body and treat the human as an information-processing machine (see [[The Body as Data]]), autopoiesis re-anchors information in "the physically embodied processes constituting all living entities," restoring the materiality that the first wave had abstracted away (see [[Embodiment vs Information]], [[Material vs Immaterial]]).

Autopoiesis also reconstructs **autonomy** without grounding it in self-possession. Hayles reads it against C. B. Macpherson's possessive individualism: where classic liberalism makes the subject autonomous because it owns itself, autopoietic theory makes the living system autonomous because it instantiates organizational closure. "Closure and recursivity, then, play the foundational role in autopoietic theory that self-possession played in classic liberal theory." Autonomy becomes a property of a self-producing network "closed on itself, by itself," not the self-ownership of the [[Posthumanism|liberal humanist]] proprietor. The system secures its own boundaries (Varela modeled this with computer simulations called tessellation automata, where the breakdown of a boundary "is equivalent to death") and structurally couples to an environment rather than representing it.

Finally, autopoiesis "points toward the **posthuman** even as it reinscribes the autonomy and individuality of the liberal subject." It does this by **demoting consciousness**. Maturana treats thinking as a secondary effect that arises when an autopoietic system recursively interacts with its own representations, and self-consciousness as a still narrower, purely linguistic effect: "consciousness for him becomes a epiphenomenon rather than a defining characteristic of the human as an autopoietic entity." The theory thus quietly dethrones the rational mind as the seat of identity — in Hayles's image, "the liberal subject, although more than ever an autonomous individual, is literally losing its mind as the seat of identity." This is the hinge by which a theory rooted in liberal autonomy nonetheless opens onto [[Posthumanism|posthumanism]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Humberto Maturana]]** — originator of the theory (developed 1960–1968), insistent that everything be subordinated to autopoiesis: reproduction, evolution, and genetic code are all "logically and practically subordinate." He pushes the closure of the system to its limit ("we do not see what we do not see, and what we do not see does not exist"), and in Hayles's critique the closure that gives the theory its "epistemological muscle" also becomes its Achilles' heel — it struggles to account for transformation, language, and evolution, vaporizing historical contingency by "continuously circulating it within the space of its interlocking assumptions."
- **[[Francisco Varela]]** — co-formulator who later diverges, restricting autopoiesis to "the domain of cells and animals" (refusing Maturana's extension to social systems), conceding a "non-naive" role for information, and moving toward *enaction* and embodied action — perception "constituted through perceptually guided actions" rather than mere triggering — and, drawing on Buddhist philosophy in *The Embodied Mind*, toward dissolving the self rather than fortifying its closure.
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — reads autopoiesis as a pivotal but double-edged moment: it rescues embodiment and the observer that first-wave cybernetics obscured, yet its relentless circularity understates the transformative force of language and embodied action. She uses it to mark the shift in the grounding of subjectivity from self-possession to organizational closure.

## Related Concepts

- [[Reflexivity]] — the parent move; autopoiesis is the second wave's attempt to make reflexivity (the observer included in the system) rigorous and operational rather than solipsistic.
- [[Systems Theory]] — autopoiesis's social extension; Maturana's claim that the paradigmatic autopoietic "cyborg" is the state, not Robocop or Terminator, feeds the application of the theory to social systems (a move Varela resisted).
- [[Cybernetics]] — autopoiesis is the defining theory of cybernetics' second wave, redefining life by autopoietic process rather than by behavior.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — in the autopoietic account "there are no messages circulating in feedback loops"; feedback is an observer's inference, not intrinsic to the system.
- [[Embodiment]] — autopoiesis "draws its strength precisely from its emphasis on" embodiment and the observer, re-grounding information in physical process.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Body as Data]] — autopoiesis directly contests the first-wave abstraction of information into a bodiless, substrate-independent flow; for autopoiesis the body is not a carrier of separable data but the precondition of information's existence.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — autopoiesis is Hayles's clearest counterweight to the disembodiment of information: it insists information without a body is only an observer's inference.

## Questions It Raises

- If "information without a body does not exist other than as an inference drawn by an observer," does autopoiesis refute the dream of uploading or substrate-independent information — or merely relocate it to the observer's domain? How far does it actually rescue materiality (see [[Material vs Immaterial]])?
- Autopoiesis grounds autonomy in organizational closure rather than self-possession, yet Hayles shows it "reinscribes" liberal autonomy even as it contests it. Is the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] subject it points toward genuinely post-liberal, or liberal individualism rewritten in biological terms?
- By demoting consciousness to an epiphenomenon, does autopoiesis liberate us from the [[Posthumanism|liberal humanist]] privileging of the rational mind, or does it merely substitute a new essence — the self-producing network — for the old one?
- Hayles charges that autopoietic closure understates how language and the environment actively shape the embodied subject. Can a theory of the [[The Body as Data|body as information]] or of embodiment be both closed (autonomous) and open (transformable) at once?
- Is organizational closure a feature of living systems "as such," or only "how a human observer perceives living systems, including itself"? The question "lies coiled around the brainstem of autopoietic theory" — and unresolved, it destabilizes the very embodiment it claims to secure.
