# The Coup from Above

## Brief Definition

Shoshana Zuboff's closing political diagnosis: surveillance capitalism as an expropriation of critical human rights best understood not as the overthrow of a state but as the overthrow of *the people's sovereignty* — a market-driven *coup de gens* that is a prominent force in the perilous drift toward democratic deconsolidation.

## Longer Explanation

*The coup from above* is the political thesis that crowns *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*. It appears first as the eighth and final clause of Zuboff's framing definition — surveillance capitalism is "[a]n expropriation of critical human rights that is best understood as a coup from above: an overthrow of the people's sovereignty" — and is forward-anchored in Chapter 1, where she writes that "surveillance capitalism is best described as a coup from above, not an overthrow of the state but rather an overthrow of the people's sovereignty and a prominent force in the perilous drift toward democratic deconsolidation that now threatens Western liberal democracies." It is only fully developed in Part IV's culminating chapter, "A Coup from Above," after the book has assembled its account of [[Behavioral Surplus]], [[Instrumentarian Power]], and [[Big Other]]. `Directly stated`

The decisive move is Zuboff's refusal of the classic political vocabulary. This is "not a coup d'état in the classic sense but rather a *coup de gens*: an overthrow of the people concealed as the technological Trojan horse that is Big Other." What is seized is not the apparatus of the state but the substrate of self-governance itself: "[o]n the strength of its annexation of human experience, this coup achieves exclusive concentrations of knowledge and power that sustain privileged influence over the division of learning in society: the privatization of the central principle of social ordering in the twenty-first century." The coup is therefore epistemic before it is coercive — it works by hijacking [[The Division of Learning in Society]], not by seizing the palace. `Directly stated`

Crucially, the coup needs no despot. "It is a form of tyranny that feeds on people but is not of the people," ruling through [[Instrumentarian Power]] materialized in [[Big Other]], "which, like the ancient tyrant, exists out of mankind while paradoxically assuming human shape." Its instruments are not "the despot's whip" or "totalitarianism's camps and gulags" but "Big Other's reassuring messages and emoticons," the sensored shirt, the listening TV, "the book that reads you." In this way it "replaces legitimate contract, the rule of law, politics, and social trust with a new form of sovereignty and its privately administered regime of reinforcements" — a "bloodless coup" that "operates more like a taming." This distinguishes Zuboff's coup sharply from the [[Digital Panopticon]] of voluntary self-exposure (Han) and from the celebratory data-religion of [[Dataism]] (Harari/Han): Zuboff reads the same instrumented life as the *political* expropriation of sovereignty, an antidemocratic order, rather than as a disciplinary form or a worldview. `Directly stated`

The closing register is one of democratic peril and obligation. The coup is "antidemocratic and antiegalitarian," arriving "with democracy already on the ropes," "hollowing out democracy's meaning and muscle," and "incapacitating democracy at its roots." Among its "insults to democracy" Zuboff counts "the unauthorized expropriation of human experience," "the hijack of the division of learning in society," the abrogation of "the elemental right to the future tense" (see [[The Right to the Future Tense]]) "and the elemental right to sanctuary," and "the degradation of the self-determining individual as the fulcrum of democratic life." Yet the diagnosis is mobilizing rather than fatalistic: "Only 'we the people' can reverse this course, first by naming the unprecedented, then by mobilizing new forms of collaborative action." The coup from above is answered only from below. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins and owns the term. The coup from above (Latinized as *coup de gens*, "an overthrow of the people") is her name for the political stakes of [[Surveillance Capitalism]]: an expropriation of human rights and sovereignty rather than a state seizure, and a driver of democratic deconsolidation. It is the book's culminating political category and the lens through which she reframes the everyday conveniences of the connected life as a quiet constitutional rupture.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic order whose political form the coup names; the coup is what surveillance capitalism *does to democracy*.
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the kind of power that rules through the coup; "surveillance capitalism rules by instrumentarian power."
- [[Big Other]] — the "technological Trojan horse" in which the coup is concealed and materialized; the tyrant that "exists out of mankind."
- [[The Division of Learning in Society]] — what the coup privatizes; "the central principle of social ordering in the twenty-first century."
- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — among the elemental rights the coup abrogates.
- [[Surveillance]] — the broader cross-author field that surveillance capitalism's coup specifies as a political (not merely technical) condition.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Digital Panopticon]] — both describe instrumented, frictionless life, but Han's panopticon turns on *voluntary self-exposure* and discipline/control, whereas Zuboff's coup is a political *expropriation of sovereignty*; cross-linked, not identical.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the coup is waged through immaterial means (data, prediction, behavioral modification) yet seizes the most material of stakes: bodies, behavior, and the conditions of self-governance.

## Questions It Raises

- If the coup is "not an overthrow of the state but rather an overthrow of the people's sovereignty," what counter-power is even constitutionally available — can the state be an ally against it, or is it implicated?
- Is "coup" the right figure for a process that "feeds on people" with their apparent consent and is "celebrated as 'personalization'" — or does the metaphor of a coup understate the absence of a moment of seizure?
- How does Zuboff's *political* reading (deconsolidation, lost sovereignty) sit beside Han's *disciplinary* reading ([[Digital Panopticon]]) and Harari's *religious/epistemic* reading ([[Dataism]]) of the same instrumented life?
- If "[o]nly 'we the people' can reverse this course," what forms of "collaborative action" and "friction" could actually reassert sovereignty against an order built precisely on radical indifference?
