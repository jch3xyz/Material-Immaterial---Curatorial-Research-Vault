# Pseudo-needs

## Brief Definition

The needs manufactured by an economy that has become an end in itself: with the satisfaction of primary human needs displaced by "an incessant fabrication of pseudo-needs, all of which ultimately come down to the single pseudo-need of maintaining the reign of the autonomous economy" (th. 51).

## Longer Explanation

In *The Society of the Spectacle* the pseudo-need is the form desire takes once the economy has "triumphed" as an independent power and lost its footing in scarcity. Debord's argument in thesis 51 turns on a substitution: because "the forces it has unleashed have eliminated the economic necessity that was the unchanging basis of earlier societies," the economy can sustain itself only by "replacing that necessity with a necessity for boundless economic development," which "can only mean replacing the satisfaction of primary human needs (now scarcely met) with an incessant fabrication of pseudo-needs." The pseudo-need is therefore not a decadent excess added on top of real satisfaction; it is what the economy produces *instead of* satisfaction, in order to keep producing. All the particular pseudo-needs "ultimately come down to the single pseudo-need of maintaining the reign of the autonomous economy" (Directly stated, th. 51). This is why the concept sits at the hinge between the [[Commodity as Spectacle]] and [[Spectacular Alienation]]: the fabricated want is the subjective face of an economy that governs from the outside.

Chapter 3 radicalizes the concept by cutting off its most obvious escape route — the appeal to a "genuine" need underneath the false one. Debord writes that "the pseudo-needs imposed by modern consumerism can not be contrasted with any genuine needs or desires that are not themselves also shaped by this society and its history" (th. 68). There is no reservoir of authentic desire held in trust beneath consumerism to which one might return; the whole field of need has already been colonized. "Commodity abundance represents a total break in the organic development of social needs," and "its mechanical accumulation unleashes an unlimited artificiality which over powers any living desires" (Directly stated, th. 68). The pseudo-need is thus historical and total rather than a matter of individual gullibility: it is a break in the *development* of need itself, an artificiality that "ends up by falsifying all social life."

This gives the concept its critical edge and its difficulty. Debord does name an outside — in thesis 51 the economy "loses all connection with authentic needs" — but thesis 68 denies that this authentic need is available for direct contrast, since any desire we could point to "is not itself also shaped by this society." The tension between these two theses is precisely what the axis [[Authentic needs vs Pseudo-needs]] names: authentic need functions as the vanishing measure that the spectacle has made unrecoverable in its positive form, present only negatively, as the "living desires" that the "unlimited artificiality" overpowers (Interpretive synthesis). The pseudo-need is what fills the gap left by that vanishing, and what binds the consumer to the reproduction of the system.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — The originating usage. The pseudo-need is fabricated by "the autonomous economy" to reproduce itself after economic necessity has been abolished (th. 51); it cannot be set against a recoverable "genuine" need, because all need has been reshaped by the society and its history (th. 68). It is the desiring correlate of the [[Commodity as Spectacle]] and a mechanism of [[Spectacular Alienation]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Commodity as Spectacle]] — the pseudo-need is the subjective demand answering to the commodity's spectacular self-presentation; th. 67–68 move from the commodity's "fervor" directly into the pseudo-need.
- [[Spectacle]] — pseudo-needs are how the spectacle secures "habitual submission," reproducing the reign of the autonomous economy at the level of desire.
- [[Spectacular Alienation]] — the fabricated need is a vector of alienation: the more the consumer wants, the more the external economic power governs.
- [[Reification]] — the "unlimited artificiality" that "over powers any living desires" is the reified system of commodities standing over against, and remaking, the human needs it claims to serve.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Authentic needs vs Pseudo-needs]] — the axis this concept grounds: "primary human needs" and "living desires" as the vanishing measure against which the fabricated need is diagnosed, even as th. 68 denies that any un-shaped "genuine" need is available for direct contrast.

## Questions It Raises

- If no "genuine needs or desires" survive un-shaped by this society (th. 68), on what standing can the pseudo-need still be called *pseudo*? Is "authentic need" a lost origin, a critical fiction, or a negative index visible only through the "living desires" that artificiality overpowers?
- Does the pseudo-need presuppose a subject who could, in other conditions, have wanted otherwise — or has the "total break in the organic development of social needs" foreclosed even that counterfactual?
- How does Debord's single pseudo-need of "maintaining the reign of the autonomous economy" relate to later diagnoses of desire fabricated by the system rather than repressed by it?
