# Cybernetic Art Matrix

## Brief Definition

The Cybernetic Art Matrix (CAM) is Roy Ascott's speculative, self-organising networked institution — a "highly retroactive organism" fusing art, learning, and information for a coming cybernated society — whose international net "might form the embryonic structure of a world brain"; it is the documented 1960s precursor of telematic art and network consciousness.

## Longer Explanation

Ascott first sets out CAM in the two-part essay "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" (*Cybernetica* 9–10, 1966–67), where it occupies the entire second half of the text. Having argued that modern art has drifted from the object to behaviour, and that the artwork is a feedback loop completed by the participating spectator, Ascott proposes a practical "framework" to house this behaviourist art at social scale. CAM is "designed as a framework concerned with: (a) servicing the needs of professionally committed artists; (b) providing amenities for creative play for the new leisured class; (c) formulating pragmatically a process of 'art' education for the young." Its defining cybernetic property is reflexive self-regulation: "CAM would need to be a self-organising system, a highly retroactive organism." Crucially, Ascott imagines not one CAM but a satellite-fed international *network* of them — and it is at the network scale that the proposal becomes a precursor of everything Ascott would later call telematic: "it may not be too far fetched to imagine a network of CAMs so deployed that they would constitute the creative element in a 'world brain.'" `Directly stated`.

CAM is specified through four constants Ascott labels **CAM/LIGA** — Learning (CAM/L, Education), Information (CAM/I, Communications), Generative (CAM/G, Development), and Amenities (CAM/A, Hardware). These he treats biologically: "CAM/LIGA serves as a sort of genetic code for the nascent organism CAM, which, while retaining certain hereditary characteristics, will evolve its unique form in response to changes in its internal and external environments." The "genetic code" image makes explicit that CAM is conceived as a living, adaptive system rather than a fixed institution — "a disposable structure rather than a permanent institution." Its learning subsystem replaces the one-way hierarchy of schooling with reversible mobility between learner-types: "The 'educational ladder' becomes the 'educational switch-back' within the CAM learning net." Its information subsystem runs on reciprocity ("All links are reciprocal") and on feedback that closes the loop on the whole organism: "Feedback from CAM participants adjusts the parameters of the system. The CAM produces its own controlling energy." `Directly stated`. (The fuller "world brain" line in the information section reads: "A highly interactive CAM network on an international level might form the embryonic structure of a world brain.")

In "Behaviourables and Futuribles" (1967) Ascott classifies CAM among his speculative future-projections: "The Cybernetic Art Matrix concept is essentially a futurible—anticipatory and speculative, depending for its viability on an understanding of the past." The same manifesto generalises CAM's organismic logic to art education at large: "If we are to keep art schools, let them be structured as homeostatic organisms, living, adaptive instruments for generating creative thought and action." This is the keystone reading of CAM — it is not primarily a building but a *homeostatic, behaviour-generating system*, the institutional form of behaviourist art. `Directly stated`.

By "The Psibernetic Arch" (*Studio International*, 1970) CAM has acquired an explicitly mental, even psychic, horizon. Ascott back-references it — "the blueprint for a Cybernetic Art Matrix [CAM; see chapter 3 above], where free and infinitely variable human behaviour interacts with a matrix of hardware and environmental amenities adaptive to the needs of inducing and reinforcing creative behaviour in a community" — and then describes the matrix as developing a collective awareness as its complexity rises: "In the CAM also, subsystems tend to become automatic as the level of complexity overall rises, and a psychic consciousness of the human group within it evolves." `Directly stated`. The emergence of "a psychic consciousness of the human group" inside CAM is the documentable seed of Ascott's later [[Network Consciousness]] and of his telematic / technoetic program. CAM is, in short, the distant ancestor of [[The Planetary Collegium]] — the realised, networked art-and-research community Ascott would direct decades later. `Strongly implied`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — CAM is Ascott's own coinage and the most fully elaborated institution in his 1960s cybernetic-art writing. He treats it as a *futurible* (a speculative projection), as a *self-organising / homeostatic organism* (programmed by its "genetic code" CAM/LIGA), and as a *network* whose international deployment could become "the creative element in a 'world brain.'" Across the three essays the emphasis migrates from infrastructure (1966–67: the four subsystems, the educational switch-back) to consciousness (1970: "a psychic consciousness of the human group"), tracing the path from cybernetic art toward telematics and network consciousness.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — CAM is the institution of the *system* against the discrete art object; it is "concerned with creating systems than with the styling of objects or products." It opposes the museum/art-school as a repository of fixed objects, proposing instead a "disposable structure rather than a permanent institution."

## Questions It Raises

- Is CAM best read as a failed-but-prophetic 1960s utopia, or as a genuine blueprint that telematic art and [[The Planetary Collegium]] later realised in altered form?
- CAM/A's "controlled environments—internal" (chemical and electrical modification of the central nervous system) couples the matrix's emancipatory rhetoric to direct neural intervention — how does that square with the surveillance-and-control anxieties the vault tracks in the Han/Harari cluster?
- What is the difference between CAM's "world brain" / "psychic consciousness of the human group" and a homogenising [[Hell of the Same]] — does a self-organising creative network preserve otherness, or absorb it?
- Does Ascott's "educational switch-back" (reversible, non-hierarchical learning) anticipate or critique the later "learning a living" automation society?
