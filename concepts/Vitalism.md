# Vitalism

## Brief Definition

Vitalism is the poetic, metaphysical doctrine that life is instigated by a non-material principle — an "entelechy," a vital essence, Bergson's *elan vital* — and, in Jack Burnham's account, the aesthetic that reigned over modern sculpture for roughly fifty years, supplying the most advanced justification for avant-garde abstraction even as it functioned as a reactionary force in biology.

## Longer Explanation

For Burnham, vitalism is first a position in the long mechanist-versus-vitalist dispute in biology: the belief that "life at its core is metaphysically instigated," consisting of "an 'entelechy,' to use Aristotle's word, a nonmaterial center of being making it different in quality and in kind from inorganic matter." Against this stands [[Mechanism]], the view that life can be interpreted entirely in terms of analyzable physical functions. Burnham narrates vitalism's steady scientific defeat — "the history of biology is the refutation of vitalism" — as the life force was forced to "redefine" itself "in terms of an ever-receding sphere of influence over the properties of living organisms" (`Directly stated`).

The book's central paradox is that this scientifically losing doctrine became the *reigning aesthetic of modern sculpture*. Burnham frames the question directly: "Why, if vitalism played such a reactionary role in the biological sciences, did it provide some of the most advanced justification for avant-garde abstract sculpture?" His answer is that vitalism, "a preeminently poetic view of life, a celebration of the natural condition," gave the abstract sculptor a way to "create 'life' as Rodin had done — but in a radical form that would appear to be anything but a copy of life." Beginning with Rodin's "romantic, vitalistic pantheism," running through Brancusi, Gaudier-Brzeska, Arp, Hepworth, and Henry Moore (who, almost alone, "saw the inappropriateness of the word" *organic* and "supplied 'vitalistic' in its stead"), and persisting into the postwar American welded idiom, vitalism "endured as one of those great sophisticated folk myths" propelling "a good portion of the modern idiom for the past fifty years" (`Directly stated`).

Crucially, Burnham reads vitalism as a *substitute* rather than an achievement. "Vitalism, by its very purpose of seeking to imbue physical mass with psychic energy, is an idealistic mechanism for controlling attitudes about inert, nonmoving matter. It is a surrogate for actual physical vitality." The vitalist sculptors "insisted on the presence of 'essential' spirits dwelling within their works"; what counted was "not the shape of a sculpture" but "some undefined 'inner life' buried within a sculpture's psychic and material being." This faith "verging on religiosity" even "became a partial surrogate for traditional religious values" for a sophisticated lay public (`Directly stated`). In Chapter 3 the same impulse is read onto the machine: modern sculpture "appears to have the externalized efficacy of a hooded machine — promising minor miracles if only its outer canopy could be lifted to reveal an inner sense of life force. Such a rationalization is vitalism applied to the machine" (`Directly stated`).

Because the aesthetic borrowed its authority from science, it could survive only on borrowed time. Burnham's thesis, stated in the Introduction, is that "vitalism, as a source of artistic reification, lasted as long as it remained tenable as a scientific philosophy." Vitalism is therefore a *transitional* step in the book's larger arc from the object toward the system: a "preparatory tendenc[y] which symbolically anticipate[s] the re-creation of life through nonbiological means." Once it became untenable, sculptors no longer content with a metaphor for life turned to actually moving, energy-controlled work — Kineticism — which "cannot help but undermine and change our attitudes and sensory apprehension of nonmoving art." At the end of the age "the 'elan vital' will be looked upon as the old prime mover, while the Greek 'kybernetes'... becomes the expression of a new and even more effective prime mover" (`Strongly implied` / `Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*, 1968) — treats vitalism both as a scientific doctrine in decline and as the dominant fifty-year aesthetic of modern sculpture; reads it as an "idealistic mechanism" and "surrogate for actual physical vitality," a reactionary biology yielding an avant-garde art, and the object-bound precursor superseded by the system (Kinetic, Cybernetic Art).
- **[[Henri Bergson]]** (*Creative Evolution*, 1907; forward-stub) — Burnham's "high priest" of neo-vitalism; the *elan vital* is "the vital impetus" by which "inert matter is entered by a life force in order to instill animation, like a block of uncut stone on the sculptor's bench." Burnham stresses that Bergson "must be studied as literature before considering [him] as philosophy" — vitalism's power was poetic, not empirical. (`Directly stated`)
- **Aristotle (via Burnham)** — the "entelechy," the original "nonmaterial center of being," is Burnham's deep antecedent for the vital principle. (`Directly stated`)
- **Herbert Read / Henry Moore (via Burnham)** — Read named the fusion of "organic appearing forms with abstraction" *vitalistic art*; Moore held "that behind the appearance of things there is some kind of spiritual essence." Rendered as plain text here (one-off critic/artist figures, no author notes). (`Directly stated`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Organicism]] — Burnham's paired creed; "Vitalism is a preeminently poetic view of life... while organicism is a beautiful view of the utilitarian evolution of systems." Cross-linked, distinct, and "in some essential ways... in polar opposition."
- [[Reification (Burnham)]] — vitalism is "a source of artistic reification"; the biomorphic idiom "relied on vitalism" as one mode of Burnham's "scientific idealism."
- [[Emergence]] — kept DISTINCT, not merged: the contemporary scientific account of life arising from material complexity is what *displaced* vitalism in biology. O'Gieblyn equates the two only polemically; cross-linked, not collapsed. (`Speculative connection`)
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — vitalism is the attempt to invest immaterial "psychic energy" into inert physical mass; a vault-spanning instance of the core tension.
- [[The Cyborg]] / [[Cybernetics]] — Burnham positions *kybernetes* (the steersman, root of cybernetics) as the "new... prime mover" replacing the *elan vital*.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Mechanism]] — the direct antagonist: "a belief in a physical basis, usually electrochemical, for all phenomena of life including human consciousness." Vitalism's whole history, for Burnham, is its defeat by mechanistic biology.

## Questions It Raises

- If an aesthetic can survive "only as long as it remained tenable as a scientific philosophy," is all avant-garde abstraction parasitic on the prestige of contemporary science?
- Is vitalism's "surrogate for actual physical vitality" merely a less honest version of what Kinetic and Cybernetic art attempt literally — and does literalizing the life-impulse dispel its aesthetic power?
- Where exactly should vitalism be kept apart from [[Emergence]]: is the difference one of a non-material *principle* versus material complexity, or only one of rhetoric?
