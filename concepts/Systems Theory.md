# Systems Theory

## General Orientation

Systems Theory is a shared note in which two lineages that share a vocabulary — *system, process, feedback, organization, real-time* — and a postwar moment are kept linked rather than merged, because they arise from different fields and do not reduce to one another. Both treat the living thing or the artwork as a process-defined structure rather than a substance; they divide over whether the system is *self-enclosing* or *interventionist*, and over whether the model is ported onto art from biology or generated inside art itself.

The first lineage is **the theory of living systems**, the cybernetic/autopoietic strand reconstructed in [[N. Katherine Hayles]]'s account of the second wave of [[Cybernetics]]. Here [[Humberto Maturana]] and [[Francisco Varela]] transform feedback-and-control into a theory of organizationally closed systems that build their own worlds: a system "constructs its environment through the 'domain of interactions' made possible by its autopoietic organization," so that "what lies outside that domain does not exist for that system." Hayles reads this register as the abstract, "analytically driven" pole against which she sets embodied narrative, valuing its recovery of [[Embodiment|embodied]] process while diagnosing its tendency toward closure; its most contested extension is the modeling of social systems, where "the machine of interest is much more likely to be the state." Maturana and Varela part company exactly there — Maturana pressing toward the all-encompassing claim, Varela restricting the theory to the "domain of cells and animals" and moving toward enaction and embodiment.

The second lineage is **the art world's "systems" turn**, in which the artwork dematerializes into an operational structure. [[Lucy Lippard]]'s *Six Years* documents it across 1968–1971 in the realist, interventionist key of Hans Haacke ("A system is not imagined, it is real"), Sol LeWitt's serial permutations, and the critic Jack Burnham's 1968 "Systems Aesthetics" essay that names the tendency. [[Jack Burnham]]'s own *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) makes the *system* the destiny sculpture must enter to survive the object's obsolescence — "the means by which sculpture gradually departs from its object state and assumes some measure of lifelike activity" — while grounding the broader field in postwar systems analysis, so that his sense is continuous with the [[Cybernetics]] / [[Feedback Loops]] / [[Autopoiesis]] cluster. [[Roy Ascott]], writing from 1966, stakes the *participatory* pole that complements Burnham's object→system spine: the artwork as an open, behavioural, self-organizing system whose closure is completed by the participator and, ultimately, by the network — "the system of systems."

Both of the above lineages, however, carry the field's vocabulary *secondhand* — reconstructed in Hayles from biology, or ported into art criticism. [[Donella Meadows]]'s *Thinking in Systems* is the third and different case: it is the **native discipline itself**, system dynamics in the Forrester lineage, speaking in its own voice. Where the art books borrow "feedback," "real-time," and "self-organization," Meadows *is* the source those words are borrowed from. She states the field's two central insights as its own founding truths — that "the system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior," and that "systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors, even if the outward appearance of these systems is completely dissimilar" — and, characteristically, insists the discipline is not an esoteric technical achievement but recovered common sense: "Modern systems theory, bound up with computers and equations, hides the fact that it traffics in truths known at some level by everyone." Her section grounds the whole cluster the art and cybernetics books draw on.

Burnham is the figure who joins the first two lineages: the critic who named the art-world tendency in *Artforum* also grounds the broader systems field in postwar systems analysis in his book. The art-specific thesis is kept on its own page — [[Systems Esthetics]] is the art thesis, Systems Theory the wider field — and a dedicated *Systems Aesthetics* note may later carry the conceptual-art sense independently. Meadows's own object-level term (the system as an entity that produces its own behavior over time) and her name for the *way of seeing* (systems thinking, the "lens") are kept distinct from this field note: **[[A System]]** is the object, **[[Systems Thinking]]** the lens, and Systems Theory the body of thought. Each author's sense is developed in its own section below.

## Brief Definition

The lineage of thought that treats living things, machines, and societies as self-maintaining *systems* defined by their internal organization rather than by what flows into them — the strand running, in Hayles's account, from second-wave [[Cybernetics]] through Maturana and Varela's [[Autopoiesis]] to the extension of autopoietic logic onto social systems, where "the machine of interest is much more likely to be the state," and, in its native system-dynamics form (Meadows), the field that studies how "systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors."

## Longer Explanation

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], systems theory is not a single doctrine but a *register* of discourse, and she defines it largely by contrast: narrative, with "its chronological thrust, polymorphous digressions, located actions, and personified agents," is "a more embodied form of discourse than is analytically driven systems theory." `Directly stated` Systems theory is thus the abstract, "analytically driven" pole against which she sets the located, embodied work of stories — yet within *How We Became Posthuman* she reconstructs its specific content as it crystallizes in the second wave of cybernetics (1960–1985), where [[Humberto Maturana]] and [[Francisco Varela]] turn the study of feedback into a theory of organizationally closed systems that build their own worlds.

The technical core is a set of distinctions Maturana forged "as a prophylactic against having anthropomorphism creep back in." A composite system has both a **structure** and an **organization**, and the two are not the same: organization is "the complex web of all possible relationships that can be realized by the autopoietic processes as they interact with one another," the invariant that makes a thing the kind of thing it is, while structure "is the particular instantiation that a composite unity enacts at a particular moment." A human female has one structure at birth, another at puberty, another in illness — but "throughout her lifetime, her organization remains the same." `Directly stated` This is the move that lets systems theory speak of a system persisting *as itself* through continuous material change, and it is also the move that gets the theory into trouble when it tries to account for evolution, where "either organization is conserved and evolutionary change is effaced, or organization changes and autopoiesis is effaced." To this Maturana adds **structural coupling** — the way a system is bound to its medium without that medium ever entering or programming it: "All living organisms must be structurally coupled to their environments to continue living." The decisive epistemological claim is that the system does not receive a pre-given world but *constructs* one: "Each living system thus constructs its environment through the 'domain of interactions' made possible by its autopoietic organization. What lies outside that domain does not exist for that system." `Directly stated` The ground of the subject correspondingly shifts from self-possession to **organizational closure** — "the system closes on itself, by itself" — which is why the theory both points toward [[Posthumanism]] (consciousness demoted to "a epiphenomenon") and recovers [[Embodiment]], since it "draws attention to the fact that 'information,' so defined, is an abstraction that has no basis in the physically embodied processes constituting all living entities." What makes the theory more than a biology is its portability: "autopoietic theory has proven readily adaptable to the analysis of social systems," and the cyborg is reversed — "the paradigmatic cyborg for autopoiesis is the state." It is here that Maturana and Varela split, Maturana pushing toward the claim that "everything be subordinated to autopoiesis" while [[Francisco Varela]] insists "autopoiesis should not be confused with organizational closure in general," restricting the term to the "domain of cells and animals" and turning toward enaction and embodiment ("The concrete is not a step towards anything: it is how we arrive and where we stay"). The social-systems extension — Niklas Luhmann's transposition of autopoietic closure onto society — is the lineage Varela resisted and the one in which "the machine of interest is much more likely to be the state."

[[Lucy Lippard]]'s *Six Years* documents a second, art-historical lineage of "systems," related by shared vocabulary and period but opposite in polarity. Its key figure is **Hans Haacke**, whose 1968 statement reconceives the work as a milieu rather than an object: a reactive sculpture "merges with the environment in a relationship that is better understood as a 'system' of interdependent processes. These processes evolve without the viewer's empathy. He becomes a witness. A system is not imagined, it is real." `Directly stated` Where Maturana's system constructs an observer-relative domain, Haacke's is precisely what is *not* imagined but found, real, operating whether or not it is witnessed — formalized by 1969 as a method ("to think in terms of systems; the production of systems, the interference with and the exposure of existing systems") whose materials "can be physical, biological or social." **Sol LeWitt** supplies the generative-procedure side: in his "Sentences on Conceptual Art" the system is a rule-set the artist designs and then executes blindly — "The artist's will is secondary to the process he initiates from idea to completion. . . . The process is mechanical and should not be tampered with. It should run its course" — a serial procedure adjacent to the [[Feedback Loops]] and process-talk of cybernetics. The tendency was contested as much as adopted: **Robert Smithson** countered that "System is a convenient word, like object. It is another abstract entity that doesn't exist." By 1971 the art-world sense acquires its institution-critical torque, where the two lineages most clearly *rhyme* without merging: Haacke turns the frame on art itself — "Real-time systems are double agents. They might run under the heading 'art,' but this culturization does not prevent them from operating as normal" — borrowing computing's "real-time" but arriving at the social-systems point from the opposite direction from Hayles, not a biologist's theory ported onto society but an artist's wager that the artwork-as-system is continuous with the systems it is embedded in. `Directly stated`

[[Jack Burnham]] makes the *system* the organizing concept of an entire history of sculpture, and gives the most explicit definition in the corpus: "A system is an aggregate of components; first, its parts are mutually dependent; and second, it may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life: self-organization, growth, internal or external mobility, irritability or sensitivity, input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death." `Directly stated` This is the same family of properties Hayles tracks (organization, closure, structural coupling) and the same realist, interdependent processuality Haacke claimed, but Burnham frames it as *life-likeness*: the book pivots on the contrast in which "the object denotes sculpture in its traditional physical form, whereas the system (an interacting assembly of varying complexity) is the means by which sculpture gradually departs from its object state and assumes some measure of lifelike activity." Crucially he grounds this not in biology alone but in **postwar systems analysis** — "the largest and most comprehensive attempt to use the analogues of biological organization has been systems analysis and development," dated to wartime "man-machine units (in bombers, gun stations, radar installations, etc.)" too complex "to be controlled by human reflexes" — and from it draws an observer-relative notion that rhymes with Hayles's Maturana: "The concept system itself is a pure abstraction, an assembly of isolable properties studied in terms of their transformations, either alone (closed) or in relation to other systems (open)," so that "the system becomes the unit by which all levels of fluid organization are analyzed up through and including living organisms." The closed/open distinction and the input-output vocabulary come straight from cybernetics, making his systems sense continuous with the [[Cybernetics]] / [[Feedback Loops]] / [[Autopoiesis]] cluster. From this he derives sculpture's survival: technology produces "the growing tendency to relate materials, energy sources, transportable objects, events, and signals into a unified network above and beyond natural order," in which "the object was bound to lose out as a cumbersome element" (a thread linking directly to Lippard's [[Dematerialization]]), and the endpoint is the system that dissolves the object into its environment — "emitted light best demonstrates one of the primary qualities of systems: the tendency to fuse art object and environment into a perceptual whole." `Directly stated; the book↔essay continuity is Interpretive synthesis grounded in Six Years' bibliographic record of the 1968 essay.`

[[Roy Ascott]] reconceives the *artwork itself* as an open, behavioural, self-organizing system, and is chronologically the earliest of the vault's art-cluster voices to do so. From 1966 his "behaviourist art" rests on the systems-theoretic primitive of feedback: "The participational, inclusive form of art has as its basic principle 'feedback,' and it is this loop that makes an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork/observer," so that "the artifact/observer system furnishes its own controlling energy: a function of an output variable (observer's response) is to act as an input variable, which introduces more variety into the system." Cybernetics for Ascott "concerns the idea of the perfectibility of systems" and "the procurement of effective action by means of self-organising systems." `Directly stated` He pushes the artifact toward becoming, literally, a self-organizing organism — "there is no prior reason why the artifact should not be a self-organising system, an organism, as it were, which derives its initial programme or code from the artist's creative activity and then evolves its specific artistic identity and function in response to the environments it encounters" — and by his 1980 "field theory" the system becomes the very ontology of the artwork: "Art does not reside in the artwork alone, nor in the activity of the artist alone, but is understood as a field of psychic probability, highly entropic, in which the viewer is actively involved," the viewer who "must be a participator and is of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system." By 1985 the frame extends to the network: telematics is "the system of systems," the Shannon sender/receiver model of [[Information]] abandoned ("there are no 'senders' and 'receivers' ... but only 'users'"), and Ascott traces "ideas of energy, information, transformation, and interconnectedness" directly to "cybernetics, systems theory, and, subsequently, telematic networks," naming the lineage this note carries — a planetary "cosmic web" in which meaning "is not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined." `Directly stated; the cross-author convergence with Hayles/Burnham is Interpretive synthesis.`

[[Donella Meadows]] supplies the register the other authors quote from without naming it: system dynamics as a native discipline. Her *Thinking in Systems* names the field's two central insights as its own founding truths — first, that behavior is latent in structure ("The system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior!"), and second, that structurally isomorphic systems are behaviorally isomorphic ("systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors, even if the outward appearance of these systems is completely dissimilar"), the very portability Hayles ("the machine of interest is much more likely to be the state") and Burnham ("the system becomes the unit by which all levels of fluid organization are analyzed") reconstruct secondhand. Meadows's distinctive move is to deny the discipline any esoteric privilege: "Modern systems theory, bound up with computers and equations, hides the fact that it traffics in truths known at some level by everyone," so that "it is often possible... to make a direct translation from systems jargon to traditional wisdom." `Directly stated` Read together, the senses occupy distinct stations: Maturana and Varela's self-enclosing biological closure, Hayles's reading of it as the analytic counter-pole to embodied narrative, Haacke and LeWitt's realist and serial art-world systems, Burnham's life-likeness of the object that must become a system, Ascott's artwork-as-open-system realized through participation and the network, and Meadows's native-discipline system dynamics that grounds the whole cluster in the study of feedback structure and its behaviors.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — reads systems theory as the "analytically driven" counter-pole to embodied narrative, reconstructing its second-wave content while valuing its recovery of embodied process and diagnosing the "relentless repetitive circularity" that leaves it unable to account for transformation; she names the family in which systems theory frames [[Cybernetics]], contains [[Autopoiesis]], and runs (via Luhmann) toward the state as paradigmatic system.
- **[[Humberto Maturana]]** — originator of the structure/organization distinction, structural coupling, and organizational closure; pushes systems theory toward an all-encompassing claim in which "everything be subordinated to autopoiesis," and toward the state as paradigmatic system.
- **[[Francisco Varela]]** — co-author who later restricts the theory, refusing to extend autopoietic closure to "social systems" (it belongs to the "domain of cells and animals") and moving toward enaction and [[Embodiment]] ("The concrete is not a step towards anything: it is how we arrive and where we stay").
- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** — documents in *Six Years* the conceptual-art "systems aesthetics" lineage: Hans Haacke's realist, interventionist systems ("A system is not imagined, it is real"; "Real-time systems are double agents"), Sol LeWitt's serial, self-running permutational procedures, and Burnham's 1968 essay that names the tendency — a sibling to the cybernetic lineage, sharing its language while keeping its own realist, institution-critical commitments.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — makes the *system* the destiny sculpture must enter to survive, giving the corpus's most explicit definition ("a system... may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life") and grounding it in postwar systems analysis, so that the object→system trajectory carries the broader systems field this note holds while his art-specific thesis is reserved for [[Systems Esthetics]].
- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — reconceives the artwork as an open, behavioural, self-organizing system whose closure is completed by participation: from the 1966 feedback-driven artifact/observer system, through the 1980 "field theory" of art as a probabilistic field, to the 1985 telematic "system of systems," the participatory and networked pole that complements Burnham's object→system esthetics.
- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — the native-discipline voice (system dynamics, the Forrester lineage): states the field's two central insights ("the system... causes its own behavior"; "systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors") as its own founding truths, and refuses it any esoteric privilege ("it traffics in truths known at some level by everyone"), translating "systems jargon" directly into "traditional wisdom." Keeps [[A System]] (the object) and [[Systems Thinking]] (the lens) distinct from this field note.
- **Niklas Luhmann** (forward reference) — extends autopoietic systems theory to society at large, the lineage in which "the machine of interest is much more likely to be the state"; his sociology of self-referential social systems is the social-systems variant Varela resisted.

## Systems Aesthetics in Conceptual Art (Lippard)

There is a *second*, art-historical lineage of "systems" that Lucy Lippard's *Six Years* documents as it unfolds across 1968–1971 — related to the cybernetic/autopoietic theory above by shared vocabulary and period, but distinct in origin and stakes. Its key figure is [[Hans Haacke]], whose 1968 Howard Wise Gallery statement reconceives the work of art as a milieu rather than an object: "A 'sculpture' that physically reacts to its environment is no longer to be regarded as an object. . . . It thus merges with the environment in a relationship that is better understood as a 'system' of interdependent processes. These processes evolve without the viewer's empathy. He becomes a witness. A system is not imagined, it is real." Where Hayles's systems theory turns on a *constructed* domain of interactions — "what lies outside that domain does not exist for that system" — Haacke insists on the reverse polarity: the system is precisely what is *not* imagined but found, real, operating whether or not it is witnessed. By 1969 he formalizes this as a working method: "The working premise is to think in terms of systems; the production of systems, the interference with and the exposure of existing systems." The art object dematerializes into an operational structure — Haacke specifies that "[s]ystems can be physical, biological or social" — and the question shifts from what a work *looks like* to what processes it sets running and exposes. This is why the Lippard sense sits within a note named *Systems Theory* without collapsing into it: both treat the work-or-organism as a process-defined structure rather than a substance, but Haacke's systems are realist and interventionist where Maturana's are observer-relative and self-enclosing.

The lineage has two other anchors in *Six Years*. The first is [[Sol LeWitt]]'s serial work, whose permutational logic supplies the "systems" idiom on the side of generative procedure rather than environmental process: his 1968 Dwan show *46 3-Part Variations on 3 Different Kinds of Cubes* runs a finite vocabulary of elements through its combinations, and his "Sentences on Conceptual Art" make the program explicit — "The artist's will is secondary to the process he initiates from idea to completion," and "The process is mechanical and should not be tampered with. It should run its course." Here the system is the rule-set; the artist designs the permutation and then executes it "blindly," a serial, self-running procedure adjacent to (though not identical with) the [[Feedback Loops]] and process-talk of cybernetics. The second anchor is the critic Jack Burnham (a forward-stub figure, no book yet in `raw/`), whose essay "Systems Aesthetics" (*Artforum*, September, 1968) Lippard logs in the 1968 bibliography and which names the tendency outright — the moment the art world's turn to systems acquires a theoretical banner. (Not everyone in the book accepts the frame: Robert Smithson tells P. A. Norvell that "System is a convenient word, like object. It is another abstract entity that doesn't exist," a useful reminder that "systems aesthetics" was a contested rather than consensual program.)

By 1971 the political torque of the art-world sense is fully visible, and it is here that the two lineages most clearly *rhyme* without merging. In Jeanne Siegel's interview Haacke turns the systems frame on the institution of art itself: "Real-time systems are double agents. They might run under the heading 'art,' but this culturization does not prevent them from operating as normal." The phrase "real-time systems" borrows directly from computing and cybernetics, but Haacke's point is institutional-critical — a work that is a real social or informational system does not stop being one just because a gallery brackets it as "art"; it keeps "operating as normal," so it can carry real information and "affect the general social fabric." This is structurally close to Hayles's observation that in the social-systems extension "the machine of interest is much more likely to be the state," yet it arrives from the opposite direction: not a biologist's theory ported onto society, but an artist's wager that the artwork-as-system is continuous with the social systems it is embedded in. The Lippard lineage thus belongs beside the cybernetic one as a sibling, not a subset — a conceptual-art "systems aesthetics" that shares the period's language of process, feedback, and real-time operation while keeping its own realist, interventionist, institution-critical commitments. *(See General Orientation: a dedicated Systems Aesthetics note may later carry this sense independently.)*

## Jack Burnham (Beyond Modern Sculpture)

If Hayles reads systems theory as a biologist's discourse ported onto society and Lippard records its art-world adoption, Jack Burnham is the figure who makes the *system* the organizing concept of an entire history of sculpture — and who, more than anyone in the vault, treats "system" as the destiny art must enter to survive. His definition is the most explicit in the corpus: "A system is an aggregate of components; first, its parts are mutually dependent; and second, it may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life: self-organization, growth, internal or external mobility, irritability or sensitivity, input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death (humans are the most dramatic instances of systems which die)." This is the same family of properties Hayles tracks (organization, closure, structural coupling) and the same realist, interdependent processuality Haacke claimed, but Burnham frames it as a *life-likeness*: the system is interesting precisely because it crosses the threshold from inert object toward the behaviors of the living. The whole book pivots on this contrast — "the object denotes sculpture in its traditional physical form, whereas the system (an interacting assembly of varying complexity) is the means by which sculpture gradually departs from its object state and assumes some measure of lifelike activity" — so that "the system becomes a logical extension of the object through intellectual domination of the environment." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

Crucially, Burnham grounds this not in biology alone but in **postwar systems analysis**, the same applied, military-industrial lineage Hayles's account brackets. "The largest and most comprehensive attempt to use the analogues of biological organization has been systems analysis and development," he writes, and dates its origin to wartime man-machine complexity: "During the Second World War man-machine units (in bombers, gun stations, radar installations, etc.) were for the, first time deemed too complex to be controlled by human reflexes. As a result, studies in the systematic organization and control of such groups were undertaken." From this he draws the abstract, observer-relative notion of system that rhymes with Hayles's Maturana: "The concept system itself is a pure abstraction, an assembly of isolable properties studied in terms of their transformations, either alone (closed) or in relation to other systems (open)," and "the system becomes the unit by which all levels of fluid organization are analyzed up through and including living organisms." The closed/open distinction, the observer who isolates a "singleness," the input-output and feedback vocabulary — Burnham takes these straight from cybernetics (Wiener, Ashby, Bertalanffy), so his systems sense is continuous with the [[Cybernetics]] / [[Feedback Loops]] / [[Autopoiesis]] cluster rather than a separate coinage. He even reads the broader culture as already systematized: when we buy an automobile "we no longer buy an object in the old sense of the word" but a lease into "an interlocking system of production and need fulfillment," so that "the object now is a replaceable component in an interlocking system." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

What is distinctive — and what makes Burnham *the* systems-art figure — is that he makes sculpture's very survival depend on its becoming a system. Technology, he argues, produces "the growing tendency to relate materials, energy sources, transportable objects, events, and signals into a unified network above and beyond natural order. In such a situation the object was bound to lose out as a cumbersome element" — the object dematerializes (a thread that links directly to Lippard's [[Dematerialization]]). The machine's promise, foreshadowed by Picabia who "long before other artists, realized that the higher power of the machine lay in its ability to be united with other machines into feeling relationships, and to assume some of the social interaction of organisms living together," is that components couple into living-like wholes. The endpoint is the system that dissolves the object into its environment: writing of Light Art, Burnham observes that "emitted light best demonstrates one of the primary qualities of systems: the tendency to fuse art object and environment into a perceptual whole" — the work ceasing to be a discrete thing and becoming "a light-modulating system sensitive to responses from organisms which invade its presence." This is the through-line from Burnham's book to his contemporaneous *Artforum* program: the broader systems field theorized here is the soil from which his art-specific thesis grows. That thesis is kept on its own page — see [[Systems Esthetics]] — while this note carries the wider field. (Confidence: Directly stated; the book↔essay continuity is Interpretive synthesis grounded in *Six Years*' bibliographic record of the 1968 essay.)

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

If Hayles ports a biologist's systems theory onto society, Lippard records its art-world adoption, and Burnham makes the *system* the destiny sculpture must enter, Roy Ascott is the figure who reconceives the *artwork itself* as an open, behavioural, self-organizing system — and who, writing from 1966 onward, is chronologically the earliest of the vault's art-cluster voices to do so. From the start his "behaviourist art" rests on the systems-theoretic primitive of feedback: "The participational, inclusive form of art has as its basic principle 'feedback,' and it is this loop that makes an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork / observer." The artifact/observer dyad becomes precisely the kind of self-controlling, variety-generating system Hayles and Burnham describe — Ascott's own gloss is that "the artifact/observer system furnishes its own controlling energy: a function of an output variable (observer's response) is to act as an input variable, which introduces more variety into the system." Cybernetics for Ascott "concerns the idea of the perfectibility of systems" and "the procurement of effective action by means of self-organising systems," so the artwork is no longer a finished object but a system held perpetually in play. (Confidence: Directly stated.)

Ascott pushes this further than the other authors in one direction: he imagines the artifact becoming, literally, a self-organizing organism. "There is no prior reason why the artifact should not be a self-organising system, an organism, as it were, which derives its initial programme or code from the artist's creative activity and then evolves its specific artistic identity and function in response to the environments it encounters." This is the same family of properties Burnham names (self-organization, growth, input/output, structural coupling to an environment) and the same observer-relative, world-constructing system Hayles tracks — but Ascott projects it forward into the design of growth systems and generative art, anchoring the participatory pole that complements Burnham's object→system [[Systems Esthetics]]. By his 1980 "field theory" essay the system has become the very ontology of the artwork: "Art does not reside in the artwork alone, nor in the activity of the artist alone, but is understood as a field of psychic probability, highly entropic, in which the viewer is actively involved." The viewer "must be a participator and is of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system," so a field theory of art is, in the end, a theory of a self-organizing system whose closure is completed by participation rather than by an organizational invariant. (Confidence: Directly stated; the cross-author convergence with Hayles/Burnham is Interpretive synthesis.)

By 1985 Ascott extends the systems frame to the network: telematics is, for him, "the system of systems," in which the discrete object dissolves into process and "the work of art does not reside in the object alone, but in a system, a negotiation between artist and spectator in the creation of meaning." The Shannon sender/receiver model of [[Information]] is explicitly abandoned — "there are no 'senders' and 'receivers' (as in the old models of information theory) but only 'users,' whose intervention will be more or less creative depending on the degree of their participation in the system" — and Ascott traces "ideas of energy, information, transformation, and interconnectedness" directly to "cybernetics, systems theory, and, subsequently, telematic networks," naming the lineage this note carries. The networked system becomes a planetary one: an interconnected "cosmic web" in which meaning "is not given but negotiated, endlessly reconstituted and redefined." Ascott thus occupies a distinct station in the systems lineage — neither Maturana's self-enclosing biological closure nor Burnham's life-likeness of the object, but the artwork-as-open-system whose self-organization is realized through participation and, ultimately, through the network. (Confidence: Directly stated; "system of systems" / the cybernetics-and-systems-theory genealogy are Ascott's own words.)

## Donella Meadows (Thinking in Systems)

Where every other author in this note carries the field's vocabulary secondhand — reconstructed by Hayles from biology, or ported into art criticism by Lippard, Burnham, and Ascott — Donella Meadows *is* the native discipline. *Thinking in Systems* is a primer of system dynamics in the Jay Forrester lineage (the MIT school that produced *The Limits to Growth*), and it speaks the field's founding truths not as an outside commentator but as a practitioner naming her own ground. This is the merge the whole note points toward: the "feedback," "real-time," "self-organization," and "input/output" vocabulary that Ascott traces "to cybernetics, systems theory, and, subsequently, telematic networks," and that Burnham draws "straight from cybernetics," is here spoken in its own voice. Meadows states the discipline's two central insights as a matched pair. The first is that behavior is latent in a system's own structure rather than imposed from outside — "The system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior!" — a claim she calls "heretical" when applied not to a Slinky but to "individuals, companies, cities, or economies." The second, which she names "one of the central insights of systems theory, as central as the observation that systems largely cause their own behavior," is that structurally isomorphic systems are behaviorally isomorphic: "systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors, even if the outward appearance of these systems is completely dissimilar." (Confidence: Directly stated.)

These two insights are the native-discipline root of what the other authors reconstruct. Meadows's portability claim — that the same feedback structure yields the same behavior across a coffee cup, a population, and an industrial economy — is the ground beneath Hayles's observation that "the machine of interest is much more likely to be the state" and Burnham's that "the system becomes the unit by which all levels of fluid organization are analyzed up through and including living organisms." What distinguishes Meadows from both is her insistence that the discipline is not an esoteric technical achievement but recovered common sense. "Modern systems theory," she writes, "bound up with computers and equations, hides the fact that it traffics in truths known at some level by everyone. It is often possible, therefore, to make a direct translation from systems jargon to traditional wisdom" — feedback delays becoming "a stitch in time saves nine," the reinforcing loop of competitive exclusion becoming "the rich get richer." This democratizing move is the opposite pole from Hayles's diagnosis of systems theory as the abstract, "analytically driven" counter to embodied narrative: for Meadows the field's authority rests precisely on its continuity with lived, embodied intuition. (Confidence: Directly stated.)

Meadows's system dynamics is a distinct lineage of the systems field — Forrester's simulation-based study of stocks, flows, and feedback structure — rather than a restatement of Maturana's biological autopoiesis or Burnham's art-side systems esthetics, and it grounds the cybernetics/feedback cluster the vault had otherwise held only secondhand. It should be read alongside, not merged into, two neighboring notes she anchors: **[[A System]]**, her term for the object ("a set of things... interconnected in such a way that they produce their own pattern of behavior over time"), and **[[Systems Thinking]]**, her name for the way of seeing (the complementary "lens" set beside the reductionist one) — this note, Systems Theory, holds the body of thought within which those two sit. (Confidence: Directly stated; the placement of system dynamics as a distinct lineage of the field is Interpretive synthesis.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Cybernetics]] — the broader field within which systems theory's second wave develops; systems theory frames and narrows it from feedback-and-control to organizational closure
- [[A System]] — Meadows's object-level term (the system as an entity that produces its own pattern of behavior over time); the *object* to this note's *field*, kept distinct
- [[Systems Thinking]] — Meadows's name for the *lens* / way of seeing (complementary to reductionist analysis); the *discipline/practice* to this note's *body of thought*, kept distinct
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's art-specific thesis (the *Artforum* program that the artwork is a system, not an object); the species to this note's genus, kept distinct: Systems Esthetics is the art thesis, Systems Theory the broader field
- [[Dematerialization]] — Burnham's "the object was bound to lose out as a cumbersome element" in the unified network is the sculptural correlate of Lippard's dematerialization of the art object
- [[Autopoiesis]] — the specific theory of self-producing living systems that gives systems theory its rigorous core; systems theory is the genus, autopoiesis the species
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the first-wave mechanism that systems theory reinterprets, insisting "there are no messages circulating in feedback loops" intrinsic to the system itself; Meadows's native-discipline exposition (reinforcing vs balancing loops) grounds the vault's feedback vocabulary
- [[Embodiment]] — recovered by systems theory's "emphasis on actual biological processes," even as the theory understates embodied action's transformative force
- [[Posthumanism]] — systems theory "points toward the posthuman" by demoting consciousness to an epiphenomenon and shifting the ground of selfhood from self-possession to closure

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — systems theory cuts both ways here: it rescues the embodied substrate by insisting information "does not exist other than as an inference drawn by an observer," yet its closure makes it "constantly in danger of solipsism," a curiously "inert and self-enclosing" account of the living
- Narrative as embodied discourse — Hayles's explicit foil: narrative is "a more embodied form of discourse than is analytically driven systems theory," so the abstraction of systems theory is opposed to the located specificity of stories

## Questions It Raises

- If a system "constructs its environment" and "what lies outside that domain does not exist for that system," what becomes of [[Embodiment]] as a shared material ground — does organizational closure quietly re-immaterialize the body into a domain of pure interaction?
- When the state becomes "the paradigmatic cyborg," is the body of the citizen modeled as data circulating within a social system ([[The Body as Data]]), and does that modeling repeat the very disembodiment of information Hayles sets out to contest ([[Material vs Immaterial]])?
- Systems theory both recovers embodied process and demotes consciousness to "a epiphenomenon": does it therefore advance [[Posthumanism]] in the dangerous sense Hayles warns against (grafting closure onto liberal autonomy), or in the corrective sense (information has no existence apart from its medium)?
- Varela restricts autopoiesis to "cells and animals" because social systems do not "physically produce the components that instantiate their organization" — so is the social-systems lineage (Luhmann) a legitimate extension of systems theory or a category error that treats an immaterial abstraction as if it were a living, material thing?
- If Meadows is right that the field "traffics in truths known at some level by everyone" and translates directly into "traditional wisdom," is Hayles's framing of systems theory as the *abstract, analytically driven* counter-pole to embodied narrative a misreading of the discipline — or do the two describe genuinely different things (the equations vs the intuition they encode)?
- **(Open, flagged for review)** Does Meadows warrant a *dedicated* `System Dynamics` note — the Forrester simulation lineage as its own concept — rather than only an author-section here? Her system dynamics is a distinct native lineage of the systems field (stocks, flows, simulated behavior-over-time), and the vault currently folds it into this shared note; a future pass may split it out and cross-link.
