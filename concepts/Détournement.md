# Détournement

## Brief Definition

Détournement is the Situationist method of reversal and hijacking — the subversive reuse of prior cultural and theoretical material against itself, defined by [[Guy Debord]] by contrast as "the opposite of quotation" (thesis 208) and as "the flexible language of anti-ideology."

## Longer Explanation

Détournement is Debord's signature critical practice and the formal counterpart, at the level of language and method, to the negation of the [[Spectacle]] argued throughout *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*. Where the spectacle petrifies life into a separate, contemplated image, détournement is the operation that turns already-existing material — a slogan, an artwork, a line of theory — back into a weapon by wrenching it out of its authorized meaning and putting it to work in a present critique. Debord develops it in Chapter 8 (theses 204–209), the book's account of how a genuinely critical style must "communicate itself in its own language, the language of contradiction" (thesis 204).

The concept is built on a sharp opposition to quotation. Quotation, for Debord, "appeal[s] to a theoretical authority that is inevitably tainted by the very fact that it has become a quotation — a fragment torn from its own context and development" (thesis 208). Détournement refuses that deference: it "cannot and need not be confirmed by any previous or supracritical reference," and instead validates the "previous kernels of truth it has brought back into play" by its own internal coherence and practical effectiveness. Its warrant is nothing external — "Détournement has grounded its cause on nothing but its own truth as present critique" (thesis 208). This is why Debord describes détournement as *anti-ideology*: it is the mode of communication proper to a critique that knows it cannot claim any inherent or definitive certainty. `Directly stated` — theses 204–208.

Détournement is also a theory of how critical truths decay and can be reactivated. Debord writes that "détournement reradicalizes previous critical conclusions that have been petrified into respectable truths and thus transformed into lies" (thesis 206). The enemy here is not falsehood but *recuperated* truth — the fate by which a once-subversive statement is absorbed, honored, and thereby neutralized. Détournement is the corrective operation that re-radicalizes such petrified conclusions; [[Recuperation]] names the inverse capture that makes the operation necessary in the first place, which is why the two are treated as twin/inverse terms. `Directly stated` (thesis 206) for the re-radicalization claim; `Interpretive synthesis` for the framing of détournement and recuperation as an inverse pair.

Debord grounds the method in a lineage he explicitly détournes: Hegel's "practice of reversing the genitive," and above all "the young Marx, inspired by Feuerbach's systematic reversal of subject and predicate," who "answers 'the philosophy of poverty' with 'the poverty of philosophy'" (thesis 206). He goes further in thesis 207, adopting Lautréamont's provocation as a maxim of method: "Ideas improve. The meaning of words plays a part in that improvement. Plagiarism is necessary. Progress depends on it. It sticks close to an author's phrasing, exploits his expressions, deletes a false idea, replaces it with the right one." Détournement is thus a *dialectical* reuse — not citation but correction, not homage but appropriation — and it is inseparable from Debord's parallel demand for the [[Détournement (Debord)|abolition and realization of art]] rather than its preservation as a museum object. `Directly stated` — theses 206–207.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — *The Society of the Spectacle* (1967), Chapter 8. The originating and canonical usage. Détournement is (1) "the opposite of quotation" (th. 208); (2) "the flexible language of anti-ideology" (th. 208); (3) a re-radicalization of critical conclusions "petrified into respectable truths and thus transformed into lies" (th. 206); (4) a method self-validated by "its own truth as present critique" rather than by any external authority (th. 208). The book itself is a performance of the method — a text stitched from détourned Hegel, Marx, Lukács, and others, whose own style "includes a critique of itself" (th. 206). See the companion definition note [[Détournement (Debord)]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Recuperation]] — the twin/inverse operation: recuperation is the spectacle's capture and neutralization of critical material into "respectable truths"; détournement is the counter-operation that re-radicalizes what recuperation has petrified.
- [[Spectacle]] — détournement is the linguistic/methodological form of the negation the whole book aims at the spectacle; it is anti-ideology to the spectacle's materialized ideology.
- [[Separation]] — quotation, in Debord, reproduces separation by tearing a fragment from its context and enshrining it as authority; détournement works against that separation by returning fragments to living, present use.
- [[Reification]] — recuperated, "petrified" truths are a reified form of critical thought; détournement is the practice that de-reifies them back into critique.
- [[Détournement (Debord)]] — the definition note that isolates Debord's verbatim formulations (thesis 208).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Détournement vs Quotation]] — the constitutive opposition Debord draws in thesis 208: détournement (self-validating present critique, anti-ideology) against quotation (deference to a tainted external authority, a fragment torn from its development). Détournement is *defined by* this contrast.

## Questions It Raises

- If détournement validates itself only by "its own truth as present critique," what protects it from becoming, in turn, a recuperated style — an aesthetic tic absorbed by advertising and the spectacle it opposes?
- Is the distinction between détournement and quotation formal (how the material is reused) or intentional (the political stance behind the reuse), and can it survive once the technique is generalized?
- How does Debord's "plagiarism is necessary" square with the vault's own provenance-first, verbatim-quotation methodology — is careful citation itself a mode of the "quotation" détournement is meant to refuse?
- Does détournement escape ideology, or does its claim to be "anti-ideology" reproduce the very gesture of self-certain authority it accuses quotation of?
