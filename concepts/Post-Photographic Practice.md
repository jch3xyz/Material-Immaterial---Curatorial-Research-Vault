# Post-Photographic Practice

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name (1992) for photography's mutation from a stable, finished material medium into an unstable, layered, viewer-transformable practice within hypermedia — where the digital image is no longer a fixed thing the viewer absorbs but a permeable datum the viewer immerses in, navigates, and transforms.

## Longer Explanation

In "Photography at the Interface," Ascott declares "the beginning of the era of post-photographic practice." The diagnosis is a change of state, not merely of style: "photography as a stable medium is giving way to a practice that celebrates instability, uncertainty, incompleteness, and transformation." What collapses is the old economy of the image, in which a finished photograph framed a "composite moment" and the viewer's task was to absorb it. The post-photographic artist's interest migrates from "things seen" toward "what cannot be seen at the surface level of reality" — "human relationships, systems, forces, and fields" — and toward "how frameworks are constructed from which image worlds can emerge, an open-ended process." This is photography reconceived along the same object→system spine that runs through the [[New Media Art]] / [[Systems Esthetics]] cluster: the photograph stops being a discrete object and becomes a node in a flow. `Directly stated`

The technology itself defines the shift. Ascott's triad is precise: "This post-photographic technology captures images ('seen' images from still, video, and other cameras), constructs images ('unseen' data from remote sensors and data banks), and generates images (from raw numbers)." Capture is the residue of the old optical, chemical photography; construction and generation are new — the image can originate in sensor data or pure number, with no scene ever having stood before a lens. The image then enters "a media flow that is — in every serious sense — unending and ubiquitous." The decisive consequence for Ascott is relational: "the processes of transformation I have described are now in the hands of the viewer as much as the artist," which makes post-photographic practice a form of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] and of [[Reciprocal Communication]] rather than a medium of authored, finished statements. `Directly stated`

Two reorientations follow, and they are the conceptual payload of the term. First, the value of the image shifts from likeness to model: "In post-photographic practice, the lust for verisimilitude gives way to the love of modeling" — the photograph is prized not for resembling the real but for letting the viewer build "a reality, of multiple realities." This is the [[Verisimilitude vs Modeling]] tension. Second, the viewer's mode of relation shifts from absorption to immersion: "The hypermedia systems in which photo data float call for sensory immersion and conceptual navigation on the part of the viewing subject" — "immersion in the images" plus "navigation through the layers of data." This is the [[Absorption vs Immersion]] tension, and Ascott marks it with an explicit figure: "The metaphor for this practice is emphatically aquatic." The viewer no longer stands before the image to receive it; the viewer enters and moves through it. `Directly stated`

Post-photographic practice is thus inseparable from [[Hypermedia]]: the photo-datum's instability, its viewer-transformability, and its existence as a layered flow rather than a framed object are all properties of the hypermedia environment in which "photo data float." It extends the dematerialization argument ([[Dematerialization]]) from the conceptual-art object into the photographic image specifically, and it positions the digital photograph on the immaterial side of [[Material vs Immaterial]] — a permeable, recomposable field rather than a fixed material print. Ascott even gestures past the human viewer to "the artificial observer, the eye of the neural net, the artificial intelligence that will surely become a part of the observing system," extending the observing apparatus itself beyond the human. `Directly stated` (the AI-observer claim is offered by Ascott as a near-future projection — `Strongly implied` as a defining property).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and defines the term in *Telematic Embrace* ("Photography at the Interface," 1992). For Ascott it is one regional instance of his larger thesis that art has become an experiential, telematic, reciprocally constructed system: the photograph is the test case for the move from the finished material image to the permeable, navigable, viewer-transformed datum. It anchors the [[Absorption vs Immersion]] and [[Verisimilitude vs Modeling]] tensions and is realized inside [[Hypermedia]].
- **Precursor — [[Lucy Lippard]]** — *(Interpretive synthesis)* Ascott's "stable medium giving way to instability, incompleteness, transformation" continues Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] of the art object, now applied to the photographic image rather than the conceptual-art object; Ascott does not cite Lippard, so the link is parallel, not derivation.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Verisimilitude vs Modeling]] — post-photographic practice abandons the pole of likeness for the pole of model-building
- [[Absorption vs Immersion]] — it replaces the absorbed, receptive viewer with the immersed, navigating viewer

## Questions It Raises

- If the image can be "generated (from raw numbers)" with no scene ever before a lens, in what sense is it still a *photograph* rather than a synthesis — and does the word "photographic" outlive its referent?
- When transformation passes "into the hands of the viewer as much as the artist," what remains of authorship, and how is provenance of an image to be tracked once it is recomposable?
- Ascott's "artificial observer, the eye of the neural net" anticipates AI as "part of the observing system" — does the post-photographic image presuppose a non-human viewer, and what becomes of the human "viewing subject" then?
- Is the aquatic metaphor (immersion/navigation) a genuine epistemic shift or a description of an interface convention that could harden into a new "stable medium"?
