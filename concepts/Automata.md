# Automata

## Brief Definition

For Jack Burnham, automata are "all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men" — a branch of subsculpture whose history "has always run close to that of technology," and the bridge-term by which sculpture passes from object to system.

## Longer Explanation

Automata are Burnham's pivot from sculpture-as-object to sculpture-as-system. They sit within what he calls *subsculpture* — "types of images aesthetically ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts" (fetishes, idols, dolls, puppets, manikins) — but they are the one branch he singles out for sustained tracking, because they are the figural form most thoroughly bound to the machine. Where static sculpture remains, in Burnham's Spenglerian framing, Apollonian, "automata possess a Faustian drive to transcend natural limitations": the animated image carries "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge," through which "Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically." (`Directly stated`)

Burnham reads automata as a register — a needle that records, in its changing outward forms, the pressure of technology on art: "Both the feats of automata and the progression of outward forms assumed by automata indicate a parallel pulse in modern art and technology." (`Directly stated`) The genealogy he traces runs from Egyptian servant-figures and oracle-statues (proto-automata, "not self-propelled" but possessing "the option of alternate positions") and Greek ritual dolls, through Hero of Alexandria's hydraulic *Automata* and mechanical theatres of Dionysus; through medieval clockwork "jacks" and the great Strasbourg cathedral clock as "one mechanical, miniature cosmos"; into the eighteenth century as "the golden age of automata," exemplified by Jacques de Vaucanson's flute-player and his digesting *Canard*; and then into decline as the nineteenth century's craving "for the impossible" and the rise of *the robot* drain automata of their old childlike delight. (`Directly stated`)

The decisive turn for Burnham's larger argument comes after 1945. The robot — Čapek's "forced labor," a clanking anthropomorphic shell — gives way to something that no longer looks human at all: "after 1945 experimental research in man-machine relationships was coordinated under the title of cybernetics, resulting in electronic pseudo-organisms quite unlike robots in appearance." (`Directly stated`) Grey Walter's tortoises, Ross Ashby's homeostat, and Claude Shannon's maze-running mice are "not clever copies of flute playing or poetry writing, but working models" — "analogues of various aspects of the nervous system," "tenuously linked with life itself." With this, automata stop being figural objects and become *behaviors*: the abstract "theory of automata — which has nothing to do with mechanical men — has become one of the most fascinating and vital branches of communication and cybernetic study." This is precisely the threshold at which Burnham's book leaves the object behind for the system. (`Strongly implied` — Burnham draws the genealogy explicitly; that it functions as the hinge of his object-to-system argument is the synthesis the chapter's placement in "Part Two — Sculpture as System" supports.)

Burnham also splits the lineage at Pascal and Leibniz, noting that since their calculating machines "the problem of designing automata… has been divided into two branches": "amusing facsimiles of men and animals" and "the designing of logic systems and programming techniques" — naming Leibniz "the spiritual father of modern cybernetics." (`Directly stated`) The anthropomorphic branch wanes; the nonanthropomorphic, logical branch becomes "progressively more important for modern automata."

## Questions It Raises

- If automata "with nothing to do with mechanical men" become a branch of cybernetic theory, has the anthropomorphic image been fully dissolved into behavior and information?
- Burnham asks it himself: "would we today have an aesthetic of moving statuary," or does mechanization inevitably "erode the soul of art"?
- Does the postwar "pseudo-organism" complete the Faustian urge to re-create life nonbiologically, or merely relocate it from form into system?
