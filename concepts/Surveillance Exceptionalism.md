# Surveillance Exceptionalism

## Brief Definition

Shoshana Zuboff's coinage for the post-9/11 "unique historical deformity" in which Google's nascent surveillance practices — days from legislative restriction — were abruptly recast as mission-critical national-security necessities, sheltering the surveillance-capitalist mutation from regulatory and legislative challenge.

## Longer Explanation

In the months before September 11, 2001, U.S. internet privacy was on the verge of being regulated. The Federal Trade Commission, having concluded that self-regulation was failing, recommended online privacy legislation in 2000 — the same year Google was discovering behavioral surplus through AdWords. Zuboff's argument is that these two trajectories were about to collide: the practices that fed the new behavioral-futures markets were "careening toward legislative action." Then the terrorist attacks intervened. The threat of comprehensive privacy law had already mobilized the business lobby to "shape or stop" any bill; in the post-9/11 environment, that lobby and the suddenly data-hungry intelligence community "converged for an easy victory," and legislation went off the table (`Directly stated`).

Zuboff frames the result through two borrowed concepts. The first is Giorgio Agamben's [[State of Exception]]: "In this environment of trauma and anxiety, a 'state of exception' was invoked to legitimate a new imperative: speed at any cost." The slow, redundant, checks-and-balances tempos of a democratic state — to which even the NSA was subject — became intolerable to agencies "scrambling" for information dominance, so they sought methods that could "rapidly bypass legal and bureaucratic restrictions" (`Directly stated`). The second is Max Weber's notion of an *elective affinity* — "a mutual magnetism that originates in shared meanings, interests, and reciprocities." The constitutionally constrained intelligence agencies and the lawless surveillance firm discovered that they craved the same thing: certainty. "Both institutions craved certainty and were determined to fulfill that craving in their respective domains at any price" (`Directly stated`). Surveillance exceptionalism is the name Zuboff gives to the deformity this affinity produced.

The mechanism is concrete, not merely ideological. The CIA had opened the venture firm In-Q-Tel in 1999 ("The CIA needs to swim in the Valley," Tenet had said); after 9/11 it became a critical conduit. John Poindexter's Total Information Awareness (TIA) program (2002) read "like an early guide to the foundational mechanisms of behavioral surplus capture and analysis"; when Congress refused it, its initiatives were quietly reassigned to the Pentagon's ARDA. Google was contracted to outfit the NSA with search technology and customized its engine for the CIA's Intelink Office; in 2004 it acquired Keyhole — an In-Q-Tel-backed satellite-mapping firm that became Google Earth. The NSA returned the flattery of imitation by building ICREACH, a "Google-like" metadata search tool. Zuboff's counterfactual claim is stark: "Had it not been for surveillance exceptionalism, it is possible that these data would not even exist, at least not in their current volume and detail" (`Directly stated`).

The deeper consequence is evolutionary. Surveillance exceptionalism "creat[ed] an environment in which Google's budding surveillance practices were coveted rather than contested," so its leaders "were not compelled to undertake the arduous and risky work of inventing an exchange-based advocacy-oriented market form when the surveillance model was so lucrative." The exception, in other words, did not merely tolerate the [[Surveillance Capitalism|surveillance-capitalist]] mutation — it selected for it, setting the bar that venture capitalists and Wall Street then imposed on every other internet business (`Strongly implied`). This is why the concept is a *deformity* of history rather than a policy episode: it shaped what kind of capitalism could survive.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins the term for the public-private *elective affinity* (Weber) that, under a *state of exception* (Agamben), sheltered Google's surveillance logic from legislative challenge after 9/11. The exception is not declared once but sustained: it becomes "the realpolitik of surveillance exceptionalism," one of the historical conditions (with second-modernity needs and the neoliberal inheritance) that birthed and nurtured the surveillance project (`Directly stated`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic order whose mutation surveillance exceptionalism sheltered from regulation; the exception is its enabling political habitat.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the raw material whose lawless extraction the exception protected from "every attempt to circumscribe their practices."
- [[State of Exception]] — the Agamben concept Zuboff explicitly invokes (via David Lyon) to name the suspension of normal democratic conditions justified by the "war on terrorism."
- [[Biopolitics]] — the prior security-rationality lineage; surveillance exceptionalism fuses state intelligence with private data extraction in a new key.
- [[Big Data]] — the "novel intelligence from massive data" both parties craved; the shared object of the elective affinity.
- [[Surveillance]] — the broader practice that the exception privatized and militarized at once.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Society of Control]] — Deleuze's continuous, ambient modulation is the *steady-state* condition; surveillance exceptionalism is its opposite genesis story — a discontinuous *emergency* that ruptures the slow democratic order ("the tempos of democracy are slow by design") to install that control. The exception is the crisis through which the control regime took root.

## Questions It Raises

- If the surveillance mutation was selected for by an emergency that has never formally ended, what would it mean to *end* the exception — and is a permanent exception still an exception?
- Zuboff concedes "history offers us no control groups": how much of surveillance capitalism is attributable to the post-9/11 affinity versus the neoliberal inheritance and second-modernity needs she names alongside it?
- Does the convergence of a constitutionally constrained agency and a lawless firm corrupt the agency, the firm, or the constitutional order that was supposed to bind both?
- If "every government in the world claims the power to compel disclosure of this data," is surveillance exceptionalism an American episode or the template for a global condition?
