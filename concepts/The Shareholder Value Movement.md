# The Shareholder Value Movement

## Brief Definition

The neoliberal reconception of the corporation, operationalized by economists Michael Jensen and William Meckling in a 1976 article, that reinterpreted the manager as "a sort of parasite feeding off the host of ownership" and enthroned the share price as the master incentive metric to which all managerial behavior must be made to bend.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], the shareholder value movement is one of the historical vectors converging on the rise of [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — specifically, it is the corporate-form vector inside the neoliberal habitat. Where neoliberalism supplied the broad ideological climate (Hayek's "self-regulating market as a natural force," the demand for "radical freedom from all forms of state oversight"), the shareholder value movement supplied a concrete theory of the firm. Zuboff is explicit that this is causal-genealogical work: Hayek's ideology "provided the intellectual superstructure and legitimation for a new theory of the firm that became another crucial antecedent to the surveillance capitalist corporation: its structure, moral content, and relationship to society." *(Directly stated)*

The movement's founding gesture was an inversion of who serves whom. In Zuboff's account, Jensen and Meckling "took an ax to the pro-social principles of the twentieth-century corporation," and "in 1976... published a landmark article in which they reinterpreted the manager as a sort of parasite feeding off the host of ownership: unavoidable, perhaps, but nonetheless an obstacle to shareholder wealth." The "structural disconnect between owners and managers," they argued, "can result in the value of the firm being substantially lower than it otherwise could be." The remedy was a single disciplinary metric: "to assert the market's signal of value, the share price, as the basis for a new incentive structure intended to finally and decisively align managerial behavior with owners' interests." Managers who resisted "the ineffable signals of Hayek's 'extended order'" would "become prey to the 'barbarians at the gate' in a new and vicious hunt for unrealized market value." *(Directly stated)*

Zuboff frames the movement against Karl Polanyi's "double movement" — the "network of measures and policies... designed to check the action of the market relative to labor, land, and money" that had tethered the market form to society's needs through the Progressive Era, the New Deal, and the Great Society. The shareholder value movement is precisely what demolished that tether: "The double movement was scheduled for demolition under the neoliberal flag," and 1976, the year of Jensen and Meckling's article, is also the year Jimmy Carter "initiated the first significant efforts to radically align the corporation with Wall Street's market metrics." By century's end "'shareholder value maximization' was widely accepted as the 'objective function' of the firm," and these "principles, culled from a once-extremist philosophy, were canonized as standard practice." *(Directly stated)*

The consequence Zuboff stresses is the hollowing-out of the corporation as a durable social institution: capitalism's logic "shifted from the profitable production of goods and services to increasingly exotic forms of financial speculation," by 1989 Jensen could proclaim "the eclipse of the public corporation," and "by 2000, US public corporations employed fewer than half as many Americans as they did in 1970." This stripped-down, owner-aligned, speculation-driven firm — answerable only to a market signal, freed from reciprocities with customers and employees — is the institutional template that the new internet firms would inherit and that surveillance capitalism would fill with a novel revenue logic ([[Behavioral Surplus]] and prediction markets). The share price as sole master, in Zuboff's genealogy, is one antecedent of an economic order that would later subordinate human experience itself to the imperatives of market value. *(Strongly implied)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — Names the movement, dates it (Jensen and Meckling, 1976), and treats it as "another crucial antecedent to the surveillance capitalist corporation." It is the operationalization of Hayek's free-market creed at the level of the firm: manager reinterpreted as parasite on ownership, share price installed as the disciplinary incentive metric, and the Polanyian "double movement" that bound the market to society slated for demolition. *(Directly stated)*

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the shareholder value movement is one of its antecedents; it produces the stripped-down, market-disciplined corporate form that surveillance capitalism inhabits and weaponizes.
- [[Second Modernity]] — the same neoliberal paradigm whose "institutionalized shifts in economic and social policies" destabilize the second modernity also expresses itself, at the corporate level, as the shareholder value movement; together they form the habitat in which surveillance capitalism takes root.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the novel revenue source that fills the hollowed-out, share-price-maximizing firm once "the profitable production of goods and services" gives way to extraction.
- [[Dictatorship of Capital]] — Han's name for capital's absolute imperative resonates with Zuboff's "absolute authority of market forces... enshrined as the ultimate source of imperative control"; the share price is that dictatorship's operative signal inside the firm.
- [[Capital as Religion]] — the "once-extremist philosophy" of shareholder value, "canonized as standard practice" and venerated as the firm's "objective function," is a corporate liturgy of the market's "version of truth."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the movement promises managerial discipline through the "freedom" of the market while imposing the share price as an instrument of "imperative control," subordinating both managers and the social compact to a single market metric.
- Polanyi's *double movement* — the countervailing "network of measures and policies" that tethered the market to society, which the shareholder value movement was explicitly designed to demolish. *(Directly stated)*

## Questions It Raises

- If the share price is installed as the sole "objective function" of the firm, what becomes of the corporation's prior reciprocities with customers, employees, and society — and is surveillance capitalism's indifference to its "users" the predictable terminus of that logic?
- Zuboff calls the movement an "antecedent" rather than a cause of surveillance capitalism — how tight is the genealogical link between maximizing shareholder value and monetizing behavioral surplus?
- Does the reinterpretation of the manager as "parasite feeding off the host of ownership" anticipate the later reinterpretation of the *user* as the host from which behavioral surplus is extracted?
- If the "double movement" once spontaneously emerged to check the self-regulating market, what countermovement, if any, could check surveillance capitalism?
