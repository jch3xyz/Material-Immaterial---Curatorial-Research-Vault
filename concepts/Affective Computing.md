# Affective Computing

## Brief Definition

Affective computing is the field of machine emotion analysis — emotion analytics and sentiment analysis — that renders both conscious and unconscious human emotion as observable behavioral data available for coding, calculation, and prediction; in Shoshana Zuboff's account it is the deepest layer of `[[Rendition]]`, reaching "the unconscious—where feelings form before there are words to express them."

## Longer Explanation

In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, affective computing names the "depth dimension" of rendition at its furthest reach. Where `[[The Body as Data]]` renders the behaving body and the location of the phone, affective computing dives "deeper toward the ocean floor," laying claim to "yet a new frontier of rendition trained not only on your personality but also on your emotional life." Zuboff treats the field, together with its synonyms "emotion analytics" and "sentiment analysis," as "a burgeoning new domain of rendition and behavioral surplus supply operations." Its precondition is total: "your unconscious—where feelings form before there are words to express them—must be recast as simply one more source of raw-material supply for machine rendition and analysis, all of it for the sake of more-perfect prediction." *Directly stated* (Ch.9). This is what makes it the *deepest* rendition: it does not stop at what the subject can articulate but targets the pre-linguistic interior where feeling forms.

Zuboff narrates a precise genealogy. The conception of emotions as observable behavioral data "first took root in the mid-1960s with the work of Paul Ekman," whose principle "actions speak louder than words" held that some nonverbal behaviors "escape control and provide leakage." In 1978 Ekman and Wallace Friesen published the Facial Action Coding System (FACS), which broke facial movement into twenty-seven "action units" and was later anchored by six "basic emotions" (anger, fear, sadness, enjoyment, disgust, and surprise). "FACS and the six-emotion model became the dominant paradigm for the study of facial expression and emotion." *Directly stated* (Ch.9). The computational program "began innocently enough" with MIT Media Lab professor Rosalind Picard, who coined the field's name and in 1997 published *Affective Computing*. "The key to affective computing, Picard argued, was to render both conscious and unconscious emotion as observable behavior for coding and calculation. A computer, she reasoned, would be able to render your emotions as behavioral information." *Directly stated* (Ch.9). Affect recognition, in Picard's terms, is "a pattern recognition problem."

The arc Zuboff draws is one of capture by economic gravity. Picard imagined her tools as benign — an "affective mirror" coaching a job interview, aids for autistic children — and insisted that "Picard's data were intended to be for you, not merely about you," even voicing fears about manipulation by "an army of sales people" and "malevolent" governments. But "safeguards lagged as surveillance capitalism flourished": by 2014 Facebook had filed an "emotion detection" patent; the affective-computing market was forecast to grow from $9.35 billion in 2015 to $53.98 billion in 2021, driven by "rising demand for mapping human emotions especially by the marketing and advertising sector." Zuboff's image is decisive: "Picard's good intentions were like so many innocent iron filings in the presence of a magnet as the market demand exerted by the prediction imperative drew affective computing into surveillance capitalism's powerful force field." *Directly stated* (Ch.9). Picard's own firm, Affectiva (cofounded with Rana el Kaliouby), enacts the trajectory — its "transformation from doing good to doing surveillance capitalism is a metaphor for the fate of the larger undertaking." Firms such as Realeyes (SEWA: "Automatic Sentiment Analysis in the Wild") scour "faces, voices, gestures, bodies, and brains" to read "micro-expressions… emotions, moods, stress, deceit, boredom, confusion, intentions… all at the speed of life," and Affectiva markets "emotion as a service."

Affective computing therefore supplies a "rarified new qualit[y] of behavioral surplus" specifically aimed at the moment of vulnerability — extracting from "your inner life in order to predict what you will buy and the precise moment at which you are most vulnerable to a push." It is the point where `[[Rendition]]` becomes, in Zuboff's words, "most pernicious": "Intimate territories of the self, like personality and emotion, are claimed as observable behavior and coveted for their rich deposits of predictive surplus." *Directly stated* (Ch.9). The technique "disrupts the very notion of the autonomous individual by rewarding 'boundarylessness,'" so that the self is left "cornered… there is no escape."

## Questions It Raises

- If affective computing claims "the unconscious—where feelings form before there are words," is there any pre-linguistic interior that rendition structurally cannot reach, or does depth-rendition abolish the very idea of an inviolable self?
- Picard intended affect data "for you, not merely about you." Can a technology of emotion-reading be designed so its outputs belong to the subject, or does `[[The Prediction Imperative]]` inevitably bend it "about" the subject for others' profit?
- The "iron filings / magnet" image makes good intentions structurally irrelevant. If individual ethics are powerless against market gravity, what level of intervention (law, design, prohibition) could resist affective rendition?
- How does machine-extracted emotion relate to Han's `[[Emotional Capitalism]]` and the distinction in `[[Feeling vs Emotion vs Affect (Han)]]` — is "affect" as the affective-computing industry measures it the same object philosophy names, or a behaviorist substitute?
