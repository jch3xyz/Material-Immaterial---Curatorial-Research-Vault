# The Cybernated Society

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for the coming automated, instant-communication, leisured social order — a state that differs from the industrial age "not merely in degree but in kind," producing a participatory, producer-oriented "cybernetic-politic" rather than a mass-production dystopia.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott the cybernated society is the social horizon already "rapidly forming" in the 1960s, in which "processes of retroaction, instant communication, and autonomic flexibility will inform every aspect of our environment" (`Directly stated`). It is not a forecast of more machines but of a qualitatively different culture: the reduction of labour "to a minimum" frees a "new leisured class," so that "the creative use of leisure time" becomes "the main preoccupation of people's lives" (`Directly stated`). Ascott treats this transition as the ground condition that the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] is designed to serve — its three functions (servicing committed artists, providing creative play for the leisured class, and re-founding art education for the young) are explicitly tied to "the needs of our forming cybernated society."

The keystone of the concept is Ascott's insistence on a difference *in kind*, not degree. He attacks "a common fallacy to equate an automated world with the old industrial era, holding that cybernation is merely mechanisation intensified" (`Directly stated`). Against this he argues that cybernation reorganizes power and production: socially and politically "the old rigid hierarchies of power are rendered obsolete," giving way through "instant communications and rapid feedback" to "more viable and adaptive procedures" — to "what might become, in short, a cybernetic-politic" (`Directly stated`). This is the link to [[Decentralization]]: the cybernated society dissolves centralized hierarchy into a feedback-governed, distributed order, and stands opposed to the [[Society of Control]] reading in which pervasive networking is domination rather than emancipation (`Interpretive synthesis`).

Ascott also overturns the mass-production critique. He grants "another mistaken fear is of an increase in the uniformity of the environment due to mass production," but counters that "the very nature of automation is such as to facilitate the production of the unique and the custom-built at assembly-line cheapness and speed" (`Directly stated`). From this follows the concept's defining inversion: "Whereas the mechanical age led to a high degree of consumer-orientation, the cybernated society will be emphatically producer-oriented. Participation of a more direct kind in all aspects of social organisation and design, entertainment and learning, will replace mere acceptance and consumption" (`Directly stated`). The cybernated society is thus, for Ascott, the social precondition of [[Reciprocal Communication]] — a two-way, participatory order in which the spectator/citizen becomes a producer.

The cybernetic vision in art is the cultural face of this society. Ascott frames the artist's choice starkly: either to "be carried along in the stream of events, mindlessly," or to "come to terms with his world, shape it and develop it by understanding its underlying cybernetic characteristics," so as to "construct a vision in art that will enhance the cybernated society as much as it will be enriched by it" (`Directly stated`). He cites Marshall McLuhan's claim (from [[The Global Village|Understanding Media]]) that media "alter sense ratios or patterns of perception steadily and without resistance" to argue that the cybernated society works on perception at the level of "deep human experience," not merely opinion (`Directly stated`). In this way the concept underwrites the vault's [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] foci: art's task is no longer to transmit "finite messages" but to "provide a matrix for ideas and feelings" within a participatory, cybernated culture (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and develops the term across "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" (1966–67): the cybernated society is the automated, leisured, instant-communication order whose difference from industrialism is "in kind," whose politics is a decentralized "cybernetic-politic," and whose economy is "producer-oriented." It is the social rationale for the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]].
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — not a user of "cybernated society," but Ascott's named authority for the claim that the electric/cybernetic environment reshapes perception below the level of conscious choice; the convergence is on the cybernated order as a perceptual and social transformation, cross-linked through [[The Global Village]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Cybernetics]] — the science whose "spirit" (l'esprit cybernétique) Ascott reads as the animating principle of the forming society.
- [[Decentralization]] — the cybernated society renders "rigid hierarchies of power" obsolete in favour of feedback-governed, distributed procedures.
- [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — the participatory framework Ascott proposes to serve the cybernated society's leisured class and creative needs.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the two-way, participatory mode of relation the producer-oriented society makes possible.
- [[New Media Art]] — the art practice native to the cybernated society's communication channels.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — art as a matrix for participant-constructed experience rather than transmitted message.
- [[The Global Village]] — McLuhan's parallel image of an electrically retribalized, interconnected social order.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Society of Control]] — Deleuze/Han's reading in which pervasive networked feedback is a refined form of domination; Ascott's emancipatory, producer-oriented cybernated society is its optimistic mirror image (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Questions It Raises

- Does cybernation's promised shift from consumer-orientation to producer-orientation actually arrive, or does it reinstate consumption in subtler form (a tension with the [[Society of Control]] and [[Digital Panopticon]] critiques)?
- Is the "cybernetic-politic" genuinely a flattening of hierarchy, or does decentralized feedback conceal new centres of control?
- Ascott grounds creative flourishing in mass leisure produced by automation; what happens to the cybernated-society thesis if automation produces precarity rather than a leisured class?
- How far does Ascott's mid-1960s optimism survive the vault's later diagnoses of networked life (Han, Harari) — is the cybernated society the same phenomenon viewed from the other side?
