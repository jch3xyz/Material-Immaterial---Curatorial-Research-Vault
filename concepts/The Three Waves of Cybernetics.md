# The Three Waves of Cybernetics

## Brief Definition

[[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s organizing periodization of [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] thought into three overlapping phases—first-wave [[Homeostasis|homeostasis]] (1945–1960), second-wave [[Reflexivity|reflexivity]]/[[Autopoiesis|autopoiesis]] (1960–1980), and third-wave virtuality/[[Emergence|emergence]] and artificial life (1980–present)—whose transitions she models not as Kuhnian ruptures but as seriated patterns of overlapping replication and innovation.

## Longer Explanation

The three waves are the architecture of *[[How We Became Posthuman]]*. Hayles states the schema in a single sentence in chapter 1: "The first, from 1945 to 1960, took homeostasis as a central concept; the second, going roughly from 1960 to 1980, revolved around reflexivity; and the third, stretching from 1980 to the present, highlights virtuality." Each wave is parented by the discipline of [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] but driven by a different organizing concept, and the book's chapters are distributed across the three: an "anchoring chapter discussing the scientific theories: on the Macy Conferences (chapter 3); on autopoiesis (chapter 6); and on artificial life (chapter 9)." The waves are thus less a chronology than a map of which concepts cohered into a dominant "constellation" at each moment, and what that constellation did to the body and to [[Information|information]].

The **first wave (1945–1960)** crystallized at the Macy Conferences, where "Norbert Wiener, John von Neumann, Claude Shannon, Warren McCulloch, and dozens of other distinguished researchers met at annual conferences sponsored by the Josiah Macy Foundation to formulate the central concepts that... would coalesce into a theory of communication and control applying equally to animals, humans, and machines." Its organizing concept is [[Homeostasis|homeostasis]]: "the ability of living organisms to maintain steady states when they are buffeted by fickle environments," now "extended to machines" that "can maintain homeostasis using feedback loops." Homeostasis served, in Hayles's reading, to keep the period's subversive pressures "under control"—it "implied a return to normalcy in more than one sense" in a postwar, McCarthyite atmosphere—while it was here that information first "lost its body."

The **second wave (1960–1980)** "grew out of attempts to incorporate reflexivity into the cybernetic paradigm at a fundamental level." Initiated by Heinz von Foerster's "second-order cybernetics," which "extended cybernetic principles to the cyberneticians themselves," it reached its mature phase with [[Humberto Maturana|Maturana]] and [[Francisco Varela|Varela]]'s theory of [[Autopoiesis|autopoiesis]], which "sees the world as a set of informationally closed systems." Here [[Reflexivity|reflexivity]]—"the movement whereby that which has been used to generate a system is made, through a changed perspective, to become part of the system it generates"—shifts "the center of interest... from the cybernetics of the observed system to the cybernetics of the observer." Autopoiesis, Hayles writes, "turns the cybernetic paradigm inside out," because in it "no information crosses the boundary separating the system from its environment."

The **third wave (1980–present)** "swelled into existence when self-organization began to be understood not merely as the (re)production of internal organization but as the springboard to [[Emergence|emergence]]." Its exemplary field is artificial life, where "computer programs are designed to allow 'creatures'... to evolve spontaneously in directions the programmer may not have anticipated"; "the intent is to evolve the capacity to evolve." If the second wave's figure is the circle, "the figure most apt to describe the third wave is a spiral"—the system is pushed to "break out of the pattern of circular self-organization and leap outward into the new." It is in this wave that the disembodying tendency reaches its limit case: the claim that informational "creatures" are not models of life but "are themselves alive," and that humans are "programs that run on the cosmic computer."

Crucially, Hayles insists the waves do not supersede one another by clean break. "The development of cybernetics followed neither a Kuhnian model of incommensurable paradigms nor a Foucauldian model of sharp epistemic breaks." Instead it followed [[Seriation|seriation]] (a term "appropriated from archaeological anthropology"): "a pattern of overlapping replication and innovation." A superseded organizing concept survives in the next wave as a *skeuomorph*—"a design feature that is no longer functional in itself but that refers back to a feature that was functional at an earlier time." "Homeostasis, a foundational concept during the first wave, functioned during the second wave as a skeuomorph," and "Reflexivity, the key concept of the second wave, is displaced in the third wave by emergence" yet "lingers on as an allusion that authenticates new elements." Varela himself embodies the seriated transition: his late turn toward "self-evolving" dynamics means "autopoietic theory begins to look less like the homeostasis of the first wave and more like the self-evolving programs... as exemplars of third-wave cybernetics." This note is the parent of the per-wave concept notes; [[Cybernetics]] is the name of the whole field, [[Seriation]] the historiographic method by which Hayles charts the waves' overlap.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — author of the periodization; treats the three waves as a seriated history (homeostasis → reflexivity → virtuality/emergence) whose purpose is to show "the complex interplays between embodied forms of subjectivity and arguments for disembodiment throughout the cybernetic tradition," and to make visible how much "had to be erased to arrive at such abstractions as bodiless information."
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** — the figure of the first wave; coined "cybernetics" and presided over the Macy synthesis, yet "did not intend to dismantle the liberal humanist subject."
- **[[Humberto Maturana]]** and **[[Francisco Varela]]** — authors of the second-wave epistemology of autopoiesis; Varela then "bridges the transition between the second and third waves," carrying autopoiesis toward enaction, embodiment, and the self-evolving systems of artificial life.

## Related Concepts

- [[Cybernetics]] — the parent discipline; the three waves are its internal history, not three separate fields.
- [[Homeostasis]] — organizing concept of the first wave (1945–1960); later a skeuomorph in the second.
- [[Reflexivity]] — organizing concept of the second wave (1960–1980); later a skeuomorph in the third.
- [[Autopoiesis]] — the mature second-wave theory; spun off "from the idea of reflexive feedback loops."
- [[Emergence]] — organizing concept of the third wave (1980–present) and of artificial life.
- [[Seriation]] — the historiographic method (overlapping replication and innovation, with skeuomorphs) by which Hayles models the wave transitions, against Kuhn and Foucault.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the device common to all three waves; "initially linked with homeostasis," it "quickly led to the more threatening and subversive idea of reflexivity."
- [[Posthumanism]] — the figure of the human the three-wave history produces; by the third wave "the human is transfigured into the posthuman."
- [[The Body as Data]] — the disembodying outcome the waves cumulatively install, as information is progressively abstracted from materiality.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the term Hayles recovers against the trajectory the three waves trace; she narrates the periodization precisely to denaturalize "the teleology of disembodiment" and to show that "the disembodiment of information was not inevitable."
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the tension the wave-history installs and that Hayles's account works to contest; the first wave divests information of its body, the second insists "information without a body does not exist," the third returns to "the strong claim" that the "logical form" of life is "separated from its material basis."

## Questions It Raises

- If each wave's organizing concept survives into the next as a skeuomorph rather than being discarded, can the "disembodiment" installed in the first wave ever be fully undone, or does it persist as an inertial pull on every subsequent constellation? (ties to [[Embodiment]], [[Seriation]])
- The first wave makes the human an information-processing system, the third makes informational "creatures" alive—does the three-wave arc describe a deepening of [[The Body as Data|the body as data]], or, via the second wave's autopoietic insistence that "information without a body does not exist," contain its own resources of resistance? (ties to [[The Body as Data]], [[Embodiment vs Information]])
- Why does Hayles insist on seriation against Kuhnian rupture—what is at stake, for the politics of [[Posthumanism|the posthuman]], in showing that the move "from embodied reality to abstract information" was "historically contingent" rather than an "inexorable logic"? (ties to [[Posthumanism]], [[Material vs Immaterial]])
- If the third wave treats "the essential function of the universe as a whole" as "processing information," what does the body's "resistance of materiality" do to that ideology—and on which side of the computer screen does it do its "useful work"? (ties to [[Material vs Immaterial]], [[Embodiment]])
