# Object and System

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's structural axis for the whole of modern sculpture: the **object** is palpable, finite, place-bound, inert, and durable — a result of the human formative urge — while the **system** is an aggregate of mutually dependent components that may manifest the characteristics of life (self-organization, growth, input and output, equilibrium, death), into which the object has been demoted to a replaceable component.

## Longer Explanation

"Object and System" is the section heading that closes Burnham's Introduction and names the two parts of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*: "The two parts of this book divide modern sculpture respectively into object and system." It is not a thesis so much as the conceptual coordinate system within which the book's argument — that science and technology have driven sculpture from inert form toward life-simulating systems — is staged. `Directly stated`

The **object** is the older, originary pole. From prehistory the object has been "palpable, finite in dimensions, varying in preciousness, a direct result of the human formative urge," and the means by which the environment "has been subdivided into transportable units." Its defining attribute is place: "Above all the object occupies a specific space: it has place, remaining inert and stationary." For Burnham the sculpted object in particular has provided "a large and unreplaceable record of all cultural history" through its durability and compactness. `Directly stated`

The decisive historical claim is that the object has lost its independent status. In a systematized, industrial environment "the object now is a replaceable component in an interlocking system of production and need fulfillment." Burnham's signature illustration is the automobile-as-leased-system: buying a car is no longer buying an object "but instead we purchase a three- to five-year lease for participation" in a web of overlapping systems (private transportation, highway, traffic-safety, parts-replacement, insurance, advertising, recreation, and "the general economic system"). "To a marked extent the object has lost its independent status in technological society." `Directly stated`

The **system** is the emergent successor pole, defined by life-properties: "A system is an aggregate of components; first, its parts are mutually dependent; and second, it may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life: self-organization, growth, internal or external mobility, irritability or sensitivity, input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death (humans are the most dramatic instances of systems which die)." The system is "a logical extension of the object through intellectual domination of the environment." `Directly stated` Across Part Two, Burnham tracks the object→system migration through specific media: Kinetic sculpture is "not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art" that "may need repair and adjustment from time to time"; emitted light "best demonstrates one of the primary qualities of systems: the tendency to fuse art object and environment into a perceptual whole"; and in cybernetic/robot art "we are moving toward a systems view of art, and, as the old attributes of the sculpted object are dropped one by one, the sculptor will begin to think about increasing the life duration and stability of his mechanical creations." `Directly stated`

The opposition is ultimately one of ontology and value. "It becomes clear that with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." `Directly stated` The system, for Burnham, becomes the universal analytic unit: "Stated differently, the system becomes the unit by which all levels of fluid organization are analyzed up through and including living organisms." This makes "Object and System" the direct sculptural correlate of the vault's canonical [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension — physical presence and place yielding to information, organization, and process. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — coins the pairing as the organizing axis of *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968). The object is the inert, place-bound result of the "formative urge"; the system is the mutually-dependent, life-property-bearing aggregate that supersedes it. The transition (object → system) is for Burnham the survival strategy of sculpture under the pressure of technics, and it culminates in [[Systems Esthetics]] and cybernetic/[[Cybernetics]] art. The vocabulary of mutually-dependent components, input/output, equilibrium, and feedback he draws explicitly from [[Systems Theory]] and from cybernetics (Wiener, Bertalanffy, Ashby, Grey Walter).

## Related Concepts

- [[Systems Theory]] — the source vocabulary; Burnham's "system" (aggregate of mutually-dependent components manifesting life-properties) is general systems theory imported into sculpture.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's name for the artistic program that follows from prioritizing the system over the object; the title concept of his 1968 *Artforum* essay (forward-stub for this ingest).
- [[Cybernetics]] — supplies the system's defining mechanisms (feedback, homeostasis, input/output, coupling) that let the work "manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life."
- [[Dematerialization]] — Lippard's parallel diagnosis of the object's dissolution in conceptual art; Burnham's object→system shift is its sculptural/technological counterpart.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the canonical vault tension this concept instantiates: the object as palpable physical presence and place versus the system as information, organization, and process ("for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor").

## Questions It Raises

- If the object has become a replaceable component "in an interlocking system," is there any residual ontological difference between artwork-as-object and commodity-as-leased-system?
- Burnham defines the system by its capacity to die ("eventual death"); does an aesthetics organized around finite life-spans abandon the museum's premise of durable preservation?
- "Where do we define where the object leaves off and the system begins?" — Burnham's own unanswered question; is the boundary a matter of scale, of feedback, or only of the observer's act of isolating a "singleness"?
