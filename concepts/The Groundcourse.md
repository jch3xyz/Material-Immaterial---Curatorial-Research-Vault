# The Groundcourse

## Brief Definition

The two-year foundation course Roy Ascott initiated and directed (Ealing School of Art, London, and later Ipswich), run as a cybernetic feedback system in which creative and pedagogic activity continuously feed back into each other — disorienting students, having them act out contrived converse personalities, organising them into six-member "sexagonal organisms," and equipping them with "calibrators" and "mind-maps" for self-monitoring.

## Longer Explanation

The Groundcourse is the practical, institutional embodiment of Ascott's claim that "my art and didactics are one." Where his "Change Painting" and kinetic constructions located the artwork in a behavioural loop between artist, work, and participating spectator, the Groundcourse turns *art education itself* into such a loop: a curriculum built so that change is its substance, in which teaching and making are not separate stages but a single regulated system. Ascott describes the course as "a microcosm of a total process of art education" occupying "the pivotal point" between secondary schooling and professional training — and, crucially, as a living thing, "an organism" still "in the course of evolution" rather than a fixed syllabus (`Directly stated`). The driving conviction is that the two activities, creative and pedagogic, "interact, each feeding back to the other," and that "Both, I believe, are enriched" — the cybernetic image of mutual regulation applied to pedagogy (`Directly stated`).

The first year sets out to dismantle the student's inherited picture of art and of his own capacities. Preconceptions ("Art is Van Gogh"; "I am no good at colour") "must first be severely shaken," and the student's "disorientation is contrived within an environment that is sometimes unexpectedly confusing, where he is faced with problems that seem absurd, aimless, or terrifying" (`Directly stated`). Disorientation here is not cruelty but method: it opens the student's behaviour to scrutiny so it can be reshaped. "Cybernetics and behavioural sciences are studied regularly," and "At the core is a concept of power, the will to shape and change" (`Directly stated`).

In the second year the apparatus of self-monitoring appears. Students are "set the task of acquiring and acting out for a limited period (ten weeks) a totally new personality, which is to be narrowly limited and largely the converse of what is considered to be their normal 'selves'" — the shy student forced into sociability, the aggressive one into cooperation. To track this contrived behaviour they "design 'calibrators' to read off their responses to situations, materials, tools, and people" and "equip themselves with handy 'mind-maps' for immediate reference to their behaviour pattern" as the limitations of "space, substance, and state" change (`Directly stated`). Calibrators and mind-maps are the feedback instruments of the cybernetic self: devices for measuring and steering one's own behaviour, the pedagogic equivalent of an organism that "knows its own internal order."

Students then "form groups of six. These sexagonal organisms, whose members are of necessity interdependent and highly conscious of one another's capabilities and limitations, are set the goal of producing an ordered entity out of substances and space in their environment" (`Directly stated`). The six-member unit is itself a system — interdependent parts whose constrained, calibrated behaviours must self-organise into a whole ("Totems, time machines, sense boxes, films, sexagonal cabinets, and cages"). The Groundcourse thus enacts at the scale of a classroom the relational, behavioural view of art Ascott was already practising at the scale of the single work, and it is the distant ancestor of his later large-scale projects — the Cybernetic Art Matrix and, decades on, the Planetary Collegium — which extend the same principle of a coordinated, feeding-back community into the telematic network (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — sole author of the concept. The Groundcourse is the concrete, institutional realization of his behaviourist-cybernetic theory of art, articulated in his earliest essay (1964) as the second half of an argument that begins by recommending the artist "turn to cybernetics." Pedagogy and studio practice are deliberately fused: "When I emphasise that my art and didactics are one, I am suggesting that the artist's discipline and experience can usefully be extended to the student."

## Related Concepts

- [[Behaviourist Art]] — the Groundcourse is this concept's pedagogic form; both treat behaviour as the medium and the artwork (or the student) as a system to be shaped within set limits.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the organising principle: creative and pedagogic activity "feeding back to the other," and the calibrators/mind-maps as instruments of self-regulating feedback.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the integrative science Ascott recommends to the artist; the Groundcourse applies its "control and communication" frame to art education.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the Groundcourse makes the educational situation an experiential, behaviour-driven technology rather than a transmission of style.
- [[New Media Art]] — the participatory/behavioural lineage of 1960s cybernetic art that this pedagogy seeds.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the mutual feeding-back between teacher and student mirrors the spectator-as-participant loop of the artwork.

## Opposing Concepts

- The traditional master-and-apprentice or style-transmission model of the art school — a fixed curriculum transmitting taste — against which the Groundcourse sets a disorienting, self-monitoring, evolving system. (`Strongly implied`; Ascott does not name an opposing concept, but the contrast with the student's inherited preconceptions ("Art is what my teacher said it was") is explicit.)

## Questions It Raises

- Is the "contrived disorientation" and the imposed converse personality a liberating opening of behaviour, or a behaviourist programme of control exercised on the student? Where is the line between feedback and conditioning?
- Can a cybernetic, self-monitoring pedagogy scale from a classroom (the six-member organism) to a planetary network (the Cybernetic Art Matrix, the Planetary Collegium) without losing the contained intimacy that makes the feedback legible?
- What is preserved and what is lost when art education is modelled on "control and communication in animal and machine" rather than on craft transmission or individual expression?
