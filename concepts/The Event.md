# The Event

## Brief Definition

The event (*Ereignis*) is the absolutely sudden, incalculable rupture that annuls the standing order, defies all calculation and prediction, and "breaks the subject open and wrests it from subjection" — for Han, the structural site of emancipation and precisely the outside that predictive Big-Data psychopolitics cannot pre-empt.

## Longer Explanation

In the chapter "Beyond the Subject," Han locates the event at the limit of neoliberal psychopolitics. Where psychopolitics is "a technology of domination that stabilizes and perpetuates the prevailing system by means of psychological programming and steering," the event is what such programming cannot anticipate. It "annuls what has held until now — the standing order" and "proves just as incalculable and abrupt as a natural disaster or act of God." Because it "defies all calculation and prediction," it falls outside the predictive grasp of Big Data; when it occurs, "an entirely new state of affairs begins." (`Directly stated`)

The event's emancipatory force is that it "brings into play an outside, which breaks the subject open and wrests it from subjection." Han routes this through Nietzsche's "naturification" (*Vernatürlichung*) of the human being, which "includes 'readiness for what is absolutely sudden and thwarting.'" Being a subject, for Han, "means being subjected, being cast under, by a higher instance"; the event — like genuine experience — "tears the subject out from subjection — out of its downcast state." It is therefore the opposite of the engineered emotion of psychopolitics, "which only ensnares the subject deeper and deeper in the state of subjection and subjugation." (`Directly stated`)

Han also follows Foucault, for whom "'Event' … means 'the reversal of a relationship of forces, the usurpation of power, the appropriation of a vocabulary against those who had once used it.'" Events are "turns (*Kehren*), where a reversal — an overthrow — of domination occurs," and after such a reversal "one suddenly speaks another language." The event "entails a break with all certainty that has held until now inasmuch as it calls to life a wholly different constellation of Being." It is thus the singular that the regime of the average and the probable cannot contain — the gap through which the system's predictive closure is broken. (`Directly stated`)

Read against the data-driven order, the event names what statistics structurally exclude: not the average and the probable that [[Dataism|dataism]] extrapolates from accumulated data, but the singular discontinuity that "was wholly absent from conditions beforehand." This is why Han treats it as the site of freedom — emancipation, on his account, depends on an outside that prediction cannot foreclose. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — The event is the incalculable rupture that the predictive apparatus of psychopolitics and Big Data cannot pre-empt; it breaks the subject open, wrests it from subjection, and opens "new spaces for action." It is the structural condition of freedom and "an unwritten future." In *The Agony of Eros* Han grounds the same anti-calculation core from the side of love, following Badiou: the event's essence is "the negativity of rupture, which allows something wholly Other to begin," and "eventfulness connects love with politics and art," all commanding "fidelity" — a "transcendental fidelity" that is "a universal quality of eros." Stripped of eros, "logos is deteriorating into data-driven calculation, which is incapable of reckoning the event, the incalculable."
- **[[Friedrich Nietzsche]]** *(forward-stub)* — Source of the "naturification" (*Vernatürlichung*) Han invokes, which includes "readiness for what is absolutely sudden and thwarting"; the event is the natural, incalculable break this readiness opens onto.
- **[[Michel Foucault]]** *(forward-stub)* — Defines the event as "the reversal of a relationship of forces, the usurpation of power, the appropriation of a vocabulary against those who had once used it"; events are *Kehren* (turns) after which "one suddenly speaks another language."
- **Alain Badiou** *(forward-stub)* — In *In Praise of Love* and *Ethics*, source (via Han) of the love-event: the "Two scene," the demand to "completely rework my ordinary way of 'living' [habiter] my situation," and the "fidelity" to the event that binds love, politics, and art.

## Related Concepts

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the event is Han's structural site of emancipation, the outside that control through prediction cannot foreclose.
- [[Big Data]] *(forward-stub)* — the predictive regime whose calculation the event "defies"; the event is its constitutive blind spot.
- [[Eros]] — for Han in *The Agony of Eros*, "fidelity" to the event "may be understood as a universal quality of eros"; without eros, logos degrades into calculation blind to the event.
- [[The Other]] — the event is "the negativity of rupture, which allows something wholly Other to begin"; the incalculable Other is precisely what data-driven calculation cannot reckon.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Dataism]] — dataism operates on the average and the probable extrapolated from accumulated data; the event is the singular discontinuity that "was wholly absent from conditions beforehand" and "defies all calculation and prediction." The opposition is the Average/Probable (Dataism) vs. the Singular/Event.
- [[Big Data]] *(forward-stub)* — Big Data seeks total predictive transparency of behavior; the event is precisely what it cannot anticipate or pre-empt.

## Questions It Raises

- If the event "defies all calculation and prediction," can it be cultivated, prepared for, or curated at all — or only made room for through Nietzschean "readiness"?
- Is the appeal to an incalculable "outside" a genuine politics of emancipation, or does it risk romanticizing rupture while leaving the standing order's everyday operations untouched?
- Does ever-expanding predictive capacity (Big Data) actually shrink the space of the event, or does the singular remain structurally irreducible to the average and the probable?
- How does the event differ from mere novelty or accident, given that for Han it specifically "wrests the subject from subjection" rather than simply surprising it?
- If the event is "a universal quality of eros," does the erosion of eros under neoliberalism (its degradation into sexuality and calculation) foreclose the event across love, politics, and thought alike?
