# Disciplinary Society

> [!note] Han-anchored realization
> This note is grounded in Han's primary text — *The Burnout Society* (2010) — where disciplinary society functions as the historical predecessor that the [[Achievement Society]] supersedes. The concept originates with [[Michel Foucault]] (*Discipline and Punish*), but Foucault has no book in `raw/` yet, so `[[Michel Foucault]]` stays a forward-stub author and his framework is cited here only as mediated through Han. A fuller treatment **awaits a future [[Michel Foucault]] author-section** when a Foucault book is ingested. The same pattern realized [[Biopolitics]] in the Psychopolitics ingest.

## Brief Definition

Disciplinary society is Foucault's regime of "hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories" — a society of negativity governed by prohibition (the modal verb "May Not") and compulsion ("Should"), which fashions the "obedience-subject" through walls, thresholds, and commandments, and which Han argues has "long been replaced" by the [[Achievement Society]] of the achievement-subject and unlimited [[Can vs Should|Can]].

## Longer Explanation

Disciplinary society enters *The Burnout Society* the way [[Biopolitics]] enters *Psychopolitics* — not on its own terms but as the historical foil that Han's title concept supersedes. Han opens the periodization flatly: "Today's society is no longer Foucault's disciplinary world of hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories. It has long been replaced by another regime, namely a society of fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories." Its inhabitants change with it: "its inhabitants are no longer 'obedience-subjects' but 'achievement-subjects.'" The institutional architecture that defined the disciplinary regime — "The walls of disciplinary institutions, which separate the normal from the abnormal" — "have come to seem archaic." (`Directly stated` — Ch.3)

The defining trait of disciplinary society for Han is its [[Negativity|negativity]]. "Disciplinary society is a society of negativity. It is defined by the negativity of prohibition. The negative modal verb that governs it is May Not. By the same token, the negativity of compulsion adheres to Should." Its products are pathological in a way specific to prohibition: "Its negativity produces madmen and criminals." This is precisely what the [[Achievement Society]] overturns — where discipline runs on "no," achievement runs on the "[[Positivity|positive]]" verb [[Can vs Should|Can]]: "Unlimited Can is the positive modal verb of achievement society." Han insists the shift is one of efficiency, not emancipation — "The positivity of Can is much more efficient than the negativity of Should," so "the social unconscious switches from Should to Can" — and that continuity, not rupture, links the two: "the Can does not revoke the Should. The obedience-subject remains disciplined. It has now completed the disciplinary stage." Importantly, Han holds that even the Deleuzian [[Society of Control]] mis-describes the present because "It still contains too much negativity" — disciplinary, control, and achievement society are not interchangeable for him. (`Directly stated` — Ch.3)

The **obedience-subject** is the disciplinary counterpart of the achievement-subject, and it belongs to this note. Han stages it most concretely through Melville's Bartleby: "The society that Melville describes is still a disciplinary society. Walls and partitions, the elements of disciplinary architecture, traverse the entire narrative." The thick-walled prison, the "Tombs," and the recurrent "dead wall" make the architecture of confinement literal — "The wall is always associated with death." Bartleby, Han concludes, "still represents an obedience-subject": he "does not develop symptoms of depression, which is a hallmark of late-modern achievement society," "does not face the imperative to be himself," and "does not fail in the project of being an 'I.'" Because his "Monotonous copying ... leaves no free space in which private initiative would prove necessary, or even possible," what makes Bartleby sick "is not excessive positivity or possibility" — he is the figure of discipline's negativity, not achievement's positivity. (`Directly stated` — Ch.7)

Disciplinary society also has a psychic correlate, which Han locates in the Freudian apparatus (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Sigmund Freud]]). "The ego, as Freud defines it, is a well-known disciplinary subject. Freud's psychic apparatus is a repressive apparatus with commandments and prohibitions that subjugate and repress. Like disciplinary society, the psychic apparatus sets up walls, thresholds, borders, and guards." The Freudian unconscious is therefore historically bounded, not eternal: "It is a product of the disciplinary society, dominated by the negativity of prohibitions and repression, that we have long since left behind." Han carries this into a precise structural shift: "In the transition from disciplinary society to achievement society, the superego positivizes itself into the ego ideal." Where "The superego is repressive" and "rules over the ego with the 'harshness and cruelty' of the 'dictatorial "Thou shalt,"'" the ego ideal instead "seduces" — "the achievement-subject projects itself ... onto the ego ideal, whereas the obedience-subject subjects itself to the superego." This is why, for Han, classical psychoanalysis "offers no way of approaching" depression, burnout, and ADHD: those maladies "indicate an excess of positivity," "not negation so much as the inability to say no." (`Directly stated` — Ch.9)

This framing is the hinge of Han's whole book. Disciplinary society defines [[Achievement Society]] by everything achievement society no longer is: prohibition vs. permission, "May Not" vs. "Can," the obedience-subject vs. the achievement-subject, [[Allo-Exploitation|allo-exploitation]] vs. [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploitation]], [[Negativity]] vs. [[Positivity]]. The disciplinary regime is the predecessor whose supersession Han is diagnosing. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Michel Foucault]]** — origin of the concept. In Han's usage the disciplinary regime is explicitly "Foucault's disciplinary world of hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories," organized through "The walls of disciplinary institutions, which separate the normal from the abnormal." *Forward-stub:* Foucault has no book in the library yet; his usage here is mediated entirely through Han's quotation and paraphrase of the Foucauldian framework (*Discipline and Punish*). This note is kept open for a future Foucault author-section. (`Directly stated`, via Han's citations)
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — adopts disciplinary society only to bound it to the past and contrast it with the present. For Han it is "a society of negativity ... defined by the negativity of prohibition" (the modal verbs "May Not" / "Should"), the regime of the "obedience-subject," with the Freudian "repressive apparatus" of "walls, thresholds, borders, and guards" as its psychic correlate. He judges it "long ... replaced" by the [[Achievement Society]] and faults Foucault's "analysis of power" for being unable to "account for the psychic and topological changes that occurred as disciplinary society transformed into achievement society." (`Directly stated`)
- **[[Sigmund Freud]]** — supplies the psychic model of the disciplinary subject. *Forward-stub:* Freud has no book in `raw/` yet. Han reads the Freudian ego as "a well-known disciplinary subject" whose "repressive apparatus" of "commandments and prohibitions" mirrors disciplinary architecture — historically bounded to "repressive societies." (`Directly stated`, via Han's citations; see [[Byung-Chul Han and Sigmund Freud]])

## Related Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — the defining structure of disciplinary society; it "is defined by the negativity of prohibition" (May Not / Should), as against the [[Positivity]] of achievement society.
- [[Society of Control]] — the Deleuzian successor formation Han considers but rejects as a name for the present, because it "still contains too much negativity"; he places it between discipline and achievement (see [[Discipline vs Control]]).
- [[Biopolitics]] — in *Psychopolitics* Han names biopolitics "the governmental technology of disciplinary power"; biopolitics is the body-and-population correlate of the disciplinary regime described here.
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — the external, dominating exploitation characteristic of the disciplinary regime, superseded by the [[Auto-Exploitation]] of the achievement-subject.
- [[The Panopticon]] — Foucault's emblematic disciplinary apparatus (Bentham via Foucault); related as a symbol, though Han's burnout diagnosis turns away from surveillance toward self-compulsion.
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault]] — the reference note tracking Han's adoption and critique of the Foucauldian framework.
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Sigmund Freud]] — the reference note tracking Han's reading of the Freudian apparatus as the disciplinary subject's psyche.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Achievement Society]] — the defining opposition of the book. Disciplinary society governs through prohibition ("May Not") and compulsion ("Should"), produces obedience-subjects and "madmen and criminals," and runs on [[Negativity]]; achievement society governs through unlimited [[Can vs Should|Can]], produces achievement-subjects and "depressives and losers," and runs on [[Positivity]]. Obedience-subject vs. achievement-subject, superego vs. ego ideal, [[Allo-Exploitation]] vs. [[Auto-Exploitation]] — disciplinary society is precisely the regime achievement society has "long since left behind."
- [[Discipline vs Achievement]] — the tension that holds this opposition.
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — the tension marking Han's third term: he rejects "control society" as a name for the present because it "still contains too much negativity."

## Questions It Raises

- Han's periodization is clean — discipline "long ... replaced" by achievement — yet he also concedes "continuity prevails" and that "The obedience-subject remains disciplined." Does this hedge undermine the rupture his whole argument depends on, or is the disciplinary stage genuinely *completed* rather than abolished? (`Strongly implied` by the Ch.3 text)
- If the disciplinary "walls, thresholds, borders, and guards" have only seemed to vanish, do they persist materially (prisons, borders, factories) for populations Han's "fitness studios and office towers" diagnosis brackets out? Does the burnout thesis describe a class position more than an epoch? (the body as material; who gets to be an achievement-subject)
- Han demotes the [[Society of Control]] for retaining "too much negativity." Is the disciplinary/control/achievement sequence stable, or does it collapse the Deleuzian and Foucauldian breaks into a single Han-shaped story?
- By routing disciplinary society through Freud's "repressive apparatus," Han historicizes the unconscious itself ("the late-modern achievement-subject would no longer have an unconscious"). Is that a genuine epochal claim about the psyche, or a rhetorical device for clearing space for his own diagnosis?
- Awaiting a [[Michel Foucault]] author-section: how much of Foucault's actual analytic of discipline (the docile body, the examination, normalization) survives Han's compression of it into "the negativity of prohibition"?
