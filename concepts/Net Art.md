# Net Art

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for art whose substrate is the Net and whose primary product is the **connectivity of minds** — art that exists as a noetic (mind-to-mind) process before it is a social, visual, or material one, and whose key behaviour is bottom-up **emergence**.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, "Net art" is not a genre of objects displayed online but a redefinition of what art *is* once its medium becomes the network itself. In "The Mind of the Museum" (1996) he writes that "Social life is the concretisation of consciousness, and Net art is the product of the connectivity of minds before it is anything else. Noetic process precedes social process." The claim is ordered deliberately: connectivity of *minds* comes first, social life is its concretisation, and visible form (if any) comes later still. Engaging with art in the Net is therefore "taking the first steps in a new way of being and a new way of ordering our experience and of shaping the world" — an epistemic and ontological act, not the consumption of a picture. It "requires a new kind of perception, [[Cyberception|cyberception]]." `Directly stated`.

The behavioural model Ascott gives for this art is **emergence**, borrowed explicitly from artificial life. "Emergence is the key behaviour in the Net. It is the key to understanding what art on the Net is all about." He draws the parallel between artificial life and interactive art through Christopher Langton's description of artificial life as "bottom-up, distributed" interactions "with no global controller responsible for the behavior of every part" — and locates that same logic "within the implicate art of the Internet." The Net artist "plant[s] conceptual seeds in the substrate of the Net" and watches "new forms emerge, as the creative energy of connectivity brings forth new ideas, new images, new life." This is why Ascott's analysis of Net art is "a kind of poetical analysis" rather than a critical one — its objects are not fixed enough to criticise; they are coming into being. `Directly stated` (the emergence claim, the Langton parallel) / `Interpretive synthesis` (reading emergence as the operative behaviour of the connectivity-of-minds claim).

Net art so understood dissolves the distinction between maker and audience. In the Net "every fibre, every node, every server is a part of me"; "I am measured by my connectivity," and one's "Net-extent" replaces the material body as the measure of identity. This is the spine of Ascott's wider telematic program: art as the joining and amplification of distributed consciousness ([[Network Consciousness]]), a [[Reciprocal Communication|reciprocal]] medium in which "the user is the content," not a one-way channel of authored meaning. `Directly stated` / `Strongly implied`.

Net art is therefore best read as the **connectivity-of-minds claim specifically**, nested inside Ascott's broader practice of [[Telematic Art]] and distinct from his treatment of the [[Network as Artwork|network as the medium/artwork]] itself: Telematic Art names the whole practice of art using telecommunications networks; Network as Artwork names the network-as-medium thesis; Net Art names the prior claim about *what such art is the product of* — minds in connection. The three are facets of one project and are cross-linked rather than collapsed. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — Net art is "the product of the connectivity of minds before it is anything else"; noetic process precedes social process; its key behaviour is bottom-up emergence (after Langton's artificial life). The artist plants "conceptual seeds in the substrate of the Net"; identity is reconfigured by connectivity ("My 'Net-extent' defines me"). It belongs to his late technoetic phase and presupposes [[Cyberception|cyberception]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Telematic Art]] — the broader practice of which Net art is the network-specific, connectivity-of-minds case; Net art folds into telematic art as its 1980s–90s Internet inflection.
- [[Network as Artwork]] — the companion thesis that the network *is* the medium/work; Net art names what that work is a product of (joined minds), Network as Artwork names what it is made of (the net).
- [[Network Consciousness]] — Net art is the artistic operation of, and the path toward, a collective/planetary mind; "Net art is the product of the connectivity of minds."
- [[Emergence]] — the key behaviour of Net art; bottom-up, distributed coming-into-being with no global controller (Langton, via Ascott).
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — Net art is two-way and participatory; "the user is the content," dissolving the author/audience division.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — the connectivity of minds is a distributed-cognition substrate; "every fibre, every node, every server is a part of me."
- [[New Media Art]] — Net art is the networked-immaterial successor within the broader new-media lineage Burnham anchored; Ascott extends it from the cybernetic object toward the connective field.
- [[Posthumanism]] — identity measured by connectivity, the body displaced by the "Net-extent," the distributed self.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object Art]] — Net art's "product" is connectivity and process, not a discrete material object; it opposes the art-as-thing tradition (Ascott: art deals with apparition rather than appearance).
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Net art sits at the immaterial pole; its work is "the connectivity of minds," not matter.

## Questions It Raises

- If "noetic process precedes social process," can connectivity of minds really be separated from the social and technical infrastructure that conditions it — or is the ordering itself an idealist move?
- What does it mean to evaluate Net art if its objects are emergent and "coming into being" rather than finished — is Ascott's "poetical analysis" a method or an evasion of criticism?
- Does identity "measured by connectivity" ("My 'Net-extent' defines me") amount to leaving embodiment behind, and how does that square with Ascott's own call for "new metaphors of embodiment"?
- Is the connectivity of minds an aesthetic claim, a spiritual one (telematic consciousness, the technoetic), or both at once?
