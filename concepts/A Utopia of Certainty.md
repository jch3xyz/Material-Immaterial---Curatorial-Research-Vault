# A Utopia of Certainty

## Brief Definition

Zuboff's name for the endgame of [[Instrumentarian Power]]: the transformation of the market into "a project of total certainty," a new collective order in which social processes are optimized toward guaranteed outcomes for the sake of market objectives — certainty purchased by foreclosing the open future.

## Longer Explanation

For Zuboff, surveillance capitalism's deepest ambition is not merely to *know* the future but to *make* it. The prediction imperative drives an escalation from forecasting behavior, to nudging it, to manufacturing it — and "the most predictive source of all is behavior that has already been modified to orient toward guaranteed outcomes." The aim, applied at the scale of society, is what she calls "a utopia of certainty": the application of instrumentarian power "to societal optimization for the sake of market objectives." Where industrial capitalism took the factory's division of labor as its template, surveillance capitalism takes machine learning's confluence as the template for a new form of collective action — a "hive mind" in which each element surrenders its freedom to the shared knowledge of the whole, in "an all-out war on accidents, mistakes, and randomness in general" (Directly stated).

Zuboff distinguishes this certainty-utopia from totalitarianism. "Totalitarianism was a transformation of the state into a project of total possession. Instrumentarianism and its materialization in [[Big Other]] signal the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty." Both yearn toward totality, but by different means: totalitarianism through violence and terror, instrumentarianism through ubiquitous computation and its control over the division of learning. Hence its distinctive promise — "a condition of certainty without terror in the form of 'guaranteed outcomes.'" Because instrumentarian power does not claim our bodies for pain and murder, Zuboff warns, we are prone to undervalue its effects and lower our guard; instead of killing us it "exiles us from our own behavior" (Directly stated).

The conceptual hinge is the word *guarantee*. Zuboff treats the corporate promise of guaranteed outcomes as a tell: "there can be no such guarantees in the absence of the power to make it so." A guarantee, in other words, is a confession of power — it presupposes the capacity to engineer the conditions it claims merely to predict. The pursuit of certainty therefore necessarily expands [[Big Other]] toward totality, deleting boundaries and overwhelming every source of friction — democratic institutions, law, rights, the moral authority of "individual human beings who have their bearings" — because friction is the residue of an open future (Strongly implied).

That open future is precisely what the utopia of certainty must abolish. Zuboff anchors her critique in what she calls the right to the future tense: the freedom to make and keep promises, to will, to act as a subject with projects. "In the absence of this freedom, the future collapses into an infinite present of mere behavior, in which there can be no subjects and no projects: only objects." Uncertainty, for Zuboff, "is not chaos but rather the necessary habitat of the present tense" — the price we pay for freedom. A utopia of *certainty* is thus, in her account, a dystopia of objecthood: the guarantee of outcomes is bought with the elimination of the will (Directly stated).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — Coins "a utopia of certainty" as the societal endgame of [[Instrumentarian Power]] (*The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, ch. 14). It is one branch of a thread running through the whole book: the market "as a project of total certainty" (ch. 1), the impossibility of "guarantees in the absence of the power to make it so" (ch. 7), behavior "already been modified to orient toward guaranteed outcomes" (ch. 10), "certainty without terror" (ch. 13), and the future collapsing into "an infinite present of mere behavior" (ch. 11). Kept as a distinct note from [[Instrumentarian Power]] to carry the certainty / guaranteed-outcomes / open-future thread specifically.
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** *(resonant, not a Zuboff claim)* — Zuboff's certainty-utopia secularizes a theological structure O'Gieblyn traces in [[The Algorithm as Providence]] and [[Algorithmic Determinism]]: the algorithm as an all-knowing, outcome-guaranteeing providence before which the human becomes object rather than agent. Where O'Gieblyn reads predictive systems as a return of Calvinist foreknowledge, Zuboff reads the same drive economically — certainty as market dominance (Speculative connection).

## Related Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the form of power whose societal application *is* the utopia of certainty; this note carries the certainty/guaranteed-outcomes sub-thread of that larger concept.
- [[Big Other]] — the ubiquitous sensate-computational infrastructure that "substitutes certainty for social relations."
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — modified behavior oriented toward guaranteed outcomes is "the most predictive source of all," i.e. the richest surplus.
- [[Behavioral Futures Markets]] — markets that trade on predictions; certainty is the asset they are engineered to manufacture.
- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the freedom the utopia of certainty must abolish; its direct counter-value.
- [[Surveillance]] — the certainty-utopia is the teleological horizon of total surveillance ("a continuous approximation to total information").
- [[Quantified Self]] — lifestyle/activity monitors are Zuboff's concrete example of devices that smuggle behavior-change techniques toward guaranteed outcomes.
- [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — O'Gieblyn's theological cognate: the algorithm as outcome-guaranteeing providence.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the philosophical correlate of certainty engineered into behavior.
- [[Dataism]] — Harari's data-worldview shares the premise that total information yields total predictability.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — Zuboff's explicit antithesis: certainty is gained only by surrendering the freedom to will an open future.
- Uncertainty as "the necessary habitat of the present tense" — for Zuboff a value, not a defect; the certainty-utopia is its negation.
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the utopia of certainty falls on the determinist pole, treating subjects as "only objects."

## Questions It Raises

- If a guarantee presupposes "the power to make it so," is *any* promise of certainty about human behavior necessarily a claim of domination?
- Can a "utopia" that abolishes uncertainty still be utopian, or is the term, in Zuboff's hands, strictly ironic?
- How does Zuboff's economic reading of guaranteed outcomes relate to O'Gieblyn's theological reading of [[The Algorithm as Providence]] — secularization, convergence, or coincidence?
- Is "certainty without terror" less dangerous than terror, or more — because it disarms resistance by feeling benign?
