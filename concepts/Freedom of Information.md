# Freedom of Information

## Brief Definition

Freedom of information is, in Yuval Noah Harari's reading of [[Dataism]], the supreme value of the data religion and "the first movement since 1789 that created a really novel value" — a freedom given not to humans but to *information itself*, holding that data ought to circulate without obstruction and that "the greatest sin is to block the data flow."

## Longer Explanation

Harari arrives at freedom of information by way of [[Dataism]]'s practical commandments. If "life is the movement of information, and if we think that life is good, it follows that we should extend, deepen and spread the flow of information in the universe." Death is redefined as a blockage — "What is death, if not a situation when information doesn't flow?" — so the cardinal prohibition becomes obstruction: "the greatest sin is to block the data flow. … Hence Dataism upholds the freedom of information as the greatest good of all" (`Directly stated`).

What makes this genuinely new, in Harari's account, is its claim to be the first invented value in over two centuries. "People rarely manage to come up with a completely new value. The last time this happened was in the eighteenth century, when the humanist revolution preached the stirring ideals of human liberty, human equality and human fraternity." Since 1789, he argues, "humans have not managed to come up with any new value"; every subsequent struggle was waged "in the name of the three humanist values, or in the name of even older values such as obeying God or serving the nation." Dataism breaks the sequence: it "is the first movement since 1789 that created a really novel value: freedom of information" (`Directly stated`).

The decisive distinction — and the reason this is a *concept* and not a synonym for the liberal freedom of expression — is the location of the right. "We mustn't confuse freedom of information with the old liberal ideal of freedom of expression. Freedom of expression was given to humans, and protected their right to think and say what they wished — including their right to keep their mouths shut and their thoughts to themselves. Freedom of information, in contrast, is not given to humans. It is given to information." The value migrates from the human subject to the data flow; the human, who under [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] was the bearer of rights, is displaced by the bit. Crucially, the two freedoms can collide: this "novel value may impinge on the traditional freedom of expression, by privileging the right of information to circulate freely over the right of humans to own data and to restrict its movement" (`Directly stated`).

Harari gives the value a martyr. "On 11 January 2013, Dataism got its first martyr when Aaron Swartz, a twenty-six-year-old American hacker, committed suicide in his apartment." Swartz, who helped develop the RSS protocol at fourteen and authored the 2008 "Guerilla Open Access Manifesto," "believed that information wants to be free, that ideas don't belong to the people who created them, and that it is wrong to lock data behind walls and charge money for entrance" — and acted on it by downloading hundreds of thousands of JSTOR papers to release onto the Internet. The clash Harari stages between Swartz and JSTOR is precisely the clash between the two freedoms: JSTOR "believes in the freedom of expression of scientists and journal editors, which includes the freedom to charge a fee for reading their articles," while Swartz held "information wants to be free." The episode dramatizes freedom of information as a value that overrides the human right to own and restrict data (`Directly stated`).

For the vault, freedom of information sits at the hinge between Harari's *cosmology of flow* and [[Byung-Chul Han|Han's]] critique. Where Harari names it the data religion's "greatest good," Han reads the same imperative ("everything must become data and information") as ideology — the [[Transparency|transparency]] that is "another name for myth." Freedom of information is thus the affirmative formulation of the same regime that Han indicts under [[Hyperinformation]] and [[Dataism]]: a world in which the unobstructed circulation of data is treated as the supreme good (`Interpretive synthesis` on the Han linkage).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — Originates the concept in the vault. Freedom of information is [[Dataism]]'s "greatest good of all," "the first movement since 1789 that created a really novel value," and — decisively — a freedom "given to information," not to humans, that "may impinge on the traditional freedom of expression." Aaron Swartz is its "first martyr." Harari reports the value with diagnostic ambivalence rather than endorsement.
- **[[Aaron Swartz]]** *(forward-stub — figure without a book in `raw/`, cited by Harari)* — The activist embodiment of the value: "We need to take information, wherever it is stored, make our copies and share them with the world. … We need to fight for Guerilla Open Access." The slogan Harari attributes to his belief — "information wants to be free" — is the popular form of the principle.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** *(critical counterpart — does not use the phrase, but theorizes its regime)* — The unobstructed "data flow" Harari sanctifies is what Han critiques as [[Dataism]] ("data totalitarianism") and the imperative that "everything must become data and information." Han supplies the negative verdict on Harari's affirmative value. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Dataism]] — Freedom of information is Dataism's *supreme value*; the concept is unintelligible apart from the data religion that posits it ("Dataism upholds the freedom of information as the greatest good of all").
- [[Information]] — The thing to which the freedom is given. Harari reads information here as a *cosmology of flow* ("the universe consists of data flows"), distinct from Han's signal or Hayles's disembodied pattern; freedom of information is the ethical corollary of that ontology.
- [[Transparency]] — The political-aesthetic regime of unobstructed circulation. Harari's "set the data free" is the affirmative face of what Han names compulsory transparency; "the greatest sin is to block the data flow" is transparency raised to a commandment.
- [[Hyperinformation]] — The condition produced when the value is realized: a flooding of information that, in Han, "brings no light into darkness." Harari's note that twenty-first-century censorship now "works by flooding people with irrelevant information" meets Han's diagnosis here.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — The freedom of *expression* belonged to this subject ("given to humans … their right to think and say what they wished"); freedom of information relocates the right to the data flow and "may impinge on" the older human freedom. The value marks the eclipse of the liberal subject as the bearer of rights.

## Questions It Raises

- If the right belongs to information rather than to its human author, what becomes of authorship, intellectual property, and the curatorial claim to control a work's circulation?
- Harari calls it the "first new value since 1789." Is freedom of information genuinely novel, or a radicalization of Enlightenment publicity (the public sphere, open access) with the human subject removed?
- When freedom of information "impinges on" freedom of expression — JSTOR's right to charge versus Swartz's right to liberate — which freedom should a researcher, archive, or institution privilege, and on what ground?
- Does treating obstruction as "the greatest sin" leave any defensible place for secrecy, privacy, refusal, or the right "to keep their mouths shut" — or does the value abolish opacity as such?
- Is the martyrdom of Aaron Swartz evidence that freedom of information is a *human* emancipatory value after all, or does Harari's framing — a freedom "given to information" — quietly conscript a human death into the data religion's mythology?
