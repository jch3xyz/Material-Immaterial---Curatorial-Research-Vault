# The Useless Class

## Brief Definition

Harari's name for a projected new mass class of the twenty-first century — people rendered economically, militarily, politically and even artistically valueless by the rise of highly intelligent non-conscious algorithms — and the "superfluous people" whose fate he calls possibly the most important question in twenty-first-century economics.

## Longer Explanation

"The useless class" is Harari's term for the first *practical* threat that automation poses to liberalism in **The Great Decoupling**. The argument is structural rather than moral: liberalism, he claims, "did not become the dominant ideology simply because its philosophical arguments were the most accurate" but because "there was much political, economic and military sense in ascribing value to every human being" — "There was value to every pair of hands that could hold a rifle or pull a lever." The useless class is what remains once that value evaporates: once "robots and computers are catching up, and may soon outperform humans in most tasks," the economic and political system "will stop attaching much value to them." The class is "massive" and "new" because it is produced not by exploitation but by *redundancy* — these are people the system does not need at all. `Directly stated`

The engine that produces the class is [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]]: "Humans are in danger of losing their value, because intelligence is decoupling from consciousness." Tasks once requiring a conscious mind — driving, diagnosing, recognising patterns — can now be done better by "non-conscious algorithms," and "for armies and corporations … intelligence is mandatory but consciousness is optional." Because [[Organisms Are Algorithms]], there is, on Harari's account, no humanly unique competence that algorithms cannot in principle reach — not even art. He explicitly extends uselessness into the aesthetic: in the twenty-first century "we might witness the creation of a new massive class: people devoid of any economic, political or even artistic value, who contribute nothing to the prosperity, power and glory of society." Even art "is not the product of some enchanted spirit or metaphysical soul, but rather of organic algorithms recognising mathematical patterns," so the artist is no sanctuary. `Directly stated`

What makes the useless class a *theoretical* problem and not merely an unemployment statistic is the disposition question Harari foregrounds: "The most important question in twenty-first-century economics may well be what to do with all the superfluous people. What will conscious humans do, once we have highly intelligent non-conscious algorithms that can do almost everything better?" He distinguishes sharply between the easy problem and the hard one — "The crucial problem isn't creating new jobs. The crucial problem is creating new jobs that humans perform better than algorithms" — and notes that even where "the technological bonanza" can "feed and support the useless masses without any effort on their side," the unanswered question is "what will keep them occupied and content." His speculative answer — "drugs and computer games," lives spent inside "3D virtual-reality worlds" — is the one he says would "deal a mortal blow to the liberal belief in the sacredness of human life." `Directly stated`

The useless class is thus the hinge between Harari's economic forecast and his political thesis. It is the point at which the [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness|decoupling]] stops being "a pastime for philosophers" and becomes "an urgent political and economic issue": if value can no longer be ascribed to every human, the practical foundation of [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)|liberal humanism]] — universal rights ascribed alongside universal usefulness (his levée en masse / Declaration of the Rights of Man parallel) — gives way. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — Coins "the useless class" / "superfluous people" / "useless masses" in *Homo Deus* as the first of three practical threats to liberalism in The Great Decoupling. His variant is distinctive in three ways: (1) uselessness is total — economic, military, political *and artistic*; (2) it is grounded in the biological thesis that organisms are algorithms plus the decoupling of intelligence from consciousness, not in a contingent labour-market shock; and (3) it is framed as a *liberalism-collapsing* condition, because liberal value was always practical (every pair of hands counted) rather than purely philosophical. He is careful to mark it "just a possibility, of course, not a prophecy."

## Related Concepts

- [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]] — the proximate cause: once high intelligence no longer requires consciousness, conscious humans lose their monopoly on valuable tasks and become superfluous.
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the deeper premise: if humans are biochemical algorithms, no human capacity (including art) is in principle beyond non-organic algorithms, so no safe niche remains.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the governance corollary: the same algorithms that displace human labour also come to know and decide for humans, deepening their dispensability as agents.
- [[Dataism]] — the worldview within which the human is "just a chip" in the data-processing system; the useless class is its social residue.
- [[Immaterial Labour]] — Han's diagnosis of post-industrial work as immaterial/affective; Harari's useless class is the limit case where even immaterial and cognitive labour is automated away.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the indivisible, free, uniquely valuable individual whose practical foundation the useless class dissolves; the existence of the class is precisely the obsolescence of this subject's economic value.
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — the ideology that ascribed value to every human; the useless class is the condition under which that ascription "might make this belief obsolete."

## Questions It Raises

- If the system can "feed and support the useless masses without any effort on their side," is the crisis economic at all, or is it a crisis of *meaning* — the disposition question Harari foregrounds?
- Does Harari's claim that art too can be automated (EMI/Annie composing à la Bach) actually establish artistic uselessness, or only that audiences can be fooled about provenance?
- Is "the useless class" a description or a normatively loaded forecast — and useless *to whom*? (Harari's qualifier is always "to the system," to "armies and corporations.")
- If liberal value was always practical (value to every pair of hands), is there any non-practical ground on which human dignity could survive the decoupling?
- Does the very name risk naturalising as "uselessness" what is in fact a distribution of power over the algorithms — the "tiny elite that owns the all-powerful algorithms"?
