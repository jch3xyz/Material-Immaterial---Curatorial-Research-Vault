# The New Priesthood

## Brief Definition

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], **the new priesthood** is the narrow caste of privately employed computational specialists — together with their privately owned machines and the economic interests for whose sake they learn — who capture the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning]] under surveillance capitalism, reverting the diffusion of knowledge to a "pre-Gutenberg order."

## Longer Explanation

Zuboff names the new priesthood at the close of Chapter 6 ("Hijacked: The Division of Learning in Society"), where she diagnoses surveillance capitalism's seizure of "the axial principle of social order in an information civilization." The figure is built as the inversion of Gutenberg. "More than six hundred years ago, the printing press put the written word into the hands of ordinary people, rescuing the prayers, bypassing the priesthood, and delivering the opportunity for spiritual communion directly into the hands of the prayerful" (Ch. 6). The internet was believed to extend that democratization, "a mighty democratizing force that exponentially realizes Gutenberg's revolution." But Zuboff argues this faith blinds us to a hidden countermovement: "In this hidden movement the competitive struggle over surveillance revenues reverts to the pre-Gutenberg order as the division of learning in society shades toward the pathological, captured by a narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists, their privately owned machines, and the economic interests for whose sake they learn" (Ch. 6). The priesthood is therefore not only the experts but the *coupling* of scarce human expertise, concentrated material machinery, and private capital. `Directly stated`.

The caste exists because two scarcities reinforce each other. First, knowledge requires a "gargantuan material infrastructure that surveillance revenues have bought": Google as "the pioneer of 'hyperscale,'" its machine intelligence "described as '80 percent infrastructure,' a system that comprises custom-built, warehouse-sized data centers spanning 15 locations and, in 2016, an estimated 2.5 million servers in four continents," plus its custom "tensor processing unit (TPU)" chip for "deep learning inference" (Ch. 6). Second, the people who can "wield the technologies of machine intelligence" are globally scarce: "an arms race among tech companies for the 10,000 or so professionals on the planet who know how to wield the technologies of machine intelligence to coax knowledge from an otherwise cacophonous data continent," with Google "the most aggressive acquirer of AI technology and talent" (Ch. 6). The consequence is hoarding: British universities already speak of a "missing generation" of data scientists, and "'The intellect and expertise is concentrated in a small number of companies'" (Ch. 6). What makes this a *priesthood* and not merely a profession is the redirection of its genius: "their genius is meant to storm the gates of human experience, transforming it into data and translating it into a new market colossus that creates wealth by predicting, influencing, and controlling human behavior" (Ch. 6). `Directly stated`.

The metaphor recurs across the book, naming the same caste from different angles. In Chapter 15, Zuboff reads MIT's Alex Pentland — the social theorist she argues "completes" B. F. Skinner — as a sacerdotal figure: "Although Pentland is not alone in this field, he is something of a high priest among an exclusive group of priests" (Ch. 15). The undefined "we" who would govern Pentland's tuned hive is, she asks, perhaps "the priesthood of data scientists like Pentland? The priesthood in collaboration with the owners of the means of behavior modification?" (Ch. 15). In Chapter 16, the figure becomes the unseen designers of the social-media hive that the young inhabit: "When young people enter this hive, they keep company with a surveillance priesthood: the world's most-sophisticated data scientists, programmers, machine learning experts, and technology designers, whose single-minded mission to tighten the glove is mandated by the economic imperatives of surveillance capital and its 'laws of motion'" (Ch. 16). Across all three appearances the priesthood is defined by *who it serves* — not the public it claims to inform, but the owners of the machines. `Directly stated`.

The new priesthood is thus the human-and-machine face of the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning]]'s privatization: the concrete answer to Zuboff's question "Who knows?" Where a democratic division of learning would diffuse the answer broadly, the priesthood re-concentrates it behind closed doors, producing knowledge that is, in the book's recurring formula, "from us, but not for us." The Gutenberg-reversion claim is the concept's signature: the technology imagined to dissolve gatekeepers has instead restored a hidden caste that monopolizes the most consequential knowledge of the age. `Strongly implied` (the Gutenberg-reversion is `Directly stated`; the synthesis with "Who knows?" is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — the new priesthood is the "narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists, their privately owned machines, and the economic interests for whose sake they learn" (Ch. 6); a hidden movement that "reverts to the pre-Gutenberg order." It is backed by hyperscale infrastructure (~2.5 million servers, the TPU) and the global scarcity of "the 10,000 or so professionals on the planet who know how to wield the technologies of machine intelligence" (Ch. 6). Restated as Pentland the "high priest among an exclusive group of priests" (Ch. 15) and the "surveillance priesthood… whose single-minded mission to tighten the glove is mandated by the economic imperatives of surveillance capital" (Ch. 16). `Directly stated`.
- **Pre-Gutenberg / Gutenberg-reversion variant** — Zuboff's controlling historical figure: the printing press "bypassing the priesthood" is inverted into a re-concentration of knowledge behind a new caste. The "priesthood" was, in the original, the institution Gutenberg dethroned; surveillance capitalism restores it in computational form. `Directly stated`.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Division of Learning in Society]] — the new priesthood is the concrete agent of this concept's "unauthorized privatization": it embodies the captured answer to "Who knows?" (Ch. 6). The priesthood is to the division of learning what a guild is to a craft.
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the priesthood serves the "economic interests for whose sake they learn"; its mission is "mandated by the economic imperatives of surveillance capital and its 'laws of motion'" (Ch. 16). The caste is a function of the accumulation logic, not of technology as such.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the priesthood's "genius is meant to storm the gates of human experience, transforming it into data" (Ch. 6); behavioral surplus is the raw material it converts into prediction and wealth.
- [[The Authority of Knowledge]] — the priesthood concentrates the authority of knowledge in private hands; whoever commands the experts and machines commands the answers society depends on.
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the priesthood is the operational human stratum of instrumentarian power; in Ch. 15 it is "the priesthood of data scientists" who would "tune the network," and in Ch. 16 the designers who "tighten the glove."
- [[Information]] — the privatized knowledge caste is the social-order consequence of a world "pervasively rendered as information" (Ch. 6): the contest is over who commands that information.
- [[Big Data]] — the priesthood's machines and methods are the engine of big-data capture; the ~10,000 experts "coax knowledge from an otherwise cacophonous data continent" (Ch. 6).
- [[Dataism]] — the priesthood is the institutional embodiment of the data-positivist creed Han and Harari name as dataism: the conviction that "the machine knows," administered by those who own the machine.
- [[Surveillance]] — the priesthood is realized in the workplace and social registers as the engineers of surveillance-as-a-service and the social-media hive (Ch. 15–16).
- [[Quantified Self]] — the rendering of lived experience into behavioral data — the quantified self — is the raw material the priesthood owns and reads, while the quantified subject is "excluded from the opportunity to learn" (Ch. 6).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's panopticon runs on *voluntary self-exposure* by a dispersed many; Zuboff's new priesthood names the asymmetric *other side* of that visibility — the few who alone can read the accumulated surplus. The opposition is one of vantage: the watched many vs. the privately employed, machine-equipped few who watch. Cross-link, not identity. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Questions It Raises

- If the diffusion of knowledge has reverted to a "pre-Gutenberg order," what would a *new Gutenberg* — a technology or institution that re-democratizes machine-intelligence knowledge — even look like?
- Is the priesthood's power a function of scarce expertise (the ~10,000 experts) or of the privately owned infrastructure (the 2.5 million servers, the TPU)? Could public investment in either dissolve the caste, or does the coupling of the two make it self-reinforcing?
- Zuboff stresses that the priests' knowledge is produced "for whose sake they learn" — the owners, not the public. Can computational expertise be professionalized against its employers (as medicine and law claim a public duty), or is the priesthood definitionally captured?
- How does Zuboff's "new priesthood" differ from older critiques of the technocratic expert (Weber's bureaucratic specialist, the managerial class), and does the *machine* — privately owned, scarce, and material — add something those critiques lacked?
