# Gardens of Hypotheses

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's constructivist replacement for the museum: knowledge landscapes planted in the interspace between the virtual and the actual, where the metaphors a culture imprisons as fixed truths are released to roam freely and reality is constantly redescribed.

## Longer Explanation

In *The Mind of the Museum* (1996) Ascott proposes the "garden of hypotheses" as the cultural institution proper to a technoetic, immaterial, network culture — what he opposes to the museum understood as a "cultural fortress." The museum, on his reading, does not control information so much as control *meaning*: it takes the metaphors and worldviews a culture lives by, fixes them, takes them "out of the flux of dynamic transformations that constitutes life," and folds them back "in their concreteness as specimens, objectified, whose province then is the past." A garden of hypotheses is the inversion of this: instead of conserving fixed metaphors as truths, it plants them as living, growing, revisable sites — "real, physical sites to inhabit" — "subject to cycles of intellectual growth and change, reflecting our shifting perspectives, values, and ambitions" (`Directly stated`).

The concept rests on Ascott's radical-constructivist premise that there is no ground of reality beneath our descriptions: "all is metaphor, that there is no ground of reality on which perceptions are based... that, in short, we talk the world into being, that our descriptions are its becoming" (`Directly stated`). Because the world is "all metaphor," the cultural task is not to authorize a single correct description but to keep "redescribing and reconstructing reality, the many realities that are ceaselessly created within our universe of metaphor and meaning." The garden is the architecture of that redescription. See [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]].

The governing image is botanical and ecological, fused with the language of artificial life: where the old museum is a cage, the garden lets metaphors "roam freely," "float out in the semantic sea," and "reside on the Internet." Ascott calls for us to "re-plough and re-seed the ecology with new metaphors and hypotheses," to "set in motion adaptive complex systems," to plant "knowledge landscapes... laid out in the interspace between the virtual and the actual." The garden is thus continuous with his account of [[Telematic Art]] and the Net as a substrate in which the artist plants "conceptual seeds... and watches them grow" — [[Emergence]] is the key behaviour (`Directly stated`). It also performs a kind of [[Dematerialization]]: "As the museum dematerialises, we might then see new metaphors of what it is to be human materialize all around us" (`Strongly implied` that the garden is the dematerialized successor institution).

Ascott contrasts the garden with the built fabric of instrumental rationalism: gardens of hypotheses are offered "as an alternative to the tenements of reason, squares of certainty, and office blocks of determinism that congest our cities." The garden is therefore not merely a curatorial reform but a cultural-cognitive one: it is "to the technoetic culture and to the world of artificial life and artificial consciousness, to the hypercortex and to human cyberception, what the neo-Georgian and postmodern boxes of the old museum were to the Cartesian conformity of classical culture" (`Directly stated`). See [[The Mind of the Museum]] and [[Cyberception]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins the metaphor in his 1996 keynote *The Mind of the Museum*. The garden of hypotheses is the positive term in his post-museum scenario: the museum is "merely the traps and cages of imprisoned metaphors," and the garden is what we plant when we let them out. Grounded in his radical constructivism ("we talk the world into being") and bound to his triad of technoetics, artificial life, and intelligent architecture. The concept is co-extensive with the metaphor of the same name (see the [[Gardens of Hypotheses]] metaphor note) — the figure and the idea it names.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott sets the garden against the museum's insistence on presenting "material artifacts (concrete instances) at all" in "an increasingly... immaterial world"; the garden is the immaterial-culture institution.
- The museum as cage / fortress — the garden's structural opposite: where the museum fixes and authorizes metaphors as truths, the garden keeps them mobile and revisable.

## Questions It Raises

- If "we talk the world into being" and "there is no 'absolute reality' to set ideology against," what prevents a garden of hypotheses from being just another regime of meaning-control under a friendlier name? Ascott concedes museums "control meaning"; can the garden avoid doing the same?
- A garden of hypotheses is offered as a "real, physical site to inhabit" yet is "laid out in the interspace between the virtual and the actual." What is the material status of a physical site that is constitutively immaterial?
- Ascott foresees "curatorial obsolescence." If no one selects, sifts, or authorizes, does the garden cultivate emergence — or merely abandon the public to "the delirious disorder of the Internet" he also describes?
- How does the garden's release of metaphors relate to Foucault's and Donald Horne's critique of the museum's "autocratic subtext," which Ascott invokes — is the garden post-power, or a redistribution of it?
