# Depth as Insight Not Point of View

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's claim that when a medium becomes a means of depth experience, "depth" means interrelation and insight — a mode of mental involvement in process — rather than a fixed visual point of view, dissolving the old highbrow/lowbrow and classical/popular categories.

## Longer Explanation

In the Phonograph chapter of *Understanding Media* McLuhan tracks how the LP, hi-fi, and stereo — alongside "TV as the visual in depth" — turned recorded sound into a depth medium. Stereo is "'all-around' or 'wrap around' sound"; where previously "sound had emanated from a single point in accordance with the bias of visual culture with its fixed point of view," the hi-fi changeover did for music "what cubism had been for painting, and what symbolism had been for literature; namely, the acceptance of multiple facets and planes in a single experience." Sound and the visual alike become "in depth." `Directly stated`

The cultural consequence is the collapse of inherited evaluative categories. "Perhaps it is not very contradictory that when a medium becomes a means of depth experience the old categories of 'classical' and 'popular' or of 'highbrow' and 'lowbrow' no longer obtain." McLuhan's own example is clinical and unaesthetic: "Watching a blue-baby heart operation on TV is an experience that will fit none of the categories." Anything approached in depth "acquires as much interest as the greatest matters," because "'depth' means 'in interrelation,' not in isolation." `Directly stated`

This is where the chapter extends [[The Medium Is the Message]] into a theory of mind. Depth is not a richer or more refined *content*; it is a structural relation among elements and a way of being involved in them. "Depth means insight, not point of view; and insight is a kind of mental involvement in process that makes the content of the item seen quite secondary." The accent falls on participation in process, not on the object viewed — which is why depth is opposed to the detached, single-station "point of view" that McLuhan associates with the visual bias of print culture. `Directly stated`

McLuhan then generalizes the point about media into a claim about consciousness itself: "Consciousness itself is an inclusive process not at all dependent on content. Consciousness does not postulate consciousness of anything in particular." Consciousness, like a depth medium, is an inclusive process of interrelation rather than a directed beam aimed at some particular content — a formulation that aligns the concept with [[The Technological Extension of Consciousness]] and the reordering of the [[Sense Ratios]] that McLuhan tracks across the electric media. The move from depth-as-insight to consciousness-as-inclusive-process is asserted in the text; reading consciousness here as content-independent in a strong philosophical sense is `Strongly implied`. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — Depth experience (LP/hi-fi/stereo/TV-in-depth) replaces the single fixed "point of view" of visual culture with interrelation and insight; insight is involvement in process that makes content secondary, and consciousness is itself "an inclusive process not at all dependent on content." This is the Phonograph-chapter formulation that turns the medium-as-message thesis into a theory of mind.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Detachment vs Participation]] — the "fixed point of view" of visual culture is the detached pole; depth-as-insight names the participatory, in-process pole. ## Questions It Raises
