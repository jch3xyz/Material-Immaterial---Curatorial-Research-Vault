# The Transformative Vocation

## Brief Definition

Art's role redefined — via the Brazilian legal-political theorist Roberto Mangabeira Unger — from an "honourable calling" into a "transformative vocation": a third idea of work that fuses self-fulfilment with the transformation of self, society, and reality through struggle against the limits of existing institutions and knowledge.

## Longer Explanation

In the closing movement of "Art @ the Edge of the NET" [[Roy Ascott]] borrows from Roberto Mangabeira Unger to reframe what the artist *is for* in the post-biological, telematic, "moist" culture he has been describing. Against two received models of work — work as "purely instrumental" and work as an "honourable calling" — Unger names a third model that has "appeared in the world," one that "connects self-fulfilment and transformation: the change of any aspect of the practical or imaginative settings of the individual's life" (Directly stated). Ascott folds this directly onto the artist: the ambition "to work without boundaries, beyond categories of learning, to build new realities, new language, and new practices" is precisely what "replaces the historical sense of the artist's role as an 'honourable calling' with the idea of such work as a 'transformative vocation'" (Directly stated).

The vocation is *transformative* in a demanding, agonistic sense. To be "fully a person," Ascott quotes Unger, one must "engage in a struggle against the defects of the limits of existing society or available knowledge" — and because "the dominant institutional and imaginative structure of a society" is itself "a major part of this constraining biographical circumstance," that structure "must therefore also be a central target of transformative resistance" (Directly stated). The vocation is therefore not self-expression but world-building: it aims its self-fulfilment *through* the dismantling of the very institutions that constrain the self. Ascott reads the present moment as ripe for it — "the imaginative structure and constraining institutions that Unger identifies are losing their dominance and appear everywhere to be crumbling" — and assigns "interactive and telematic media" the role of accelerant, given "the widespread diffusion of ideas" they make possible (Directly stated).

The transformative vocation is the social-ethical face of Ascott's larger argument; its epistemic-methodological face is [[Visionary Pragmatism]], the "flying with your feet on the ground" that combines the artist's visionary impulse (after Rorty) with a pragmatist refusal of canonical, ready-made descriptions of reality. Where visionary pragmatism describes *how* the artist works — through language understood as "a tool for bringing the world into existence" — the transformative vocation describes *why* and *toward what*: the redefinition of the artist's work so that it acquires "relevance in the social context" (Interpretive synthesis). Ascott's [[The Planetary Collegium]] is offered as the institutional embodiment of this pairing — a transdisciplinary research community "concerned with learning and self-creation," whose graduates are "destined to act instrumentally within the post-biological culture" to "seed change... bottom-up" (Strongly implied).

This note is minted standalone so the Unger reference resolves; conceptually it sits very close to [[Visionary Pragmatism]] and could later be folded into it. It also gives [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — both realized in the Burnham ingest and extended by Ascott — their normative horizon: the technologically experiential artwork is not merely a new medium but the instrument of a vocation aimed at transforming the structures of the self and society.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — adopts the term wholesale from Unger to redefine the contemporary, networked, post-biological artist: the artist's work, no longer an "honourable calling," becomes a "transformative vocation" that connects self-fulfilment to the transformation of the practical and imaginative settings of life, pursued through struggle against the limits of existing society. Telematic and moist media are its preferred instruments.
- **Roberto Mangabeira Unger** *(named source, not ingested)* — the originating thinker. In his programme for social reconstruction, work-as-transformative-vocation is the "third idea of work" beyond the instrumental and the honourable, requiring "transformative resistance" against a society's "dominant institutional and imaginative structure." Ascott imports the concept; Unger himself frames it for persons and societies in general, not specifically for artists. Remains a forward-stub author (no book in `raw/`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Visionary Pragmatism]] — the methodological twin; the transformative vocation is the social/ethical aim, visionary pragmatism the practice that pursues it. Strong candidate parent if this note is ever folded.
- [[The Planetary Collegium]] — Ascott's institutional embodiment of the vocation: a transdisciplinary research community for "learning and self-creation" that seeks to "seed change... bottom-up."
- [[New Media Art]] — the art form whose normative horizon the vocation supplies; "build[ing] new realities, new language, and new practices" redefines what new-media work is for.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the experiential, technologically mediated artwork understood as the instrument of transformation rather than a neutral medium.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Visionary Pragmatism]] — listed under both related and opposing only in the structural sense that the transformative vocation *opposes* the prior model it displaces (the artist's role as an "honourable calling"); the genuine internal opposition the concept carries is **transformative vocation vs. honourable calling / instrumental work** (Unger's three ideas of work). No standalone note exists for "honourable calling," so that opposition is captured here in prose rather than as a link.

## Questions It Raises

- If the artist's vocation is to wage "transformative resistance" against "the dominant institutional and imaginative structure of a society," how does it avoid being recuperated by the very networked institutions (the telematic, ubiquitous-computing infrastructure) Ascott celebrates as its instruments?
- Is the "transformative vocation" durable as a distinct concept, or is it best read as the social-ethical aspect of [[Visionary Pragmatism]] and folded into it?
- Unger frames the third idea of work for *persons* in general; Ascott reassigns it to *artists*. Is the artist genuinely exemplary of transformative work, or does the move quietly privilege the avant-garde?
- How does the vocation's demand to make institutions "a central target of transformative resistance" sit beside Ascott's largely optimistic, even utopian, account of "moist" culture and "telenoia"?
