# The Clockwork Universe

## Brief Definition

The Cartesian-Newtonian cosmos imagined as a single vast clock wound by God, running cycle after cycle in flawless order and never running down — the metaphysics that, for Burnham, underwrites the eighteenth-century golden age of [[Automata]].

## Longer Explanation

For Jack Burnham, "clockwork" is not merely a class of machinery but a picture of the whole world. He traces a chain from the medieval mechanical clock through Descartes's mechanical similes for the body to Newton's *Principia* (1687), at which point conjecture hardens into a total cosmology: with universal gravitation explaining planetary orbits, tides, comets, eclipses, and pendulum motion alike, "what could prevent the inquiring man from understanding all earthly phenomena in terms of a series of mechanical models?" The answer Burnham reports for the age is none: "The world for all earthly intentions was a great clock, operating cycle after cycle, imperturbably, always in order, and probably never running down. The winder of this clock of course was God." *Directly stated.* This is the founding metaphor — [[Technology as Metaphor]] in its purest historical form — in which a contemporary machine becomes the model for nature itself, and studying the cosmos becomes a way of studying "the beauty of God's mechanism."

The concept does double duty in Burnham's argument. First, it is the metaphysical climate that makes the eighteenth century "the golden age of automata": once the universe is a divinely wound mechanism, the inventor who builds a mechanical duck or flute-player is not transgressing nature but imitating its Maker, and "what could be more pious than studying the beauty of God's mechanism?" The clockwork cosmos thus licenses the [[Automata|automaton]] as a legitimate, even devout, project — the [[Mechanism (Burnham)|mechanistic]] re-creation of life rather than its biological generation. *Strongly implied.*

Second, and earlier in the chapter, Burnham makes the mechanical clock the concrete engine of the whole worldview rather than just its emblem: "the development of the mechanical clock must be regarded as the guiding spirit behind early modern automata." *Directly stated.* The clock did more than the book to "produce an atmosphere that made possible modern rhythms of living": men stopped marking the day "by a series of fluctuating periods, such as the seven canonical hours," and submitted instead to "the regularity of hours and minutes." Crucially, this regularity has a social cost. "This precise periodicity and fragmentation, found only in mechanically regulated time, was a major step in breaking down the organic work pattern of the hand artisan, replacing it with the beginnings of piecework mass production." *Directly stated.* Here the clockwork universe is not benign cosmology but the historical hinge by which time is fragmented and labour is mechanized — a precursor that resonates with later vault arguments about [[Acceleration]] and the dissolution of organic rhythm.

The metaphor names its own historical limit. Burnham writes that "the idea of 'clockwork' gradually gave man a physical conception of the universe which lasted until the present century" — that is, until cybernetics and the computer replaced the wound, deterministic clock with feedback and information. In the vault's terms the clockwork universe is the ancestor that [[The Computational Universe]] supersedes, and within Burnham's own book the clockwork god as prime mover is displaced by [[The Kybernetes as the New Prime Mover]], the cybernetic steersman.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — the clockwork universe is the eighteenth-century metaphysics behind the golden age of [[Automata]]: a God-wound cosmic mechanism, with the mechanical clock as its "guiding spirit," whose precise periodicity also fragments organic artisan time into piecework mass production.

## Related Concepts

- [[Automata]] — the art form the clockwork universe legitimates; the automaton is the mechanistic re-creation of life that the wound cosmos makes pious rather than profane.
- [[Mechanism (Burnham)]] — the machine as a discrete, closed system for producing work; the clockwork universe is mechanism elevated to cosmology.
- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — the clockwork universe is the paradigm case of a contemporary machine becoming the literal model for nature, the cosmos, and the body.
- [[Acceleration]] — the clock's "precise periodicity and fragmentation" of organic time is a historical precursor to later regimes of temporal regulation and speed.
- [[The Strasbourg Cathedral Clock]] — the working "miniature cosmos," a model of "a man-interpreted universe controlled by God," is the clockwork universe made into a single machine.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the modern successor metaphor; the wound, deterministic clock gives way to information and feedback.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the same mechanistic logic extended from the cosmos to the living body and, eventually, the mind.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Mechanism vs Vitalism]] — the clockwork universe is the mechanist pole; vitalism's "élan vital" denies that life and cosmos reduce to a wound mechanism.
- [[The Kybernetes as the New Prime Mover]] — Burnham's own argument that the cybernetic steersman replaces the clockwork god as the source of motion, ending the clockwork era.

## Questions It Raises

- If the clockwork universe made the automaton pious (imitating God's mechanism), what changes in the maker's self-understanding once the prime mover is no longer a winding God but a cybernetic steersman?
- Burnham ties the clock's "precise periodicity and fragmentation" to the breakdown of artisan labour and the rise of piecework mass production — is the clockwork universe better read as cosmology or as the metaphysics of industrial time-discipline?
- The clockwork conception "lasted until the present century" before yielding to [[The Computational Universe]]: is the computational metaphor a genuine break, or the clockwork universe by other means — deterministic order recoded as information?
