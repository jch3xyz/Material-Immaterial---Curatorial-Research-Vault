# Organic Reciprocities

## Brief Definition

The historic mutual dependencies between capitalism and its societies — firms relying on people as both employees and customers — that bound market capitalism to the rise of democracy, and that surveillance capitalism "formally rescinds," producing a structural independence from people that breeds exclusion and radical indifference.

## Longer Explanation

For Shoshana Zuboff, *organic reciprocities* name the productive social relations that have long made market capitalism durable and adaptable: a firm depends on people on both sides of its operations — as the workers who produce and the consumers who buy — so its prosperity is materially bound to theirs. She traces the idea back to Adam Smith's insight that price increases "had to be balanced with wage increases," and finds its twentieth-century emblem in Ford's five-dollar day. These reciprocities constituted a "centuries-old social contract between capitalism and its communities." `Directly stated`

Zuboff's decisive claim is that surveillance capitalism marks "another decisive break with capitalism's past" by abandoning these reciprocities altogether. The shareholder-value movement and globalization had already gone "a long way toward destroying" the contract, "substituting formal indifference for reciprocity"; surveillance capitalism "goes further," not only jettisoning Smith but "formally rescind[ing] any remaining reciprocities with its societies." Its revenues derive from behavioral surplus rather than from selling goods or services back to a population it must keep employed and solvent — so it has no organic dependence on people "as either sources of consumers or employees." `Directly stated`

This matters because Zuboff ties the *origins of democracy* in both America and Britain to these very reciprocities. In America, the violation of consumer reciprocities — the Stamp Act, the nonimportation movement she glosses as a consumer boycott, the American Revolution as a war over "producer-consumer interdependencies" — translated economic power into political power. In Britain, "a grudging, practical, self-interested respect for the necessary interdependence of capital and labor" translated into the gradual expansion of the franchise and a nonviolent shift toward inclusive democratic institutions. Where Britain's industrial capitalists made pragmatic concessions because clamping down would have destroyed their own gains, "surveillance capitalists' extreme structural independence from people breeds exclusion rather than inclusion and lays the foundation for the unique approach that we have called 'radical indifference.'" `Directly stated`

The concept thus functions as the hinge of Zuboff's argument that surveillance capitalism is "a profoundly antidemocratic social force." Because the firm no longer needs people, it has no structural reason to reciprocate with them; the void "where reciprocities once thrived" is one "surveillance capitalists will not fill because doing so would violate their own logic of accumulation." The same severance recurs at the level of content: professional journalism's "organic reciprocities with its readers" — its duty to separate truth from falsehood — are "erased" once content is subjected to the equivalence metrics of clicks and likes. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins the term in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (Ch. 18) as one of three "startling" departures of surveillance capitalism from market capitalism's history. The reciprocities are "organic" in the sense of arising from the firm's material dependence on people; their formal rescission is what distinguishes surveillance capitalism from both classical (Smith/Hayek) and shareholder-value capitalism, and what severs the historic link between capitalism and democracy.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic logic that "formally rescinds" the reciprocities; this concept names what it abandons.
- [[The Coup from Above]] — the political result: with no reciprocal tie to people, the firm mounts an antidemocratic seizure of knowledge and power.
- [[Hyperscale]] — the operational form of structural independence: a firm can reach planetary scale while needing few people as employees or customers.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the alternative revenue source that makes people dispensable as the firm's customers, dissolving the reciprocal bond.
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the mode of power that the absence of reciprocity enables and that treats people as raw-material supply.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Radical Indifference]] — the managerial disposition that *replaces* reciprocity once it is rescinded: people and their distinct human meanings become equivalent units measured by clicks, likes, and dwell times. Reciprocity binds the firm to people; radical indifference releases it from them.

## Questions It Raises

- If democracy in America and Britain grew out of capitalism's reciprocal dependence on people, what political institution can compel accountability from a firm that no longer needs people at all?
- Is "formal indifference" (shareholder-value capitalism) different in kind from surveillance capitalism's "radical indifference," or only in degree?
- Can reciprocities be reconstructed by regulation, or are they "organic" — arising only from genuine material dependence that surveillance revenues have dissolved?
- Does the erasure of journalism's reciprocities with readers point to a general law: that wherever behavioral surplus becomes the revenue source, the producer's duty to the audience evaporates?
