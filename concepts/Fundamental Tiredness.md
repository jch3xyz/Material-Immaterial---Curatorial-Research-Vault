# Fundamental Tiredness

## Brief Definition

The affirmative, healing "we-tiredness" — a "tiredness that trusts in the world" that loosens the strictures of the ego, shifts the gravity of being from the ego to the world, restores the duality of seeing and being-seen, and *inspires*; not a state of exhaustion but, in Han's words, "a singular capacity."

## Longer Explanation

In the closing chapter of *The Burnout Society*, Han turns from diagnosis to remedy and lifts a redemptive figure of tiredness out of Peter Handke's "Essay on Tiredness." He sharply distinguishes two tirednesses. The first is the pathological foil — the tiredness native to the achievement society he has been describing: a "solitary tiredness" that "has a separating and isolating effect," what Handke calls "divisive tiredness." This *I-tiredness* strikes one "mute and blind," fills the entire field of vision with the isolated ego, and "proves violent because it destroys all that is common or shared, all proximity, and even language itself." It is "worldless, world-destroying tiredness" — the exhaustion of the overheated, self-exploiting ego that can no longer reach the Other (`Directly stated`).

Against this Han sets *fundamental tiredness* itself: the affirmative pole this note carries. As "more of less of me," it "opens a between by loosening the strictures of the ego." Here the ego does not collapse inward but thins out: "As the I grows smaller, the gravity of being shifts from the ego to the world." Han names it "tiredness that trusts in the world" [weltvertrauende Müdigkeit] — a tiredness that "'opens' the I and 'makes room' for the world." Crucially, it "reestablishes the 'duality' that solitary tiredness destroys utterly. One sees, and one is seen. One touches, and one is touched." Less I means more world (`Directly stated`).

This redemptive tiredness is explicitly *not* exhaustion. "However, it hardly amounts to a state of exhaustion in which one proves unable to do anything. Instead, it represents a singular capacity. 'Fundamental tiredness' inspires." It allows spirit/intellect [Geist] to emerge, and its inspiration "involves not-doing." Han threads this directly into his vocabulary of potency (see [[Negative Potency]]): "The tiredness of exhaustion is the tiredness of positive potency. It makes one incapable of doing something. Tiredness that inspires is tiredness of negative potency, namely of not-to." Fundamental tiredness is therefore continuous with the Sabbath, "a day of not-to," a time of play freed from all in-order-to (`Directly stated`).

Its social register is communal rather than solitary, which is why the keystone affirmative term is *we-tiredness*. Han writes that Handke's tiredness "is not the tiredness of an exhausted ego. He calls it 'we-tiredness.' I am not tired 'of you,' as he puts it, but rather I am tired 'with you.'" This shared tiredness "founds a deep friendship and makes it possible to conceive of a community that requires neither belonging nor relation" — the [[Community of Singularities]] prefigured in a Dutch still life, where heterogeneous things "are all there together" connected by a "friendly and" (`Directly stated`). The "Pentecostal company" that inspires not-doing names, for Han, a possible "society of the future": "the society to come might also be called a society of tiredness" (`Strongly implied` — Han offers it conditionally, as Handke's image extended into a social proposal).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Recovers fundamental / we-tiredness from Handke as the one affirmative counter-figure to the achievement society's burnout. He folds it into his own conceptual apparatus: it is the tiredness of *negative potency* (the not-to), aligned with the Sabbath, calm [Gelassenheit], and serene not-doing, and it founds a community of singularities. The polar opposition with its pathological foil (I-tiredness) is carried by the tension note [[I-Tiredness vs We-Tiredness]].
- **Peter Handke** (via Han) — The source of "divisive," "we-," "candid," and "fundamental" tiredness and of the salvific imagery (Pentecost, Orpheus, the still life). Han reads Handke as conceiving "an immanent religion of tiredness." See the reference note [[Byung-Chul Han and Peter Handke]]. (Handke is a forward-stub author — no book in `raw/`.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Negative Potency]] — Fundamental tiredness *is* the tiredness of negative potency, "namely of not-to"; its opposite, the tiredness of exhaustion, is the tiredness of positive potency.
- [[Community of Singularities]] — Fundamental / we-tiredness founds "a community that requires neither belonging nor relation," the friendly *and* of singular beings.
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Peter Handke]] — The reference grounding Han's reading of Handke's "Essay on Tiredness."
- [[Negativity]] — Fundamental tiredness is a recovery of negativity (the not-to, the interval, not-doing) against a world "poor in negativity and in turn dominated by excess positivity."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Achievement Society]] — The society of "absolutized activity" whose flipside is "excessive tiredness and exhaustion"; fundamental tiredness gathers up "all the forms of existence and coexistence that vanish in the course of absolutized activity." Its internal pathological foil, I-tiredness, is the exhaustion proper to this society. The polar opposition is held in the tension [[I-Tiredness vs We-Tiredness]].

## Questions It Raises

- If burnout is the tiredness of positive potency (the inability to do), can a culture cultivate the tiredness of negative potency without merely prescribing more "self-care" that re-enters the logic of optimization?
- Is the "society of tiredness" a genuine political proposal or only a poetic-religious image (Pentecost, the Sabbath) — and what would its institutions look like?
- How does restoring the "duality" of seeing/being-seen and touching/being-touched relate to Han's diagnosis elsewhere of the abolition of the Other?
