# Seeking the Wrong Goal

## Brief Definition

Seeking the wrong goal is the system trap in which a badly defined goal — the system's direction-setter — makes the system obediently produce exactly the wrong result, most often because the goal was set to measure effort or throughput rather than the real welfare it was supposed to serve.

## Longer Explanation

In [[Donella Meadows]]'s *Thinking in Systems*, seeking the wrong goal is one of the recurring [[Systems Theory|system traps and opportunities]] treated in Chapter Five. Its premise is a claim she has already made earlier in the book about where power in a system lives: "one of the most powerful ways to influence the behavior of a system is through its purpose or goal. That's because the goal is the direction-setter of the system, the definer of discrepancies that require action, the indicator of compliance, failure, or success toward which balancing feedback loops work." The goal is the reference point that every [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] in the system is driving toward. So the trap follows with brutal logic: "If the goal is defined badly, if it doesn't measure what it's supposed to measure, if it doesn't reflect the real welfare of the system, then the system can't possibly produce a desirable result." The machinery is not broken; it is working perfectly toward the wrong target.

Meadows dramatizes the mechanism with a fairy tale: "Systems, like the three wishes in the traditional fairy tale, have a terrible tendency to produce exactly and only what you ask them to produce. Be careful what you ask them to produce." A goal defined as military spending produces military spending, which may or may not produce national security. A goal defined as money spent per student produces money spent per student; a goal defined as standardized-test performance produces standardized-test performance — whether either correlates with good education is, she notes drily, "at least worth thinking about." In the early days of family planning in India, program goals were set in terms of the number of IUDs implanted, so doctors, eager to meet their targets, "put loops into women without patient approval." The common error running through all of these is naming a proxy for effort as if it were the result: "These examples confuse effort with result, one of the most common mistakes in designing systems around the wrong goal." That confusion — effort mistaken for result — is the diagnostic signature of the trap.

The paradigm case, "maybe the worst mistake of this kind," is the adoption of GNP as the measure of national economic success. Meadows lays out the indictment: GNP "lumps together goods and bads" (more car accidents and repair bills push it up), counts only marketed goods and services, ignores distributional equity, and "measures effort rather than achievement, gross production and consumption rather than efficiency." A more efficient light bulb makes GNP go *down*. Above all, "GNP is a measure of throughput—flows of stuff made and purchased in a year—rather than capital stocks... that are the source of real wealth and real pleasure." Because governments treat this badly defined indicator as the goal, they act to keep it rising even by wasteful or destructive means — "such as overharvesting forests in order to stimulate the economy in the short term." Meadows states the trap's law in its most quotable form: "If you define the goal of a society as GNP, that society will do its best to produce GNP. It will not produce welfare, equity, justice, or efficiency unless you define a goal and regularly measure and report the state of welfare, equity, justice, or efficiency." The system is not disobedient; it is obedient to the wrong command.

This is why Meadows carefully distinguishes seeking the wrong goal from its near-neighbor, [[Rule Beating]]: the two are "almost opposite." In rule beating, "the system is out to evade an unpopular or badly designed rule, while giving the appearance of obeying it"; in seeking the wrong goal, "the system obediently follows the rule and produces its specified result—which is not necessarily what anyone actually wants." The perversity here is fidelity, not evasion. And because the goal is the system's direction-setter, the trap is a direct preview of Chapter Six's leverage points: the goal of a system sits near the top of Meadows's ranked list of places to intervene (leverage point #3), because a change in the goal twists everything lower in the system — its rules, information flows, feedback loops, and physical stocks — to conform to it. Get the goal wrong and the whole hierarchy below it is bent, faithfully, toward the wrong end. The way out is correspondingly high-leverage but conceptually simple: "Specify indicators and goals that reflect the real welfare of the system. Be especially careful not to confuse effort with result or you will end up with a system that is producing effort, not result."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — seeking the wrong goal is a [[Systems Theory|system trap]]: because the goal is "the direction-setter of the system," a goal that is "defined inaccurately or incompletely" makes the system "obediently work to produce a result that is not really intended or wanted." Its core error is to "confuse effort with result," and its paradigm case is GNP, which measures throughput ("flows of stuff made and purchased in a year") rather than welfare. It previews leverage point #3 (goals): fix the goal or the whole system faithfully pursues the wrong end.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — the goal is the reference point every balancing loop drives toward; "System behavior is particularly sensitive to the goals of feedback loops."
- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — the goal is "the indicator... toward which balancing feedback loops work," so a wrong goal aims the corrective machinery at the wrong target.
- [[Systems Theory]] — one of the recurring system traps/archetypes ("frequently found system structures that produce familiar behavior patterns") catalogued in Chapter Five.
- [[The Growth Imperative]] — Harari's diagnosis of the economy's mandate to grow is the value-loaded twin of Meadows's structural critique of GNP-as-goal: a society that makes throughput its goal will produce throughput, not welfare.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Rule Beating]] — Meadows names it the "almost opposite" trap: rule beating evades the rule while appearing to obey; seeking the wrong goal genuinely obeys the rule and produces its specified — but unwanted — result. The perversity is fidelity, not evasion.

## Questions It Raises

- If the goal is the system's direction-setter (leverage point #3), who sets the goal — and does the very act of naming a measurable indicator already commit the system to confusing effort with result?
- Meadows says "no particular reason to want the GNP to go up," yet governments act to keep it rising. When a wrong goal is deeply institutionalized, is correcting the indicator (the high-leverage move) actually available, or does the system defend its own goal?
- Every proxy is imperfect; is "seeking the wrong goal" avoidable in principle, or is it an ineliminable hazard of the fact that systems can only be steered toward what can be measured and reported?
