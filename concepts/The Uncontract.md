# The Uncontract

## Brief Definition

Zuboff's term for the annihilation of contract: a unilateral machine execution that makes contractual social relations unnecessary, substituting the determinism of programmed machine processes for the indeterminacy of social processes — promise, dialogue, shared meaning, dispute resolution, and trust.

## Longer Explanation

The uncontract is Zuboff's diagnosis of what surveillance capitalism does to the millennia-old institution of the contract. Her exemplary case is Google chief economist Hal Varian's celebration of vehicular monitoring systems: if a borrower stops making car payments, "it's a lot easier just to instruct the vehicular monitoring system not to allow the car to be started." Varian calls this a "new contract form," but Zuboff argues the label is itself a symptom — "a perfect example of the horseless-carriage syndrome," naming an unprecedented thing in the obsolete vocabulary of the thing it replaces (see [[The Horseless Carriage]]). What Varian describes is not a new kind of contract but its abolition: "It is, in fact, the annihilation of contract; this invention is better understood as the uncontract" (`Directly stated`).

Where a contract is a *social* technology for managing uncertainty between parties — built on promises, will, and the possibility of dispute — the uncontract dispenses with the parties altogether. It "is not a space of contractual relations but rather a unilateral execution that makes those relations unnecessary." It "desocializes the contract, manufacturing certainty through the substitution of automated procedures for promises, dialogue, shared meaning, problem solving, dispute resolution, and trust." Crucially, Zuboff insists this is not merely the automation of existing arrangements: "This is not the automation of society, as some might think, but rather the replacement of society with machine action dictated by economic imperatives" (`Directly stated`). The uncontract is therefore one of the sharpest expressions of the [[Object vs System]] spine running through the vault: social processes (open, indeterminate, between subjects) are replaced by a machine *system* whose outputs are guaranteed.

The uncontract is not a freestanding invention but "a feature of the larger complex that is the means of behavioral modification" — it is the contractual face of [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] and one of the techniques of [[Economies of Action]]. In Chapter 10 Zuboff classes it specifically as a *herding* technique: "'Herding' is a second approach that relies on controlling key elements in a person's immediate context. The uncontract is an example of a herding technique. Shutting down a car's engine irreversibly changes the driver's immediate context, herding her out the car door." Rather than persuade or even predict, the uncontract *forecloses action alternatives* by reaching into the material world and changing the conditions of action directly — it "replac[es] the indeterminacy of social processes with the determinism of programmed machine processes" (`Directly stated`). This is what makes it serve [[A Utopia of Certainty]]: guaranteed outcomes purchased by deleting the open future.

In Chapter 11 Zuboff develops the normative stakes. The uncontract is destructive precisely because it "abandons the human world of legally binding promises and substitutes instead the positivist calculations of automated machine processes." Following the philosopher Hannah Arendt's account of promising as the human capacity to bind an otherwise unpredictable future, Zuboff frames the contract as "a grand achievement of shared will" institutionalized over millennia — the institutional form of the will to will, of [[The Right to the Future Tense]]. Its replacement is "less contract utopia than uncontract dystopia": a world in which "social trust eventually withers," repo men no longer share tea with debtors, and "all that's left to matter are the rules that translate reasons into action, the objective measures of behavior, and the degree of conformance between the two." The uncontract thus extinguishes exactly the friction — uncertainty, negotiation, mercy, the chance of an alternate route through the maze — that Zuboff treats as "the necessary habitat of the present tense" and the price of freedom (`Directly stated` / `Strongly implied` for the Arendtian gloss).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins "the uncontract" in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (ch. 7), elaborates it as a herding technique in ch. 10, and draws out its dystopian stakes in ch. 11. It is the contractual modality of [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]], an instrument of [[Economies of Action]] and [[Instrumentarian Power]], and a building block of [[A Utopia of Certainty]]. Its grounding case is insurance/lending telematics — Varian's vehicular monitoring system — which Zuboff reads through [[The Horseless Carriage]] syndrome as unprecedented power misnamed in old language.

## Related Concepts

- [[Contracts of Adhesion]] — the precursor and counterpoint: the take-it-or-leave-it digital "agreement" that already hollowed out consent; the uncontract goes further, dispensing with the contractual form altogether.
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the larger complex of which the uncontract is "a feature"; the uncontract is its contractual face.
- [[Economies of Action]] — the operational phase (tuning, herding, conditioning) in which the uncontract executes; Zuboff classes it specifically as a *herding* technique.
- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — the societal endgame the uncontract serves: guaranteed outcomes via the deletion of the open future.
- [[The Horseless Carriage]] — the metaphor through which Zuboff exposes "new contract form" as a misnomer for unprecedented unilateral power.
- [[The Reality Business]] / [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the chapter and the economic order that make the uncontract possible (Google's "dominance of the division of learning").
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] / [[Big Other]] — the power form and ubiquitous apparatus that "actuate" the uncontract.
- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the freedom (to promise, to will) the uncontract abolishes.
- [[Society of Control]] — Deleuze's modulating, dividual control resonates with the uncontract's continuous remote actuation, but the uncontract is starker: not modulation of conduct but the *foreclosure* of action by machine.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the uncontract is the institutional substitution of machine determinism for the indeterminacy of the willing subject; it does not merely predict the will, it preempts it.
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the uncontract converts the friction of freedom (negotiation, dispute, mercy) into "unnecessary friction in the march toward guaranteed outcomes."

## Questions It Raises

- If a contract is a social technology for binding an open future through promise, can any "uncontract" preserve the moral relations (trust, mutual obligation) that gave contract its authority — or is the loss constitutive?
- Zuboff distinguishes the uncontract from mere automation ("the replacement of society with machine action"). Where is the line between automating a social process and abolishing it?
- Does the uncontract's logic of guaranteed outcomes have an outside — can there be a remainder of the indeterminate, the negotiable, the merciful — or does it tend, like [[A Utopia of Certainty]], toward totality?
- Curatorially: how would an artwork *stage* the uncontract — render the silent unilateral machine execution perceptible against the social ritual (the repo man's tea, the give-and-take) it deletes?
