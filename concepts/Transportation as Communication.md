# Transportation as Communication

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's thesis that the physical movement of bodies and goods (roads, bridges, sea routes, rivers, canals) and the movement of information are one continuous phenomenon — both are "communication" — so that the electric age is best defined as the historical transition from transport to information by means of electricity.

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 10 of *Understanding Media* McLuhan treats roads and paper routes as a single subject because, for him, carrying goods and carrying messages are the same act of transport at different speeds. He notes that the word "communication" was already in extensive use for "roads and bridges, sea routes, rivers, and canals" long "before it became transformed into 'information movement' in the electric age." The history of media can therefore be read as the rise of the idea of transportation as communication, followed by its mutation into pure information once the telegraph let messages outrun the messenger. `Directly stated`

McLuhan licenses the equation etymologically. The word "metaphor" derives from the Greek *meta* plus *pherein*, "to carry across or transport" — so to make a metaphor and to move a parcel are, at root, the same operation of carrying-across. On this basis he declares the book "concerned with all forms of transport of goods and information, both as metaphor and exchange," collapsing the usual boundary between the literal haulage of matter and the figurative transport of meaning. `Directly stated`

The concept's payload is that no form of transport is a neutral conduit. "Each form of transport not only carries, but translates and transforms, the sender, the receiver, and the message." This is the precise hinge that ties Transportation as Communication to `The Medium Is the Message`: because the carrying medium reshapes what it carries (and the people at both ends), the choice of road, ship, railway, or electric signal is itself the content with social consequence. McLuhan tracks this through Part II case studies — the Roman paper routes and phonetic alphabet that built and then lost an empire, the post roads of England "paid for by the newspapers," the railway, the motorcar, the airplane — each a transport form whose speed reorganizes space, separates functions, and finally, at electric speed, implodes the center-margin structure into "the global village." `Directly stated`

The electric age marks the limit case: once "information itself is the main traffic" and moves "at the speed of signals in the central nervous system," the wheel and the road undergo "recession and obsolescence," and the old logic by which speed-up separated functions reverses into implosion. Transportation as Communication is thus both the through-line of the chapter and the measuring stick by which McLuhan dates the present — not by calendar but by the displacement of moved matter by moved information. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — physical transport and information movement are one continuous "communication"; the electric age is the transition from the former to the latter by electricity, and every transport form translates and transforms sender, receiver, and message.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Medium Is the Message]] — the parent principle: because each transport form "translates and transforms" what it carries, the carrying medium is itself the consequential content.
- [[Information]] — the destination of the historical arc; McLuhan describes information detaching from "solid commodities as stone and papyrus" the way money detached from bullion.
- [[Acceleration]] — McLuhan names "acceleration and disruption" as the principal factors in media impact; speed is the variable that turns transport into a force that reorganizes social space.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Transportation as Communication deliberately refuses this opposition, treating the haulage of matter and the movement of information as the same act of "carrying-across"; the note registers McLuhan's collapse of the material/immaterial boundary that other authors in the vault hold apart.

## Questions It Raises

- If carrying goods and carrying meaning are the same operation, does the "immaterial" in information ever fully detach from a material substrate, or only change its commodity (from papyrus to electric signal)?
- When transport collapses into instantaneous information, what becomes of the social organization that earlier speeds produced — does the "global village" restore the village, or only its image?
- Is the etymology of "metaphor" a genuine warrant for the thesis, or a rhetorical move that makes a historical claim look like a necessary truth?
