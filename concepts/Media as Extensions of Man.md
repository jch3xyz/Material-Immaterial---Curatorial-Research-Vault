# Media as Extensions of Man

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's master frame, and the subtitle of *Understanding Media*: every medium or technology is an extension of a human body part, sense, or faculty — the mechanical age extended the body in space, while the electric age extends the central nervous system itself.

## Longer Explanation

For Marshall McLuhan, "media" is not a synonym for the press or broadcasting; it names any extension of ourselves. The wheel extends the foot, clothing extends the skin, the book extends the eye, money extends the sense lives of peoples — and each such extension reorganizes the whole sensory and social field around it. This is the unifying thesis the book carries across its Part II case studies: as McLuhan writes in Chapter 10, "It is a persistent theme of this book that all technologies are extensions of our physical and nervous systems to increase power and speed." The extensions thesis is what lets him treat games, clocks, roads, the photograph, and the motorcar as media in the same register. `Directly stated`

The frame is periodized. In the Introduction McLuhan distinguishes two great ages: "During the mechanical ages we had extended our bodies in space. Today, after more than a century of electric technology, we have extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace, abolishing both space and time as far as our planet is concerned." Mechanical extensions were partial and fragmentary, extending single organs outward; electric extension is total, externalizing the nervous system and consciousness as such. The book's announced horizon is "the final phase of the extensions of man— the technological simulation of consciousness." `Directly stated`

Extension is never gain alone. In Chapter 4 (Narcissus as Narcosis) McLuhan ties extension to amputation and numbness: "Any invention or technology is an extension or self-amputation of our physical bodies, and such extension also demands new ratios or new equilibriums among the other organs and extensions of the body." Each new medium forces a recalibration of the sense ratios; the price of amplifying one faculty is a corresponding numbing or self-amputation of the others. And because technology is "part of our bodies" (Chapter 7), the relation is not optional or external — the urge to keep media running is, for McLuhan, testimony that they are bodily. `Directly stated`

The electric phase folds the body back inside its own extensions. In Chapter 6 (Media as Translators) McLuhan describes a reversal: "By putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems, by means of electric media, we set up a dynamic by which all previous technologies that are mere extensions of hands and feet and teeth and bodily heat-controls—all such extensions of our bodies, including cities—will be translated into information systems." This is the hinge that connects McLuhan's extensions thesis to the vault's later cluster on `Informatics`, `The Body as Data`, and the translation of the world into a problem of coding: for McLuhan the body and its prior tools are already being rendered as information. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — the originating frame. Media are extensions of the body, senses, and nervous system; the subtitle *The Extensions of Man* names the thesis, and the closing case-study chapter is itself titled "Games: The Extensions of Man." The mechanical age extends the body in space; the electric age extends the central nervous system and, finally, consciousness. Every extension is also a self-amputation that resets the sense ratios. See the coexisting definition [[Extensions of Man (McLuhan)]].
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** (cross-link, not merged) — McLuhan's claim that prior extensions are "translated into information systems" anticipates, in a different key, the informatic dissolution of the body Hayles tracks under [[The Body as Data]] and `Embodiment`. McLuhan keeps the body as the origin and measure of every medium; Hayles documents the cybernetic tradition's drive to make embodiment incidental to pattern. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **[[Donna Haraway]]** (cross-link, not merged) — Haraway's [[The Cyborg]] makes the human-machine boundary breakdown a political ontology; McLuhan's extensions thesis is an earlier, less critical statement that the machine is "part of our bodies." McLuhan describes the merger as a sensory fact, Haraway as an occasion for politics. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Related Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — McLuhan's media are extensions *of the body*; the thesis is grounded in the sensing, organ-bearing body rather than in disembodied information.
- [[The Body as Data]] — McLuhan's electric phase, in which prior extensions are "translated into information systems," is a precursor to the rendering of the body as data.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the human whose nervous system is externalized in technology is, in McLuhan's terms, already a hybrid of organism and machine.
- [[Extensions of Man (McLuhan)]] — the dedicated definition note for McLuhan's term; this concept note develops it as the book's master frame.
- [[The Medium Is the Message]] — the companion thesis; the "message" of any medium is the change of scale and sense-ratio its extension introduces, "quite independently of their 'content'."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]] — the view that the essential self is a pattern that can be lifted free of any body runs opposite to McLuhan's insistence that media are extensions *of* the physical and nervous body, and that "technology is part of our bodies." `Interpretive synthesis`

## Questions It Raises

- If every medium is an extension of the body, is there any technology that is *not* an extension of man — and does the "technological simulation of consciousness" McLuhan forecasts remain an extension, or does it cut the cord to the body altogether?
- McLuhan pairs every extension with a "self-amputation" and numbing of other senses; what does the electric extension of the whole nervous system amputate?
- When prior extensions are "translated into information systems," does the body remain the origin and measure of media, or does it become just one more datum — the question the vault's `The Body as Data` cluster inherits?
- Is McLuhan's body-centered frame compatible with, or opposed to, accounts (Hayles, the cybernetic tradition) that treat embodiment as incidental to the pattern of information?
