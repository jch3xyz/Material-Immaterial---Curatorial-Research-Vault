# The Anthropocene

## Brief Definition

In Yuval Noah Harari's usage, the Anthropocene is the proposed geological epoch — the last roughly 70,000 years — in which a single species, *Homo sapiens*, became the dominant force shaping the entire global ecology, collapsing the planet's many separate ecosystems into one human-managed unit and bringing it to the threshold of replacing natural selection with intelligent design.

## Longer Explanation

Harari reframes the official Holocene as the **Anthropocene** to mark a qualitative break: not merely that humans live in this period, but that *Homo sapiens became the single most important agent of change in the global ecology* (`Directly stated`). The claim is scaled against deep time — for four billion years, ecological revolutions and mass extinctions were driven by *mighty natural forces such as climate change, tectonic plate movement, volcanic eruptions and asteroid collisions*, never by one species. Harari's emblem of the new regime is biomass distribution: *more than 90 per cent of the large animals of the world (i.e. those weighing more than a few kilograms) are either humans or domesticated animals* (`Directly stated`). The wolves of the Brothers Grimm are gone; the world is *populated mainly by humans and their domesticated animals*.

A second, structural sense of the Anthropocene is ecological *unification*. Where the planet *never constituted a single ecosystem; rather, it was a collection of many loosely connected ecosystems*, Sapiens *broke the barriers that had separated the globe into independent ecological zones. In the Anthropocene, the planet became for the first time a single ecological unit* (`Directly stated`). Distinct, locally adapting populations (his jellyfish in the Persian Gulf and in Tokyo Bay) are replaced by a torrent of human-mediated mixing — *aeroplanes, oil tankers and giant cargo ships* — that binds every island and continent into one managed whole. This collapse of plurality into a single administered unit is the ecological correlate of the book's broader argument about human dominion.

The epoch is also, for Harari, a *threshold*. The asteroid that killed the dinosaurs *changed the trajectory of terrestrial evolution, but not its fundamental rules*; all life *remained confined to the organic realm*. The Anthropocene's defining novelty is the prospect of breaking those two fixed rules at once: *Now humankind is poised to replace natural selection with intelligent design, and to extend life from the organic realm into the inorganic* (`Directly stated`). This is the hinge on which *Homo Deus* turns the Anthropocene from a story about ecology into a story about the engineering of life itself — the migration of life off its organic, embodied substrate, which sets the concept against [[Embodiment]] and connects it to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension (`Interpretive synthesis`).

The ecological *stakes* of this regime are supplied by *The Modern Covenant* (Ch. 6), which names the brittleness underneath the growth that the Anthropocene makes possible. There Harari writes that *both scientific progress and economic growth take place within a brittle biosphere*, that *the real nemesis of the modern economy is ecological collapse*, and that *an ecological meltdown will cause economic ruin, political turmoil, a fall in human standards of living, and it might threaten the very existence of human civilisation* (`Directly stated`). The species that became a planetary force is also the one whose growth-creed (the modern deal of *power* in exchange for *meaning*) destabilises the very biosphere it now governs — and which, in the "hi-tech Ark" fantasy, contemplates engineering its way out rather than slowing down (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — Anchored in Ch. 2/3, *The Anthropocene*. Harari uses the term less as a stratigraphic dispute than as a narrative device: the epoch in which humans *became gods* with respect to other animals, made the planet a single ecological unit, and now stand poised to replace natural selection with intelligent design and to extend life into the inorganic. The Anthropocene is thus the launch-pad for the book's "upgrading of Sapiens into gods." Note: Harari himself dates it to the last 70,000 years (from the Cognitive Revolution), a deliberately *longer* span than the industrial-era datings common elsewhere — a variant to flag rather than smooth over (`Directly stated` for the dating).

## Related Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the proposed extension of life *from the organic realm into the inorganic* is precisely a crossing of the material/immaterial boundary this vault tracks.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the Anthropocene's promised move to extend life *into the inorganic* points away from the organic, embodied substrate; the concept's horizon is in tension with embodiment as a condition of life.

## Questions It Raises

- Is the Anthropocene best dated from the Cognitive Revolution (Harari's ~70,000 years), the Agricultural Revolution, or the industrial / Great Acceleration — and what does each dating make visible or hide?
- If the planet has become *a single ecological unit*, is ecological plurality recoverable, or is unification irreversible?
- Does "replacing natural selection with intelligent design" describe a genuine break in life's rules, or a rhetorical inflation of incremental biotechnology?
- Harari frames the Anthropocene as human triumph and human peril simultaneously; does the "brittle biosphere" framing undercut, or merely qualify, the narrative of dominion?
- What is the curatorial/material stake when life is imagined as migrating off its organic substrate into the inorganic?
