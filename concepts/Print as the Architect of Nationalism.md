# Print as the Architect of Nationalism

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's thesis that the nation-state is a by-product of print technology: by turning each vernacular into a mass medium and exploding the blood tribe into a uniform aggregate of trained individuals, typography reassembled those interchangeable units into the nation, defined as "continuity and competition in homogeneous space."

## Longer Explanation

For McLuhan, nationalism is not a primordial political fact but "of the many unforeseen consequences of typography," and the most familiar one. The argument runs through a single mechanism. First, print acts on language itself: "Political unification of populations by means of vernacular and language groupings was unthinkable before printing turned each vernacular into an extensive mass medium." The dialect a region happened to speak became, once set in repeatable type, a standardized public surface around which a population could be administered, taught, and addressed at scale. Second, print acts on the social bond: "The tribe, an extended form of a family of blood relatives, is exploded by print, and is replaced by an association of men homogeneously trained to be individuals." The kinship network of "close family bonds" is detonated, and its members are re-formed as uniform, separable units. Third, these units are recombined. Print "broke the individual out of the traditional group while providing a model of how to add individual to individual in massive agglomeration of power" — the nation is that agglomeration. McLuhan's compressed definition is that nationalism is "continuity and competition in homogeneous space," a space whose homogeneity is the residue of "a principle of extension by homogenization that is the key to understanding Western power" (Directly stated).

What makes the nation specifically a *visual* artifact is print's bias toward the eye. "Nationalism itself came as an intense new visual image of group destiny and status, and depended on a speed of information movement unknown before printing." The nation is something seen — a uniform image of belonging — and it could not cohere until print accelerated the movement of information enough to hold a dispersed population in a single picture of itself. Two sub-facets of the print effect feed this directly and are best understood as part of this same concept rather than as separate notes. The first is **extension by homogenization**: the uniform, repeatable typographic page processes diverse regions into interchangeable parts, so that "the open society is open by virtue of a uniform typographic educational processing that permits indefinite expansion of any group by additive means." The second is **detachment, the power to act without reacting** — "the most significant of the gifts of typography to man is that of detachment and noninvolvement — the power to act without reacting," the "specialization by dissociation that has created Western power and efficiency." It is this dissociation of action from feeling that "split literate man out of the tribal world of close family bonds," supplying the detached, individuated unit nationalism requires (Directly stated).

The concept is built historically rather than abstractly. McLuhan dates the mechanism to the Renaissance — "In the Renaissance it was the speed of print and the ensuing market and commercial developments that made nationalism (which is continuity and competition in homogeneous space) as natural as it was new" — and reads it across his Part II case studies as the same logic that produced "industrialism, mass markets, and universal literacy and education." The "heterogeneities and noncompetitive discontinuities of medieval guilds and family organization" became "a great nuisance" once print demanded "more fragmentation and uniformity of function"; the polymath Cellini, the "goldsmith-cum-painter-cum-sculptor-cum-writer-cum-condottiere, became obsolete." Nationalism and the specialized, homogeneous individual rise together (Directly stated).

Finally, the thesis is explicitly historical and therefore reversible — print built nationalism, and electricity is now dismantling it. "Today nationalism as an image still depends on the press but has all the electric media against it." Because the nation was a function of print-borne information speed, the instant speed of electric media undoes its precondition: "the effect of even jet-plane speeds is to render the older national groupings of social organization quite unworkable." The same explosion that "shattered the bonds of tribal man, thus exploding him into agglomeration of individuals" reverses under electricity — "He becomes tribal once more. The human family becomes one tribe again." This is why nationalism, an artifact of the typographic age, is obsolescing into the electric retribalization McLuhan elsewhere names the global village (Directly stated).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** (*[[Understanding Media]]*, Ch. 18) — Nationalism is the titular, headline effect of the printed word: print homogenizes vernaculars and individuals, then aggregates them into the nation as "continuity and competition in homogeneous space," a "visual image of group destiny" dependent on print-borne information speed and now obsolescing under electric media. Print is treated not as a carrier of national content but as the formal cause of national form — a direct application of the principle that [[The Medium is the Message]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] — print's detribalizing explosion of the blood tribe is the precondition for nationalism; the same process reverses under electricity.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Global Village]] — the electric retribalization that runs against nationalism; where print gave "an intense new visual image of group destiny," electric speed re-collapses the agglomerated individuals so that "the human family becomes one tribe again."

## Questions It Raises

- If the nation-state is a by-product of print, is the contemporary erosion of national cohesion better read as a media-environmental shift (electric/digital speed) than as a political failure?
- McLuhan calls "extension by homogenization" the key to *Western* power — does the print-nationalism mechanism travel to non-Western print cultures, or is it specific to the phonetic-alphabet/typographic lineage?
- "Detachment, the power to act without reacting" is offered as print's greatest gift and, in the electric age, "an embarrassment." Can the detached, disinterested liberal subject survive the loss of its typographic infrastructure?
- If electric media are "against" nationalism, what replaces the nation as the unit of large-scale belonging — and is retribalization a return to community or to its more dangerous forms?
