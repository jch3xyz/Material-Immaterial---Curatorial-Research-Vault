# The Authority of Knowledge

## Brief Definition

In McLuhan's account of the electric media, the *authority of knowledge* is the nonlineal, nonvisual, inclusive form of power that replaces delegated chain-of-command authority once electric speed makes it "not feasible to exercise delegated authority by telephone."

## Longer Explanation

McLuhan develops the concept in the Telephone chapter as the answer to a precise question: when the speed of the telephone bypasses "all hierarchical arrangements" so that "the pyramidal structure of job-division and description and delegated powers cannot withstand" it, "what sort of authority does come into play?" His reply is that "on the telephone only the authority of knowledge will work." He then sets the two authorities against each other term for term: "Delegated authority is lineal, visual, hierarchical. The authority of knowledge is nonlineal, nonvisual, and inclusive." Delegated authority operates by sequence and clearance — "To act, the delegated person must always get clearance from the chain-of-command" — whereas the electric situation "eliminates such patterns; such 'checks and balances' are alien to the inclusive authority of knowledge." (Directly stated.)

The shift is structural, not merely managerial. McLuhan describes the telephone as introducing a "'seamless web' of interlaced patterns in management and decision-making," a web that "by-pass[es] all hierarchical arrangements, and... involve[s] people in depth." Because the medium "demands complete participation" and "involve[s] people in depth," authority can no longer be parceled out as discrete delegated jobs radiating from a center; it is redistributed across whoever holds the relevant knowledge in the moment of decision. This is why McLuhan reads the electric reversal as a movement against the Western form of freedom that "has always taken the form of the explosive, the divisive": the one-way movement "outward from center-to-margin" is reversed, and "in an electric structure there are, so far as the time and space of this planet are concerned, no margins. There can, therefore, be dialogue only among centers and among equals." (Directly stated.)

The Typewriter chapter corroborates and generalizes the concept by tying it to the nature of electric *work*. McLuhan distinguishes the "mechanical age," in which "work had meant the processing of various materials by assembly-line fragmentation of operations and hierarchically delegated authority," from the electric age, in which "the mere interrelating of people by selected information is now the principal source of wealth." Electric circuits, he writes, "eliminate both the assembly line and the delegated authority." The computer is his sharpest example: "Especially with the computer, the work effort is applied at the 'programming' level, and such effort is one of information and knowledge." It is on this basis that "the telephone and other such speed-ups of information have ended the divisions of delegated authority in favor of the 'authority of knowledge.'" (Directly stated.) The work is no longer delegated down a pyramid; it is applied once, at the level of program and knowledge — the same logic by which "the telephone fuses functions" and the typewriter "fuses composition and publication."

For the vault's power/control thread, the concept marks McLuhan's distinctive position. Where [[Byung-Chul Han]] reads the same dissolution of disciplinary, hierarchical structure as the rise of a smoother, more total domination (the [[Digital Panopticon]], [[Smart Power]], the [[Society of Control]]), McLuhan presents the substitution of delegated authority by the authority of knowledge as descriptively neutral and even emancipatory in form — though he flags its danger by noting that "restraints on electric absolutist power can be achieved, not by the separation of powers, but by a pluralism of centers." The two accounts converge on the diagnosis (electric speed dissolves the lineal hierarchy) and diverge on its valence. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — coins and defines the concept in the Telephone chapter ("on the telephone only the authority of knowledge will work"; it is "nonlineal, nonvisual, and inclusive"), and generalizes it across the electric media in the Typewriter chapter, where "the telephone and other such speed-ups of information have ended the divisions of delegated authority in favor of the 'authority of knowledge,'" the computer applying work "at the 'programming' level."
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — does not use the term, but his account of [[Smart Power]] and the [[Society of Control]] describes the same collapse of the disciplinary chain-of-command pyramid under digital networks, reading it as a more pervasive form of domination rather than as a leveling toward "dialogue... among equals." (Speculative connection.)

## Related Concepts

- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the broader claim that grounds it: electric speed extends the nervous system into a global field where lineal delegation cannot survive.
- [[Center-Margin Structure]] — the lineal/visual/hierarchical order (single center radiating power outward to margins) that the authority of knowledge dissolves; McLuhan's "no margins" follows directly.
- [[A Pluralism of Centers]] — McLuhan's proposed restraint on "electric absolutist power": not separation of powers, but many co-equal centers in dialogue.
- [[Decentralization]] — "The power of the telephone to decentralize every operation"; the authority of knowledge is the form of power proper to a decentralized field.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's later account of the same structural dissolution read as intensified control rather than emancipation.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — delegated, lineal, visual, hierarchical authority belongs to the mechanical pole; the inclusive authority of knowledge to the electric pole.
- [[Center vs Margin]] — the authority of knowledge is precisely what obtains when, at electric speed, "there are... no margins" and power can no longer radiate from center to periphery.

## Questions It Raises

- Is the authority of knowledge genuinely less coercive than delegated authority, or does its inclusiveness and "complete participation" make domination more total, as Han's reading of digital control implies?
- If "checks and balances" are "alien to the inclusive authority of knowledge," does McLuhan's "pluralism of centers" actually constrain "electric absolutist power," or merely redescribe it?
- Does applying work "at the 'programming' level" relocate hierarchy (toward whoever programs) rather than abolish it?
