# Reflexivity

## Brief Definition

The movement whereby that which generates a system is folded back into the system it generates—the observer drawn into the observed—the organizing concept of second-wave (1960–1980) cybernetics and the bridge from [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] to [[Autopoiesis|autopoiesis]].

## Longer Explanation

For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], reflexivity is the second of the three concepts around which the cybernetic tradition successively organized itself, the one that names the inward turn the discipline took once it stopped treating the observer as standing safely outside the system. Her working definition is deliberately spare because the concept has a "tendency to mutate": "Reflexivity is the movement whereby that which has been used to generate a system is made, through a changed perspective, to become part of the system it generates." Gödel's coding, Escher's two hands drawing each other, Borges's dreamer who is himself dreamed—each entangles "that which generates the system with the system," and each tends, as Hayles stresses, "notoriously toward infinite regress." Reflexivity is therefore not a neutral refinement but a destabilizer: it "has subversive effects because it confuses and entangles the boundaries we impose on the world in order to make sense of that world."

Reflexivity is born from the same informational [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] that first underwrote [[Homeostasis|homeostasis]], but it carries that loop in the opposite direction. Where homeostasis stabilizes a system around a setpoint, reflexivity lets the loop run through the observer: "feedback can also loop through the observers, drawing them in to become part of the system being observed." It "entered cybernetics primarily through discussions about the observer," and at the [[Cybernetics|Macy Conferences]] it had no name—"the word “reflexivity” does not appear in the transcripts"—so it "remained diffuse," a nebulous cluster the physical scientists could feel but not grasp. The decisive contest was over what information *is* versus what it *does*: "Making information an action links it with reflexivity, for then its effect on the receiver must be taken into account, and measuring this effect sets up the potential for a reflexive spiral through an infinite regress of observers." Reflexivity "lost" at Macy "because specifying and delimiting context quickly ballooned into an unmanageable project"—it threatened quantification, and the chain of association binding it to subjectivity (above all through Lawrence Kubie's psychoanalytic reading of every utterance as a "mirror reflecting the speaker’s psyche") was "enough to relegate reflexivity to the category of “nonscience” rather than “science.”"

What was relegated was not discarded. As Hayles puts it, "the idea hung in the air, and a few key thinkers—especially Margaret Mead, Gregory Bateson, and Heinz von Foerster—resolved to pursue it after the Macy Conferences ran out of steam," and the second wave "grew out of attempts to incorporate reflexivity into the cybernetic paradigm at a fundamental level." [[Heinz von Foerster|Von Foerster]]'s punning *Observing Systems* "announces reflexivity as a central theme"—the observer of systems can himself be constituted as a system to be observed—and his "second-order cybernetics" "extended cybernetic principles to the cyberneticians themselves." [[Gregory Bateson|Bateson]] reintroduced "the reflexive dimension that McCulloch had fought so hard to exorcise," but in a form that, in Hayles's words, did not depend on "a psychoanalytic entanglement of conscious and unconscious meanings in scientific statements," recasting the inner world as "a metaphor for the outer world." The rehabilitation hinged on insulation: "if reflexivity was to be credible, it had to be insulated against subjectivity and presented in a context in which it had at least the potential for rigorous (preferably mathematical) formulation."

Reflexivity is the bridge concept to [[Autopoiesis|autopoiesis]]: it reaches "its mature phase" when [[Humberto Maturana|Maturana]] and Varela turn "the reflexive turn into a fully articulated epistemology that sees the world as a set of informationally closed systems." Here reflexivity becomes the very mechanism that generates the subject—"the system’s reflexive doubling back on its own representations generates the human subject as an observer"—and closure and recursivity come to "play the foundational role in autopoietic theory that self-possession played in classic liberal theory." This is why reflexivity matters to Hayles's larger argument: by drawing [[The Observer|the observer]] into the system, it reopens the question of embodiment that first-wave information theory had foreclosed. Reflexivity does not vanish in the third wave; like homeostasis before it, it "lingers on as an allusion that authenticates new elements," "displaced in the third wave by emergence."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — treats reflexivity as the protean organizing concept of second-wave cybernetics and the conceptual hinge by which the observer (and, with the observer, the body) re-enters a discipline that had tried to externalize both; she also notes its kinship with the "reflexive move" of contemporary critical theory, "produced by the reflexivity that it also produces."
- **[[Heinz von Foerster]]** — formalizes reflexivity as "second-order cybernetics," the cybernetics of cyberneticians; *Observing Systems* "announces reflexivity as a central theme," and he works to make it "manageable" by distinguishing the observer as "a discrete system" rather than a psychoanalytic depth.
- **[[Gregory Bateson]]** — reintroduces the reflexive dimension as a non-psychoanalytic epistemology built on "concluding that the internal world of subjective experience is" a metaphor for the external world, refusing decontextualization as "a systematic distortion."
- **[[Humberto Maturana]]** — carries reflexivity into [[Autopoiesis|autopoiesis]], where "the observer must be taken into account" and the subject is produced by "recursively generating representations of our interactions"; his observer "depends on positionality rather than personality"—relativism, not subjectivism.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — reflexivity is the feedback loop turned on the observer; it "grew out of" the loop that first served homeostasis.
- [[The Observer]] — the figure through whom reflexivity enters cybernetics; "the important problems were how to locate the observer inside the system and the system inside the observer."
- [[Autopoiesis]] — reflexivity's mature form; the reflexive turn becomes "a fully articulated epistemology" of informationally closed, self-making systems.
- [[Self-Organization]] — von Foerster's "On Self-Organizing Systems and Their Environments" is where reflexivity first appears "as a circular dynamic"; the second wave runs from reflexivity to self-organization.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the parent discipline; reflexivity is the concept around which its second (second-order) wave is organized.
- [[Systems Theory]] — second-wave reflexivity feeds the systems-theoretic lineage (Maturana, Varela, Luhmann) of informationally closed systems.
- [[Information]] — reflexivity is allied with information defined as *action* (a "difference that makes a difference"), against information defined as a transportable thing.
- [[Posthumanism]] — by making the observer part of the observed and grounding the subject in recursive closure, reflexivity participates in the displacement of the autonomous liberal subject.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Homeostasis]] — the first-wave concept reflexivity unsettles and partly replaces: "Making information a thing allies it with homeostasis... Making information an action links it with reflexivity." Homeostasis "won in the first wave largely because it was more manageable quantitatively"; reflexivity threatened exactly that manageability. In the second wave homeostasis survives only as a "skeuomorph."

## Questions It Raises

- If reflexivity folds [[The Observer|the observer]] back into the system, does it return the *body* of the observer too—or only a positional, bodiless observer "without further specification," as in Maturana? (ties to [[Embodiment]], [[The Body as Data]])
- Reflexivity was relegated to "nonscience" because it dissolved the line between objective fact and the observer's "internal states." Is that line ever recoverable once information is defined as what it *does* to a receiver? (ties to [[Embodiment vs Information]], [[Information]])
- When autopoiesis makes closure and recursivity do the work "that self-possession played in classic liberal theory," does reflexivity dismantle the liberal humanist subject or merely re-found it on new ground? (ties to [[Posthumanism]], [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]])
- Reflexivity privileges pattern, recursion, and informational closure over material instantiation. Does drawing the observer into the loop re-materialize the system, or further abstract it from flesh? (ties to [[Material vs Immaterial]], [[The Body as Data]])
