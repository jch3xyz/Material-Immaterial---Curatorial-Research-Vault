# Homing to the Herd

## Brief Definition

The ethological figure — borrowed by Zuboff from biology — for the human orientation toward the crowd rather than to territory: a natural instinct that social media environments are engineered to "induce and exaggerate," particularly among the young, who are lured to the social mirror while commercial harvesters quietly cast their nets.

## Longer Explanation

"Homing to the herd" is the term ethologists give to the adaptation of certain species — Zuboff names passenger pigeons and herring — that "home to the crowd rather than to a particular territory." Zuboff transplants this biological figure onto the human social being to name a disposition we all share to varying degrees but which is "most pronounced in the stages of life that we call adolescence and emerging adulthood." This orientation toward the group is, for Zuboff, natural; what is unnatural is its industrial intensification. Facebook and social media in general are, in her words, "environments engineered to induce and exaggerate this homing to the human herd, particularly among the young." (`Directly stated`)

The danger lies in what happens when this orientation meets a predator. Zuboff cites biologist Bernd Heinrich's account of the passenger pigeons, whose "social sense was so strong that it drew the new predator, technologically equipped humans, from afar," making them "not only easy targets, but easily duped." Commercial harvesters tracked the flocks, used huge nets to catch thousands at a time, and exploited the birds' bonds of empathy with the "stool pigeon" technique — a few birds blinded and tethered to a perch so that the flock would descend to "attend to them," and so be slaughtered together. The pigeon "had no home boundaries over which to spread itself and continued to orient only to itself"; the only "home" it knew "was in the crowd, and now they had become victims of it." (`Directly stated`)

The analogy maps the pigeon's doom onto the networked young. As we "fixate on the crowd, the technologically equipped commercial harvesters circle quietly and cast their nets." The social mirror's "dark charms of social comparison, social pressure, social influence" rivet attention; the natural self-other balance that adolescence must negotiate is complicated, delayed, or impeded; and the orientation that was once an adaptation becomes, under the conditions of [[Surveillance Capitalism]], a vulnerability to be harvested. Where the passenger pigeon's fatal flaw was the absence of "territorial boundaries of human predators," the young person's is the absence of boundaries against [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the herding instinct turned against itself in the service of others' commercial ends. (`Strongly implied` for the mapping; the pigeon facts and the Facebook claim are `Directly stated`.)

This makes homing to the herd a keystone of Zuboff's account of [[The Hive]]: the social orientation is the natural raw material that surveillance capitalism reshapes, "tuning the pitch of our behavior with the rewards and punishments of social pressure, herding the human heart toward confluence." It is the human-nature analogue to industrial capitalism's exploitation of nature — the instinct that the [[Surveillance]] economy discovers, induces, exaggerates, and ultimately farms. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — adopts the ethologists' phrase and Bernd Heinrich's passenger-pigeon case to argue that social media deliberately amplifies the natural orientation toward the herd, especially in the young, exploiting it for surveillance revenues. The figure is hers by adoption, not coinage: she presents it as an existing biological term repurposed for diagnosis of the networked social mirror.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Hive]] — homing to the herd is the natural instinct that the hive-life of instrumentarian society is engineered around; the herd, intensified and confined, becomes the hive.
- [[Social Comparison]] — the specific psychological process the social mirror releases; homing to the herd is what makes us susceptible to it ("I want to be like you" as the risk of exclusion threatens negative social comparison).
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] / [[Surveillance]] — the economic order that depends on "the exploitation and control of human nature," of which the herding instinct is a prime resource.
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the power that tunes and herds behavior toward "confluence"; homing to the herd is the human disposition it acts upon.
- [[Tuning, Herding, and Conditioning]] — the operational triad through which social pressure and social comparison are harnessed; the "herding" term is literal here.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-grounding individual whose self-other balance homing to the herd "complicates, delays, or impedes"; the herd-oriented young are precisely the subject the liberal-humanist ideal cannot account for under networked conditions.

## Metaphors and Imagery

- **The passenger pigeon and the harvester's nets.** The governing image: a species whose "social sense was so strong that it drew the new predator," caught by the thousand in huge nets because "the only 'home' they knew was in the crowd." The "stool pigeon" — a bird blinded and tethered so the flock descends to attend to it and is slaughtered — is the cruelest figure: empathy itself turned into the lure. (`Directly stated`)
- **The social mirror.** "We are lured to the social mirror, our attention riveted by its dark charms of social comparison, social pressure, social influence." The young are to the social mirror as the pigeons were to the decoyed perch. (`Directly stated`)
- **The harvesters who circle and cast their nets.** "As we fixate on the crowd, the technologically equipped commercial harvesters circle quietly and cast their nets." The surveillance capitalist occupies the predator's position the pigeon's adaptation made fatal. (`Directly stated`)

## Questions It Raises

- If homing to the herd is a natural adaptation, can its industrial intensification be distinguished — in principle or in measurement — from its ordinary expression, or does the social mirror collapse the difference?
- Zuboff stresses that the instinct is "most pronounced" in adolescence; does the harm she describes attach to the developmental stage, to the medium, or only to their conjunction under surveillance revenues?
- The passenger pigeon went extinct (the last died in the Cincinnati Zoo in 1914). What is the human-developmental equivalent of extinction that the analogy implies — and is it meant literally for the self-other balance, or as warning?
- If "Men made it" — if the hive is engineered rather than natural — what would a connective architecture that did not "depend upon surveillance revenues" do with the same herding instinct?
