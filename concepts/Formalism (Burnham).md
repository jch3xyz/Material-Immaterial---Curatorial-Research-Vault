# Formalism (Burnham)

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's name for the analysis of sculpture through "those material-optical-tactile features of sculpture which can be identified and logically examined as separate physical entities" — shape, proportion, scale, structure, texture, color, context — a prescientific, intuition-based methodology that displaced figuration, became "a strategic necessity," and which Burnham reframes as "the prehistory of scientific technique itself."

## Longer Explanation

For Burnham, formalism is the working method that replaced figuration once sculpture ceased to be "universally figura tive." He defines its objects precisely: the formal qualities are "those that still give form in the original classical sense: shape, proportion, scale, structure, texture, color, context-those aspects of the visual reality to which we turn for our nonliterary meanings in objects." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 3.) When sculpture was figurative, these same categories had biological referents — composition was the positioning of the human figure, proportion derived from anatomical canons, structure was anatomy, texture the simulation of natural surfaces, color "all but unheard of." Burnham reads the whole figurative vocabulary as "nothing more than a nineteenth-century biologist's morphological conception, a categorizing of form templates according to the visual properties of different species." As biology shed its reliance on external organic appearance, so did sculpture. (`Directly stated`, Ch. 3.)

Formalism is not, for Burnham, a comfortable or honored term. "Formalism has been a strategic necessity; it does not fit comfortably into the traditional lexicon of art." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 3.) The word carries "antipathetic connotations" — stiffness, boredom, soullessness — and Burnham locates it within a Hegelian endgame: art's "truth-bearing efficacy" yielding to a "scientific-philosophical spirit" that holds "a more exacting standard of truth." Formalism thus appears as "a kind of transitional phase, a 'phasing out' of art toward scientific rationalism." Yet it is also generative: "as a manifestation of materialism, formalism has both revitalized and demoralized sculpture," and "as formalism grows more daring in its techniques, it becomes more pervasively influential within the plastic arts." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 3.)

The methodology's signature, and its central instability, is the reliance on intuition. Where figurative formalism "had the exactness and surety of geometrical regularity," modern formalist analysis "increasingly depended on intuition rather than regulation"; "no sets of rules exist today in sculpture as they did in the past," and the surviving canons are "the more or less acknowledged property of their inventors." From this Burnham draws the formula that governs the chapter: "The problem of formalism remains that of sustaining irrationality within a framework of increasingly rational technique." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 3.) Formalism is "a stricter application of prescientific methodology to the arts" — it apes the analytic drive of science (the desire "to reveal and analyze underlying truths") without science's access to "more fundamental realms of order (usually invisible)," and so leads not to discovery but to "growing stasis."

In Chapter 4 Burnham promotes formalism from a method to a historical hinge. Sculpture analysis "in the present century has been achieved through formalism"; through it "we have learned, as Paul Klee observed, 'the prehistory of the visible.'" But Burnham presses the claim further: "This technique is not just the prehistory of the visible, it is the prehistory of scientific technique itself-that part made possible by visual study." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 4.) Its decline is therefore a cosmological event, not merely a stylistic one: "The decline of formalism is the decline of a world fashioned to operate on a strictly visual-geometrical level." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 4.) Once science discloses "a world of invisible values," a strictly visual-geometrical art is left with "a gradually contracting spectrum of possibilities" — the depletion described under [[Form Exhaustion]] — and invention pushes "sculpture" out of its original domain toward [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]], where the work stages the *experience* of perception rather than supplying a new form. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — defines formalism as the logical examination of sculpture's separate "material-optical-tactile features"; treats it as a prescientific, intuition-based "strategic necessity" that displaced figuration; and reframes it in Chapter 4 as "the prehistory of scientific technique itself," whose decline mirrors the collapse of a visual-geometrical world-picture. The `(Burnham)` suffix marks this sculptural-historical sense and leaves room for other authors' formalisms.

## Related Concepts

- [[Form Exhaustion]] — the fate of formalism: the formalist vocabulary, failing to "change and grow," contracts in "a world of invisible values"; form exhaustion is the depletion of the very stock formalism works with.
- [[Scientific Idealism]] — formalism's idealist face. Where formalism is the *method* of examining sculpture's separable visual qualities, scientific idealism is the *belief* that views essential reality through provisional scientific models; Burnham finds the "aura of scientific idealism" pervading even avowedly de-idealized formalist heirs.
- [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] — the successor program. As formalism's visual-geometrical means run out, invention pushes sculpture toward staging perceptual experience rather than form.
- [[Vitalism]] — the parallel idiom whose "essential 'inner life'" Burnham sets against formalism's analytic, surface-reading attention; both reach exhaustion around 1960.
- [[Technics]] — the larger force. Formalism's drive "to reveal and analyze underlying truths" is the analytic spirit of science applied to form; its "increasingly rational technique" is technics working on the archaic activity of sculpture.
- [[Dematerialization]] (Lippard) — Burnham's account of formalist sculpture's drift toward "seeming immateriality" runs parallel to Lippard's later dematerialization of the art object; distinct theses, cross-linked.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — formalism's decline is one drift of the vault's master axis: the contraction of substantial visual form as science moves into "invisible values."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] — the nature of the opposition is successional and structural: formalism makes the *object* and its examinable visual qualities the locus of meaning; phenomenalism shifts the locus to the *viewer's* act of perception, treating the form as "only the means-the vehicle-by which perceptual experiences are made possible."

## Questions It Raises

- If formalism is "the prehistory of scientific technique itself," is its decline an aesthetic verdict on sculpture or a symptom of science outgrowing visual modelling — and would an art of "invisible values" still be formalism?
- Can "irrationality" really be "sustain[ed]... within a framework of increasingly rational technique," or does the rational frame inevitably consume the intuition formalism depends on?
- When figurative form-categories (anatomy, proportion, texture) are detached from their biological referents and kept as abstract "formal qualities," do they retain meaning, or become the "weary vocabulary" of the chapter's title?
- Does the move from formalism to phenomenalism genuinely escape exhaustion, or relocate it from a depleted repertoire of forms to an open-ended burden placed on the viewer's perception?
