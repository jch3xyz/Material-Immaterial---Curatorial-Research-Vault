# The Measurement Problem

## Brief Definition

The quantum phenomenon in which a particle exists as a spread-out probability wave (the "wave function") until it is measured, at which point the wave function collapses into a single definite outcome — so that the observer appears to "conjure" the quantum world by looking at it. For O'Gieblyn it is the paradigm case in which the perceiving subject cannot be eliminated from a supposedly objective account of reality.

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 7 ("Paradox") O'Gieblyn introduces the measurement problem as the moment when the disenchanted, quantifiable world that Descartes and Galileo bequeathed to modern science turns back on itself. Empiricism had been the most durable answer to Cartesian doubt: if the senses are unreliable, then the only path to knowledge is to test, weigh, count, and measure the world independently of the mind. The measurement problem is unsettling precisely because it implies that "the physicist changes, or perhaps conjures, the quantum world simply by observing it." When unobserved, particles behave like waves "hovering in a cloud of probabilities … as though they existed everywhere at once"; only when a measurement is made does the wave function collapse and the particle appear in one location. The world, in other words, seems to require our participation — a possibility that threatens the foundational premise of materialism: that reality "behaves predictably and deterministically, independently of our minds."

O'Gieblyn treats this as structurally parallel to the hard problem of consciousness. Quantum physics, like neurobiology, is "very good at predicting the behavior of particles without knowing anything about what this behavior ultimately means about the world at its most fundamental level." Both impasses pry open the joint between subject and object, "implying that there might be some things in the world that can be perceived only from a first-person point of view." This is the crux of O'Gieblyn's interest: the measurement problem is the place where the subject re-enters physics through the front door, refusing to be reduced to one more object among objects.

The dominant "solutions," she argues, are best read as maneuvers to expel that subject and restore an objective, deterministic materialism. Hugh Everett's many-worlds theory (an offshoot of the multiverse framework) holds that the wave function never collapses at all — the observer instead "splits into multiple observers in multiple different universes," each registering a different outcome. Its appeal, O'Gieblyn notes, is that it "gets rid of the idea that the observer has any role in changing reality" and so "preserves the purely objective and deterministic materialism that once defined Newtonian physics." She voices her suspicion plainly: she "had always wondered whether these scenarios were not merely desperate attempts to get rid of the perceiving subject — a dodge that had led to many of the popular theories of consciousness and artificial intelligence as well." The same evasive move underwrites computationalist readings (Wheeler's "participatory universe," Lloyd's literal cosmic computer), which dissolve the mind-body problem by positing information as a third substance that reduces both mind and matter to code — see [[The Computational Universe]] and [[Information]].

Against these, O'Gieblyn favors the reading she attributes to Niels Bohr and the Copenhagen interpretation: not that the mind causes collapse, but that "the observer was fundamental to the quantum experiment," that the physicist "became entangled with the world," and that "we could not speak of reality without speaking of ourselves." Bohr's deeper move is epistemological: "Physics is not about how the world is … it is about what we can say about the world." Where the multiverse treats equations as transcendent descriptions of a mind-independent reality, Bohr treats the wave function as a metaphor — Kantian "spectacles" that translate the quantum world into terms a human mind can hold. The recurring presence of contradiction is, for Bohr, diagnostic rather than defective: "whenever we encountered a paradox, it was a sign that we were hitting on something true and real." Paradox becomes a method, marking the disjuncture between mind and world that the quantum realm makes unavoidable.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — reads the measurement problem as the paradigm case of the irreducible perceiving subject, and reads the multiverse, Everett's many-worlds, and computationalism as kindred "dodges" for getting rid of that subject and restoring objective deterministic materialism.
- **[[Niels Bohr]]** — the observer is fundamental and entangled with the observed; physics describes "what we can say about the world," not "how the world is." Paradox signals contact with something real (paradox-as-epistemology).
- **[[Hugh Everett]]** — many-worlds: the wave function never collapses; the observer splits across branching universes, removing both randomness and any observer role, thereby salvaging determinism.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Observer]] — Hayles's category for the figure who cannot be removed from the system; the measurement problem is O'Gieblyn's physics-grounded version of the same insistence on a situated, embodied vantage.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — Wheeler's "participatory universe" and Lloyd's literal cosmic computer are the informational reading of measurement; O'Gieblyn frames them as a sleight of hand that dissolves the subject into [[Information]].
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — many-worlds reinterprets quantum probabilities as patterns "all actualized somewhere," draining randomness from the world to preserve determinism.
- [[Cybernetics]] — O'Gieblyn notes that the quantum and Gödelian crises of foundations helped birth cybernetics as a unifying system, with information becoming "the ultimate metaphor."
- [[Mind as Machine]] — Wheeler's claim that interacting minds and universe exchange code "the same sort of mathematical exchange that might take place between two machines" extends the mind-as-machine metaphor to the cosmos.
- [[Emergence]] — both the hard problem and the measurement problem mark gaps where higher-order phenomena (experience, definite outcomes) fail to reduce cleanly to the underlying physics.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — and the broader objectivist materialism that assumes a view from nowhere; the measurement problem, on O'Gieblyn's and Bohr's reading, denies any "purely 'objective' view of the world that took into account the whole picture," reinstating the situated observer that disembodied objectivism tries to erase.

## Questions It Raises

- If the perceiving subject cannot be deleted from physics, what does this do to [[Posthumanism]]'s project of treating the body as merely contingent hardware for [[The Body as Data]] — is embodied, first-person observation itself a kind of irreducible data?
- Are the multiverse, many-worlds, and computationalist "solutions" scientific hypotheses or, as O'Gieblyn implies, metaphysical maneuvers to preserve a worldview — and how would we tell the difference?
- If [[The Computational Universe]] resolves the measurement problem only by reducing both mind and matter to [[Information]], does it explain the subject or merely abolish it by redefinition?
- Does Bohr's paradox-as-epistemology — that contradiction signals contact with the real — offer a model of meaning that survives disenchantment, or does it simply relocate mystery from theology to physics?
- If "we could not speak of reality without speaking of ourselves," how reliable is human perception as an instrument, given that our minds are "vulnerable to delusions and petty self-interest"?
