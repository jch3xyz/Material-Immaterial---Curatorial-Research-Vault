# Gesamtdatenwerk

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's keystone coinage — the "integrated data work" — naming a planet-scale, permanently open, reciprocal networked artwork whose site is not an opera house but the planet itself, its data space, its electronic noosphere, and whose indeterminate duration and constitutive interactivity abolish the distinction between artist and viewer.

## Longer Explanation

Ascott builds the term by analogy with Richard Wagner's *Gesamtkunstwerk*, the "total artwork" that fused music, image, and poetry: "We search, in short, for what I call, in German, Gesamtdatenwerk, or 'integrated data work,' echoing the Gesamtkunstwerk, or 'total artwork,' conceived by Richard Wagner" (ch. 18; `Directly stated`). The coinage names the synthesis of "image, sound, text" and "human and artificial movements, environmental dynamics, and ambient transformations all together into a more seamless whole" that artists pursue in data space. But where Wagner gathered the arts into a single staged spectacle for a seated audience, Ascott relocates and dissolves the site: "Whereas Wagner's Gesamtkunstwerk was performed in an opera house, however, the site of the Gesamtdatenwerk must be the planet as a whole, its data space, its electronic noosphere" (ch. 18; `Directly stated`).

Three properties distinguish the Gesamtdatenwerk from the bounded, finished artwork. First, **indeterminate duration**: "The duration of the work will, of course, ultimately, be indeterminate, since this must be a work in flux and flow, permitting an infinity of interactions, inputs and outputs, collaborations and conjunctions between its many participants" (ch. 18; `Directly stated`). Second, **the abolition of the artist/viewer split**: "Since reciprocity and interaction are of its essence, such work cannot differentiate between 'artist' and 'viewer,' producer and consumer. To participate in such a network is always to be involved in the creation of meaning and experience" (ch. 18; `Directly stated`). One is no longer "at the window, looking in on a scene composed by another," but "invited to enter the doorway into a world where interaction is all." Third, **immateriality of content**: meaning is not made by the artist and received by the viewer but is "the product of interaction between the observer and the system," whose content is "embodied in data that is itself immaterial, it is pure electronic *différance*, until it has been reconstituted at the interface as image, text, or sound" (ch. 20; `Directly stated`).

The Gesamtdatenwerk is therefore the unit that carries Ascott's answer to the recurring objection that art made with computers and telecommunications is technological form drained of human content. In "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" he routes the whole question of content through this concept: "The question of content must therefore be addressed to the Gesamtdatenwerk—the integrated data work— and to its capacity to engage the intellect, emotions, and sensibility of the observer" (ch. 20; `Directly stated`). The answer is that content cannot be a fixed payload, because "every aspect of networking in data space is in a state of transformation and of becoming"; content is "created rather than received" at the interface, then disposed back "into the network for storage, distribution, and eventual transformation at the interface of other users" (ch. 20). The work is thus a circulation system, not an object — "a work in flux and flow" sited in what Ascott elsewhere calls the planetary "noosphere" and "mind at large."

Ascott's editor, Edward Shanken, attributes the first appearance of the neologism to an earlier essay in the volume: a footnote in ch. 20 reads "[Ascott's neologism Gesamtdatenwerk applies this concept to telematic art. See chapter 14.—Ed.]" (`Directly stated`, editorial). Chapter 18 ("Gesamtdatenwerk: Connectivity, Transformation, and Transcendence," 1989) is the originating standalone exposition under the title; ch. 20 ("Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?," 1990) is the strong secondary anchor where the concept is made to bear the burden of the content question. If ch. 14 is ingested in a later chunk, its earlier use should be coordinated as the primary anchor.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins the term *Gesamtdatenwerk* ("integrated data work"), modeled on Wagner's *Gesamtkunstwerk*, as the name for a permanently open, planet-sited, reciprocal networked artwork of indeterminate duration in which artist and viewer are indistinguishable and content is immaterial data reconstituted at the interface. It is the unit through which he reframes the question of content in [[Telematic Art]].
- **Richard Wagner** (forward-stub author) — the *Gesamtkunstwerk*, the operatic "total artwork" fusing the arts in one staged performance, is the explicit model Ascott both echoes and overturns by replacing the opera house with "the planet as a whole."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the Gesamtdatenwerk falls decisively on the immaterial pole: its content is "data that is itself immaterial," and it abandons the art object for the interface and the data stream. It sits in opposition to the bounded material art object ("the artwork as a window onto a composed, resolved, and ordered reality").
- The finished, single-author artwork — Ascott opposes the integrated data work to "an art of finalities, of completion," to "the artist as creator and owner of images and ideas, the artist as controller of context and content." The classical sender→receiver communication model is its explicit antagonist.

## Questions It Raises

- If the work has indeterminate duration and dispersed authorship, in what sense is it ever a single *work* at all — and who, if anyone, can be said to have made it?
- Ascott routes "content" through the Gesamtdatenwerk to rebut the charge that telematic art is form without human content. Does relocating content to "the product of interaction" answer the charge, or restate it?
- Wagner's *Gesamtkunstwerk* has been read as totalizing and authoritarian; does siting the *total* work on "the planet as a whole" amplify that ambition, or does dispersed authorship defuse it? (Ascott anticipates this in ch. 20 — "uncritically euphoric… too grandiose" — and answers with "telematic politics.")
- How does the immaterial, ever-transforming content of the Gesamtdatenwerk relate to the surveillance and exposure stakes other vault authors attach to planet-scale networks?
