# The New Tendency and Field Kinetics

## Brief Definition

The c. 1955–65 European revival of kinetic art — Fontana, Klein, Vasarely, Soto, Tinguely, Group Zero, NUL, G.R.A.V., Group N/T — that turned kineticism away from machine-kinematics toward [[Material vs Immaterial|immateriality]], the active spectator, depersonalization, and the "repetitive field structure" (raster) modeled on the physicist's energy field, extendable to infinity.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Jack Burnham]], writing in *[[Beyond Modern Sculpture]]*, the New Tendency (Nove Tendencije) is the moment when Kinetic Art stops being merely "motorized sculpture — with the motor tucked away" and becomes the first concrete movement of his book's master migration from [[Object and System|object to system]]. What unites the New Tendency artists, he argues, is not a style but a shared appetite: "a common quest for the physically sensed but insubstantial. This quest embraced infinite spatial extension, immateriality, control through invisible forces, and the use in art of the temporal dimension." Klein supplies the slogan — "'Immateriality' was Yves Klein's rallying cry" — and the field, "even within the borders of the Kinetic relief," becomes "the symbol of an artistic yearning for immateriality." This is why the chapter is load-bearing for the vault's titular axis: Burnham's *immateriality* (1968) is the explicit precursor to [[Dematerialization|Lippard's dematerialization]] of the art object. (`Directly stated`)

The decisive plastic device is the **repetitive field structure**, or *raster*: "Plastically — both in static and Kinetic art — the compositional device that has overwhelmingly united New Tendency artists is the format of the repetitive field structure." Modeled (loosely) on the physicist's energy field — Faraday's lines of force, Maxwell's equations, Einstein's curved space — the raster is the discrete element "implacably regimented with identical members to its right and left, to the front and back." Its two great virtues are extensibility and concealment: "In theory the field structure could be extended infinitely; like Yves Klein's plans to make an art work of the sky, the boundless raster became a symbol of infinity," and it "dispelled the impression tha t Kinetic Art was solely tied to the activity of machine kinematics" (though "if not obviously, machines were to remain the controlling forces"). The field thus rescues kineticism from "the impasse of stagnant, deterministic mechanical motion" — from the "closed kinematic chain" of the first age of machines — by substituting a fluid, statistical, energy-field aesthetic for the clattering machine. (`Directly stated`)

The revival is also a politics of the object. The New Tendency questioned "the entire ritual of seeing which presupposes a codified viewer-object relationship," dissolving the static beholder into an *active spectator* whose own movement generates the work's perception-time, and it sought to abolish the cult of the unique work, the artist's signature, and the gallery market. The G.R.A.V.'s 1961 "General Propositions" demand, among other things, "[t]o eliminate the category 'Work of Art' and its myths," "[t]o create reproducible works," and to free art from "[t]he dependence of art on the marketplace." Burnham reads this depersonalization as a turn toward the "cool": New Tendency works "depersonalized, made 'cool,' and objectively structured 'subject matter,' so that they approached the impartiality of graphed data in a laboratory." These anti-object, anti-signature, multiples-and-reproducibility commitments are the direct precursors of [[Conceptual Art|conceptual art's]] anti-market politics and ground the vault's committed research-foci [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]. (`Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis` for the precursor links)

Burnham is careful, however, to deny that the science is a borrowed method rather than a found analogy. "As intoxicating as it is to tie New Tendency field configurations to Einsteinian mechanics, the analogy stops short of physical reality and rests upon visual correspondences"; the innovators of a style "pull them from the air, but in any case do not borrow them from scientific sources as a matter of rational decision." This is his general [[Reification|reification]] thesis — the resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a "disclosed form-world of scientific theory" — stated for kineticism. The New Tendency is therefore the keystone precursor in this vault's chain: from kinetic-field immateriality, through Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics|systems esthetics]], to the dematerialized and conceptual art that follows it. (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, ch. 6 ("Kineticism: The Unrequited Art"). The New Tendency is the first movement of "sculpture as system": a revival driven by immateriality (Klein), unified by the repetitive field structure / raster (Vasarely, Soto, Mondrian's lozenge precedent), and committed to depersonalization, the active spectator, reproducible multiples, and the refusal of the unique market object (G.R.A.V., Group Zero, NUL, Group N/T). Burnham presents it as the European turn from machine-kinematics toward field dynamics and the dimension of time itself.
- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** — *Interpretive synthesis*: Burnham's "immateriality" (1968) and the G.R.A.V.'s "eliminate the category 'Work of Art'" prefigure Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] and the anti-object, anti-market politics she tracks in [[Conceptual Art]]. The pairing is drawn by this vault; Tinguely even uses the literal word — "Dematerialization, for example, that will enable people to travel by becoming sound waves or something. Why not?"

## Questions It Raises

- If "immateriality" is the New Tendency's standard, in what sense is field kinetics *material* at all — does the raster dematerialize the object, or merely relocate matter into light, motion, and the energy-field analogy?
- Burnham insists the science is "visual correspondence," not borrowed method. Does the raster's claim to model the physicist's field therefore overstate itself — is it metaphor masquerading as physics?
- The New Tendency demanded the end of the "category 'Work of Art'" and its market, yet (per Burnham) every group fractured over recognition and the gallery system. Is depersonalized, reproducible, anti-market art structurally possible, or does it inevitably reabsorb the signature it disavows?
- How far is the New Tendency's "active spectator" the same figure as the participatory subject of later [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]?
