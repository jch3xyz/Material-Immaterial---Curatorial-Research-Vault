# The New Human Agenda

## Brief Definition

Harari's organizing frame for the twenty-first century: having demoted famine, plague and war from incomprehensible forces of nature into manageable challenges, humankind's next projects — given its past record and its current values — are immortality, happiness and divinity.

## Longer Explanation

The New Human Agenda is the opening thesis of *Homo Deus* and the home of the triad that structures the rest of the book. Harari's argument runs in two movements. First, a historical claim about the recent past: the three problems that "preoccupied the people of twentieth-century China, of medieval India and of ancient Egypt" — famine, plague and war — have, in the last few decades, been "transformed from incomprehensible and uncontrollable forces of nature into manageable challenges." We no longer pray to be rescued from them; when they break out of control we "set up a commission of inquiry" and assume "somebody must have screwed up." Harari condenses this inversion into a single statistical figure: for the first time in history, more people die from eating too much than too little, more from old age than from infectious disease, and more by suicide than are killed by soldiers, terrorists and criminals combined. *(Directly stated)*

The second movement is a prediction by extrapolation. Harari's premise is psychological: "the most common reaction of the human mind to achievement is not satisfaction, but craving for more." Because "history does not tolerate a vacuum," something must replace famine, plague and war at the top of the agenda. The successor projects are not arbitrary; each is the old project carried past its former ceiling. "Having reduced mortality from starvation, disease and violence, we will now aim to overcome old age and even death itself. Having saved people from abject misery, we will now aim to make them positively happy. And having raised humanity above the beastly level of survival struggles, we will now aim to upgrade humans into gods, and turn Homo sapiens into Homo deus." Immortality, happiness and divinity are thus the New Human Agenda. *(Directly stated)*

Harari is careful about the epistemic status of the claim, and these qualifications belong to the concept. The agenda is "what humankind as a collective will do," not what most individuals will do; it is "a historical prediction, not a political manifesto" ("Adopting these particular projects might be a big mistake"); and "reaching out is not the same as obtaining" — the prediction concerns what the twenty-first century will *try* to achieve, not what it will succeed in achieving. Most decisively, the agenda is "less of a prophecy and more a way of discussing our present choices": "If the discussion makes us choose differently, so that the prediction is proven wrong, all the better." *(Directly stated)*

The third project — divinity — is the hinge that connects this frame to the rest of the book without absorbing it. Harari notes that the third project "subsumes the first two projects, and is fuelled by them," so that "we may well think of the new human agenda as consisting really of only one project (with many branches): attaining divinity." The *content* of that upgrade — what divinity means (Greek gods and Hindu devas, not the omnipotent biblical father), and the technical routes to it (biological engineering, cyborg engineering, the engineering of non-organic beings) — is developed under [[Homo Deus]] and [[Three Paths to the Upgrade]]; this note holds only the agenda-level claim that divinity is the third target and the reason the three projects collapse into one. *(Directly stated; the routing across notes is an Interpretive synthesis of the vault structure)*

Harari grounds the whole agenda in a deeper ideology. "For 300 years the world has been dominated by humanism, which sanctifies the life, happiness and power of Homo sapiens. The attempt to gain immortality, bliss and divinity merely takes the long-standing humanist ideals to their logical conclusion." The triad of immortality/bliss/divinity maps onto humanism's triad of life/happiness/power — which is why Harari can later argue that fulfilling the humanist dream is what exposes humanism's flaws and threatens to undo it (the "gun in Act I"). *(Directly stated)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — coins and frames the New Human Agenda in the opening chapter of *Homo Deus*. The triad's three terms each have a longer source for their machinery: immortality is underwritten by "the supreme value of contemporary culture: the worth of human life" and named figures (the gerontologist Aubrey de Grey, [[Ray Kurzweil]], Google's Calico, Peter Thiel); happiness descends from [[Jeremy Bentham]]'s "greatest happiness of the greatest number" and [[Epicurus]], routed through the modern shift from the "right to the pursuit of happiness" to a "right to happiness"; divinity is the upgrade of Homo sapiens into Homo deus. Harari frames all three as the "logical conclusion" of 300 years of humanism.

## Related Concepts

- [[Homo Deus]] — the *product* of the third project (the upgraded human / Homo deus); this note holds the *agenda*, that note holds the upgraded being and the meaning of "divinity."
- [[Three Paths to the Upgrade]] — the three technical routes (biological, cyborg, non-organic) by which the divinity project would be pursued; the agenda names the target, that note names the means.
- [[Transhumanism]] — the broader contemporary movement of which Harari's immortality/divinity projects are an articulation; O'Gieblyn anchors the term in the vault.
- [[Disenchantment]] — Harari's claim that "for modern people death is a technical problem that we can and should solve" is a disenchanted reframing: death stripped of metaphysical meaning and reduced to "some technical glitch."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the agenda *extends* humanism's sanctification of human life, happiness and power, yet Harari argues that carrying humanism to its conclusion exposes its flaws and renders the human subject potentially irrelevant; the agenda is both heir to and undoing of the humanist subject. The opposition is internal/dialectical, not flat contradiction.

## Questions It Raises

- Is the move from "rein in famine, plague and war" to "therefore immortality, happiness and divinity" a genuine inference, or does the "craving for more" premise smuggle in the conclusion?
- Harari insists the prediction is meant to be self-defeating ("if the discussion makes us choose differently... all the better"). Can a concept that is explicitly a provocation against itself also function as a descriptive claim about the future?
- The agenda is "what humankind as a collective will do," reached only by elites while "billions... will continue to deal with poverty, illness and violence." Does framing immortality/bliss/divinity as *humanity's* agenda obscure that it is the agenda of "those living in palaces"?
- If the three projects collapse into "attaining divinity," does immortality or happiness retain any independent standing, or are they only stations on the way to the upgrade?
