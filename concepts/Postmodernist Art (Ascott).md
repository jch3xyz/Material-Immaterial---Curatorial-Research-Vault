# Postmodernist Art (Ascott)

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's non-stylistic definition of postmodernism: a cyclical, reversible, multilayered, ambiguous, networked art of *art and consciousness* — defined by negation against formalist modernism and announcing a transition from materialist culture toward the spiritual in art.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, "postmodern" names not a style or a period-marker but a **paradigm** — a "connective paradigm" whose focus is "not form but behaviour." Where the formalist modernist aesthetic ran "like an arrow, directed to the future," irreversible and "stylistically irreversible, always rushing for the future," postmodernist art "recognizes time and periodicity, but, rather than being tied to one-way time series, it can move back and forth in time." Ascott explicitly associates this reversibility "with the new physics" (Heisenberg's distribution of possible futures, Wiener's transmission of alternatives), and reads formalist modernism — for all its rejection of classical perspective — as having "a totally Newtonian structure," "faux modernist" by its own terms. `Directly stated`

Ascott builds the definition **negatively**: to grasp the paradigm "it is first necessary to know what formalist modernism was not. It was not connective, inclusive, transactional, associative, referential, interactive, changeable, discontinuous, multilayered, impure, ambiguous; it ignored autobiographical data and questions of personality." Postmodernist art is precisely the encompassing of those rejected qualities. This list is the working content of the concept: connectivity, inclusion, transaction, association, reference, interaction, changeability, discontinuity, multilayeredness, impurity, ambiguity, and an attention to the autobiographical and personal that formalism suppressed. `Directly stated`

Because it "presents a connective paradigm, which in turn demands a connective criticism," the concept is inseparable from [[Connective Criticism]] and from [[A Field Theory of Art]] — the field-theoretic apparatus in which "art does not reside in the artwork alone, nor in the activity of the artist alone, but is understood as a field of psychic probability, highly entropic, in which the viewer is actively involved." The artwork becomes "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer)," and final responsibility for meaning "lies with the viewer." Postmodernist art for Ascott "moves across and through different areas of knowledge, and in this networking, it can be associated with cybernetics," with "both new linguistics and the old Sufi thought," mixing "the left and right hemispheres" and employing "both reason, passion and chance." `Directly stated`

Crucially, the concept carries a **spiritual / re-enchanting** stake. Ascott frames the moment as a passage: "We are in transition from materialist culture to one in which value is put upon the full dimensions of personal, psychic, and social life. In moving towards the spiritual in art, the postmodernist artist is simply closing the loop to the early aspirations of formalist modernism before it became trapped in a preoccupation with materialism." Postmodernism thus sits on the immaterial / spiritual pole against the materialist object-culture (see [[Material vs Immaterial]], [[Re-enchantment]]), and is the curatorial-theoretical ground on which Ascott extends [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]]. Its defining temper is **inconclusiveness**: "Networking is the antithesis of reductionism, and to be inconclusive in this sense is the essence of the postmodernist condition." `Directly stated`

## Questions It Raises

- If postmodernism is defined purely by negation of formalist modernism, does the concept survive once formalism is no longer the live antagonist — or does it require that opponent to remain coherent?
- Ascott grounds reversibility and the participator-observer in "the new physics" (Heisenberg, Wiener) and Eastern spiritual models (Capra's *Tao of Physics*). How much of this is rigorous analogy and how much is the kind of metaphor-borrowing the vault flags elsewhere as `Technology as Metaphor`?
- Does locating "final responsibility for meaning" with the viewer dissolve the artwork into pure reception, or does the "matrix between two sets of behaviours" preserve a genuine artist-pole?
- How does Ascott's "from materialist culture … towards the spiritual" map onto Lippard's [[Dematerialization]] and Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]] — convergent immaterial turns, or distinct (curatorial vs. esthetic vs. consciousness-oriented) trajectories?
