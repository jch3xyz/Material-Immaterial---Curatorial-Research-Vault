# Leverage Points

## Brief Definition

Leverage points are "places in the system where a small change could lead to a large shift in behavior" — the points of power at which an intervention pays off out of all proportion to its size; Meadows's keystone claim is that these points can be ranked, that they rise in power as they grow more immaterial, and that people intuitively find them yet "more often than not push the change in the wrong direction."

## Longer Explanation

The concept is not Meadows's own coinage. She credits it to MIT's Jay Forrester, whose decades of modelling corporate and global systems produced the counterintuitive observation at the heart of the chapter: "the average manager can define the current problem very cogently, identify the system structure that leads to the problem, and guess with great accuracy where to look for leverage points" — and then, having found them, push in exactly the wrong direction (`Directly stated`). Forrester's *World* model, built for the Club of Rome, located a single leverage point behind poverty, hunger, environmental destruction and unemployment — *growth* — and found that "the world's leaders are correctly fixated on economic growth as the answer to virtually all problems, but they're pushing with all their might in the wrong direction." "Counterintuitive," Meadows writes, "that's Forrester's word to describe complex systems." The idea is old and mythic — "the silver bullet; the trimtab; the miracle cure; the secret passage; the magic password" — but the systems lesson is that we want the levers so badly we grab them backward.

Meadows offers a ranked list of twelve "places to intervene in a system," ordered from least to most powerful (numbered 12 down to 1), with the explicit warning that "it is a tentative list and its order is slithery." The deep thesis of the ordering is a movement from the material to the immaterial. The lowest rungs are physical and numerical — the sizes of flows, the sizes of buffering stocks, the plumbing of stock-and-flow structures — and they carry almost no leverage: "Numbers, the sizes of flows, are dead last on my list of powerful interventions. Diddling with the details, arranging the deck chairs on the Titanic" (`Directly stated`). The crossover is announced at rung 8: "Now we're beginning to move from the physical part of the system to the information and control parts, where more leverage can be found." The higher one climbs — through information flows, rules, self-organization, goals, and finally the paradigm out of which the whole system arises — the less physical, expensive or slow the intervention becomes and the more transformative its effect. Paradigm change, second from the top, "can happen in a millisecond … a click in the mind, a falling of scales from the eyes." This inversion — that the most powerful place to intervene is the least material one — is why the note anchors the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension in Meadows's native systems discipline (`Interpretive synthesis`).

The lower rungs of the list are captured here rather than as separate notes, because Meadows presents them precisely as the *weak* end of a spectrum whose meaning is relational — each is defined by how little leverage it holds compared to what lies above it. Parameters are "numbers" — subsidies, taxes, standards, interest rates, the minimum wage — and "probably 90—no 95, no 99 percent—of our attention goes to parameters, but there's not a lot of leverage in them"; they become leverage points only "when they go into ranges that kick off one of the items higher on this list." Buffers are "the sizes of stabilizing stocks relative to their flows" — "in chemistry and other fields, a big, stabilizing stock is known as a buffer" — and "there's leverage, sometimes magical, in changing the size of buffers," but "buffers are usually physical entities, not easy to change." Stock-and-flow structures — "the plumbing structure, the stocks and flows and their physical arrangement" — "can have an enormous effect on how the system operates," yet "physical rebuilding is the slowest and most expensive kind of change to make in a system," so "the leverage point is in proper design in the first place."

## The Ranked List (12 → 1)

Meadows's twelve places to intervene, from lowest to highest leverage (the list "is tentative and its order is slithery"; higher points hold more power but "the more the system will resist changing it"):

- **12. Numbers** — constants and parameters such as subsidies, taxes, standards. Dead last; "diddling with the details."
- **11. Buffers** — the sizes of stabilizing stocks relative to their flows. Magical when changeable, but usually physical and fixed.
- **10. Stock-and-Flow Structures** — the physical plumbing and its nodes of intersection. Powerful but rarely quick or cheap to rebuild; the leverage is in the original design (see [[Stocks and Flows]]).
- **9. [[Delays]]** — the lengths of time relative to rates of change. Critical to oscillation, but "things take as long as they take."
- **8. Balancing Feedback Loops** — the strength of the feedbacks relative to the impacts they correct. The threshold where intervention crosses "from the physical part of the system to the information and control parts" (see [[Balancing Feedback Loop]]).
- **7. Reinforcing Feedback Loops** — the strength of the gain of driving loops; slowing the gain "is usually a more" powerful move than speeding a balancing loop (see [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]]).
- **6. Information Flows** — the structure of who does and does not have access to information; "adding or restoring information can be a powerful intervention, usually much easier and cheaper than rebuilding physical infrastructure."
- **5. Rules** — incentives, punishments, constraints. "Power over the rules is real power."
- **4. [[Self-Organization]]** — the power to add, change, or evolve system structure; "the strongest form of system resilience."
- **3. Goals** — the purpose or function of the system; superior even to self-organization, because a system's goal bends everything below it into conformity.
- **2. Paradigms** — "the mind-set out of which the system … arises." Hardest to change in a society, yet in an individual it "can happen in a millisecond."
- **1. Transcending Paradigms** — to hold oneself "unattached in the arena of paradigms," to realize that no paradigm is "true." The highest and least material point of all; "there are no cheap tickets to mastery."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — the concept as it appears in *Thinking in Systems*: a rankable spectrum of intervention points, from material parameters to the immaterial transcendence of paradigms, credited to Jay Forrester and framed by his finding that people locate leverage points intuitively but push them backward.
- **Jay Forrester** (via Meadows) — the originator of the term in systems-dynamics practice; his *World* model and urban-dynamics studies supplied the canonical examples (growth as the master leverage point; subsidized low-income housing as a lever pushed the wrong way). Forrester has no book in the vault; his usage is reported here through Meadows.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — not an opponent so much as the tension the ranked list dramatizes: leverage *rises* as the intervention point becomes more immaterial, so the material end (numbers, buffers, physical structures) is precisely the low-power pole.

## Questions It Raises

- If the highest-leverage points are the least material — paradigms and their transcendence — why does "90—no 95, no 99 percent" of collective attention go to the lowest, the parameters?
- Forrester found that people locate leverage points intuitively yet push them in the wrong direction. Is the wrong-direction reflex cognitive, or is it that "the higher the leverage point, the more the system will resist changing it"?
- Does the movement from material to immaterial leverage in Meadows converge with, or merely echo, the vault's other material→immaterial spines (dematerialization in art, disembodiment of information), and where does it diverge?
- If a leverage point can be a paradigm changed "in a millisecond," what is the systems status of an idea — is it inside the system, or the place from which the system is written?
