# Women of Color as a Cyborg Identity

## Brief Definition

Haraway's exemplary cyborg identity: "women of color" as a self-consciously constructed, coalitional political identity built not on a natural or essential ground but "by conscious appropriation of negation" — a potent subjectivity fused from outsider identities rather than born of original wholeness.

## Longer Explanation

In "Fractured Identities" (ch. 02), Haraway introduces "women of color" through Chela Sandoval's theory of "oppositional consciousness," "born of the skills for reading webs of power by those refused stable membership in the social categories of race, sex, or class." The identity is not given; it is achieved. Sandoval, Haraway reports, "emphasizes the lack of any essential criterion for identifying who is a woman of color" and notes that "the definition of the group has been by conscious appropriation of negation" — a Chicana or U.S. black woman, "at the bottom of a cascade of negative identities," could not speak as a woman, as a black person, or as a Chicano, and so the affinity is wrested from that exclusion. This identity therefore "constructs a kind of postmodernist identity out of otherness, difference, and specificity," and "this postmodernist identity is fully political" (`Directly stated`). Crucially it stands as Haraway's model of unity-through-affinity rather than unity-through-domination: "coalition—affinity, not identity," "a self-consciously constructed space that cannot affirm the capacity to act on the basis of natural identification, but only on the basis of conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship."

In "Cyborgs: A Myth of Political Identity" (ch. 06), Haraway names this construction explicitly as a cyborg identity: "Earlier I suggested that 'women of color' might be understood as a cyborg identity, a potent subjectivity synthesized from fusions of 'outsider' identities, sedimented in the complex political-historical layerings of Audre Lorde's 'biomythography,' Zami." The cyborg here is not chrome and metal but a figure of self construction without origin — a being "not of Woman born," whose unity is welded from fragments rather than inherited from a natural matrix (`Directly stated`). This makes the concept a specific, lived instance of [[The Cyborg]]: the affinity-identity of ch. 02 is retroactively revealed to have been a cyborg all along.

Haraway folds in literary figures as evidence of the construction. Audre Lorde's *Sister Outsider* furnishes the central image — "Sister Outsider is the offshore woman," whose survival comes "not because of her innocence but because of her ability to live on the boundaries, to write without the founding myth of original wholeness." The Chicana retellings of Malinche, "mother of the mestizo 'bastard' race of the new world, master of languages, and mistress of Cortes," and Cherríe Moraga's *Loving in the War Years* supply the same logic of identity without an original language or a fall from innocence: Moraga's writing "is self-consciously spliced, a chimera of English and Spanish, both conquerors' languages," and "it is this chimeric monster, without claim to an original language before violation, that crafts the erotic, competent, potent identities of women of color." For Haraway, "writing is preeminently the technology of cyborgs," so the literacy of women of color — "seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other" — is itself the cyborg act (`Strongly implied` for the synthesis of Lorde/Malinche/Moraga as a single evidentiary thread).

The concept thus does double work in the *Manifesto*: it grounds Haraway's polemic against essentialist feminisms (Marxist/socialist-feminism's ontology of labor, MacKinnon's radical feminism's "essential" nonexistence of women) by showing a real, effective coalition that refuses any "natural matrix of unity," and it provides her preferred political form — partial, ironic, "without claim to an original language before violation" — against the "logic of appropriation, incorporation, and taxonomic identification."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — coins the framing across two registers: in ch. 02 "women of color" is an affinity-identity theorized via Chela Sandoval's "oppositional consciousness," constructed "by conscious appropriation of negation"; in ch. 06 she explicitly calls it "a cyborg identity, a potent subjectivity synthesized from fusions of 'outsider' identities," evidenced through Lorde's *Zami* / *Sister Outsider*, Malinche, and Moraga.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — "women of color" is Haraway's concrete, political instance of the cyborg figure: a hybrid subjectivity assembled without an origin myth, "not of Woman born."
- [[Donna Haraway]] — the author whose *A Cyborg Manifesto* originates the concept.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the cyborg identity is fused from sign, story, and writing ("writing is preeminently the technology of cyborgs") yet is materially located in the bodies of "real-life cyborgs" and in the labor markets of "science-based industries," so it holds the material/immaterial poles in productive tension rather than resolving either way.

## Questions It Raises

- If a coalitional identity is achieved "by conscious appropriation of negation," what protects it from collapsing back into the essentialism Haraway critiques — or into "boundless difference" with no ground for "partial, real connection"?
- Can a positively valued cyborg identity be separated from the conditions that produce it — the "homework economy," the recruitment of "young Korean women" into "the integrated circuit," the "informatics of domination" — without aestheticizing exploitation?
- Does naming "women of color" a *cyborg* identity empower the coalition (survival through "seizing the tools") or risk dissolving lived racial and colonial specificity into a general theory of hybridity?
