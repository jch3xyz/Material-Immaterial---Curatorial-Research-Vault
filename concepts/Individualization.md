# Individualization

## Brief Definition

The centuries-long process of modernization that loosened each life from inherited fate and made it "an open-ended reality to be discovered rather than a certainty to be enacted" — the historical urge toward psychological self-determination that surveillance capitalism's neoliberal habitat would collide with and try to reverse.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], *individualization* is one of two "opposing historical forces" whose "destructive collision" makes surveillance capitalism possible. It belongs to "the longer history of modernization and the centuries-long societal shift from the mass to the individual"; the opposing vector is the neoliberal economic paradigm, "its aim to reverse, subdue, impede, and even destroy the individual urge toward psychological self-determination and moral agency" (`Directly stated`, Ch.2). Individualization is thus a *vector*, not a value or an ideology — "a consequence of long-term processes of modernization," "the human signature of the modern era" (`Directly stated`, Ch.2).

Zuboff is emphatic that the term must not be confused with two near-homonyms. It is not neoliberal *individualism* — "the neoliberal ideology of 'individualism' that shifts all responsibility for success or failure to a mythical, atomized, isolated individual, doomed to a life of perpetual competition and disconnected from relationships, community, and society." Nor is it psychological *individuation*, "the psychological process of 'individuation' that is associated with the lifelong exploration of self-development" (`Directly stated`, Ch.2). The disambiguation is load-bearing: the atomized competitor of individualism is precisely what the neoliberal vector *manufactures* against individualization, and the note carries its own definition page `[[Individualization (Zuboff)]]` to hold this distinction.

The phenomenology Zuboff gives the process is almost mythic. "Until the last few minutes of human history, each life was foretold in blood and geography, sex and kin, rank and religion. I am my mother's daughter. I am my father's son. The sense of the human being as an individual emerged gradually over centuries, clawed from this ancient vise" (`Directly stated`, Ch.2). The "first modernity" began roughly two hundred years ago when "life became 'individualized' for great numbers of people as they separated from traditional norms, meanings, and rules" — the moment "each life became an open-ended reality to be discovered rather than a certainty to be enacted" (`Directly stated`, Ch.2). Since the mid-twentieth century the process took "a new turn toward a 'second modernity'," producing "a new society of people born to a sense of psychological individuality, with its double-edged birthright of liberation and necessity" — "the right and the requirement to choose our own lives" (`Directly stated`, Ch.2).

This achievement is also a wound. "In spite of its liberating potential, the second modernity was slated to become a hard place to live," its instability "the result of institutionalized shifts in economic and social policies and practices associated with the neoliberal paradigm and its rise to dominance" — a paradigm "aimed at containing, rechanneling, and reversing the secular wave of second-modernity claims to self-determination" (`Directly stated`, Ch.2). Individualization names what surveillance capitalism feeds on and frustrates at once: it "has sent each one of us on the prowl for the resources we need to ensure effective life," even as "neoliberal ideology and policy" bend "second-modernity individuals to the draconian quid pro quo at the heart of surveillance capitalism's logic of accumulation," ransoming "information and connection" for [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral data]] (`Directly stated`, Ch.2). It is therefore the historical precondition for the harms Zuboff catalogues later — the assault on [[The Right to the Future Tense]] and the rise of [[Instrumentarian Power]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — the canonical use. Individualization is the modernization vector "from the mass to the individual," distinct from neoliberal *individualism* (the atomized perpetual competitor) and from psychological *individuation* (lifelong self-development). Staged across a "first" and "second" modernity, it supplies both the emancipatory hunger that summoned Google and Facebook into our lives and the vulnerability that surveillance capitalism exploits.

> Adjacent in the vault, not Zuboff's coinage: [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s neoliberal subject of the [[Achievement Society]] and [[Auto-Exploitation]] describes the *individualism* pole Zuboff warns against — the self made wholly responsible for its own success or failure — rather than the modernization vector itself. The two are cross-referenced as a `Speculative connection`.

## Questions It Raises

- If individualization is a centuries-old vector and neoliberalism a decades-old one, is the "collision" Zuboff describes a contingent historical accident or a structural inevitability of late modernity?
- Can the "right and the requirement to choose our own lives" survive an economy that profits by predicting and modifying those very choices?
- Does surveillance capitalism reverse individualization, or complete its degradation into individualism — turning the self-determining individual back into "anonymous members of the mass"?
- Zuboff invokes Bauman's hope for a "third modernity that transcends the collision"; what would individualization look like if its emancipatory promise were not ransomed for behavioral data?
