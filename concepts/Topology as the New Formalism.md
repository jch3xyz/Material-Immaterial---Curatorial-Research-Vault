# Topology as the New Formalism

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's name for the migration of sculptural form away from metric, proportional, Euclidean geometry toward the innate, position- and magnitude-indifferent topological properties of objects in space — insideness/outsideness, connectivity, complex symmetry, ambiguity — which "became the basis of new sculptural formalism."

## Longer Explanation

In *Beyond Modern Sculpture*'s chapter on formalism, Burnham traces a "progressive tendency" by which a few sculptors "expropriated three-dimensional forms from areas of geometry which grew less sensual and metric." The terminus of that tendency is topology. Burnham defines it tightly: "Topology is the study of the innate properties of objects in space. These properties remain unaffected by changes in magnitude, position, or proportion and are only altered by the tearing or joining of an object's parts." The decisive move is that the sculptor stops caring about the classical formal variables — shape, proportion, scale — and attends instead to what survives all deformation. Recalling "Spengler's characterization of modern mathematics," Burnham writes that "many nonobjective sculptors, including Gabo, became less concerned with proportional metric relationships and more interested in the universal properties of planes, edges, penetrations, and spaces. This, then, became the basis of new sculptural formalism." [`Directly stated`]

The concept is anchored in two artists. Naum Gabo's *Spheric Theme* series (begun 1937) is "a symbol of space folding back upon itself as an infinite loop," a surface "impossible to fabricate from a single flat sheet of plastic"; topologically it is a circular disk "stretched or deformed so as to actually contain two flat disks cut open and attached edge to edge." Burnham names the four topological properties that, "while they were intriguing properties for the topologist to ponder from the point of view of mathematical logic," became "the most important plastic considerations for Gabo and other formalist sculptors": insideness and outsideness, connectivity, complex symmetry, and ambiguity. Max Bill carries the same logic into his Möbius variations, where "topological properties are the most innate and unchanging to be found in three-dimensional objects." Of the *Endless Ribbon*, *Monoangulated Surface in Space*, *Continuous Surface in the Form of a Column*, and *Transition*, Burnham observes: "Fashioned in different materials, placed in different positions, and designed to exploit different plastic properties of the same configuration, topologically these four sculptures are the same piece." [`Directly stated`] This is the crux of the new formalism — that material identity dissolves into a structural invariant; the four objects are "the same piece" because what is sculpturally essential is no longer the matter, the size, or the placement but the unchanging topology.

For Burnham this is not a local stylistic curiosity but the leading edge of a long dematerializing drift. He frames the whole chapter as the exhaustion of the classical formal vocabulary ("shape, proportion, scale, structure, texture, color, context"), and reads topology as the endpoint of sculpture's pursuit of "the 'truer' physicist's reality" through scientific models — the same impulse he elsewhere calls [[Scientific Idealism]] and [[The Scientific Demiurge]]. Topology is the geometry that has finally severed form from the visual: it "denies appearances," concerning itself less with "the local proportional properties of objects and their magnitudes" and more with "the general morphology of mathematical operation." [`Strongly implied`]

The endgame Burnham forecasts is set-theory **Object sculpture**, which "completely realizes Spengler's analysis of modern geometry — 'the liberation of geometry from the visual.'" Donald Judd's wall reliefs employ "set theory in positioning the modular units," where "sets, defined as numerical values, have nothing to do with dimension or finite proportion" but "transcend the intuitive-concrete to become extensions of pure process and transformation." Here "Sculpture becomes 'thingified' by means which cannot be perceived except through the rules for ordering finite or infinite sets of abstract points in a given continuum." Burnham closes with Spengler's prophecy that geometry "dissolved all concrete forms," replacing "the mathematical bodies from which the rigid geometrical values had been obtained, by abstract spatial relations which in the end ceased to have any application at all to sense-present phenomena." [`Directly stated`] The trajectory thus runs from topology's deformation-proof invariants to the fully disembodied, set-theoretic Object — the immaterial limit of sculptural form. [`Interpretive synthesis`]

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — Topology is the geometry in which the innate, unchanging properties of an object survive any deformation short of tearing or joining; once sculptors privilege those invariants (insideness/outsideness, connectivity, complex symmetry, ambiguity) over metric proportion, topology "became the basis of new sculptural formalism" (Gabo's *Spheric Theme*, Bill's Möbius variations). Its endgame — Judd's set-theory Object — realizes Spengler's "liberation of geometry from the visual."

## Related Concepts

- [[Scientific Idealism]] — topology is the geometry through which formalist sculptors pursue the non-material, model-derived "truer" reality of science; the new formalism is Scientific Idealism's most rigorous geometric instrument.
- [[The Scientific Demiurge]] — the analytic, "underlying truths" impulse Burnham diagnoses behind modern formalism; topology is where that drive carries sculpture furthest from sense-present form.
- [[Object Art]] — the set-theory, Pythagorean-solid Object sculpture (Judd, Morris) is the endgame of topology-as-formalism: form handled as "manipulatable n values" rather than measured magnitude.
- [[Object and System]] — Burnham's larger arc from the discrete sculptural object toward systems and process; the set-theoretic Object marks where the object becomes "extensions of pure process and transformation."
- [[Dematerialization]] — the steady "dematerialization of sculpture from 1910 to the 1950's" that topology's deformation-proof, magnitude-indifferent invariants drive toward an immaterial limit.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Reification]] — topology evacuates the sculpture's material thinghood in favor of an abstract structural invariant ("the same piece" across four different objects), the inverse of treating the concrete object as the locus of value.

## Questions It Raises

- If four physically distinct objects are "topologically the same piece," where does the sculpture actually reside — in the matter, in the configuration, or in the mathematical invariant?
- Does "the liberation of geometry from the visual" liberate sculpture or abolish it, given that Burnham frames the set-theoretic Object as the point where geometry "ceased to have any application at all to sense-present phenomena"?
- Is topology a genuine formal vocabulary, or — as Burnham implies of the "used up" mathematical model — a borrowed scientific resource destined to suffer "conceptual invisibility" once its visual novelty is exhausted?
