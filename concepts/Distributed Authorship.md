# Distributed Authorship

## Brief Definition

Distributed authorship is Roy Ascott's term — coined in 1983 for his telematic project *La Plissure du Texte* — for the dissolution of the single, solitary author into a field of many networked participators who collectively "pleat" a text or work, so that creativity becomes a property of the system of interaction itself rather than of any individual.

## Longer Explanation

Ascott coins the term to name the form of creativity made possible by [[Reciprocal Communication]] across computer networks: "It is from telematics in art that we have the concept of 'distributed authorship.' I coined this term in 1983 to describe the process of creativity involved in my project for Frank Popper's exhibition 'Electra'" (ch.23). The demonstration was *La Plissure du Texte* ("The Pleating of the Text"), a "planetary fairy tale" in which collaborators at eleven locations on four continents each took a fairy-tale character and contributed to a single, collectively woven story — authorship dispersed across the nodes rather than held in one hand (`Directly stated`, ch.23; framing in ch.04).

The concept is, for Ascott, frankly subversive of the inherited apparatus of culture. In Shanken's editorial framing of telematic art, it "subverts the idea of authorship bound up within the solitary individual" and "subverts the idea of individual ownership of the works of imagination" (ch.04) — and by extension the bricks-and-mortar institutions (the gallery, the museum, the book) built to house single-author objects, since telematic networks are "ubiquitous and can be accessed from virtually any location" (ch.17), at once everywhere and nowhere. In place of the Renaissance paradigm of the artist standing apart and depicting the world, "the new composite role becomes that simply of participant in a system creating meaning seen as art" (ch.17): the producer/consumer, artist/viewer distinction is rendered obsolete (`Directly stated`, ch.17).

Ascott uses two near-synonymous terms — "distributed authorship" and "dispersed authorship" — interchangeably across the essays. "Through telematic networks, intelligence is amplified, authorship is dispersed, and memory becomes time-free and global in its dimensions" (ch.19); "Authorship is distributed, meaning is negotiated" (ch.15). The crucial move is that the locus of authorship shifts to the interaction: "If there is an author of this 'meaning,' then it may be the system of interaction itself, in all its particulars, that should be described as the author, or we might want to refer to a 'dispersed authorship,' covering all those involved in negotiating for meaning in a given context" (ch.17) (`Directly stated`). The author becomes "a complex and widely distributed system, in which both human and artificial cognition and perception play their part" (ch.23) — a formulation that pulls the concept toward [[Distributed Cognition]].

Ascott explicitly threads his coinage through Roland Barthes's "The Death of the Author." Telematic process "celebrates the death of the 'individual,' 'original' author of images or texts" and "brings into being, instead, a new 'dispersed authorship' generating massive variety within the virtual 'immateriality' of the networks" — embodying and amplifying, in Ascott's words, "Barthes's earlier insight" that the text is "not a line of words releasing a single 'theological' meaning (the 'message' of an 'Author God') but a multidimensional space" and "a tissue of quotations" (ch.16) (`Directly stated`). The phrase "the virtual 'immateriality' of the networks" anchors distributed authorship to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] spine: the dispersal of the author is also a dematerialization of the work into the network.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the coiner (1983, for *La Plissure du Texte*). Uses "distributed authorship" and "dispersed authorship" interchangeably; locates the author in the system of interaction; frames it as the death of the solitary author/owner and the obsolescence of the producer/consumer distinction; ties it to Barthes, to the "immateriality" of networks, and to an "emerging new order of art... of contingency and uncertainty" (ch.20).
- **Lawrence Alloway / Edward Shanken (editorial framing, ch.04)** — Shanken, glossing the project, articulates the subversive stakes: authorship no longer "bound up within the solitary individual," individual ownership of works of imagination undone, the result a "poetics of disembodied collaboration and weightless network rambling" (Bull, quoted in ch.04).
- **Roland Barthes (precursor, named by Ascott)** — "The Death of the Author": the text as a multidimensional tissue of quotations with no single originating "Author God." Ascott presents distributed authorship as the telematic realization and amplification of Barthes's literary insight. (No Barthes note in the vault; plain-text reference.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — distributed authorship is the authorial consequence of two-way, interactive (rather than broadcast) network exchange; the participators co-author because they communicate reciprocally.
- [[Decentralization]] — the structural correlate: no center, no single origin, authorship spread across nodes "at once everywhere and nowhere."
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — the author as "a complex and widely distributed system, in which both human and artificial cognition... play their part" extends authorship into the same cognitive-systems frame.
- [[New Media Art]] — distributed authorship is one of the defining moves of the telematic strand of new media art that Ascott inaugurates.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the work exists as a participatory, system-level experience rather than a finished object, the precondition for authorship to be shared.
- [[Conceptual Art]] — a precursor in dematerializing the art object and privileging the idea/process over the unique handmade thing; Ascott's networked version disperses not just the object but the author.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-possessing individual who owns his thoughts and works; distributed authorship dissolves exactly this figure as the seat and proprietor of creativity.

## Questions It Raises

- If "the system of interaction itself" is the author, who is responsible for the work — and who, if anyone, can own it? Does distributed authorship abolish authorship or merely relocate it?
- Ascott calls the resulting canon "one of contingency and uncertainty." What becomes of evaluation, attribution, and the archive when the work has no fixed final state and no single maker?
- Is Ascott's celebratory "death of the author" continuous with Barthes's literary thesis, or does the telematic, technical apparatus change its political stakes (cf. the surveillance/decentralization ambivalence elsewhere in the vault)?
- How does the "immateriality of the networks" that enables dispersed authorship relate to the dematerialization of the art object that [[Conceptual Art]] had already pursued by other means?
