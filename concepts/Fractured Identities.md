# Fractured Identities

## Brief Definition

The condition in which identity (gender, race, class, even "woman") is contradictory, partial, strategic, and socially and historically constituted, so that no category can ground an "essential" unity — the situation the cyborg figures and that affinity-politics answers.

## Longer Explanation

In the second section of *A Cyborg Manifesto*, Donna Haraway names a "crisis in political identity": the difficulty of naming "one's feminism by a single adjective—or even to insist in every circumstance on the noun." Once the social and historical constitution of categories is recognized, gender, race, and class "cannot provide the basis for belief in 'essential' unity." Identities, on this account, "seem contradictory, partial, and strategic" — not natural givens but achievements forced on us by "the terrible historical experience of the contradictory social realities of patriarchy, colonialism, and capitalism." `Directly stated`

Crucially, Haraway radicalizes the claim past social construction into ontology: there is "nothing about being 'female' that naturally binds women," and "not even such a state as 'being' female," since "female" is "itself a highly complex category constructed in contested sexual scientific discourses and other social practices." The fracture therefore runs all the way down — it is not that a stable subject wears shifting masks, but that there is no prior natural matrix beneath them. This is why Haraway insists that "no construction is whole" and that cyborg feminists "do not want any more natural matrix of unity." `Directly stated`

Haraway reads this fragmentation as historically specific and tied to a larger collapse. "It is no accident that the symbolic system of the family of man—and so the essence of woman—breaks up at the same moment that networks of connection among people on the planet are unprecedentedly multiple, pregnant, and complex." In her terms, "In the 'Western' sense, the end of man is at stake. It is no accident that woman disintegrates into women in our time." The dissolution of the universal "Man" (the liberal-humanist self, "the one who is not animal, barbarian, or woman") and the dispersal of "woman" into "women" are the same event seen from two angles. `Directly stated`

This is a diagnosis, not a despair. Haraway warns that the recognition of fracture risks "lapsing into boundless difference and giving up on the confusing task of making partial, real connection." Her response is not a new essential unity but coalition: "affinity, not identity." Fractured identities is thus the problem to which `[[Affinity Not Identity]]` is the proposed answer, and the condition the `[[The Cyborg|cyborg]]` figures as an ironic political identity built "out of otherness, difference, and specificity" rather than out of a natural ground. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — Identities are "contradictory, partial, and strategic," socially and historically constituted, incapable of grounding essential unity; "woman disintegrates into women" as the universal "Man" of the West reaches "the end." She draws on Chela Sandoval's "oppositional consciousness" and Katie King's critique of feminist taxonomies, and reads both Marxist/socialist-feminism and MacKinnon's radical feminism as failed attempts to reconstitute a totalizing unified subject.

## Related Concepts

- [[Affinity Not Identity]] — the proposed political response to this diagnosis; affinity replaces a now-impossible identity-grounded unity. Distinct from Fractured Identities, which names the problem rather than the answer.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the figure that does not need an origin story or a natural matrix; it embodies and answers fractured identity.
- [[Posthumanism]] — Haraway's "end of man" is one route into the broader posthuman dissolution of the universal humanist subject.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the bounded, self-possessing, naturally unified "Man" whose dissolution *is* the fracturing; fractured identities is the negation of that subject's claim to an essential, organic ground.

## Questions It Raises

- If no category names a natural unity, what holds a political "us" together — and who is authorized to say "us"? Haraway asks directly: "who counts as 'us' in my own rhetoric?"
- Does the move from identity to "partial, contradictory, permanently unclosed constructions of personal and collective selves" disable politics, or only the *kind* of politics that demands a unified revolutionary subject?
- Is "the end of man" (the fracture of the Western humanist self) a loss to be mourned or, as Haraway suggests, an opening for "weaving something other than a shroud"?
- How does Haraway's distinction between differences that are "playful" and differences that are "poles of world historical systems of domination" survive the refusal of any stable identity?
