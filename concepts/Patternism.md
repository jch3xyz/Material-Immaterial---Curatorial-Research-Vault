# Patternism

## Brief Definition

Patternism is Ray Kurzweil's self-applied label for the functionalist doctrine that the self is not its physical substrate but a persisting pattern of information — "the distinctive algorithm that we think of as our identity" — which, being computational, is substrate-independent and therefore in principle transferable, or uploadable, to another medium.

## General Orientation

This note holds two distinct registers, which must not be collapsed.

- **Patternism in Kurzweil's own voice (primary source).** [[Ray Kurzweil]] coins and owns the term. In *The Singularity Is Near* he declares it a first-person identity ("I describe myself as a 'patternist'") and a metaphysics ("someone who views patterns of information as the fundamental reality"). On this view the self is the persisting pattern, the changing matter is incidental, and consciousness is therefore portable to a "more suitable computational substrate" by gradual transfer. This is the celebratory, realized-primary-source pole, set out in the section below.
- **Patternism as O'Gieblyn reads it (critical commentary).** [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] is neither coiner nor advocate. In *God, Human, Animal, Machine* she narrates patternism's pull while genealogically relocating it in Christian eschatology — [[Origen]]'s *eidos*, "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter" — and stages the opposing pole through Susan Schneider's zombie objection and [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s critique of "disembodied information [as] the ultimate Platonic Form." Her account, formerly the whole of this note, is preserved in full below as the critical and historicizing frame.

Both registers share the same word "pattern" but point in opposite directions on the question of whether the pattern can float free of its substrate. Keep this concept note distinct from `definitions/Patternism (O'Gieblyn)` (the dedicated definition file) and from [[Pattern and Randomness]] (Hayles's critical dialectic, which this note does not subsume).

## Patternism in Kurzweil's Own Voice (primary source)

Patternism is, in Kurzweil's own framing, both a self-description and an ontology. He introduces it in the first person and grounds it in the body's material flux: "In chapter 7, I describe myself as a 'patternist,' someone who views patterns of information as the fundamental reality. For example, the particles composing my brain and body change within weeks, but there is a continuity to the patterns that these particles make" (`Directly stated`). The argument O'Gieblyn paraphrases secondhand is here in the source's own words: identity is the persisting pattern, not the perpetually replaced matter that momentarily instantiates it. This is the metaphysical premise on which the rest of *The Singularity Is Near* — mind-uploading, [[The Body as Data|the body read as data]], the merger of biological and nonbiological intelligence — depends.

From this premise Kurzweil derives **substrate-independence** as an engineering conclusion rather than a metaphor. Because, on his account, "most of the complexity of a human neuron is devoted to maintaining its life-support functions, not its information-processing capabilities," the information-processing pattern can be detached from its biological hardware: "Ultimately, we will be able to port our mental processes to a more suitable computational substrate. Then our minds won't have to stay so small" (`Directly stated`). The body is recast as inefficient life-support overhead; the self is the portable pattern. Where [[Embodiment]] and [[The Materiality of Informatics]] insist information is never substrate-independent, Kurzweil treats the biological substrate as a contingent and improvable platform — the celebratory inverse of the critique O'Gieblyn imports from Hayles.

The continuity-of-identity problem — the worry that an uploaded self is only a copy — Kurzweil answers with a **gradual-transfer thesis** that dissolves the seam between original and copy: "So we will have effectively uploaded ourselves, albeit gradually, never quite noticing the transfer. There will be no 'old Ray' and 'new Ray,' just an increasingly capable Ray" (`Directly stated`). Rather than a sudden scan-and-replace, the nonbiological portion of intelligence accretes (neural implants, then nanobots augmenting sensory processing, memory, skill, pattern recognition) until it predominates, so that uploading is experienced as continuous self-extension rather than death-and-copy. His empirical warrant for the pattern's primacy is the compressibility of biological design: "There are only eight hundred million bytes of information in the entire human genome, and only about thirty to one hundred million bytes after data compression is applied. This is about one hundred million times less information than is represented by all of the interneuronal connections and neurotransmitter concentration patterns in a fully formed human brain" (`Directly stated`). The genome-as-compressed-design figure is offered as evidence that what matters is the comparatively tiny generative *pattern*, not the vast material structure it unfolds into — the same transience-to-permanence inversion O'Gieblyn diagnoses, asserted here from the inside as a reason for optimism. (`Interpretive synthesis` for the connective claim that these four passages together constitute a single substrate-independence argument; each quoted clause is `Directly stated`.)

## Longer Explanation

In O'Gieblyn's account, patternism is the secular afterlife. "Transhumanists do not believe in the soul, but they subscribe to a concept that is not so dissimilar. Kurzweil calls himself a 'patternist.'" The pattern occupies the structural position the soul once held: an essential, irreducible identity that survives the constant turnover of the body's matter. Kurzweil locates consciousness not in "the hardware of our brains — the cells and atoms and neurons, which are always changing — but in the computational patterns…which together form the distinctive algorithm that we think of as our identity." This is, as O'Gieblyn notes, "essentially a functionalist account of the mind" — the view that consciousness resides in organization and causal relations rather than physical material — dressed in Kurzweil's preferred organic metaphor of water making a pattern in a stream while its molecules change every millisecond.

The crucial move is from transience to permanence. O'Gieblyn traces the river/whirlpool image backward: Kurzweil's metaphor "was not original," descending from [[Norbert Wiener]], "the grandfather of cybernetics," who wrote that "we are but whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water. We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves," and behind Wiener to [[Heraclitus]] and the impossibility of stepping into the same river twice. But where Wiener and Heraclitus invoked the fluid pattern to emphasize the *transience* of identity, "for Kurzweil, patternism was precisely what made possible the most decisive form of permanence: immortality." The inversion turns on a single inference: "A pattern, after all, is essentially computational, which means it can, at least in theory, be transferred onto a computer." Substrate-independence — the claim that "consciousness can be transferred onto all sorts of different substrates: our new bodies might be supercomputers, robotic surrogates, or human clones" — is what licenses mind-uploading, [[The Body as Data|the body read as information]], and the dream of "the mind as pure information, pure spirit."

O'Gieblyn's capstone is to historicize patternism not in the Enlightenment lineage transhumanism claims for itself but in Christian eschatology. Her chapter closes on [[Origen]] of Alexandria, the third-century theologian who, facing the same continuity-of-identity problem ("Which 'person' would God resurrect, the eight-year-old child or the eighty-year-old man?"), argued that "the material substratum is never the same" and that what survives resurrection is the *eidos* — "a kind of Platonic form or plan," which Caroline Walker Bynum glosses as "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter." O'Gieblyn's verdict: this Origenist eidos "resembles more than anything…Kurzweil's understanding of consciousness as a pattern of information." Patternism is thus the resurrection of the dead recoded as a copy-and-transfer operation — "the transcendent — and essentially religious — hopes that science itself obliterated," restored through science.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — coins and owns the term; consciousness is the computational pattern that constitutes identity, persisting independent of changing biological hardware and therefore transferable to a new substrate (immortality via mind-uploading). The celebratory pole.
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** — the cybernetic ancestor of the metaphor ("patterns that perpetuate themselves"), but invoked to stress the *transience*, not the permanence, of identity. Source of the [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] lineage patternism inherits.
- **[[Heraclitus]]** — the pre-Socratic root of the river image (one cannot step into the same river twice), emphasizing flux and the impermanence of the self.
- **[[Origen]]** — O'Gieblyn's theological double for Kurzweil: the resurrected self is not its flesh but its *eidos*, "a pattern that organizes the flux of matter." The third-century precedent that exposes patternism's eschatological structure.
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — neither coiner nor advocate; she narrates patternism's pull (it "satisfied a much deeper, existential longing") while genealogically exposing it as recycled Christian resurrection theology.

## Related Concepts

Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s dialectic frames pattern as a contingent, historically constructed dispositive, whereas patternism celebrates the pattern as the vehicle of immortality. Cross-linked deliberately; **do not collapse the two**.
- [[The Body as Data]] — patternism is the metaphysics that licenses reading the body as transferable information.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — Kurzweil's cosmology (information organizing into intelligence since the Big Bang) is the universal frame in which patternism sits.
- [[Posthumanism]] — Kurzweil's "posthumans, or spiritual machines" are the beings patternism promises to produce.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — patternism is a strong form of the functionalist claim that mind is realizable as computation.
- [[Cybernetics]] — supplies the universal "information processing" metaphor patternism extends to selfhood.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — patternism's premise that the pattern floats free of its substrate is exactly what [[Embodiment|embodiment]] denies: there is no disembodiment, only different embodiment.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — against patternism's dream of "the mind as pure information, pure spirit," materiality insists information is never substrate-independent. O'Gieblyn cites [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] directly: transhumanism is "an ideology…in which 'disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form.'"
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — patternism preserves the autonomous, self-identical interior subject by relocating it from the soul to the algorithm, the move Hayles critiques as smuggling liberal humanism into the posthuman.

## Questions It Raises

- If the self is a substrate-independent pattern, what work is the body doing — and does treating the body as replaceable hardware (the cocktail-waitress dissociation O'Gieblyn describes) repeat, rather than resolve, Cartesian dualism? (ties to *posthumanism* and *the body as data*)
- Does the "continuity of identity" problem (Susan Schneider's zombie objection; "the new person will be a zombie with no subjective experience") show that patternism preserves only *functional* similarity, not the first-person self it promises to immortalize? (ties to *meaning* and *the body as data*)
- When the same river/whirlpool image yields *transience* for Heraclitus and Wiener but *immortality* for Kurzweil, what does the reversal reveal about how a metaphor's valence depends on the technology of perception available to read it? (ties to *technology and perception*)
- If patternism is structurally Origen's *eidos* in computational dress, has the secular project of finding meaning in materialism merely re-imported the religious telos it claimed to escape — and can a pattern bear the weight of meaning a soul once did? (ties to *meaning*)
