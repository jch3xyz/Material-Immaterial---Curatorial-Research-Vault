# Big Data

## Brief Definition

For Han, Big Data is the central instrument of digital psychopolitics: an aperspectival surveillance-and-analytic apparatus that, unlike demographic statistics, can draw a psychogram of the unconscious, predict and pre-empt behaviour, and so "has announced the end of the person who possesses free will."

## Longer Explanation

Han treats Big Data not as a neutral technology of measurement but as the *practical instrument* by which power passes from passive surveillance to active steering. In the closing pages of "The Crisis of Freedom" he writes that "we are entering the age of digital psychopolitics. It means passing from passive surveillance to active steering," and names the tool by which this is achieved: "Big Data is a highly efficient psychopolitical instrument that makes it possible to achieve comprehensive knowledge of the dynamics of social communication." This is "knowledge for the sake of domination and control (Herrschaftswissen)" — knowledge that "facilitates intervention in the psyche and enables influence to take place on a pre-reflexive level." Because Big Data makes behaviour predictable, "the future is becoming calculable and controllable," and what had been the open horizon required for free action closes; persons are "positivized into things, which can be quantified, measured and steered." The chapter ends on Han's strongest claim: "Big Data has announced the end of the person who possesses free will." (Directly stated — Ch1.)

The decisive distinction is between Big Data and the older biopolitical instrument of *statistics*. In "Biopolitics," Han argues that demographic statistics — the census, birth and death rates, life expectancy — administers populations from the outside but "can deliver no material for drawing up a psychogram of the population": "Demography is not the same thing as psych-ography." Against this, "statistics and Big Data lie worlds apart. Big Data provides the means for establishing not just an individual but a collective psychogram - perhaps even the psychogram of the unconscious itself." Where biopolitical statistics is "too crude to penetrate into the deeper layers of the soul," Big Data "may yet shine a light into the depths of the psyche and exploit the unconscious entirely." This is what makes it the engine of [[Psychopolitics]] rather than mere [[Biopolitics]]. (Directly stated — Ch4.)

In the chapter "Big Data" itself, Han locates the apparatus's power in its *aperspectival* optics, updating the [[Digital Panopticon]]: "Digital surveillance proves so efficient because it is aperspectival. It does not suffer from the perspectival limitations characterizing analogue optical systems. Digital optics enables surveillance from any and every angle. It eliminates all blind spots. In contrast to analogue and perspectival optics, it can peer into the human soul itself." Where Bentham's panopticon left blind spots in which "prisoners could indulge in secret wishes and thoughts without being observed," Big Data offers a "360-degree" view and, unlike the forgetful Big Brother, "never forgets anything at all." (Directly stated — Ch11.)

### Micro-targeting as data-driven psychopolitics

Big Data becomes operational power through *micro-targeting*. From precise voter or consumer profiles, "personalized messages are devised to address and influence" their recipients, and Han names this directly: "As the practical microphysics of power, micro-targeting is data-driven psychopolitics." This intervention is prospective, not merely retrospective — "digital psychopolitics manages to intervene in psychic processes in a prospective fashion. Quite possibly, it is even faster than free will. As such, it could overtake it. If so, this would herald the end of freedom." (Directly stated — Ch11.)

### The digital unconscious

Following Walter Benjamin's "optical unconscious," Han proposes that data-mining functions "in analogy to a movie camera," a "digital magnifying glass" that discloses "behind the framework of consciousness" a scene "shot through with unconscious elements." He names this "the digital unconscious": a "microphysical or micropsychical web of relations" of "actomes" — "micro-actions that elude detection by the waking mind." Big Data could thereby render "the collective unconscious accessible," and "digital psychopolitics would be in the position to take control of mass behaviour on a level that escapes detection." Han links the unknown impulse — the pregnant woman who buys a product without knowing why, the "that's-how-it-is (Es-ist-so)" — to "the Freudian id (Es)," so that "Big Data is making the id into an ego to be exploited psychopolitically." (Directly stated — Ch11.)

### Big Brother and Big Business / the ban-opticon

Han stresses that Big Data is not only a surveillance state but a market: "Today, Big Data is not just taking the stage as Big Brother - it is also taking the form of Big Business... Big Brother and Big Business have formed an alliance. The surveillance state and the market are merging." Human beings "have become a commodity," "treated and traded as packages of data." This produces "a new digital class society," and to the panopticon is "joined a 'ban-opticon'" — "a dispositive that identifies persons who stand outside the system or are hostile to it, and excludes them," sorting those "without economic value as waste." (Directly stated — Ch11.)

### Big Data versus Spirit, narrative, and the event

Han's critique is that Big Data is purely *additive* and therefore yields correlation without comprehension: it "affords only extremely rudimentary knowledge, that is, correlations in which nothing is comprehended... it lacks the Concept - and thus it lacks Spirit." "The absolute knowledge intimated by Big Data coincides with absolute ignorance." Because it "calculates average values" and "has no access to what is unique," it is "wholly blind to the event" and so "blind to the future too." This is why Han opposes Big Data to [[Narrativity]] and the syllogism: "Counting is not recounting." Its homogenizing tendency is also tied to [[Gleichschaltung]] and the [[Hell of the Same]] — "Dataism is augmenting Zunehmen im Gleichwerden, or hypertrophied sameness." (Directly stated — Ch11.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Big Data is the technical condition of possibility for psychopolitics: an aperspectival, never-forgetting, market-and-state apparatus that draws a collective psychogram of the unconscious, predicts and pre-empts behaviour through micro-targeting, sorts persons via the ban-opticon, and — being additive rather than narrative — produces correlation without Spirit, announcing "the end of the person who possesses free will."
- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — Big Data is the *supply side* of surveillance capitalism's logic of accumulation: human experience is unilaterally claimed as free raw material, refined by "machine intelligence" (the "ultimate tapeworm" that "reaches its full potential for quality only as it approximates totality") into prediction products traded in behavioral futures markets. Where Han diagnoses the apparatus from the subject's side, Zuboff supplies the *political economy* — behavioral surplus, the prediction imperative, and the digital dossiers of the "shadow text" that "evaluate, categorize, and predict our behavior in millions of ways that we can neither know nor combat." Its instrumentarian endpoint is "the substitution of certainty for society."
- **[[Jeremy Bentham]]** — *Foil.* Han measures Big Data against Bentham's perspectival panopticon, which left blind spots and lacked an efficient recording system; Big Data is "Columbus's egg" for digital control society, more efficient than Bentham's design. (Directly stated — Ch11.)
- **[[Walter Benjamin]]** — *Source of the analogy.* Han borrows Benjamin's "optical unconscious" from the artwork essay to coin the "digital unconscious." (Directly stated — Ch11.)
- **[[Sigmund Freud]]** — *Source of the analogy.* The "id (Es)" supplies the model for the unconscious inclinations Big Data is said to access and exploit. (Directly stated — Ch11.)
- **[[Hegel]]** — *Foil.* Hegel's Concept and syllogism furnish the standard against which Big Data's "absolute knowledge" is revealed as "absolute ignorance." (Directly stated — Ch11.)
- **[[Michel Foucault]]** — Han builds the biopolitics/statistics contrast on Foucault's account of disciplinary and biopolitical power; Big Data marks the threshold biopolitics cannot cross. (Directly stated — Ch4.)

## Shoshana Zuboff (The Age of Surveillance Capitalism)

For Zuboff, Big Data is not the master term it is for Han — she subordinates it to the economic logic that *consumes* it. Big Data is the raw-material flow that surveillance capitalism turns into power. The pipeline begins with **behavioral surplus**: "These behavioral data available for uses beyond service improvement constituted a surplus, and it was on the strength of this behavioral surplus that the young company would find its way to the 'sustained and exponential profits' that would be necessary for survival." That surplus is refined by **machine intelligence**, whose appetite is total: "As the ultimate tapeworm, the machine's intelligence depends upon how much data it eats," and it "reaches its full potential for quality only as it approximates totality." The output is **prediction products** — "Machine intelligence processes behavioral surplus into prediction products designed to forecast what we will feel, think, and do: now, soon, and later" — sold "into a new kind of market that trades exclusively in future behavior." This is the *political economy* beneath the apparatus Han diagnoses: where Han stresses the subject's voluntary self-exposure, Zuboff stresses unilateral claiming and asymmetry — the data are accumulated "from us, but not for us." (Directly stated.)

The accumulation of behavioral surplus reorganizes society around what Zuboff calls the **division of learning in society**, whose three questions — "Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?" — are answered by surveillance capital. The surplus fills a hidden "shadow text": "a burgeoning accumulation of behavioral surplus and its analyses, and it says more about us than we can know about ourselves," and "the work of the shadow text is to evaluate, categorize, and predict our behavior in millions of ways that we can neither know nor combat—these are our digital dossiers." Big Data thus produces the same asymmetries of knowledge and power Han locates in the [[Digital Panopticon]], but Zuboff names their *ownership*: the apparatus is operated by "a narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists, their privately owned machines, and the economic interests for whose sake they learn." (Directly stated.)

In its instrumentarian phase, the data project aims past prediction at modification — the dream that total knowledge will, as Han also argues, dissolve free will into calculability. Zuboff's verdict converges with Han's "end of the person who possesses free will" while routing it through the market: the apparatus pursues "a project of total certainty," "substituting machines for social relations, which amounts to the substitution of certainty for society," a "future of certainty accomplished without violence" in which "the price we pay is not with our bodies but with our freedom." Big Data, on this reading, is the engine of an [[Dataism]] turned into a logic of accumulation — and resonant with the surveillance-and-control cluster of [[Digital Panopticon]], [[Society of Control]], and [[Psychopolitics]]. (Directly stated.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Psychopolitics]] — Big Data is its instrument; micro-targeting *is* "data-driven psychopolitics."
- [[Dataism]] — the *ideology* (the "second Enlightenment," "data totalitarianism") that absolutizes Big Data; distinct from the apparatus itself.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Big Data supplies its aperspectival, never-forgetting optics; the apparatus's aperspectival update.
- [[Biopolitics]] — the prior, statistical-demographic technology Big Data supersedes; "demography is not the same thing as psych-ography."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the self-tracking, self-surveilling subject ("Quantified Self") feeds Big Data its own data, becoming "a panopticon of itself."
- [[Society of Control]] — the political form whose completion ("digital control society") Web 3.0 and Big Data bring about.
- [[Narrativity]] — Big Data is additive, not narrative; "counting is not recounting," and digital memory cannot remember.
- [[Gleichschaltung]] / [[Hell of the Same]] — Big Data augments "hypertrophied sameness" and is blind to the deviant, the singular, the event.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Free Will]] — Han's central antagonism: Big Data is "even faster than free will," predicts and pre-empts decisions, and "has announced the end of the person who possesses free will." (See [[Free Will vs Determinism]].)
- [[Narrativity]] — narration, the syllogism, and meaning ("recounting") stand against the purely additive counting of data; "Big Data lacks the Concept - and thus it lacks Spirit."
- [[The Event]] — the singular and improbable, which "will shape history," is precisely what statistical correlation cannot register.

## Questions It Raises

- If Big Data can act "faster than free will," what conception of freedom — if any — survives, and is the "open future" Han requires recoverable?
- Where exactly is the line between Big Data as *apparatus* and [[Dataism]] as *ideology*, given that Han runs both critiques together in this chapter?
- Does Han's "digital unconscious" describe a real epistemic capacity of data-mining, or is it (like Benjamin's "optical unconscious") a metaphor whose literalization is itself part of the dataist faith?
- Can the ban-opticon's sorting of persons into "waste" be resisted from within a regime where subjects voluntarily supply the data that classifies them?
- If correlation without the Concept is "absolute ignorance," what would a *narrative* or *contemplative* counter-practice to Big Data look like?
- Han and Zuboff both arrive at "the end of free will," one via the psyche and one via the market — is Big Data's threat to freedom essentially psychopolitical (an intervention in the subject) or essentially economic (a logic of accumulation), and does the difference change what resistance is possible?
