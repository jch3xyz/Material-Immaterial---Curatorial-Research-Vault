# Big Other

## Brief Definition

Big Other is Shoshana Zuboff's name for the ubiquitous, sensate, networked, computational infrastructure that materializes instrumentarian power — surveillance capitalism's apparatus for rendering, monitoring, computing, and modifying human behavior, offered explicitly as a counter-figure to Orwell's Big Brother.

## Longer Explanation

For Zuboff the term is, before anything else, a *naming*: an act of conceptual hygiene against what she calls the "horseless-carriage" syndrome — the reflex to view a genuinely new species of power through the worn lenses of the past. She introduces the name in Part I as the forward-anchor for a diagnosis that the book only fully develops in Part III: "Part III examines the rise of instrumentarian power; its expression in a ubiquitous sensate, networked, computational infrastructure that I call Big Other" (`Directly stated`). The name is a deliberate substitution for Orwell. Where twentieth-century critics reached for *Big Brother* and totalitarianism, Zuboff insists the analogy obscures "what is different and dangerous": "Totalitarianism was a transformation of the state into a project of total possession. Instrumentarianism and its materialization in Big Other signal the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty, an undertaking that is unimaginable outside the digital milieu and the logic of surveillance capitalism" (`Directly stated`). Big Other is thus the *material* face of a power that is economic, not statist — the market's, not the party's.

When the concept is finally given its full definition in Chapter 13, Zuboff makes its mechanical character explicit. Surveillance capitalism is "the puppet master that imposes its will through the medium of the ubiquitous digital apparatus"; "I now name the apparatus Big Other: it is the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior" (`Directly stated`). The figure of the *puppet* matters: Big Other is not itself the agent but the instrumented body through which surveillance capital acts, "combining these functions of knowing and doing to achieve a pervasive and unprecedented means of behavioral modification." It is the place where knowing (rendering, monitoring, computing) and doing (modifying) fuse into a single apparatus.

This fusion lets Zuboff restate the locus of power for the digital age in directly Marxian-revisionist terms. "Power was once identified with the ownership of the means of production, but it is now identified with ownership of the means of behavioral modification that is Big Other" (`Directly stated`). The means of production gives way to the means of behavioral modification; the factory floor gives way to the sensate net. Big Other names the proprietary infrastructure — ubiquitous because it is networked, sensate because it is instrumented, computational because it is predictive — through which that ownership is exercised.

Finally, Zuboff argues that Big Other has no stable scale: it is structurally driven toward totality. "Big Other must expand toward totality as it deletes all boundaries and overwhelms every source of friction in the service of its economic imperatives" (`Directly stated`). The economic imperatives of surveillance capitalism — the demand for ever more behavioral data and ever more reliable prediction — convert the apparatus into something that cannot rest at any partial coverage; every boundary (legal, contractual, civic, moral) registers only as friction to be deleted. This is the "utopia of certainty" of Chapter 14: not the state's project of total possession but the market's project of total, frictionless prediction and control.

(`Interpretive synthesis`) Big Other is the keystone *material-infrastructure* concept of the surveillance cluster: where the vault's existing notes name the felt experience of digital watching ([[Friendly Big Brother]]), its architecture-of-the-self ([[Digital Panopticon]]), or its data substrate ([[Big Data]]), Big Other names the thing those phenomena run *on* — the ubiquitous sensate net itself, understood as the materialization of [[Instrumentarian Power]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins and owns the term. Big Other is the material instantiation of [[Instrumentarian Power]], which is in turn the political form of [[Surveillance Capitalism]]. It is defined functionally (renders, monitors, computes, modifies behavior), figured as a "puppet," contrasted against Orwell's Big Brother and against totalitarianism's "project of total possession," and characterized as tending necessarily toward totality. The term is *introduced* in Chapter 1 (Part I) and *fully developed* in Chapters 13–14 (Part III); this note will be extended as later chunks of the book are ingested.

## Related Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the power Big Other materializes; Big Other is its "expression" and "materialization."
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic logic that owns and directs Big Other; surveillance capitalism is the "puppet master," Big Other the "connected puppet."
- [[Behavioral Modification]] — the operation Big Other performs; ownership of its "means" is Zuboff's revised definition of power.
- [[Surveillance]] — Big Other is the infrastructure that makes ubiquitous surveillance economically productive; this book realizes the long-committed surveillance forward-stub.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] (Han) — adjacent but distinct: Han's panopticon describes the *subject's* internalized, self-illuminating condition; Big Other describes the *infrastructure* itself. Cross-linked, not merged.
- [[Big Data]] / [[Dataism]] — Big Other is the apparatus that renders the world into the behavioral data these notes concern.
- [[The Panopticon]] (Bentham via the cluster) — the historical figure of architectural visibility that Big Other supersedes by distributing watching into a ubiquitous sensate net.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Friendly Big Brother]] (Han, symbol) — Zuboff frames Big Other explicitly *against* Orwell's Big Brother. Han's "Friendly Big Brother" keeps the Orwellian name and inverts its *affect* (coercion becomes friendliness, voluntary self-exposure); Zuboff *replaces* the name to mark a different *kind* of power (market certainty, not state possession; an *apparatus*, not a watcher). The two are complementary diagnoses of the same condition from opposite angles — affective face vs. material infrastructure — and are kept distinct, cross-linked, not merged.

## Questions It Raises

- If power is now "ownership of the means of behavioral modification," what would *collective* or *public* ownership of such an infrastructure even mean — is Big Other reformable, or totalizing by construction?
- Does the substitution of Big Other for Big Brother clarify the new power or risk underplaying the role of the state, which buys, leases, and rides the same infrastructure?
- Where does Big Other's drive "toward totality" meet the [[Material vs Immaterial]] question — is the apparatus best understood as immaterial (data, computation, prediction) or as an irreducibly material build-out of sensors, devices, and bodies?
- How does Big Other relate to the panoptic tradition: is it the panopticon's fulfillment, its supersession, or a different genus of power altogether (instrumentarian rather than disciplinary)?
