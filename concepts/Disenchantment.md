# Disenchantment

## Brief Definition

Disenchantment is the modern condition — Max Weber's term, taken up by [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] — in which the once-porous boundary between spirit and matter collapses, everything from mind to planetary motion is reduced to physical-causal mechanism, and the world is thereby left "devoid of intrinsic meaning."

## Longer Explanation

For O'Gieblyn, disenchantment is the book-spanning ground condition against which every chapter on artificial intelligence, transhumanism, and consciousness is set. She names Weber as its source: before the Enlightenment the world was "a great enchanted garden," a place where "the realms of spirit and matter were porous and not easily distinguishable from one another," and where "faith was not opposed to knowledge, nor myth to reason." Modern science dissolved that garden by turning nature from "a source of wonder" into "a force to be mastered, a system to be figured out." At its root, she writes, "disenchantment describes the fact that everything in modern life, from our minds to the rotation of the planets, can be reduced to the causal mechanism of physical laws." What remains in place of the *pneuma* — the spirit-force that "once infused and unified all living things" — is "an empty carapace of gears and levers," Weber's "mechanism of a world robbed of gods."

O'Gieblyn's central move is to insist that the loss of magic is not what makes disenchantment traumatic. Answering critics like Bruno Latour and Charles Taylor who note that science breeds its own wonders, she relocates the wound: "For Weber, disenchantment was not merely an ontological hollowing-out... nor was it the simple fact that the universe could be reduced to causal mechanisms. The true trauma of disenchantment is that the world, as seen through the lens of modern science, is devoid of intrinsic meaning." The mechanistic philosophy "divorced not only body from mind but also matter from meaning"; once Aristotelian final causes were banished, ethics and spirit were "relegated to that ghostly and uncertain realm of the subjective mind." Science can describe the world "objectively... without presuppositions" but "cannot even affirm its own intrinsic value." This is why O'Gieblyn frames her own loss of faith as a "personal disenchantment" — secularization compressed into "an instant."

The genealogy O'Gieblyn traces makes [[René Descartes]] the hinge figure: "in the narratives of disenchantment, Descartes is often positioned as the serpent in the garden, the devil who sundered the world." Where medieval thinkers held that "all living matter was ensouled, to different degrees," Descartes and the seventeenth-century mechanists "envisioned the world as a passive mechanism," a clockwork later "solidified into Newtonian physics." Crucially, O'Gieblyn argues in the Ch09 [Metonymy] chapter that this disenchanted worldview rests on no empirical proof: "No one ever proved that the mind was not part of the world or that the universe was entirely passive and mechanistic. Modern materialism was a philosophical project, a thought experiment dreamed up in an armchair by the fire." Its great unpaid debt — "its inability to explain consciousness and the intrinsic nature of matter" — is what keeps generating the [[Re-enchantment]] narratives she reads transhumanism and panpsychism as.

This is the load-bearing link to her treatment of AI and posthumanism. Disenchantment is the metaphysics that makes the [[Mind as Machine]] thinkable and that licenses the reduction of persons to [[Information]] and [[The Body as Data]]. The transhumanist dream of mind-uploading, she argues, only completes disenchantment rather than escaping it: it is "merely another attempt to argue that humans are nothing more than computation," reiterating "the fundamental problem of our disenchanted age: the inability to account for the mind."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Max Weber]]** — coined the term (*Entzauberung*, "the disenchantment of the world") and supplied O'Gieblyn's framing: the modern world as "the mechanism of a world robbed of gods," driven by "an inner compulsion to understand the world as a meaningful cosmos" that science can no longer satisfy. In *Science as a Vocation* (1917) he holds that "science is meaningless because it gives no answer to our question... 'What shall we do and how shall we live?'" - **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — inherits Weber's term but sharpens its emphasis from the loss of magic to the loss of *intrinsic meaning*, and extends it forward into artificial intelligence, neuroscience, and transhumanism. For her, disenchantment is both a cultural-historical diagnosis and an autobiographical one (her exit from fundamentalism as "my own personal disenchantment").
- *Forward-reference:* O'Gieblyn's reading of disenchantment as an unfinished, self-undermining project resonates with Hans Blumenberg's account of secularization and modern self-assertion; a dedicated section is open should that source be ingested.

## Related Concepts

- [[Mind as Machine]] — the reductive image of personhood that disenchantment makes thinkable; the "empty carapace of gears and levers."
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the contemporary, information-theoretic heir of mechanism; disenchantment recast as universal computation.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the somatic consequence: matter, including the body, drained of intrinsic meaning and rendered as pattern.
- [[Information]] — the form to which disenchanted matter is reduced; O'Gieblyn ties "the firing of neurons, the flow of information" to her skepticism about the disenchanted self.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous self whose "essential and irreducible" core disenchantment calls into question.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the horizon disenchantment opens toward, where the human is "nothing more than computation."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Re-enchantment]] — its counter-movement. O'Gieblyn reads transhumanism, panpsychism, and AI mysticism as "reenchantment narratives," but argues they remain "encoded with the assumptions of disenchantment" and so cannot truly reverse it.

## Questions It Raises

- If disenchantment empties matter of intrinsic meaning, does posthumanism complete it or secretly smuggle meaning back in? O'Gieblyn suggests transhumanism does the former while pretending to the latter.
- The body as data: once the body is "an empty carapace of gears and levers," what grounds embodiment as anything more than substrate? (See [[Embodiment]], [[The Body as Data]].)
- Technology and perception: if home assistants and smartphones are "no less magical than Moses's staff" (Latour's challenge), is the disenchanted/enchanted distinction even stable, or is enchantment merely displaced into the technological object?
- Meaning: can a worldview that "cannot even affirm its own intrinsic value" sustain the projects — knowledge, technical mastery, AI itself — it relentlessly pursues?
- Is disenchantment an empirical discovery or, as O'Gieblyn claims, "a thought experiment dreamed up in an armchair by the fire" — and if the latter, what authority does it hold over the question of mind?
