# The Will to Will

## Brief Definition

The will to will is the human capacity to make and keep promises — Hannah Arendt's "organ" by which we summon futures into existence — the existential ground of freedom and of the right to the future tense, and a capacity that can be expressed only in the medium of uncertainty.

## Longer Explanation

For Shoshana Zuboff, *the will to will* is the philosophical bedrock of the entire normative argument of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*. It names the self-reflexive act in which a person wills herself to will: the determination to close the gap between making a promise and keeping it. A promise, in this account, is "an anchor that girds me against the vagaries of my moods and temptations," the product of "my will to will and a compass that steers my course toward a desired future that is not yet real." To assert this capacity is to make a "claim to the future tense" — to assert that "through my will I can influence the future," not with total authority but "only over my piece of it." `Directly stated` (ch.11).

Zuboff anchors the concept explicitly in Arendt: "Will is the organ with which we summon our futures into existence," echoing Arendt's metaphor of will as the "mental organ of our future" — "something built into us: organic, intrinsic, inalienable." Arendt's promises are "islands of predictability" and "guideposts of reliability" in "an ocean of uncertainty," and they are "the only alternative" to a "mastery" grounded in "domination of one's self and rule over others." The will to will is therefore not merely a private faculty but the origin of collective life: when wills and promises are joined, they create "the possibility of collective action toward a shared future" — the origin of the institution of contract. `Directly stated` (ch.11).

Crucially, the will to will is expressible **only in the medium of uncertainty**. "Most simply put, there is no freedom without uncertainty; it is the medium in which human will is expressed in promises." Uncertainty "is not chaos but rather the necessary habitat of the present tense." We choose "the fallibility of shared promises and problem solving over the certain tyranny imposed by a dominant power or plan" because that fallibility is "the price we pay for the freedom to will." This is precisely why surveillance capitalism's drive toward [[A Utopia of Certainty|guaranteed outcomes]] is existentially corrosive: by replacing uncertainty with engineered certainty through [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|behavior modification]], it dissolves the medium in which will, freedom, and promising can exist at all. In its absence, Zuboff warns, "the future collapses into an infinite present of mere behavior, in which there can be no subjects and no projects: only objects." `Directly stated` (ch.11).

By ch.18 the stakes are cast in civilizational terms. Where the "sixth extinction" befell the natural world, the rise of [[Instrumentarian Power|instrumentarian power]] augurs a "seventh extinction" — not of nature but "of what has been held most precious in human nature: the will to will, the sanctity of the individual, the ties of intimacy, the sociality that binds us together in promises, and the trust they breed." Instrumentarian power "overcomes and replaces the human inwardness that feeds the will to will and gives sustenance to our voices in the first person, incapacitating democracy at its roots." The will to will is thus the durable existential foundation on which Zuboff grounds the [[The Right to the Future Tense|right to the future tense]] — and the thing surveillance capitalism does not extinguish so much as usurp. `Directly stated` (ch.18).

The concept is seeded earlier (ch.9) through Sartre: the "elaboration of freedom" by which a person summons herself into being is the same self-authoring movement Arendt names as the will's organ for the future — Zuboff draws the two existential traditions together to ground first-person sovereignty against the rendering of life as data. `Interpretive synthesis` (Sartrean seeding noted in the scope; the explicit Arendtian formulation is ch.11).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — *The will to will* is Zuboff's adopted term (after Arendt's "mental organ of our future" and, by seeding, Sartre's "elaboration of freedom") for the self-reflexive capacity to make and keep promises. It is the existential ground of freedom, of the right to the future tense, and of first-person voice; it can be expressed only in the medium of uncertainty; and it is the human capacity most endangered by surveillance capitalism's "seventh extinction."

## Related Concepts

- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the right the will to will grounds; freedom of will *is* the assertion of the right to the future tense "as a condition of a fully human life."
- [[The Uncontract]] — the joining of wills into shared promises is the origin of contract; surveillance capitalism's "uncontract" voids promising by substituting guaranteed execution for the freedom to keep one's word.
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the power that "overcomes and replaces the human inwardness that feeds the will to will."
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the apparatus that closes the medium of uncertainty in which will is expressed.
- [[Digital Dispossession]] — the broader pattern by which the will to will is not extinguished but usurped, "commandeered and accumulated by surveillance capital."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — the will to will requires uncertainty as its medium; the engineered certainty of guaranteed outcomes is its antithesis. "There is no freedom without uncertainty."
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — Zuboff's defense of the will to will is a direct stake in this tension against the [[Dataism|dataist]]/[[Big Data|behaviorist]] reduction of the human to a predictable, modifiable organism.

## Questions It Raises

- If the will to will can be expressed only in the medium of uncertainty, can any technology that promises "guaranteed outcomes" coexist with human freedom, or is the conflict irreducible?
- Is the will to will, as Arendt suggests, truly "intrinsic" and "inalienable" — or, as Zuboff's "usurpation" framing implies, an achievement that can be commandeered and lost?
- How does Zuboff's Arendtian/Sartrean will to will stand against the [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)|claim that free will is a fiction]] advanced from the dataist side of the [[Digital Panopticon|behavioral-data]] debate?
- If "the sociality that binds us together in promises" is endangered, what becomes of contract, democracy, and collective action — the institutions built from joined wills?
