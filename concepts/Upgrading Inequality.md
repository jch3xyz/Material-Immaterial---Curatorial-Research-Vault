# Upgrading Inequality

## Brief Definition

The third of Harari's three threats from the decoupling of intelligence and consciousness: as twenty-first-century medicine pivots from the egalitarian project of *healing the sick* to the elitist project of *upgrading the healthy*, the social gap between rich and poor hardens into a permanent *biological* gap, splitting humankind into a "small and privileged elite of upgraded humans" — a superhuman caste — and an inferior caste of un-upgraded "useless" masses.

## Longer Explanation

In "The Great Decoupling," [[Yuval Noah Harari]] lists three practical developments that might make the liberal belief in the individual obsolete. The first is that humans lose their economic and military value; the second is that the system still values humans collectively but not as individuals. The third — the subject of this note — is that "some people will remain both indispensable and undecipherable, but they will constitute a small and privileged elite of upgraded humans. These superhumans will enjoy unheard-of abilities and unprecedented creativity," while "most humans will not be upgraded, and they will consequently become an inferior caste, dominated by both computer algorithms and the new superhumans." (`Directly stated`)

The hinge of the argument is a conceptual revolution in medicine. "Twentieth-century medicine aimed to heal the sick. Twenty-first-century medicine is increasingly aiming to upgrade the healthy." Harari spells out why this shifts medicine from an egalitarian to an elitist enterprise: "Healing the sick was an egalitarian project, because it assumed that there is a normative standard of physical and mental health that everyone can and should enjoy." Upgrading, by contrast, "is an elitist project, because it rejects the idea of a universal standard applicable to all, and seeks to give some individuals an edge over others." Worse, the gains never settle into a shared floor: "If some form of upgrade becomes so cheap and common that everyone enjoys it, it will simply be considered the new baseline, which the next generation of treatments will strive to surpass." The treadmill structurally guarantees that "the elites will always remain a couple of steps ahead." (`Directly stated`)

Harari's distinctive claim is that this gap is unprecedented in *kind*, not merely degree. Liberalism, he notes, "can coexist with socio-economic gaps" — it "favours liberty over equality" and so "takes such gaps for granted" — but it "still presupposes that all human beings have equal value and authority." The liberal solution to inequality "is to give equal value to different human experiences, instead of trying to create the same experiences for everyone." That solution collapses "once rich and poor are separated not merely by wealth, but also by real biological gaps." Where "medieval aristocrats claimed that superior blue blood was flowing through their veins... this was pure fiction," but "in the future... we may see real gaps in physical and cognitive abilities opening between an upgraded upper class and the rest of society." The danger is that a new superhuman caste "will abandon its liberal roots and treat normal humans no better than nineteenth-century Europeans treated Africans." (`Directly stated`)

The argument turns on the premise developed elsewhere in the book that the masses lose their value: because the twentieth century "was the age of the masses," armies and economies "needed millions of healthy soldiers" and "millions of healthy workers," giving elites a vested interest in mass public health. As "human soldiers and workers give way to algorithms," that interest evaporates — "at least some elites may conclude that there is no point in providing improved or even standard conditions of health for masses of useless poor people, and it is far more sensible to focus on upgrading a handful of superhumans beyond the norm." Harari dramatizes this with the image that gives the note its tension: developing countries "resemble a long train," where first-class carriages enjoy first-world health and education while "hundreds of millions of ordinary citizens who crowd the third-class carriages still suffer from widespread diseases, ignorance and poverty." The "ruthless" strategy of the coming century "may be to let go of the useless third-class carriages, and dash forward with the first class only." (`Directly stated`)

Crucially, Harari has already foreshadowed the healing-to-upgrading slide in Chapter 1, where he insists the boundary is porous from the start: "No clear line separates healing from upgrading. Medicine almost always begins by saving people from falling below the norm, but the same tools and know-how can then be used to surpass the norm." His examples — Viagra (a blood-pressure drug that became an enhancement), modern plastic surgery (born treating WWI facial injuries, now "upgrading the healthy and beautify the wealthy") — establish the general law: "Healing is the initial justification for every upgrade... Once you achieve a momentous breakthrough, you cannot restrict its use to healing and completely forbid using it for upgrading." Chapter 13 restates the same pivot inside the project of techno-humanism: "medicine is increasingly focused on upgrading the healthy rather than healing the sick. Doctors, engineers and customers no longer want merely to fix mental problems — they seek to upgrade the mind." Upgrading Inequality is thus the *social-stratification* consequence of a slide Harari treats as already underway. (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (*Homo Deus*) — the third threat from the decoupling. The healing→upgrading conceptual revolution (Ch. 1 foreshadows it as a porous boundary; Ch. 12 makes it the engine of a biological caste split; Ch. 13 restates it within techno-humanism). Egalitarian healing assumes a universal norm everyone can reach; elitist upgrading rejects that norm and is structurally a treadmill, so it produces a permanent superhuman elite and a "useless" un-upgraded mass — the first-class/third-class carriages.
- **Resonance with [[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Han's [[Self-Optimization]] and [[Healing as Killing]] name a *psychic* version of the same drive: "healing" as the euphemism for boundless self-optimization with no normative endpoint. Harari's frame is biological and stratifying (who gets upgraded), Han's is psychopolitical and self-exploiting (the optimizing self destroying itself). The shared move is the dissolution of a stable norm of health into an open-ended imperative to surpass. ## Related Concepts

- [[Transhumanism]] — the project of engineering upgraded, "superhuman" capacities; Upgrading Inequality is its distributional shadow — what happens to liberal equality when transhuman enhancement is real but unequally distributed.
- [[Self-Optimization]] — Han's neoliberal imperative to optimize the self without limit; the psychic correlate of upgrading-the-healthy, sharing the dissolution of a fixed health norm.
- [[Healing as Killing]] — Han's paradox that boundless "healing" / optimization destroys the subject; a cross-author rhyme on the same healing→optimization slippage Harari diagnoses biologically.
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the premise that makes upgrading conceivable: if humans are improvable organic algorithms, there is no in-principle ceiling on enhancement.
- [[Homo Deus]] — the upgraded superhuman as a species project; Upgrading Inequality is the claim that this project will be available only to a few.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — the creed that all human beings have equal value and authority; Upgrading Inequality is the scenario that destroys its foundations by replacing a removable socio-economic gap with an irreversible biological one.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, equally-valued individual; a biological caste split means some subjects literally "have fundamentally different experiences," voiding the liberal premise of commensurable human experience.

## Questions It Raises

- If "no clear line separates healing from upgrading," can any regulatory or ethical boundary hold the line — or is every therapy a future enhancement, making the caste split a matter of *when*, not *whether*?
- Harari notes that twentieth-century breakthroughs "began with the rich, but eventually benefited the whole population." What, structurally, breaks the trickle-down in his account — the treadmill ("the new baseline"), the loss of the masses' economic value, or both?
- Is the "biological gap" a genuinely new category of inequality, or a re-description of compounding socio-economic advantage (the $3,000 genetic test, the 62 billionaires) that liberalism already failed to redress?
- For a curatorial and art-making practice concerned with embodiment: if superhumans "have fundamentally different experiences to normal Sapiens," what happens to art's wager on shared, commensurable human experience — the very wager that "liberal authors write long novels about the experiences of poor peasants"?
