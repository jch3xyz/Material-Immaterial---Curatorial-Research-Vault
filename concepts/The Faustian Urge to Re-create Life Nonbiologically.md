# The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically

## Brief Definition

The deep, transhistorical drive Burnham diagnoses beneath the entire history of automata: Western man's desire to "bring to life" the human-shaped image — to re-create life by means other than natural biological creation — which he names the "aesthetic core of the Faustian urge."

## Longer Explanation

In *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Jack Burnham treats automata as one branch of "subsculpture" — the fetishes, idols, dolls, manikins, puppets, and self-animated images historically ranked below sculpture as a fine art. Tracking the *progressive mechanization* of automata across ten thousand years, he argues that what characterizes Western culture beyond all the other forms of subsculpture is this mechanizing drive, and that in it "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge shows itself; Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically." This concept names the **WHY** — the desire and its psychic root — as distinct from [[Object and System]], which names the **structural shift** (the migration of art from discrete object to working system) that the desire propels (`Interpretive synthesis`).

The urge is not, for Burnham, primarily about realism or beauty. The "ultimate concern of the maker of anthropomorphic images is to 'bring to life' the human-shaped entity, though not through natural biological creation" — achieved historically by the metaphorical subtlety of art, by mimetic representation, by supposed magical properties, or, "as in the case of automata, by literal movement and animation" (`Directly stated`). Burnham reads the drive psychoanalytically: because the male plays "only a brief role in procreation," the impulse to create images and technical inventions is "in part, a sublimation of the act of birth," an attempt "to bring ideal life into one's midst." He frames it through Spengler's Apollonian/Faustian distinction: sculpture remains Apollonian in its quietude and static order, while "automata possess a Faustian drive to transcend natural limitations," where "the soul of the artist is tied to mechanical fallibility and madness lies in the indefinite possibilities of technology" (`Directly stated`).

Burnham traces the urge through Egyptian servant-figures and speaking oracle-statues, the Greek mechanical Dionysus, the alchemical homunculus and the clockwork "jacks," Descartes's man-as-mechanism and Vaucanson's digesting *Canard*, down to Čapek's robots and postwar cybernetic "pseudo-organisms" — Grey Walter's tortoises, Ross Ashby's homeostat, Claude Shannon's maze-running mice — which he calls "tenuously linked with life itself." The trajectory matters: the early anthropomorphic facsimile gives way to the nonanthropomorphic, autonomous logic-machine (Pascal's calculator, Leibniz's programmable cogwheel — "the spiritual father of modern cybernetics"). This is precisely the movement from imitating the *outside* of life to modeling its *processes* — the engine of the object-to-system migration (`Strongly implied`).

Read forward, Burnham's 1968 thesis deeply anticipates the vault's later authors. The desire to re-create life nonbiologically becomes Hayles's question of [[Artificial Life]] and the [[The Body as Data|body as data]]; it becomes Kurzweil's program of [[Transcending Biology]] and re-creating the self as a [[Patternism|pattern of information]] via [[Mind Uploading]]; and it becomes Haraway's [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] — "the machine is us, an aspect of our embodiment" — in which the organism/machine boundary has already been breached. Burnham supplies the long art-historical genealogy of the impulse these writers theorize in informatic, posthuman terms (`Speculative connection`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — names the urge directly: the progressive mechanization of automata reveals "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge," Western man's drive to "re-create life nonbiologically." He grounds it in the maker's wish to "bring to life" the anthropomorphic image by nonbiological means, and reads it as a sublimation of birth and a Faustian transcendence of natural limits — the desire underwriting the migration toward [[Object and System|art as system]].
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — the same urge, recast informatically: the drive to realize [[Artificial Life]] and to treat life as pattern detachable from its biological substrate. Hayles is critical where Burnham is diagnostic, insisting that disembodiment is impossible and there is only different [[Embodiment]].
- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — the breached boundary between organism and machine: "the machine is us, an aspect of our embodiment." The [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] is the fulfilled figure of the nonbiological re-creation of the living, though Haraway claims it as a liberating ontology rather than a Faustian fall.
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — the urge in its most literal, celebratory form: [[Transcending Biology]], re-creating the human as [[Patternism|a pattern of information]] uploadable beyond flesh ([[Mind Uploading]]) — biology as "dramatically suboptimal," to be surpassed rather than honored.

## Related Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — the structural correlate: this note names the *desire*; that names the *shift* (object → working system) the desire drives.
- [[Artificial Life]] — the explicit informatic-era project of re-creating the living nonbiologically.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the realized fusion of organism and machine; the figure the urge produces.
- [[Transcending Biology]] / [[Mind Uploading]] / [[Patternism]] — Kurzweil's literalization of re-creating life and self outside the body.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the body reconceived as information, a precondition for nonbiological re-creation.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the horizon in which biological humanity ceases to be the privileged carrier of life or mind.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the counter-claim (Hayles especially) that life and cognition cannot be abstracted from their material, biological substrate; the urge to re-create life nonbiologically runs directly against the thesis that there is only different embodiment.

## Questions It Raises

- Is the drive to re-create life nonbiologically genuinely "universal," as Burnham asserts, or specifically Western and Faustian — bound to a particular metaphysics of transcendence?
- If the urge migrates from copying life's *appearance* to modeling its *processes* (Pascal, Leibniz, cybernetics), does success mean the disappearance of the anthropomorphic image — the very thing the urge first sought to animate?
- Does Burnham's "sublimation of the act of birth" reading survive Haraway's refusal of origin myths, or does the cyborg dissolve the procreative frame Burnham assumes?
- Where Burnham diagnoses a Faustian pathos doomed to "a final sense of inconviction," Kurzweil sees an engineering triumph — is the difference one of evidence, or of metaphysics?
