# Telenoia

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's 1992 coinage (Greek *tele*, "far off," + *nous*, "mind") for networked consciousness — interactive awareness, "mind at large" — posed as the convivial, connective opposite of the paranoia of the isolated industrial self.

## Longer Explanation

Telenoia is the name Ascott gives to both the *means* and the *meaning* of the telematic process. He coined it in November 1992 to label a specific twenty-four-hour global networking project at the V2 Organisation in 's-Hertogenbosch, Holland, and at the same time to "stand for the nature of the telematic process in general" (`Directly stated`, ch. 23). It is not a technology but a quality of consciousness produced *by* the network: "networked consciousness, interactive awareness, mind at large (to use Gregory Bateson's term)." Where a painting or sculpture externalises a fixed meaning to be viewed from outside, telematic art "generates meaning through our intimate participation in its evolution" — telenoia is the awareness proper to being *inside* the net rather than in front of an object.

The concept is built as a polemical inversion. Ascott reads the "old industrial culture" as a regime of *paranoia*: "the secrecy and suspicion sustained by the paradigm of paranoia, which is what the smokestack culture engendered," with its cult of "the unified, indivisible human being, one body, one soul, one self" (`Directly stated`, ch. 23). Telenoia "is thus set to replace the paranoia of the isolated, alienated self of the old industrial society." The shift it names is from the solitary, hermetically bounded ego to "the multiple, distributed presences of a set of many selves" — *l'homme éclaté*, in Paul Virilio's phrase. Connectivity, for Ascott, is "a significant feature of the evolutionary process"; "the undivided unity we seek is in mind at large" (`Directly stated`, ch. 23). He even gives it a deep provenance, suggesting telenoia "perhaps should have been the very first word to be uttered as we emerged into our humanness," lost as left-hemisphere, "linear, mechanical thinking" gained cultural dominance.

Ascott frames telenoia as a goal to be reached, not a state already achieved. The five qualities he isolates — "Connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence" — are "key elements in our cultural transition," and together "mark what I would describe as the five-fold path towards telenoia, towards a convivial and constructive life in the Net" (`Directly stated`, ch. 23). The framing is explicitly affirmative and even spiritual: "Telematic technology is the technology of consciousness; it is spiritual before it is in any way utilitarian."

Across the later chapters the word stabilises into Ascott's standing shorthand for the optimistic, connective pole of the telematic condition. In *Technoetic Aesthetics* it is glossed as celebration: "Telenoia celebrates the networked consciousness of global connectivity. It replaces the anxious, alienated, secretive, and neurotically private paranoia of the old industrial culture" (`Directly stated`, ch. 32). In *Cyberception* and *The Mind of the Museum* he repeats the etymology and the "mind-at-large" gloss almost verbatim; in *The Planetary Collegium* he distils it to "computer-mediated, distributed mind-at-large: asynchronous global connectivity. Not paranoia!" (`Directly stated`, ch. 26). By *Art @ the Edge of the NET* the historical claim is total: "We are only now moving from the industrial age of paranoia into the telenoia of universal connectivity and transparency" (`Directly stated`, ch. 31).

## Related Concepts

- [[Network Consciousness]] — telenoia *is* networked consciousness; this is its closest synonym in Ascott, the consciousness produced by being inside the net.
- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the relational/affective register of the same connectivity; "Love in the telematic embrace is a many-splendoured thing."
- [[The Holomatic Principle]] — Ascott's principle that "to be at any one interface is to be in the virtual presence of all other interfaces"; the structural condition that makes telenoia possible.
- [[Telematics (Ascott)]] — the fusion of computers and telecommunications that is the medium of telenoia.
- [[Distributed Authorship]] — the dispersal of the creating "self" across nodes that telenoia presupposes ("the artist becomes a complex and widely distributed system").
- [[Telematic Art]] / [[Connective Criticism]] — the art practice and critical mode that telenoia is meant to describe and sustain.
- [[The Global Village]] — McLuhan's earlier name for the retribalised, all-at-once electric collectivity that telenoia extends and revalues as a positive program.
- [[Dataism]] — the cross-vault worldview of total connectivity and the free flow of information; telenoia is the *celebratory consciousness* Ascott attaches to that flow (the network as conviviality rather than control).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Telenoia vs Paranoia]] — the constitutive opposition: telenoia (connective, convivial, distributed mind) against the paranoia of "the isolated, alienated self of the old industrial society."
- [[Transparency vs Opacity]] — Ascott pairs telenoia with "transparency" as an unambiguous good ("universal connectivity and transparency," ch. 31), the affirmative valence directly opposed by Han's later critique of compulsory transparency. (`Speculative connection` — a cross-author bridge, not asserted by Ascott.)
- [[Posthumanism]] — telenoia leads, Ascott insists, "not to a disembodiment of the person, nor to an immateriality, but to the rematerialisation... the reinvention of the human being" — a posthuman reframing that resists the disembodiment thesis even as it dissolves the unitary self. (`Strongly implied`.)

## Questions It Raises

- Is telenoia a description of an actually emerging consciousness, or a normative ideal — Ascott's "five-fold path *towards* telenoia" — that the same networks could equally route into surveillance and control?
- Ascott celebrates "universal connectivity and transparency" as liberation; Han names compulsory transparency as a neoliberal dispositive of control. Is the difference one of fact about the network, or of which historical moment each writer addresses?
- If the unitary self is "lunacy" and the many-selves the norm, what becomes of responsibility, since Ascott locates "the human value of the meaning generated" in "the intervention of the (human) viewer or user of the system"?
- Telenoia is offered as the convivial *opposite* of paranoia — but does pervasive connectivity dissolve the boundaries (privacy, secrecy) that paranoia at least named as real? Is there a loss the optimism cannot account for?
