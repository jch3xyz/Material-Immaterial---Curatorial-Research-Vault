# Moistmedia

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for the third domain — between the dry silicon of digital virtuality and the wet biology of organic nature — where bits, atoms, neurons, and genes converge into the fluid substrate of twenty-first-century art: "I have seen the future—and it's moist."

## Longer Explanation

**Moistmedia** is Ascott's late-period (1990s–2000s) coinage for the convergence of the *dry* and the *wet*. The twentieth century's interactive media, he argues, "replaced the cult of the objet d'art with a process-based culture"; the twenty-first century pushes that shift further, "as silicon and pixels merge with molecules and matter." The space opened by that merger is neither purely digital nor purely biological but a third interspace: "Between the dry world of virtuality and the wet world of biology lies a moist domain, a new interspace of potentiality and promise." Moistmedia — "comprising bits, atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination" — is the material of a "transformative art concerned with the construction of a fluid reality" (`Directly stated`, ch.31).

The figure is built on a clean tripartition that recurs across the late chapters. In the "100 Terms" glossary Ascott fixes the three states: "Dry reality is found in the arid spaces of VR. Wet reality is the nature that we nurture. Moist reality emerges from the biotechnology of artificial life" (`Directly stated`, ch.32). Moist is thus where the digital, the biological, and the spiritual coincide — "MOIST ART is digitally dry, biologically wet, and spiritually numinous." The convergence is grounded in the rise of the artificial-life sciences, which for Ascott span "both dry (pixel) and moist (molecular) artificial organisms and the whole prospectus of nanotechnology" (`Directly stated`, ch.30). The same erosion of boundaries runs through the substrate of art itself: as molecular construction (the scanning tunnelling microscope, nano-engineering, genetic code) joins computation, hardware and "wetware" stop being separable categories.

Moistmedia is the explicit realization of the **moist convergence** seeded one chapter earlier in the museum essay, where Ascott declares the future "is where artificial life and artificial consciousness meet our own wet biology and our telematic society of mind." There he projects that art's auto-constructed agents "might become artistic identities, whether in silicon or in the moist terrain of artificial life as it integrates with our own wet biology," and that "artificial life, engendered in silicon, may increasingly take on a moister reality, eventually integrating with the wet reality we call nature" (`Directly stated`, ch.29). Moistmedia names that integration as the new ground of artistic practice — the [[The Rematerialization of Art|re-materialization]] of a culture that the digital had rendered immaterial, now resolved back into "molecules and matter."

Crucially, Moistmedia is not a return to brute materiality but a synthesis that carries the immaterial forward. It is bound to Ascott's [[Technoetics]] (techne + gnōsis): "MOIST MIND is technoetic multiconsciousness." Where Burnham's 1968 [[Object and System]] thesis tracked art's movement from the discrete object to the immaterial system, Ascott pushes that same trajectory into the biological and spiritual register — system becomes a *living*, self-organizing, sentient substrate. This makes Moistmedia the chronological and conceptual heir to the vault's [[Systems Esthetics]] lineage and the moist successor to both [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Moistmedia does not pick a pole but dissolves the opposition: the dry/immaterial digital is re-materialized into "molecules and atoms, neurons and genes," yet without forfeiting the immaterial (informational, technoetic, numinous). It is the synthesis the eight-author tension keeps circling — material *and* immaterial at once.

## Questions It Raises

- Is the "moist" a genuine third ontological state, or a rhetorical bridge that simply re-describes the convergence of computation and biotechnology?
- If art's substrate becomes living and self-organizing (artificial life, genetic intervention, sentient architecture), where does the artist's authorship reside — in seeding, or in cultivating an autonomous system?
- Ascott reads the digital re-materialization as emancipatory and "spiritually numinous"; does the moist convergence carry the surveillance and control implications that Han locates in the digital, or does the biological substrate change the political stakes?
- Does Moistmedia complete the dematerialization → rematerialization arc (Lippard → Burnham → Ascott), or open a new one?
