# Neoliberalism

## Brief Definition

The decades-long elaboration and implementation of a radical free-market doctrine — descended from Hayek and Friedman — that aimed to reverse, subdue, and even destroy second-modernity claims to psychological self-determination, and which supplied the institutional "habitat in which surveillance capitalism could flourish."

## Longer Explanation

For Shoshana Zuboff, neoliberalism is not the cause of surveillance capitalism but its enabling context — the larger institutional landscape that protects and sustains it. Surveillance capitalism, she argues, owes its success to "the destructive collision of two opposing historical forces": one vector is the centuries-long story of individualization that produced the self-determining individual of the [[Second Modernity]]; the opposing vector is "the decades-long elaboration and implementation of the neoliberal economic paradigm," whose aim is "to reverse, subdue, impede, and even destroy the individual urge toward psychological self-determination and moral agency" (`Directly stated`). Neoliberalism is therefore staged in the book as the answer to a question of historical possibility: why did a self-determining people accept the draconian quid pro quo in which "information and connection are ransomed for the lucrative behavioral data" that funds surveillance capitalism's growth?

Zuboff anchors the doctrine in named figures and texts, treated here as plain history rather than as their own notes. Led by the Austrian economist Friedrich Hayek and his American counterpart Milton Friedman, neoliberal economists had honed a radical free-market theory at the fringe of their profession before flowing into the "policy vacuum" of the 1970s. Its keystone is Hayek's "extended order" — the self-regulating market reconceived as an unknowable, super-human "information-gathering process… that no central planning agency, let alone any individual, could know as a whole, possess, or control." This demand for "absolute individual and collective submission to the exacting disciplines of the market" supersedes the political authority vested in the state (`Directly stated`). Hayek's ideology, in turn, furnished the legitimation for a new theory of the firm: the "shareholder value movement" operationalized by Jensen and Meckling in 1976, which recast the manager as a parasite on ownership and enshrined the share price as the sole signal of value — a structure Zuboff names as a crucial antecedent of the surveillance-capitalist corporation (`Directly stated`).

The constituting move, for Zuboff, is the demolition of what Karl Polanyi called the double movement: the "network of measures and policies… integrated into powerful institutions designed to check the action of the market relative to labor, land, and money" that "supports the market form while tethering it to society." The double movement was achieved in the US through the Progressive Era, the New Deal, and the Great Society; under the neoliberal flag it "was scheduled for demolition," beginning with Carter-era deregulation and defining "the Reagan and Thatcher eras, virtually every subsequent US presidency, and much of the rest of the world" (`Directly stated`). The result is an "ideology of atomized individuals sentenced to perpetual competition for scarce resources," in which "the absolute authority of market forces" displaces "democratic contest and deliberation." This is why neoliberalism does not merely accompany surveillance capitalism but renders second-modernity individuals pliable to it: a society stripped of countervailing institutions, preoccupied with survival, and taught to read its own dignity as a market position is a society that will trade its future for connection.

Read against the rest of the vault, Zuboff's economic-history account converges with Byung-Chul Han's psycho-political one. Where Zuboff narrates neoliberalism as the dismantling of Polanyi's double movement, Han treats neoliberalism as a regime of power that operates through freedom itself — the [[Achievement Society]] in which the subject exploits itself voluntarily ([[Exploitation of Freedom]], [[Self-Optimization]]). Han's full account of transparency and data as a "neoliberal dispositive" is held in his [[Psychopolitics]] cluster (a standalone *Neoliberal Dispositive* note remains a forward-stub); his economic-theology of [[Capital as Religion]] and the [[Dictatorship of Capital]] name the same condition Zuboff diagnoses institutionally. The two are complementary diagnoses of one habitat: neoliberalism removes the social tethers (Zuboff) and rewires the self to mistake its captivity for freedom (Han) (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — neoliberalism as the "neoliberal habitat": the radical free-market paradigm (Hayek's extended order, the shareholder-value firm, the demolition of Polanyi's double movement) that reverses second-modernity self-determination and provides the institutional shelter in which surveillance capitalism can root and flourish.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — neoliberalism as a regime of psychopolitical power that rules through freedom rather than against it; the achievement-subject auto-exploits, and transparency/data function as a neoliberal dispositive (see [[Psychopolitics]], [[Achievement Society]], [[Exploitation of Freedom]]).

## Related Concepts

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the elemental claim to individual sovereignty and self-determination that neoliberalism is designed to thwart, and that democratic action (e.g. the "right to be forgotten") reasserts.

## Questions It Raises

- Is neoliberalism merely the *context* for surveillance capitalism, or one of its constitutive causes? Zuboff insists on "habitat," not origin — but how separable are they?
- If the demolished double movement once tethered raw capitalism to society, what would a twenty-first-century equivalent — Zuboff's "third modernity" — look like?
- Does Zuboff's institutional account and Han's psycho-political account name two halves of one mechanism, or two competing diagnoses of neoliberal power?
- Hayek's "extended order" is itself described as an "information-gathering process" no one can possess — does surveillance capitalism realize, invert, or betray that ideal?
