# Anthropomorphism in Sculpture

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's thesis that "in the deepest sense sculpture has always been anthropomorphic" — that fashioned images, even the nonobjective and Constructivist sculpture that explicitly denies any human reference, are "extensions of man's psychic and physical existence" — so that "it is doubtful if non-anthropomorphic sculpture can exist," and that modern formalism is "a unique stage of anthropomorphism, ... a direct prelude to the re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means."

## Longer Explanation

Burnham makes the claim at its broadest in Chapter 8 of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*: "In the deepest sense sculpture has always been anthropomorphic. Whether imitating animals or the god-spirits determining the destinies of men, fashioned images have always been extensions of man's psychic and physical existence." (`Directly stated`.) The anthropomorphism is therefore not a matter of literal human likeness — animal images and god-images count too — but of the sculpture being a projection of the maker, a vehicle for "an indefatigable human urge to project life into inanimate substances." The reach of the thesis is what makes it radical: it survives the entire modernist repudiation of the figure.

This is where Burnham confronts nonobjective art directly. "It is doubtful if non-anthropomorphic sculpture can exist. Since the creation of the first nonobjective and Constructivist sculptures in the early part of the twentieth century, artists have consistently denied the anthropomorphic and mimetic content of their works. Each successive generation of nonobjective (or to use the most recent term: 'literalist') sculptors has accused the previous generation of anthropomorphism. Even the present generation of Object sculptors do not escape this charge." (`Directly stated`.) The repeated charge of anthropomorphism leveled by each new generation against the last is, for Burnham, evidence that the drive cannot be exited — it can only be relocated and denied. The denial is itself part of the pattern.

The hinge of the argument is the reframing of [[Formalism (Burnham)|formalism]] as a stage of this same drive rather than an escape from it. "Earlier, in Chapter Three, we have considered modern formalism as a unique stage of anthropomorphism, as a direct prelude to the re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means. This drive toward successively more efficacious forms of anthropomorphism seems to be a cultural characteristic of the human organism." (`Directly stated`.) Formalism's analytic, de-idealized vocabulary of shape, proportion, structure and texture looks like the purgation of the human from sculpture; Burnham reads it instead as anthropomorphism in a more "efficacious" register — no longer copying the human surface but reproducing the *organization* of life, which is the precondition for literally re-making intelligent life by nonbiological means.

The thesis thus carries a teleology. The historic yearning to make images that do not merely "indicate, imitate, simulate, but literally replace" the living thing leads, through formalism and Kinetic Art, toward [[Cyborg Art|Cyborg or Post-Kinetic art]] — "really the first attempt to simulate the structure of life literally." (`Directly stated`.) At that endpoint the impulse consumes its own medium: "sculpture seeks its own obliteration by moving toward integration with the intelligent life forms it has always imitated." (`Directly stated`.) Anthropomorphism in sculpture is, on this account, a [[Sculpture as Biological Signal|biological signal]] of the human organism — a cultural characteristic of the species pursuing successively more powerful means of re-creating life, of which the actual cybernetic organism is the terminus and the static carved figure was only the first, weakest stage. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — argues that anthropomorphism is the deep and inescapable content of all sculpture, including avowedly nonobjective work; that modern formalism is "a unique stage of anthropomorphism" and "a direct prelude to the re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means"; and that the drive toward "successively more efficacious forms of anthropomorphism" is "a cultural characteristic of the human organism" whose endpoint is the cyborg, at which sculpture "seeks its own obliteration."

## Related Concepts

- [[Formalism (Burnham)]] — the keystone link: formalism, which presents itself as the purgation of the human from sculpture, is recast as anthropomorphism in a more efficacious, organizational register — "a unique stage of anthropomorphism."
- [[Cyborg Art]] — the terminus of the drive: the first art "to simulate the structure of life literally," toward which anthropomorphism has always been tending.
- [[Sculpture as Biological Signal]] — the framing that anthropomorphism is "a cultural characteristic of the human organism," a species-level drive rather than an aesthetic choice.
- [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] — the broader Burnham thesis that anthropomorphism feeds: the craving to wrest the secrets of natural order and "literally replace" living reality.
- [[The Robot]] — the anthropomorphic machine-form sculptors fixate on; "for our generation much significant anthropomorphic sculpture does not imitate man, but imitates robots trying to become human."
- [[Subsculpture]] — the doll, waxwork, and manikin as degraded anthropomorphism, "shells inhabited by human souls."
- [[Automata]] — the historical precursors whose "yearning to break down the psychic and physical barriers between art and living reality" anthropomorphism continues.
- [[Organicism]] — the cybernetic fulfillment of the organic ideal toward which more "efficacious" anthropomorphism aims.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the literal cybernetic organism; anthropomorphism's culmination as integration with, not imitation of, intelligent life.
- [[Posthumanism]] (Hayles, Haraway) — the later framing of the human/machine boundary breakdown that Burnham's "re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means" anticipates from within sculpture. (`Speculative connection`.)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] — the successor program that relocates meaning from the object to the viewer's act of perception; where anthropomorphism makes the sculpted figure an extension of the maker, phenomenalism dissolves the figural object altogether — a different exit from formalism than the cybernetic one.

## Questions It Raises

- If "it is doubtful if non-anthropomorphic sculpture can exist," is the thesis falsifiable, or does the recurring charge of hidden anthropomorphism make it unfalsifiable by design?
- When formalism is read as "a unique stage of anthropomorphism," does the term still name human *likeness*, or has it been stretched to mean any projection of the maker's "psychic and physical existence" into matter?
- If sculpture's deepest drive is "the re-creation of intelligent life by non biological means," does the realized cyborg complete sculpture or abolish it — and what, if anything, distinguishes an artwork from a manufactured intelligent system at that point?
- Is "a cultural characteristic of the human organism" an explanation or a restatement — what would it mean for anthropomorphism to be a *biological* signal rather than a cultural one?
