# Nature II

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for a new, second-order, emergent nature — produced by the convergence of telematics with molecular technology (nanotechnology, artificial life) — within which we are participants in nature's creative processes rather than antagonists or outsiders looking in.

## Longer Explanation

"Nature II" is the title concept of Ascott's 1995 essay "Back to Nature II," and it answers a deliberate paradox: how can "the most advanced technologies, electronic and molecular, the very epitome of the artificial," bring us *back to nature*? Ascott's claim is that the old metaphor of nature — the pure, the pastoral, the god-given, staged for us in the "timeless immobility" of landscape painting — is exhausted, "past its 'sell by' date," and that the binary oppositions that sustained it (town/country, natural/artificial, material/immaterial) are collapsing under ubiquitous telematic networks and molecular biology. In its place he proposes "the metaphors of a new nature, second-order nature, emergent nature, Nature II, a new creativity whose 'engines of creation' will embrace artificial life" (the phrase "engines of creation" borrowed from K. Eric Drexler's nanotechnology). `Directly stated`

The "second-order" qualifier is loaded: Ascott draws it from second-order cybernetics, the move that included the observer of a system *within* the system. Just as cybernetics had to fold the observer into its objects of study — rendering their boundaries "fuzzy, permeable, uncertain, and transformable" — so the human relation to nature is redefined as participatory. "Rather than being outsiders looking in on nature or antagonists opposed to nature, we become participants in the creative processes of nature": not a passive or genetically programmed participation but "a conscious involvement in the evolution of those forms and emergent behaviours that we identify with life," which "will come to constitute the new nature: Nature II." `Directly stated` Nature II is therefore continuous with `[[Emergence]]` (it is "emergent nature") and with `[[Artificial Life]]` (its substrate is our "direct involvement in the evolution of artificial life").

In the 1996 lexicon "Technoetic Aesthetics," Ascott compresses the term into a single dictionary entry that makes its mechanism explicit: "Our direct involvement in the evolution of artificial life, assisted by bio-telematic connectivity, is leading to the replacement of the old authorised nature by Nature II." `Directly stated` The same lexicon supplies a sibling term, **Paranature**, which "absorbs, recontextualises, and goes beyond nature, technologically assisting in its many inadequacies" — Nature II framed from the side of the paranormal and the technologically supplemented rather than the side of artificial life. `Directly stated` Both belong to what Ascott calls the "Post-Biological Era."

Nature II is the seed of Ascott's later "moistmedia" thesis (`[[Moistmedia]]`) and of his prediction of `[[The Rematerialization of Art]]`: the virtualising (electronic) trajectory and the materialising (biological/nano) trajectory converge in "a marriage of the immaterial and the material." It is also re-enchanting (`[[Re-enchantment]]`): by 2000 Ascott describes technology as "providing us with the tools and insights to see more deeply into its richness and fecundity, and above all to recognise its sentience and to understand how intelligence, indeed, consciousness, pervades its every living part." `Directly stated` Nature is no longer an antagonist or a fragile victim "over there" but a sentient, intelligent system of which we are a connected part. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and develops Nature II as second-order, emergent nature: the artificial returning us to a participatory, post-biological "new nature" built through bio-telematic connectivity and artificial life. Within the same vocabulary he distinguishes **Paranature** (the technological supplementation/going-beyond of nature, and the reintegration of suppressed paranormal experience). `Directly stated`

> Single-author note. Cross-author resonances are captured by link (Burnham's `[[Artificial Life]]` and `[[Cyborg Art]]`; O'Gieblyn's `[[Re-enchantment]]`; the eight-author `[[Material vs Immaterial]]` tension Ascott uniquely proposes to *reconcile*) rather than as separate author-sections here.

## Related Concepts

- [[Emergence]] — Nature II *is* "emergent nature": a bottom-up, observer-included nature whose new forms arise rather than being designed.
- [[Artificial Life]] — the substrate of Nature II; our "direct involvement in the evolution of artificial life" is what produces the new nature.
- [[Connectivism]] — the connectivist paradigm ("everything is connected, everything interacts with and affects everything else") that grounds Nature II as a participatory, second-order order.
- [[The Rematerialization of Art]] — Nature II's art-side corollary: the convergence of the immaterial (electronic) and the material (molecular/biological) trajectories.
- [[Moistmedia]] — the later naming of the dry/wet → moist convergence ("the future will be moist") that Nature II anticipates.
- [[Re-enchantment]] — Nature II is re-enchanting: nature recognized as sentient, intelligent, conscious "in its every living part," with paranormal experience reintegrated (Paranature).
- [[Cyborg Art]] — Burnham's 1968 art of cybernetic organisms, of which Ascott's bio-telematic, post-biological nature is a later development.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott does not take a side on this keystone vault tension so much as propose its *reconciliation*: Nature II is "a marriage of the immaterial and the material," a rematerialization that follows from "the immateriality of the information society." It thus stands against one-directional dematerialization (Lippard, Burnham) and against pure immateriality theses.

## Questions It Raises

- Is "second-order nature" a genuinely new ontology, or a re-description of the same world once the observer is acknowledged to be inside it?
- If the line between natural and artificial systems becomes "meaningless," does "participation in the creative processes of nature" remain meaningfully distinct from engineering and control?
- Ascott frames Nature II as re-enchantment (nature as sentient, conscious). Is this a recovery of nature or its final absorption into the technological — i.e., does Paranature "go beyond nature" by replacing it?
- How does the optimism of the participatory "new nature" sit against accounts that read pervasive bio-telematic connectivity as domination rather than communion?
