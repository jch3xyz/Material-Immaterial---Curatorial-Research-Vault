# Psychotechnology

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's 1967 claim that the post-industrial future will be carried not by technology but by **psychotechnology** — a successor that pushes past Skinner's behavioural engineering into parapsychology, ESP, astrology, out-of-body states, and "total telepathy" / "instant communications with no media."

## Longer Explanation

In the closing movement of his 1967 broadside manifesto "Behaviourables and Futuribles" (reprinted in *Control 5*, 1970), Ascott names the term that will quietly seed the rest of his life's work. After spending the manifesto recasting art as a cybernetic, feedback-driven form of behaviour, he turns to the question of what will succeed the machine age. His answer is a wager against technological determinism: "In the post-industrial society, it is not technology that will carry us through so much as psychotechnology." Where the post-industrial discourse of the late 1960s (Bell, Toffler) assumed that information machines would steer the coming culture, Ascott relocates the operative force inward, to the psyche and its (then) speculative capacities (Directly stated).

Psychotechnology is defined by what it exceeds. It "may take us beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering into the shadow lands, the futuribles, the speculative, astrological, dreamed-up, out-of-body, future behaviours." This is a pointed move within Ascott's own vocabulary: his art is *behaviourist* — he borrows the behaviourist register of stimulus, response, and conditioning — yet psychotechnology marks the point where he refuses Skinnerian behavioural engineering as a limit and reaches past it into the parapsychological. The "shadow lands" are continuous with the manifesto's earlier program — ESP, "astrology, divination by tarot, the whole psychic scene," "scenarios for the astral plane" — so psychotechnology is the conceptual name for that whole "psychic scene" reframed as the carrier of post-industrial culture (Directly stated; the parapsychological content is itself visionary/speculative).

Its horizon is a fully immaterial, mediumless communication. At the frontier of parapsychology, Ascott imagines the collapse of the technical apparatus altogether: "We may not have reached the frontiers of parapsychology, but when we do—wham! Instant communications with no media. Total telepathy, waves of alternative behaviours surging on from creative impulses of the mind." The phrase "no media" is striking in a thinker who will become a foundational *media* theorist of telematic and networked art: the deep wish of psychotechnology is for connectivity so total it dissolves its own infrastructure (Strongly implied — the aspiration to a vanishing interface recurs in the same essay: "Cybernetics will have come of age when we no longer notice the hardware, where the interface is minimal").

This passage is the **earliest documentable seed of Ascott's later technoetics and psi cluster**. The 1990s essays — on the "Shamantic Web," cyberception, the "moist" media of consciousness, and "technoetic aesthetics" — develop a full theory of mind, technology, and altered states; but the kernel is already here in 1967, embedded inside a cybernetic-art manifesto. Read forward, psychotechnology is where Ascott's cybernetics turns toward consciousness; read against the vault, it is an early art-world instance of the re-enchantment impulse that O'Gieblyn later diagnoses in technological culture (Interpretive synthesis).

## Questions It Raises
