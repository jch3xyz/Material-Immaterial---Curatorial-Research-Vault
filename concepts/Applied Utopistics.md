# Applied Utopistics

## Brief Definition

Zuboff's name (borrowed from Frank and Fritzie Manuel's profile of the modern utopianist, and from their description of Lenin as a specialist in "applied utopistics") for the surveillance capitalists as *sui generis* utopianists who translate their visions into fact directly in practice — without soldiers, and crucially without first articulating a contestable theory — thereby reversing the normal theory-then-practice sequence and foreclosing the space for critical inquiry that the theory–practice gap ordinarily opens.

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 14 ("A Utopia of Certainty") Zuboff fits the leading surveillance capitalists into the Manuels' classic profile of the most ambitious modern utopianists — tunnel vision, an early grasp of a "new state of being," obsessive pursuit of an *idée fixe*, belief in the inevitability of one's ideas, and the drive for total reformation "at the level of the species and the entire world system." But she isolates the one element where they *break* the rule. The Manuels held that the historical utopian "cannot make a world out of nothing"; his gadgets "rarely go beyond the mechanical potentialities of his age." The surveillance capitalists "can and do make such a world — a genuinely historic deviation from the norm" (`Directly stated`). With the cash, market capitalization, and instrumentarian apparatus of Microsoft, Google, and Facebook behind them, "these are the rare utopianists who can oversee the translation of their imaginations into fact without soldiers to pave the way in blood" (`Directly stated`).

This is what makes them *sui generis*. Zuboff stages the contrast through Marx and Lenin: Marx "grasped the world with his thickly articulated theory, but with only the power of his ideas, he could not implement his vision"; it took Lenin, Stalin, and Mao to apply Marxism to real life — and the Manuels describe Lenin as a specialist in "applied utopistics." The surveillance capitalists invert this. "In contrast, the surveillance capitalists seize the world in practice. Their theories are thin — at least this is true of the thinking that they share with the public. The opposite is true of their power, which is monumental and largely unimpeded" (`Directly stated`). Where classical utopianism ran theory → (failed or contested) practice, applied utopistics runs practice → (reverse-engineered, secretive) theory.

The political stakes lie in this reversal. Zuboff argues that "the usual sequence is that theory is available to inspect, interrogate, and debate before action is initiated," and that "the unavoidable gap between theory and practice creates a space for critical inquiry" — the space in which citizens test a law against a constitution and act to close the gap when it grows too wide. "The surveillance capitalists reverse the normal sequence of theory and practice. Their practices move ahead at high velocity in the absence of an explicit and contestable theory. They specialize in displays of instrumentarianism's unique brand of shock and awe, leaving onlookers dazed, uncertain, and helpless" (`Directly stated`). With no theory on the table, there is nothing to inspect, no gap to measure, and so no purchase for critique — "the only way to grasp the theory advanced in their applied utopistics is to reverse engineer their operations." Applied utopistics is thus both a description of how the dreams get built and an account of why they are so hard to resist.

In Chapter 15 ("The Instrumentarian Collective") Zuboff supplies the theory that the executives withhold by reading it off the work of B. F. Skinner and the MIT data scientist Alex Pentland, "the rare applied utopianist who … has vigorously articulated, researched, and disseminated a theory of instrumentarian society." Among the five social principles she infers, the fourth is itself titled "Applied Utopistics": "Both Skinner and Pentland believe in the authority of the utopianists to impose their plan. Instrumentarian society is a planned society, produced through total control of the means of behavioral modification. Neither Skinner's planners nor Pentland's tuners shrink from their responsibility to wield the power that shapes the superorganism" (`Directly stated`). Applied utopistics, in this register, names not only the velocity of practice over theory but the *content* of the suppressed theory: a planned society legitimated by the planners' presumed authority and realized through behavioral control (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — adapts the Manuels' term (their phrase for Lenin) to name the surveillance capitalists as *sui generis* utopianists who realize their visions in practice without armies and without a public theory, reversing the theory–practice sequence and so foreclosing critical inquiry. The withheld theory is recovered by reverse-engineering operations and by reading it off Skinner and Pentland; its substance is a planned society built on total control of [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]].

## Related Concepts

- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — the social end-state that applied utopistics builds: a frictionless, guaranteed-outcome world purged of uncertainty. (Listed here as the *vision realized*; see Opposing Concepts for the sense in which the certainty-utopia is also what Zuboff resists.)
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the operative power (tuning, herding, conditioning at scale) that lets thin theory be seized in practice; applied utopistics is what instrumentarian power does when pointed at "society."
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic logic whose frontier of behavioral-surplus extraction advances into the societal realm under the banner of applied utopistics.
- [[Inevitabilism]] — the rhetoric of foregone conclusion ("an unshakable belief in the inevitability of one's ideas coming to fruition") that both screens the missing theory and mimics utopian conviction.
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the apparatus of control through which the utopianists' plan is imposed; per Skinner and Pentland, the instrumentarian society is "produced through total control" of it.
- [[Big Other]] — the ubiquitous sensing-and-actuating architecture whose "shock and awe" displays substitute for an articulated, contestable theory.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — opposition in the normative sense: the certainty-utopia that applied utopistics constructs is precisely the foreclosed, post-political condition Zuboff argues against, because perfect predictive certainty extinguishes the freedom and futurity on which human autonomy depends. Applied utopistics is the *method*; the utopia of certainty is the *end* — and Zuboff treats both as threats, not goods. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Questions It Raises

- If a theory is never publicly articulated, can it be democratically contested at all — or does applied utopistics structurally route around politics, as Pentland's "plans replace politics" principle suggests?
- Is "reverse engineering" the operations a sufficient substitute for the inspection-and-debate the theory–practice gap normally affords, or does it always arrive too late, after the practice is entrenched?
- How does applied utopistics differ from older technocratic planning (Skinner's "planned society," Lenin's applied utopistics) — is the novelty the *absence of soldiers*, the *thinness of theory*, the *velocity*, or the privately owned means of behavioral modification?
- What grounds the "authority of the utopianists to impose their plan" once it is decoupled from any public theory or consent?
