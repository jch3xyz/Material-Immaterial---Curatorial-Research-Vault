# Achievement Society

## Brief Definition

Han's name (Leistungsgesellschaft) for the social formation in which the subject is master and entrepreneur of itself and exploits itself voluntarily because the compulsion to perform is experienced as freedom.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], the **achievement society** displaces the older [[Disciplinary Society]] analyzed by Foucault. Where the disciplinary subject was coerced from outside — by walls, supervisors, and the despotic gaze of the [[The Panopticon|Benthamian panopticon]] — the **achievement-subject** (*Leistungssubjekt*) "operates independently of external domination forcing it to work and exploiting it. One is the master and entrepreneur of oneself" (Directly stated). Domination has not disappeared; it has been internalized. The same person is now perpetrator and victim, exploiter and exploited.

Han's key claim is that this arrangement is more, not less, efficient than overt coercion. "[[Auto-Exploitation|Auto-exploitation]] proves more efficient than allo-exploitation because a feeling of freedom attends it." The subject submits to "freely willed, self-generated constraint," and that experienced freedom is precisely what makes the constraint hold. This is what Han names the [[Freedom vs Control|Dialectic of Freedom]]: freedom that "turns out to be a form of control."

In *The Transparency Society*, the category does specific work: it explains why transparency is not imposed but demanded by subjects themselves. The [[The Transparency Society|Transparency Society]] "obeys the logic of the society of achievement entirely." Just as the achievement-subject auto-exploits, it also auto-illuminates: "Utter auto illumination functions more efficiently than utter allo-illumination because it is attended by the sensation of freedom." The [[Digital Panopticon]] is built and maintained by its own inhabitants because exposure feels like self-expression rather than surveillance. The transparent customer who voluntarily denudes themselves is, on Han's reading, the "[[Homo Sacer]] of the digital panopticon" — the achievement society's logic carried into the field of visibility (Directly stated).

## The Burnout Society (Han) — the achievement society at book length (PRIMARY source)

*The Burnout Society* is the book that develops *Leistungsgesellschaft* at length, and it is the **primary source** for this concept; the Transparency Society and Psychopolitics treatments above repeatedly forward-link it. Its opening move is a historical supersession of Foucault: "Today's society is no longer Foucault's disciplinary world of hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories. It has long been replaced by another regime, namely a society of fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories. Twenty-first-century society is no longer a disciplinary society, but rather an achievement society [Leistungsgesellschaft]. Also, its inhabitants are no longer 'obedience-subjects' but 'achievement-subjects.' They are entrepreneurs of themselves" (Directly stated). The supersession is also a *modal* shift, and a productivity-driven one. The [[Disciplinary Society]] is "a society of negativity," governed by the prohibitive *May Not* and the compulsive *Should*; achievement society "is in the process of discarding negativity," and "Unlimited [[Can vs Should|Can]] is the positive modal verb of achievement society." Crucially Han grounds the switch in production, not liberation: "The positivity of Can is much more efficient than the negativity of Should. Therefore, the social unconscious switches from Should to Can." Yet — and this is decisive against any liberatory reading — "the Can does not revoke the Should": "Where increasing productivity is concerned, no break exists between Should and Can; continuity prevails." The two regimes also produce different casualties: "Its [disciplinary society's] negativity produces madmen and criminals. In contrast, achievement society creates depressives and losers" (Directly stated). Pointedly, Han rejects the [[Society of Control|control society]] as the name for the successor: "Nor does the commonly employed concept of 'control society' do justice to this change. It still contains too much negativity" (Directly stated).

The book's deepest claim, developed in "Vita Activa" (ch. 5), is an inversion of Hegel that totalizes labour. The freedom won from the master is not freedom at all: "The society of laboring and achievement is not a free society. It generates new constraints. Ultimately, the dialectic of master and slave does not yield a society where everyone is free and capable of leisure, too. Rather, it leads to a society of work in which the master himself has become a laboring slave" (Directly stated). Han pictures this as an *internal work camp*: "In this society of compulsion, everyone carries a work camp inside. This labor camp is defined by the fact that one is simultaneously prisoner and guard, victim and perpetrator. One exploits oneself. It means that exploitation is possible even without domination." This is the figure that *Psychopolitics* will later sharpen into the "absolute slave" (see below) and the *totalization of labour*. The same totalization is what makes "Vita Activa" rewrite Arendt: contemporary "labor society, as a society of achievement and business, fosters individuality [Die Arbeitsgesellschaft hat sich individualisiert zur Leistungs- und Aktivgesellschaft]," so the late-modern *animal laborans* "is hyperactive and hyperneurotic," not the passive species-being Arendt described (Directly stated).

Chapter 9 ("Burnout Society") names the result and the intensification. Auto-exploitation is the engine: "Achievement society is the society of self-exploitation. The achievement-subject exploits itself until it burns out" (Directly stated). The achievement-subject is "simultaneously perpetrator and victim, master and slave"; "the achievement-subject that understands itself as its own master, as *homo liber*, turns out to be *homo sacer*" — placed not under a sovereign injunction but under "the injunction to achieve [Bann der Leistung]." This is the same [[Homo Sacer]]-of-the-digital-panopticon figure the Transparency Society material above tracked, now grounded in its source text. Finally, Han traces the achievement society's own internal evolution into a **[[Doping Society]]** — the *society of activeness* pushed to chemical intensification: "The inner logic of achievement society dictates its evolution into a doping society. Life reduced to bare, vital functioning is life to be kept healthy unconditionally. Health is the new goddess." The achievement-subject, stripped of all narrativity and reduced to bare vital functioning, is finally neither killable nor livable: "They are too alive to die, and too dead to live" (Directly stated). The terminal pathology this whole formation produces — voluntary self-exploitation overheating into collapse — is what the realized note [[Burnout]] develops.

## Psychopolitics (Han) — the achievement-subject as absolute slave

*Psychopolitics* sharpens the category that *The Transparency Society* introduced. Where the earlier book established that the achievement-subject "is the master and entrepreneur of oneself," *Psychopolitics* presses the master-and-slave figure to its limit and inverts Hegel: "Although the achievement-subject deems itself free, in reality it is a slave. In so far as it willingly exploits itself without a master, it is an absolute slave" (Directly stated). The point is not that domination has merely gone internal but that it has become total. The Hegelian slave at least labored toward freedom; "counter to what Hegel assumed, labouring does not make the slave free. The slave remains enslaved to labour. Now, the slave is forcing the master to work too." Han names the result the *totalization of labour*: "Today's dialectic of master and slave means the totalization of labour" (Directly stated). The achievement-subject lacks even the sovereignty Hegel granted the master, who "performs no labour at all and only enjoys" — it is left "absolutizing bare life and labour" while believing itself free.

This is the same inversion the original note tracked in the field of visibility, now read as a [[Freedom vs Control|crisis of freedom]] in its own right: "freedom itself is bringing forth compulsion and constraint," because "the freedom of *Can* generates even more coercion than the disciplinarian *Should*" — *Should* has a limit, *Can* has none, so its compulsion is unlimited (Directly stated). The achievement-subject experiences this unlimited *Can* as endless self-optimization, and "Foucault's Dilemma" specifies why Foucault could not see it: he "did not see that the neoliberal regime utterly claims the technology of the self for its own purposes," such that "self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, fall into one" (Directly stated). What looks like a [[Auto-Exploitation|self made into a work of art]] is "a beautiful but deceptive illusion that the neoliberal regime maintains in order to exhaust its resources entirely."

"Healing as Killing" supplies the terminus the original note anticipated when it forward-linked exhaustion and the *Burnout Society*: the achievement-subject does not so much resist as collapse. "The neoliberal subject is running aground on the imperative of self-optimization, that is, on the compulsion always to achieve more and more" (Directly stated). Because perpetual self-optimization "coincides point-for-point with the optimization of the system" and tolerates no [[Negativity|negativity]] — "the only pain that is tolerated is pain that can be exploited for the purposes of optimization" — it ends in "mental collapse," in [[Burnout|depression and burnout]]: "Healing, it turns out, means killing" (Directly stated). The "outside" the original note asked after is not liberation but breakdown.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Coins/uses *Leistungsgesellschaft* as a cross-corpus signature category. In *[[The Burnout Society]]* (the **primary source**) it grounds the historical supersession of [[Disciplinary Society|Foucault's disciplinary society]] (fitness studios/office towers vs prisons/factories; obedience-subject→achievement-subject), the *Should*→*Can* modal shift driven by productivity, the master/slave inversion (the *totalization of labour*; the internal work camp), and the evolution into a [[Doping Society]] — terminating in [[Burnout]]. In *The Transparency Society* the category grounds the claim that surveillance is auto-administered. In *Psychopolitics* the same category is radicalized into the figure of the "absolute slave" who exploits himself without a master (Hegel inverted), and "Healing as Killing" delivers the exhaustion/burnout terminus.
- **[[Guy Debord]]** — Speculative connection. The [[The Society of the Spectacle]] anticipates a regime where subjects participate in their own subordination through images, though Debord does not yet thematize self-entrepreneurship as the structural form.
- **[[Herbert Marcuse]]** — Speculative connection. *Eros and Civilization*'s account of "surplus repression" and the performance principle could be read as a precursor to Han's *Leistung*, though Marcuse still presumes an external dominating apparatus.

## Related Concepts

- [[Burnout]] — the achievement society's title malady; the terminal pathology of voluntary self-exploitation that *The Burnout Society* develops at length.
- [[Doping Society]] — the achievement society's own internal evolution; "the inner logic of achievement society dictates its evolution into a doping society."
- [[Can vs Should]] — the modal shift that distinguishes the achievement society from the disciplinary society; "Unlimited Can is the positive modal verb of achievement society."
- [[Freedom vs Control|Dialectic of Freedom]] — the formal structure by which freedom collapses into control.
- [[The Transparency Society]] — the visibility-side analogue of the achievement society; same logic applied to exposure.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — the institutional form of the achievement society's surveillance regime, voluntarily inhabited.
- [[Society of Control]] — the broader Deleuzean horizon Han is rewriting; the achievement society specifies its subjective form.
- [[Homo Sacer]] — figure Han borrows to name the transparent customer produced by achievement-society logic.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Disciplinary Society]] — the historical predecessor; coercion is external, walls and supervisors visible, "occupants of the Benthamian panopticon are aware of the supervisor's constant presence, [whereas] the inhabitants of the digital panopticon think that they are free" (Directly stated).
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — exploitation by another; the inefficient form that auto-exploitation supersedes.

## Questions It Raises

- If the achievement-subject's freedom is the very medium of its exploitation, what would resistance look like that did not simply re-perform self-mastery?
- Does Han's category presuppose a prior, non-achievement self that could in principle refuse — or is the achievement-subject coextensive with contemporary subjectivity as such?
- How does *Leistungsgesellschaft* relate to the older Marxist concept of [[Alienation]]? Is auto-exploitation a deepening of alienation or its dissolution into self-identification?
- What is the relation between achievement and exhaustion developed in *[[The Burnout Society]]* (→ [[Burnout]]), and is exhaustion the achievement society's only "outside"? Han's answer in ch. 9 is bleak: the formation evolves not toward an outside but inward, into a [[Doping Society]] of bare, "undead" vital functioning.
