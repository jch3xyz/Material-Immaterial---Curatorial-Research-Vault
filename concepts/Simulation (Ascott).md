# Simulation (Ascott)

> **Disambiguation:** This is Roy Ascott's *aesthetic* sense of simulation — the mode of telematic art, defined by opposition to representation. It is DISTINCT from [[The Simulation Hypothesis]] (O'Gieblyn), the cosmological thesis that reality itself is a computer simulation. Ascott is not claiming the world is unreal; he is naming how networked art works.

## Brief Definition

For Roy Ascott, **simulation** is the aesthetic mode of telematic art — it reproduces "the unstable, chaotic, fleeting, and numinous quality of its subject" and renders "what is invisible visible," substituting negotiation, participation, and collaboration for the assertion of representation.

## Longer Explanation

Ascott builds the concept entirely by opposition. **Representation**, in his account, is the mode of "the old order": it "makes demands on art as object and on a fixed separation of creator and observer," it "demands coherence in space and time," and it "produces an art of assertion… in which the observing subject must be essentially passive." Worse, "in its insistence on clarity and coherence, representation always masks the inherent instability, the chaos, of process in the world." Representation, for Ascott, is a lie of fixity told about a world that is actually flux. (`Directly stated`.)

**Simulation** is the opposite move. Rather than asserting a stable image, telematic art "rejects representation in favour of simulation," "seeking to simulate in its digital space-time the unstable, chaotic, fleeting, and numinous quality of its subject." What it gives up is assertion; what it gains is "negotiation, participation, collaboration" — the active, two-way involvement of a user who is, in Ascott's formula elsewhere, neither autonomous creator nor passive viewer. Simulation is thus not a copy of an appearance but a re-enactment of a *process*: it stages indeterminacy, uncertainty, and flow instead of suppressing them. (`Directly stated`.)

The second defining clause is revelatory rather than mimetic: telematic art "uses simulation to render what is invisible visible, to bring the virtual, the potential, the unseen, and unrealised into view." Where representation re-presents an already-given visible object, simulation discloses the connections, fields, and forces that "lie outside the rather gross level of material perception afforded by our natural senses." This is why Ascott can say telematic art moves "beyond object art and time-based art": the work is no longer a discrete object to be perceived, but a generative system that brings potentials into appearance — the [[Virtuality|virtual]] made actual within "digital space-time." (`Directly stated` for the clause; `Interpretive synthesis` for the link to the immaterial/object→system spine.)

Ascott anchors this aesthetic mode in the new physics — the many-worlds interpretation, the observer who creates reality by observation — so that simulation becomes the art-form proper to a chaotic, indeterminate, observer-dependent cosmos. Against "the deconstructive pessimism of late postmodern art," he frames simulation as a *constructive* rather than nihilistic gesture: not "representations of representation" turned in on themselves, but a "radically constructive art" that opens "the ceaselessly bifurcating semantic pathways of virtuality and simulation." (`Strongly implied` — the constructive framing is explicit; the cosmological grounding is developed across the chapter.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** (*Telematic Embrace*, ch. 19, 1990) — simulation is the aesthetic mode of telematic art, defined strictly against representation: it simulates chaotic/numinous *process* and renders the invisible visible, replacing assertion with negotiation and participation. An affirmative, constructive, world-disclosing concept.
- **Meghan O'Gieblyn** — uses "simulation" in the entirely different, cosmological sense of [[The Simulation Hypothesis]] (the Bostrom-style claim that reality is itself a computed simulation). The two share a word, not a concept; see the disambiguation note above. (`Directly stated` for the distinction.)

## Questions It Raises

- If simulation "renders what is invisible visible," is it disclosure (revealing a real but unseen order) or production (constructing a reality that did not exist before)? Ascott's reliance on the observer-creates-reality physics blurs the line.
- Does simulating "the unstable, chaotic, fleeting" risk dissolving the artwork into pure process — and if so, what, if anything, remains for a viewer to encounter that representation provided?
- How far does Ascott's affirmative "simulation" stand from Baudrillardian simulation (the simulacrum that has no referent)? Ascott's is constructive and world-disclosing rather than referent-erasing — a contrast worth tracing.
- Is the opposition to representation total, or does simulation smuggle representational elements back in (the images, sound, and text the user still constructs)?
