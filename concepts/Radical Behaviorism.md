# Radical Behaviorism

## Brief Definition

The science of behavior championed by B. F. Skinner — inherited from John B. Watson, reformed via Max Meyer and Max Planck — that restricts itself to observable operant behavior, purges all reference to inwardness, treats freedom as a synonym for ignorance, and aims at the prediction and control of behavior through what Skinner called a "technology of behavior." Zuboff identifies it as the intellectual point of origin for [[Instrumentarian Power]].

## Longer Explanation

In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, Shoshana Zuboff traces the deep history of instrumentarian power not to twentieth-century totalitarianism but to the intellectual domain "that came to be known as 'radical behaviorism,' most notably championed by B. F. Skinner and his dream of a 'technology of behavior.'" Where totalitarian power sought to possess the human soul through terror, radical behaviorism dissolves the soul altogether: it brackets inwardness as scientifically meaningless and takes the only legitimate object of study to be observable action — "what an organism is doing—or more accurately what it is observed by another organism to be doing." Skinner called the observable doing "operant" behavior, and he called the program "radical" precisely because its vocabulary "was to be purged of inwardness: an organism cannot be said to 'see' but rather to 'look toward.'" (Directly stated.)

Zuboff reconstructs the program's lineage. Watson announced the behaviorist viewpoint in 1913 — psychology as "a purely objective experimental branch of natural science" whose "theoretical goal is the prediction and control of behavior" — but Skinner blamed Watson's showmanship and "shortage of facts" for the field's disrepute. The repair came through Max Meyer, whose dissertation advisor was the physicist Max Planck. Planck "insisted on the unity of the physical world and the discoverability of the natural laws… including the laws of human behavior," and Meyer carried this into psychology: study "only those facts which can be objectively observed in the behavior of one person." Zuboff calls this stance "the viewpoint of the Other-One" — the human regarded from the outside as an organism among organisms, in whom "the world within the skin of the Other-One loses its preferred status." (Directly stated.)

The decisive move is the equation of freedom with ignorance. Meyer held that "freedom of action in the animal world signifies the same that is meant by accidents in the world of physics" — that is, a phenomenon whose causes are simply not yet known. Skinner pressed the same claim across six decades, arguing in *Beyond Freedom & Dignity* that "knowledge does not make us free but rather releases us from the illusion of freedom." What we experience as free will is, in his student Richard Herrnstein's phrase, only "the vortex of stimuli" that produced an action and "cannot yet be adequately specified." This is the figure Zuboff develops as the **vortex of stimuli**: ignorance is "a melting iceberg," destined to "yield to the mounting heat as we invent the means and methods smart enough to first decipher and then control" it. (Directly stated.)

From this conviction follows the program's practical demand — Zuboff's **a technology of behavior** figure. In the opening pages of *Beyond Freedom & Dignity* Skinner declared that solving humanity's ills requires "a technology of behavior… comparable in power and precision to physical and biological technology." Zuboff's central historical claim is that Skinner could imagine this apparatus but could not build it: "It was Skinner's hard destiny to foresee the possibility of instrumentarian power and its operationalization in [[Big Other]] before the instruments existed to fulfill that vision." Surveillance capitalism, on her account, supplies precisely the missing "instruments and methods" — the ubiquitous digital apparatus that observes, computes, and "automatically reinforced behavior" — and thereby finally operationalizes Skinner's program, though now in the service of "others' wealth and power" rather than the universal harmony Skinner imagined. The program's aim, restated as [[A Utopia of Certainty]], "amounts to an all-out war on accidents, mistakes, and randomness in general." (Directly stated for the operationalization claim; the service-of-profit inflection is Directly stated.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — treats radical behaviorism as the intellectual prehistory of [[Instrumentarian Power]] and [[Big Other]]. She is a critic, not an adherent: she reports Skinner's program faithfully (drawing on her own graduate-school debates with him at Harvard) in order to show that surveillance capitalism has resurrected its "viewpoint of the Other-One" as a profit-seeking [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|means of behavioral modification]], substituting "the engineering of behavior" for "the engineering of souls."
- **B. F. Skinner** (reported by Zuboff) — the program's chief champion; coined "operant" behavior and called for a "technology of behavior." Held that freedom and ignorance are synonyms and that privacy norms would inevitably fall to "technical advances." His utopian novel *Walden Two* (1948) is the "after" case to Orwell's *1984* — the antidote rather than the nightmare, a "utopia of technique."
- **Max Meyer** (reported by Zuboff) — supplied the epistemological foundation in *Psychology of the Other-One* (1921): study only objectively observable behavior; treat freedom as the name for unexplained causes.
- **Max Planck** (reported by Zuboff) — the physicist whose insistence on universal natural law, "including the laws of human behavior," Meyer carried into psychology. Zuboff repeatedly names "Planck, Meyer, and Skinner" as the joint behavioral lineage.
- **John B. Watson** (reported by Zuboff) — announced the behaviorist viewpoint in 1913 (prediction and control as the goal; no dividing line "between man and brute"); Skinner credited him as founder but blamed his excesses.

## Related Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the species of power for which radical behaviorism is, in Zuboff's account, the direct intellectual ancestor.
- [[Big Other]] — the digital apparatus that "operationaliz[es]" Skinner's vision; it "encodes the viewpoint of the Other-One as a global presence."
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the instrumental fulfillment of Skinner's call for a technology able to predict and shape behavior.
- [[Economies of Action]] — the actuation tier (tuning, herding, conditioning) that realizes operant reinforcement at scale.
- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — Skinner's *Walden Two* as the template for instrumentarian society's "war on accidents."
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the raw material extracted by the apparatus Skinner could only dream of.
- [[Society of Control]] — Deleuze's modulatory power, a parallel diagnosis of distributed behavioral control.
- [[Dataism]] — the worldview (Han, Harari) that, like behaviorism, reduces the human to processable behavioral data.
- [[Surveillance]] — the practice through which the behaviorist viewpoint is enacted on populations.
- [[Quantified Self]] — the self rendered as observable, measurable behavior, the behaviorist's object made consumer practice.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the will to will, autonomy, and authorship of one's own action that radical behaviorism declares an illusion ("freedom is ignorance").
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — radical behaviorism resolves the tension wholly on the determinist side: free will is a "lacuna of ignorance" awaiting explanation.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's voluntary-exposure account names the same surveillant condition behaviorism rationalizes, but locates the coercion in self-exploitation rather than external reinforcement.

## Questions It Raises

- If "freedom is ignorance," is the disappearance of the unpredictable in human conduct a scientific advance or the extinction of agency? Zuboff's answer is the latter; Skinner held the former.
- What changes when Skinner's universal, ostensibly disinterested behavioral utopia is privatized — when "the knowledge that now displaces our freedom is proprietary"?
- Is the "viewpoint of the Other-One" a methodological choice that can be confined to the laboratory, or, once equipped with [[Big Other]], an ontology that reconstitutes the human as "an organism among organisms"?
- Skinner counseled that observation should be "unobtrusive, ideally remaining outside the awareness of the organism." How far does this anticipate surveillance capitalism's design imperative to bypass awareness?
