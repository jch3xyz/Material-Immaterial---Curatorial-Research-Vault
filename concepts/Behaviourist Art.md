# Behaviourist Art

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's keystone term for the art of his 1960s cybernetic phase: art understood not as object but as behaviour — an open-ended, feedback-driven process that the participating spectator completes, in which the artist sets a context but the work's specific evolution is unpredictable.

## Longer Explanation

For Roy Ascott, the decisive event of modern art is a migration of its very field: "The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour, and its function has become less descriptive and more purposive" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/07 Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision]]). Where the "deterministic aesthetic" of the past transmitted a "clearly defined message to the spectator, as a more or less passive receptor," behaviourist art *initiates events*. Ascott proposes "behavioural" as a more inclusive replacement for the word "visual," because the new art is constituted not by what is seen on a surface but by the conduct of a whole triad — artist, artwork, and observer — bound into one loop. *Directly stated.*

The animating principle is feedback. "The participational, inclusive form of art has as its basic principle 'feedback,' and it is this loop that makes an integral whole of the triad artist/artwork / observer." Because "the effort to establish a final resolution must come from the observer," the artwork "exists in a perpetual state of transition." Ascott figures this through the metaphor of the game: in the past "the artist played to win"; "Nowadays, art is moving towards a situation in which the game is never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play. While the general context of the art experience is set by the artist, its evolution in any specific sense is unpredictable and dependent on the total involvement of the spectator" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/07 Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision]]). The artist designs the limits; the spectator's participation supplies the outcome. This is the participatory completion of the work that also grounds Ascott's later restatement in "Table" (1975): "only the intervention of observers can generate meaning and value. Art, then, resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/10 Table]]). *Directly stated.*

The concept's earliest documentable form is Ascott's own studio practice: the **Change Painting** and kinetic constructions of c.1959–64, made of interchangeable transparent panels and hinged reliefs the spectator manipulates. In "The Construction of Change" (1964) Ascott describes structures "in which the relationships of parts are not fixed and may be changed by the intervention of the spectator," so that "the participant becomes responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning. He becomes a decision maker in the symbolic world that confronts him" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/05 The Construction of Change]]). The work "occupies a pivotal point between two sets of behaviour, the artist's and the spectator's. It is essentially a matrix, the substance between." By 1967 Ascott had compressed the position into a manifesto vocabulary — folding the sub-term **behaviourable** (the art object read as behaviour in its history: "a behaviourable in its history, a futurible in its structure, a trigger in its effect") into the larger claim that "We recognise process at the human level as behaviour, and we are beginning to understand art now as being essentially behaviourist" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/08 Behaviourables and Futuribles]]). The manifesto ends on the bare credo: "Art is now a form of behaviour. Message ends." *Directly stated.*

Within the vault's art/systems cluster, behaviourist art is the **participatory pole** of 1960s cybernetic art, complementary to — and predating — [[Systems Esthetics|Jack Burnham's]] object-to-system [[Systems Esthetics]] (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*, 1968). Ascott (1964–67) locates the system in the behavioural loop between artist, work, and participant; Burnham (1968) locates it in the autonomous art object's internal migration from [[Object and System|object to system]]. Both put process before object, and both converge on [[Reciprocal Communication]] and [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] as the criterion of the new art — but Ascott reaches it from the side of participation and the spectator rather than from sculpture's formal history. The two are best read as the two poles of one moment. *Interpretive synthesis.*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coiner and primary theorist. Behaviourist art is art as behaviour: an open feedback system completed by the participating spectator, whose "evolution in any specific sense is unpredictable." Sub-terms folded here: the **behaviourable** (the artwork read as behaviour-in-its-history) and the 1964 **Change Painting** practice (the spectator-alterable construction that is its keystone artwork). Notably, Ascott holds that behaviourist art "need not mean the end of the object" but gives it "new values" — he revalues rather than abolishes the object, a nuance that distinguishes him from a pure dematerialization reading.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (parallel, not a variant) — Burnham's contemporaneous [[Systems Esthetics]] reaches the same object-to-system conclusion from the side of the sculptural object; Ascott is the earlier, participatory/behavioural pole. See [[Reciprocal Communication]] (Burnham's one-way-stimulation → two-way-communication criterion) for the shared feedback ground.

## Related Concepts

- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the criterion behaviourist art operationalizes: the artist/artwork/observer triad as a sustained two-way loop rather than a one-way message. Ascott (1966) is the participatory precursor/complement to Burnham's account.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the mechanism: "its basic principle 'feedback,' and it is this loop that makes an integral whole of the triad."
- [[Object and System]] — the object-to-system / process-over-product move that behaviourist art performs from the participatory side ("system supersedes structure").
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's object-side twin; behaviourist art is its complementary participatory pole within 1960s cybernetic art.
- [[New Media Art]] — behaviourist/cybernetic art is the participatory genealogy this concept descends into (extended by Ascott).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — behaviourist art makes the artwork an experiential, feedback-driven technology the spectator completes rather than an object contemplated.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the framing science: Ascott's 1964 call that "the artist must turn to cybernetics" is the discipline behind behaviourist art.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object vs System]] — behaviourist art is the system/behaviour pole against the object pole; "art resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system."
- The "deterministic aesthetic" of one-way message-transmission to a "passive receptor" — the closed, finished, artist-dominated work behaviourist art replaces with open, unresolved play. (Captured as plain text; Ascott's master opposition of deterministic vs cybernetic vision.)

## Questions It Raises

- If "the game is never won" and the work's evolution is "unpredictable and dependent on the total involvement of the spectator," in what sense is there still an authored *work* — and where does the artist's responsibility end and the participant's begin?
- Ascott insists behaviourist art "need not mean the end of the object." How does his revaluation of the object differ from the contemporaneous dematerialization of the art object (Lippard) and from Burnham's object-to-system thesis?
- Behaviourist art borrows the *behaviourist* register but Ascott pushes "beyond Skinner's behavioural engineering." Is the spectator here genuinely free, or is participation a context the artist controls — and how does that bear on the surveillance/control reading of feedback systems in the vault's later (Han, Harari) cluster?
