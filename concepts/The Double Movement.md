# The Double Movement

## Brief Definition

Karl Polanyi's name (carried by Zuboff) for the countervailing network of laws, policies, and institutions that "tether" the market form to society — balancing, moderating, and mitigating its destructive excesses — whose deliberate "demolition" under neoliberalism released raw, untethered capitalism.

## Longer Explanation

`The Double Movement (Polanyi via Zuboff)` — The double movement is the historian Karl Polanyi's account of how a self-regulating market is held in check. Polanyi concluded that "the operations of a self-regulating market are profoundly destructive when allowed to run free of countervailing laws and policies," and named the corrective response the *double movement*: "a network of measures and policies… integrated into powerful institutions designed to check the action of the market relative to labor, land, and money." The market expands; society, in a second movement, throws up institutions that contain it. Zuboff adopts Polanyi's term wholesale, summarizing it as the force that "supports the market form while tethering it to society: balancing, moderating, and mitigating its destructive excesses." `Directly stated`

In Zuboff's setting of the stage for surveillance capitalism, the double movement is a concrete historical arc, not an abstraction. Such countermeasures "emerged spontaneously in every European society during the second half of the nineteenth century," each constructing legislative, regulatory, and institutional solutions over contested arenas — workers' compensation, factory inspection, food safety, child labor. In the United States the double movement "was achieved through decades of social contest that harnessed industrial production, however imperfectly, to society's needs": the Progressive Era's trust busting, the New Deal and post-war Keynesian institutionalization, and the civil-rights and environmental legislation of the Great Society. Many scholars credit these countermeasures with the success of market democracy itself. `Directly stated`

The chapter's central historical claim is that this tethering was then deliberately dismantled. "The double movement was scheduled for demolition under the neoliberal flag, and implementation began immediately." Zuboff dates the beginning to 1976 and Carter-era deregulation — a "ripple" that "turned into 'a tidal wave that washed away controls from large segments of the economy in the last two decades of the twentieth century'" — and traces it through the Reagan and Thatcher eras and beyond. The collapse of the double movement is what cleared the ground; surveillance capitalism, on this reading, is what grew in the absence of countervailing institutions. `Directly stated`

This grounds the chapter's "capitalism should not be eaten raw" argument, which Zuboff draws from Thomas Piketty: "Capitalism, like sausage, is meant to be cooked by a democratic society and its institutions because raw capitalism is antisocial." The double movement is precisely the cooking — the democratic institutional work that renders capitalism social. Its demolition under neoliberalism is therefore the release of *raw* capitalism, of which surveillance capitalism is the contemporary, mutant form. Zuboff closes the thought forward into the present: "We have yet to invent the politics and new forms of collaborative action—this century's equivalent of the social movements of the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries that aimed to tether raw capitalism to society." A new double movement, in other words, has not yet been built. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — adopts Polanyi's term as the historical hinge of her account: the double movement is the institutional tethering of the market to society, its neoliberal "demolition" is what released raw capitalism, and the missing twenty-first-century double movement is what surveillance capitalism exploits. She fuses it with Piketty's "capitalism should not be eaten raw" to argue that surveillance capitalism is undomesticated capitalism let loose by the failure to re-tether.
- **Karl Polanyi** (via Zuboff, not yet ingested) — the originating source. In *The Great Transformation* the double movement names the spontaneous countermovement by which society protects itself (labor, land, money) from the self-regulating market. Zuboff quotes and paraphrases him directly; Polanyi himself remains a forward figure in the vault.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the mutant form of raw capitalism that flourishes precisely where the double movement has been demolished; this note supplies the historical "stage-setting" for that master concept.
- [[Inevitabilism]] — the ideology that markets and technological development cannot be checked; functions as the rhetorical solvent of any new double movement.
- [[Dictatorship of Capital]] — Han's name for capital freed of countervailing limits; resonates with Zuboff's "raw"/demolished-double-movement diagnosis.
- [[Capital as Religion]] — capital as a cult without atonement; the absence of an external check parallels the missing double movement.
- [[Society of Control]] — Deleuze's modulating successor to disciplinary enclosure; a companion diagnosis of post-institutional, untethered power.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the form of power surveillance capitalism wields once the double movement is gone; where the double movement tethers the market *to* society, instrumentarian power restructures society *for* the market.

## Questions It Raises

- If the historical double movements were "harnessed industrial production… to society's needs," what would a double movement adequate to *behavioral* production — to surveillance capitalism rather than industrial capitalism — even look like?
- Is the absence of a new double movement a contingent political failure (Zuboff's hopeful reading) or a structural feature of [[Inevitabilism]] and the [[Society of Control]] that makes re-tethering categorically harder than it was in 1900?
- Does the "capitalism should not be eaten raw" metaphor concede that capitalism *as such* is acceptable once "cooked," or does surveillance capitalism's novelty (claiming human experience itself as raw material) exceed anything a double movement could domesticate?
