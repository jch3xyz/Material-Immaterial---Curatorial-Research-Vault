# Double Consciousness

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's term for "the state of being that gives access, at one and the same time, to two distinctly different fields of experience" — paradigmatically the shamanic trance and the virtual-reality user, who each inhabit a second world (psychic space, cyberspace) without leaving the material one.

## Longer Explanation

Ascott introduces *double consciousness* in "Weaving the Shamantic Web" (ch. 30) as the shared phenomenology that links his two "technologies of consciousness": the digital/telematic (virtual reality, hypermedia, the Net) and the ancient "plant technology" of shamanism (the Amazonian brew *ayahuasca*, the "vine of the soul"). Reporting first-person fieldwork at both poles — VR labs in Seattle and Chapel Hill, and the Kuikuru *pagés* (shamans) of the Mato Grosso — he finds the same structure in each: a being who is simultaneously present in two fields of experience. His definition is given verbatim: "By 'double consciousness,' I mean the state of being that gives access, at one and the same time, to two distinctly different fields of experience." (Directly stated.)

The decisive move is the *parallel* between the two technologies. The shamanic version is modeled on the trance; "In post-biological terms, this is mirrored by our ability, aided by computer technology, to move effortlessly through the infinities of cyberspace, while at the same time accommodating ourselves within the structures of the material world." (Directly stated.) Ascott likens this to the Sufi injunction "to be in the world but not of the world." Double consciousness is the perceptual-existential counterpart of [[Cyberception]] (technologically augmented perception) and of its shamanic analogue, *psi-perception*; it is paired with [[The Double Gaze]], "seeing at once both inward realities and the outward surfaces of the world," and Ascott states plainly that "The double gaze and double consciousness are related." (Directly stated.)

The ch. 32 glossary entry refines the concept's two-field structure into a set of explicit pairings: "Double consciousness The state of being that gives access, at one and the same time, to two distinctly different fields of experience: psychic space and cyberspace, the material world and the virtual, in an artwork and outside of it." (Directly stated.) This makes clear that double consciousness is not only about VR-vs-physical but also about the participant's reflexive position inside and outside the work of art — a refinement of Ascott's long-running participation thesis.

In the "Moist Manifesto" (ch. 31), double consciousness is carried forward into the [[Moistmedia]] ecology and given a constructive, world-making role: "Our double consciousness simultaneously constructs and navigates a world that is at once virtual and actual: a technoetic fabric of minds, set in a moist ecology. We are at home in this indeterminacy." (Directly stated.) Here the two fields are no longer merely co-inhabited but actively woven together; double consciousness becomes the cognitive condition of the moist, technoetic subject, and Ascott embraces the resulting indeterminacy rather than treating it as a loss of grounding. (Interpretive synthesis — the "at home in indeterminacy" valuation is Ascott's, but its framing as a *positive* condition versus the anxiety of split selfhood is the synthesis.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and develops the term across his late "technoetic" phase. Double consciousness is the bridge between the shamanic trance (the *pagé* under ayahuasca) and the VR/telematic user; in ch. 30 it is modeled on the trance and mirrored by the computer; in ch. 32 it is glossed as paired access to psychic space and cyberspace, the material and the virtual, the inside and outside of an artwork; in ch. 31 it becomes the constructive, navigating consciousness of the moist ecology. Note: Ascott's usage is unrelated to W. E. B. Du Bois's earlier sociological term of the same name; the vault note records only Ascott's sense.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Double Gaze]] — the perceptual correlate ("seeing at once both inward realities and the outward surfaces of the world"); Ascott states the two are related.
- [[Cyberception]] — the technologically augmented faculty that, with its shamanic counterpart *psi-perception*, makes the double-field experience possible.
- [[The Shamantic Web]] — the bio-telematic art practice (shamanic + semantic) within which double consciousness operates.
- [[Technoetics]] — the broader "technology plus mind" aesthetic; double consciousness is one of its signature states.
- [[Moistmedia]] — the dry/wet/moist ecology in which double consciousness "constructs and navigates" its dual world.
- [[Full-Immersion Virtual Reality]] — the technological pole of the parallel (the VR user simultaneously in cyberspace and the material world).
- [[Virtuality]] — the field of the virtual that double consciousness inhabits alongside the actual.
- [[The Post-Biological Era]] — the framing epoch in which double consciousness is generalized as the human condition.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the unified, self-coincident, single-field self that double consciousness (and Ascott's "constructively schizophrenic" cyberself, "I connect, therefore I am multiple") supersedes; double consciousness presupposes a divided, distributed subject rather than an integral one. (Interpretive synthesis — the opposition is drawn across the vault's Hayles/Haraway cluster, not asserted by Ascott in these chapters.)

## Questions It Raises

- Is double consciousness genuinely novel to telematic and psychedelic experience, or a heightening of an everyday cognitive split (attention, imagination, reading) that art merely foregrounds?
- Ascott treats the trance and the VR session as structurally equivalent technologies of consciousness — does the parallel hold, or does it flatten a sharp difference between a pharmacological alteration of the brain and an external sensory interface?
- If "we are at home in this indeterminacy," what becomes of the grounding the material world once provided — and is the affirmation of indeterminacy a freedom or a new vulnerability (cf. the surveillance and control readings of pervasive connectivity)?
- How does double consciousness relate to the disembodiment Hayles warns against — is the "second field" a genuine extension of embodiment or a flight from it?
