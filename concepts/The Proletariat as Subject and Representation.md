# The Proletariat as Subject and Representation

## Brief Definition

The dialectic in which the proletariat is the potential *subject* of a conscious history — the self-producing "class of consciousness" — yet in its historical defeat became a mere *representation*, a working class alienated into its own externalized image, whose parties, unions, and states came to speak for it and finally against it.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Guy Debord]] the proletariat is the only pretender to what Marx called the "subject of history." History, once real, "has no object distinct from what it creates from out of itself"; its subject "can be nothing other than the self production of the living-living people becoming masters and possessors of their own historical world and of their own fully conscious adventures" (thesis 74). The bourgeoisie won because it was "the class of the developing economy," but the proletariat "cannot embody its own new form of power except by becoming the class of consciousness" (thesis 88). Where the bourgeois revolution could ride the automatic development of productive forces, the proletarian project has no automatic economic guarantee: it must be a *conscious* appropriation of history or nothing. This is the "subject" pole — the proletariat as the agent that could finally supersede all [[Separation]] and realize the conscious history Marx envisioned.

The chapter's central movement is the collapse of that subject into its own *representation*. Because the organizational question was "the weakest aspect of radical theory," the forms the workers movement built to liberate itself — parties, unions, states — hardened into a separate power standing over the class and speaking in its name. Debord dates the inflection precisely: the moment Bolshevism triumphed in Russia and social democracy fought for the old order marks "the state of affairs that is at the heart of the modern spectacle's domination: the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class" (thesis 100). Rosa Luxemburg's observation that the old order's troops now fight "under the banner of a 'social-democratic party'" lets Debord name the historic secret of the new conditions: "The revolutionary representation of the prole tariat had at this stage become both the primary cause and the central result of the general falsification of society" (thesis 101). The proletariat's own power, externalized, returns as its enemy — the same reversal Debord names, in the book at large, the [[Spectacle]].

The Bolshevik Party is Debord's paradigm case of representation devouring subject. "By seizing a state monopoly as sole representative and defender of working-class power, the Bolshevik Party justified itself and became what it already was: the party of the owners of the proletariat, a party ownership that essentially eliminated earlier forms of property" (thesis 102). The proletariat could not maintain the power of the soviets against "its own representation which had become externalized and alienated in the form of a working-class party" (thesis 103). This externalization is a species of [[Reification]] and [[Spectacular Alienation]]: the class's living activity congeals into a thing — a party, a state, an ideology — that then dominates it. Debord extends the diagnosis to the industrial proletariat of the advanced countries, which "when the proletariat discovers that its own externalized power contributes to the constant re inforcement of capitalist society... in the form of the labor unions, political parties, and state powers that it had created in the effort to liberate itself," must become "the class that must totally oppose all rigidified externalizations and all specializations of power" (thesis 114).

The resolution Debord proposes is the recovery of the subject *against* every form of representation. Revolutionary organization "cannot claim to represent the working class"; its task "is to embody a radical separation from the world of separation" (thesis 119) and, at the revolutionary moment, "the organization must dissolve itself as a separate organization" (thesis 120). Only in the workers councils — where "active direct communication is realized, marking the end of specialization, hierarchy and separation" — does "the proletarian movement become its own product," a product that "is nothing other than the producers themselves" (theses 116–117). The subject reclaims itself by abolishing its representation; anything short of that leaves the class trapped in the spectacular image of its own emancipation.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — the concept is the title and spine of Chapter 4 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*. Debord fuses Marx's "subject of history" with his own theory of the [[Spectacle]]: the proletariat's defeat is not merely military but *representational* — the class survives as an image that its own institutions administer against it. The keystone is thesis 100 ("the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class") and thesis 101, which names that representation "both the primary cause and the central result of the general falsification of society."

## Related Concepts

- [[Spectacle]] — the general condition of which the proletariat's alienation into its own representation is the founding political instance; the party-as-representation is where the "social reign of appearances" first organizes the ruling order's defense.
- [[Representation]] — the mechanism by which the subject is displaced; here representation is not neutral depiction but the externalized, alienated form of the class's own power.
- [[Reification]] — the class's living self-activity congeals into things (party, union, state) that then stand over and against it.
- [[Spectacular Alienation]] — the proletariat's "own externalized power" returning to reinforce the society it sought to negate.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the subject/representation inflection of the tension: the proletariat as "condensed" material force versus its dematerialized, spectacular image that comes to rule in its place.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Separation]] — the proletariat as subject is precisely the force that would abolish all separation ("a total critique of separation," thesis 114); its degradation into representation is separation triumphant — the class split from its own power.

## Questions It Raises

- If every institution that speaks *for* the class tends to congeal into a power *over* it, can emancipation ever be organized without producing its own representation?
- Is the subject/representation reversal specific to the proletariat, or the general logic by which any collective agency becomes spectacle?
- Does the council form actually escape representation, or only defer it — and what prevents the councils, too, from externalizing into a separate power?
- If the proletariat "has lost its illusions... but has not lost its being" (thesis 114), what would reactivate the subject once its representation has become its enemy?
