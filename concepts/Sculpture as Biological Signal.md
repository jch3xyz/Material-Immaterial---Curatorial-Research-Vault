# Sculpture as Biological Signal

## Brief Definition

The speculative teleological climax of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*: Burnham's proposal that art — and sculpture above all — may be a "biological signal" or "psychical radar signal," an image-making drive whose deep evolutionary function is to prepare the human race for self-inflicted evolution into a "post-human species."

## Longer Explanation

In the final part of *Beyond Modern Sculpture* — a section Burnham titles "A Teleological Theory of Modern Sculpture" — the book turns from art history to outright speculation about the destiny of the species. Burnham himself flags the move as presumptuous and conjectural ("it may seem presumptuous for anyone to offer a theory uniting all human efforts to produce sculpture"), so every claim below should be read as the book's *speculative* register, not its documented argument (`Directly stated` that it is offered as speculation). His hypothesis is that "culture is fundamentally a means for implementing qualitative transformations of man's biological status," and that "art, then, and the whole image-making drive may be means for preparing man for physical and mental changes which he will in time make upon himself" (`Directly stated`).

From this premise comes the book's central image. Sculpture, "functioning so, becomes a kind of psychical radar signal preparing the human race thousands (or now perhaps only scores) of years in advance." The compression of that timescale — from millennia to "scores" of years — is the point: where biological adaptation in lower animals takes tens of thousands of years, "the human brain remains the only organism capable of re-forming biological patterns in a matter of only dozens of years," so that "we near a point where self-inflicted evolution becomes an imminent possibility." Art is thus recast as advance preparation for a change the species will deliberately make to itself (`Directly stated`).

The teleology has a specific art-historical anchor. Leaning on Sigfried Giedion and Herbert Read, Burnham notes that free-standing figure sculpture "rose to its highest development only after man had severed himself from the animal world" — in classical Greece, concurrent with the birth of science. He then asks whether this is coincidence: "Is it inconceivable that free-standing figure sculpture arose concurrent with the beginnings of science in Greece, preparing us spiritually and psychologically for the conscious task of radically altering the human race far in the future?" The detached human image and the scientific worldview emerge together, on this reading, as two faces of one preparation for self-re-creation (`Strongly implied` — Burnham poses it as a rhetorical question, not an assertion).

Burnham frames the whole proposal as a question about *biological signaling*: "Is it possible then — at least in the case of sculpture — that art is a form of biological signal?" If humanity is approaching "a time of radical change, one not controlled by natural selection and mutation," then the "spiritually motivated activity of artificially forming images of organic origin" would be the best nonscientific means of "anticipating self-re-creation (not procreation)" — and modern sculpture would be "this process vastly accelerated." This is the teleological capstone of [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] (the *desire*) and [[Object and System]] (the structural *shift* from art-object to working system): the present dissolution of the sculpted object is, in this frame, the signal firing at last (`Interpretive synthesis`).

Read forward into the vault, Burnham's 1968 "post-human species" prefigures the later authors' vocabularies. His "self-inflicted evolution" is, in informatic and engineering terms, what Kurzweil calls [[Transcending Biology]] and the Burnham–era cybernetic dream of [[Artificial Life]]; the "post-human species" anticipates [[Posthumanism]] and the discourse of [[Transhumanism]] and [[The Singularity]]; and the cyborg automata he surveys earlier in the chapter make the breached organism/machine boundary of [[The Cyborg]] literal. Burnham supplies an art-historical teleology for the same horizon these writers theorize — though, crucially, he leaves it as a speculation he can neither prove nor wholly endorse, even floating an alternative in which the whole drive is "merely a large-scale death wish" (`Speculative connection`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — sole source. Proposes, speculatively, that sculpture is a "psychical radar signal" / "biological signal" within "a post-biological logic for technological development," preparing humanity for "self-inflicted evolution" into a "post-human species"; ties the rise of free-standing Greek figure sculpture to the birth of science as joint spiritual preparation for self-re-creation. Burnham repeatedly hedges (rhetorical questions, the "death wish" counter-scenario), so the concept is held at medium confidence.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the counter-thesis (Hayles especially) that life and mind cannot be abstracted from a biological substrate; if there is only different embodiment, then "self-inflicted evolution" into a substrate-free "post-human species" loses its ground, and the "biological signal" would be signaling toward an impossibility.

## Questions It Raises

- Burnham flags the theory as speculation and even offers a "death wish" counter-reading — should "biological signal" be taken as a genuine claim about art's function, or as a provocation about how the dissolution of the art-object *feels* teleological from inside it?
- Is the pairing of free-standing Greek sculpture with the birth of science evidence of a shared evolutionary preparation, or a retrospective pattern imposed on two independent historical events?
- If sculpture is "this process vastly accelerated," does its own predicted disappearance (the object giving way to the system) mean the signal succeeds by ending — that art completes itself by ceasing to be art?
- Does Burnham's 1968 "post-human species" name the same horizon as later [[Posthumanism]] and [[Transhumanism]], or a distinct, art-historical teleology that those informatic/engineering discourses do not share?
