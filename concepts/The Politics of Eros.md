# The Politics of Eros

## Brief Definition

The claim that eros is not confined to the intimate dyad but is the ground of the *We* — of communal action, political revolt, and the courage for radical change — so that the erosion of eros (its degradation into sexuality and pornography under neoliberalism) is at the same time the erosion of the political.

## Longer Explanation

For Byung-Chul Han, in the seventh chapter of *The Agony of Eros*, love carries "something universal" that reaches beyond the couple. Following Plato, he holds that eros "guides the soul" and "holds sway over all its parts: pleasure-based desire (epithumia), spiritedness or courage (thumos), and reason (logos)." Each part enjoys and interprets beauty in its own way, but eros stands *above* desire and spiritedness and organizes them. The political stakes lie in the middle term. **Thumos** — spiritedness, courage, the capacity for rage that "radically breaks with convention and inaugurates a new state of affairs" — is the register where, in Han's words, "eros and politics touch." Eros is what "makes thumos bring forth beautiful deeds." Where eros withers, thumos withers with it, and political action loses its motor. *(Directly stated — Han's reading of the Platonic tripartite soul, with thumos as the hinge between eros and politics.)*

This is why Han reads the neoliberal degradation of love as a *depoliticization*. When love is "profaned into sexuality," the soul is dominated by epithumia — bare pleasure-based desire — while thumos "is also withering away" in "a burnout society of isolated, self-alienated achievement-subjects." At the same time, "without eros, logos is deteriorating into data-driven calculation, which is incapable of reckoning the event, the incalculable." Rage, the thumotic capacity for rupture, yields to mere "annoyance, or dissatisfaction, which lacks the negativity of rupture and instead allows circumstances to persist." The upshot is stark: "Communal action—a we—now proves impossible." The erosion of eros is thus continuous with the exhaustion Han diagnoses in the [[Achievement Society]] and with the general loss of [[Negativity]]; neoliberalism depoliticizes "not least of all, by replacing eros with sexuality and pornography." *(Directly stated.)*

Han develops the political charge of eros through three frames. The **Platonic** frame supplies the tripartite soul and locates the political in thumos (above). The **Badiouian** frame recasts love as an *event*: love is a "Two scene," which "interrupting the isolated perspective of the One... makes the world arise anew from the vantage point of the Other, or Difference." As an event it carries "the negativity of rupture, which allows something wholly Other to begin," and demands "fidelity." Crucially, "eventfulness connects love with politics and art. They all command 'fidelity' to the event." Sexuality and [[Pornography (Han)]], by contrast, occupy the narcissistic "One scene": the sexual object "always remains self-identical," "never calls me into question," and belongs "to the order of the habitual, which reproduces the Same" — so pornography "intensifies narcissification" while love "is dehabitualizing and denarcissifying." The **Surrealist** frame, finally, treats "the reinvention of love" as "an artistic, existential, and political gesture," citing André Breton's ascription of "a universal power to eros." Here eros is "the energetic source of renewal, which also feeds political action" — a "revolutionary yearning for an entirely different way of loving and another kind of society" that "remains faithful to what is yet to come." *(Directly stated across all three frames; the synthesis of the three into a single "politics of eros" is Han's own.)*

Politically, then, Han does not claim there will ever be "a politics of love" — "Politics remains antagonistic" — but that "political action occurs in a sphere that intersects with eros on manifold levels," so that "eros can be transformed politically." "Political action is mutual desire for another way of living—a more just world aligned with eros on every register," and "eros represents a source of energy for political revolt and engagement." The concept names precisely this convergence: the futural, utopian, rupture-bearing power that love, politics, and art share. *(Directly stated; Han cites Badiou's "secret resonance... like two musical instruments... which mysteriously converge.")*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (*The Agony of Eros*, ch. 7) — eros as the ground of the We and the source-energy of political revolt; its neoliberal collapse into epithumia (sexuality, pornography) is the collapse of thumos and thus of the political. Han synthesizes Plato (the tripartite soul; thumos as the touchpoint of eros and politics), Badiou (love as the "Two scene" event that interrupts the Same in favor of the Other), and surrealism (Breton's revolutionary eros faithful to what is yet to come).
- **Alain Badiou** (via Han) — love as a "Two scene," an *event* demanding fidelity; the "secret resonance" between a life lived under a political idea and the intensity of love. Han draws on *In Praise of Love* and *Ethics*; Badiou himself "rejects a direct link between politics and love" but grants their mysterious convergence.
- **André Breton / surrealism** (via Han) — eros as "the medium of a poetic revolution in language and existence," an artistic-existential-political gesture endowed with "universal power."

## Related Concepts

- [[Eros]] — the parent concept; the politics of eros is eros extended from the dyad to the We.
- [[The Other]] — the "Two scene" and the event arise "from the vantage point of the Other, or Difference"; without the Other there is no rupture and no politics.
- [[The Event]] — Han's Badiou-derived event of rupture; here love, politics, and art all "command 'fidelity' to the event."
- [[Negativity]] — the "negativity of rupture" that thumotic rage and the loving event share, and that annoyance and sexuality lack.
- [[Achievement Society]] — the "burnout society of isolated, self-alienated achievement-subjects" in which thumos and the We wither.
- [[Beyond the Subject]] — the loving subject undone by the Other resonates with Han's account of a subjectivity opened past the isolated ego.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Pornography (Han)]] — the "One scene" of auto-affection and narcissification that erases the Other; the antithesis of the erotic-political "Two scene."
- [[Positivity]] — the smooth, habitual order that "reproduces the Same"; eros' politics turns on the negativity of rupture that positivity forecloses.
- [[Hell of the Same]] — the depoliticized world of the Same into which sexuality and pornography deliver us; the politics of eros is its refusal.

## Questions It Raises

- If communal action, revolt, and the courage for change all depend on eros, can politics be reanimated by any means *other* than a reinvention of love — or is Han's diagnosis a counsel of despair?
- Is thumos as "the place where eros and politics touch" a Platonic anthropology that survives the neoliberal soul it is used to diagnose, or does the argument presuppose the very soul it says has been hollowed out?
- Does grounding the political in the "negativity of rupture" (the event) leave any room for slow, institutional, non-eventful politics — or does it, like Badiou, privilege the exceptional break over the ordinary?
- Han insists "a politics of love will never exist" because "politics remains antagonistic"; how far can eros be "transformed politically" before it either dissolves into aestheticized yearning or hardens into the antagonism it was meant to exceed?
