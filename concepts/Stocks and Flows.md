# Stocks and Flows

## Brief Definition

The core primitive of system dynamics: a **stock** is an accumulation you can see, feel, count, or measure at a given moment — the memory of the history of changing flows — while **flows** are the rates of filling and draining that raise or lower it over time.

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows, the stock is the foundation of any system. A stock is "just what it sounds like: a store, a quantity, an accumulation" — the water in a bathtub, a population, the money in a bank, the wood in the living trees of a forest. Crucially it need not be physical: "Your reserve of good will toward others or your supply of hope that the world can be better are both stocks." What unifies them is that a stock is, at any instant, a static quantity — and, dynamically, "the present memory of the history of changing flows within the system." Read the level of a stock and you are reading the integrated record of every inflow and outflow that has ever acted on it.

Stocks change only through flows: "filling and draining, births and deaths, purchases and sales, growth and decay, deposits and withdrawals, successes and failures." In Meadows's stock-and-flow diagrams the stock is drawn as a box and each flow as an arrow-headed "pipe" with a faucet that can be turned up or down. From this simple bathtub picture a set of principles follows: as long as inflows exceed outflows the stock rises; as long as outflows exceed inflows it falls; and if the two are equal the stock holds at a constant level in *dynamic equilibrium* even while water keeps running through it. A stock therefore decouples its inflows from its outflows, allowing them "to be independent and temporarily out of balance with each other" — which is why we build banks, reservoirs, and inventories.

Meadows presses a cognitive point that the diagrams make visible: "The human mind seems to focus more easily on stocks than on flows," and when we do attend to flows we favor inflows over outflows. Hence her recurring correction — "A stock can be increased by decreasing its outflow rate as well as by increasing its inflow rate. There's more than one way to fill a bathtub!" A breakthrough in energy efficiency raises the stock of available oil exactly as a new oilfield does; a firm can enlarge its workforce by slowing quitting and firing rather than by hiring. This is the same principle she restates in the capital-growth model of the Systems Zoo: "You can make a stock grow by decreasing its outflow rate as well as by increasing" its inflow.

Because a stock is a level and not a rate, it changes slowly: "A stock takes time to change, because flows take time to flow." This gives stocks their systemic role as delays, lags, buffers, ballast, and sources of momentum. Large stocks — a population, the ozone-depleting pollutants in the stratosphere, an economy's installed base of oil-burning furnaces — respond even to sudden changes in their flows only by gradual filling or emptying, so that "changes in stocks set the pace of the dynamics of systems." The time lags this imposes can cause problems, but they are equally a source of stability and "allow room to maneuver, to experiment, and to revise policies that aren't working." A stock is thus the material carrier of a system's inertia, and the first place a systems thinker looks: a stock whose level is monitored and whose flows are adjusted to control it is the germ of a [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] — "A feedback loop is formed when changes in a stock affect the flows into or out of that same stock."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — the stock-and-flow pair is her foundational primitive, inherited from Jay Forrester's system dynamics: the stock is "the memory of the history of changing flows within the system," flows are its rates of change, and their joint behavior over time is what one must grasp to understand any complex system. Meadows insists a stock need not be physical (good will, hope, self-confidence) and stresses the counterintuitive outflow lever and the delay/buffer role of large stocks.

## Related Concepts

- [[A System]] — the whole of which stocks are the visible, measurable elements; "A stock is the foundation of any system."
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the control structure that arises when the level of a stock feeds back to adjust its own inflows or outflows; stocks are the things feedback loops hold within a range, grow, or deplete.
- [[Delays]] — the temporal consequence of stocks: because stocks change slowly, they "act as delays, lags, buffers, ballast, and sources of momentum in a system," setting the pace of the system's dynamics.
- [[Stock-Limited vs Flow-Limited Resources]] — a distinction that turns entirely on whether a resource is drawn from a finite stock or replenished by an ongoing flow.
- [[Systems Theory]] — stocks and flows are the elementary vocabulary in which system-dynamics models are built.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Homeostasis]] — not an opposite so much as a limiting case: a balancing feedback loop acting on a stock holds it in dynamic equilibrium, the level unchanging while flows continue. The concepts sit in tension because homeostasis names the *held* state, whereas stocks-and-flows names the underlying capacity for that state to change and accumulate history.

## Questions It Raises

- If a stock is "the memory of the history of changing flows," in what sense do slow-changing stocks (populations, installed infrastructure, accumulated skill) constitute a system's material memory — and does this reframe [[Material vs Immaterial|the material/immaterial divide]] as a matter of accumulation and rate?
- Why does the mind grasp stocks more readily than flows, and outflows least of all — and what habitual policy errors follow from that asymmetry?
- If the delays imposed by large stocks are simultaneously the cause of systemic surprise and a source of stability, when is a stock's momentum an asset to be steered and when a trap?
