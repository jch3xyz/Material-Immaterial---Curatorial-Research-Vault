# A Pluralism of Centers

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's name for the only form restraint of electric power can take: because an electric structure has no margins, absolutist electric power can be checked not by the Western separation of powers but "by a pluralism of centers," in which there is "dialogue only among centers and among equals."

## Longer Explanation

In the Telephone chapter, McLuhan moves from the perceptual character of the medium to a sharp political claim. The telephone bypasses "all hierarchical arrangements": delegated, chain-of-command authority "won't work by telephone but only by written instruction." What does work is the [[The Authority of Knowledge]] — which McLuhan calls "nonlineal, nonvisual, and inclusive," as against delegated authority that is "lineal, visual, hierarchical." The pyramidal structure of job-division and delegated powers "cannot withstand the speed of the phone." A Pluralism of Centers is the political face of that authority: once power is electric and inclusive rather than delegated and lineal, the device that restrained it must change form too.

The crux is structural, not merely procedural. The separation of powers, McLuhan argues, "had been a technique for restraining action in a centralist structure radiating out to remote margins" — a check that presupposes a [[Center-Margin Structure]], a single center radiating authority outward toward a periphery. But "in an electric structure there are, so far as the time and space of this planet are concerned, no margins." Remove the margin and the center-to-margin restraint loses its object; you cannot separate powers along a radius that no longer exists. What remains is the relation of one center to another. Hence "dialogue only among centers and among equals" — restraint becomes lateral and mutual rather than vertical and divisive. His concrete instance is "the direct private line from the Kremlin to the White House": two centers, no margin between them, restraining each other by dialogue rather than by an internal architecture of checks and balances. (McLuhan notes Kennedy's "natural Western bias" in preferring teletype, the more visual and lineal form, over the telephone.)

This is the no-margins facet of [[The Global Village]] turned toward politics. The same electric implosion that collapses distance into a global village collapses the center-margin geometry on which Western political restraint was built. So the concept belongs to McLuhan's larger insistence that [[Electric implosion must be met by decentralism not expansion]]: "Freedom in the Western world has always taken the form of the explosive, the divisive, advancing the separation of the individual from the state," and the reversal of "that one-way movement outward from center-to-margin" is owing to electricity. A Pluralism of Centers is the institutional shape of [[Decentralization]] under electric conditions — many co-equal centers in dialogue, rather than one center governed by its own internal separations.

It is worth marking what the concept does and does not promise. McLuhan calls the power being restrained "electric absolutist power," and the restraint he names is not a guarantee but a different mechanism — pluralism in place of separation. The danger ([[The Authority of Knowledge]] as "inclusive," the seamless web of instant decision-making) and its check (a pluralism of centers) are two descriptions of the same electric condition, which is why the claim sits inside the [[Mechanical vs Electric]] contrast rather than offering a way out of it.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Center vs Margin]] — the Western political logic A Pluralism of Centers displaces. The separation of powers restrains action in "a centralist structure radiating out to remote margins"; an electric structure has "no margins," so that center-to-margin restraint can no longer apply.

## Questions It Raises

- If restraint of electric power requires co-equal centers in dialogue, what happens when one center is far more powerful than another — does "among equals" describe a fact or only an aspiration?
- McLuhan treats the hot line as a model of plural-center restraint; do later networked/surveillance structures (Han's [[Digital Panopticon]], Harari's [[Dataism]]) realize "a pluralism of centers" or re-concentrate power in a single processing center despite the absence of margins?
- Is a pluralism of centers genuinely a restraint, or merely a different distribution of the same "electric absolutist power" — given that McLuhan offers no internal check within any one center?
- Does the disappearance of margins make the separation of powers obsolete in all electric institutions, or only in those (like instant diplomacy) where speed truly bypasses the chain of command?
