# Be the Friction

## Brief Definition

Zuboff's signature remedy against surveillance capitalism: a deliberate refusal of "the smooth flows of coercive confluence" in which friction, courage, and bearings become the resources of resistance and the means of reclaiming the digital future as a human place.

## Longer Explanation

"Be the Friction" is the title of section VI of the book's final chapter, "A Coup from Above," and it names Zuboff's positive program — the counter-move to the message of inevitability she has spent the book diagnosing. Where surveillance capitalism's instrumentarian power works by *smoothing* the path of behavior into frictionless, automated, predictable flows ("the smooth flows of coercive confluence"), the human response is to introduce friction deliberately: to refuse, to slow, to resist enthrallment. `Directly stated` — Zuboff writes: "We answer his call when we bend ourselves toward friction, rejecting the smooth flows of coercive confluence."

The "his call" is George Orwell's. Zuboff routes her remedy through Orwell's 1946 review of James Burnham's *The Managerial Revolution*, in which Orwell attacks Burnham's cowardly habit of "predicting a continuation of the thing that is happening" and his "instinct to bow down before the conqueror of the moment, to accept the existing trend as irreversible." For Zuboff this is precisely the disease of inevitabilism, a "loss of bearings" and a "failure of moral effort." Orwell's *courage* is the antidote. `Directly stated`: "Friction, courage, and bearings are the resources we require to begin the shared work of synthetic declarations that claim the digital future as a human place." (Note: the Burnham named here is the political theorist James Burnham, not the sculpture critic of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*.)

The concept is therefore inseparable from the rejection of inevitability. `Directly stated`: "I reject inevitability, and it is my hope that as a result of our journey together, you will too." Friction is what reclaiming agency looks like when "technological 'inevitability'" is exposed as "an existential narcotic prescribed to induce resignation." The chapter's most-quoted line condenses the program into a slogan of priority — the human comes first, the digital second: "Let there be a digital future, but let it be a human future first." `Directly stated`.

Zuboff closes on the language of collective declaration and beginning, drawing on Hannah Arendt's "capacity to begin" and the fall of the Berlin Wall as the model of a refusal that changes history. `Directly stated`: "The Berlin Wall fell for many reasons, but above all it was because the people of East Berlin said, 'No more!' We too can be the authors of many 'great and beautiful' new facts that reclaim the digital future as humanity's home. No more! Let this be our declaration." Being the friction is thus not merely individual obstinacy but the shared work of "synthetic declarations" that assert sovereignty over one's own life and "authorship of one's own experience."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — friction is the deliberate refusal of frictionless coercive flow; its resources are "friction, courage, and bearings," its model the Berlin Wall's "No more!", its priority "let it be a human future first." It is the practical face of her rejection of [[Inevitabilism]] and the answer to [[The Coup from Above]].

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Smoothness]] — Zuboff's "smooth flows of coercive confluence" name exactly what friction refuses. This resonates strongly with Han's `Smoothness` (the aesthetic-political category of the frictionless positive surface that eliminates negativity and resistance), but the two are kept as linked, distinct notes rather than merged: Han theorizes smoothness as a general regime of positivity, while Zuboff names a specific operational smoothing of behavior by surveillance capital. `Speculative connection`

## Questions It Raises

- Is "friction" a personal ethic of refusal, a collective political program, or both — and can individual friction scale to the "synthetic declarations" Zuboff calls for?
- How does Zuboff's positive valuation of friction relate to Han's diagnosis of `Smoothness` as the eliminated negativity of late-capitalist culture? Are they describing one phenomenon from two directions?
- If smoothness is engineered to feel like freedom and convenience, what makes friction recognizable and choosable rather than merely felt as malfunction or loss?
- Can a remedy built on "courage" and "bearings" — moral resources — counter a power whose strength lies precisely in evading moral and political contest?
