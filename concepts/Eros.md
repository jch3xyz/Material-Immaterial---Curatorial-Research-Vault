# Eros

## Brief Definition

Eros is the relation to the wholly [[The Other|Other]] beyond achievement, performance, and ability — a relationship bound intrinsically to negativity and death, in which the subject is pulled out of itself toward an atopic Other that "interrupts the exchange rate."

## Longer Explanation

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], eros is not a feeling, a technology of choice, or an intensification of the self; it is a relation whose whole condition is the *[[Negativity|negativity]]* of the [[The Other|Other]]. "Eros concerns the Other in the strong sense, namely, what cannot be encompassed by the regime of the ego" (*Melancholia*). This Other is *atopos* — placeless, removed from the language of sameness, refusing consumption. Han's central polemic is that contemporary society, by flattening everything into consumable, comparable difference, has eroded precisely this atopic otherness, and with it the possibility of erotic experience: "in the inferno of the same … erotic experience does not exist." Eros is therefore the direct antagonist of the [[Hell of the Same]] — where the Same admits only heterotopic differences to be consumed, eros presumes "the asymmetry and exteriority of the Other." *(Directly stated.)*

Eros stands *beyond* the entire register of the [[Achievement Society]]. "Eros is a relationship to the Other situated beyond achievement, performance, and ability" (*Being Able Not to Be Able*), and its constitutive movement is [[Negative Potency|negative potency]] — the "being able not to be able" (*Nicht-Können-Können*) that lets the Other appear at all. "Absolutizing ability is precisely what annihilates the Other"; a successful relationship with the Other "finds expression as a kind of failure." Because otherness cannot be earned, produced, or optimized, eros is structurally illegible to the achievement-subject and to capital: "Eros … represents an asymmetrical relationship to the Other. As such, it interrupts the exchange rate. Otherness admits no bookkeeping. It does not appear in the balance of debt and credit" (*Being Able Not to Be Able*). *(Directly stated.)*

Eros is bound intrinsically to death — this is what most sharply separates it from mere survival and from [[Bare Life|bare life]]. Han reads eroticism through Bataille and Ficino as a *Liebestod*, a love-death: "Eroticism, it may be said, is assenting to life up to the point of death." What is affirmed is not bare life, "which flees the negativity of death," but life heightened to the point where "the impulse to live … approaches the impulse to die." "Eros is the medium for intensifying life to the point of death" (*Bare Life*). The self that loves does not assert itself; it undergoes a self-loss that is the gift of the Other — "the power (*Macht*) of eros implies powerlessness and unconsciousness (*Ohn-Macht*); instead of affirming myself, I lose myself in (or for) the Other, who then rights me again." This is why "the slave, who holds fast to life and labors, proves incapable of erotic experience," and why the [[Achievement Society|achievement-subject]] — master and slave at once, exploiting himself — is equally foreclosed from it. *(Directly stated.)*

Finally, eros grounds fantasy, desire, and art, all of which presuppose the negativity of absence. "Desire is always desire for the Other. The negativity of privation and absence nourishes it" (*Fantasy*). Where the [[Positivity|positive]], hypervisible order of [[Transparency]] and unchecked freedom of choice dissolves thresholds, the Other — and so desire and fantasy — withers: "The contemporary crisis in literature and the arts stems from a crisis of fantasy: the disappearance of the Other. This is the agony of eros." *(Directly stated.)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (*The Agony of Eros*, 2012/2017) — eros as the relation to the atopic Other beyond achievement and ability, bound to negativity and death (the *Liebestod*), and dying today of the "erosion of the Other" in the [[Hell of the Same]]. Han's counter-concept to [[Negative Potency|negative potency]] here is "being able not to be able."
- **Alain Badiou** (Foreword, *The Reinvention of Love*) — supplies the framing thesis Han's book answers to: love as a "scene of the Two," a "truth procedure" through which the world is experienced from the standpoint of difference rather than the ego. Badiou's "scene of the Two" is his own construction, not Han's; it frames the master-thesis but should not be attributed to Han. *(Strongly implied — Badiou is glossing Han's argument from outside.)*
- **Herbert Marcuse** *(open forward section)* — Marcuse's *Eros and Civilization* is the unwritten precursor for eros as a force of non-repressive, non-performative life against the reality principle. Realize when/if Marcuse is ingested.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Other]] — eros is *the* relation to the atopic Other; the erosion of the Other is the death of eros.
- [[Negative Potency]] — "being able not to be able" (*Nicht-Können-Können*) is the mode in which the Other appears; eros is its enactment.
- [[Bare Life]] — eros as excess and transgression *denies* bare life; the two are opposed forms of the relation to death.
- [[Negativity]] — eros is constituted by the negativity of the Other (asymmetry, absence, privation, death) that a positivized society dissolves.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Hell of the Same]] — the inferno of the same eliminates atopic otherness, so "erotic experience does not exist" within it.
- [[Achievement Society]] — eros lies "beyond achievement, performance, and ability"; the self-exploiting achievement-subject is foreclosed from it.
- [[Positivity]] — the positive order of choice, transparency, and consumable difference erodes the negativity eros requires; see also [[Pornography (Han)]] as "the antagonist of eros."

## Questions It Raises

- If eros requires an Other that "admits no bookkeeping," can any relation survive inside an economy that renders everything comparable and consumable?
- Is Han's binding of eros to death (the *Liebestod*) a necessary condition of erotic experience, or a Romantic-tragic inflection specific to his sources (Bataille, Wagner, Ficino)?
- Where Badiou locates love in a "scene of the Two" and Han locates eros in the atopia of the Other, are these the same relation named differently, or rival ontologies of love?
- Does the "agony of eros" name a contingent cultural pathology (curable) or a structural consequence of positivization ([[Transparency]], [[Dataism]]) that cannot be reversed from within?
