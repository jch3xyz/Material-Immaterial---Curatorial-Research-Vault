# Network as Artwork

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's reconception of the artwork not as a discrete object but as a connective network — a distributed, participatory system into which art, theory, criticism, education, the museum, and the art market are all reorganized.

## Longer Explanation

"Network as Artwork" is the seed of Ascott's entire network program. The phrase comes from a short 1978 conference abstract — "Abstract for the World Future Society Education Section Meeting, University of Houston, Clear Lake City, October, 1978. Previously unpublished" — written for an *education* audience, and it is from that apparently institutional/pedagogical angle that the idea first appears. Ascott's claim is that the emerging mode of participatory art will keep developing "further, leading to a connective, network paradigm for art and art theory, calling for a connective criticism." The artwork, in other words, is reframed as a network; and once art is a network, everything around it — its theory, its criticism, its teaching, its museums, its market — must be reorganized as networks and systems too. `Directly stated`.

The chapter develops this most concretely through post-secondary art education, which Ascott insists "must be examined in a post-institutional context" where "[m]obility and networking will become important constraints in educational theory and on curricular planning." The discipline-based curriculum (the "rigid compartmentalization and specialization" of departments of painting, sculpture, etc.) is to be dissolved into a global, exploratory "learning net" — "an exploratory and speculative field with polarities of information/structure/concept and probes into cultural/ technological/noetic areas" — accessed by travel and teleconferencing rather than by attendance. (The triad's term "noetic" is an early, pre-coinage seed of what Ascott will later name *technoetics*; it is not realized as that concept from this abstract alone — `Speculative connection`.) Notably, Ascott dissolves the *institution*, not media-specific *skill*: "skill banks allowing for media-specific instruction will remain." `Directly stated`.

The institutional/curatorial face of the idea is dematerialization. Networked access "will lead to fewer permanent, monolithic buildings for art education, fewer permanent institutions," replaced by "[a] pattern of leasing, adapting to locally available resources and short-term structures." The same logic dissolves the art world's fixed centres: "Museums, too, will tend to physically disintegrate, becoming instead exhibition systems. Art centres (New York, London, etc.) as a concept and a marketplace will be replaced by moving centres." This is the museum-and-market version of the object→system passage that Lippard reads in the art object and Burnham reads in sculpture: the permanent, place-bound institution (an object) gives way to a mobile, distributed system. `Directly stated`.

Chronologically and conceptually this 1978 abstract is the precursor to "the telematic embrace" and to `Network Consciousness`: the artwork-as-network here grows into the planetary, telematic, distributed-mind program of Ascott's 1980s–90s writing. It sits on the vault's `Object vs System` and `Material vs Immaterial` spine and translates McLuhan's global village into a pedagogical and curatorial project — "a learning landscape will be available to the student that will be global in scope and call on information and amenities from many disciplines and pursuits." `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins the phrase and the program: the artwork is a connective network, and art, theory, criticism (a "connective criticism"), education (a global "learning net"), the museum ("exhibition systems"), and the market ("moving centres") are all reorganized as networks/systems. The 1978 abstract is the institutional/pedagogical germ that becomes telematic art and network consciousness. Cross-link: the participatory pole of 1960s–70s systems art, complementary to Burnham's object→system [[Systems Esthetics]].

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object vs System]] — the abstract's structuring opposition: the permanent, monolithic, departmentalized, place-bound *institution* (college, museum, art centre) vs the mobile, distributed, networked *system* (learning net, exhibition systems, moving centres). The network-as-artwork is the system pole's victory.

## Questions It Raises

- If the artwork becomes a network, what is the unit of authorship, ownership, and conservation? A "moving centre" and an "exhibition system" have no permanent object to collect — what does the museum become?
- Ascott dissolves the discipline-based institution but keeps "skill banks." Does media-specific skill survive coherently once the departments that taught and transmitted it are gone?
- A "connective, network paradigm … calling for a connective criticism" — what would a criticism adequate to a network (rather than to an object) actually look like? (See the deferred Connective Criticism material.)
- Is the 1978 prediction of teleconferenced, post-institutional, globally indexed learning a description of today's networked art education — or did the institution prove far more durable than Ascott foresaw?
- Where does the "noetic" pole of the triad lead? Is the network-as-artwork ultimately a network of *minds* (technoetics / network consciousness) rather than of objects or even of images?
