# The Scientific Demiurge

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's name for the impersonal creative force — the psychic and intellectual authority invested in science and technology — that has subsumed religion, art, and craft, and that "moves ceaselessly in a single irreversible direction," rendering art as a "dwindling part of an unstoppable scheme of events" and making the old art-historical cycle of styles impossible.

## Longer Explanation

In *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Burnham argues that twentieth-century sculpture is not driven by stylistic fashion or individual genius but by a single coercive force he names the scientific (or technological) demiurge — the rising authority that culture has invested in science and technics. This force is impersonal and totalizing: "Religion, literary imagery, craft technique, drama, architecture, painting — all the forces which have touched sculpture in the past — have not escaped the technological demiurge." Its effect on sculpture is a rupture: "Both physically and spiritually technics are responsible for sculpture's impending rupture with the past" (`Directly stated`).

The decisive structural claim is **irreversibilist and teleological**. Because art has become "a psychical manifestation of the scientific demiurge, which moves ceaselessly in a single irreversible direction," the inherited art-historical "karma of stylistic cycles: Archaic, Classical, Mannerist, Romantic, Abstract, and so on" becomes "a self-evident impossibility." Style no longer recurs in cycles; art is carried forward by an underlying current toward an end-state and so "becomes a dwindling part of an unstoppable scheme of events." Burnham thus replaces the cyclic, recurrent model of art history with a one-directional, irreversible one anchored in the growth of science (`Directly stated`).

In Chapter 3, "Formalism: The Weary Vocabulary," Burnham locates the *psychic* root of the demiurge in the shared drive of science and formalist art: "The desire to explain the quantitative and qualitative consistency of physical phenomena stands behind the spirit of scientific inquiry. This drive also provides the psychic basis for modern formalism in sculpture. The passion of each is to reveal and analyze underlying truths." But where this drive yields ever-deeper (and increasingly invisible) order in science, in sculpture it produces "growing stasis" — "A present dilemma of sculpture is its inability to move beyond phenomenological means and into those very areas of nonvisual analysis established by science." The demiurge that powers science strands sculpture, which cannot follow science into the nonvisual without ceasing to be sculpture in the traditional sense (`Strongly implied`).

The concept is tightly bound to Burnham's account of [[Reification]] — the constant "resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory" — and to [[Technics]], the single word he coins for science-and-technology as a coercive metaphysical force. The demiurge is the *agent*; reification is the *mechanism* by which the world is remade through artifacts in a scientific culture (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — Coins and develops the concept in *Beyond Modern Sculpture*. Uses "scientific demiurge" and "technological demiurge" near-interchangeably; the demiurge names the invested authority of science/technics that has "fluctuated in philosophical emphasis... a rising influence but not a monolithic one." It is irreversibilist and teleological: art is its "psychical manifestation," and stylistic cycles are abolished because the force runs in "a single irreversible direction." Burnham frames it as both destructive and creative — the "destructive-creative duality of technics."

## Related Concepts

- [[Technics]] — The demiurge is the creative agency of technics; technics is "the metaphysics of our century" whose coercive nature drives sculpture's rupture with the past.
- [[Reification]] — The operational mechanism: how the world (and sculpture) is "made anew" in a scientific culture by resynchronizing artistic sensibility with the scientific form-world.
- [[Dematerialization]] — Burnham reads the "tortuous dematerialization of post-World War II sculpture" as a symptom of the demiurge's irreversible pull away from mass and material presence; cross-links to Lippard's later use of the term.
- [[Acceleration]] — The demiurge's single-direction, ever-quickening turnover of forms resonates with (though is distinct from) Han's pathological acceleration; here the directionality is celebrated-as-inevitable rather than diagnosed as crisis.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Disenchantment]] — The demiurge is the inverse posture: rather than the world being emptied of immanent meaning by science, science itself becomes "an operational system as psychologically and intellectually pervasive as any monolithic religion," a re-enchanting metaphysical force that has *subsumed* religion and art rather than merely disenchanting them.
- The cyclic "art history karma of stylistic cycles" (Archaic, Classical, Mannerist, Romantic, Abstract) — the recurrent, style-driven model of art history that the demiurge's irreversibility renders impossible.

## Questions It Raises

- If art is merely a "psychical manifestation" of an impersonal force moving in one irreversible direction, what remains of artistic agency, intention, or autonomy?
- Is Burnham's irreversibilism falsifiable — or does the abolition of stylistic cycles make any return of figuration or recurrence unaccountable within the theory?
- How does a *teleological* account of art (toward an end-state of life-simulating systems) differ from, and relate to, secular narratives of progress that Burnham elsewhere doubts can attach to art at all?
- Does the demiurge "subsume" religion and art, or merely displace them — and what is the difference for an artist working inside it?
