# The Other

## Brief Definition

The wholly other — an alterity so radical that it "eludes all ability" and cannot be possessed, grasped, or known; for Byung-Chul Han the Other is the atopic term whose erosion, not whose over-supply, is the true cause of the contemporary crisis of love.

## Longer Explanation

The Other is the note that realizes a long-committed forward-stub in the vault (previously reached only through inbound links such as `Otherness` and the banned variant `The Other (Han)`). It is here anchored in **[[Byung-Chul Han]]**'s *The Agony of Eros* (2012/2017), where the concept becomes the pivot of the whole book, but it is a genuinely cross-author term — Han builds his account explicitly out of Emmanuel Levinas, Martin Buber, and [[Roland Barthes|Barthes]], and the note is open to their sections when those sources enter the vault.

For Han, the Other is defined by a **negativity** that resists all appropriation. His decisive formula, borrowed from Levinas, is that "the negativity of otherness—that is, the atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability—is constitutive of erotic experience." The Other cannot be reached by *can* (the modal verb of [[Achievement Society|achievement society]]); it appears "only by way of being able not to be able," making the Other the object toward which [[Negative Potency|the power not-to]] is directed. Grasping is the wrong verb entirely: "If one could possess, grasp, and know the other, it would not be other. Possessing, knowing, and grasping are synonyms of power." The Other is thus **atopos** — placeless, removed from the language of sameness — which is why Han calls Socrates the lover *atopos* and, following Barthes, holds that "one cannot speak of the Other" without every attribute turning "false, painful, erroneous, awkward." *(Directly stated — chs. 2, 3.)*

Han's central diagnostic claim inverts the usual sociology of love (associated with [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz|Eva Illouz]]): "The crisis of love does not derive from too many others so much as from the erosion of the Other." What corrodes love is not an overabundance of choices but the disappearance of alterity as such, whose corollary is "the mounting narcissification of the Self." Today's culture of constant comparison flattens everything into consumable, "heterotopic" differences and so eliminates the atopic Other, which "refuses consumption." In the resulting [[Hell of the Same|inferno of the same]] there is no erotic experience, because "erotic experience presumes the asymmetry and exteriority of the Other." Where [[Positivity]] and [[Transparency|hypervisibility]] prevail, otherness is dismantled: "Information, as such, is positivity that leads to a dismantling of the Other's negativity," and pornography — maximal visual [[Information]] — destroys the fantasy that alone "brings forth the Other in its atopic otherness." *(Directly stated — chs. 2, 6.)*

The priority of the Other is also, for Han, an ethical and political priority. Reading Buber's "primal distance" (*Urdistanz*) and Levinas's "countenance," he argues that the Other must be held at the distance that "frees" it into its otherness rather than reified into an "it" or fragmented into sexual "part-objects." Erotic power is not domination: "The priority of the Other distinguishes the power of Eros from the violence of Ares" — the lover loses and recovers the self *through* the Other, so that strength "is not the achievement of the One, but the gift of the Other." Politically (via Badiou's "Two scene"), love "makes the world arise anew from the vantage point of the Other," and "the essence of the event is the negativity of rupture, which allows something wholly Other to begin." Even thought depends on it: without "seduction by the atopic Other," thinking "withers into mere work, which always reproduces the Same." The sexual object, by contrast, "always remains self-identical … it never calls me into question." *(Directly stated / Strongly implied — chs. 3, 4, 7, 8.)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** *(The Agony of Eros)* — the Other as the atopic, wholly other whose alterity eludes all *can*; its erosion (not a surplus of others) is the root of the agony of [[Eros]], and its disappearance into the [[Hell of the Same]] abolishes erotic experience, fantasy, event, and thought. Han's account is a synthesis of Levinas (*Time and the Other*), Buber (primal distance), and Barthes (*A Lover's Discourse*). This is the note's anchoring usage; it is open to a future Levinas, Buber, or Barthes section, and to [[Herbert Marcuse|Marcuse]] via the shared horizon of [[Eros]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Eros]] — the mode of relation that presumes and preserves the Other; "Eros concerns the Other in the strong sense."
- [[Atopia]] — the placelessness of the Other; the two concepts are nearly co-extensive in Han (the "atopic Other").
- [[Negative Potency]] — "being able not to be able" is the faculty by which the Other alone appears; the Other is what it is directed toward.
- [[Negativity]] — the Other is constituted by negativity (withdrawal, distance, absence); positivity dismantles it.
- [[The Event]] — the eruption of the wholly Other; "the negativity of rupture, which allows something wholly Other to begin."
- [[Transparency]] — the society of transparency, as hypervisibility, is the regime under which the Other is eroded.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Hell of the Same]] — the narcissistic inferno of the same is precisely the abolition of the Other; the two are strict opposites.
- [[Positivity]] — flattens alterity into consumable, "heterotopic" difference; "in contrast to otherness, difference is positive."
- [[Pornography (Han)]] — erases otherness altogether, occupying "the One scene"; the pornographic image "emanates no resistance of the Other."

## Questions It Raises

- If the Other "eludes all ability," is any ethics or politics that would *secure* a relation to the Other already a betrayal of it? (Badiou raises this against Han in the Foreword: must one "abolish the self … in order to encounter the Other"?)
- Is Han's "erosion of the Other" a genuinely new historical condition, or a re-description of long-standing critiques of reification and narcissism?
- Can the atopic Other survive digital mediation at all, or does every increase of information necessarily dismantle its negativity?
- How does Han's Levinasian, asymmetrical Other relate to Badiou's symmetrical "Two scene" of love — priority of the Other versus a shared "we two"?
