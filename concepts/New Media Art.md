# New Media Art

## Brief Definition

Art whose medium is technology itself — motion, light, electricity, feedback, and information — such that the static, hand-made object gives way to time-based, systemic, and responsive work.

## Longer Explanation

New Media Art names the family of practices in which the art-work's medium is no longer wood, bronze, or pigment but a technological process: real-time motion (kinetic art), emitted light and electromechanical display (light art), and cybernetic feedback coupling work and viewer (robot and cyborg art). Jack Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) supplies the direct ancestry of this category. Its master thesis — the migration of art from the OBJECT to the SYSTEM — runs through three successive chapters that this note draws on: Kineticism (Ch. 6), Light as a Sculpture Medium (Ch. 7), and Robot and Cyborg Art (Ch. 8). Across them, Burnham tracks how sculpture sheds its "thingness" and becomes a technologically-driven, temporal, systemic, and finally communicative thing. `Directly stated` (Burnham organizes the book's second half, "Sculpture as System," around exactly this migration).

In the kinetic phase, the work is reconstituted as a serviceable mechanism: "the Kinetic construction is not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art" — a system of interacting components that, like a living thing, needs repair and adjustment. `Directly stated`. With light, the medium becomes pure emitted energy, and the trend, in Burnham's words, "is to eliminate the specific art object and to transform the environment into a light-modulating system sensitive to responses from organisms which invade its presence" — i.e. the work dissolves into a responsive, participatory environment. `Directly stated`. With cybernetics, the work acquires reciprocal communication: where all prior sculpture (even kinetic art) delivers a one-way message, Burnham's "Cyborg Art" pursues "a sustained two-way experience" between work and observer, defining a cyborg as "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism." `Directly stated`. The throughline is a shift in the unit of art: "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." `Directly stated`.

These three technological lineages — kinetic, luminous, and cybernetic/responsive — are the structural genealogy of what later practice calls new media art: art that is time-based rather than fixed, systemic rather than objectal, and responsive/interactive rather than contemplated at a distance. Burnham also foregrounds the institutional and social conditions of such art: the new "artist–engineer symbiosis" (Experiments in Art and Technology, Inc., founded in the late 1960s) and the rising dependence of art on outside technical labor as it "accepts the technological scale of values." `Directly stated` (Ch. 6, Ch. 8). The immateriality that Klein made the New Tendency's "rallying cry" prefigures the dematerialized, energy-and-information condition Burnham sees art trending toward — a precursor to Lippard's later dematerialization. `Strongly implied`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — primary anchor. In *Beyond Modern Sculpture* the ancestry of new media art is the object-to-system migration across kinetic, light, and cybernetic art: the work becomes a `systeme d'art`, then a responsive light environment, then a feedback-coupled cyborg system in which "information" supplants physical presence.
- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** — conceptual-art precursor. Lippard's dematerialization of the art object (in *Six Years*) is the parallel/precursor lineage on the conceptual side; Burnham's technologically-driven immateriality and Lippard's dematerialization converge on the disappearance of the discrete material object. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Related Concepts

- [[Kinetic Art]] — the kinetic lineage: nonrepresentational art using real time and motion; the first move from object to `systeme d'art`.
- [[Light as a Sculpture Medium]] — the luminous lineage: emitted light dissolving the object into a responsive environmental system.
- [[Cyborg Art]] — the cybernetic lineage: reciprocal feedback between work and organism; the terminal, communicative stage.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's framing of art as systems rather than objects; the theoretical ground of new media art.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — sibling research-focus: the participatory, responsive, viewer-activated dimension of technologically-mediated art.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — new media art sits on the "system" pole of this axis; it is defined against the static, place-bound, physically-present art object.

## Questions It Raises

- If the unit of art shifts from physical presence to information, what becomes of provenance, conservation, and the singular work?
- Does the artist–engineer symbiosis dilute or extend authorship, and does it produce a "technology gap" between subsidized and unsubsidized artists?
- Is technologically-driven immateriality a genuine break from the art object, or — as Burnham hints — does the immaterial aspiration remain shackled to fragile material apparatus?
- How does Burnham's 1968 ancestry (kinetic/light/cybernetic) extend to the telematic, networked art that Ascott will add to this note?
