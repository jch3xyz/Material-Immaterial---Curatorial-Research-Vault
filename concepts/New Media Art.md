# New Media Art

## Brief Definition

Art whose medium is technology itself — motion, light, electricity, feedback, and information — such that the static, hand-made object gives way to time-based, systemic, and responsive work.

## Longer Explanation

New Media Art names the family of practices in which the art-work's medium is no longer wood, bronze, or pigment but a technological process: real-time motion (kinetic art), emitted light and electromechanical display (light art), and cybernetic feedback coupling work and viewer (robot and cyborg art). Jack Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) supplies the direct ancestry of this category. Its master thesis — the migration of art from the OBJECT to the SYSTEM — runs through three successive chapters that this note draws on: Kineticism (Ch. 6), Light as a Sculpture Medium (Ch. 7), and Robot and Cyborg Art (Ch. 8). Across them, Burnham tracks how sculpture sheds its "thingness" and becomes a technologically-driven, temporal, systemic, and finally communicative thing. `Directly stated` (Burnham organizes the book's second half, "Sculpture as System," around exactly this migration).

In the kinetic phase, the work is reconstituted as a serviceable mechanism: "the Kinetic construction is not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art" — a system of interacting components that, like a living thing, needs repair and adjustment. `Directly stated`. With light, the medium becomes pure emitted energy, and the trend, in Burnham's words, "is to eliminate the specific art object and to transform the environment into a light-modulating system sensitive to responses from organisms which invade its presence" — i.e. the work dissolves into a responsive, participatory environment. `Directly stated`. With cybernetics, the work acquires reciprocal communication: where all prior sculpture (even kinetic art) delivers a one-way message, Burnham's "Cyborg Art" pursues "a sustained two-way experience" between work and observer, defining a cyborg as "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism." `Directly stated`. The throughline is a shift in the unit of art: "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." `Directly stated`.

These three technological lineages — kinetic, luminous, and cybernetic/responsive — are the structural genealogy of what later practice calls new media art: art that is time-based rather than fixed, systemic rather than objectal, and responsive/interactive rather than contemplated at a distance. Burnham also foregrounds the institutional and social conditions of such art: the new "artist–engineer symbiosis" (Experiments in Art and Technology, Inc., founded in the late 1960s) and the rising dependence of art on outside technical labor as it "accepts the technological scale of values." `Directly stated` (Ch. 6, Ch. 8). The immateriality that Klein made the New Tendency's "rallying cry" prefigures the dematerialized, energy-and-information condition Burnham sees art trending toward — a precursor to Lippard's later dematerialization. `Strongly implied`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — primary anchor. In *Beyond Modern Sculpture* the ancestry of new media art is the object-to-system migration across kinetic, light, and cybernetic art: the work becomes a `systeme d'art`, then a responsive light environment, then a feedback-coupled cyborg system in which "information" supplants physical presence.
- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — telematic extension. In *Telematic Embrace* Ascott carries the object→system spine into the networked/telematic phase: art "not as finite object but as process and system," "a web of relationships ... to which no single authorship is attributable," in which the spectator becomes a "participator who, in accessing the system, transforms it."
- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — adversarial/political extension. In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* the technological art-system reappears as the object of resistance: Zuboff's "art and science of hiding" is a vanguard of artists making anti-surveillance wearables, masks, and software against Big Other — new media art turned against the very sensate apparatus that earlier new media art celebrated.
- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** — conceptual-art precursor. Lippard's dematerialization of the art object (in *Six Years*) is the parallel/precursor lineage on the conceptual side; Burnham's technologically-driven immateriality and Lippard's dematerialization converge on the disappearance of the discrete material object. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — new media art sits on the "system" pole of this axis; it is defined against the static, place-bound, physically-present art object.

## Questions It Raises

- If the unit of art shifts from physical presence to information, what becomes of provenance, conservation, and the singular work?
- Does the artist–engineer symbiosis dilute or extend authorship, and does it produce a "technology gap" between subsidized and unsubsidized artists?
- Is technologically-driven immateriality a genuine break from the art object, or — as Burnham hints — does the immaterial aspiration remain shackled to fragile material apparatus?
- How does Burnham's 1968 ancestry (kinetic/light/cybernetic) extend to the telematic, networked art that Ascott will add to this note?
- When the technological system art once celebrated becomes a surveillance apparatus, does new media art turn against its own medium — and does that resistance (Zuboff's "art and science of hiding") count as mere adaptation or as outrage?

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

If Burnham supplies the cybernetic-art ancestry of new media art (object → system, across kinetic, light, and cyborg art), Roy Ascott carries the same spine forward into its **networked, telematic** phase — the dimension this note was explicitly built to receive. In *Telematic Embrace*, Ascott names the convergence of computers and communications networks as a new artistic medium ("telematics," after Nora and Minc), in which the discrete art object dissolves into a participatory process: "art itself becomes, not a discrete set of entities, but rather a web of relationships between ideas and images in constant flux, to which no single authorship is attributable, and whose meanings depend on the active participation of whoever enters the network." `Directly stated` (Ch. 15, "Art and Telematics," 1984). The viewer is no longer a spectator before a finished thing but an agent inside a live system: "The observer of the 'artwork' is a participator who, in accessing the system, transforms it." `Directly stated` (Ch. 15). This is Burnham's two-way / cyborg-feedback move (the work as `systeme d'art`, the responsive light environment, the reciprocally communicating cyborg) pushed onto the network — the participatory pole of the systems turn. `Interpretive synthesis`.

Ascott's 1988 statement makes the object-to-process migration explicit and total: "Art does not reside in the object alone, nor is meaning fixed or stable within the physical limits of the artist's work. Art is all process, all system." `Directly stated` (Ch. 17, "Art and Education in the Telematic Culture," 1988). Here the term "telematics" is sourced to its coinage — "the new electronic technology derived from the convergence of computers and telecommunications systems" — and interactivity is named "the essence of all telematic systems," collapsing the artist/viewer (producer/consumer) distinction into the "new composite role" of participant. `Directly stated` (Ch. 17). The networked artwork is never complete; it is, in Ascott's later formulation, "art not as finite object but as process and system, a fluid, moving stream of data configurations." `Directly stated` (Ch. 25, "The ARS Electronica Center Datapool," 1993).

By 1993 Ascott condenses the medium into a canonical schema — "Connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence are the key elements of the electronic arts in the 1990s" — which gives new media art its mature working vocabulary: data flows ("the datapool") into which the public must "plunge" rather than contemplate from a distance, and from which new forms emerge. `Directly stated` (Ch. 25). Across these three decades Ascott extends new media art from Burnham's 1968 cybernetic object-system into the dematerialized, distributed, network-scaled condition of [[Telematic Art]] and [[Network Consciousness]] — the telematic dimension the Burnham-anchored note above does not yet cover. `Interpretive synthesis`. (Ch. 04, Shanken's editor's introduction, frames this lineage but is Shanken's prose, not Ascott's; the durable claims above are Ascott's own essays, chs. 15/17/25.)

## Shoshana Zuboff (The Age of Surveillance Capitalism)

Where Burnham and Ascott build new media art *out of* the technological system — celebrating the migration from object to feedback-coupled, networked process — Shoshana Zuboff registers the moment that system becomes an instrument of power, and art turns *against* its own medium. In Chapter 17, "The Right to Sanctuary," she describes a contemporary artistic vanguard mobilized against Big Other, the ubiquitous sensate computational apparatus: "a new generation of activists, artists, and inventors feels itself called to create the art and science of hiding. The intolerable conditions of glass life compel these young artists to dedicate their genius to the prospects of human invisibility." `Directly stated`. This is new media art at its inflection point — work whose medium is still technology (computer vision, facial recognition, the network) but whose aim is evasion rather than embrace: Adam Harvey's thermal-reflective Stealth Wear and "Today's Selfie Is Tomorrow's Biometric Profile" mirror, Leo Selvaggio's prosthetic masks, Benjamin Grosser's "demetricators," Trevor Paglen, Ai Weiwei, the Backslash Tool Kit. `Strongly implied` (Zuboff catalogues these works; the framing as "new media art" is the vault's cross-cluster synthesis).

Zuboff casts these artists as "canaries in the coal mine" — "Our artists, like our young people, are canaries in the coal mine." `Directly stated`. Yet she is wary that an art of hiding settles for too little: like every counter-declaration it leaves the offending facts intact and "risks becomes an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage… Tunnels under this wall are not enough. This wall must come down." `Directly stated`. For this note the Zuboff chapter marks the keystone meeting of the vault's art/new-media cluster and its surveillance cluster: the participatory, responsive, system-coupled artwork that Burnham's [[Cyborg Art]] and Ascott's [[Telematic Art]] envisioned has, in the surveillance-capitalist present, become indistinguishable from the apparatus of [[Big Other]] — so that new media art's frontier is now resistance to the very rendition, [[Behavioral Surplus]], and prediction it once made its medium. `Interpretive synthesis`.
