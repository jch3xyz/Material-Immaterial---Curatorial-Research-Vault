# The Vanishing Base

## Brief Definition

The diachronic decline of the sculpture base — from a device of psychological segregation, to mere physical isolation, to outright disappearance — which Jack Burnham reads as the visible symptom of sculpture's migration from the idealized, earthbound object toward "a physical system including invisible forces."

## Longer Explanation

For Burnham the base is not a minor detail of the sculptor's craft but a diagnostic instrument: tracking what happens to it tracks what happens to sculpture itself. He narrates a withering in stages. In the representational tradition the base performed *psychological segregation* — it conferred "an aura of distance and dignity" and isolated "the particular psychology and anecdotal content of the activity it supports." With modern abstract and nonobjective art the base "declined in its purpose of psychological segregation to become the means of physically isolating one class of objects from all others — namely, sculpture." Once any object set on a base could be read as sculpture, the base "was rendered meaningless — both physically and psychically," and sculpture "could only regain meaning free of its confines." (`Directly stated`)

Burnham resolves this withering into two dominant trends. The first *incorporates the base into the sculpture itself*, until the work "rests directly on the floor or ground" (floor-bound sculpture — Picasso, David Smith, the Minimalists). The second *frees sculpture from all earthly contact*, "making sculpture not so much airborne as gravity defying" (the Constructivists' near-invisible struts, Lippold's guy-wired suspensions, the tensegrity and telemagnetic works). The terminus of the second trend is Alberto Collie's feedback-stabilized levitating titanium disk: "Any attempt to dislodge the disk relays a feedback signal which strengthens the magnetic field opposite the force pushing the disk out of equilibrium. Hence, the base has vanished altogether!" (`Directly stated`)

The reason the base *can* vanish is biological and metaphysical at once. The base "is a biological reaffirmation that man is constructed to walk the ground" — so "it was preordained that for sculpture not rooted to the ground or made inanimate, the base had lost its reason for being." Its withering is therefore the "secularization" of sculpture: the abandonment of the "old reverberations and implications of the 'art object'" in favor of "a naturalness where things, both objects and organisms, are accepted for what they are, not for what they represent." (`Directly stated`) When sculpture itself becomes intangible, the polarity can invert: "When sculpture is less and less tangible, the base becomes the only aspect which continues to show presence and substantiality" — and the base can be promoted to sculpture in its own right ("If Brancusi can incorporate the base into his sculptures, what is to prevent the base, in later hands, from becoming sculpture?"). This is the *base-as-anti-base* movement, treated alongside Duchamp's Readymades. (`Directly stated`)

Read at vault scale, the vanishing base is Burnham's sculptural register of the shift from object to system: when "sculpture completely liberated from its base... becomes a different animal," its "raison d'etre is no longer that it embodies formal qualities, but that it exists as a physical system including invisible forces" — and "the duality between matter and energy enters a new phase." The base is the index of where sculpture sits on the material/immaterial spectrum, and its disappearance is the moment matter yields to force. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — The vanishing base is Burnham's coinage and the title figure of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*'s first chapter. It is a *symptom* to be read, not a technique to be praised: "the base is symptomatic and deserves to be given the closest attention." Its two trends (incorporate / liberate) and its inversion (base-as-anti-base) chart sculpture's exit from the idealized object toward the physical system. (`Directly stated`)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Sculpture Base]] — the vanishing base names the *negation* of the base's traditional functions (psychological segregation, biotic orientation, fixed repose); the relation is the dialectical decline of the very thing.

## Questions It Raises

- If the base's disappearance "secularizes" sculpture by stripping the "art object" of its transcendental aura, what survives as the locus of aesthetic distance once even substantiality migrates into invisible forces?
- When the base can itself be promoted to sculpture (base-as-anti-base), has the base truly vanished, or merely changed places with the work it once supported?
- Is Collie's feedback-stabilized disk the *end* of the base's history, or the point at which "base" and "system" become indistinguishable?
