# Visionary Pragmatism

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name (drawn through Richard Rorty's pragmatism) for what artists do and have always done — the visionary impulse joined to grounded pragmatism: "flying with your feet on the ground… working with the future in the present."

## Longer Explanation

Visionary pragmatism is Ascott's organizing methodological category in the "moist" essay "Art @ the Edge of the Net: the Future Will Be Moist!" (the "Moist Manifesto," 2000). It names the fusion of two attitudes usually held apart — the seer's reach toward what does not yet exist and the maker's grounded competence in making it real. Ascott coins the term explicitly: "This visionary pragmatism, as I shall call it, is about flying with your feet on the ground. It's about working with the future in the present. It is living life acausally but not casually." `Directly stated` At its most efficacious, he writes, it "combines, within the artistic domain, the perennial wisdom of shamans and gnostics with contemporary insights of scientists, engineers, and philosophers" — making it a near-synonym for the [[Technoetics|technoetic]] (techne + gnōsis) program that runs through the late Ascott.

The philosophical backbone is Richard Rorty's pragmatism, which Ascott reads as licensing art to construct rather than merely describe reality: art "can only be evaluated by the new language it produces," and Rorty's pragmatism "eschews the sanctity of philosophy in favour of the artist's visionary impulse and search for metaphor that leads to the continual construction of reality and of the self." `Directly stated` So visionary pragmatism is not a vague optimism; it is a constructivist account of the artist's job — bringing dreams to earth so they materialise as new languages, tools, societies, and selves. Ascott insists it "is an attribute to be fostered in all aspiring artists… least understood but most in need of support." `Directly stated`

Crucially for this vault, Ascott credits visionary pragmatism with two concrete, opposite-seeming consequences. In digital culture it "has led to what is known as ubiquitous computing, where intelligence spreads throughout the network and into every facet of the environment." `Directly stated` And, at the same time, "it is visionary pragmatism that has pushed the consequences of a screen-based, immaterial world into the re-materialisation of culture involving molecules and atoms, nanotechnology and neurons." `Directly stated` This double move — dematerialising the world into the network *and* re-materialising it into [[Moistmedia]] — is why visionary pragmatism is the methodological complement to Ascott's moist thesis: "Visionary pragmatism finds in moistmedia its creative ally, with its triad of computational exactitude, biological fluidity, and technoetic complexity." `Directly stated` It thereby sits across the vault's spine tension [[Material vs Immaterial]], refusing to settle on either pole.

Ascott frames visionary pragmatism as a corrective to the mood of its moment: "It corners the nihilism and despair of late postmodernism" and "springs forth into the post-biological culture with a radical constructivism" — "a case of 'bye-bye Baudrillard,'" reversing the era's "sense of terminal decline." `Directly stated` This is a pointed disagreement with the simulacral pessimism associated with Jean Baudrillard, and it pairs with Ascott's affirmative reading of "the [[Telenoia|telenoia]] of universal connectivity and transparency." `Strongly implied`

Folded here is **the transformative vocation** — Ascott's re-description, via the Brazilian thinker and legal theorist Roberto Mangabeira Unger, of the artist's role. It "replaces the historical sense of the artist's role as an 'honourable calling' with the idea of such work as a 'transformative vocation'" — work that, in Unger's words, "connects self-fulfilment and transformation." `Directly stated` The transformative vocation is the social/ethical face of visionary pragmatism: not contemplation of the given but the construction of change. Ascott's architectural example — that "Visionary pragmatism, in architecture as in other creative practice, must embrace the anticipatory design science advocated by Buckminster Fuller" — extends the same logic toward [[The Building of Sentience|the building of sentience]]. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins "visionary pragmatism" in the Moist Manifesto (ch. 31) as a description of "what it is that artists do, or seek to achieve, and have done throughout cultural time": the visionary impulse joined to a Rortyan, constructivist pragmatism. Its creative ally is [[Moistmedia]]; its social face is the (Unger-derived) transformative vocation; its named consequences are ubiquitous computing and the re-materialisation of culture.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — visionary pragmatism does not resolve to either pole; it drives *both* the immaterial screen-based network and the re-materialisation of culture into molecules, atoms, nanotechnology, and neurons, and so sits across this tension rather than on one side.

## Questions It Raises

- Is visionary pragmatism a genuinely distinct methodology, or a re-description of the avant-garde's perennial self-image ("what artists do… throughout cultural time")?
- Ascott reads "universal connectivity and transparency" as emancipatory; [[Transparency]] in Han is a neoliberal dispositive of control. Does visionary pragmatism's optimism underwrite the very [[Digital Panopticon]] it overlooks?
- Can a single attitude legitimately claim to have produced both ubiquitous computing and the re-materialisation of culture, or does that breadth dilute the concept?
- Does "cornering the nihilism of late postmodernism" defeat Baudrillardian critique or merely change the subject ("bye-bye Baudrillard")?
