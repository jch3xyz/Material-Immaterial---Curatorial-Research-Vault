# Nanobots

## Brief Definition

In Ray Kurzweil's *The Singularity Is Near*, nanobots are robots "the size of human blood cells (seven to eight microns) or even smaller" that, by the 2020s, will travel through the brain's capillaries in their billions to scan every salient neural feature from inside the living brain — the route Kurzweil proposes to the scan resolution that reverse-engineering and ultimately uploading the brain would require.

## Longer Explanation

The concept enters Chapter 4 ("How to Reverse Engineer the Human Brain") as the answer to a scanning problem. Kurzweil distinguishes scanning the brain *from outside* the skull (improving but limited) from "the most powerful approach to capturing every salient neural detail," which "will be to scan it from inside." Nanobots are the instrument of this internal scan. He defines them by scale — blood-cell size, "seven to eight microns," or smaller — and by number: "Billions of them could travel through every brain capillary, scanning each relevant neural feature from up close" (`Directly stated`).

Crucially, the nanobots are not isolated probes but a **networked swarm**. "Using high-speed wireless communication, the nanobots would communicate with one another and with computers compiling the brain-scan database," so that "the nanobots and computers will all be on a wireless local area network." The brain-scan, in this picture, is a distributed, real-time data-gathering operation: Kurzweil writes that "sending billions of nanobots through its capillaries will enable us to noninvasively scan an entire working brain in real time." The body's interior is recast as a sensor field — millions or billions of "highly sensitive and very high-resolution sensors (in the form of nanobots)" placed throughout the brain to "witness in breathtaking detail living brains in action" (`Directly stated`).

Kurzweil treats the chief obstacle as biological rather than computational: "A key technical challenge to interfacing nanobots with biological brain structures is the blood-brain barrier (BBB)." He catalogs strategies for crossing it — shrinking the nanobot below twenty nanometers, projecting a robotic arm through the barrier while staying in the bloodstream, fully noninvasive scanning from within the capillaries, chemically opening the BBB, or (a tactic he attributes to Robert Freitas) having the nanobot "literally to barge its way past the BBB by breaking a hole in it, exit the blood vessel, and then repair the damage," "crudely analogous to a burrowing mole" (`Directly stated`). The timeline is explicit and recurrent: nanobot technology "will be viable" and "brain scanning will be one of its prominent applications" "by the 2020s," with the scanning prerequisites for [[Mind Uploading]] available "by the late 2020s" (`Directly stated`).

Because nanobots recur across the second half of the book — as the bridge between biological and nonbiological intelligence, and as a load-bearing element of the GNR (genetics, nanotechnology, robotics) program — this note is the standalone entity rather than a subsection of the reverse-engineering chapter. This Half-1 pass establishes the concept and its brain-scanning role; the GNR and nanotechnology chapters (to be ingested later) extend it toward Kurzweil's broader claims about nanobots augmenting cognition, entering the bloodstream for health, and constituting the substrate of the post-Singularity body (`Interpretive synthesis`). Already in Chapter 4 the augmentation arc appears: "In the 2020s we will use nanobots to begin augmenting our brains with nonbiological intelligence," so that "we will have effectively uploaded ourselves, albeit gradually, never quite noticing the transfer" (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — coins the operative usage in the vault: blood-cell-scale robots, deployed in the billions, wirelessly networked, scanning the brain from within its capillaries. For Kurzweil the nanobot is simultaneously a *measuring* device (the scan that captures the brain as [[Information]], feeding [[Patternism]] and [[Mind Uploading]]) and an *augmenting* device (the gradual replacement that makes the nonbiological portion of intelligence predominate). The figure belongs to his broader [[Transhumanism]] and his treatment of [[The Body as Data]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Mind Uploading]] — nanobot scanning supplies the "noninvasive" internal scan that the upload scenario depends on; Kurzweil dates the scanning prerequisites of uploading to the late 2020s / early 2030s.
- [[Patternism]] — nanobots harvest the neural "pattern of information" that, on Kurzweil's view, constitutes identity.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the nanobot turns the brain's interior into a readable, networked dataset.
- [[Transhumanism]] — nanobots are a concrete mechanism in Kurzweil's program for transcending biological limits.
- [[Information]] — the scan reduces neural structure to a brain-scan database compiled over a wireless network.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the nanobot scan presumes that the brain's "salient" features can be extracted as information and re-instantiated, a premise the embodiment tradition (e.g. [[N. Katherine Hayles]]) resists by holding that mind is inseparable from its material, lived body. The opposition is over whether scanning the body yields the person.

## Questions It Raises

- If a scan is gathered by billions of nanobots over time rather than instantaneously, does the "upload" capture a person at an instant or only "over a period of time"? Kurzweil raises and dismisses this himself, arguing it "will not interfere with an upload's passing a 'Jane Smith' Turing test."
- Does scanning "each relevant neural feature" presuppose that the relevant features are knowable in advance — and that mind is exhausted by them?
- What is the status of consent and bodily integrity when the route to knowledge of the brain runs through breaching the blood-brain barrier from inside the bloodstream?
- Does the same swarm that scans the brain also surveil it? The wireless brain-scan database invites the surveillance and dataist concerns elsewhere in the vault (cf. [[Dataism]]).
