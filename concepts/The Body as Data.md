# The Body as Data

## Brief Definition

The conception — at once dream and nightmare — that the human body is essentially an informational pattern, such that flesh becomes a derivative, inessential, or even disposable instantiation of a code that could in principle be read off, traded, ported, or made to survive the body's destruction.

## General Orientation

The authors gathered here all describe the same inversion — physicality demoted, information installed as the operative reality — but they disagree sharply over whether it is a fantasy to contest, a condition to diagnose, a fact to report, or a goal to hasten. They sort into recognizable camps.

**The critics** treat the body-as-data as an ideologically loaded construction to be unmasked rather than endorsed. [[N. Katherine Hayles]] names "the body as information" a "dream or nightmare" generated by the prior, contingent separation of [[Information]] from materiality, and her recovery of [[Embodiment]] and [[The Materiality of Informatics]] is designed to reverse it. [[Donna Haraway]] gives the thesis its earliest and most explicitly feminist-critical statement: under "the informatics of domination," "organisms have ceased to exist as objects of knowledge, giving way to biotic components, i.e., special kinds of information-processing devices," and biotechnology and microelectronics "have recently textualized our bodies as code problems on the grid of C3I" — a reduction she alone reads through *gendered* stakes, asking whose bodies are made permeable to "visualization" and "intervention." [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] reports the same construction as already arrived in lived form: not the someday upload but the "second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes" that big data treats as "most valuable and real," closing on the felt reduction in which "the soul is little more than a data set."

**The surveillance-capital diagnosticians** relocate the body-as-data from metaphysics to infrastructure and economy. For [[Byung-Chul Han]] the stored trace of "the clicks we make and the search words we type" condenses into a "digital habitus" that supplies "an extremely precise likeness of our persons - of our very souls," read by the aperspectival [[Digital Panopticon]], steered by micro-targeting "faster than free will," and traded as commodity — yet it "counts without recounting," so it can control and sell the self without knowing it. [[Shoshana Zuboff]] turns Han's likeness into a supply operation she names **rendition**: "the body is simply a set of coordinates in time and space where sensation and action are translated as data," "reimagined as a behaving object to be tracked and calculated for indexing and search" and converted into [[Behavioral Surplus]] for prediction. The apparatus, [[Big Other]], "poaches our behavior for surplus and leaves behind all the meaning lodged in our bodies," so "you are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass" — the body-as-data at its sharpest political-economic stakes.

**The literalizers and celebrants** affirm the equation the critics resist. [[Yuval Noah Harari]] reports it as biology's reigning dogma — not the body recoded as data but the body declared to *be* data-processing: "the baboon's entire body is the calculator," "what we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms," with a carbon/silicon substrate-indifference whose practical face is the [[Quantified Self]]'s "self-knowledge through numbers." [[Ray Kurzweil]] preaches it as goal: the body is "version 1.0" hardware, "frail and subject to a myriad of failure modes," whose complexity is mostly "life-support functions, not its information-processing capabilities," so the mind can be "ported" to "a more suitable computational substrate" — an avowed "patternist" for whom "patterns of information" are "the fundamental reality" and we are, "at least for bits of information," what "we comprise."

**The precursors and the art-theoretical horizon** supply the construction's deeper grounds. [[Marshall McLuhan]] gives the thesis its broadest formulation: electric media "extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace," and by "putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems" all prior bodily extensions "will be translated into information systems," so that "our private and corporate lives have become information processes" — the planetary informational field that is the precondition of every later variant. [[Jack Burnham]] reaches the thesis from sculpture: where "with Object Art physical presence is everything," in "Systems Art 'information' is the key factor," and the living body becomes a "life-bearing artifact" to be re-created in information. [[Roy Ascott]] describes a body already redistributed into the telematic field by "the cybernet," which "redefines our individual body, just as it connects all our bodies into a planetary whole" — but he treats this less as the flesh's erasure than as its convergence with the immaterial, "the post-biological body as interface." Beneath all of them lies [[Norbert Wiener]]'s proto-formulation, the source Hayles traces: "We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves."

## Longer Explanation

The body-as-data thesis rests on a single move: the prior separation of [[Information]] from materiality. Once "information lost its body" and came to be theorized as a pattern independent of any particular medium, the human body could be recoded as one more substrate that the pattern merely happens to occupy. From there it is a short step to the posthuman assumption that "embodiment in a biological substrate is seen as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life." [[N. Katherine Hayles]] makes this move visible in order to reverse it, naming "the body as information" a "dream or nightmare" that runs through cybernetics, molecular biology, and cyberpunk alike. Its genealogy runs back through [[Norbert Wiener]] — "We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves" — through Hans Moravec's "roboticist's dream" of downloading consciousness into a computer, and into William Gibson's *Neuromancer*, where "pattern is the essential reality, presence an optical illusion" and the body is "data made flesh." Hayles reads that phrase exactly — "Information is the putative origin, physicality the derivative manifestation" — to show the hierarchy inverted, and insists against it that "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium" and that embodiment "can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated."

[[Donna Haraway]] had already diagnosed the construction's emergence as a historical and political event. Her "informatics of domination" charts the transitions "organism → biotic component" and "physiology → communications engineering": "organisms have ceased to exist as objects of knowledge, giving way to biotic components, i.e., special kinds of information-processing devices." Like Hayles she advances this as critique — situating it within "C3I, command-control-communication-intelligence" and the sociobiology that rests on "a high-tech view of the body as a biotic component or cybernetic communications system" — but she alone makes *whose* body the central question, naming reproductive medicine, where "women's bodies have boundaries newly permeable to both 'visualization' and 'intervention,'" and answering neither with the organic goddess nor with disembodiment but with "feminist cyborg stories" that "recod[e] communication and intelligence to subvert command and control."

Where these critics treat the body-as-data as fantasy or as domination to be contested, others report it as already operative. [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] collapses the speculative distance: "we are already living the dualistic existence that [[Ray Kurzweil]] promised," a "second self that is purely informational and immaterial" that big data treats as "most valuable and real," so that the flesh is demoted from the inside as a felt loss — "the soul is little more than a data set." [[Byung-Chul Han]] gives the dossier its surveillance-capital infrastructure and power: the "digital habitus" is "an extremely precise likeness of our persons - of our very souls," potentially "fuller and more accurate than the images we otherwise make of ourselves," read by an aperspectival [[Digital Panopticon]] and traded as commodity — yet "Numbers do not recount anything about the self. Counting is not recounting," so the likeness is exhaustive and empty at once. [[Shoshana Zuboff]] names the operation that manufactures the likeness: **rendition**, in which "no thing counts until it is rendered as behavior" and "the body is simply a set of coordinates in time and space where sensation and action are translated as data," "reimagined as a behaving object to be tracked and calculated for indexing and search" across "organs, blood, eyes, brain waves, faces, gait, posture." Her [[Big Other]] is steadfastly indifferent — it "poaches our behavior for surplus and leaves behind all the meaning lodged in our bodies," so against O'Gieblyn's most-valuable double Zuboff insists "you are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass."

Against all of these, [[Yuval Noah Harari]] and [[Ray Kurzweil]] affirm the equation the critics resist. For Harari the flesh is not the bearer of a peelable pattern but is itself the running computation: "the baboon's entire body is the calculator. What we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms," a "current scientific dogma" whose corollary — "what does it matter whether the algorithms are manifested in carbon or silicon?" — is precisely the disembodiment Hayles denaturalizes, now reported as fact and turned into a method by the [[Quantified Self]] movement's motto, "Self-knowledge through numbers." Kurzweil preaches the same disembodiment as emancipation: the body is "version 1.0" hardware whose neuronal complexity is mostly "life-support functions, not its information-processing capabilities," so "we will be able to port our mental processes to a more suitable computational substrate." His [[Patternism|patternism]] lands squarely on Wiener and Gibson, stripped of Hayles's ambivalence: we are, "at least for bits of information," what "we comprise" — the body-as-data as the good news, the premise of [[Transcending Biology]].

Underneath the metaphysics lie the precursors and the art-theoretical horizon. [[Marshall McLuhan]] supplies the cluster's deepest root: electric media "extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace," approaching "the technological simulation of consciousness," and by "putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems" all bodily extensions "will be translated into information systems" until "our private and corporate lives have become information processes" — the same demotion Hayles names, reached from the opposite direction by relocating the body *inside* its informational extensions, and tinged with the dread of putting "one's nerves outside." [[Jack Burnham]] reaches it through sculpture's migration from object to system — "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor" — and reconceives the living body as a "life-bearing artifact" to be re-created in information. [[Roy Ascott]] describes the body already dispersed into "the cybernet," which "redefines our individual body, just as it connects all our bodies into a planetary whole," so that "human presence becomes virtualised and the individual self is distributed"; but his is the body-as-data as *convergence* rather than erasure — "the post-biological body as interface," the immaterial made "as palpably and immediately" perceptible as material objects, forecasting a "marriage of the immaterial and the material." Together the cluster names the recurring stakes: what, exactly, is lost when flesh is treated as a derivative manifestation of pattern, and whether that loss is a cost to mourn (Hayles, Han, O'Gieblyn, Zuboff), a fact to accept (Harari), a goal to hasten (Kurzweil), or a re-enchantment to welcome (Ascott).

## The Organism as Biotic Component (Haraway)

Before Hayles named the "dream or nightmare of the body as information," **[[Donna Haraway]]** had already diagnosed its emergence as a historical and political event. In *A Cyborg Manifesto* (1985) she charts a "movement from an organic, industrial society to a polymorphous, information system," tabulated as a series of transitions from "the comfortable old hierarchical dominations to the scary new networks I have called the informatics of domination" — among them "organism → biotic component," "physiology → communications engineering," and "biology as clinical practice → biology as inscription." The decisive formulation is unambiguous: "In a sense, organisms have ceased to exist as objects of knowledge, giving way to biotic components, i.e., special kinds of information-processing devices." The body, in other words, is recoded as data not by a future upload but by the present-day convergence of "communications sciences and modern biologies," which are "constructed by a common move—the translation of the world into a problem of coding, a search for a common language in which all resistance to instrumental control disappears and all heterogeneity can be submitted to disassembly, reassembly, investment, and exchange." This is the earliest statement in the cluster of the thesis Hayles would later set out to denaturalize and Han would later relocate to the surveillance-capital infrastructure: the organism rendered as [[Information]]-processing device, its "essential properties" replaced by "design, boundary constraints, rates of flows, systems logics." `Directly stated`

Like Hayles, O'Gieblyn, and Han — and unlike Harari and Kurzweil — Haraway advances this *as critique*, situating the reduction within "the informatics of domination" and tracing its operation through the technical metaphor "C3I, command-control-communication-intelligence, the military's symbol for its operations theory." She is especially attentive to how biology itself performs the translation: "The organism has been translated into problems of genetic coding and readout," so that "biology here is a kind of cryptography" and "research is necessarily a kind of intelligence activity." Her sharpest example is the sociobiological account of the body, which she names as the discipline that most nakedly literalizes the equation: "These sociobiological stories depend on a high-tech view of the body as a biotic component or cybernetic communications system." This anticipates by decades exactly the [[Organisms Are Algorithms|biological literalism]] Harari would later report as "current scientific dogma" — except that where Harari endorses "the baboon's entire body is the calculator," Haraway flags "high-tech view of the body as a biotic component" as an *ideological* construction with "costs of lowering constraints," to be unmasked rather than affirmed. `Directly stated`

What distinguishes Haraway's account from every other author's in this note is the gendered and political stakes she attaches to the body-as-data: she insists on asking *whose* bodies are rendered code, and to what end. The reduction is not neutral but a "world system of production/reproduction and communication" into which women are integrated and exploited — "the body itself—all can be dispersed and interfaced in nearly infinite, polymorphous ways, with large consequences for women and others." Reproductive medicine is her paradigm case: "Among the many transformations of reproductive situations is the medical one, where women's bodies have boundaries newly permeable to both 'visualization' and 'intervention.' Of course, who controls the interpretation of bodily boundaries in medical hermeneutics is a major feminist issue." The textualization of the body is therefore, for Haraway, simultaneously a technique of domination and a site of contest: "phallogocentric origin stories... built into the literal technologies—technologies that write the world, biotechnology and microelectronics—that have recently textualized our bodies as code problems on the grid of C3I. Feminist cyborg stories have the task of recoding communication and intelligence to subvert command and control." This recoding ambition is what keeps Haraway from collapsing into either pole of the [[Embodiment vs Information]] tension: she neither defends the organic body against technology (she famously "would rather be a cyborg than a goddess") nor celebrates disembodiment, but seizes the code-grid as terrain for a "powerful infidel heteroglossia" against the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|"integrity" or "sincerity" of the Western self]] that "gives way to decision procedures and expert systems." She is thus the cluster's genealogical origin: the first to name the body's translation into data, the first to read it as domination, and the only one to make whose-body-and-to-what-end the central question. `Directly stated` (the verbatim formulations are exact; the framing of Haraway as the cluster's genealogical root and the cross-author positioning is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## The Second Self and the Data Double (O'Gieblyn)

Where Hayles diagnoses the body-as-data as a fantasy still to come, O'Gieblyn argues that we already inhabit it. In *God, Human, Animal, Machine* she follows Hayles's own genealogy — citing *How We Became Posthuman* on how, during the Macy Conferences, information "became decontextualized and began to seem like 'an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates,'" until it was rendered "a mathematical quantity weightless as sunshine" and so became "the materialist's substitute for the soul." But she then collapses the speculative distance between that abstraction and the present. "In a way," she writes, "we are already living the dualistic existence that [[Ray Kurzweil]] promised. In addition to our physical bodies, there exists—somewhere in the ether—a second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes that lingers not in some transcendent nirvana but rather in the shadowy dossiers of third-party aggregators." This is the body-as-data not as upload but as dossier — the lived realization of the very [[Mind as Machine]] equation O'Gieblyn traces back through transhumanism.

The decisive inversion is one of value, and it echoes Hayles's reading of physicality as "the derivative manifestation." These second selves, O'Gieblyn notes, "are entirely without agency or consciousness; they have no preferences, no desires, no hopes or spiritual impulses, and yet in the purely informational sphere of big data, it is they, not we, that are most valuable and real." The flesh is demoted; the data double is promoted to the status of "most valuable and real." Citing Jaron Lanier, she names the ideological work this performs — "a digital climate in which 'bits are presented as if they were alive, while humans are transient fragments'" — which makes O'Gieblyn's data double the present-tense, market-driven form of exactly the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] hierarchy Hayles contests, and aligns it with the [[Transparency]] cluster's account of the quantified, exhibited self.

The book closes its ninth chapter on the lived testimony of this condition. Immersed in the internet and feeling herself dissolve into a [[The Computational Universe|collective]] mind, O'Gieblyn describes "my own blurred boundaries, seized by the suspicion that I am not forming new opinions so much as assimilating them, that all my preferences can be predicted and neatly reduced to type, that the soul is little more than a data set." Where Hayles works to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," O'Gieblyn registers the erasure from the inside, as a felt loss — the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist self]] experiencing its own reduction to [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]]. "I don't know exactly what to call this state of affairs," she ends, "but it does not feel like the Kingdom of God."

## Psychopolitics (Han) — the digital habitus as a likeness of the soul

In the "Big Data" chapter of *Psychopolitics*, Han gives the body-as-data thesis its surveillance-capital inflection. Where Hayles diagnoses a fantasy and O'Gieblyn an already-living data double, Han describes the *infrastructure* that produces the data-likeness and the power that reads it. "Today," he writes, "the clicks we make and the search words we type are stored. Every step is watched and recorded. A complete picture of our lives exists on the internet. Our digital habitus provides an extremely precise likeness of our persons - of our very souls. Perhaps it is even fuller and more accurate than the images we otherwise make of ourselves." The reduction here is not merely of the body to a pattern but of the *soul* to a habitus of stored traces — a likeness that, Han suggests, may out-resolve self-knowledge itself. This is the [[Mind as Machine|equation of self and data set]] that O'Gieblyn registers as felt loss, restated as an accomplished technical fact: the [[Digital Panopticon]] "can peer into the human soul itself" because, unlike Bentham's perspectival optics, it is aperspectival and "eliminates all blind spots." `Directly stated`.

What makes Han's body-as-data load-bearing for the surveillance-and-control cluster is its political and economic edge. The data-likeness is not a contemplative self-portrait but an object of power and of trade. Big Data, Han argues, "is making the id into an ego to be exploited psychopolitically": through the "digital unconscious" it can "read desires we do not know we harbour," and through micro-targeting — "the practical microphysics of power" — it intervenes "in psychic processes in a prospective fashion," quite possibly "even faster than free will," so that "it could overtake it. If so, this would herald the end of freedom." Simultaneously the likeness is monetized: "personal data are unceasingly monetized and commercialized... people are treated and traded as packages of data for economic use. That is, human beings have become a commodity." The digital habitus is thus the hinge where the [[The Body as Data|body-as-data]] thesis crosses into [[Freedom vs Control]], [[Auto-Exploitation]], and the [[Exhibition Value|attention-commodity]] economy. `Directly stated`.

Han's account also sharpens *why* the data-likeness, however precise, is not self-knowledge — a point that converges with Hayles's insistence on the irreducibility of [[Embodiment]] and O'Gieblyn's worry about reduction "to type." For Han the digital habitus counts but cannot recount: "no insight into the self can result from data and numbers alone... Numbers do not recount anything about the self. Counting is not recounting." The "Quantified Self," outfitting "the body... with sensors that automatically register data," yields a mounting pile that "does nothing to answer the simple question. Who am I?" The likeness is exhaustive yet empty — a data-likeness of the person that, lacking [[Narrativity|narration]], "empties the self of any and all meaning." Han's body-as-data is therefore at once the most precise and the most soulless: a [[Gleichschaltung|statistical likeness]] adequate to control and to commerce but blind to the singular self it claims to mirror. `Strongly implied` (Han ties the precision of the likeness and its emptiness to one and the same Big Data regime, but the explicit reconciliation of "extremely precise likeness" with "empties the self of meaning" is the synthesis of two passages in the chapter).

## Organisms as Biochemical Algorithms (Harari)

If Hayles diagnoses the body-as-data as a contested fantasy, O'Gieblyn as an already-living dossier, and Han as a surveillance-capital likeness, **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** marks the point where the equation stops being figurative and is asserted as biological fact. In *Homo Deus* the body is not *recoded as* data; it *is* a data-processing system — the body-as-data thesis given its [[Organisms Are Algorithms|biological face]] and presented as "current scientific dogma." Harari builds the claim up in "The Anthropocene" (Ch. 2 in the file numbering), where emotions are reframed not as "some mysterious spiritual phenomenon" but as "biochemical algorithms that are vital for the survival and reproduction of all mammals." The vending-machine analogy is then extended to the human: "the man pressing the buttons and drinking the tea is also an algorithm. A much more complicated algorithm than the vending machine, no doubt, but still an algorithm." The decisive literalization comes when he asks how a baboon weighs a lion against a bunch of bananas and answers that there is no separate computer doing the sums: "the baboon's entire body is the calculator. What we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms." The flesh is not the bearer of an informational pattern that could be peeled off it (Hayles's "data made flesh"); the flesh is *itself* the running computation. This is the body-as-data thesis at its most uncompromising — not the body abstracted into data, but the body declared to have been calculation all along. `Directly stated`

The thesis is load-bearing because of its substrate-independence corollary, which is exactly the move Hayles names as the body-as-data's founding error. In "The Great Decoupling" Harari compresses it to three principles: "Organisms are algorithms. Every animal – including Homo sapiens – is an assemblage of organic algorithms shaped by natural selection over millions of years of evolution"; "Algorithmic calculations are not affected by the materials from which you build the calculator"; and "As long as the calculations remain valid, what does it matter whether the algorithms are manifested in carbon or silicon?" The carbon/silicon indifference is precisely the disembodiment Hayles contests — "embodiment in a biological substrate is seen as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life" — except that Harari advances it not as a "dream or nightmare" but as the reigning view of "the life sciences." Where Hayles works to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," Harari treats the erasability of the flesh as a settled empirical result: if the calculation is what matters, the carbon it runs on is incidental. The two authors thus stand at opposite poles of the same [[Embodiment vs Information]] tension — Harari reporting as fact the proposition Hayles spent a book denaturalizing. `Directly stated`

The practical face of this literalized body-as-data is biometric self-tracking, which Harari treats as the everyday method that follows once the self is read off numbers rather than introspection. In "The Great Decoupling" he describes wearables and DNA tests that "record diverse biometric data" and feed it to algorithms that "tell you who you are and what you should do," and he names the ideology directly: "The Quantified Self movement argues that the self is nothing but mathematical patterns... So if you wish to obey the old adage and know thyself, you should not waste your time on philosophy, meditation or psychoanalysis, but rather you should systematically collect biometric data... The movement's motto is 'Self-knowledge through numbers.'" The self, on this account, is to be discovered the way Angelina Jolie's risk was discovered — by listening to genes "whose voice manifested not in feelings but in numbers" — so that one consults an "inner genetic parliament" instead of an inner voice. This is the inversion O'Gieblyn registers as felt loss and Han diagnoses as a likeness that "counts but cannot recount," restated by Harari as a *program of self-knowledge*: where Han insists "counting is not recounting," Harari reports a movement that holds counting to *be* recounting, the only reliable route to "know thyself." The biological body-as-data thus completes the cluster — Harari supplies the empirical-sounding warrant ("organisms are algorithms") that the surveillance and market apparatus described by Han and O'Gieblyn presupposes, and turns the [[Quantified Self]] from symptom into method. `Directly stated` (the Quantified Self passages and the "Self-knowledge through numbers" motto are verbatim; the synthesis tying them to the body-as-data cluster is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## The Body as Version 1.0 Hardware (Kurzweil)

If Hayles diagnoses the body-as-data as a contested fantasy, O'Gieblyn as an already-living dossier, Han as a surveillance-capital likeness, and Harari as biology's reigning dogma, **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** supplies the series' celebratory extreme: the body-as-data not as cost or fact but as *goal*. In *The Singularity Is Near* the flesh is provisional engineering — "version 1.0" hardware awaiting upgrade. "Our version 1.0 biological bodies," he writes in "The Six Epochs," "are likewise frail and subject to a myriad of failure modes, not to mention the cumbersome maintenance rituals they require." The framing is the inverse of Hayles's: where she works to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," Kurzweil treats the flesh as a draft to be superseded, its frailty an argument for porting the pattern elsewhere. This is the [[Version 1.0 Biological Bodies|version-1.0]] figure that anchors his case for [[Transcending Biology]]. `Directly stated`

The decisive move — and the one that places Kurzweil at the opposite pole from Hayles on the [[Embodiment vs Information]] tension — is his claim that the body's complexity is mostly *not* informational at all, and so is dispensable. In "Achieving the Computational Capacity of the Human Brain" he writes: "Most of the complexity of a human neuron is devoted to maintaining its life-support functions, not its information-processing capabilities. Ultimately, we will be able to port our mental processes to a more suitable computational substrate. Then our minds won't have to stay so small." Where Hayles insists that "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium" and that embodiment "can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated," Kurzweil partitions the body into information-processing (the part that matters, portable) and life-support (mere overhead, discardable), so that the pattern can be lifted clean off its biological substrate. This is precisely the separation of [[Information]] from materiality that Hayles set out to denaturalize — here advanced not as illusion but as the engineering rationale for [[Mind Uploading]]. `Directly stated`

Underwriting both moves is Kurzweil's avowed [[Patternism|patternism]]: he describes himself in the Prologue as "a 'patternist,' someone who views patterns of information as the fundamental reality," noting that "the particles composing my brain and body change within weeks, but there is a continuity to the patterns that these particles make" — a formulation that lands squarely on Wiener's "patterns that perpetuate themselves" and Gibson's "data made flesh," except stripped of Hayles's ambivalence. The book's governing equation is compressed in the same Prologue, where Kurzweil entertains time-reversal "at least for bits of information, which essentially is what we comprise." We *are* bits of information: this is the body-as-data thesis at its most affirmative, the construction O'Gieblyn registers as felt loss and Han as a likeness that "counts but cannot recount," restated by Kurzweil as a destiny worth engineering toward. Where every prior author treats the reduction of the self to pattern as at minimum a tension and at most a catastrophe, Kurzweil treats it as the good news — the premise of [[Transcending Biology]] and the [[The Singularity Is Near|Singularity]] itself. `Directly stated` (the patternist and "what we comprise" formulations are verbatim; the synthesis tying them to the body-as-data cluster's value-inversion is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## The Technological Simulation of Consciousness (McLuhan)

If Hayles dates the body-as-data to the postwar cybernetics that "decontextualized" information, **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** had already, in *Understanding Media* (1964), given the thesis its broadest and earliest formulation — not as a fantasy of uploading a particular self but as the structural endpoint of the entire history of media. His governing thesis is that all media are "extensions of man": the mechanical ages "extended our bodies in space," but with electricity something categorically different occurs. "Today," he writes in the Introduction, "after more than a century of electric technology, we have extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace, abolishing both space and time as far as our planet is concerned. Rapidly, we approach the final phase of the extensions of man— the technological simulation of consciousness, when the creative process of knowing will be collectively and corporately extended to the whole of human society." This is the body-as-data thesis at its most cosmological: not the flesh recoded as a dossier (O'Gieblyn) or declared a calculator (Harari), but the central nervous system itself externalized into a planetary informational field — the precondition that makes every later version of the cluster thinkable. `Directly stated`

The decisive McLuhan move, and the one that most directly anticipates the vault's body-as-information cluster, is his claim that electric media invert the body's relation to its own extensions, so that the body and its prior technologies are *translated into information systems*. In "Media as Translators" he writes: "By putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems, by means of electric media, we set up a dynamic by which all previous technologies that are mere extensions of hands and feet and teeth and bodily heat-controls—all such extensions of our bodies, including cities—will be translated into information systems." The figure of the body now "wear[ing] its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide" reverses the ordinary topology of inside and outside, and the consequence, stated flatly in "Hybrid Energy: Les Liaisons Dangereuses," is that "our private and corporate lives have become information processes just because we have put our central nervous systems outside us in electric technology." This is the same hierarchy Hayles names — physicality demoted, information installed as origin — arrived at three decades earlier and from the opposite direction: not by abstracting information *out of* the body but by relocating the body *inside* its informational extensions, until the lived life is itself "an information process." `Directly stated`

What keeps McLuhan from being merely a celebrant of this transformation — and connects him to the surveillance-and-control register Han would later develop — is his sense of its ambivalence and its susceptibility to instrumental capture. He registers, in "Number: Profile of the Crowd," "the frankly expressed object of manipulation of population for varieties of power purposes" at the far end of statistical aggregation, anticipating the [[Digital Panopticon]]'s reading of the population as data; and he notes in "Telegraph: The Social Hormone" that the externalized nervous system runs both ways — "it is also common now for biologists like Hans Selye to think of the physical organism as a communication network," so that the body is recoded as information at the very moment the social field is recoded as a nervous system. McLuhan calls the externalization of the nerves the inauguration of "the Age of Anxiety and of Pervasive Dread": "To put one's nerves outside, and one's physical organs inside the nervous system, or the brain, is to initiate a situation-if not a concept-of dread." He neither contests the body-as-data as Hayles does nor preaches it as Kurzweil does; he announces it as the irreversible condition of the electric epoch, the planetary "single consciousness" toward which "our current translation of our entire lives into the spiritual form of information" tends. This makes McLuhan the cluster's deepest precursor: the author who first described body and mind externalized into a global informational field, and who supplies the [[Information]]-systems vocabulary that Haraway, Hayles, Han, Harari, and Kurzweil each inherit and inflect. `Directly stated` (the verbatim formulations are exact; the positioning of McLuhan as the cluster's genealogical precursor and the cross-author bridging is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## Jack Burnham (Beyond Modern Sculpture)

Two decades before Haraway named the organism's translation into a "biotic component," **[[Jack Burnham]]** had already, in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968), reached the body-as-data thesis from the side of art theory — by treating the cybernetic organism as the terminal stage of sculpture itself. For his "Cyborg Art" — "the cybernetic organism as an art form" — the organism is grasped as an information-processing system: where the older "Object Art physical presence is everything," in the new "Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." The body, in this frame, ceases to be palpable mass and becomes a node in an "interacting assembly," defined (like any system) by "input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death" rather than by matter. This is the same inversion the rest of the cluster names — physicality demoted, information installed as the operative reality — arrived at through the migration of sculpture from the object to the system. `Directly stated` (the cross-reading of Burnham's art-theoretical "information" against the body-as-data cluster is `Interpretive synthesis`).

Burnham's most direct contribution to this note is his reconception of the body itself as something to be *re-created in information*. In the Introduction he frames modern sculpture as "a preparatory stage representing steps toward the simulation of biological life," whose telos is the machine as "the only means by which man would eventually reconstruct intelligent life, or what might be called life-bearing artifacts." The living body is thereby reconceived not as irreducible flesh but as an effect of organization that technology can re-instantiate — a "life-bearing artifact." His definition closes the circuit: "A cyborg, then, is any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism." Where Hayles contests the reduction of the body to portable pattern and Harari and Kurzweil embrace it, Burnham occupies a distinct, earlier position — the body-as-data not as upload, dossier, or dogma, but as the engineering horizon of an art that aspires to make matter live. `Directly stated` (the positioning of Burnham as a 1968 art-theoretical precursor to the body-as-data cluster is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

Where the other authors debate whether the body *is* or *becomes* data, **[[Roy Ascott]]** describes a body already redistributed into the telematic field — rendered as data not by upload or dossier but by the fusion of the human sensorium with the network. In *Telematic Embrace* (the essays "The Architecture of Cyberception," 1994, and "From Appearance to Apparition," 1993) he names a new "post-biological faculty," **cyberception**, and the planetary substrate it runs on, the **cybernet** — "the sum of all the interactive computer-mediated systems and telematic networks in the world," which "is part of our sensory apparatus. It redefines our individual body, just as it connects all our bodies into a planetary whole." On the holomatic principle, "to be in or at any one interface is to be in the virtual presence of all the other interfaces throughout the network," so that the bounded individual gives way to distributed [[Telepresence|telepresence]]: "human presence becomes virtualised and the individual self is distributed." This is Ascott's variant of the body-as-data — the self dispersed across "the connection matrix," "here and not here," "both here and elsewhere at the same time" — and where Hayles contests disembodiment and Kurzweil preaches it, Ascott reports it as the felt condition of network life: "We are all interface. We are computer-mediated and computer-enhanced." `Directly stated`

What makes Ascott's contribution distinctive in this cluster is that his body-as-data is not the demotion of the material but its *convergence* with the immaterial. Cyberception, he argues, lets us "cyberceive transformative relationships and connectivity as immaterial process just as palpably and immediately as we commonly perceive material objects in material locations" — the immaterial made perceptible, not the body abstracted away. This is the apparition pole of his [[Material vs Immaterial]] thesis ("the inward and emergent processes of becoming" displacing "the outward and visible look of things"), and it forecasts a [[Material vs Immaterial|marriage of material and immaterial]]: as "the self is a complex system that interconnects with all other systems in a way that renders the line between natural and artificial systems meaningless. We are all becoming, to a greater or lesser extent, bionic," the virtualizing trajectory turns back toward "the reinstatement... of a natural materiality." Ascott names the body-as-interface directly — "the post-biological body as interface," with the systems interface moving "closer and closer to the body and into the brain" — but unlike the disembodiment cluster he treats this less as the flesh's erasure than as its re-enchanted extension into "another level of evolutionary development," art coming "to constitute a form of mediation between human and post-human consciousness." `Directly stated` (the verbatim formulations are exact; the positioning of Ascott as the cluster's moist-convergence / telepresence variant is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## Shoshana Zuboff (The Age of Surveillance Capitalism)

Where every prior author treats the body-as-data in a register of metaphysics, art, or media-cosmology, **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019) re-describes it as a concrete economic operation she names **rendition** — "the concrete operational practices through which dispossession is accomplished, as human experience is claimed as raw material for datafication." In "The Reality Business" (Ch. 7) she states the body's reduction with engineering flatness: in the flow of extracted data "no thing counts until it is rendered as behavior, translated into electronic data flows, and channeled into the light as observable data," and "the body is simply a set of coordinates in time and space where sensation and action are translated as data." This is the same hierarchy Hayles names — physicality demoted, information installed as the operative reality — but arrived at neither as fantasy (Hayles) nor as dogma (Harari) nor as goal (Kurzweil): the flesh is rendered into [[Behavioral Surplus]] because the [[The Prediction Imperative]] requires it, the body datafied so that its future behavior can be predicted and sold. Where Han names the *power* that reads the data-likeness, Zuboff names the *supply operation* that manufactures it: the body-as-data as the first link in a chain "fed into advanced manufacturing processes" for "fabrication into predictions and eventual exchange in the new behavioral futures markets." `Directly stated`

"Rendition: From Experience to Data" (Ch. 8) makes the body itself the explicit object. "Your body," Zuboff writes, "is reimagined as a behaving object to be tracked and calculated for indexing and search," and she catalogues the frontier: "There are many new territories of body rendition: organs, blood, eyes, brain waves, faces, gait, posture." This is O'Gieblyn's "data double" and Han's "digital habitus" given an operational anatomy — not the soul abstracted into a likeness but the living body parceled into extractable surfaces, "all that is moist and alive" made to "hand over its facts." "Rendition from the Depths" (Ch. 9) then drives the operation inward, past the body's surfaces to "the dark data continent of your inner life—your intentions and motives, meanings and needs, preferences and desires, moods and emotions, personality and disposition... summoned into the light for others' profit." The progression Zuboff names — "from web crawling to reality crawling to life crawling to self crawling" — is the body-as-data thesis recast as a logic of conquest: not whether the body *is* information (Harari) but how completely it can be *made* to yield information, with the unconscious itself "recast as simply one more source of raw-material supply for machine rendition and analysis." `Directly stated`

What distinguishes Zuboff's variant from Han's — the cluster's other surveillance-capital voice — is the [[Radical Indifference|indifference]] she locates at the heart of the operation, and the figure she draws from it. The apparatus she names [[Big Other]] "does not care what we think, feel, or do as long as its... sensate, actuating, computational eyes and ears can observe, render, datafy, and instrumentalize the vast reservoirs of behavioral surplus." This is the body-as-data stripped of even Han's interest in the soul: [[Instrumentarian Power|instrumentarian power]] "reduces human experience to measurable observable behavior while remaining steadfastly indifferent to the meaning of that experience." The consequence is Zuboff's sharpest inversion of the cluster's value-hierarchy. Against O'Gieblyn's data double that becomes "most valuable and real," Zuboff insists the rendered body is precisely what is *discarded*: "Forget the cliché that if it's free, 'You are the product.' You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass. The 'product' derives from the surplus that is ripped from your life." Big Other, she writes, "poaches our behavior for surplus and leaves behind all the meaning lodged in our bodies." Where Hayles fights to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," Zuboff documents the economic machinery that erases it — and turns the cluster's recurring question (what is lost when flesh becomes pattern?) into a political demand, that the "right to sanctuary" and the inwardness "born in sanctuary, that finally distinguishes us from the machines" be defended against the rendition of the body and the self alike. `Directly stated` (the verbatim formulations are exact; the positioning of Zuboff as the cluster's rendition / radical-indifference variant is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — the feminist-critical variant. Under "the informatics of domination," "organisms have ceased to exist as objects of knowledge, giving way to biotic components, i.e., special kinds of information-processing devices"; sociobiology "depend[s] on a high-tech view of the body as a biotic component or cybernetic communications system"; biotechnology and microelectronics "have recently textualized our bodies as code problems on the grid of C3I." Diagnoses this critically, foregrounds the gendered stakes — whose bodies are made permeable to "visualization" and "intervention" — and answers with "feminist cyborg stories" that "recod[e] communication and intelligence to subvert command and control." `Directly stated`
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — treats the body-as-data as a historically specific fantasy ("dream or nightmare") generated by the prior separation of information and materiality; contests it by recovering embodiment and the materiality of the medium. `Directly stated`
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** — proto-formulation: humans are "whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water," "not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves," yet Wiener ambivalently tries to keep this pattern-self contained within the autonomous liberal subject.
- **Hans Moravec** (as cited by Hayles) — the strong version: consciousness as downloadable information, the body emptied and discarded while the pattern persists in a new medium.
- **William Gibson** (as cited by Hayles) — the literary icon: the body as "meat," subjectivity as pattern, "data made flesh."
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — the present-tense, lived variant: not the future upload but the "second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes" that big data treats as "most valuable and real"; closes on the felt reduction in which "the soul is little more than a data set." `Directly stated`
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — the surveillance-capital-infrastructure variant: the stored trace of every click and search word condenses into a "digital habitus" that is "an extremely precise likeness of our persons - of our very souls," potentially "fuller and more accurate than the images we otherwise make of ourselves." Read by the aperspectival [[Digital Panopticon]], steered by micro-targeting "faster than free will," and traded as commodity — yet it counts without recounting, so it can control and sell the self without knowing it. `Directly stated`
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — the biological-literalist variant: not the body recoded as data but the body declared to *be* data-processing. "The baboon's entire body is the calculator"; "what we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms." Asserted as "current scientific dogma," with a carbon/silicon substrate-indifference that is the disembodiment Hayles contests; its practical face is the [[Quantified Self]] — "self-knowledge through numbers," reading the self off DNA and wearables rather than introspection. The [[Organisms Are Algorithms|biological face]] of the thesis, claimed as fact. `Directly stated`
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — the celebratory-goal variant: the body as "version 1.0" hardware, "frail and subject to a myriad of failure modes," whose neuronal complexity is mostly "life-support functions, not its information-processing capabilities," so the mind can be "ported" to "a more suitable computational substrate." Avowed "patternist" for whom "patterns of information" are "the fundamental reality" and humans are, "at least for bits of information," what "we comprise." The [[Version 1.0 Biological Bodies|version-1.0]] figure is its emblem and the premise of [[Transcending Biology]]. `Directly stated`
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — the media-cosmological precursor: not a particular self's data but the central nervous system itself "extended... in a global embrace," whose "final phase" is "the technological simulation of consciousness." By "putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems, by means of electric media," all bodily extensions "will be translated into information systems," so that "our private and corporate lives have become information processes" — the planetary [[Information]]-systems field that is the precondition of every later variant. `Directly stated`
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — the art-theoretical horizon: reached through sculpture's migration from object to system, "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." The living body is reconceived not as irreducible flesh but as a "life-bearing artifact" to be re-created in information — the body-as-data as the engineering horizon of an art that aspires to make matter live. `Directly stated`
- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the telematic-convergence variant: the body already redistributed into "the cybernet," which "redefines our individual body, just as it connects all our bodies into a planetary whole," so that "human presence becomes virtualised and the individual self is distributed." Names the "post-biological body as interface," but treats the result less as the flesh's erasure than as its convergence with the immaterial — the immaterial made "as palpably and immediately" perceptible as material objects, forecasting a "marriage of the immaterial and the material." `Directly stated`
- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — the surveillance-capital / rendition variant: the body-as-data not as metaphysics but as economic operation. The body is *rendered* — "a set of coordinates in time and space where sensation and action are translated as data," "reimagined as a behaving object to be tracked and calculated for indexing and search" across "organs, blood, eyes, brain waves, faces, gait, posture." Flesh becomes [[Behavioral Surplus]] for prediction; [[Big Other]] "poaches our behavior for surplus and leaves behind all the meaning lodged in our bodies," so "you are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass." The [[Instrumentarian Power|species of power]] and its [[Radical Indifference|indifference]] to the meaning of what it extracts — the cluster's sharpest political-economic stakes. `Directly stated`

## Related Concepts

- [[Information (Hayles)]] — the technical concept of information-as-pattern that, once detached from materiality, makes the body recodable as data; the body-as-data is its application to the human form
- [[Posthumanism]] — the body-as-data realizes the posthuman privileging of informational pattern over material instantiation
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the related move by which the lived body is projected into and replaced by its informational or prosthetic extension
- [[Cybernetics]] — the discourse, especially in Wiener, in which the body is first reconstrued as a flow of information and a pattern of organization
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the body-as-data presupposes and reinforces the hierarchy that subordinates the material to the immaterial

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — Hayles's direct rebuttal: embodiment is instantiated, local, specific, and cannot be replicated; it is precisely the flesh the body-as-data fantasy erases. The two concepts name the two poles of [[Embodiment vs Information]].

## Questions It Raises

- If the body is "data made flesh," what exactly is lost in treating flesh as a derivative manifestation of pattern — and why does Hayles insist that loss is non-recoverable?
- Does the body-as-data merely extend the liberal humanist erasure of embodiment, or does it constitute a genuinely new posthuman construction of the subject?
- How does the prior, "imaginary" separation of information from materiality make the body-as-data thinkable in the first place — and can the separation be contested without rejecting information theory itself?
- Why is the fantasy of disembodiment experienced as simultaneously a "dream" (immortality, freedom from material constraint) and a "nightmare" (the body reduced to a fashion accessory rather than the ground of being)?
- What does it cost, ethically and politically, to imagine that "we can do away with the body" because "we are essentially information"?
- If the body is *rendered* rather than merely *recoded* — converted into behavioral surplus for profit while "the meaning lodged in our bodies" is discarded as carcass — does the body-as-data thesis become less a metaphysical claim than an economic one, and what would defending the flesh against rendition require?
