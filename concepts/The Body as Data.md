# The Body as Data

## Brief Definition

The fantasy — at once dream and nightmare — that the human body is essentially an informational pattern, such that flesh becomes a derivative, inessential, or even disposable instantiation of a code that could in principle survive the body's destruction.

## General Orientation

Three authors treat the body-as-data, in three registers. For **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** it is a *future-tense fantasy* she contests: the speculative "dream or nightmare of the body as information" generated by the prior, contingent separation of [[Information]] from materiality, and the construction her recovery of [[Embodiment]] and [[The Materiality of Informatics]] is designed to reverse. For **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** the same fantasy has already arrived in *present-tense, lived* form: not the [[Hans Moravec]]/[[Ray Kurzweil]] dream of uploading consciousness someday, but the mundane "second self" that big data has *already* extracted from each of us — the immaterial data double of clicks, purchases, and likes. Where Hayles asks what the body-as-data fantasy *would* cost, O'Gieblyn reports what its informational form *does* cost now, closing on the testimony that "the soul is little more than a data set." The two senses are continuous: O'Gieblyn explicitly builds on Hayles's account of how information became "decontextualized" at the Macy Conferences, and her data double resonates with the [[Transparency]] cluster's account of the quantified self. For **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** the body-as-data is neither future fantasy nor neutral dossier but the *surveillance-capital infrastructure* of [[Psychopolitics]]: under Big Data, the stored trace of "the clicks we make and the search words we type" condenses into a "digital habitus" that supplies "an extremely precise likeness of our persons — of our very souls," a likeness Han judges potentially "fuller and more accurate than the images we otherwise make of ourselves." Where O'Gieblyn names the data double's value-inversion, Han names its *power* — the [[Digital Panopticon|aperspectival]] reading of the soul itself, monetized as [[Exhibition Value|commodity]] and steered by micro-targeting. This makes Han's section the curatorial bridge between the body-as-data thesis and the surveillance-and-control cluster of the vault. For **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** the body-as-data is no longer fantasy, dossier, or even infrastructure but *biology's own reigning dogma* — the body **literally is** data-processing. Where Hayles contests the equation, Harari endorses it as "current scientific dogma": organisms are biochemical algorithms, "the baboon's entire body is the calculator," and "what we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms." This is the [[Organisms Are Algorithms|biological face]] of the body-as-data thesis, now claimed not as metaphor but as fact, and it turns biometric self-tracking — the Quantified Self's "self-knowledge through numbers" — into the natural method of reading the self off DNA and wearables rather than introspection. Harari thus literalizes precisely the construction Hayles set out to denaturalize: he is the author for whom the body-as-data has ceased to be a "dream or nightmare" and become a textbook claim.

## Longer Explanation

In *How We Became Posthuman*, Hayles names "the body as information" as a "dream or nightmare" that runs through cybernetics, molecular biology, and cyberpunk fiction alike. It is the conception she contests, not endorses. Its premise is the prior separation of information from materiality: once "information lost its body" and came to be theorized as a pattern independent of any particular medium, the human body could be recoded as one more substrate that the pattern merely happens to occupy. From there it is a short step to the posthuman assumption that "embodiment in a biological substrate is seen as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life." This is the move Hayles works to make visible and reverse.

The concept has a long genealogy in her account. Norbert Wiener supplied an early and influential formulation: identity, he argued, cannot consist in physical continuity, because the matter composing a body is replaced over a lifetime. "We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves." Hans Moravec radicalized the same logic into the "roboticist's dream" of downloading human consciousness into a computer, leaving the cranial cavity empty while the patient wakes unchanged in a metallic body — the scenario Hayles "struck me as a nightmare" with which the book opens. Marvin Minsky's prediction that human memories could be extracted from the brain and imported "intact and unchanged, to computer disks" belongs to the same family of dreams. In each case the body is treated as superfluous flesh once the Form of "ones and zeros" can be captured in a nonbiological medium.

William Gibson gives the fantasy its most condensed literary figure. In *Neuromancer*, cyberspace is a regime in which "pattern is the essential reality, presence an optical illusion"; the cowboy Case regards his body as so much "meat" that exists only to sustain his consciousness between runs, and Dixie Flatline has ceased to be a physical body entirely, living on as a personality construct defined by magnetic patterns. The narrator's phrase for this is exact: physicality is "data made flesh." Hayles reads the elliptical phrase carefully — "Information is the putative origin, physicality the derivative manifestation" — to show how the hierarchy is inverted, with the informational code installed as origin and the body demoted to a manifestation that can be left behind: "If flesh is data incarnate, why not go back to the source and leave the perils of physicality behind?"

Crucially, Hayles frames the body-as-data not as a neutral technical description but as an ideologically loaded construction with costs. It continues, rather than disrupts, the liberal humanist erasure of embodiment — the tradition in which the subject "possessed a body but was not usually represented as being a body." Her entire project is to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," to insist that "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium," and that embodiment "can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated." The body-as-data is thus the target of her critique: the seductive abstraction whose erasures she sets out to chronicle.

## The Second Self and the Data Double (O'Gieblyn)

Where Hayles diagnoses the body-as-data as a fantasy still to come, O'Gieblyn argues that we already inhabit it. In *God, Human, Animal, Machine* she follows Hayles's own genealogy — citing *How We Became Posthuman* on how, during the Macy Conferences, information "became decontextualized and began to seem like 'an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates,'" until it was rendered "a mathematical quantity weightless as sunshine" and so became "the materialist's substitute for the soul." But she then collapses the speculative distance between that abstraction and the present. "In a way," she writes, "we are already living the dualistic existence that [[Ray Kurzweil]] promised. In addition to our physical bodies, there exists—somewhere in the ether—a second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes that lingers not in some transcendent nirvana but rather in the shadowy dossiers of third-party aggregators." This is the body-as-data not as upload but as dossier — the lived realization of the very [[Mind as Machine]] equation O'Gieblyn traces back through transhumanism.

The decisive inversion is one of value, and it echoes Hayles's reading of physicality as "the derivative manifestation." These second selves, O'Gieblyn notes, "are entirely without agency or consciousness; they have no preferences, no desires, no hopes or spiritual impulses, and yet in the purely informational sphere of big data, it is they, not we, that are most valuable and real." The flesh is demoted; the data double is promoted to the status of "most valuable and real." Citing Jaron Lanier, she names the ideological work this performs — "a digital climate in which 'bits are presented as if they were alive, while humans are transient fragments'" — which makes O'Gieblyn's data double the present-tense, market-driven form of exactly the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] hierarchy Hayles contests, and aligns it with the [[Transparency]] cluster's account of the quantified, exhibited self.

The book closes its ninth chapter on the lived testimony of this condition. Immersed in the internet and feeling herself dissolve into a [[The Computational Universe|collective]] mind, O'Gieblyn describes "my own blurred boundaries, seized by the suspicion that I am not forming new opinions so much as assimilating them, that all my preferences can be predicted and neatly reduced to type, that the soul is little more than a data set." Where Hayles works to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," O'Gieblyn registers the erasure from the inside, as a felt loss — the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist self]] experiencing its own reduction to [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]]. "I don't know exactly what to call this state of affairs," she ends, "but it does not feel like the Kingdom of God."

## Psychopolitics (Han) — the digital habitus as a likeness of the soul

In the "Big Data" chapter of *Psychopolitics*, Han gives the body-as-data thesis its surveillance-capital inflection. Where Hayles diagnoses a fantasy and O'Gieblyn an already-living data double, Han describes the *infrastructure* that produces the data-likeness and the power that reads it. "Today," he writes, "the clicks we make and the search words we type are stored. Every step is watched and recorded. A complete picture of our lives exists on the internet. Our digital habitus provides an extremely precise likeness of our persons - of our very souls. Perhaps it is even fuller and more accurate than the images we otherwise make of ourselves." The reduction here is not merely of the body to a pattern but of the *soul* to a habitus of stored traces — a likeness that, Han suggests, may out-resolve self-knowledge itself. This is the [[Mind as Machine|equation of self and data set]] that O'Gieblyn registers as felt loss, restated as an accomplished technical fact: the [[Digital Panopticon]] "can peer into the human soul itself" because, unlike Bentham's perspectival optics, it is aperspectival and "eliminates all blind spots." `Directly stated`.

What makes Han's body-as-data load-bearing for the surveillance-and-control cluster is its political and economic edge. The data-likeness is not a contemplative self-portrait but an object of power and of trade. Big Data, Han argues, "is making the id into an ego to be exploited psychopolitically": through the "digital unconscious" it can "read desires we do not know we harbour," and through micro-targeting — "the practical microphysics of power" — it intervenes "in psychic processes in a prospective fashion," quite possibly "even faster than free will," so that "it could overtake it. If so, this would herald the end of freedom." Simultaneously the likeness is monetized: "personal data are unceasingly monetized and commercialized... people are treated and traded as packages of data for economic use. That is, human beings have become a commodity." The digital habitus is thus the hinge where the [[The Body as Data|body-as-data]] thesis crosses into [[Freedom vs Control]], [[Auto-Exploitation]], and the [[Exhibition Value|attention-commodity]] economy. `Directly stated`.

Han's account also sharpens *why* the data-likeness, however precise, is not self-knowledge — a point that converges with Hayles's insistence on the irreducibility of [[Embodiment]] and O'Gieblyn's worry about reduction "to type." For Han the digital habitus counts but cannot recount: "no insight into the self can result from data and numbers alone... Numbers do not recount anything about the self. Counting is not recounting." The "Quantified Self," outfitting "the body... with sensors that automatically register data," yields a mounting pile that "does nothing to answer the simple question. Who am I?" The likeness is exhaustive yet empty — a data-likeness of the person that, lacking [[Narrativity|narration]], "empties the self of any and all meaning." Han's body-as-data is therefore at once the most precise and the most soulless: a [[Gleichschaltung|statistical likeness]] adequate to control and to commerce but blind to the singular self it claims to mirror. `Strongly implied` (Han ties the precision of the likeness and its emptiness to one and the same Big Data regime, but the explicit reconciliation of "extremely precise likeness" with "empties the self of meaning" is the synthesis of two passages in the chapter).

## Organisms as Biochemical Algorithms (Harari)

If Hayles diagnoses the body-as-data as a contested fantasy, O'Gieblyn as an already-living dossier, and Han as a surveillance-capital likeness, **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** marks the point where the equation stops being figurative and is asserted as biological fact. In *Homo Deus* the body is not *recoded as* data; it *is* a data-processing system — the body-as-data thesis given its [[Organisms Are Algorithms|biological face]] and presented as "current scientific dogma." Harari builds the claim up in "The Anthropocene" (Ch. 2 in the file numbering), where emotions are reframed not as "some mysterious spiritual phenomenon" but as "biochemical algorithms that are vital for the survival and reproduction of all mammals." The vending-machine analogy is then extended to the human: "the man pressing the buttons and drinking the tea is also an algorithm. A much more complicated algorithm than the vending machine, no doubt, but still an algorithm." The decisive literalization comes when he asks how a baboon weighs a lion against a bunch of bananas and answers that there is no separate computer doing the sums: "the baboon's entire body is the calculator. What we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms." The flesh is not the bearer of an informational pattern that could be peeled off it (Hayles's "data made flesh"); the flesh is *itself* the running computation. This is the body-as-data thesis at its most uncompromising — not the body abstracted into data, but the body declared to have been calculation all along. `Directly stated`

The thesis is load-bearing because of its substrate-independence corollary, which is exactly the move Hayles names as the body-as-data's founding error. In "The Great Decoupling" Harari compresses it to three principles: "Organisms are algorithms. Every animal – including Homo sapiens – is an assemblage of organic algorithms shaped by natural selection over millions of years of evolution"; "Algorithmic calculations are not affected by the materials from which you build the calculator"; and "As long as the calculations remain valid, what does it matter whether the algorithms are manifested in carbon or silicon?" The carbon/silicon indifference is precisely the disembodiment Hayles contests — "embodiment in a biological substrate is seen as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life" — except that Harari advances it not as a "dream or nightmare" but as the reigning view of "the life sciences." Where Hayles works to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," Harari treats the erasability of the flesh as a settled empirical result: if the calculation is what matters, the carbon it runs on is incidental. The two authors thus stand at opposite poles of the same [[Embodiment vs Information]] tension — Harari reporting as fact the proposition Hayles spent a book denaturalizing. `Directly stated`

The practical face of this literalized body-as-data is biometric self-tracking, which Harari treats as the everyday method that follows once the self is read off numbers rather than introspection. In "The Great Decoupling" he describes wearables and DNA tests that "record diverse biometric data" and feed it to algorithms that "tell you who you are and what you should do," and he names the ideology directly: "The Quantified Self movement argues that the self is nothing but mathematical patterns... So if you wish to obey the old adage and know thyself, you should not waste your time on philosophy, meditation or psychoanalysis, but rather you should systematically collect biometric data... The movement's motto is 'Self-knowledge through numbers.'" The self, on this account, is to be discovered the way Angelina Jolie's risk was discovered — by listening to genes "whose voice manifested not in feelings but in numbers" — so that one consults an "inner genetic parliament" instead of an inner voice. This is the inversion O'Gieblyn registers as felt loss and Han diagnoses as a likeness that "counts but cannot recount," restated by Harari as a *program of self-knowledge*: where Han insists "counting is not recounting," Harari reports a movement that holds counting to *be* recounting, the only reliable route to "know thyself." The biological body-as-data thus completes the cluster — Harari supplies the empirical-sounding warrant ("organisms are algorithms") that the surveillance and market apparatus described by Han and O'Gieblyn presupposes, and turns the [[Quantified Self]] from symptom into method. `Directly stated` (the Quantified Self passages and the "Self-knowledge through numbers" motto are verbatim; the synthesis tying them to the body-as-data cluster is `Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — treats the body-as-data as a historically specific fantasy ("dream or nightmare") generated by the prior separation of information and materiality; she contests it by recovering embodiment and the materiality of the medium.
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** — proto-formulation: humans are "whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water," patterns rather than abiding stuff; yet Wiener ambivalently tries to keep this pattern-self contained within the autonomous liberal subject.
- **Hans Moravec** (as cited by Hayles) — the strong version: consciousness as downloadable information, the body emptied and discarded while the pattern persists in a new medium.
- **William Gibson** (as cited by Hayles) — the literary icon: the body as "meat," subjectivity as pattern, "data made flesh."
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — the present-tense, lived variant: not the future upload but the "second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes" that big data treats as "most valuable and real"; closes on the felt reduction in which "the soul is little more than a data set."
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — the surveillance-capital infrastructure variant: the stored trace of every click and search word condenses into a "digital habitus" that is "an extremely precise likeness of our persons - of our very souls," potentially "fuller and more accurate than the images we otherwise make of ourselves." The data-likeness is read by the aperspectival [[Digital Panopticon]], steered by micro-targeting "faster than free will," and traded as commodity — yet it counts without recounting, so it can control and sell the self without knowing it.
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — the biological-literalist variant: not the body recoded as data but the body declared to *be* data-processing. "Organisms are algorithms"; "the baboon's entire body is the calculator"; "what we call sensations and emotions are in fact algorithms." Asserted as "current scientific dogma," not as Hayles's "dream or nightmare," and with an explicit carbon/silicon substrate-indifference that is the disembodiment Hayles contests. Its practical face is the [[Quantified Self]] — "self-knowledge through numbers," reading the self off DNA and wearables rather than introspection. This is the [[Organisms Are Algorithms|biological face]] of the body-as-data fantasy now claimed as fact.

## Related Concepts

- [[Information (Hayles)]] — the technical concept of information-as-pattern that, once detached from materiality, makes the body recodable as data; the body-as-data is its application to the human form
- [[Posthumanism]] — the body-as-data realizes the posthuman privileging of informational pattern over material instantiation
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the related move by which the lived body is projected into and replaced by its informational or prosthetic extension
- [[Cybernetics]] — the discourse, especially in Wiener, in which the body is first reconstrued as a flow of information and a pattern of organization
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the body-as-data presupposes and reinforces the hierarchy that subordinates the material to the immaterial

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — Hayles's direct rebuttal: embodiment is instantiated, local, specific, and cannot be replicated; it is precisely the flesh the body-as-data fantasy erases. The two concepts name the two poles of [[Embodiment vs Information]].

## Questions It Raises

- If the body is "data made flesh," what exactly is lost in treating flesh as a derivative manifestation of pattern — and why does Hayles insist that loss is non-recoverable?
- Does the body-as-data merely extend the liberal humanist erasure of embodiment, or does it constitute a genuinely new posthuman construction of the subject?
- How does the prior, "imaginary" separation of information from materiality make the body-as-data thinkable in the first place — and can the separation be contested without rejecting information theory itself?
- Why is the fantasy of disembodiment experienced as simultaneously a "dream" (immortality, freedom from material constraint) and a "nightmare" (the body reduced to a fashion accessory rather than the ground of being)?
- What does it cost, ethically and politically, to imagine that "we can do away with the body" because "we are essentially information"?
