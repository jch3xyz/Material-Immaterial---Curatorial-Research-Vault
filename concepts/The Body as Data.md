# The Body as Data

## Brief Definition

The fantasy — at once dream and nightmare — that the human body is essentially an informational pattern, such that flesh becomes a derivative, inessential, or even disposable instantiation of a code that could in principle survive the body's destruction.

## General Orientation

Two authors treat the body-as-data, in two registers. For **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** it is a *future-tense fantasy* she contests: the speculative "dream or nightmare of the body as information" generated by the prior, contingent separation of [[Information]] from materiality, and the construction her recovery of [[Embodiment]] and [[The Materiality of Informatics]] is designed to reverse. For **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** the same fantasy has already arrived in *present-tense, lived* form: not the [[Hans Moravec]]/[[Ray Kurzweil]] dream of uploading consciousness someday, but the mundane "second self" that big data has *already* extracted from each of us — the immaterial data double of clicks, purchases, and likes. Where Hayles asks what the body-as-data fantasy *would* cost, O'Gieblyn reports what its informational form *does* cost now, closing on the testimony that "the soul is little more than a data set." The two senses are continuous: O'Gieblyn explicitly builds on Hayles's account of how information became "decontextualized" at the Macy Conferences, and her data double resonates with the [[Transparency]] cluster's account of the quantified self.

## Longer Explanation

In *How We Became Posthuman*, Hayles names "the body as information" as a "dream or nightmare" that runs through cybernetics, molecular biology, and cyberpunk fiction alike. It is the conception she contests, not endorses. Its premise is the prior separation of information from materiality: once "information lost its body" and came to be theorized as a pattern independent of any particular medium, the human body could be recoded as one more substrate that the pattern merely happens to occupy. From there it is a short step to the posthuman assumption that "embodiment in a biological substrate is seen as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life." This is the move Hayles works to make visible and reverse.

The concept has a long genealogy in her account. Norbert Wiener supplied an early and influential formulation: identity, he argued, cannot consist in physical continuity, because the matter composing a body is replaced over a lifetime. "We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves." Hans Moravec radicalized the same logic into the "roboticist's dream" of downloading human consciousness into a computer, leaving the cranial cavity empty while the patient wakes unchanged in a metallic body — the scenario Hayles "struck me as a nightmare" with which the book opens. Marvin Minsky's prediction that human memories could be extracted from the brain and imported "intact and unchanged, to computer disks" belongs to the same family of dreams. In each case the body is treated as superfluous flesh once the Form of "ones and zeros" can be captured in a nonbiological medium.

William Gibson gives the fantasy its most condensed literary figure. In *Neuromancer*, cyberspace is a regime in which "pattern is the essential reality, presence an optical illusion"; the cowboy Case regards his body as so much "meat" that exists only to sustain his consciousness between runs, and Dixie Flatline has ceased to be a physical body entirely, living on as a personality construct defined by magnetic patterns. The narrator's phrase for this is exact: physicality is "data made flesh." Hayles reads the elliptical phrase carefully — "Information is the putative origin, physicality the derivative manifestation" — to show how the hierarchy is inverted, with the informational code installed as origin and the body demoted to a manifestation that can be left behind: "If flesh is data incarnate, why not go back to the source and leave the perils of physicality behind?"

Crucially, Hayles frames the body-as-data not as a neutral technical description but as an ideologically loaded construction with costs. It continues, rather than disrupts, the liberal humanist erasure of embodiment — the tradition in which the subject "possessed a body but was not usually represented as being a body." Her entire project is to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," to insist that "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium," and that embodiment "can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated." The body-as-data is thus the target of her critique: the seductive abstraction whose erasures she sets out to chronicle.

## The Second Self and the Data Double (O'Gieblyn)

Where Hayles diagnoses the body-as-data as a fantasy still to come, O'Gieblyn argues that we already inhabit it. In *God, Human, Animal, Machine* she follows Hayles's own genealogy — citing *How We Became Posthuman* on how, during the Macy Conferences, information "became decontextualized and began to seem like 'an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates,'" until it was rendered "a mathematical quantity weightless as sunshine" and so became "the materialist's substitute for the soul." But she then collapses the speculative distance between that abstraction and the present. "In a way," she writes, "we are already living the dualistic existence that [[Ray Kurzweil]] promised. In addition to our physical bodies, there exists—somewhere in the ether—a second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes that lingers not in some transcendent nirvana but rather in the shadowy dossiers of third-party aggregators." This is the body-as-data not as upload but as dossier — the lived realization of the very [[Mind as Machine]] equation O'Gieblyn traces back through transhumanism.

The decisive inversion is one of value, and it echoes Hayles's reading of physicality as "the derivative manifestation." These second selves, O'Gieblyn notes, "are entirely without agency or consciousness; they have no preferences, no desires, no hopes or spiritual impulses, and yet in the purely informational sphere of big data, it is they, not we, that are most valuable and real." The flesh is demoted; the data double is promoted to the status of "most valuable and real." Citing Jaron Lanier, she names the ideological work this performs — "a digital climate in which 'bits are presented as if they were alive, while humans are transient fragments'" — which makes O'Gieblyn's data double the present-tense, market-driven form of exactly the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] hierarchy Hayles contests, and aligns it with the [[Transparency]] cluster's account of the quantified, exhibited self.

The book closes its ninth chapter on the lived testimony of this condition. Immersed in the internet and feeling herself dissolve into a [[The Computational Universe|collective]] mind, O'Gieblyn describes "my own blurred boundaries, seized by the suspicion that I am not forming new opinions so much as assimilating them, that all my preferences can be predicted and neatly reduced to type, that the soul is little more than a data set." Where Hayles works to "put back into the picture the flesh that continues to be erased," O'Gieblyn registers the erasure from the inside, as a felt loss — the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanist self]] experiencing its own reduction to [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]]. "I don't know exactly what to call this state of affairs," she ends, "but it does not feel like the Kingdom of God."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — treats the body-as-data as a historically specific fantasy ("dream or nightmare") generated by the prior separation of information and materiality; she contests it by recovering embodiment and the materiality of the medium.
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** — proto-formulation: humans are "whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water," patterns rather than abiding stuff; yet Wiener ambivalently tries to keep this pattern-self contained within the autonomous liberal subject.
- **Hans Moravec** (as cited by Hayles) — the strong version: consciousness as downloadable information, the body emptied and discarded while the pattern persists in a new medium.
- **William Gibson** (as cited by Hayles) — the literary icon: the body as "meat," subjectivity as pattern, "data made flesh."
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — the present-tense, lived variant: not the future upload but the "second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes" that big data treats as "most valuable and real"; closes on the felt reduction in which "the soul is little more than a data set."

## Related Concepts

- [[Information (Hayles)]] — the technical concept of information-as-pattern that, once detached from materiality, makes the body recodable as data; the body-as-data is its application to the human form
- [[Posthumanism]] — the body-as-data realizes the posthuman privileging of informational pattern over material instantiation
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the related move by which the lived body is projected into and replaced by its informational or prosthetic extension
- [[Cybernetics]] — the discourse, especially in Wiener, in which the body is first reconstrued as a flow of information and a pattern of organization
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the body-as-data presupposes and reinforces the hierarchy that subordinates the material to the immaterial

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — Hayles's direct rebuttal: embodiment is instantiated, local, specific, and cannot be replicated; it is precisely the flesh the body-as-data fantasy erases. The two concepts name the two poles of [[Embodiment vs Information]].

## Questions It Raises

- If the body is "data made flesh," what exactly is lost in treating flesh as a derivative manifestation of pattern — and why does Hayles insist that loss is non-recoverable?
- Does the body-as-data merely extend the liberal humanist erasure of embodiment, or does it constitute a genuinely new posthuman construction of the subject?
- How does the prior, "imaginary" separation of information from materiality make the body-as-data thinkable in the first place — and can the separation be contested without rejecting information theory itself?
- Why is the fantasy of disembodiment experienced as simultaneously a "dream" (immortality, freedom from material constraint) and a "nightmare" (the body reduced to a fashion accessory rather than the ground of being)?
- What does it cost, ethically and politically, to imagine that "we can do away with the body" because "we are essentially information"?
