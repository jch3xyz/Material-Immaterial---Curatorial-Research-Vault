# Smart Power

## Brief Definition

Smart power is Byung-Chul Han's name for the dominant neoliberal technology of power: permissive, seductive and pleasing rather than coercive, it targets the psyche, says "yes" more than "no," presents itself as freedom, and works most efficiently by remaining invisible.

## Longer Explanation

For Han, smart power is the affective inverse of disciplinary or repressive power. Disciplinary power "is still commanded by negativity" — its mode is "inhibitive, not permissive" — and so it cannot describe the neoliberal regime, "which beams forth in positivity." Smart power sheds that negativity: instead of forbidding, threatening and decreeing, it "activates, motivates and optimizes," operating "by pleasing and fulfilling" rather than "by means of forbidding and depriving." Where coercive power operates frontally, against the will of its subjects, smart power "guides their will to its own benefit" and "meets the subject halfway." Its efficiency is precisely a function of its invisibility: "Friendly power proves more powerful, as it were, than purely repressive power. It manages not to be seen at all." Under it, "the subject thinks itself free" (*Directly stated*, ch. 2).

The crucial mechanism is that smart power "cosies up to the psyche rather than disciplining it through coercion or prohibitions." It does not impose silence but constantly solicits confession, sharing and participation — calling on subjects "to communicate our opinions, needs, wishes and preferences." This is why Han locates today's "crisis of freedom" not in the negation of freedom but in its exploitation: smart power "does not negate or repress freedom so much as exploit it," and free choice (*Wahl*) gives way to a mere free selection (*Auswahl*) from items on offer. Its "signal and seal is the Like button"; neoliberalism is "the capitalism of 'Like'" (*Directly stated*, ch. 2). This is the same engineering of "self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation" that Han, in his reading of Foucault, says "escaped" Foucault — power that "ensures that individuals act on themselves so that power relations are interiorized — and then interpreted as freedom" (*Directly stated*, ch. 5). The note that smart power "reads and appraises our conscious and unconscious thoughts" and "places its stock in voluntary self-organization and self-optimization" links it directly to [[Auto-Exploitation]] and [[Exploitation of Freedom]].

In "Shock," Han sharpens smart power against its disciplinary opposite. Electroshock and the "shock doctrine" are "genuinely disciplinary" — they belong to "the biopolitical measures of coercion," and their "essential trait is negativity," working "by paralyzing and annihilating the contents of the psyche." Neoliberal psychopolitics inverts this: "Instead of working with negative threats, it works with positive stimuli. Instead of administering 'bitter medicine', it enlists Liking. It flatters the psyche instead of shaking it and paralyzing it with shocks." Here Han names the same power "SmartPolitics" — a register of smart power, not a separate concept: "Neoliberal psychopolitics is SmartPolitics: it seeks to please and fulfil, not to repress" (*Directly stated*, ch. 7).

In "Friendly Big Brother," smart power acquires its most memorable image. The neoliberal technology of power "does not prohibit, protect or repress; instead, it prospects, permits and projects"; it generates "a superabundance of positivity" and replaces "confession obtained by force" with "voluntary disclosure." Surveillance no longer requires a coercive overseer: "Big Brother now wears a friendly face. His friendliness is what makes surveillance so efficient." This friendliness is what makes the [[Digital Panopticon]] run on its occupants' "voluntary self-exposure" — a society where "everyone feels free," yet "precisely this feeling of freedom . . . is now the problem" (*Directly stated*, ch. 8). Smart power is thus the affective engine of the [[Society of Control]] without being identical to it: the Deleuzian control society names the structural diagram, while smart power names the seductive, psyche-targeting *modality* through which it operates (*Interpretive synthesis*).

## Shoshana Zuboff (The Age of Surveillance Capitalism)

Zuboff's account of surveillance capitalism supplies the *economic logic* beneath Han's smart power: where Han diagnoses the seductive, psyche-targeting modality from the subject's side, Zuboff names the market imperatives and the apparatus that wield it. The reorientation she traces "from knowledge to power" culminates not in coercion but in shaping: "it is no longer enough to automate information flows about us; the goal now is to automate us" (*Directly stated*, ch. 1). Crucially, this power does not impose norms but pleases, nudges and herds. The means of behavioral modification aims "not to impose behavioral norms, such as conformity or obedience, but rather to produce behavior that reliably, definitively, and certainly leads to desired commercial results"; its interventions "nudge, tune, herd, manipulate, and modify behavior" through cues "as subtle as inserting a specific phrase into your Facebook news feed" (*Directly stated*, ch. 7). The marketing rhetoric that carries it — "personalization," the friendly "assistance" of the digital assistant — is Han's smart power read as an economic euphemism: the "personal digital assistant" is "a market avatar, another Trojan horse in which the determination to render and monetize your life is secreted under the veil of 'assistance' and embellished with the poetry of 'personalization'" (*Directly stated*, ch. 9). The slogan of "smart"/"connected" devices is, in Zuboff's flat reading, "a euphemism for rendition: intelligence that is designed to render some tiny corner of lived experience as behavioral data" (*Directly stated*, ch. 8) — a direct counterpoint to Han's smart/friendly power.

The chapter "Make Them Dance" gives smart power its operational triad — *tuning*, *herding*, *conditioning* — and its most exact formulation: "We are learning how to write the music, and then we let the music make them dance." Tuning works precisely by evading awareness ("subliminal cues designed to subtly shape the flow of behavior at the precise time and place for maximally efficient influence"), exploiting "the natural empathy present in its population of users," so that "human consciousness itself is a threat to surveillance revenues, as awareness endangers the larger project of behavior modification" (*Directly stated*, ch. 10). Where Han's smart power "meets the subject halfway" and is welcomed as freedom, Zuboff insists the power that pleases is in the service of others' ends: it "effectively exiles us from our own behavior, shifting the locus of control over the future tense from 'I will' to 'You will'" (*Directly stated*, ch. 10).

In the book's theoretical keystone, this pleasing, non-coercive power becomes *instrumentarian power* and its apparatus *Big Other* — "the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior" (*Directly stated*, ch. 13). Zuboff sets it against terror exactly as Han sets smart power against the shock and prohibition of disciplinary power: instrumentarian power "does not grow through terror, murder, the suspension of democratic institutions, massacre, or expulsion. Instead, it grows through declaration, self-authorization, rhetorical misdirection, euphemism, and the quiet, audacious backstage moves specifically crafted to elude awareness," aiming for "a condition of certainty without terror in the form of 'guaranteed outcomes'" (*Directly stated*, ch. 13). Its closing formula — "a future of certainty accomplished without violence. The price we pay is not with our bodies but with our freedom" (*Directly stated*, ch. 13) — is the surveillance-capitalist restatement of Han's "everyone feels free" / "this feeling of freedom . . . is now the problem." The two diverge productively on consent: Han stresses the subject's *voluntary* self-exposure, while Zuboff stresses the *unilateral*, secret, coercive character of the apparatus (rendition is "typically unauthorized, unilateral, gluttonous, secret, and brazen"), and the social theory of "the instrumentarian collective" extends this from the individual psyche to the tuned hive, where social pressure — "well-known to psychologists for its dangerous production of obedience and conformity" — "is elevated to the highest good" (*Directly stated*, chs. 8, 15) (*Interpretive synthesis* for the Han bridge).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — *Smart power* is Han's term in *Psychopolitics* for the dominant neoliberal technology of power. Chapter 2, "Smart Power," is its home text; it is also called **SmartPolitics** (ch. 7) and **permissive / friendly / smart-and-friendly power** (chs. 2, 8). These are registers of one concept, not distinct concepts. Han defines it strictly against disciplinary, repressive, coercive and "shock"-based power: smart power is permissive where they are inhibitive, positive where they are negative, seductive where they are repressive, and invisible where they are frontal.
- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — Zuboff does not use the phrase "smart power," but her **instrumentarian power** (materialized in **Big Other**, operationalized as **tuning, herding, and conditioning** and dressed in the rhetoric of **"personalization"** and **"smart"** devices) is the political-economic counterpart to Han's concept: a non-coercive, pleasing, awareness-evading power that "automate[s] us" toward "guaranteed outcomes" without terror. Where Han emphasizes the subject's voluntary self-exposure and the will guided as freedom, Zuboff emphasizes the unilateral economic imperatives and the ownership of the means of behavioral modification behind the friendly face — a productive divergence on consent vs. coercion.

## Related Concepts

- [[Positivity]] — smart power is the technology of power proper to the regime that "beams forth in positivity"; it works with "positive stimuli" and "a superabundance of positivity."
- [[Smoothness]] — a sibling figure of neoliberal positivity in Han; smart power shares its frictionless, non-confrontational, pleasing character. Linked, not merged: smoothness is the aesthetic/tactile register, smart power the political-technological one (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- [[Society of Control]] — the structural diagram of post-disciplinary power; smart power is the seductive, psyche-targeting modality through which control operates. Linked, not merged (*Interpretive synthesis*).
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — smart power's "friendly face" is what makes the panopticon's surveillance efficient and voluntary; Zuboff's Big Other is the apparatus-side, capital-driven counterpart.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — smart power induces subjects to "act on themselves," making self-optimization itself a mode of domination.
- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — smart power "does not negate or repress freedom so much as exploit it."
- [[Achievement Society]] — the neoliberal "achievement-subject" is the subject smart power produces and exhausts.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — smart power is defined by the shedding of negativity; disciplinary, repressive, coercive and shock power are its negative opposite, operating by "no," prohibition, threat and the paralysis of the psyche.

## Questions It Raises

- If smart power presents itself as freedom and "meets the subject halfway," what would resistance even look like, given that there is "no resistance" for it to overcome?
- Is "SmartPolitics" fully reducible to "smart power," or does the ch. 7 register add a specifically predictive/anticipatory dimension ("by means of calculated prognoses, it anticipates actions") absent from ch. 2?
- How sharp is Han's separation of smart power from the [[Society of Control]] and from Foucauldian biopolitics — is smart power a successor *to* control, a *modality of* it, or a polemical renaming?
- Does the curatorial/exhibitionary logic of the "Like button" make smart power a power that operates through display — and if so, how does it relate to surveillance that no longer needs to hide?
- Han stresses the *voluntary* (self-exposure, the will guided as freedom) while Zuboff stresses the *unilateral* and secret (rendition "unauthorized, unilateral, gluttonous, secret, and brazen"): is the pleasing, non-coercive power one phenomenon described from two sides, or do consent and coercion mark a real difference between the two accounts?
