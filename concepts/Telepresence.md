# Telepresence

## Brief Definition

In Roy Ascott's telematic art, telepresence is presence distributed throughout the Internet — being simultaneously here and there, out-of-body and reembodied, dematerialised and reconfigured at one and the same time.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, telepresence is the condition of presence under telematic networks: a presence no longer fixed to one body in one place but dispersed across the Net. His own lexical definition is exact: "In telematic art, our telepresence is distributed throughout the Internet. We are both here and there, out-of-body and reembodied, dematerialised, and reconfigured at one and the same time." Presence ceases to be a property of a located, bounded organism and becomes a networked event — multiplied across sites, virtualised, available at many nodes at once. `Directly stated`

Ascott historicises the term rather than treating it as a sudden technological novelty. Art, he argues, has always been a discipline of presence: "Art has always concerned itself with presence: of gods in religious art, heroes in classical art, the artist in romantic art; of presence itself in abstract art." Telematic art inherits this long concern and transforms its object — where earlier art made present a god, a hero, an artist, or presence-as-such, telematic art distributes the presence of the networked subject across the Internet. Telepresence is thus, for Ascott, the latest and most radical chapter of art's perennial work with presence, not a break from it. `Directly stated`

Telepresence is the mechanism of [[The Distributed Self]]: it is the means by which "the individual human self, a unitary and undivided personality" becomes "the multiple, distributed presences of a set of many selves." Where the distributed self names the *subject* that telematics produces, telepresence names the *operation* — the spreading of presence across the Net that makes such a subject possible. Ascott welcomes this affirmatively, classing telepresence among the technologically realised forms of an old "human desire for transcendence": "The human desire for transcendence takes many forms: telepathy, telekinesis, out-of-body experience. Now we have telematics, telepresence, and the aesthetics of apparition." `Directly stated`

This affirmation is also what makes telepresence a flashpoint in the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension. Ascott's "out-of-body... dematerialised" presence courts exactly the disembodiment that other authors treat as loss or domination, and it presupposes that "embodied representation is giving way to disembodied reconstruction of ourselves." Edward A. Shanken, the volume's editor, marks the ambivalence in his own voice: "Cyberspace is a double-edged sword that reproduces the physical world, simultaneously intensifying and dematerializing it." Telepresence both reproduces and dissolves the body's place — it is the medium of the immaterial pole of the tension at the very moment it claims to extend, rather than abandon, embodied life. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — Telepresence is presence distributed throughout the Internet: the networked subject "both here and there, out-of-body and reembodied." Ascott historicises art's concern with presence (gods, heroes, the artist) and recasts telepresence as its telematic culmination, affirmed as a form of transcendence and the operation of [[The Distributed Self]].
- **Edward A. Shanken** (editor, *Telematic Embrace*) — frames the stakes critically: cyberspace, by which telepresence operates, simultaneously intensifies and dematerialises the physical world. Shanken also records the narrower engineering sense Ascott's milieu inherited (Eduardo Kac's "the experience of being physically present and having one's own point of view at a remote location"), against which Ascott's distributed, multiplied telepresence is the more radical reading.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Distributed Self]] — telepresence is its operation; the distribution of presence is what makes the unitary self into "the multiple, distributed presences of a set of many selves."
- [[Dematerialization]] — telepresence is explicitly "dematerialised," extending the dematerialization-of-the-art-object lineage (Lippard, with Burnham as precursor) into the telematic distribution of presence itself.
- [[Virtuality]] — telepresence operates through virtualised presence and place; presence becomes "here and not here," a virtual presence in virtual spaces.
- [[Posthumanism]] — telepresence is presence "leaving behind embodiment as the primary measure and sign of reality," a hallmark of Ascott's posthuman subject.
- [[The Cyborg]] — "cyborg living" and the psychically extended organism are the bodily correlates of a presence that can be at many sites at once.
- [[New Media Art]] — telepresence is a core term of the late new-media vocabulary Ascott codifies; it names the medium-specific condition of networked art.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — telematic art makes presence itself the experiential medium, distributing the spectator/participant across the network.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — telepresence is defined against embodied location: it is "out-of-body," "dematerialised," and presumes that "embodied representation is giving way to disembodied reconstruction of ourselves." Where embodiment ties presence to a single situated body, telepresence disperses it — the standing argument with Hayles, for whom disembodied information is always a dangerous abstraction.

## Questions It Raises

- If presence can be "both here and there, out-of-body and reembodied" at once, does telepresence genuinely overcome the body, or merely conceal a still-located body whose labour and vulnerability are displaced rather than dissolved?
- Ascott affirms telepresence as transcendence; can the same distribution of presence be read instead as the infrastructure of distributed surveillance and capture that the vault's surveillance-critical authors diagnose?
- Is art's "perennial concern with presence" really continuous from the gods of religious art to networked telepresence, or does distributing presence across the Net change its kind, not just its medium?
