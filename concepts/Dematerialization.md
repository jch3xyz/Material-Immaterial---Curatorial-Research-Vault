# Dematerialization

## Brief Definition

The titular, organizing concept of Lucy Lippard's *Six Years*: the art-historical shift, roughly 1966–1972, away from the art object as a unique material commodity toward work in which the idea is paramount and the material vehicle is ephemeral, cheap, or absent.

## Longer Explanation

For Lippard, dematerialization names not a style but a phenomenological tendency she tracked across "widely differing phenomena within a time span." Her operative gloss is a **deemphasis** rather than an abolition: she refers to "a process of dematerialization, or a deemphasis on material aspects (uniqueness, permanence, decorative attractive[ness])." In her companion definition of Conceptual art, the same axis appears as a hierarchy of attention — "work in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized.'" The object does not vanish so much as recede behind the idea; "the studio is again becoming a study."

The term enters the literature with the **Lippard & Chandler 1968 essay** "The Dematerialization of Art" (*Art International*, February 1968), where they diagnose an "ultra-conceptual art that emphasizes the thinking process almost exclusively" and conclude that "such a trend appears to be provoking a profound dematerialization of art." They split the tendency into two vectors — art as idea (where "matter is denied, as sensation has been transformed into concept") and art as action (where "matter has been transformed into energy and motion").

Crucially, Lippard concedes from the start that the term is **inaccurate**. By her own report it "has often been pointed out to me that dematerialization is an inaccurate term, that a piece of paper or a photograph is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead. Granted. But for lack of a better term I have continued to refer to a process of dematerialization." The note carries this self-correction in its bones: dematerialization is a useful metaphor for a real reweighting of values, not a literal claim that art escaped matter.

The concept is best read as the **art-historical instance** of the broader [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis. Where that tension is the cross-author home of the opposition (object vs. information, body vs. pattern), dematerialization is Lippard's specific 1960s case: an attempt to make art "free of art-world commodity status" by lodging it in "the inexpensive, ephemeral, unintimidating character of the Conceptual mediums themselves (video, performance, photography, narrative, text, actions)." The story ends in recuperation. In the Postface, Lippard records that the hope of escaping commercialization was "for the most part unfounded": "whatever minor revolutions in communication have been achieved by the process of dematerializing the object . . . , art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** — coins and qualifies the term; uses it as the loose organizing thread of *Six Years*, explicitly as a "deemphasis on material aspects" and explicitly conceding its inaccuracy. With critic John Chandler she authors the 1968 source essay.
- **Internal dissent — [[Art & Language]]** — in a March 1968 letter-essay to Lippard and Chandler, the Coventry group rebuts the metaphor on physical grounds: the works "may not be an art-object as we know it in its traditional matter-state, but they are nevertheless matter in one of its forms, either solid-state, gas-state, liquid-state." Matter merely changes state; it is never dematerialized.
- **Internal dissent — Mel Bochner** — flatly denies the premise: "There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality. To deny it is to descend to irony."
- **Buckminster Fuller (cited variant)** — Lippard glosses a kindred term, **"Ephemeralization,"** which Fuller uses for "the design science strategy of doing even more with even less per unit of energy, space, and time."

## Related Concepts

- [[Virtuality]] — Hayles's pattern/presence dialectic; dematerialized art prefigures the cultural valuation of information over instantiation.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — the counter-insistence (shared with Art & Language and Bochner) that "immaterial" information always has a material substrate.
- [[Information]] — Conceptual art's substitution of "information and systems" for "traditional formal concerns of composition, color, technique, and physical presence."
- [[Systems Theory]] — the period's "systems aesthetics" (Haacke, Burnham); the working premise "to think in terms of systems."
- [[New Media Art]] — the ephemeral Conceptual mediums (video, performance, photo-text, mail art) seed later new-media practice.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the turn "from object to place" and toward "direct experience" reframes art as an instrument of perception rather than a possessable thing.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — dematerialization sits on the immaterial pole, but its own internal critics (Art & Language, Bochner) re-assert the material pole, making the concept the staging ground for the opposition rather than a settled resolution of it.

## Questions It Raises

- If dematerialization is conceded to be inaccurate — paper and photographs remain objects — what exactly is being de-emphasized: matter, or *value placed on* matter (uniqueness, permanence, possession)? How does this map onto the [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis as the user reads it?
- The Art & Language "matter-state" rebuttal anticipates [[The Materiality of Informatics]]: can any art ever be immaterial, or only differently embodied? What does this imply for the body as the persistent substrate of even "idea" art?
- If even the most dematerialized work "failed to escape the market" and "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries," is immateriality a real escape route from commodity status, or a new commodity form?
- Dematerialization moves art toward [[Information]], systems, and [[New Media Art]]. Does treating art as transmissible information turn it into an experiential technology — and what is lost when the encounter is "read about" rather than bodily "looked at"?
