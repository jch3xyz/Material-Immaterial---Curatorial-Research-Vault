# Telematic Art

## Brief Definition

Telematic art is art whose medium is computer-mediated telecommunications networks — art that liberates the work from the unique physical object and fixed location, locating it instead in process, distributed and asynchronous exchange, and a collective, participatory state of mind.

## Longer Explanation

The book's keystone concept, framed by editor Edward A. Shanken in the introduction and anchored in Roy Ascott's own essays, takes its name from "telematics," a term Shanken and Ascott trace to Simon Nora: "It was Simon Nora who coined the term telematics to describe the new electronic technology derived from the convergence of computers and telecommunications systems" (ch. 17). For Ascott this convergence is not a new tool but a new condition — "the convergence of these two, in themselves vastly powerful media, constitutes a paradigm change in our culture" (ch. 15). The rubric "telematics," he writes, "not only refers to the convergence of computers and telecommunications systems but qualifies a whole class of consciousness and culture in which new modalities of knowledge and the means of their distribution are being tested and extended" (ch. 16). [Directly stated]

The decisive move is from object to system. "Art does not reside in the object alone, nor is meaning fixed or stable within the physical limits of the artist's work. Art is all process, all system" (ch. 17). This restates, in a networked register, the object→system spine that [[Jack Burnham]] gave [[Systems Esthetics]]: the computer, Ascott insists elsewhere in the book, is "not primarily a thing, an object, but a set of behaviours, a system, actually, a system of systems." Telematic art accordingly "challenges the traditional relationship between active viewing subjects and passive art objects by creating interactive, behavioral contexts for remote aesthetic encounters" (Shanken, ch. 04). The art object is replaced by the interface; the viewer is replaced by the participator. [Directly stated]

Meaning, in consequence, is not transmitted but produced. Ascott rejects the classical sender→channel→receiver picture: "In a telematic art, meaning is not created by the artist, distributed through the network, and received by the observer. Meaning is the product of interaction between the observer and the system, the content of which is in a state of flux, of endless change and transformation" (ch. 20). What looks like a property special to the network is in fact, he argues, a making-explicit of all aesthetic experience: "telematic networking makes explicit in its technology and protocols what is implicit in all aesthetic experience, where that experience is seen as being as much creative in the act of the viewer's perception as it is in the artist's production" (ch. 20). From this follow [[Dispersed Authorship]] (authorship distributed across all those negotiating meaning) and the asynchronous, distributed exchange that lets participants "interact in electronic space, via computer memory and beyond the normal constraints of time" (ch. 17). [Directly stated]

The horizon of the concept is collective and spiritual. The networked aesthetic, for Ascott, is oriented toward a [[Network Consciousness]] — the planetary "subtle body," noosphere, or global brain emerging from the symbiosis of human mind and networked processing; the "networked consciousness that is greater than the sum of its parts" Shanken names as the integral conceptual core of Ascott's work (ch. 04). Telematic art is thus future-facing and constructive: "Thus telematic art, moving beyond object art and time-based art, uses simulation to render what is invisible visible, to bring the virtual, the potential, the unseen, and unrealised into view" (ch. 19). Note: this note is oriented in the editor's introduction and the surrounding essays; the telematic essays proper (chs. 15–24, 26–32) are the primary text and re-list there. [Strongly implied]

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and develops telematic art as the central concept of *Telematic Embrace*: art of the computer/telecommunications network, defined by interactivity, [[Dispersed Authorship]], process over object, asynchronous distributed exchange, and a planetary [[Network Consciousness]]. The keystone passages are anchored in his own essays (chs. 15–20); the framing definition is supplied by editor Edward A. Shanken in the introduction (ch. 04).
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (precursor) — Burnham's 1968 [[Systems Esthetics]] and [[Object and System]] supply the object→system spine and the [[Reciprocal Communication]] (two-way) ideal that Ascott extends into the networked, participatory register. Telematic art is the behavioural/participatory pole of cybernetic-systems art to Burnham's object→system formalism. [Interpretive synthesis]

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object vs System]] — telematic art sits decisively on the system pole; the autonomous, unique, located art object is precisely what it dissolves.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Ascott's telematic content is immaterial ("pure electronic différance") until reconstituted at the interface, his contribution to this multi-author tension.
- [[One-Way Stimulation vs Two-Way Communication]] — telematic art rejects the one-way Shannon–Weaver/Renaissance "despatch" of meaning for participatory, two-way interaction.

## Questions It Raises

- If meaning is "the product of interaction between the observer and the system," in what sense, if any, does the artist still author the work — and what becomes of intention, originality, and critique?
- Is the leap from networked interactivity to a planetary "network consciousness greater than the sum of its parts" an empirical claim, a metaphor, or a spiritual aspiration — and does Ascott's "techno-utopian rhetoric" (Shanken's phrase) survive Han's later reading of the same networked condition as a [[Digital Panopticon]]?
- If telematic art "liberates" art from the object and unique location, does the immaterial network simply substitute new forms of dependence (infrastructure, platforms, surveillance) for the old materiality?
- How does telematic art's "make the invisible visible" ambition relate to [[Dematerialization]] in conceptual art — continuation, fulfillment, or departure?
