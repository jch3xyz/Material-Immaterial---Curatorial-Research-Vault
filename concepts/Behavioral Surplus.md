# Behavioral Surplus

## Brief Definition

The portion of behavioral data claimed beyond what service improvement requires — declared proprietary, fed to "machine intelligence," and fabricated into prediction products; the "crucial surplus" of which human beings are the source rather than the customer.

## Longer Explanation

Behavioral surplus is the foundational economic term of [[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s [[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]]. Human experience is unilaterally claimed as free raw material and translated into behavioral data; some of that data is returned to the user as service quality, but the *rest* — the residue beyond what improvement needs — is declared a proprietary surplus, processed by "machine intelligence," and turned into prediction products sold in behavioral futures markets (`Directly stated`, Ch.1). The surplus is therefore not a by-product but the very source of value: Zuboff narrates its "discovery" at Google as the mutation that produced a 3,590 percent revenue increase in under four years (`Directly stated`, Ch.3).

The concept reframes who and what we are in this economy. We are "not surveillance capitalism's 'customers'"; the familiar adage that "if it's free, then you are the product" is also wrong — we are instead "the sources" of the surplus, the objects of an inescapable "raw-material-extraction operation" whose actual customers are the enterprises trading in markets for our future behavior (`Directly stated`, Ch.1). This is the move that aligns behavioral surplus with [[The Body as Data]] and with the surveillance diagnoses of [[Big Data]] and [[Dataism]]: the self becomes an extraction site.

Once discovered, the surplus expands without natural limit. Zuboff catalogs its widening scope — content itself, the behavior of those who produce content, their patterns of connection and mobility, thoughts and feelings, and the metadata of "emoticons, exclamation points, lists, contractions, and salutations" (`Directly stated`, Ch.4) — until Google's stores "embrace everything in the online milieu" (`Directly stated`, Ch.5). The frontier then turns inward to "the dark data continent of your inner life," rendering intentions, moods, and personality "into the light for others' profit" (`Directly stated`, Ch.9).

The endpoint of the logic is no longer prediction but production: "the most predictive source of all is behavior that has already been modified to orient toward guaranteed outcomes" (`Directly stated`, Ch.10) — surplus extraction shading into behavior modification, the hinge toward [[Surveillance]] as a mode of power rather than merely of watching.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — the originating use. Behavioral surplus is behavioral data claimed *beyond service improvement*, declared proprietary, and processed by "machine intelligence" into prediction products. Its discovery is the founding act of surveillance capitalism; its expansion runs from online traces, to inner life, to behavior pre-modified for guaranteed outcomes.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Body as Data]] — the surplus is the operational form data takes once experience is rendered extractable; behavioral surplus is the economic name for that rendering.
- [[Big Data]] — behavioral surplus is the raw material whose aggregation *is* the big-data archive in its surveillance-capitalist application.
- [[Dataism]] — supplies the worldview (data as supreme value) that behavioral surplus monetizes as an extraction logic.
- [[Surveillance]] — behavioral surplus is the economic engine that drives surveillance from observation toward extraction and modification (realized by this book).
- [[Quantified Self]] — self-tracking devices (e.g., activity monitors) are among the apps Zuboff cites as surplus-extraction and behavior-change instruments (realized by this book).
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's voluntary-exposure diagnosis names the subjective condition that Zuboff names economically as surplus extraction. `Speculative connection`

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Narrativity]] — surplus reduces lived experience to "immeasurably tiny bits of behavior available for calculation," the opposite of narrated, irreducible experience. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Questions It Raises

- Where is the line between data used for service improvement and the "surplus" claimed beyond it — and who is positioned to see or contest it?
- If we are the *source* and not the *customer*, what would a non-extractive relation to behavioral data even look like?
- When the most predictive surplus is behavior already modified, has prediction collapsed into control? (Anchors the move into [[Surveillance]] as power.)
