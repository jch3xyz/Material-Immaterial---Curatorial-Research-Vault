# The Artist as Integral Awareness

## Brief Definition

Marshall McLuhan's name for the figure — in any field, scientific or humanistic — who grasps the formal implications of a new technology decades before its impact lands, and whose art therefore functions as "exact information of how to rearrange one's psyche" so a culture can meet the next technological blow consciously rather than be numbed by it.

## Longer Explanation

"The Artist as Integral Awareness" is the keystone and most ethically charged proposal of Chapter 7. Having argued throughout Part I that every new medium performs a [[Media Change as Collective Surgery|collective surgery]] on the social body — and that the incised area is anesthetized by the built-in [[Narcosis|numbing]] apparatus, so that "the area of impact and incision is numb" while "it is the entire system that is changed" — McLuhan asks whether there is any counter-faculty that can break the involuntary trance. His answer is the artist. The artist is defined not by medium or guild but by a mode of perception: "The artist is the man in any field, scientific or humanistic, who grasps the implications of his actions and of new knowledge in his own time. He is the man of integral awareness." [`Directly stated`]

Integral awareness is the conscious counter-pole to the involuntary alteration of [[Sense Ratios|sense ratios]] that ordinarily proceeds "steadily and without any resistance." Where the percussed victim is reorganized perceptually before any judgment can form, the artist "can correct the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures." This early-warning capacity is, for McLuhan, age-old: "The artist picks up the message of cultural and technological challenge decades before its transforming impact occurs," building "models or Noah's arks for facing the change that is at hand." Quoting Wyndham Lewis — "The artist is always engaged in writing a detailed history of the future because he is the only person aware of the nature of the present" — McLuhan insists that in the electric age this is no longer a luxury: "Knowledge of this simple fact is now needed for human survival." [`Directly stated`]

This reframes art itself. Set against the surgery metaphor, art supplies the missing immunity — "To have a disease without its symptoms is to be immune… Today we have begun to sense that art may be able to provide such immunity" — and McLuhan stakes the whole proposal on a redefinition of what art *is*: "I am curious to know what would happen if art were suddenly seen for what it is, namely, exact information of how to rearrange one's psyche in order to anticipate the next blow from our own extended faculties." Art is no longer ornament or "art appreciation" but "precise advance knowledge of how to cope with the psychic and social consequences of the next technology" — to be read like "social navigation charts." From this follows the chapter's most quoted gesture: "To prevent undue wreckage in society, the artist tends now to move from the ivory tower to the control tower of society," indispensable "in the shaping and analysis and understanding of the life of forms" created by electric technology. [`Directly stated`]

McLuhan folds in a second, allied technique borrowed from Bertrand Russell. Where the new technology delivers a "numbing or narcotic effect… that lulls attention while the new form slams the gates of judgment and perception," Russell's "technique of the suspended judgment" is its deliberate antidote: "Now the 'technique of the suspended judgment' presents the possibility of rejecting the narcotic and of postponing indefinitely the operation of inserting the new technology in the social psyche." Integral awareness and suspended judgment are thus the two faces of the same conscious counter-faculty — the artist's perceptual anticipation and the analyst's refusal to be operated on automatically — and together they hold out the hope of "a new stasis," a chosen rather than sleepwalked relation to one's own extensions. [`Strongly implied`]

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — *Understanding Media* (1964), Ch. 7. The artist is "the man of integral awareness" in any field; art is "exact information of how to rearrange one's psyche" to anticipate the next technological blow, and so the artist moves "from the ivory tower to the control tower of society." This is the ethical payoff of the chapter's surgery/narcosis diagnosis: the artist is the only faculty that can correct the sense ratios *before* numbness sets in.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Narcosis]] — the involuntary, anesthetic counter-pole: the narcotic "lulls attention while the new form slams the gates of judgment and perception," whereas integral awareness keeps those gates open. [`Directly stated`]
- The world of "art appreciation" — McLuhan treats the bureaucracy of celebrating artists as "a conspiracy to make the artist a frill," a way of "ignoring their prophetic work, and preventing its timely use for survival." [`Directly stated`]

## Questions It Raises

- If only the artist of "integral awareness" can encounter technology with impunity, what becomes of everyone else — are the "percussed victims" simply condemned to "take it on the chin"?
- Is "exact information of how to rearrange one's psyche" a literal cognitive claim or a heightened metaphor? McLuhan's "navigation charts" language pushes toward the literal.
- Does the move "from the ivory tower to the control tower" make the artist a technocratic adviser — and at what cost to art's autonomy? (Galbraith recommending new art "to the careful study of businessmen who want to stay in business.")
- How does integral awareness relate to Russell's "suspended judgment" — are they the same faculty, or does suspended judgment merely buy the time in which integral awareness can do its work?
- Can a culture institutionalize integral awareness, or is it irreducibly the gift of "only the dedicated artist… [who] seems to have the power for encountering the present actuality"?
