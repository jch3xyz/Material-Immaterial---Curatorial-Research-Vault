# Network Consciousness

## Brief Definition

The emergent, distributed field of mind that telematic exchange produces — a decentralized yet collective state of mind in which the networked artwork creates a field of consciousness greater than the sum of its parts.

## Longer Explanation

**Network consciousness** is the destination of Roy Ascott's [[Telematic Art|telematic]] project: the claim that the global convergence of computers and telecommunications does not merely move information faster but engenders a new, shared, planetary state of mind. Where McLuhan's [[The Global Village|global village]] described the electric retribalization of the planet through extensions of the central nervous system, Ascott's network consciousness is its networked successor — a mind no longer figured as a village but as a web with "no centre, or hierarchy, no top nor bottom," in which awareness itself is the emergent property of billions of distributed exchanges. The editor of his collected writings, Edward A. Shanken, names this "the conceptual idea of Ascott's telematic art — that electronic telecommunications technologies may contribute to the creation of a networked consciousness that is greater than the sum of its parts." (*Strongly implied / Directly stated* — Shanken states it as the book's thesis; Ascott develops it across the essays.)

The concept has a phenomenological pole and a cosmic pole. Phenomenologically, networking is for Ascott a felt expansion of mind: logging on "puts you, in a sense, out of body, linking your mind into a kind of timeless sea," a "shared activity of mind" in which "every idea is a part of every other idea" and "every participant reflects every other participant in the whole." This is not McLuhan's tribal immersion alone but a precise, navigable, distributed cognition — closer to [[Distributed Cognition]] than to mysticism, though Ascott deliberately courts both. Cosmically, he reaches for Teilhard de Chardin's **noosphere** ("a thinking layer, enveloping the biosphere of the earth") and Peter Russell's **global brain**: with the convergence of computers and telecommunications "the 'thinking system' becomes planetary," and we may be "approaching the next stage of planetary awareness — global consciousness." (*Directly stated*, ch. 17.)

Crucially, for Ascott this is an aesthetic claim before it is a technological one. The telematic artwork is not an object but a process — "a web of relationships between ideas and images in constant flux" completed by whoever enters the network — so the consciousness it generates is itself part of the work, an [[Emergence|emergent]] field arising from [[Reciprocal Communication|reciprocal]] exchange rather than from any single transmitting author. Network consciousness is therefore the consciousness-register of his larger argument that art has shifted "from the field of objects to the field of behaviour": the network is the medium, distributed mind is the message. He traces the seed of the idea back to his 1960s cybernetic art (the Cybernetic Art Matrix, in which "a psychic consciousness of the human group within it evolves") and even to his speculative 1970 "psibernetic" fusion of cybernetics and ESP as "a kind of instant global sympathy."

The concept carries Ascott's characteristic utopian charge, which the vault should hold at arm's length. He reads the planetary network as convivial liberation, a "harmonisation and creative development of the whole planet" — the affirmative inverse of the same global connectivity that Han diagnoses as the [[Digital Panopticon]] and Haraway reads as the informatics of domination. Network consciousness is thus both a genuine theory of emergent collective mind and a piece of late-twentieth-century techno-optimism; its descriptive force and its prophetic register should be cited separately. (*Interpretive synthesis*.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and anchors the concept across his telematic essays (1984–1988). Telematics is "a decentralized yet collective state of mind"; networking is "a shared activity of mind"; the convergence of computers and telecommunications makes "the 'thinking system' planetary," approaching "global consciousness." Network consciousness is the consciousness-pole of his network paradigm for art, distinct from but inseparable from [[Telematic Art]] (the medium/practice) and [[Reciprocal Communication]] (the two-way structure that produces it).
- **Edward A. Shanken (editor, via Ascott)** — frames it for the volume: telematics "emphasizes the emergence of a collective consciousness," and the networked consciousness "greater than the sum of its parts" is "an integral part of [Ascott's] work and the history of the genre." Attribute ch. 04 quotes to Shanken writing *about* Ascott, not to Ascott himself.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Global Village]] — the direct predecessor; Ascott's network consciousness is McLuhan's electric global village pushed into the computer-network era (whole-earth image, planetary all-at-onceness).
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — the cognitive-science register of the same claim; Bateson's "human plus computer system plus environment constitutes a thinking system," scaled to the planet.
- [[Emergence]] — network consciousness is figured as emergent: a collective mind "greater than the sum of its parts," arising bottom-up from distributed exchange.
- [[Telematic Art]] — the medium and practice whose destination network consciousness is; the artwork as a web in flux generates the field of mind.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the two-way, sender↔receiver symmetry that makes a collective rather than broadcast consciousness possible.
- [[New Media Art]] / [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the art-historical lineage (Burnham-anchored) that Ascott extends into the networked, consciousness-expanding register.
- [[Re-enchantment]] — the noosphere / global-brain / "spiritual interchange" framing aligns network consciousness with the technological re-enchantment cluster (O'Gieblyn).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Hell of the Same]] — Han's diagnosis that hyperconnectivity produces not a richer collective mind but a smooth, frictionless echo of the same; the pessimistic counter-reading of Ascott's convivial network.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — the same planetary network read as pervasive control rather than expanded consciousness; the surveillance counter-image to Ascott's "global sympathy."

## Questions It Raises

- Is "network consciousness" a genuine emergent property of mind, or a metaphor projected onto an infrastructure of information exchange? Where is the line between distributed cognition and mystified collective mind?
- Does a decentralized network necessarily produce a *collective* consciousness, or can the same architecture produce Han's [[Hell of the Same]] and the [[Digital Panopticon]] — homogenization and control rather than enriched shared awareness?
- If the telematic artwork is "greater than the sum of its parts," who or what is the subject of the resulting consciousness — and can it be the subject of an artwork at all?
- Ascott's claim is dated 1984–1988, before the commercial internet. Which of its predictions about planetary consciousness have been realized, inverted, or rendered moot by the actual network that emerged?
