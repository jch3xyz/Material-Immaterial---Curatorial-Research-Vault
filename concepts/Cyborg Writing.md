# Cyborg Writing

## Brief Definition

Cyborg writing is Donna Haraway's name for the seizure, by colonized and othered groups, of the very tools of writing and signification that had marked them as other — a politics of language that survives not by appeal to original innocence but by repurposing the conqueror's code against itself, insisting on "noise" and "pollution" rather than on perfect, transparent communication.

## Longer Explanation

For Haraway, writing is "preeminently the technology of cyborgs, etched surfaces of the late twentieth century." Writing was central to the Western myth that separated oral from written cultures, "primitive" from "civilized" mentalities, and it underwrote what she calls the phallogocentrism of the West — "its worship of the monotheistic, phallic, authoritative, and singular work, the unique and perfect name." Cyborg writing turns this technology back on its makers. It refuses the founding plot of the Fall — the fantasy of "a once-upon-a-time wholeness before language, before writing, before Man." Instead it is, in her exact words, "about the power to survive, not on the basis of original innocence, but on the basis of seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other." `Directly stated`

The political content of cyborg writing is its stance toward communication itself. Haraway frames cyborg politics as "the struggle for language and the struggle against perfect communication, against the one code that translates all meaning perfectly, the central dogma of phallogocentrism." This is the conceptual heart of the idea: domination operates through the dream of a single, total, transparent code, and resistance therefore takes the form of deliberate impurity — "that is why cyborg politics insist on noise and advocate pollution, rejoicing in the illegitimate fusions of animal and machine." Cyborg writing does not aspire to clarity, wholeness, or the recovery of a lost mother tongue; it weaponizes illegitimacy, hybridity, and the refusal of a "finally privileged reading or salvation history." `Directly stated`

Haraway grounds the concept in concrete literary evidence. The writing of U.S. women of color is "repeatedly about writing, about access to the power to signify; but this time that power must be neither phallic nor innocent." Her central figure is Cherríe Moraga's *Loving in the War Years*, whose language she reads as **a chimera of two conquerors' tongues**: "Moraga's language is not 'whole'; it is self-consciously spliced, a chimera of English and Spanish, both conquerors' languages. But it is this chimeric monster, without claim to an original language before violation, that crafts the erotic, competent, potent identities of women of color." She aligns Moraga's "superb literacy" with Malinche's "mastery of the conqueror's language — a violation, an illegitimate production, that allows survival." The mestiza writer who "never possessed the original language, never told the original story" is precisely the cyborg author, because she "cannot base identity on a myth or a fall from innocence and right to natural names." `Directly stated`

The concept culminates in Haraway's closing image for the manifesto as a whole — **infidel heteroglossia**. Cyborg imagery, she writes, "can suggest a way out of the maze of dualisms in which we have explained our bodies and our tools to ourselves. This is a dream not of a common language, but of a powerful infidel heteroglossia." The phrase folds cyborg writing's two refusals into one figure: it is *infidel* (it betrays the central dogma, the one perfect code) and *heteroglot* (it is irreducibly many-voiced, impure, untranslatable into a single tongue). It is, she adds, "an imagination of a feminist speaking in tongues to strike fear into the circuits of the supersavers of the new right." Cyborg writing thus names not a style but a survival strategy: rewriting "the texts of their bodies and societies" without the alibi of innocence. `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — The concept originates entirely in chapter 6 of *A Cyborg Manifesto* ("Cyborgs: A Myth of Political Identity"). For Haraway it is inseparable from her account of "women of color" as a cyborg identity and from her larger polemic against the "informatics of domination." Cyborg writing is the linguistic and political practice that follows from being, in her terms, a hybrid, mosaic, chimera with "no available original dream of a common language." She does not propose recovering a pure prior speech; she proposes "recoding communication and intelligence to subvert command and control." `Directly stated`

## Related Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — Cyborg writing is the political-linguistic practice proper to the cyborg figure; Haraway calls writing "preeminently the technology of cyborgs."
- [[Embodiment]] — The marked, spliced text is continuous with the marked body: "Writing marks Moraga's body, affirms it as the body of a woman of color." Cyborg writing is a politics of embodied signification, not disembodied meaning.
- [[Posthumanism]] — Cyborg writing's refusal of the unitary "Man," of original wholeness, and of the central dogma of phallogocentrism participates in the broader displacement of the liberal humanist subject. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Transparency]] — Cyborg writing is explicitly the "struggle against perfect communication, against the one code that translates all meaning perfectly." Where transparency dreams of frictionless, total, single-code legibility, cyborg writing "insists on noise and advocates pollution." ## Questions It Raises

- If resistance must take the form of "noise" and "pollution," can cyborg writing ever consolidate into a shared political program, or does its refusal of a "common language" preclude collective speech?
- Haraway insists the tool be "seized" by those it marked as other — does the strategy generalize beyond the colonized and feminized subjects she names, or is its force bound to that specific history of literacy "acquired... through a history of risking death to learn and to teach reading and writing"?
- How does cyborg writing's celebration of the "spliced" and "chimeric" relate to its parallel suspicion of "the reproductive matrix" and preference for "regeneration, not rebirth" — is impurity in language the same gesture as monstrosity in the body?
