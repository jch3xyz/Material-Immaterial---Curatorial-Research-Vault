# Cyclical Time

## Brief Definition

For [[Guy Debord]], **cyclical time** is the time of static, myth-governed agrarian societies: time experienced not as passage but as eternal return, materially grounded in the rhythm of the seasons, and secured by a myth that guarantees the cosmic order matches the established social order.

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 5 of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]* Debord narrates a history of time itself, and cyclical time is its first fully developed form. It belongs to the "static type of society" that, on becoming conscious of time, "tries to negate it, for it views time not as something that passes, but as something that returns" (thesis 126). Time is not abolished but organized as repetition: "This static type of society organizes time in a cyclical manner, in accordance with its own direct experience of nature" (thesis 126). Its material substrate is agrarian labor keyed to the seasons — "The agrarian mode of produc tion, governed by the rhythm of the seasons, is the basis for fully developed cyclical time" (thesis 127). Eternity is not a beyond but an immanence: "Eternity is within this time, it is the return of the same here on earth" (thesis 127). What holds the whole structure in place is myth, and Debord defines its function precisely: "Myth is the unitary mental construct which guarantees that the cosmic order conforms with the order that this society has in fact already established within its frontiers" (thesis 127). Myth is thus ideology in its archaic form — a total mental image that projects the *given* social arrangement onto the cosmos and receives it back as necessity. Cyclical time is, "in itself, a time without conflict" (thesis 129): a time of the *same*, of total conformism, in which "the members of these societies remain human at the price of always remaining the same" (thesis 130).

Cyclical time is not, however, self-sufficient in Debord's account; it is the base from which a ruling class skims a temporal surplus. The society of cyclical time is already divided into classes, and "the class that organizes this social labor and appropriates its limited surplus value, simultaneously appropriates the temporal surplus value resulting from its organization of social time: it alone possesses the irreversible time of the living" (thesis 128). This is the pivot of the chapter: [[Irreversible Time]] — the linear, adventuring, world-making time of masters, dynasties, writing, and eventually the bourgeois economy — is *skimmed off the top* of cyclical time as a kind of temporal surplus value, leaving the "peasant masses" in "the indifferent oblivion of cyclical time (the only time known to the peasant masses who, during the rise and fall of all the empires and their chronologies, never change)" (thesis 132). Cyclical time is therefore the shared, repetitive, seasonal ground; irreversible time is the historical time appropriated by and for a ruling minority. The eventual triumph of the bourgeoisie is precisely the moment "the cyclical time that remained at the base of society" is "eradicated" by "the irreversible time of the bourgeois economy" (thesis 141), which then "tends to socially eliminate such lived time" altogether (thesis 142). The tension between these two times is the substance of [[Cyclical vs Irreversible Time]].

Cyclical time returns, transformed, in Chapter 6. Once the commodity economy has abolished the real cyclical time of agrarian life, the [[Spectacle|spectacle]] manufactures a counterfeit of it — [[Pseudo-cyclical Time|pseudo-cyclical time]], "in fact merely a consumable disguise of the production system's commodified time" (thesis 149). This pseudo-nature "echoes the old cyclical rhythm that governed survival in preindustrial societies, incorporating the natural vestiges of cyclical time while generating new variants: day and night, work and weekend, periodic vacations" (thesis 150). What was once lived is now sold back as image and schedule. Debord fixes the difference in one of the book's sharpest chiasmi: "Cyclical time was the really lived time of unchanging illusions. Spectacular time is the illusorily lived time of a constantly changi ng reality" (thesis 155). Cyclical time, for all that it negated passage and enclosed a whole society in the *same*, was at least *really lived* and consistent with the real labor of its societies; [[Spectacular Time|spectacular time]] is not lived at all, only consumed. This makes cyclical time, paradoxically, the residue of authenticity the spectacle can only mimic — the vanished "really lived time" whose loss the whole of Chapter 6 mourns and whose counterfeit it anatomizes.

Because its "eternal return of the same here on earth" is at once a material rhythm (the seasons) and an immaterial guarantee (myth, the "unitary mental construct" that welds cosmos to social order), cyclical time is one of Debord's clearest instances of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension — a lived material temporality that survives only as an *illusion*, and that the spectacle later reconstructs as pure image. It also stands as the primary-source counterpart to [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s later diagnosis: Han's [[Fragrance of Time]] mourns the emptied, homogenized, dis-narrated time whose loss Debord had already located in the passage from really-lived cyclical time to consumable spectacular time (see [[Byung-Chul Han and Guy Debord]]).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — *The Society of the Spectacle* (1967), Chapters 5–6. Cyclical time is the time of static agrarian societies: time negated as passage and organized as eternal return (theses 126–127), materially grounded in the seasonal rhythm of the agrarian mode of production, and secured by myth as "the unitary mental construct which guarantees that the cosmic order conforms with the order that this society has in fact already established" (thesis 127). In Chapter 5 it is the *base* from which a ruling class appropriates "temporal surplus value" as [[Irreversible Time]] (thesis 128); in Chapter 6 it returns as "the really lived time of unchanging illusions" that the [[Spectacle|spectacle]] counterfeits as [[Pseudo-cyclical Time|pseudo-cyclical time]] (thesis 155).

## Related Concepts

- [[Irreversible Time]] — the linear time of rulers, writing, and the bourgeois economy, skimmed off cyclical time as "temporal surplus value" (thesis 128); listed under both related and opposing because it is *derived from* cyclical time even as it negates it.
- [[Pseudo-cyclical Time]] — the spectacle's manufactured counterfeit of cyclical time, "a consumable disguise of the production system's commodified time" that "echoes the old cyclical rhythm" (theses 149–150).
- [[Spectacular Time]] — "the illusorily lived time of a constantly changi ng reality," defined by direct contrast with cyclical time's "really lived time of unchanging illusions" (thesis 155).
- [[Spectacle]] — the regime that abolishes real cyclical time and reconstructs it as pseudo-cyclical, consumable time.
- [[Separation]] — cyclical time already contains the class separation by which the masters appropriate irreversible time from the base (thesis 128).
- [[Reification]] — the seasonal rhythm's survival as "unchanging illusion" and its later reconstruction as consumable, commodified time is the temporal face of reification.
- [[Fragrance of Time]] — [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s metaphor for the lost, gathered, narrative time whose disappearance Debord's cyclical→spectacular sequence anticipates.
- [[Narrativity]] — Han's category for the durational, story-bearing time that both cyclical time (in its own myth-bound way) and its loss put in question.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Irreversible Time]] — the constitutive opposition of Chapter 5 ([[Cyclical vs Irreversible Time]]): the returning, conflict-free, myth-governed time of the agrarian base against the linear, adventuring, historical time of the masters. Cyclical time "is a time without conflict" (thesis 129); irreversible time is the time of "adventure and war" (thesis 128) that eventually "eradicates" the cyclical vestiges (thesis 141).
- [[Pseudo-cyclical Time]] / [[Spectacular Time]] — in the register of Chapter 6, cyclical time as "really lived" stands against the spectacle's *illusorily lived* time, its degraded counterfeit (thesis 155).

## Questions It Raises

- If myth is "the unitary mental construct which guarantees that the cosmic order conforms with the order that this society has in fact already established" (thesis 127), is cyclical time's myth the archaic ancestor of the spectacle's ideology — and is the spectacle simply myth without the seasons?
- Debord grants cyclical time the dignity of being "really lived" even as he calls its illusions "unchanging" (thesis 155). Is really-lived illusion preferable to illusorily-lived reality — and does the whole critique of the spectacle presuppose that it is?
- If irreversible time is "temporal surplus value" extracted from cyclical time (thesis 128), is the emancipatory "federation of independent times" Debord names at the chapter's end (a "federation of playful individual and collective forms of irreversible time") a return to cyclical time, a completion of irreversible time, or the abolition of both?
- Does [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s lament for the lost "[[Fragrance of Time|fragrance of time]]" secretly mourn cyclical time — a return of the pre-modern rhythm Debord treats as already-lost and not to be restored?
