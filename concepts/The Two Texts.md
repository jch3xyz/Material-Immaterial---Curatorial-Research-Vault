# The Two Texts

## Brief Definition

Surveillance capitalism compels the production of two "electronic texts": a public-facing **first text** that we author and read (search results, the News Feed, our posts and likes), and a hidden **shadow text** — a "read only" accumulation of behavioral surplus that "is about us, but it is not for us."

## Longer Explanation

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], the command of [[The Division of Learning in Society]] "begins with what I call the problem of the two texts." The first of these is familiar and celebrated: it is the universe of information and connection at our fingertips, much of it composed of "what we inscribe on its pages" — posts, photos, conversations, "likes," tweets, "and all the great massing hubbub of our lives captured and communicated." When it comes to this first text, Zuboff writes, "we are its authors and readers." (`Directly stated`)

The decisive move of the regime is that the first text "does not stand alone; it trails a shadow close behind." The first text "actually functions as the supply operation for the second text: the shadow text." Everything contributed to the first text, "no matter how trivial or fleeting, becomes a target for surplus extraction" — and that [[Behavioral Surplus]] "fills the pages of the second text." The shadow text is therefore not a separate sphere but the parasite of, and the reason for, the first: the public-facing page is the harvesting surface, and the hidden page is "where the treasure flows." (`Directly stated`)

The shadow text's defining quality is its **asymmetry of access and address**. It is "hidden from our view: 'read only' for surveillance capitalists." As "the source from which all the treasure flows, this second text is about us, but it is not for us"; it is "created, maintained, and exploited outside our awareness for others' benefit." In Chapter 18 Zuboff sharpens the formula: the shadow text is "the one that is for them but not for us." Where Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] turns on what subjects voluntarily expose, the two-texts thesis names the inverse and complementary secret: that the most consequential text — the one composed of [[Behavioral Surplus]] refined into [[Prediction Products]] — is, by economic design, unreadable to the very people it is about. (`Directly stated`)

This asymmetry is structurally guarded, not incidental. Zuboff's running case study is the self-service "download your data" tools that "promise to give users access to their personal data." These deliver only the first text: when you download your "personal information," she writes, "you access the stage, not the backstage: the curtain, not the wizard." The shadow text — targeting analyses, prediction products, the fate of those predictions in behavioral modification — remains a "crucial proprietary source of advantage," and "no surveillance capitalist will voluntarily provide data from the shadow text. Only law can compel this challenge to the pathological division of learning." The two texts are thus the textual face of the asymmetries of knowledge that constitute [[Instrumentarian Power]]. (`Directly stated`)

A corollary developed in Chapter 18: because the second text is the only one that economically matters, the integrity of the first becomes expendable. Under [[Radical Indifference]] "content is judged by its volume, range, and depth of surplus," so the first text "is vulnerable to corruption with content that would normally be perceived as repugnant: lies, systematic disinformation, fraud, violence, hate speech." Disinformation breaks through only when it "threatens to interrupt the flow of surplus into the second 'shadow' text." Radical indifference "sustains the pathological division of learning in society by forfeiting the integrity of public knowledge for the sake of the volume and scope of the shadow text." The public text can be poisoned without consequence precisely because it is not the text that is read for profit. (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** (*The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*) — coins "the problem of the two texts." The **first text** is public-facing, authored and read by us; the **shadow text** (also "second text") is read-only for surveillance capitalists, a "burgeoning accumulation of behavioral surplus and its analyses" that "says more about us than we can know about ourselves." The first is the supply operation for the second; the second "is about us, but it is not for us." The pair is the literary form of the asymmetry at the heart of [[The Division of Learning in Society]].
- The shadow-text figure (the stage/backstage, the curtain/wizard) is folded into this concept as Zuboff's central image for the regime's hidden second register; it is not minted as a separate metaphor note.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Visibility vs Understanding]] — the two texts invert the usual transparency promise: what is made visible (the first text) is precisely not what is known (the shadow text), and what is known is made invisible.
- The democratic ideal of reciprocal, readable public knowledge — against which the shadow text is "read only" for one party, structurally beyond contest.

## Questions It Raises

- If the shadow text "says more about us than we can know about ourselves," what becomes of self-knowledge, autonomy, and the right to contest decisions made from a text we cannot read?
- Can "only law" — as Zuboff insists — actually compel disclosure of the shadow text, given that its proprietary opacity is the source of competitive advantage?
- Does the two-texts structure complete or contradict Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] of voluntary self-exposure — exposure into a text the self can never see?
- If the integrity of the first text is economically expendable, is "content moderation" anything more than a defensive measure to protect the flow of surplus?
