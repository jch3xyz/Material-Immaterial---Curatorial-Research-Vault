# The City as Zone of Transformation

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's reconception of the city not as edificial surfaces and structures but as a cybernetic, feedback-driven, interactive *living system* — a "zone of transformation" designed across four zones (underground, street level, sky/sea, cyberspace), "grown" rather than built, to accommodate telepresence and the transpersonal technologies of a global culture.

## Longer Explanation

In "The Architecture of Cyberception" (1994), Ascott turns the faculty of [[Cyberception]] onto the built environment and indicts Western architecture for its fixation on appearance. "The problem with Western architecture is that it is too much concerned with surfaces and structures and too little concerned with living systems. There is no biology of building, simply the physics of space" (Directly stated). The contemporary city, for Ascott, is "a battle zone" of competing styles defined by "Edificial images, superficial surfaces" — protecting inertia, indifferent to "radical change or intimations of the future." Against this, he proposes the city be reconceived from the everyday user's standpoint as "a zone of negotiation made up of a multitude of networks and systems," requiring "designers of such spaces" who work in "systems interfaces and network nodes" rather than "traditional architectural discourse" (Directly stated).

The positive program is that "we need bring into being the idea of zones of transformation, to accommodate the transpersonal technologies that are shaping our global culture" (Directly stated). The city becomes "dynamic, evolving zones of transformation, just as interactive art itself is about transformation and change" — the design principles Ascott takes from interactive art (connectivity and interaction) are to become "primary principles" of urban planning. Concretely, planning must abandon the merely three-dimensional and "apply connectivity and interaction to four quite different zones: underground, street level, sky/sea, and cyberspace," substituting "wormholes... tunnelling between separate realities, real and virtual" for "streets, alleyways, avenues and boulevards" (Directly stated). The architect's new task is "to fuse together material structures and cyberspace organisms into a new continuum."

Crucially, the city is to be *grown*, not designed. "It will only be with the understanding that buildings must be planted and 'grown' that architecture will flourish. It's a growbag culture that is needed, in which seeding replaces designing. Architectural practice should find its guiding metaphors in horticulture, rather than in warfare" (Directly stated). The new city exists "both in its visible immateriality and its invisible construction," and "will grow into a fruitful reality only if it is seeded with imagination and vision." The agent of that seeding is the artist: "It is artists who can become the sowers of these seeds... It is their cyberception that equips them with the global awareness and conceptual dexterity to re-see, rethink, and rebuild our world" (Directly stated).

This is, structurally, [[Jack Burnham]]'s [[Object and System|object→system]] thesis applied to the built environment (Interpretive synthesis). Where Burnham traces sculpture's passage from the discrete material object to the responsive cybernetic system, Ascott carries the same logic into architecture and urbanism: the building-as-surface gives way to the city-as-system, "surfaces and structures" give way to "living systems." Ascott reaches further than Burnham by binding the transformation to telepresence and the distributed self — architecture must answer to "the realities of the teleself and distributed presence," which contemporary architecture has ignored, leaving it "more or less the same thing" as shopping (Directly stated). The city of "dense and intractable materiality" is replaced by "the environmental fluidity of faster-than-light pathways, intelligent surfaces and structures, and transformable habitations" — an explicitly de-materializing claim that makes the chapter a strong [[Material vs Immaterial]] anchor for Ascott.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** (*Telematic Embrace*, ch. 27, "The Architecture of Cyberception," 1994) — the city as a cybernetic "zone of transformation": a living, feedback-rich system of interfaces and nodes, designed across four zones, "grown" not built (a "growbag culture"), with the cyberceptive artist as the "sower of seeds." The application of his [[Cyberception]] / network-consciousness vocabulary to architecture and urbanism.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*, 1968) — the precursor logic (Interpretive synthesis). Burnham's [[Systems Esthetics]] and his [[Object and System|object→system]] passage in sculpture are the art-historical engine that Ascott extends from the gallery object into the city; the "surfaces and structures vs living systems" indictment is the architectural form of Burnham's object-vs-system spine.

## Related Concepts

- [[Cyberception]] — the faculty (whole-seeing, pattern-perceiving, transpersonal) that lets planners and artists "re-see, rethink, and rebuild" the city; the city of the twenty-first century is reached "at the level of city planning and architecture" only through cyberception.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's art-specific object→system thesis; Ascott's "zone of transformation" is its urbanistic extension.
- [[Object and System]] — the keystone Burnham↔Ascott bridge: the building/object yielding to the city/system.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the city must "articulate these meanings within integrated, cybernetic systems"; feedback and interaction become design principles.
- [[New Media Art]] — interactive, transformational art supplies the model ("interactive art itself is about transformation and change") for the interactive city.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the city as an experiential, navigable, immersive environment ("immerse yourself in its cyberspace").
- [[The Global Village]] — McLuhan's planetary nervous system is the lineage of Ascott's networked, "transpersonal" city (Interpretive synthesis).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Surface and Structure vs Living System]] — the tension this concept resolves toward the "living system" pole: edificial surfaces / "the physics of space" against "a biology of building" and cybernetic systems.

## Questions It Raises

- If a city is "grown" rather than designed — "seeding replaces designing" — who or what governs the growth, and does horticulture-not-warfare actually displace power, or merely re-describe it?
- Does designing for "the teleself and distributed presence" dissolve the public, shared, material city, or enrich it — and for whom (Ascott's user is already cyberceptive)?
- Is the "biology of building" a genuine systems-ecology of the built environment, or a metaphor that risks naturalizing — and thereby de-politicizing — infrastructure?
- How far is the immaterial city ("visible immateriality and invisible construction") compatible with the irreducibly material substrate (energy, hardware, land) it still depends on?
