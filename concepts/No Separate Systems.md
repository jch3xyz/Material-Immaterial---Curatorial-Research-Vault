# No Separate Systems

## Brief Definition

Donella Meadows's thesis that systems rarely have real edges: the world is a continuum, and every boundary drawn around a system is an artifact of "word, thought, perception, and social agreement," invented by an observer for the purpose of a particular discussion — the questions being asked — not a seam in reality.

## Longer Explanation

For Meadows the hardest lesson in systems thinking is that "there is no single, legitimate boundary to draw around a system." Boundaries are not found in the world; they are made. To understand anything at all we must simplify, and to simplify we must cut the continuum somewhere — but the place we cut is chosen, not given. "We have to invent boundaries for clarity and sanity; and boundaries can produce problems when we forget that we've artificially created them." The trouble begins precisely at the moment the observer's own act of drawing disappears from view and the invented edge starts to look like a natural fact.

Because the boundary is chosen for a purpose, no boundary is correct in the abstract; it can only be well- or ill-fitted to the question at hand. Meadows shows both failure modes. Draw the boundary **too narrowly** and the system surprises you: plan urban traffic without settlement patterns and your new highways breed the housing developments that re-clog them; dump sewage into a river as though the river ended at the town limit and the towns downstream force you to redraw the boundary around the whole watershed. Draw the boundary **too broadly** and you fall into the analyst's opposite trap — diagrams that "cover several pages with small print and many arrows connecting everything with everything," a "my model is bigger than your model" game that buries the answer under information. Modeling the earth's climate in full detail may be fascinating and still be useless for figuring out how one country should cut its emissions. Both the too-small and the too-large boundary are sources of systemic surprise.

The right boundary, Meadows insists, "rarely coincides with the boundary of an academic discipline, or with a political boundary." Rivers make handy borders between countries and the worst possible borders for managing water; air is worse still, and "national boundaries mean nothing when it comes to ozone depletion in the stratosphere." The edges that organize our institutions — disciplinary, national, categorical — are answers to old questions, and "we get attached to the boundaries our minds happen to be accustomed to." Universities become "living monuments to boundary rigidity." The remedy is not to abolish boundaries (that would abolish thought) but to hold them lightly: "It's a great art to remember that boundaries are of our own making, and that they can and should be reconsidered for each new discussion, problem, or purpose." A boundary should not stop your thinking at the system's edge but *start* it thinking beyond that edge — where do the fuel rods go after replacement, what becomes of the unemployed person whose registration lapses.

The claim generalizes past space into time. "In a strict systems sense, there is no long-term, short-term distinction. Phenomena at different time-scales are nested within each other." The fast and the slow are continuously coupling and uncoupling; the tidy partition between the immediate and the eventual is one more line the observer draws across an unbroken flow. This is why the thesis bridges directly to [[The Observer]]: the boundary is the observer's construction, drawn for the purpose of a discussion, so that where a system *is* becomes inseparable from who is asking and what they want to know. It also unsettles the [[Material vs Immaterial|material/immaterial divide]] — Meadows treats that partition, too, as a chosen boundary rather than a found fact, since the stocks that make up a system need not be physical at all and the edge between a "material" system and its "immaterial" surroundings is exactly the kind of artificial line the thesis warns us not to reify.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — coins the thesis in *Thinking in Systems* as a corrective against boundary-reification. The boundary is an epistemic act ("we have to invent boundaries"), purpose-relative (it "depends on the purpose of the discussion—the questions we want to ask"), and double-edged (both too-narrow and too-broad boundaries produce surprise). The right boundary is discovered anew for each question and "rarely coincides with the boundary of an academic discipline, or with a political boundary." She extends the same point to time: there is no strict long-term/short-term distinction, only nested time-scales.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Observer]] — the keystone bridge: if a boundary is invented "for the purpose of the discussion," then the system's edge is the observer's construction, and where a system stops is a fact about the questioner, not only about the world. Meadows's boundaries claim is a systems-dynamics restatement of the cybernetic point that everything said is said by an observer.
- [[A System]] — the entity whose supposed edge Meadows problematizes; a system is real, but its boundary is drawn by us around it.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the discipline within which "there are no separate systems" is a foundational methodological caution against mistaking analytical cuts for natural joints.
- [[Hierarchy (Systems)]] — nested subsystems within subsystems are one reason the world is a continuum with no single legitimate boundary; hierarchy explains why the edge you pick is always one layer among many you could have picked.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Meadows treats the material/immaterial partition as itself a drawn boundary rather than an ontological seam: stocks need not be physical, and the line separating a "material" system from its "immaterial" context is precisely the kind of artificial, purpose-relative edge the thesis cautions against reifying.

## Questions It Raises

- If every boundary is "of our own making," on what grounds do we adjudicate between a too-narrow and a too-broad boundary — is there any criterion beyond fitness to the question being asked?
- If the system's edge is an observer's construction drawn for a purpose, does the observer ever stand *outside* the system they bound, or does the act of bounding fold them in (see [[The Observer]])?
- When institutions ossify their boundaries — disciplinary, national, categorical — into "living monuments to boundary rigidity," what does it cost, and how would a practice of continually redrawn boundaries actually be governed?
- If there is likewise "no long-term, short-term distinction" and time-scales are nested, does the same warning apply to every temporal deadline and planning horizon we treat as natural?
