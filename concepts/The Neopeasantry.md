# The Neopeasantry

## Brief Definition

Debord's name for the atomized, apathetic inhabitants of the spectacle's "pseudo-countryside" — a "technological pseudo-peasantry" whose passivity, unlike the old peasantry's natural fragmentation, must be actively and historically manufactured through "the organized spectacle of falsifications."

## Longer Explanation

The neopeasantry appears in Chapter 7 ("Territorial Management") of [[The Society of the Spectacle]], where Debord turns from the spectacle's temporal logic to its spatial one. Urbanism — "capitalism's method for taking over the natural and human environment" (thesis 169) — does not merely build cities; as it destroys them it "recreates a pseudo-countryside devoid both of the natural relations of the traditional countryside and of the direct (and directly challenged) social relations of the historical city" (thesis 177). The population produced by this "planned environment" Debord calls "an artificial neopeasantry." *(Directly stated, thesis 177.)*

The core of the concept is a diagnosis of manufactured apathy. Debord grants that the historical peasantry was already fragmented and passive — its "geographical dispersal and the narrow-mindedness" prevented it from "becoming a creative historical force" — but that passivity was *natural*, a byproduct of agrarian isolation. The neopeasantry differs precisely in that "its apathy must now be historically manufactured and maintained; natural ignorance has been replaced by the organized spectacle of falsifications" (thesis 177). Where the old peasant was ignorant because the world was genuinely inaccessible to him, the neopeasant is kept ignorant by design, held in a passivity that the system must continuously produce because it no longer arises on its own. *(Directly stated, thesis 177.)*

This spatial argument folds back into the book's earlier account of the [[Spectacle|spectacle's]] repression of historical time (developed across the raw chapters *Time and History* and *Spectacular Time*). The neopeasantry's habitat — the "new cities" — is for Debord "a glaring expression of the repression of historical time on which they have been built. Their motto could be: 'Nothing will ever happen here, and nothing ever has'" (thesis 177). The frozen, event-less landscape of the planned suburb is not incidental but the built form of a society that has abolished lived history: "The forces of historical absence have begun to create their own landscape because historical liberation, which must take place in the cities, has not yet occurred" (thesis 177). *(Strongly implied — the tie back to Ch5-6 is Debord's own framing of urbanism as the spatialization of temporal repression.)*

The neopeasantry is thus continuous with the spectacle's larger work of [[Separation]]. Urbanism is "the very technology of separation" (thesis 171), atomizing workers into "isolated individuals" who are then given a "controlled reintegration" — "bringing isolated individuals together as isolated individuals" — in factories, tourist resorts, and housing developments engineered to foster "pseudo-community" (thesis 172). The neopeasant is the subject of that pseudo-community: dispersed, sedated by the "omnipresent receivers of spectacular messages," and cut off from any "world of their own making." *(Interpretive synthesis — reads the neopeasantry as the human product of the separation-machine Debord names in theses 171-172.)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — coins "artificial neopeasantry" / "technological pseudo-peasantry" in thesis 177 to name the inhabitants of urbanism's pseudo-countryside, defining them against the classical peasantry by the *manufactured* (rather than natural) character of their apathy.

## Related Concepts

- [[Spectacle]] — the neopeasantry is the human landscape of the spectacle; "the organized spectacle of falsifications" is what replaces the old peasantry's natural ignorance.
- [[Separation]] — urbanism as "the very technology of separation" atomizes the population into the isolated neopeasant.
- [[Society of Control]] — the neopeasant's dispersed, administered passivity is a precursor image of environmental, distributed control (cf. Mumford, quoted by Debord, on isolation as a means of "keeping a population under control").

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Representation]] — the neopeasantry names a stilled, event-less spatial order ("nothing will ever happen here"), the negation of the "directly experienced time" and self-made history Debord opposes to spectacular representation in theses 178-179.

## Questions It Raises

- If apathy must be "historically manufactured and maintained," what is the ongoing cost and labor of producing it — and where does the manufacture become visible or fail?
- How does Debord's manufactured pseudo-countryside relate to later, screen-mediated diagnoses of the same landscape (Mumford's "sprawling isolation," the [[Society of Control]], the [[Digital Panopticon]])?
- Does the "new city" whose motto is "Nothing will ever happen here, and nothing ever has" survive only through the "repression of historical time," and what would its supersession by "directly experienced time" (thesis 178) actually require?
