# The Seventh Extinction

## Brief Definition

The "seventh extinction" is Shoshana Zuboff's closing figure for the endpoint of instrumentarian power: where industrialization's assault on the natural world augured a "sixth extinction" of vertebrate species, surveillance capitalism augurs an extinction not of nature but of human nature itself — "the will to will, the sanctity of the individual, the ties of intimacy, the sociality that binds us together in promises, and the trust they breed."

## Longer Explanation

Zuboff builds the metaphor by analogy. We have been alerted, she writes, to a "sixth extinction" — "vertebrate species disappear faster than at any time since the end of the dinosaurs." That cataclysm is "the unintended consequence of the reckless and opportunistic methods, also exalted as inevitable, with which industrialization imposed itself on the natural world because its own market forms did not hold it to account." The extinction figure thus carries a structural argument before it carries a moral one: an unaccountable market form, declaring its own expansion inevitable, produces a die-off it never intended. Industrial capitalism's logic was the conquest of nature; surveillance capitalism inherits "the same dangerous arrogance" but turns it on a new object (Directly stated).

The pivot is the change of target. "The aim now is not to dominate nature but rather human nature. The focus has shifted from machines that overcome the limits of bodies to machines that modify the behavior of individuals, groups, and populations in the service of market objectives." This is the keystone of the concept: the [[The Means of Behavioral Modification|means of behavioral modification]] replace the machines of industrial production, and the project of [[Instrumentarian Power|instrumentarian power]] "overcomes and replaces the human inwardness that feeds the will to will and gives sustenance to our voices in the first person, incapacitating democracy at its roots" (Directly stated). The seventh extinction is, precisely, the dying-off of that inwardness.

What goes extinct is therefore not biological but ontological — the constituents of selfhood, intimacy, promise, and trust that liberal humanism took as given. Zuboff names them in a list that reads as an inventory of what is at stake: "the will to will, the sanctity of the individual, the ties of intimacy, the sociality that binds us together in promises, and the trust they breed. The dying off of this human future will be just as unintended as any other" (Directly stated). The clause "just as unintended" preserves the analogy's force: like the sixth extinction, the seventh is a by-product of an accountability-free market mechanism, not a malevolent plan — which is exactly what makes it, in Zuboff's framing, structural rather than incidental, and therefore preventable only by re-imposing democratic accountability.

Zuboff insists this extinction is not yet accomplished, and the metaphor is mobilized as a warning rather than an obituary. In the conquest of nature, she observes, "industrial capitalism's victims were mute. Those who would try to conquer human nature will find their intended victims full of voice, ready to name danger and defeat it." The asymmetry between the silent victims of the sixth extinction and the potentially vocal victims of the seventh is the book's closing hope: what is at stake — "the human expectation of sovereignty over one's own life and authorship of one's own experience," "the inward experience from which we form the will to will" — can still be defended. "Let there be a digital future, but let it be a human future first" (Directly stated).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins the figure in the final chapter ("A Coup from Above") as the culminating image of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*. The seventh extinction is the endpoint of [[Instrumentarian Power]] operating within an [[Information Civilization]]: an unintended, market-driven die-off of human nature paralleling industrialization's unintended die-off of natural species. The extinction metaphor is inseparable from the human-nature-versus-nature argument that frames the whole book — the shift from "machines that overcome the limits of bodies" to "machines that modify the behavior" of people.

## Related Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the "signature expression of surveillance capitalism" whose rise "augurs" the seventh extinction; the seventh extinction names what instrumentarian power, scaled to society, ultimately consumes.
- [[Information Civilization]] — the "new arc" that "repeats the same dangerous arrogance" of industrialization; the civilizational stage within which the seventh extinction unfolds.
- [[The Will to Will]] — the first item in Zuboff's inventory of the threatened; the inward capacity to author one's own purposes that instrumentarian power "overcomes and replaces."
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the parent economic order; the seventh extinction is its terminal consequence if left unaccountable, as the sixth was industrial capitalism's.
- [[Big Other]] — the material apparatus through which behavioral modification is enacted at scale, and so the operative instrument of the threatened extinction.
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — the "machines that modify the behavior of individuals, groups, and populations"; the productive means whose aim is now human nature rather than nature.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, free-willing, self-authoring individual whose constituent faculties (the will to will, the sanctity of the individual, the ties of intimacy) are precisely what the seventh extinction would erase; the concept names the loss of the liberal subject as an event on the scale of a mass extinction.
- Conquest of Nature (industrial capitalism) — the original target Zuboff sets against the new one; the seventh extinction is defined by the *shift* of object from nature to human nature, so the industrial-era project is both the precedent and the contrast.

## Questions It Raises

- Is "extinction" a literal claim about an irreversible loss of human capacities, or a rhetorical figure meant to confer on instrumentarian power the gravity we now grant to ecological collapse?
- If the seventh extinction is "just as unintended" as the sixth, does locating the cause in unaccountable market forms (rather than malice) strengthen or weaken the case for democratic remedy?
- Zuboff stakes her hope on the asymmetry that the seventh extinction's victims, unlike the sixth's, are "full of voice" — but instrumentarian power's specialty is the quiet modification of behavior. Can a voice survive the very process that would extinguish the will to will?
- What is the relation between Zuboff's "extinction of human nature" and the posthuman/transhuman dissolutions of the liberal subject argued elsewhere in the vault (Hayles, Harari, Kurzweil) — catastrophe to be resisted, or continuation by other means?
