# The War of the Icons

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's thesis that the cold war is not a war of hardware but "an electric battle of information and of images" in which rivals are defeated by "the eroding of the collective countenance of one's rivals" — the sputnik, the lady cosmonaut, the corporate image — rather than by killing soldiers one by one.

## Longer Explanation

For McLuhan the cold war is misnamed when imagined as a stalled or merely deferred hot war. It is a different kind of war altogether: "It is really an electric battle of information and of images that goes far deeper and is more obsessional than the old hot wars of industrial hardware." The old "hot" wars used weapons "that knocked off the enemy, one by one," and even ideological warfare proceeded "by persuading individuals to adopt new points of view, one at a time." Electric warfare abandons this fragmentary, single-target logic. "Electric persuasion by photo and movie and TV works, instead, by dunking entire populations in new imagery." The munition is the image, not the missile; the casualty is the rival's collective self-image, not the rival's body. `Directly stated`

The historic instances McLuhan reads as engagements in this war are media events, not battles: Valentina Tereshkova's untrained orbit "as a kind of defacing of the images of the male astronauts"; the first sputnik as "a witty taunting of the capitalist world by means of a new kind of technological image or icon"; Madison Avenue's shift "from the promotion of the individual product to the collective involvement in the 'corporate image.'" The Russians, he argues, won early rounds simply by adapting "their traditions of Eastern icon and image-building to the new electric media." The decisive formula compresses the thesis: "In fact, the war of the icons, or the eroding of the collective countenance of one's rivals, has long been under way. Ink and photo are supplanting soldiery and tanks. The pen daily becomes mightier than the sword." `Directly stated`

The corollary, captured inside this concept rather than as a separate note, is that in the electric age *all* technology can be regarded as a weapon, because all technology processes information. "Since our new electric technology is not an extension of our bodies but of our central nervous systems, we now see all technology, including language, as a means of processing experience, a means of storing and speeding information. And in such a situation all technology can plausibly be regarded as weapons." This collapses the distinction between weapon and medium: a language, a photograph, a satellite, and a rifle are alike means of processing and speeding information, and so alike capable of aggression. Past wars are reread as "the processing of difficult and resistant materials by the latest technology," and war as such "as a process of achieving equilibrium among unequal technologies." `Directly stated`

The war of the icons therefore sits at the hinge of the [[Mechanical vs Electric]] transition: where mechanical weaponry fragmented and "knocked off the enemy, one by one," the extension of the central nervous system makes the image-weapon total and instantaneous, and paradoxically makes the human family visible to itself. "With the extension of the central nervous system by electric technology, even weaponry makes more vivid the fact of the unity of the human family." `Strongly implied` — the unity claim is directly stated; reading it as the redemptive obverse of the war of the icons is the synthesis. At the limit weaponry becomes "a self-liquidating fact," since electric techniques "cannot be used aggressively except to end all life at once, like the turning off of a light." `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — coins the phrase in *Understanding Media* (ch. 32, "Weapons: War of the Icons"). The war of the icons is the electric replacement of soldiery and tanks by ink, photo, movie, and TV; its corollary is that, because the electric age extends the central nervous system and processes information, all technology — including language — can be regarded as a weapon. The image is the munition; the target is the rival's "collective countenance."

## Related Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the image-weapon and "language as weapon" are special cases: a medium is an extension of man, and in the electric age an extension that can be wielded aggressively.
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the explicit ground of the corollary: because electric technology extends the central nervous system rather than the body, all technology is reread as information processing and so as potential weaponry.
- [[The Global Village]] — the same instant electric information that arms the war of the icons dissolves the city ("the globe itself can never again be more than a village") and makes "the unity of the human family" vivid.
- [[Information]] — "the very inclusiveness of information as a weapon"; the cold war is fought by "informational technology," information being the medium and the munition at once.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Mechanical vs Electric]] — the war of the icons names the electric pole against the mechanical: image-saturation of whole populations versus weapons that "knocked off the enemy, one by one"; total and instantaneous versus fragmentary and sequential.

## Questions It Raises

- If all technology, including language, is a weapon in the electric age, does the distinction between medium and munition — or between communication and aggression — survive at all?
- When the casualty is a rival's "collective countenance" rather than its soldiers, who or what counts as defeated, and can such a defeat be measured or ended?
- Does the same electric inclusiveness that makes information a weapon also make "the unity of the human family" inescapable — and is that unity a peace or only a shared theatre of image-war?
- Is McLuhan describing a genuine demilitarization (war becoming "a self-liquidating fact") or a totalization of war into every medium we inhabit?
