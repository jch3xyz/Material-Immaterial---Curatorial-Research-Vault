# The Participator

## Brief Definition

For Roy Ascott, **the participator** is the active, meaning-generating subject who completes the artwork by interacting with it — the figure that must replace the passive "spectator" or "observer" once art is understood as a behavioural system rather than a fixed object.

## Longer Explanation

Across forty years of Ascott's writing the same move recurs: art is not a message dispatched from an artist to a receiver, but a system, field, or matrix whose meaning is produced only through the intervention of those who engage it. The subject of that engagement Ascott names not the spectator or observer but the **participator**. The term is programmatic. In his 1980 "Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art" he states it flatly: "Thus the viewer/observer must be a participator and is of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system. A field theory of art must pay much attention to the participator" (`Directly stated`). The participator is not completing a closed message but "interrogating and interacting with the system 'artwork' to generate meaning" — an active process opposed to the "passive process" of decoding a fixed transmission. (See [[A Field Theory of Art]], [[Behaviourist Art]].)

The chronological seed is his earliest essay in the volume, "The Construction of Change" (1964). There the participator emerges from Ascott's behaviourist art: the parts of the work "are not fixed and may be changed by the intervention of the spectator," so that "the participant becomes responsible for the extension of the artwork's meaning. He becomes a decision maker in the symbolic world that confronts him" (`Directly stated`). The artist sets behavioural limits within which the participant behaves; authorship is shared but bounded. By the 1975 "Table" essay this hardens into an ontology of the open system: "The table-top supports an open system, in which only the intervention of observers can generate meaning and value. Art, then, resides not on a surface or within material objects alone, but between the behaviours of all parts of the system" (`Directly stated`). The participator is thus the human pole of [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the two-way behavioural loop that, in the [[Object vs System|object→system]] turn, supplants one-way stimulation.

Ascott grounds the figure philosophically in the **observer→participator turn** of twentieth-century science. He borrows directly from John Wheeler's "participatory universe": "To describe what has happened one has to cross out that old word 'observer' and put in its place 'participator.' In some strange sense the universe is a participatory universe" (Wheeler, quoted by Ascott; `Directly stated`). He aligns this with second-order cybernetics' shift "from the analysis of observed systems to the (second-order) cybernetics of observing systems" and with the quantum measurement insight that the observer's consciousness "are a part of what he is observing." The art participator and the quantum/cybernetic observer, for Ascott, are the same structural figure: in both, the observer who once stood safely outside the system is folded into it as a constitutive part. (Cross-link [[The Measurement Problem]], [[The Observer]], [[Reflexivity]].)

In the telematic phase the participator becomes the networked **"user."** In "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" (1990) Ascott declares "the observer in an interactive telematic system is by definition a participator," and meaning "is the product of interaction between the observer and the system." In "Beyond Time-Based Art" the same year he renames the figure precisely: "To insert oneself into the flow of data as a user of an art-directed telematic system ('user,' i.e., neither autonomous creator nor passive viewer) is never to interact with the same set of data (images, text, sound) twice" (`Directly stated`). The participator, dispersed across the network, helps activate what Ascott calls a [[Network Consciousness|global consciousness]] — and, in the dissolution of the autonomous author into [[Distributed Authorship|dispersed authorship]], ceases to be the bounded liberal subject confronting an object at all.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** (*Telematic Embrace*) — the participator is Ascott's preferred name for the active networked subject who completes the work. Across the corpus it appears as: the **decision-making spectator** of the 1960s Change Paintings and behaviourist art (ch. 05); the **observer-as-participator** "of operational importance in the total behaviour of the system" in the field theory (ch. 13); the intervening **observer** whose engagement alone generates meaning in the open-system "Table" (ch. 10); and the **"user"** — "neither autonomous creator nor passive viewer" — of the telematic network (chs. 19, 20). The figure is explicitly modelled on Wheeler's "participatory universe" and on second-order cybernetics' observing systems (chs. 13, 20). Ch. 04 is Edward A. Shanken's editorial introduction, which periodizes this observer→participator turn across Ascott's career and supplies the Wheeler-and-Zurek source quotation.

## Related Concepts

- [[Behaviourist Art]] — the participator is the human pole of behaviourist art; the artwork is "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer)," and the participator supplies the second.
- [[A Field Theory of Art]] — the field theory is built around the participator: "A field theory of art must pay much attention to the participator."
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the participator completes the two-way feedback loop that distinguishes reciprocal communication from one-way stimulation.
- [[Telematic Art]] — in telematic art the participator becomes the networked "user" who transforms the data flow.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the dispersed participators of a network together activate a collective/global consciousness.
- [[Distributed Authorship]] — as participation deepens, the line between participator and author dissolves.
- [[The Observer]] (Hayles) — the reflexive observer of second-order cybernetics, folded into the system being observed; Ascott's participator is the art-theoretic kin of this figure (cross-linked, kept distinct).
- [[Reflexivity]] — the observer-included-in-the-observed loop that the participator instantiates in aesthetics.
- [[The Measurement Problem]] (O'Gieblyn) — the quantum observer who "creates the reality of the act by observation"; Ascott's stated model for the participator.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] · [[New Media Art]] — the participator makes the viewer's experience/behaviour constitutive of the work, the lineage these Burnham-realized notes were committed to extend.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-possessed individual standing outside and prior to the work; the participator dissolves this subject into the system (the "user… neither autonomous creator nor passive viewer," authorship "dispersed").
- The **passive spectator / observer** (the "old word" Ascott crosses out) — the receiver who decodes a fixed message transmitted by the artist; the participator is defined by negation against it.

## Questions It Raises

- If the participator only ever behaves "within limits the artist sets," how much authorship is genuinely transferred — is participation real co-authorship or a designed effect of the artist's system? (The control-vs-openness tension Ascott names but does not fully resolve.)
- Does importing the quantum "observer creates reality" into aesthetics establish a real structural identity between the art participator and the physical observer, or only a suggestive analogy?
- When the participator becomes the dispersed networked "user," does anything of the embodied, situated subject survive — or is this the same erasure of embodiment that [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal humanism]] and the posthuman both perform? (Cross-link [[The Cyborg]], [[Virtuality]].)
- Ascott reads participation as love and emancipation; the same networked participation can be read as voluntary self-exposure and capture. Is the participator a liberated subject or a captured one?
