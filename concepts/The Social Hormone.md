# The Social Hormone

## Brief Definition

Marshall McLuhan's title figure for the Telegraph chapter: under electric conditions, instant information functions as the chemical messenger of the social body — coordinating the distant "organs" (institutions) of society into a single living organism, so that "political and commercial institutions take on a biological character by means of electric communications."

## Longer Explanation

The chapter's title, "Telegraph: The Social Hormone," compresses McLuhan's claim that electric media do not merely speed up an existing mechanical society but biologize it. Where the literate, mechanical world is built on "the separation of functions, and the division of stages, spaces, and tasks," the telegraph's instant information dissolves those divisions: "[t]he tendency of electric media is to create a kind of organic interdependence among all the institutions of society." McLuhan reads this as confirmation of "de Chardin's view that the discovery of electromagnetism is to be regarded as 'a prodigious biological event'" (Directly stated). The phrase "organic interdependence among all the institutions of society" is the concept's core: institutions become organs of one body rather than separate, delegated, hierarchical units.

The "hormone" half of the figure is a deliberate splice. Having said that institutions "take on a biological character" through electric communication, McLuhan immediately inverts the analogy: "it is also common now for biologists like Hans Selye to think of the physical organism as a communication network." He then quotes Selye's definition verbatim — "Hormone is a specific chemical messenger-substance, made by an endocrine gland and secreted into the blood, to regulate and coordinate the functions of distant organs" (Directly stated). By placing the social claim and the physiological definition side by side, McLuhan makes instant electric information *be* the hormone of the social organism: the messenger-substance secreted into the body politic to "regulate and coordinate the functions of distant organs." The two-way traffic of the analogy — society becoming organism, organism understood as communication network — is the substance of the splice.

This biological reframing is grounded in the companion claim of the same chapter, the externalization of the nervous system (see [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]]): "electricity may be said to have outered the central nervous system itself, including the brain," and "[o]ur central nervous system is a unified field quite without segments." Because the social body now shares the unsegmented, simultaneous form of the private nervous system, "the world of public interaction has the same inclusive scope of integral interplay that has hitherto characterized only our private nervous systems." The social hormone is the messenger of that newly unified body: "electric media instantly and constantly create a total field of interacting events in which all men participate" (Directly stated). McLuhan explicitly aligns this with cybernetic thinking — "cybernation … looks at the production problem as an integrated system of information handling" — making the figure a bridge to [[Cybernetics]].

The consequence McLuhan stresses is not centralization but its opposite. The organic interdependence of the social hormone runs through decentralization, not a single command center: the telegraph "freed the marginal provincial press from dependence on the big metropolitan press," and "[i]n the whole field of the electric revolution, this pattern of [[Decentralization|decentralization]] appears in multiple guises." The organism is coordinated by a circulating messenger rather than governed from a head — "[t]he organic everywhere supplants the mechanical" — which is why "[a] collapse of delegated authority and a dissolution of the pyramid and management structures" follows the onset of instant information (Strongly implied that the "social hormone" names this messenger-coordinated, head-less organicity).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** (*Understanding Media*, Ch. 25) — the originating and only use. The social hormone is McLuhan's name for instant electric information as the coordinating messenger of a society that has become a single organism. He builds it by splicing Teilhard de Chardin's "prodigious biological event" (electromagnetism as a turning point in life's evolution) with Hans Selye's clinical definition of a hormone, so that the social and the physiological analogies feed into each other. Held, as elsewhere in the book, ambivalently: the same organic interdependence that gives "powerful voices to the weak and suffering" also lets orders pass "direct … to the lowest levels, where, on account of the absolute authority behind them, they were carried out uncritically" (Albert Speer, quoted) and ushers in "the Age of Anxiety and of Pervasive Dread."

## Related Concepts

- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the sibling figure from the same chapter; the social hormone is the *messenger* circulating through the social body that the externalized, unsegmented nervous system has made into one organism.
- [[Cybernetics]] — McLuhan glosses the organic-electric as "cybernation … an integrated system of information handling," and Selye's "organism as a communication network" is the cybernetic move applied to physiology; interpretive resonance with the vault's biology-as-information thread.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the obverse of the splice: if society becomes a body coordinated by information, the body is read as a communication network coordinated by chemical messages (interpretive resonance, not citation).
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — Harari's later reduction of life to information-processing is prefigured by Selye's "organism as a communication network" as McLuhan deploys it (interpretive synthesis; McLuhan and Harari do not cite each other).
- [[Decentralization]] — the social organism is coordinated by a circulating messenger, not a command center; McLuhan insists the electric pattern is decentralizing, dissolving the metropolitan/provincial center-margin monopoly.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]] — the social-hormone figure is anti-disembodiment: information is the *secretion of a body*, a messenger-substance "secreted into the blood," not a pattern liberated from matter; the analogy works precisely because both society and organism remain corporeal (interpretive synthesis).

## Questions It Raises

- McLuhan splices a theological-evolutionary claim (Teilhard's "prodigious biological event") with a clinical definition (Selye's hormone). Is "the social hormone" an argument, or a metaphor doing the work of one — and what does the splice license that neither source alone would?
- If institutions are "distant organs" coordinated by an information-messenger, who or what is the endocrine gland? Does the figure name a coordinated body without a head (decentralization), or quietly smuggle one back in?
- The same organic interdependence yields both "powerful voices to the weak and suffering" and uncritical obedience to "absolute authority" (Speer). Is the social hormone neutral as to domination or emancipation, or does its headless, all-at-once form tilt one way?
- Does reading "the physical organism as a communication network" (Selye via McLuhan, 1964) belong to the same lineage that later treats [[Organisms Are Algorithms|organisms as algorithms]] — or is McLuhan's organicism resistant to the informational reduction it seems to anticipate?
