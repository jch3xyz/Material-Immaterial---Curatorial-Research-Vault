# Freedom Is Ignorance

## Brief Definition

The behaviorist axiom — inherited from Max Planck through Max Meyer to B. F. Skinner — that freedom and will are illusions filling the void of our ignorance about the determinants of behavior, so that knowledge and freedom are necessarily adversaries; Zuboff appropriates the formula to expose its decisive twist under [[Surveillance Capitalism]], where the displacing knowledge is *proprietary* and the lost freedom is therefore ours alone.

## Longer Explanation

For the radical behaviorists, what we call freedom is simply the not-yet-explained — the anomaly, the "accident" — a lacuna of ignorance that advancing science is destined to fill. Zuboff traces the lineage precisely: the physicist Max Planck insisted on "the unity of the physical world and the discoverability of the natural laws… including the laws of human behavior"; his student Max Meyer carried this into psychology, positing in his 1921 *Psychology of the Other-One* that "freedom of action in the animal world signifies the same that is meant by accidents in the world of physics"; and Skinner made it the load-bearing claim of his radical behaviorism. On this view, as Zuboff renders Skinner, "knowledge does not make us free but rather releases us from the illusion of freedom. In reality… freedom and ignorance are synonyms" `Directly stated`. The acquisition of knowledge is at once heroic, because it rescues us from ignorance, and tragic, because it reveals the impossibility of freedom — "the behavior we admire is therefore the behavior we cannot yet explain."

The mechanism is determinist: "The environment determines behavior, and our ignorance of precisely how it does so is the void that we fill with the fantasy of freedom." Will, autonomy, purpose, and agency become defense mechanisms — "an undigested bit of fear, a crumb of denial" — protecting us from the discomfort of human ignorance. Because the liberal idea of freedom "persists in an inverse relationship to the growth of scientific knowledge," the proposition reduces to a single adversarial law: "Knowledge and freedom are necessarily adversaries" `Directly stated`. Skinner's twin utopias of technique (Walden Two against Orwell's 1984) imagine a "superorganism" in which all the ideals of a liberal society — freedom, autonomy, privacy, self-rule — "must be forfeit" as the price of an anomaly-free harmony.

Zuboff's decisive twist is that surveillance capitalism inherits the *logic* of Planck, Meyer, and Skinner — "the forfeit of freedom for knowledge" — but on terms those scientists never anticipated. Earlier instrumentation (she cites MacKay's telemetry) "traded animals' freedom for the sake of scientific knowledge intended to benefit the animals themselves." Surveillance capitalism severs that reciprocity: "The knowledge that now displaces our freedom is proprietary. The knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us" `Directly stated`. The behaviorist's symmetrical mystery — freedom and ignorance as "two sides of the same coin" — is broken open. Under this regime, Zuboff argues in Ch.18, "freedom and ignorance are no longer twin born… Surveillance capitalism is instead defined by an unprecedented convergence of freedom and knowledge," with the convergence corresponding exactly to the scope of [[Instrumentarian Power]]. The capitalists "claim the freedom to order knowledge, and then they leverage that knowledge advantage in order to protect and expand their freedom" — hijacking the [[The Division of Learning in Society|division of learning]]. Her normative inversion follows: "surveillance capitalists know too much to qualify for freedom" `Directly stated`.

Chapter 14 reapplies the axiom to "confluence" — the war on anomaly made operational. In Nadella's instrumented factory, "machine knowledge instantaneously replaces ignorance, herding all machine behavior to preestablished norms"; "the 'freedom' of each individual machine is surrendered to the knowledge that they share." Zuboff names the continuity directly: "Just as the behavioral theorists Planck, Meyer, and Skinner predicted, this sacrifice amounts to an all-out war on accidents, mistakes, and randomness in general" `Directly stated`. The anomaly — the behaviorist's "accident," the residue of freedom — becomes a thing to be preemptively eliminated, in machines first and then, by the same template, in humans: "In this human hive, individual freedom is forfeit to collective knowledge and action." The axiom that began as an epistemology of behavior ends as an economic mandate for certainty under [[Big Other]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — does not endorse the axiom but excavates it as the intellectual "origin story" of instrumentarian power and surveillance capitalism's [[A Utopia of Certainty|utopia of certainty]]. Her contribution is the twist: the displacing knowledge is proprietary, so the lost freedom is "solely" ours, and the convergence of freedom and knowledge in a private few becomes itself the ground for disqualifying them from freedom.
- **B. F. Skinner (via Zuboff)** — the axiom's most relentless champion: "freedom is ignorance"; "freedom and ignorance are synonyms"; freedom is merely "an accident," a lacuna queuing up for explanation by a "technology of behavior." Skinner is the vault's `Radical Behaviorism` source (forward-stub author; no author note until his work is ingested).
- **Max Meyer and Max Planck (via Zuboff)** — the lineage Skinner himself credits: Planck's physics of absolute natural law extended to behavior, Meyer's reduction of freedom to physical "accident" in the *viewpoint of the Other-One*. ## Related Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the species of power whose scope equals the degree to which freedom and knowledge converge; freedom-is-ignorance is its inherited epistemology.
- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — the anomaly-free, confluence-driven society that operationalizes the axiom; the war on accidents.
- [[The Division of Learning in Society]] — the asymmetry the axiom now serves; the few accumulate the knowledge for which the many forfeit freedom.
- [[Big Other]] — the ubiquitous apparatus that detects and preempts the "anomaly" (the residue of freedom) in real time.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] and [[The Prediction Imperative]] — the economic engine that makes the behaviorist forfeit *profitable*, converting eliminated anomalies into prediction.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the wider determinist worldview (O'Gieblyn, Harari) of which the behaviorist axiom is a foundational instance.
- [[Dataism]] and [[Big Data]] — kindred faiths that knowledge of behavior supersedes the liberal subject's claim to self-authorship.
- [[Surveillance]] and [[Quantified Self]] — the practices through which proprietary behavioral knowledge is amassed at the expense of freedom.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the axiom is determinism's strongest behaviorist statement; the tension between it and liberal freedom is its native habitat. Zuboff sides against the axiom's *political* use while conceding its analytic force.
- [[Free Will as Fiction (Harari)]] — Harari arrives at a parallel conclusion from biology/algorithms; Zuboff differs by foregrounding *whose* knowledge displaces *whose* freedom, making the question political and proprietary rather than merely scientific.

## Questions It Raises

- If freedom really is only the not-yet-explained, does the *ownership* of the explaining knowledge change the moral status of the forfeit, as Zuboff insists — or only its distribution?
- Can a society wage an "all-out war on accidents, mistakes, and randomness" without also abolishing the conditions of novelty, contestation, and the right to the future tense?
- Does Zuboff's inversion ("surveillance capitalists know too much to qualify for freedom") establish a defensible principle, or does it merely re-weaponize the same adversarial premise against its holders?
- Is the convergence of freedom and knowledge in a private few a contingent feature of surveillance capitalism, or the logical endpoint of any technology of behavior?
