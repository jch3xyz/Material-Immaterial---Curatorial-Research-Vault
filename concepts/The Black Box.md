# The Black Box

## Brief Definition

The black box is O'Gieblyn's name for any system — a deep-learning model, the Calvinist God, or the human heart — whose outputs we receive but whose internal logic remains constitutively inaccessible, delivering, in the phrase she borrows from Chris Anderson, "objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension."

## Longer Explanation

In its narrow engineering sense, the black box is the opacity of machine learning. "Many forms of machine learning are considered 'black box' technologies," O'Gieblyn writes. "They are composed of many hidden layers of neural networks, and there's no way to ascertain what kind of model they are building from their experience." During training these systems "develop internal nodes that represent abstract features or relationships they discover, none of which correspond to any terms in human language" — even an algorithm "preternaturally good at recognizing, say, dogs in a photo" has "no idea what a dog actually is." To "print out everything the networks do between input and output" would yield "billions of arithmetic operations — an 'explanation' that would be impossible to understand." The opacity is not a temporary defect awaiting better tools: the technology "appears to be no more reducible to explanation than the human brain." With deep learning the [[Mind as Machine|computer metaphor of mind]] reaches its terminus — these algorithms have "fulfilled Anderson's call for objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension," so that "to obtain the superior knowledge the machines possess, we must give up our desire to know 'why' and accept their outputs as pure revelation."

O'Gieblyn's distinctive move is to run the figure across three registers at once. The **engineering** black box (the opaque [[Algorithm|algorithm]]) shades into a **theological** one: the boundless, unaccountable deity who issues verdicts "beyond dispute or appeal." This is why "God is the machine," in the words of the researcher Jure Leskovec — "The black box is the truth." The God in question is not the comprehensible cosmos of the Scholastics but "the God of Calvin and Luther — a thoroughly modern entity," the nominalist sovereign whose will is "remotely hidden from human understanding" and before whom the modern subject stands "much like Job's," "forced to accept the decisions of our algorithms blindly, like Job accepting his punishment." The third register is **psychological**: predestination eradicates "not only free will but any coherent sense of self," so that "the heart is also a black box, full of hidden desires and shadowy motivations whose true causes remain hidden from the conscious mind." Selfhood, divinity, and machine intelligence become structurally identical — three sealed boxes that issue outputs without reasons.

The crucial polemical turn comes in Chapter 12, where O'Gieblyn refuses the technologist's alibi that opacity is intrinsic. Defenders insist that "human judgment is no more transparent" — "the human brain is also a black box," says the criminologist Richard Berk — but O'Gieblyn argues that this symmetry is "specious," because "we evolved as a species to have similarities in our method of reasoning," whereas machines "reach conclusions in a way that is totally unlike human reason" ("That's not a human move"). More damning still, "the debate about technical explanations and their supposed impossibility is a sleight of hand meant to distract from the real obstacles to transparency, which are legal and economic." The COMPAS system used to sentence Eric Loomis "was not in fact a black-box model; it was developed by a private company and was protected by proprietary law." Between "machines that are esoteric by nature and those that are obscured to protect the powerful" the boundary grows "increasingly shadowy," producing her sharpest formulation: "Not only are we not permitted to know the information these systems have about us; we are not permitted to know why we're not permitted to know."

Against the black box stands the demand for its opening. The EU's 2016 General Data Protection Regulation enshrined a "right to an explanation," and the tech industry now pays "lip service to 'transparency' and 'explainability.'" But O'Gieblyn is sceptical that such explanations are real: post-hoc accounts are "at best intelligent guesses," sometimes generated by "an entirely different system trained to generate responses that are meant to account convincingly, in semantic terms, for decisions the original machine made" — so that the explanation "merely contributing another layer of opacity," and "even the existence of a lack of explanation is concealed." The black box thus names not only an epistemic condition but a structure of power: faceless, neutral-seeming, and for that reason "more invulnerable to the charge of injustice."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — the black box is the keystone figure of her "Algorithm" chapters and the literalized terminus of [[Mind as Machine|the mind-as-machine metaphor]]. She works it in three registers (engineering / theological / psychological) and insists the obstacle to opening it is "legal and economic," not technical.
- **Engineering variant** — the deep-learning model whose hidden layers build internal representations that "correspond to [no] terms in human language"; the locus of the field's slogan "God is the machine. The black box is the truth."
- **Theological variant** — Calvin's God, "incomprehensible" and "remotely hidden from human understanding," whose decrees Job must accept "without asking a reason"; the nominalist deity O'Gieblyn argues the [[Algorithm|algorithm]] reincarnates.
- **Psychological variant** — the heart as black box: under predestination "your mind is guided by God's prompting rather than by your own freedom to choose," dissolving [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the autonomous self]] into a determined mechanism whose "true causes remain hidden from the conscious mind."

## Related Concepts

- [[Algorithm]] — the black box is the algorithm's defining property; the two notes are paired (the algorithm is the thing, opacity is its mode).
- [[Mind as Machine]] — once human consciousness is "black boxed," "it now appears just as reasonable to trust the opaque logic of an algorithm as it is to trust our own minds"; the metaphor runs in reverse.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the black box is the disenchanted world's final irony: instruments built to "restore our broken relationship with the absolute" deliver knowledge stripped of comprehension, returning us to a condition of revelation without understanding.
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — the model "knows" only correlations and patterns, having been built (after Shannon) to "exclude the need for a conscious subject"; "correlation is enough."
- [[The Body as Data]] — predictive systems treat the person "purely in terms of pattern and probabilities, without any concern for interiority," reading the subject as a data set rather than a self.
- [[Information]] — the black box is the endpoint of information theory's deliberate elision of meaning; "'understanding,' like meaning, is an anthropocentric concept — one that information technologies were deliberately built to elide."
- [[Transparency]] — Han's transparency is the conceptual mirror of O'Gieblyn's black box: where Han diagnoses a society compelled to total illumination, O'Gieblyn diagnoses systems whose opacity is engineered and legally protected. Linked as a deliberate parallel; **do not merge** — they belong to different authors and analyses.
- [[Arcana of Power]] — Han's *arcanum* (the constitutive secrecy that power requires) is the political counterpart to O'Gieblyn's "obscured to protect the powerful"; the corporate black box is a privatized arcanum. Cross-linked as parallel, not identity.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the black box dissolves the autonomous, rational, self-transparent subject of liberal humanism; both the algorithm and the predestined self are mechanisms whose reasons are inaccessible even to themselves.
- [[Embodiment]] — predictive systems work by abstracting the person into measurable external states because, as one MIT study notes, it is impossible to determine "the internal states of the human"; the black box presumes the lived, embodied interior is irrelevant.
- The "right to an explanation" — the legal-ethical demand (GDPR, 2016) that opposes the black box by asserting "citizens have a right to know the reason behind automated decisions that involve them," though O'Gieblyn doubts the explanations on offer are genuine.

## Questions It Raises

- If a system can deliver "objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension," does forfeiting the question "why" amount to forfeiting *meaning* itself — the "implicitly human category that cannot be reduced to quantification" — and can a posthuman epistemology survive that loss? (ties to *meaning*, *posthumanism*)
- When the same word — "black box" — names a neural network, the Calvinist God, and the human heart, is the analogy revealing a real structural identity, or is it [[Mind as Machine|the mind-as-machine metaphor]] sliding into literalism in reverse, so that we mistake ourselves for the machines we built? (ties to *technology and perception*, *the body as data*)
- If predictive systems read us "purely in terms of pattern and probabilities, without any concern for interiority," and "the internal states of the human" cannot be measured, does the black box quietly enact the disappearance of embodied selfhood that [[Posthumanism|posthumanism]] describes? (ties to *posthumanism*, *the body as data*)
- If the real obstacles to opening the box are "legal and economic" rather than technical, is the rhetoric of "explainability" a genuine remedy or a further layer of opacity that conceals "the existence of a lack of explanation"? (ties to *technology and perception*, *meaning*)
