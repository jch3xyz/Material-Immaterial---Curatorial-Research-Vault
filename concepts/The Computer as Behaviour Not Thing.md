# The Computer as Behaviour Not Thing

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's signature redefinition of the digital computer away from object, apparatus, and machine and toward "a set of behaviours, a system, actually, a system of systems" — "the agent of the data field" — with the corollary that the computer's value to art depends on making its visible material presence disappear.

## Longer Explanation

Ascott restates the proposition twice in *[[Telematic Embrace]]*, almost word for word, and in both places it does the same work: it pries the computer loose from the category of *thing*. In "Gesamtdatenwerk" (ch.18) he writes, "The computer is not primarily a thing, but a set of behaviours. Its purpose is not only computation but transformation; not only storage but dissemination. It is the agent of the data field, a construct of data space." In "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" (ch.20) the formula is sharpened — "The computer is not primarily a thing, an object, but a set of behaviours, a system, actually, a system of systems. Data constitute its lingua franca." The computer's reality, on this account, is not the box, the screen, or the keyboard but the field of transformations and exchanges it conducts; its native medium is data, its mode is dissemination and flux rather than storage. `Directly stated`

The redefinition carries an explicit polemic against the computer-as-thing. The machine "as object, as apparatus, as machine—is too much with us, too dominant," "a computational block to poetry and imagination," and so long as it is "seen simply as a screen presenting the pages of an illuminated book, or as an internally lit painting, it is artistically valueless." Ascott's diagnosis is that the computer "is not transparent, it is not understood as pure system, universal transformative matrix" — the object-reading actively obscures the system-reading. The corollary is a demand for disappearance: "we must recognise the necessity of making the currently very visible computer invisible." In ch.20 the same demand returns through an organic figure — "It is the computer that is at the heart of this circulation system, and, like the heart, it works best when it becomes invisible." The computer is to be effaced not because it is unimportant but because, like the heart, it works best when it is no longer noticed as a discrete part. `Directly stated`

This is why, for Ascott, the [[The Interface as Doorway|interface]] matters more than the apparatus. If the computer is a set of behaviours rather than a thing, then the consequential design problem is the boundary at which a human enters that field. He insists "the power of the interface cannot be overestimated," rejects the reading of "the computer interface as a membrane separating out the computer as a discrete object from ourselves," and asks instead that we "design it as a doorway into data space, a synaptic interval in a human-computer symbiosis." Where the interface is "constrained by a typewriter keyboard or the gestural configurations of a 'mouse,'" the user "is forced into the posture of a clerk," and the result is an "art of the 'bottom line'... an art of finalities, of completion" — the opposite of the "open-endedness, nonlinearity, and fecund incompleteness" Ascott wants. Making the computer invisible is thus the same gesture as dissolving the user into the system. `Directly stated`

The concept is the Ascott-side counterpart to [[Jack Burnham|Burnham's]] [[Object and System|object→system]] thesis, but it is **not identical** to it and is deliberately kept distinct. Burnham (1968) tracks the demotion of the sculpted *object* into a life-simulating *system* across all of modern sculpture; Ascott applies the same system-reading specifically to the *digital computer* and to the *interface*, two decades later, inside an explicitly [[Telematic Art|telematic]] and networked art. Where Burnham concludes that "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor," Ascott pushes the same inversion onto the machine itself: the computer's physical, material presence is precisely what must be made to vanish so that its behaviour — its "lingua franca" of data — can be apprehended. Both moves are instances of the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension; Ascott carries the system out of the gallery and into the data field and the network. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins and twice restates the formula in *Telematic Embrace* (ch.18 "Gesamtdatenwerk," 1989; ch.20 "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?," 1990). The computer is "not primarily a thing... but a set of behaviours," "a system of systems," "the agent of the data field"; data are its "lingua franca"; and its visible material presence must be made invisible so that "pure system, universal transformative matrix" can be grasped. The doctrine is inseparable from his elevation of the interface as "a doorway into data space" and from his telematic program of [[Distributed Authorship|dispersed authorship]] and [[Network Consciousness|network consciousness]].
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — the immediate precursor (variant, not identity): [[Object and System|object → system]] in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) makes the same passage from inert thing to life-bearing system for sculpture at large. Ascott specializes the move to the computer and the interface; the two are cross-linked, not merged. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Related Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — Burnham's structural axis (object → system) of which this is the digital-computer-specific counterpart; the source of the "physical presence" vs "information" inversion Ascott applies to the machine.
- [[Telematic Art]] — the art for which the computer-as-system is the substrate: meaning "is the product of interaction between the observer and the system," its content "immaterial... pure electronic différance, until it has been reconstituted at the interface."
- [[The Interface as Doorway]] — the design corollary of the concept: if the computer is a behaviour not a thing, the interface is "a doorway into data space, a synaptic interval in a human-computer symbiosis," not a "membrane separating out the computer as a discrete object from ourselves."
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the larger system the computer is "at the heart of"; the "circulation system" within which the invisible computer functions like a heart.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's program for prioritizing the system over the object; the artistic-theoretical horizon Ascott's telematic art extends from object and gallery into data field and network.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — Hayles's insistence that information is always instantiated in a material substrate; a productive counter-pressure on Ascott's call to render the computer's materiality invisible.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the canonical vault tension this concept instantiates and intensifies: the computer's "physical, material presence" (object) set against its behaviour, its data "lingua franca," and its "data space" (immaterial system). Ascott's demand to make the computer invisible is a bid for the immaterial pole.
- [[Object vs System]] — the same opposition in its structural form; Ascott reads the computer firmly on the *system* side and treats its persistence as an *object* as an obstacle to art.

## Questions It Raises

- If the computer "works best when it becomes invisible," does invisibility serve apprehension of the system (Ascott's claim) or does it conceal the apparatus — and with it the labour, power, and surveillance bound up in the machine? (Cf. the worry Ascott himself names: "the machine coming to dominate the human will.")
- Can a "set of behaviours" really be separated from its "thing"? Hayles's [[The Materiality of Informatics|materiality of informatics]] holds that information is never disembodied; is Ascott's invisible computer a genuine ontology or a designer's aspiration?
- Ascott says "the 'content' of a telematic art will depend in large measure on the nature of the interface" — if the medium of the system is the interface, has the interface simply become the new object, the new thing "too much with us"?
- Where exactly does Ascott's computer-specific system-reading add to Burnham's general object→system thesis, and where does it merely rename it? Is "the agent of the data field" a new claim or the 1968 system pole carried into the network age?
