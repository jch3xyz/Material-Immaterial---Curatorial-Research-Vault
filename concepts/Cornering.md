# Cornering

## Brief Definition

Shoshana Zuboff's redefinition of monopoly under surveillance capitalism: anticompetitive practices function not to fix prices but to *corner* the user-derived raw-material supply routes that feed [[The Extraction Imperative]] — and in the end it is *we* who are cornered, as the source of the coveted commodity.

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 5 ("Kidnap, Corner, Compete") Zuboff argues that the practices twentieth-century regulators would label monopolistic look entirely different once read through the logic of [[Surveillance Capitalism]]. The traditional account holds that "monopolies on goods and services disfigure markets by unfairly eliminating competition in order to raise prices at will." But under surveillance capitalism "many of the practices defined as monopolistic actually function as means of cornering user-derived raw-material supplies. There is no monetary price for the user to pay, only an opportunity for the company to extract data." Cornering practices "are not designed to protect product niches but rather to protect critical supply routes for the unregulated commodity that is behavioral surplus." The corporation impedes competitors not "primarily to fix prices" but to defend the dominance of its most important supply route. (`Directly stated`, Ch.5.)

The decisive operational sub-concept that cornering carries is the **supply route**. Because the extraction imperative "demands that everything be possessed," every good and service is recast as a conduit to behavior: "In this new context, goods and services are merely surveillance-bound supply routes. It's not the car; it's the behavioral data from driving the car. It's not the map; it's the behavioral data from interacting with the map." The product is never the point; the [[Behavioral Surplus]] generated through using the product is. Supply routes are continuously constructed and tested — those that "reliably produce scale" (Zuboff names Android and Gmail) are "elaborated and institutionalized," and "if one route is blocked, another is found," like a river running to the sea. Cornering is therefore "not simply a technological achievement" but "a highly orchestrated and carefully phased amalgam of political, communicative, administrative, legal, and material strategies" that asserts and defends claims to new ground. (`Directly stated`, Ch.5.)

The conceptual punch line inverts the figure of the cornered market. In the old story, "rogue market actors might corner markets in copper or magnesium," with distant downstream effects on the price of goods. Here the cornering operations "are not abstractions, with distant effects on minerals or crops." Rather, "In this picture it is we who are 'cornered.' We are the source of the coveted commodity; our experience is the target of extraction." As surveillance capitalism spreads from Silicon Valley across sectors, we find ourselves "in a world of no escape," cornered by "converging, overlapping, and relentlessly expanding dispossession operations." The human being is no longer the customer who might be overcharged; the human being is the cornered *supply*. (`Directly stated`, Ch.5.)

This is why, for Zuboff, twentieth-century antitrust misses the salient elements. The tilt toward exclusivity does produce practices "considered 'monopolistic' in the perspective of twentieth-century regulatory frameworks," but "these characterizations, while valid, omit the most-salient elements of the new order." Antitrust frameworks were built to police price and competition in markets for *goods and services*; they have no purchase on a logic where the good is free, the price is zero, and the real prize is the commodification of behavior. Consequently Zuboff warns that "regulatory interventions designed to constrain Google's monopoly practices are likely to have little effect on the fundamental operations of this market form" — new supply routes are continuously discovered and secured, and dispossession circumvents every obstacle "short of a genuine existential threat." Cornering thus diagnoses a structural mismatch between the law we have and the harm we face. (`Directly stated`, Ch.5.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins this redefinition of monopoly in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* Ch.5. Cornering is one of the three verbs of the chapter's title (kidnap, corner, compete); it is the moment in the extraction cycle when a firm secures and defends its *supply routes* against rivals. Two moves are essential to Zuboff's usage: (1) anticompetitive practice serves supply, not price; (2) the cornered party is the user, not a competing producer. The sub-concept of the **supply route** — goods and services as mere surveillance-bound conduits to behavioral data — is held here as the operational core of the idea.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — cornering literalizes this tension as an economic logic: the *material* product (the car, the map) is hollowed out, and value migrates entirely to the *immaterial* behavioral data extracted through using it. "It's not the car; it's the behavioral data from driving the car."

## Questions It Raises

- If antitrust law omits "the most-salient elements of the new order," what would a regulatory framework that targets *cornering of supply routes* rather than price-fixing actually look like — and could any framework that leaves behavioral surplus an "unregulated commodity" address the harm?
- Zuboff says it is *we* who are cornered. Does naming the user as the cornered supply (rather than as customer, product, or worker) change the political remedies available — closer to anti-dispossession or to the protection of a commons than to consumer protection?
- If "if one route is blocked, another is found," is the blocking of any individual supply route ever more than a delay — and what would the "genuine existential threat" to the market form be?
- The car and the map become "merely surveillance-bound supply routes." Does this generalize without limit, such that *every* designed object becomes a conduit to behavior, or are there goods whose use yields no extractable surplus?
