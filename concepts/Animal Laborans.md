# Animal Laborans

## Brief Definition

The "laboring animal" — the human being reduced to a beast of burden absorbed entirely in the process of work; a term Byung-Chul Han takes from Hannah Arendt but re-reads, so that the late-modern animal laborans is not Arendt's serene, de-individualized creature merged into the species but a hyperactive ego that exploits itself.

## Longer Explanation

Han adopts Arendt's diagnosis — the triumph of *animal laborans*, the absolutization of labor — while overturning the picture she draws of it. In Arendt's *The Human Condition* (which Han glosses at length in "Vita Activa"), modern society as a "society of 'laboring'" "nullifies any possibility for action when it degrades the human being into an animal laborans, a beast of burden," and the individual so degraded is "submerged in the over-all life process of the species," whose "only active decision" is "to let go . . . to abandon . . . individuality" and "acquiesce" to "a functional type of behavior." For Arendt the laboring beast de-individualizes and sinks, passively, into the species; modernity "began with an unprecedented, heroic activation of human capacity, yet it ends in mortal passivity." This is `Directly stated` in Han's paraphrase of Arendt.

Han accepts the symptom but rejects the explanation: "Arendt's descriptions of the modern animal laborans do not correspond to what we can observe in today's achievement society." Where Arendt's laboring beast dissolves serenely into the species, Han's late-modern one does the opposite. "The late-modern animal laborans is equipped with an ego just short of bursting. And it is anything but passive." It does not "give up its individuality or ego in order to merge . . . with the anonymous life process of the species"; on the contrary, "contemporary labor society, as a society of achievement and business, fosters individuality." Han presses the inversion to its sharpest point: "if one abandoned one's individuality and dissolved into the life process of the species entirely, one would at least have the serenity [Gelassenheit] of an animal. But the late-modern animal laborans is anything but animalian. It is hyperactive and hyperneurotic." This is the conceptual core of Han's re-reading (`Directly stated`).

The reason the late-modern laborer cannot reach the animal's brute serenity is that it is alone: "the late-modern ego [Ich] stands utterly alone," its work radically fleeting and stripped of any horizon of meaning, so that bare laboring corresponds to bare life and breeds "hyperactivity, hysterical work, and production" (`Directly stated`). Crucially, this animal is at once perpetrator and victim. In "Beyond Disciplinary Society" Han names its pathological form: "the depressive human being is an animal laborans that exploits itself—and it does so voluntarily, without external constraints. It is predator and prey at once." The figure thus folds directly into Han's account of the [[Achievement Society]] and of [[Auto-Exploitation]] — exploitation that operates without an external master because the achievement-subject is "lord and master of itself" (`Directly stated`).

Han also locates the animal laborans historically against the disciplinary world it succeeds. In "The Bartleby Case," Melville's "Story of Wall-Street" "describes an inhumane working world whose inhabitants have all degraded to the state of animal laborans" — but Han reads Bartleby and his fellow clerks as still belonging to a *disciplinary* society of walls and partitions, obedience-subjects suffering neurasthenia rather than the depressive I-tiredness of late modernity. The degraded office worker is thus the animal laborans in its earlier, still-disciplinary key, distinct from the self-exploiting depressive of the achievement society (`Strongly implied` — Han keeps the term while marking the epochal shift). *Awaits Arendt:* the full philosophical stakes of the term — vita activa against vita contemplativa, labor against work and action, natality, the "rise of the species" — belong to Arendt's own framework and will be realized when *The Human Condition* enters the vault; here the concept is anchored in Han's polemical re-reading, and the disagreement is housed in [[Byung-Chul Han and Hannah Arendt]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Hannah Arendt]]** *(forward-stub — no book yet in `raw/`)* — coins the term in *The Human Condition*: the *animal laborans* is the laboring beast of burden into which modern "laboring society" degrades the human, de-individualized and "submerged in the over-all life process of the species," its triumph marking modernity's slide from heroic activation into "mortal passivity." Han reports this usage; Arendt's own development of it awaits her ingest.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — accepts Arendt's symptom (the absolutization of labor, the triumph of the laboring beast) but inverts her picture: the *late-modern* animal laborans is "equipped with an ego just short of bursting," "anything but passive," "hyperactive and hyperneurotic." Its truth is the depressive who "exploits itself . . . voluntarily" and is "predator and prey at once." Where Arendt's beast loses its ego in the species, Han's bursts with ego and exhausts itself.

## Related Concepts

- [[Achievement Society]] — the social order whose subject *is* the late-modern animal laborans; the term names the achievement-subject in its laboring, self-exploiting aspect.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — the mode of the depressive animal laborans: it is "predator and prey at once," exploiting itself without external constraint.
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — the exploitation-by-another that auto-exploitation supersedes; the animal laborans needs no external master.
- [[Acceleration]] — "the acceleration of contemporary life" contributes to the lack of being that drives the laborer's hyperactivity.
- [[The Burnout Society]] — the book in which Han mounts the Arendt re-reading.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — the animal laborans is the subject of an achievement society defined by excessive positivity (unlimited "Can"); the loss of negativity, prohibition, and limit is what lets bare laboring become boundless and self-exploiting.

## Questions It Raises

- If Han keeps Arendt's term while reversing its content, is "animal laborans" still her concept, or a homonym? Where exactly does the symptom end and the diagnosis begin?
- Arendt's beast finds at least the "serenity of an animal"; Han's cannot. Is the loss of that serenity a gain in lucidity or simply a deeper degradation?
- Han ties the term to the loss of the vita contemplativa. Does his remedy (contemplative life, the "scent of time") recover Arendt's vita activa or quietly abandon it?
- Does locating the term across both disciplinary (Bartleby's clerks) and achievement-society (the self-exploiting depressive) settings strengthen the concept or dilute it?
