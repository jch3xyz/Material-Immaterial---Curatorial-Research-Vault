# Cyberbotany

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for the field that investigates artificial-life forms within the cyber and nano ecologies *together with* the technoetic dimensions and psychoactivity of plants such as ayahuasca — "the wise application of plant technology, in the technoetic context," extended into a horticultural model of culture itself: "hyperculture seen as horticulture."

## Longer Explanation

**Cyberbotany** appears in Ascott's 1999/2000 "Moist Manifesto" essay (ch.31, "Art @ the Edge of the NET: the Future Will Be Moist!") as the meeting point of two of his late preoccupations: the artificial-life sciences that populate the dry-and-moist [[Moistmedia]] substrate, and the ancient "plant technology" of shamanism he had explored in [[The Shamantic Web]] one chapter earlier. Within the increasing semantic and technological complexity of net-based, post-biological art, Ascott foresees "the insertion of a new but very ancient technology, that of the psychoactive plant. A sort of cyberbotany may arise, for example, around the instrumentality of such plants as the shamanic liana, ayahuasca (*Banisteriopsis caapi*), the vine of the soul" (`Directly stated`, ch.31).

The term names two interlocking strands. The first is investigative and biological: cyberbotany "covers a wide spectrum of activity and investigation into the properties and potential of artificial life forms within the cyber and nano ecologies" — the silicon-and-molecular organisms and nanotechnology that Ascott elsewhere folds into [[Artificial Life]] and the [[Nanobots|nano]] domain. The second is technoetic and psychoactive: the same field covers "the technoetic dimensions and psychoactivity induced by such plants as the ayahuasca and other vegetal products of nature" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). The bridge between them is Ascott's central wager that these are two technologies *of the same kind* — both are instruments for navigating consciousness — so the synthetic and the vegetal can be coupled rather than opposed.

Ascott concretizes this in a named project, **ayahuascatec.net**: "My project ayahuascatec.net is designed to explore this field." The editor's note glosses it as an attempt "to link a number of ayahuasceros telematically and telepathically (employing their double consciousness to navigate both psychic space and cyberspace) with spiritual guides who always appear as helpers to those in advanced use of the psychoactive brew," building on Ascott's 1997 fieldwork with Santo Daime and the Kuikuru Indians in the Mato Grosso, and aimed at constructing a "Psibernetic Arch" (`Directly stated`, editor's note, ch.31). The project is thus the operational form of the [[The Shamantic Web|shamantic-web]] parallelism (digital and plant technologies as twin consciousness-instruments) carried into the [[Moistmedia|moist]] convergence.

From this strand Ascott draws his strongest ontological claim: "It is my contention that vegetal reality and virtual reality will combine to create a new ontology, just as our notions of outer space and inner space will coalesce into another order of cosmography" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). Vegetal reality + virtual reality is the botanical inflection of the [[Moistmedia|dry/wet/moist]] tripartition — the wet, living, psychoactive pole fused with the dry pixel pole into a third state. (Ascott's own running term for this fusion mode is "visionary pragmatism"; the note keeps it as plain text here pending a dedicated note.)

Finally, "cyberbotany" widens from a literal practice into a governing metaphor for the whole moist culture. In the essay's concluding "Inclusion" section Ascott writes that the notion "thus extends from the wise application of plant technology, in the technoetic context, to the creative employment of horticultural metaphor in envisioning outcomes at the material level of construction: hyperculture seen as horticulture, the moist synthesis of artificial and natural systems" (`Directly stated`, ch.31). Cyberbotany becomes the figure for an architecture and a culture that are *seeded, grown, and cultivated* rather than designed and built — bottom-up, emergent, self-organizing — which is the same [[Emergence|emergent]] logic Ascott attaches to the [[The Rematerialization of Art|re-materialization]] of art into molecules, atoms, neurons, and genes (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — sole coiner and user of the term, in ch.31 of *Telematic Embrace*. He deploys it in two registers within the one essay: (1) the narrow, project-anchored sense — artificial-life forms in cyber/nano ecologies *plus* the technoetic psychoactivity of plants (ayahuasca), realized in ayahuascatec.net; and (2) the broad, metaphoric sense — "hyperculture seen as horticulture," culture and construction as horticulture. The concept presupposes [[The Shamantic Web]] (ch.30, the shamanic/psi parallel) and [[Moistmedia]] (ch.31/32, the dry/wet/moist substrate) and should be read as their botanical specialization.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — like Moistmedia, cyberbotany refuses the pole: it fuses the immaterial/virtual (cyberspace, the technoetic, the psychoactive vision) with the material/vegetal (the living plant, nano/molecular construction) into "a new ontology." It is a synthesis term, not a side in the tension.

## Questions It Raises

- Is "vegetal reality and virtual reality will combine to create a new ontology" a literal technical forecast, a description of an art practice, or a cosmological metaphor — and does cyberbotany need to be true in any of these senses to be useful?
- If the psychoactive plant is treated as "a new but very ancient technology," does framing ayahuasca as instrumentality risk severing it from the ritual, communal, and Amerindian contexts (Santo Daime, the União do Vegetal, the Kuikuru) that Ascott himself draws on?
- "Hyperculture seen as horticulture" replaces designing with cultivating; where does artistic authorship and responsibility sit when culture is seeded and grown rather than made?
- How does cyberbotany's optimistic fusion of vegetal and virtual relate to the surveillance and control readings of pervasive computing (Han, Harari) — does routing consciousness through plant *and* network change the political stakes, or compound them?
