# Data Space

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's term for the ephemeral, asynchronous, discontinuous "virtual space" (or "phase space") that data occupy — the spatial field, grounded in a "physics of information," within which the [[Gesamtdatenwerk]] and human-computer symbiosis are enacted.

## Longer Explanation

In "Gesamtdatenwerk: Connectivity, Transformation, and Transcendence" (1989) Ascott proposes that computer science can be modelled on quantum physics: where the subject of quantum physics is the transformation of energy and quanta are its object, the subject of computer science is the transformation of information and data are its object. Replacing "quanta" with "data" yields what he calls "a physics of information," and the natural question such a physics asks is *where* data are — its answer is **data space**: "the phase space, the virtual space that data occupy." Data space is therefore not the substance of information nor a condition of being, but the *field* or *site* in which data exist, flow, and are transformed (`Directly stated`).

The space is defined by the peculiar physics of its contents. Data, for Ascott, "exist in streams; data flow is ephemeral, transient, shifting"; data is "everywhere and nowhere"; and, although processed in time at the beat of the computer's pulse, data are "time-free and time-resistant" so far as their transformation across networks is concerned — users can access, interact, and collaborate independently of time or place. Data space is thus **asynchronous and non-linear**: networking "becomes a non-linear creative medium," and data, "as with quantum behaviour, are discontinuous; they 'jump' between semantic states" (`Directly stated`). This discontinuity is not a defect but a model of creative behaviour itself, "which is always nonlinear, indeterminate, and uncertain."

Data space is the operative site of Ascott's larger telematic program. The computer is redefined as "the agent of the data field, a construct of data space" rather than a thing; the interface should be designed "as a doorway into data space, a synaptic interval in a human-computer symbiosis" rather than a membrane sealing off the machine; the interface is itself "an arena of data space in which art comprising this human-computer symbiosis can be acted out." Scaled to the planet, data space becomes the site of the [[Gesamtdatenwerk]]: "the site of the Gesamtdatenwerk must be the planet as a whole, its data space, its electronic noosphere" (`Directly stated`). Data space is the field; the Gesamtdatenwerk is the planet-wide work staged within it.

Ascott grounds the immateriality of data space in a constructivist epistemology drawn from quantum physics: "it is in the quantum world that reality is the production of observation." From "the ground of undifferentiated wholeness we construct virtual realities, knowing that they are transient, impermanent, ephemeral constructs of mind." Data space is thus continuous with his claim that networking lets us re-evaluate reality as plural, provisional, and "virtual" — but it should be kept distinct from [[Virtuality]] as Hayles theorises it (a *condition*: the cultural perception that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns) and from [[Information]] (the *substance* whose transformation the medium performs). Data space is the spatial *field* where that substance lives and that condition obtains (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins "data space" / "data field" within a proposed "physics of information": the virtual ("phase") space data occupy, ephemeral, asynchronous, discontinuous, "everywhere and nowhere," "time-free and time-resistant"; the site of human-computer symbiosis, the interface (as doorway), and, at planetary scale, the [[Gesamtdatenwerk]] and its "electronic noosphere."

## Related Concepts

- [[Information]] — data space is the *field* in which information lives; Ascott's "physics of information" treats data as the object whose transformation is the medium's subject. (Distinct: substance vs. site.)
- [[Virtuality]] (Hayles) — Ascott's "virtual space that data occupy" parallels Hayles's virtuality, but data space names the spatial *field/site*, not the *condition* of pattern/presence. Cross-linked, distinct.
- [[Gesamtdatenwerk]] — the planet-scale, permanently open networked artwork sited *in* data space ("its data space, its electronic noosphere"); data space is its arena.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the collective, distributed planetary consciousness that the field of data space hosts and enables.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — data space is where "mind to mind, memory to memory" interaction with humans, artificial intelligences, sensing systems, and memory stores occurs.
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — data's quantum-like discontinuity ("they 'jump' between semantic states") and the "stochastic leap" from chaos to new order play out in data space.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] (Burnham) — data space is the immaterial *field* that the computer-as-object dissolves into once it is understood not as a thing but as "the agent of the data field, a construct of data space." Ascott's system→network telematics extends Burnham's object→system thesis; the polarity is object/thing vs. field/behaviour.

## Questions It Raises

- If data are "everywhere and nowhere" and "time-free," is "space" the right figure for the field they occupy, or is data space a metaphor borrowed from a physics it only loosely resembles?
- Does grounding a "physics of information" on the quantum analogy (quanta:data) clarify the ontology of data, or import quantum mystique to license a "biotechnological theology"?
- How does data space relate to embodiment, when its promise is to "reach the immaterial, the spiritual," "out of body," yet its interfaces are increasingly "body-responsive"?
- Is data space a genuinely new site of art, or a re-description of the network — and what distinguishes it from Hayles's [[Virtuality]] and from [[Information]] as such?
