# Luminosity

## Brief Definition

For Jack Burnham, **luminosity** is the perceptual property of *emitted* light — "the visual impression received when an area of light exceeds a threshold of brilliance relative to its surroundings" — that defines [[Light as a Sculpture Medium|Light Art]] and distinguishes it from the reflected and radiant light long handled by painters.

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 7 of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Burnham makes luminosity the conceptual basis of Light Art. He separates **emitted light** — "light directly discharged from the art object or system" — from the reflected and radiant light that painters had analyzed "for hundreds of years." What unites every source of emitted light, "the product of sun, moon, fire, electric lamps, luminescent chemicals, and various electrical aerial phenomenon," is "the relative quality of luminosity." Luminosity is therefore not a substance but a *threshold effect*: a perceptual impression that arises only when a patch of light is brilliant enough relative to what surrounds it. `Directly stated`

Crucially, Burnham defines luminosity in **relational and perceptual** terms rather than physical ones, which is consistent with the book's larger thesis that "light-not form-determines our perception of sculpture." The glow is *received* by an observer; to that observer "this glowing quality appears to be the result of internal energy in the source itself." The apparent self-luminous interiority of the source — its seeming to burn from within rather than to be lit from without — is what makes emitted light read as alive, primary, and irreducible to the depicted illumination of painting. `Directly stated`

Burnham then grounds luminosity's expressive force in an **affective, even primeval** register: "Possibly this explains why luminosity acts as an emotional stimulant: primeval man's response was to be drawn toward the life-giving energy sources of heat and light." Luminosity is thus a durable stimulant because it taps an archaic orientation toward heat and light as sources of life — the campfire, the sun. This is why, for Burnham, Light Art has a perceptual basis that earlier "color music" experiments lacked: it works on the nervous system directly, not by analogy to musical notation. `Directly stated`

The luminosity concept underwrites Burnham's developmental claim that direct emitted light could only enter the plastic arts once "systems devised from various types of electric light and its control apparatus" matured — that "until the 1920's-without exception-attempts to use emitted light were aesthetically misconceived, without a perceptual basis, and technologically primitive." Luminosity is the durable perceptual fact; the apparatus and the public sensibility that finally let artists exploit it are historical and contingent. The chapter closes by folding luminosity into a broader "cultural trend toward intangibility," recalling that "Light, as Otto Piene has pointed out, is the incarnation of visible energy," and that Marshall McLuhan, "pointed to a glowing light bulb and remarked that it radiated pure information." `Strongly implied`

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Vitalism]] — Burnham frames luminosity perceptually and systemically (a threshold effect read by an observer), against the older vitalist account of sculpture as inner living form; emitted light's apparent "internal energy" is an *impression*, not an inner life force. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Questions It Raises

- If luminosity is defined as a *relative* threshold ("brilliance relative to its surroundings"), is it a property of the light source, of the perceptual situation, or of the system that includes the viewer?
- Burnham distinguishes emitted from reflected/radiant light — but his examples (Moholy-Nagy's Light-Space Modulator works through "moving shadows," Whitman's laser "imprint... on his body") often mix emission with reflection. Where exactly is the boundary?
- Why does luminosity "act as an emotional stimulant" — is the appeal to "primeval man's response" an explanation or a just-so story, and does it survive the move from fire and sun to neon and lasers?
- If "pure energy and information seem to be the essences of art," does luminosity mark a step in the dematerialization of the art object, or a new material medium with its own "variable life expectancy"?
