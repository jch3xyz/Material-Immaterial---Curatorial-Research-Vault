# The Unrequited Art

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's governing figure for Kinetic Art: an art whose aspiration — life-like motion, immateriality, and real time — chronically outruns the deterministic means of the first age of machines, making it a heroic, productive failure that survives precisely because it remains unrealized.

## Longer Explanation

"The Unrequited Art" is the title and central metaphor of Chapter 6 of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, where Burnham anatomizes Kinetic Art. The phrase names a structural condition rather than a passing weakness: the desire of avant-garde painters and sculptors to make art move — to work in the parameters of real time and motion — is bound to a technology that is constitutionally hostile to that desire. Burnham locates the cause squarely in lineage: "If Kineticism has been designated 'The Unrequited Art,' the cause is its close ties to the methods and attitudes of the first age of machines." The classical machine, in Franz Reuleaux's formulation that Burnham repeatedly invokes, is "a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies"; an art that aspires to psychic ambiguity, liberation, and immateriality cannot be requited by a medium conceived "upon the precepts of physical restraint." (`Directly stated`)

The unrequitedness is therefore not the failure of particular artists but the gap between an artistic urge and its available means. Burnham reads the relative aesthetic failure of Kinetic Art as significant in itself, "since the desire to make art kinetic is one of the prime artistic urges of the present century." The motor must be hidden, "tucked away and well out of view," because — as Gabo warned — the mechanism competes with and threatens to kill the motion it produces. The Kinetic artist "has united his art with forces inherently at odds with artistic endeavor." Each of the pivotal early figures (Duchamp, Gabo, Calder, Moholy-Nagy) made a few experiments and withdrew; their reputations rest on their fixed, not their kinetic, work. (`Directly stated`)

Crucially, Burnham frames the failure as heroic and generative rather than merely deficient. He weighs unrealized Kinetic art against the alternative of an inert object-sculpture that has surrendered to its own materiality: "Yet unrealized art has its heroic aspects, certainly much more than sculpture which flabbily accepts its 'thingness' and giantism almost as a signal of defeat." Kineticism "represents a willingness to attempt the logically inevitable though at present this may be technologically unrealizable." The unrequited condition is thus a sign of life — a "dialectical tension" that "continues to build around Kineticism" — precisely because the goal is still ahead of the means. (`Directly stated`)

The metaphor carries a paradoxical theory of artistic survival: an art lives by *not* arriving. "The real way to kill an art movement is for it to realize its goals — an objective which Kineticism has yet to achieve." On this logic the unrealized state "should remain a sign of encouragement for future artists." Burnham contrasts this productive deferral with the self-destroying machines of Tinguely (urged on by Philip Leider) — "only a Luddite solution," a death wish rather than a fulfillment. The Unrequited Art is therefore both a diagnosis (Kineticism is technologically premature) and a value (its prematurity is the source of its heroism and its futurity). (`Directly stated` for the quotations; `Interpretive synthesis` for "theory of artistic survival" framing.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — The Unrequited Art is Burnham's own coinage and the chapter title. He uses it to name Kinetic Art's chronic shortfall against the deterministic, "closed kinematic chain" technology of the first age of machines, and to recast that shortfall as a heroic, future-oriented condition: an art kept alive by remaining unrealized. The companion figure is real-time motion and immateriality (Gabo's "essential immateriality of matter," Moholy-Nagy's "dematerialized" sculpture-as-volume, Klein's "immaterial sensitivity"), all of which the available mechanics cannot yet deliver.

## Related Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — Kineticism's immateriality drive (mass dissolved into virtual volume; "the essential immateriality of matter") is a sculptural precursor to Lippard's later dematerialization of the art object; both name an art straining away from its material support.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's own subsequent program; the Unrequited Art's reaching past the closed classical machine toward open, real-time process anticipates his shift from object to system.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the Unrequited Art lives inside this tension, aspiring to the immaterial (motion, light, real time) while bound to the resolutely material apparatus of the machine.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Conceptual Art]] — a counter-resolution to the same impasse: where Kineticism remains heroically unrealized in matter, conceptual art dematerializes by relocating the work into language, document, and idea, escaping the mechanical-determinist trap rather than wrestling with it. (`Speculative connection`)

## Questions It Raises

- Is an art that survives only by remaining unrealized a permanent category, or merely an artifact of one technological era (the "first age of machines") that a later technology — electronics, light, real time — could finally requite?
- If realizing its goals kills a movement, what distinguishes productive unrequitedness from simple failure or technical incompetence?
- How does Burnham's heroizing of unrealized art relate to the romantic / Shelleyan register he invokes around Klein's "immaterial sensitivity" — is "unrequited" a love metaphor, and what does that imply about the artist's relation to the machine?
- Does the Unrequited Art's deferral converge with or diverge from conceptual art's dematerialization as two responses to the same crisis of the object?
