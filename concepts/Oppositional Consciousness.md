# Oppositional Consciousness

## Brief Definition

Chela Sandoval's model of political identity, reported by Donna Haraway in *A Cyborg Manifesto*: a "hopeful model of political identity" born of the skills for reading webs of power among those denied stable membership in the categories of race, sex, or class.

## Longer Explanation

Haraway introduces *oppositional consciousness* not as her own coinage but as Chela Sandoval's, derived from "a consideration of specific historical moments in the formation of the new political voice called women of color." It names a consciousness "born of the skills for reading webs of power by those refused stable membership in the social categories of race, sex, or class." The key move is that political subjectivity here is not grounded in a natural essence (in "being" female, black, or working-class) but in the learned skill of those whom the dominant categories have failed to contain. (Directly stated.)

For Haraway the concept does the work of an alternative to two failed feminist responses to the crisis of political identity — "endless splitting and searches for a new essential unity." Oppositional consciousness "constructs a kind of postmodernist identity out of otherness, difference, and specificity," an identity that is "fully political." Crucially, Haraway distinguishes it from soft postmodern relativism: "Sandoval's oppositional consciousness is about contradictory locations and heterochronic calendars, not about relativisms and pluralisms." Difference here is not a flattening of all positions into interchangeable points of view; it is a map of contradictory, temporally non-aligned locations within systems of domination. (Directly stated.)

Haraway reports that Sandoval "emphasizes the lack of any essential criterion for identifying who is a woman of color" and that "the definition of the group has been by conscious appropriation of negation" — a Chicana or U.S. black woman placed "at the bottom of a cascade of negative identities." Out of this negation a "self-consciously constructed space" emerges that "cannot affirm the capacity to act on the basis of natural identification, but only on the basis of conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship." This makes oppositional consciousness Haraway's chief example in the chapter of *affinity, not identity* — the cyborg politics of building unity without a natural matrix. (Directly stated.)

Within the *Cyborg Manifesto* argument, oppositional consciousness is one of the concrete, lived precursors of cyborg subjectivity: a position that refuses both the totalizing revolutionary subject of "previous Marxisms and feminisms" and the innocent, organic ground of identity. It models the kind of "partial, contradictory, permanently unclosed constructions of personal and collective selves" that Haraway's cyborg myth seeks. (Strongly implied.)

## Authors and Variants

- **Chela Sandoval** (reported in **[[Donna Haraway]]**, *A Cyborg Manifesto*) — the originating theorist. Haraway attributes the concept, the "women of color" derivation, the "appropriation of negation," and the "contradictory locations and heterochronic calendars" formulation to Sandoval, not to herself.
- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — adopts Sandoval's concept as a building block for cyborg politics, reading it as a working instance of "affinity, not identity" and of partial, constructed, non-innocent selves.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — oppositional consciousness is one of Haraway's lived models for the cyborg's partial, coalitional, anti-essentialist political identity.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — oppositional consciousness rejects the autonomous, self-grounding, naturally unified subject; identity is a constructed, contradictory location, not an essence or natural standpoint.

## Questions It Raises

- Can a politics grounded in "affinity, not identity" sustain effective collective action without quietly smuggling in a new essential unity?
- What distinguishes "contradictory locations and heterochronic calendars" from the relativism Haraway explicitly rejects — and who decides which differences are "playful" and which are "poles of world historical systems of domination"?
- If oppositional consciousness is a learned skill of reading webs of power, is it transferable beyond the historical location ("women of color") from which Sandoval derived it?
