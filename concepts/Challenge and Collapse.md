# Challenge and Collapse

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's media recasting of Arnold Toynbee's law of historical challenge: the mere coexistence of two forms of organization generates tension, and the more complex civilization disintegrates the simpler society "by a rain of psychic energy" — so that the proper response to a technology thrusting hard in one direction is not to match its expansion but to mount a "countervailing thrust" of decentralism.

## Longer Explanation

Chapter 7 takes its title and its frame from Toynbee's *A Study of History*, but McLuhan converts Toynbee's account of how cultures meet challenge into a thesis about media and the sense ratios. The decisive move is to treat the simple *adjacency* of two unlike social forms as itself an energetic event. "Mere existence side by side of any two forms of organization generates a great deal of tension" (`Directly stated`). Following Toynbee, McLuhan notes that "the challenge of a civilization set side by side with a tribal society has over and over demonstrated that the simpler society finds its integral economy and institutions 'disintegrated by a rain of psychic energy generated by the civilization' of the more complex culture." Recast as a media law: "When two societies exist side by side, the psychic challenge of the more complex one acts as an explosive release of energy in the simpler one" (`Directly stated`). Collapse here is not conquest but a *psychic* discharge across a boundary.

McLuhan's most vivid case is the teenager, and the mechanism he names is television. The adolescent "compelled to share the life of a city that cannot accept him as an adult, collapses into 'rebellion without a cause'"; where the older adolescent "had been provided with a rain check" and "was prepared to wait it out," the new electric medium destroys that holding pattern: "But since TV the drive to participation has ended adolescence, and every American home has its Berlin wall" (`Directly stated`). The wall is the line of challenge-and-collapse drawn *inside* the home — TV's tactile drive to participation makes the teenager a kind of barbarian on the far side of a frontier, no longer waiting out a deferral but pressing for immediate, total involvement.

The chapter's constructive turn is its theory of the **countervailing thrust**. McLuhan reads Toynbee against himself: real growth is not enlargement (Toynbee "abandons the concept of enlargement as a criterion of real growth of society"), and the futurist or archaist reflex — pointing back to the horse or forward to antigravity vehicles — is "not an adequate response to the challenge of the motorcar." Instead: "When the technology of a time is powerfully thrusting in one direction, wisdom may well call for a countervailing thrust" (`Directly stated`). Because the present age is one of electric contraction rather than mechanical expansion, the countervailing thrust cannot itself be expansionist. This is the chapter's pivot into [[Explosion vs Implosion]]: "The implosion of electric energy in our century cannot be met by explosion or expansion, but it can be met by decentralism and the flexibility of multiple small centers" (`Directly stated`). His worked examples are organizational — answer the "implosion" of students not by enlarging the university but by "numerous groups of autonomous colleges," just as the "revival of the decentralized medieval parliament saved English society from the monopoly of centralism that seized the continent."

The chapter therefore sits at the hinge of *Understanding Media*'s Part I theory and folds three of its other concepts together. The collapse-by-coexistence mechanism is the social face of [[Detribalization and Retribalization]] (the civilized contact that drives the barbarian to "furious restlessness" and "mass migration" is the same pressure that, electrically reversed, retribalizes the teenager); the "explosive release of energy" between adjacent forms is a charged variant of the [[Hybrid Energy|hybrid energy]] released when two media cross; and the proper strategic answer — decentralism, multiple small centers — is the political form of the electric [[The Global Village|global village]] and of [[Decentralization]]. McLuhan also names the figure who can meet challenge without collapse: "the dedicated artist," who "picks up the message of cultural and technological challenge decades before its transforming impact occurs" and so can "correct the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — coins "challenge and collapse" by recasting Toynbee's challenge-and-response in media terms. The unit of analysis is the *boundary* between two organizational forms; the more complex one disintegrates the simpler "by a rain of psychic energy," and electric media (TV) carry this challenge into ever finer-grained spaces ("every American home has its Berlin wall"). His correction to Toynbee is twofold: growth is not enlargement, and the right response to a one-directional technological thrust is a *countervailing thrust* — which, in an imploding electric age, must take the form of decentralism and "multiple small centers," not further expansion.

## Questions It Raises

- If "mere existence side by side of any two forms of organization generates a great deal of tension," is collapse a property of the boundary itself rather than of either society — and can the more complex culture ever be the one that collapses?
- "Every American home has its Berlin wall": does TV's drive to participation collapse adolescence by *erasing* a frontier or by multiplying frontiers down to the scale of the household?
- The "countervailing thrust" is decentralism for an imploding age. What would the countervailing thrust be for a *later* technology — and does the prescription presuppose that we can correctly diagnose the present's dominant direction?
- McLuhan locates the only non-collapsing response in "the dedicated artist." Is integral, anticipatory perception genuinely transmissible as cultural strategy, or does it remain "the puny and peripheral efforts of artists"?
