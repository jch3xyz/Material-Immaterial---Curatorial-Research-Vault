# The Backstage

## Brief Definition

The backstage is the region — drawn from Erving Goffman — where the self retreats from the performative demands of social life to "be ourselves," buffering itself from the deterministic demands of its surroundings; under [[Big Other]] that region is collapsing.

## Longer Explanation

Borrowing from the mid-century social psychologist Erving Goffman's *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life*, Zuboff treats the backstage as the structural condition of an authentic self. Goffman's theatrical metaphor of "backstage" and "onstage" named "the universal need for a place of retreat in which we can 'be ourselves.'" Backstage is where the impression fostered by performance can be knowingly contradicted, where conversation is relaxed and truthful, where vital secrets can be visible, and where one can surrender to the uncomposed face. Crucially, Goffman held that "control of the backstage" allows individuals "to buffer themselves from the deterministic demands that surround them." Without such respite, where a "real" self can incubate and grow, the self has nowhere to recover from the labor of self-presentation. *(Directly stated)*

Zuboff's intervention is to mark what surveillance capitalism does to this region: it is disappearing. She frames the difference through a student's testimony — Goffman "assumed a backstage where you could be your true self," but now "the backstage is shrinking. There is almost no place left where I can be my true self." Even walking alone, the moment one assumes one is backstage, "an ad appears on my phone or someone takes a photo," and one discovers one is onstage again. The instruments of [[Big Other]] and the [[Digital Panopticon]] follow the self into every supposed refuge, dissolving the boundary between performance and retreat. *(Directly stated)*

Zuboff connects this collapse to what scholars call "chilling effects" — the continuous curation of one's photos, comments, and profile to maximize "likes" — and to what British researchers term an "extended chilling effect," in which people censor and curate even their real-world, offline behavior in anticipation of imagined online audiences. The shrinking of the backstage is therefore not merely a private psychological loss but a behavioral one: when there is no place left to be unobserved, the self is permanently rehearsing for an audience it can never escape. *(Directly stated)*

This concept is the structural precursor to Zuboff's [[The Right to Sanctuary]] argument. The sanctuary claim — the assertion of an inviolable space of refuge against the will to total visibility — only becomes intelligible once one sees that the backstage, the everyday region where the self was once safe to lapse out of performance, has been annexed by surveillance. *(Interpretive synthesis)*

## Questions It Raises

- If the backstage is the precondition of an authentic self, does its disappearance dissolve the very self that the right to sanctuary would protect?
- Is the "extended chilling effect" a temporary adaptation or a permanent reorganization of behavior around imagined audiences?
- Can a backstage be reconstructed by design — through law or refusal — or is it irrecoverable once total sensing is normalized?
