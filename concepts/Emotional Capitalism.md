# Emotional Capitalism

## Brief Definition

A mode of capitalism that exploits emotion as a productive force and as a vehicle of limitless consumption — the affective register of neoliberal psychopolitics, which mobilizes the dynamic, situative and performative character of emotion to drive productivity, achievement and desire.

## Longer Explanation

For Han, emotional capitalism is what becomes possible once production switches from the material to the immaterial. He turns on a conceptual distinction his sources blur: *feeling*, *emotion* and *affect* are not interchangeable. Feeling is objective, durative and narratable; affect is eruptive and momentary; *emotion* is "dynamic, situative and performative." It is precisely this performativity that makes emotion exploitable — and feelings unexploitable, since "feelings, in contrast, cannot be readily exploited inasmuch as they have no performativity." Because emotions "refer to actions and deeds" and are "intentional and goal-oriented," they can be enlisted directly as a "raw material" for productivity and as energy for action.

The term is Eva Illouz's coinage in *Cold Intimacies: The Making of Emotional Capitalism*, and Han adopts the phrase while rejecting her genealogy. Illouz traces the emotionalization of economic life back to capitalism's Weberian, Protestant-ascetic origins; Han counters that Weber analyzes "the ascetic capitalism of accumulation, which obeys rational logic more than it follows emotional logic," and that such capitalism "does not feed into consumer capitalism, which derives its profits from emotions." The decisive point against Illouz is historical and material: "emotion comes to possess value for capitalism only when a switch to immaterial production occurs. Emotions have become a means of production only in our own times." This is why the concept is keyed to [[Material vs Immaterial]] — emotion is not a perennial feature of capitalism but a productive force specific to the immaterial mode of production in which communicative interaction predominates.

Emotional capitalism works through, not against, freedom. It "banks on freedom" and "hails emotion as the expression of unbridled subjectivity" — and "neoliberal technologies of power exploit this same subjectivity mercilessly." Where the disciplinary society treated emotion as a disturbance to be weeded out so the human being could "function" like "an unfeeling machine," neoliberalism reverses this: once rationality hits a limit and is felt as constraint, "emotionality takes its place, which is attended by the feeling of liberty." Acceleration compounds the shift, since "rationality is slower than emotionality," so "the pressure of acceleration now is leading to a dictatorship of emotion." This is the affective face of the [[Exploitation of Freedom]] and of [[Auto-Exploitation]]: the achievement-subject is moved at a pre-reflexive level, because "neoliberal psychopolitics seizes on emotion in order to influence actions on this pre-reflexive level," operating "deep inside" via the limbic system.

On the consumption side, emotional capitalism opens a field that "knows no limit." Han argues that "today we do not consume things so much as emotions," and where things "cannot be consumed without end," emotions can — they "assume dimensions beyond the scope of use value." This connects emotional capitalism to Han's account of value beyond use value (see [[Exhibition Value]]): consumer capitalism sells "meanings and emotions," not utilities. [[Gamification]] (ch. 10) is the applied case: emotional capitalism "enlists playing and games," lending "an emotional, indeed a dramatic, charge to working," so that the emotionally invested player out-produces the merely functioning worker — and, in doing so, "gamification is destroying play's potential to set free."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — adopts Illouz's *term* but rejects her Weberian/ascetic genealogy; defines emotional capitalism by the exploitability of emotion's performativity, tying its emergence strictly to immaterial production and the neoliberal mobilization of freedom and subjectivity.
- **Eva Illouz** *(forward-stub — Cold Intimacies, not yet in raw/)* — coins "emotional capitalism" but, on Han's reading, "fails to account for the fact that emotion comes to possess value for capitalism only when a switch to immaterial production occurs," equates feeling with emotion, and traces the phenomenon to capitalism's inaugural (Weberian) stages.

## Related Concepts

- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — emotional capitalism is freedom's affective exploitation; it "banks on freedom" and exploits "unbridled subjectivity."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — emotion drives the achievement-subject to exploit itself at a pre-reflexive level.
- [[Achievement Society]] — emotion is deployed "as resources in order to bring about heightened productivity and achievement."
- [[Acceleration]] — accelerated communication emotionalizes production; the "pressure of acceleration" yields a "dictatorship of emotion."
- [[Exhibition Value]] — consumer capitalism profits from "emotive or cultic value," not use value; emotion as limitless consumption parallels value forms beyond use value.
- [[Gamification]] — the applied case (ch. 10): games lend an emotional charge to work, maximizing productivity while destroying play's freedom.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the load-bearing opposition: emotion becomes a means of production only with the switch *to* immaterial production; in material/ascetic-accumulative capitalism it has no productive value. Han also opposes emotion to *rationality* (the medium of disciplinary society) and to *feeling* and *affect* (neither of which is exploitable in the same way).

## Questions It Raises

- If emotion is exploitable only because it is performative, is there any affective register that escapes capture — and does Han's "feeling" (durative, narratable, non-performative) name a genuine outside, or only a slower form of capture?
- Han faults Illouz for collapsing feeling, emotion and affect; does his own sharp tripartite distinction hold across languages and cases, or does it depend on the German lexicon (Gefühl / Emotion / Affekt / Stimmung) he invokes?
- If "we do not consume things so much as emotions," what becomes of the materiality of the art object and of commodities — and how does emotional capitalism relate to the dematerialization narratives elsewhere in the vault?
- Does the gamified, emotionalized workplace represent a new dispositive distinct from disciplinary "concerted orthopaedy," or merely its inversion (emotion mobilized rather than weeded out)?
