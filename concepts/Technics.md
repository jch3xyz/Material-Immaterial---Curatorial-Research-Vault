# Technics

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's compression of science and technology "into a single word" — a coercive, autonomous, self-aggrandizing, irreversible force of which most of Western culture is a ramification, and which is responsible "both physically and spiritually" for sculpture's impending rupture with its entire past.

## Longer Explanation

In *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Burnham coins **technics** as a deliberate fusion: "science and technology (or technics-to link them into a single word)." The move is not casual. For Burnham the deep cause of sculpture's twentieth-century metamorphosis is not stylistic fashion, journalism, or biography but "the impetus generated by the psychic and intellectual authority increasingly invested in science and technology." Naming that fused authority *technics* lets him treat science and technology as one continuous pressure rather than two influences that might be weighed separately — a unity he reasserts in the cyborg chapter, where he calls them "the science-technology syndrome" and sees "scant reason to believe that these two forces should be regarded separately since they complement and stimulate the advancement of each other." `Directly stated`

Technics for Burnham is **coercive and total**. "Most aspects of Western culture are to some degree ramifications of technics," he writes; religion, literary imagery, craft, drama, architecture, and painting "have not escaped the technological demiurge." Crucially the claim is not merely material: "Both physically and spiritually technics are responsible for sculpture's impending rupture with the past." This is the sense in which Burnham frames technics as a *metaphysics* rather than a toolkit, citing Friedrich Junger's concession "that technology is the metaphysics of our century" — an apprehension Burnham says "time has not diminished." `Directly stated`

Burnham routes the concept through **Jacques Ellul's *technique***. "A systems-oriented world is a milieu completely given over to what Jacques Ellul calls technique. Science and technology for Ellul are simply the most efficient forms of technique." From Ellul, Burnham takes technique's defining attributes — "its flower is modern Western science; it has no psychic or ethical strictures; it is self-aggrandizing and, most alarming, irreversible" — and applies them to the predicament of art. He also borrows technique's **principal law of the age** ("technique uses what is at hand, regardless of the implications") and Soustelle's atomic-bomb formula "Since it was possible, it was necessary," which Burnham predicts "should become apparent" across the rest of the book. The Ellulian sense is carried in the sibling definition [[Technique (Ellul via Burnham)]]. `Directly stated`

The force is **self-augmenting**. Naming Ellul's "technique" alongside "what Lewis Mumford has called 'technics,'" Burnham underlines its "property of 'self-augmentation'": "there is no such thing as introducing 'a little technology.' Beyond a certain plateau, technology breeds technology." And it is **autonomous to the point of indifference toward its makers**: "The fearful quality about technology is that it is self-aggrandizing; it moves almost as if men were not its instigators; a self-propelled force, it evolves oblivious of the ambitions and contentments of the human race." For Burnham, technics is finally close to all-consuming where art is concerned — "Only madness is inaccessible to the machine. Every other 'art' form can be reduced to technique." `Directly stated`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*) — coins *technics* as the welded unity of science and technology; reads it as a coercive, self-aggrandizing, irreversible metaphysics, after Friedrich Junger ("technology is the metaphysics of our century") and Jacques Ellul (whose *technique* names science and technology as its "most efficient forms").
- **Jacques Ellul** (via Burnham, *The Technological Society* / *La Technique*) — *technique*: the totalizing milieu of which science and technology are merely the most efficient instances; without psychic or ethical strictures, self-aggrandizing, irreversible. Forward-stub author; captured here through Burnham's reading and in [[Technique (Ellul via Burnham)]].
- **Lewis Mumford** (named by Burnham) — the prior English coinage *technics*, glossed by Burnham via Ellul's "self-augmentation." Forward-stub author.
- **Friedrich Junger** (named by Burnham) — "technology is the metaphysics of our century," the apothegm Burnham uses to stake technics' spiritual reach. Forward-stub author.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Technology as Metaphor]] (O'Gieblyn) — kept DISTINCT. O'Gieblyn's concern is the *figurative* slide by which a technological model (the computer, the algorithm) becomes the literal language for mind, God, and cosmos. Burnham's *technics* is not a metaphor but an asserted material-and-spiritual *force* — "self-propelled," "irreversible," indifferent to its makers. The opposition is one of register: metaphor that hardens into literalism versus an autonomous power that needs no metaphor to act. Cross-linked, not merged. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Questions It Raises

- If technics is genuinely "irreversible" and "self-aggrandizing," is Burnham's own hope for the "temporary survival of sculpture" through the move to systems anything more than collaboration with the force he diagnoses?
- Does welding science and technology "into a single word" clarify or obscure? What is lost by refusing to "regard them separately"?
- Is technics best understood as a metaphysics (Junger), a milieu (Ellul's technique), or a quasi-organism that "moves almost as if men were not its instigators"? Burnham invokes all three — are they compatible?
- Where O'Gieblyn worries that we mistake [[Technology as Metaphor|a metaphor]] for reality, Burnham insists technics *is* the reality. Which diagnosis better fits the present — captured language, or an autonomous force?
