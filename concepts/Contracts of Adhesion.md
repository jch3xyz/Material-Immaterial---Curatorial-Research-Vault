# Contracts of Adhesion

## Brief Definition

The legal form of the digital terms-of-service agreement — a "take-it-or-leave-it" contract that binds the user without negotiation and, in Margaret Radin's phrase, performs a "private eminent domain," "a unilateral seizure of rights without consent."

## Longer Explanation

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], *contracts of adhesion* are the legal infrastructure through which the felt promises of the digital — connection, search, sociality — are converted into a one-sided expropriation of rights. Among the "many violations of advocacy expectations," she writes, "ubiquitous 'terms-of-service agreements' were among the most pernicious." Legal experts call them contracts of adhesion "because they impose take-it-or-leave-it conditions on users that stick to them whether they like it or not." The metaphor is adhesive: the user does not agree so much as get *stuck* — the contract adheres to them on the firm's terms. *(Directly stated)*

The mechanism has several folded-together forms. Online "contracts" are also called *click-wrap* "because, as a great deal of research shows, most people get wrapped in these oppressive contract terms by simply clicking on the box that says 'I agree' without ever reading the agreement." Their close kin is *browse-wrap*: "In many cases, simply browsing a website obligates you to its terms-of-service agreement even if you don't know it." The documents are "excessively long and complex in part to discourage users from actually reading the terms, safe in the knowledge that most courts have upheld the legitimacy of click-wrap agreements despite the obvious lack of meaningful consent" — a regime so normalized that even Chief Justice John Roberts "admitted that he 'doesn't read the computer fine print.'" Worse, "terms of service can be altered unilaterally by the firm at any time, without specific user knowledge or consent," and they implicate "other companies (partners, suppliers, marketers, advertising intermediaries, etc.) without stating or accepting responsibility for their terms of service" — what law professor Nancy Kim describes as a "sadistic," "unwinnable infinite regress." *(Directly stated)*

Zuboff's sharpest framing comes through legal scholar Margaret Radin, who "observes the Alice-in-Wonderland quality of such 'contracts.'" The "sacred notions of 'agreement' and 'promise'" central to contract "since Roman times have devolved to a 'talismanic' signal 'merely indicating that the firm deploying the boilerplate wants the recipient to be bound.'" Radin "calls this 'private eminent domain,' a unilateral seizure of rights without consent." The analogy to *eminent domain* — the state's power to seize property — is exact and damning: it names a *taking*, but one performed unilaterally by a private firm, with the legal vocabulary of "consent" preserved as an empty ritual. Radin regards such contracts as "a moral and democratic 'degradation' of the rule of law and the institution of contract," a perversion that "restructures the rights of users granted through democratic processes," substituting "the system that the firm wishes to impose." *(Directly stated)*

What makes the regime possible is, for Zuboff, a question of *materiality* — the load-bearing link to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] spine. Kim points out that "paper documents once imposed natural restraints on contracting behavior simply by virtue of their cost to produce, distribute, and archive." A paper contract "requires a physical signature, limiting the burden a firm is likely to impose." Digital terms, by contrast, "are 'weightless.' They can be expanded, reproduced, distributed, and archived at no additional cost." It is precisely the *dematerialization* of the contract that lifts the natural friction restraining it: once courts validated click-wrap and browse-wrap, "there was nothing to stop them from expanding the reach of these degraded contracts." The weightlessness of the form is what allows the rights-seizure to scale; the legal mechanism becomes, in Zuboff's account, the instrument through which [[Behavioral Surplus]] is extracted — terms-of-service agreements were extended into "baroque and perverse 'privacy policies,' establishing another infinite regress of these terms of expropriation," letting firms "acquire rights without bargaining and to stealthily establish and embed practices before users, and regulators, realize what has happened." *(Strongly implied)*

## Opposing Concepts

## Questions It Raises

- If "consent" survives only as a talismanic click, what would meaningful consent to data collection actually require — and is it achievable at digital scale?
- Radin names a *private* eminent domain. What would it mean to subject such private takings to the constitutional constraints (just compensation, public use, due process) that govern the state's power of eminent domain?
- Is the "weightlessness" of the digital contract a contingent property of the medium, or has dematerialization permanently dissolved the material frictions that once protected the user?
- Can the institution of contract — "since Roman times" grounded in agreement and promise — be repaired, or has adhesion irreversibly hollowed it out?
