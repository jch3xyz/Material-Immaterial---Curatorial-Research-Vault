# The Post-Biological Era

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for the emerging epoch in which electronic, computer-mediated, and post-biological systems become fundamental to daily life and culture co-evolves with artificial life — an age whose creative goal is "no longer to imitate life but to emulate it."

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott the **post-biological era** is a periodizing claim, not merely a metaphor. He states it flatly: "Electronic, computer-mediated, and post-biological systems are now a fundamental part of our daily life and are playing a significant role in the transformation and globalisation of culture" (`Directly stated`). The hinge of the concept is a shift in the very aim of art and culture, captured in his most-quoted formulation: "The attempt is no longer to imitate life but to emulate it, as part of the co-evolution with nature that is our contemporary goal. We are entering the post-biological era" (ch.25). Mimesis (imitating the surface appearance of the world) gives way to emulation (generating life-like process from the inside), so the post-biological is bound up with Ascott's parallel theses of the "end of the era of mimetic art" and of art as world-making rather than representation.

The era is identified with a wholesale change of governing metaphors. Where 1980s technoculture drew on "quantum physics, chaos science, and cosmology, often deconstructionist and inevitably postmodern," the new century's art "will come from the new biology, artificial life, neural networks, cognitive science, and radical constructivism" (`Directly stated`). The post-biological is thus the cultural correlate of artificial life: data pools are figured as a primeval soup from which post-biological culture emerges as proto-organisms once emerged from rock pools. It is explicitly a programmatic and pedagogical horizon too — Ascott titles a 1994 essay "The Planetary Collegium: Art and Education in the Post-Biological Era," and frames art's task as bringing "new values and new understandings of our postbiological condition" and helping define "the new responsibilities we carry for our own bionic evolution" (ch.26). Note Ascott's orthography varies — hyphenated "post-biological" and closed "postbiological" both appear in the source; the canonical note name uses the hyphenated form.

By the mid-to-late 1990s the term hardens into a fixed Ascott coinage. In the alphabetical glossary "Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions for the Post-Biological Era" (1996), "Post-biological era" is itself an entry: "The old disputes between modernism and poststructuralism, grand narrative and semantic negation dissolve before the prospect of postbiological life" (ch.32) — the post-biological supersedes the modern/postmodern quarrel rather than taking sides in it. The body is redefined accordingly: "Previously the property of nature; now the site of bionic transformation at which we can recreate ourselves and redefine what it is to be human" (the seed of the sibling concept [[The Post-Biological Body]]). In the "Moist Manifesto" (1999) the era becomes the destination of art's recovery — art "springs forth into the post-biological culture with a radical constructivism" — and the post-biological condition is given its material form in the moist convergence of "the dry world of virtuality and the wet world of biology." Ascott can even gloss the term phenomenologically: in "Weaving the Shamantic Web" the post-biological names "our ability, aided by computer technology, to move effortlessly through the infinities of cyberspace, while at the same time accommodating ourselves within the structures of the material world," and he asks whether art is "moving towards a post-biological re-materialization?" (ch.30) — a striking re-materialization that runs counter to a naive dematerialization narrative (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the originator and sole vault author of the term. A durable, recurring coinage spanning at least 1993–1999 (chs.25, 26, 30, 31, 32), used periodizing (the era after biological culture), programmatic (the curriculum of the Planetary Collegium), and ontological (emulation over imitation; "bionic evolution"; the body as "site of bionic transformation"). It anchors his late "moist" / technoetic vocabulary and the sibling concept [[The Post-Biological Body]].

## Related Concepts

- [[Posthumanism]] — adjacent but distinct: both name a condition after the unmodified human, but Ascott's accent is on co-evolution with artificial life and telematic consciousness rather than on the critique of the liberal humanist subject. Cross-linked, not merged.
- [[Artificial Life]] — the generative engine of the era ("the attempt is no longer to imitate life but to emulate it"); the new biological metaphor-set that displaces the 1980s physics/cosmology set.
- [[The Post-Biological Body]] — the sibling concept: the body redefined as "the site of bionic transformation"; the Net-extent that "defines us in telematic space just as the material body defined us in the old biological culture." Cross-linked, not merged.
- [[New Media Art]] — the post-biological era is the cultural epoch new-media/electronic art belongs to; this Ascott material EXTENDS that note.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — art as emulative, immersive, world-making experience for the post-biological participant; EXTENDED by this material.
- [[Cyborg Art]] — Burnham's 1968 "art of cybernetic organisms" anticipates Ascott's bionic / post-biological body and "smart architecture ... to support the realities of cyborg living."
- [[Telematic Art]] — the practice through which the post-biological subject is "distributed throughout the Internet," "both here and there, out-of-body and reembodied."
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the consciousness-side correlate: post-biological mind as the "world-mind," planetary self-awareness "arising from the technoetic Web."
- [[Emergence]] — the post-biological is figured as an emergent phenomenon (proto-organisms from rock pools; post-biological culture from data pools).
- [[The Cyborg]] — the post-biological body as a coupling of organism and machine; shared lineage with Haraway/Hayles.
- [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] — Burnham's name for the same impulse Ascott reframes as emulation: re-creating life by non-biological means.
- [[Re-enchantment]] — Ascott's post-biological era is "spiritually numinous" and technoetic, putting "the relationship between technology and consciousness ... on the agenda" — a re-enchantment register distinct from a purely secular posthumanism.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the era stands at the immaterial pole of this eight-author tension (Net-extent replacing the material body, output "fluid and immaterial"), yet Ascott complicates the opposition by asking whether art moves toward a "post-biological **re-materialization**" — moist, wet, biological re-embodiment rather than pure disembodiment. The relation is dialectical, not flat opposition.

## Questions It Raises

- Is "post-biological" a genuine ontological break or a rhetorical periodization — does anything actually cease to be biological, or is "moist" / "bionic" merely biology continued by other means?
- How does Ascott's "post-biological re-materialization" square with the dematerialization lineage (Lippard, Burnham)? Is the era a culmination of dematerialization or its reversal?
- If "the attempt is no longer to imitate life but to emulate it," what is the difference between emulation and imitation, and does artificial life actually achieve it?
- What "new responsibilities ... for our own bionic evolution" follow, and who holds them — is the post-biological a freedom or a new governance of the body?
- Does the post-biological era's "co-evolution with nature" temper or accelerate the Faustian, mastery-seeking drive Burnham diagnosed?
