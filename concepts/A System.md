# A System

## Brief Definition

A system is a set of interconnected things—elements, interconnections, and a function or purpose—organized in such a way that they produce their own pattern of behavior over time.

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows this is the vault's foundational native-discipline unit: the system understood not as a mere aggregate of parts but as a *whole that behaves*. Her opening definition is deliberately spare and inclusive: "A system is a set of things—people, cells, molecules, or whatever—interconnected in such a way that they produce their own pattern of behavior over time" (`Directly stated`). The load-bearing word is *interconnected*: a heap of sand is not a system, but a body, a football team, a market economy, or a university is, because its parts act on one another and the resulting behavior belongs to the whole rather than to any part.

Meadows resolves the definition into three constituents: "a system must consist of three kinds of things: elements, interconnections, and a function or purpose" (`Directly stated`). The elements are the visible parts (a digestive system's teeth and enzymes; a team's players and ball); the interconnections are the relationships and flows that bind them (the flow of food, the rules of the game); and the function or purpose is the least obvious yet often the most decisive constituent. This tripartite anatomy is developed in its own right in [[Elements Interconnections and Purpose]].

The system's most important property, for Meadows, is that it is a source of behavior, not merely a receiver of it: "The system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior!" An outside event may trigger a response, but "the same outside event applied to a different system is likely to produce a different result." This is why she insists that recessions, lost market share, and social outcomes are inherent in structure rather than caused by external villains—the claim carried by the concept [[Structure Determines Behavior]] (`Directly stated`).

A system also has identity and persistence. It "generally goes on being itself, changing only slowly if at all, even with complete substitutions of its elements—as long as its interconnections and purposes remain intact" (`Directly stated`). General Motors and the U.S. Congress remain themselves while every member changes; change the interconnections (football's rules to basketball's) or the purpose (winning to losing) and the system is transformed or destroyed. This is the primary-source grounding that the art and cybernetics books in the vault carry only obliquely—Burnham's shift from the [[Object (Burnham)|object]] to the system, and Hayles's account of [[Systems Theory]]—here stated in its home discipline (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — the primary-source, native-discipline definition: a set of elements interconnected by relationships and organized around a function or purpose, producing its own pattern of behavior over time. Distinguished from a mere collection by interconnection, and from a static structure by its self-generated behavior over time.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — approaches "system" from art history as the successor to the discrete art [[Object (Burnham)|object]] (his "systems esthetics"); his `System (Burnham)` definition is the art-world variant that Meadows's native-discipline account grounds. Kept as a distinct definition note.
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — carries the system obliquely through [[Systems Theory]] and second-order cybernetics (the observer inside the system); Meadows supplies the concrete object-level unit that Hayles's field-level account presupposes.

## Related Concepts

- [[Structure Determines Behavior]] — the central consequence: a system's behavior is produced by its own structure, not merely imposed from outside.
- [[Elements Interconnections and Purpose]] — the three constituents into which a system resolves.
- [[Stocks and Flows]] — the building blocks that give a system's structure its concrete, quantifiable form.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the interconnections through which a system regulates and drives its own behavior.
- [[Self-Organization]] — a system's capacity to elaborate its own structure and produce novelty from within (kept distinct from [[Emergence]] and [[Autopoiesis]]).
- [[Emergence]] — the whole exhibiting a pattern of behavior not reducible to its parts.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the field for which "a system" is the object of study.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object (Burnham)]] — the discrete, self-contained art object; Meadows's system is the whole-with-behavior that displaces the isolated thing, the object→system spine the art-cybernetics cluster turns on.

## Questions It Raises

- If a system "causes its own behavior," where does responsibility lie when a system produces harm—in the elements, the interconnections, or the purpose?
- When elements can be wholly replaced while the system persists, in what does a system's identity actually consist?
- Meadows says asking which constituent is "most important" is "an unsystemic question"—so how does one act on a system without privileging one constituent over the others?
- Where is the boundary of a system drawn, and who draws it?
