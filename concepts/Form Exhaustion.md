# Form Exhaustion

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's claim that sculptural form is "a decidedly finite grouping" whose biotic and formalist stock is used up once the archetypes of a class have been repeatedly worked — so that the vitalist idiom died not by attack but by running out of new forms.

## Longer Explanation

Form exhaustion is the mechanism by which, in Burnham's account, the two governing programs of twentieth-century sculpture — abstract [[Vitalism]] and "formal-reductivism" — reached their end around 1960. The argument is framed as a direct contradiction of Henri Focillon's promise, in *La Vie des formes*, that art enjoys an inexhaustible morphogenetic fertility (see [[The Life of Forms (Focillon)]]). Where Focillon held that form begets progeny endlessly, Burnham counters that "no artistic analogue to biotic generation of form types can assure art of an infinite supply of new forms," because "sculptural form and its varieties constitute a decidedly finite grouping." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 2.)

The mechanism is depletion through repetition. Abstract vitalistic sculpture "has been dependent on form possibilities"; once those possibilities were used up, "the idiom was finished." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 2.) Because vitalist sculpture drew its repertoire from a bounded stock of biotic archetypes (the seed, the bone, the embryo, the totem), each succeeding generation could only re-work an inheritance already optimized by its innovators — Brancusi, Moore, Arp — and so "each succeeding generation of sculptors has had to answer the charge of imitation-without too much success." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 3.) Burnham reads the proliferation of biomorphic "totemistic or insectile cliches," culminating in the *reductio ad absurdum* of Sam Goodman's *No-Sculptures*, as the symptom of an idiom that had exhausted its means while continuing to mass-produce them.

The same logic governs formalism. The avant-garde's belief that formalism would mature into a "new language of vision" was realized, "but what was not anticipated was that the formalist vocabulary, in order to maintain its vigor, must constantly change and grow. This has happened only marginally." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 3.) In Chapter 4 Burnham generalizes the diagnosis: "since 1960 the vitalist and formal-reductivist tendencies of sculpture have approached exhaustion," and "in a world of invisible values formalism has quickly become a language with a gradually contracting spectrum of possibilities and variations." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 4.) Crucially, the cause is not merely internal fatigue but a change in the world: a science of "invisible values" (wave mechanics, electronic circuitry, statistical formulae) can no longer be mirrored by a strictly visual-geometrical art.

Form exhaustion is therefore the hinge of the whole book. As the visible stock of forms ran out, "invention, the creative force which has propelled formalism, has succeeded in pushing 'sculpture' further and further out of the scope of its original domain." (`Directly stated`, Ch. 4.) That expulsion from the object's "original domain" motivates the succession announced in the rest of Part I — the turn toward [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]], where the artwork stages the *experience* of perception rather than supplying a new form — and prepares the larger migration of Part II ("Sculpture as System"), where life is no longer *figured* in a finite repertoire of biotic shapes but *simulated* through [[Kinetic Art]], [[Cybernetics]], and [[Systems Theory]]. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — coins and develops the idea across the closing chapters of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*'s Part I. Form is a finite stock; the vitalist idiom dies by depletion, not refutation; formalism contracts in a world of invisible values; the result drives sculpture out of the object and toward system. The claim is explicitly framed *against* Focillon's infinite-supply optimism (see [[Jack Burnham and Henri Focillon]]).
- **[[Henri Focillon]]** *(forward-stub — the opposed source)* — in *La Vie des formes* holds that forms propagate endlessly, "form begets progeny," guaranteeing art a perpetually renewing supply. This is precisely the promise Burnham denies.

## Related Concepts

- [[Vitalism]] — the idiom whose exhaustion form exhaustion explains; vitalism's dependence on a bounded stock of biotic "form possibilities" is what makes it exhaustible.
- [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] — the successor program; with the form-stock depleted, sculpture shifts from making new forms to staging perceptual experience.
- [[Kinetic Art]] — the first movement of Part II ("Sculpture as System"); once biotic forms are exhausted, life is simulated through real-time motion rather than figured in static shape.
- [[Dematerialization]] (Lippard) — a parallel, slightly later thesis; Burnham's sculptural "gravitation toward seeming immateriality" predates and prefigures Lippard's dematerialization of the art object. Distinct theses, cross-linked not merged.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — form exhaustion is one driver of the vault's master axis: the depletion of substantial form is part of sculpture's drift toward "seeming immateriality."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Life of Forms (Focillon)]] — the direct antithesis: Focillon's morphogenetic promise of an infinite supply of forms, which Burnham names and rejects ("Contrary to what Focillon has written…").

## Questions It Raises

- Is the finitude of form a property of *sculpture* (the bounded repertoire of solid geometry and biotic archetypes), or of any medium — and would a medium of "invisible values" (electronic, systemic, informational) escape exhaustion by no longer dealing in discrete forms at all?
- If the vitalist idiom died by depletion rather than refutation, is "exhaustion" an aesthetic verdict or a historical one — and could a stock of forms be *re-opened* by a later culture, as Focillon's morphogenesis would predict?
- Does the move from finite *form* to simulated *life* (kinetic, cybernetic, system art) genuinely solve exhaustion, or merely relocate it from the repertoire of shapes to the repertoire of behaviors?
- How closely does Burnham's "gravitation toward seeming immateriality" map onto Lippard's later dematerialization — convergent diagnoses of the same 1960s pressure, or distinct mechanisms (form-stock depletion vs. the conceptual displacement of the object)?
