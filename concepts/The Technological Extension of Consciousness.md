# The Technological Extension of Consciousness

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's escalation of his core thesis — that media are extensions of the human body — to its terminal stage: in the electric age the medium that gets extended is no longer a sense or a limb but *consciousness itself*, as humans are "translated more and more into the form of information" and move toward transferring consciousness to the computer and fusing the globe into "a single consciousness."

## Longer Explanation

In *Understanding Media*, McLuhan reads every technology as an extension of some bodily organ or faculty — the wheel extends the foot, print extends the eye, electricity extends the central nervous system. Chapter 6, "Media as Translators," takes the decisive step beyond the body's parts to its interior. Because all media "translate" experience from one form into another, and because the electric medium is "total and inclusive" rather than "partial and fragmentary," the electric age translates the human being *as such* into information: "In this electric age we see ourselves being translated more and more into the form of information, moving toward the technological extension of consciousness" (`Directly stated`). The extension thesis has reached the one faculty the earlier chapters left untouched.

McLuhan then names the operation that makes this thinkable. The "common sense" — the old power of integrating the senses into a single unified image — can in the computer age be re-engineered: "it is now possible to program ratios among the senses that approach the condition of consciousness." Having already "extended or translated our central nervous system into the electromagnetic technology," McLuhan argues, "it is but a further stage to transfer our consciousness to the computer world as well," and beyond Mallarmé's book "we are now in a position to go beyond that and to transfer the entire show to the memory of a computer" (`Directly stated`). The chapter closes on the planetary terminus: the "translation of our entire lives into the spiritual form of information" might "make of the entire globe, and of the human family, a single consciousness" — the consciousness-side of [[The Global Village]] (`Directly stated`).

McLuhan supplies the idea's longer arc across two further chapters. Chapter 8 ("The Spoken Word") locates the *first* technical extension of consciousness in speech itself — "this technical extension of consciousness that is speech" — which "extends and amplifies man but it also divides his faculties," diminishing the prior "collective consciousness or intuitive awareness." Electricity then promises to undo that fall: it "points the way to an extension of the process of consciousness itself, on a world scale, and without any verbalization whatever" — a post-verbal "cosmic consciousness" (`Directly stated`). Chapter 13 ("Housing") gives the thesis its near-future date and its bodily reversal: the same electric technology that obsolesces houses ("global thermostatic controls") and language ("consciousness-without-walls") effects "an immediate simulation of consciousness" by "massive extrasensory perception," and McLuhan flatly predicts that "such an extension of the process of consciousness by electric simulation may easily occur in the 1960s" (`Directly stated`).

The value of the concept to the vault is that McLuhan supplies, in 1964, the founding *gesture* that later authors take up from sharply opposed stances. Three of them **literalize or universalize** what McLuhan offers as poetic-technological destiny. [[Ray Kurzweil]] reframes the "transfer" of consciousness to the computer as an engineering roadmap ([[Mind Uploading]], [[Patternism]]): we are a pattern of information that can be re-instantiated in a faster substrate, and biology will be transcended. [[Yuval Noah Harari]] universalizes McLuhan's "single consciousness" and "spiritual form of information" into a cosmology, [[Dataism]] — the cosmos as one data-processing system into which the human dissolves. [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] reads the same "spiritual form of information" and its Pentecostal promise of "universal understanding and unity" as a theology in disguise, the migration of religious concepts into the language of computation that produces [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]]. Against these stands the lone **critical counter-reading**: [[N. Katherine Hayles]] argues that the very idea consciousness could be "transferred" presupposes that information is a disembodied pattern separable from its material substrate — a contingent, ideologically loaded move *How We Became Posthuman* sets out to historicize and resist; for her there is no disembodiment, only different embodiment. The dividing line is the body: McLuhan, Kurzweil, Harari, and O'Gieblyn read the human translated into information as metamorphosis, cosmology, or destiny, while Hayles reads it as a contingent erasure of the flesh. The concept therefore sits on one pole of [[Embodiment vs Information]] and contributes a McLuhan reading to [[Material vs Immaterial]] (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — the originating use. Consciousness is the final and most "total" organ to be extended; the electric medium translates the whole human into information and moves toward literally transferring consciousness to the computer and unifying humanity into "a single consciousness." Speech was the first, dividing extension of consciousness; electricity is the reunifying, post-verbal one. McLuhan frames this as metamorphosis and "the golden age," not loss (`Directly stated`).
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — the engineering literalization. What McLuhan offers as metaphor ("transfer our consciousness to the computer world") Kurzweil reframes as a roadmap: [[Mind Uploading]] and [[Patternism]] — we are a pattern of information that can be re-instantiated in a faster substrate, and biology will be transcended (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — the cosmological universalization. McLuhan's "single consciousness" / "the spiritual form of information" anticipates [[Dataism]]: the cosmos as one data-processing system into which the human dissolves (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — the theological diagnosis. McLuhan's "spiritual form of information" and his Pentecostal "universal understanding and unity" prefigure the modern conversion of information into [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]] — the migration of religious concepts into the language of computation (`Interpretive synthesis`).
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — the critical counter-reading. The dream that consciousness can be "transferred" presupposes that information is a disembodied pattern separable from its material substrate — exactly the contingent, ideologically loaded move *How We Became Posthuman* sets out to historicize and resist; for Hayles there is no disembodiment, only different embodiment (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the parent thesis; this concept is its escalation from organ/limb to consciousness.
- [[Media as Translators]] — the mechanism: media "translate" experience between forms, so the human can be translated "into the form of information."
- [[The Global Village]] — the spatial face of the same process; "a single consciousness" is its inner face.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — programming "ratios among the senses" to "approach the condition of consciousness" is the technical operation underwriting the transfer.
- [[Mind Uploading]] — Kurzweil's literalization of "transfer our consciousness to the computer world."
- [[The Body as Data]] — "translated more and more into the form of information"; the human rendered as information.
- [[Dataism]] — Harari's worldview that universalizes "the spiritual form of information" into a cosmology.
- [[Disembodied Information as the Soul]] — O'Gieblyn's diagnosis of information reconceived as soul, presaged by McLuhan's "spiritual form of information."
- [[Patternism]] — Kurzweil's claim that the transferable self is a pattern of information.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the standing objection (Hayles): consciousness is not a substrate-independent pattern that can be "transferred," so the extension thesis, pushed to consciousness, smuggles in a disembodiment that the body refuses.

## Questions It Raises

- Is McLuhan describing an *extension* of consciousness (consciousness amplified, still ours) or its *transfer* (consciousness relocated, possibly without us)? His own phrasing slides between the two within a single paragraph.
- If speech was the *dividing* extension of consciousness and electricity the *reunifying* one, is the "single consciousness" a recovery of a lost tribal whole or the manufacture of something new?
- When McLuhan calls information "the spiritual form" of human life, is this metaphor or, as O'Gieblyn would press, a covert theology?
- Does "programming ratios among the senses that approach the condition of consciousness" describe simulation of consciousness or its genuine reproduction — and does McLuhan need to settle the difference for his thesis to hold?
