# Systems Consciousness

## Brief Definition

Burnham's term for the new aesthetic awareness that replaces the cultural fixation on the art object — a refocusing of perception from the direct shaping of matter to the organizing of quantities of energy and information, and to the relationships within and beyond the visible "skin" of objects.

## Longer Explanation

In the closing pages of "Robot and Cyborg Art" (ch. 8), Jack Burnham names the cultural shift he has been tracking across the whole book: "the cultural obsession with the art object is slowly disappearing and being replaced by what might be called 'systems consciousness.'" The shift is from making solid artifacts to managing flows — "from the direct shaping of matter to a concern for organizing quantities of energy and information." Where traditional sculpture asked the viewer to attend to the surface and contour of a discrete thing, systems consciousness "prompt[s] us not to look at the 'skin' of objects, but at those meaningful relationships within and beyond their visible boundaries" (`Directly stated`). It is, in Burnham's words, "a refocusing of aesthetic awareness" — a change in what perception is trained on, not merely a new style of object.

Burnham grounds this awareness in the cybernetic and systems-biological worldview he develops earlier in the chapter (via Wiener, Bertalanffy, Cannon, and Ashby): that "everyone lives intermeshed daily with a hierarchy of synthetic systems," "the new biological reality which both man and art must abide by." Systems consciousness is the perceptual correlate of that reality — the capacity to "see" the matter-energy-information exchanges that the body and its environment normally hide from us. Following Landers, Burnham notes that "we do not 'see' how we exchange chemicals with our environment; therefore, we do not view the organism as part of a larger system"; systems consciousness is precisely the dawning ability to sense those "unseen relationships between man-machine-environment" (`Strongly implied`).

Crucially, Burnham frames this not as an art-historical preference but as an evolutionary necessity. "The steady infusion of systems consciousness into three-dimensional art will, temporarily at least, be regarded as no less than a biological survival mechanism." Because technology has made "the total environment less habitable" while rendering humans "increasingly responsible for [their] own existence within a maze of artificial systems," the perceptual habit of seeing systems becomes a condition of survival. "By rendering the invisible visible through systems consciousness, we are beginning to accept responsibility for the well-being and continued existence of life upon the Earth." Systems consciousness thus carries an ethical charge — Burnham even speculates it may have "some aspects of Kant's moral imperative" — and a self-critical reflexivity: "greatly increasing general systems consciousness may soon convince us that our technologies are unsound" (`Directly stated`).

Systems consciousness should be distinguished from [[Systems Esthetics]], the art *program* Burnham would name in his 1968 essay: systems consciousness is the broad cultural and biological *awareness* (a change in perception and responsibility), whereas systems esthetics is the specific artistic methodology that follows from it. The two are continuous — the latter is the studio-and-gallery realization of the former — but the concept here is the wider perceptual ground (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — coins "systems consciousness" in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968, ch. 8) as the successor to object-fixation: the redirection of aesthetic awareness from solid artifacts to energy/information flows and to relations within and beyond the "skin" of objects, framed as a biological survival mechanism that renders the invisible visible.

## Related Concepts

- [[Systems Esthetics]] — the art program that operationalizes this awareness; systems consciousness is the broader cultural/biological ground, systems esthetics the studio methodology.
- [[Object and System]] — the very shift systems consciousness performs: away from the discrete art object, toward the system as the unit of analysis.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the Bertalanffy lineage Burnham draws on; systems consciousness is the perceptual uptake of the systems worldview.
- [[Cybernetics]] — "preeminently the science of organization"; supplies the feedback/coupling/communication vocabulary that systems consciousness learns to perceive.
- [[Information]] — one of the "quantities" systems consciousness attends to in place of matter ("organizing quantities of energy and information").
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the relational mechanism (negative/positive feedback, homeostasis) that systems consciousness makes visible "within and beyond" objects.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the chapter's culminating art form; cyborg/Post-Kinetic work is where systems consciousness is enacted, the human-machine system as art.
- [[Dematerialization]] — Lippard's parallel diagnosis; both name the eclipse of the solid art object, Burnham via systems flows, Lippard via the idea/document.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — systems consciousness is defined *against* the object pole: "the cultural obsession with the art object is slowly disappearing." It opposes the "form-shaping approaches" and the attention to the "skin" of objects that traditional sculpture demanded.

## Questions It Raises

- If systems consciousness is a "biological survival mechanism," is it primarily an aesthetic category at all, or an ecological/ethical one that merely passes through art?
- Can perception of "matter-energy-information exchanges" ever be fully achieved, or does the "skin" of objects keep reasserting itself? What would art that fully dissolved the object look like?
- How does Burnham's optimism that systems consciousness lets us "accept responsibility for the well-being and continued existence of life upon the Earth" sit beside his fear that technology is "self-aggrandizing" and "self-propelled" — does seeing systems give us control, or only show us our entrapment?
- Is systems consciousness compatible with [[Dematerialization]]'s later verdict that the dematerialized object "failed to escape the market," or does the system simply become the new commodity?
