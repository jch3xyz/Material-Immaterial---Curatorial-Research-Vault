# The Content Obsession

## Brief Definition

The vault's name for McLuhan's diagnosis of the habitual fixation on a medium's *content* — its program, message, or payload — which blinds us to the medium's own form and structural effects; the "conventional response to all media, namely that it is how they are used that counts," which McLuhan calls "the numb stance of the technological idiot."

## Longer Explanation

The content obsession is the negative corollary of [[The Medium Is the Message]]. McLuhan's whole argument turns on the claim that a medium's consequences flow from the new scale, pace, and pattern it introduces, "quite independent of the freight or content" it carries. The reflex that resists this — that locates a technology's meaning in *what is done with it* — is for McLuhan not a harmless common-sense position but the very mechanism by which media work on us unexamined. "Our conventional response to all media, namely that it is how they are used that counts, is the numb stance of the technological idiot" (Directly stated, Ch. 1). He identifies the same reflex in General Sarnoff's neutral-instrument creed — "The products of modern science are not in themselves good or bad; it is the way they are used that determines their value" — which he dismisses as "the voice of the current somnambulism."

The reason the obsession is so effective is structural: "the 'content' of any medium is always another medium" (the content of writing is speech, of print the written word, of the telegraph print — see [[The Content of Any Medium Is Another Medium]]). Because that nested content engrosses attention, the carrying medium itself slips past notice. McLuhan states the consequence flatly: "it is only too typical that the 'content' of any medium blinds us to the character of the medium." This is the burglar's-meat structure (see the metaphor [[The Burglar's Meat]]): the content is "like the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind," and "the effect of the medium is made strong and intense just because it is given another medium as 'content.'" Studying only the content is therefore the surest way to fail to study media at all.

The limiting case that makes the point unmistakable is [[The Electric Light]], which McLuhan calls "pure information" — "a medium without a message, as it were, unless it is used to spell out some verbal ad or name." Precisely because it has no content, "the electric light escapes attention as a communication medium just because it has no 'content,'" and so becomes "an invaluable instance of how people fail to study media at all." Only when the light is bent into a brand name is it noticed at all — and then "it is not the light but the 'content' (or what is really another medium) that is noticed" (Directly stated, Ch. 1). The electric light thus exposes the obsession by removing its food: a medium with nothing to fixate on still produces radical, pervasive, decentralized effects.

Because all media are [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — extensions whose alteration of our sense ratios proceeds "steadily and without any resistance" — the content obsession is not merely an intellectual error but a perceptual anesthesia. It keeps attention on the payload while the extension reshapes the one who attends.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — *Understanding Media* (1964). McLuhan names the fixation on content as the chief obstacle to perceiving any medium and as the means by which a medium's effect is intensified unnoticed. The obsession is the standing target of the book: he returns to it across the case studies, diagnosing the "'content' obsession of the man of print culture" as the very thing that makes media-form invisible to him.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Medium Is the Message]] — the corrective stance the content obsession blocks; where the obsession says the use is what counts, the thesis says the medium's form is the message.

## Questions It Raises

- If attending to content is "the numb stance of the technological idiot," is there any legitimate place for asking *what* a medium carries — for the politics or ethics of content — or does McLuhan's framing concede the entire field of use too readily?
- Does the electric-light case really generalize? A "contentless" medium may be the exception that proves the rule, or a special case from which little follows for media saturated with content.
- If the content always "blinds us to the character of the medium," what method could ever pierce the blindness — and is McLuhan's own analysis exempt from the obsession it diagnoses?
- How does the content obsession relate to later accounts in which the *quantity* and *pattern* of information (Han, Hayles, Harari) is itself the decisive force — is data a "content" McLuhan would warn us not to fixate on, or has it become the medium?
