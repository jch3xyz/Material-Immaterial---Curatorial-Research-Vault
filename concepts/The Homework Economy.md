# The Homework Economy

## Brief Definition

The **homework economy** is a term [[Donna Haraway]] borrows from economist Richard Gordon and enlarges: the worldwide restructuring of work so that paid labor broadly takes on "the characteristics formerly ascribed to female jobs" — vulnerable, disassemblable, deskilled, valued as *server* rather than *worker* — "whether performed by men or women."

## Longer Explanation

The term is **Gordon's**; the conceptual expansion is **Haraway's**. In "The Homework Economy 'Outside the Home'" (ch. 4 of *A Cyborg Manifesto*) she is explicit about the provenance: "Richard Gordon has called this new situation the 'homework economy.'" Gordon's literal referent is "the phenomenon of literal homework emerging in connection with electronics assembly," but Haraway stresses that "Gordon intends homework economy to name a restructuring of work that broadly has the characteristics formerly ascribed to female jobs, jobs literally done only by women. Work is being redefined as both literally female and feminized, whether performed by men or women." `Directly stated`. The decisive move is the de-coupling of *feminization* from biological women: the homework economy names a general condition of late-twentieth-century labor under the "New Industrial Revolution," made possible (Haraway is careful: "made possible by (not caused by)") by the new communications technologies and the international division of labor.

**Feminization** is the defining operation of the homework economy, and Haraway gives it a precise, brutal content. "To be feminized means to be made extremely vulnerable; able to be disassembled, reassembled, exploited as a reserve labor force; seen less as workers than as servers; subjected to time arrangements on and off the paid job that make a mockery of a limited workday; leading an existence that always borders on being obscene, out of place, and reducible to sex." `Directly stated`. Feminization is thus a structural position, not a sex: it is what happens to labor when factory, home, and market "are integrated on a new scale," when "deskilling is an old strategy newly applicable to formerly privileged workers," and when "relatively privileged, mostly white, men's unionized jobs" are dissolved by technologies "to integrate and control labor despite extensive dispersion and decentralization." `Directly stated`. In ch. 5, "Women in the Integrated Circuit," Haraway operationalizes the concept across her idealized loci — Home, Market, Paid Workplace, State, School, Clinic-Hospital, Church — where the **Paid Workplace** entry registers "development of new time arrangements to facilitate the homework economy (flex time, part time, over time, no time)" and concludes that "most labor [is] 'marginal' or 'feminized,'" while the **Home** locus catalogues "paid homework, reemergence of home sweatshops, home-based businesses and telecommuting, electronic cottage industry." `Directly stated`.

**The feminization of poverty** is the homework economy's consequence for daily life. As the welfare state collapses, demands on women to "sustain daily life for themselves as well as for men, children, and old people" intensify. "The feminization of poverty—generated by dismantling the welfare state, by the homework economy where stable jobs become the exception, and sustained by the expectation that women's wages will not be matched by a male income for the support of children—has become an urgent focus." `Directly stated`. Haraway situates this inside a periodizing scheme borrowed in part from Fredric Jameson: "three major stages of capitalism (commercial/early industrial, monopoly, multinational)" correlated with "three forms of family" — the patriarchal nuclear family, the welfare-state-mediated modern family, and "the 'family' of the homework economy with its oxymoronic structure of women-headed households and its explosion of feminisms and the paradoxical intensification and erosion of gender itself." `Directly stated`. The homework economy is therefore the labor-form proper to multinational capital, with its own distinctive (dis)order of kinship.

**The bimodal social structure** is the homework economy's political danger, and it is here that the concept touches the surveillance cluster. Haraway names "the formation of a strongly bimodal social structure, with the masses of women and men of all ethnic groups, but especially people of color, confined to a homework economy, illiteracy of several varieties, and general redundancy and impotence, controlled by high-tech repressive apparatuses ranging from entertainment to surveillance and disappearance." `Directly stated`. The bimodal society splits into "highly educated, numerous elites" and a feminized mass managed not only by deprivation but by control technologies — a diagnosis that runs parallel to the surveillance apparatus of Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and the dispersed, decentralized regime of the [[Society of Control]]. Haraway's "surveillance and disappearance" is the homework economy's enforcement mechanism: the same decentralization that disassembles labor also instruments it. `Interpretive synthesis` (the cross-link to the surveillance cluster is the vault's; Haraway's text states the bimodal danger and its "high-tech repressive apparatuses" directly).

Read forward, the homework economy is a **precursor to Han's [[Immaterial Labour]]**. Where Haraway (1985) sees work redefined as "literally female and feminized" — released from the limited workday, reducible to the whole person, controlled by communications technologies "despite extensive dispersion and decentralization" — Han's later diagnosis of immaterial production names the same dissolution of the worker into a self-exploiting, always-on, whole-person productivity. Haraway frames it as the feminization of *all* labor and locates the burden on women and people of color; Han frames it as the auto-exploitation of the lone neoliberal entrepreneur and largely de-genders it. The lineage is conceptual, not citational — Han does not cite Haraway. `Speculative connection`.

## Authors and Variants

- **Richard Gordon** (economist; cited, not a vault note) — coined "homework economy" to name a restructuring of work with the characteristics formerly ascribed to female jobs, including the literal homework of electronics assembly.
- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — borrows Gordon's term and enlarges it into a general thesis about the feminization of labor under multinational capital: feminization as a structural position ("whether performed by men or women"), the feminization of poverty, the bimodal social structure, and the three-stages-of-capitalism / three-forms-of-family schema.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (precursor relation, not a variant) — his [[Immaterial Labour]] re-describes a kindred restructuring (the whole person installed in production, the body released from production, dispersed control) without the gendered framing or the citation of Haraway.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — the homework economy is the labor-economic face of Haraway's "informatics of domination"; the feminized, disassemblable worker is a cyborg subject.
- [[Informatics]] — the "new communications technologies" that make the homework economy possible are the same informatic substrate; Haraway's "integrated circuit" is its spatial figure.
- [[Immaterial Labour]] — Han's later, de-gendered descendant: production of the immaterial, the body freed from production, the integral person conscripted.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — the "surveillance and disappearance" that enforces the bimodal structure; the homework economy's control apparatus.
- [[Society of Control]] — decentralized, dispersed control "despite extensive dispersion and decentralization" rhymes with the post-disciplinary control diagnosis.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the homework economy sits on the immaterial-labor side of this tension: work redefined away from the production of material objects toward dispersed, informatic, server-not-worker activity.

## Questions It Raises

- If feminization is a structural position rather than a sex ("whether performed by men or women"), what is lost — analytically and politically — when Han later de-genders the same diagnosis as auto-exploitation?
- Does the "surveillance and disappearance" that enforces the bimodal structure make the homework economy a surveillance regime in its own right, or is surveillance merely one of several "high-tech repressive apparatuses"?
- Haraway insists the homework economy is "made possible by (not caused by)" the new technologies — what work does this refusal of technological determinism do, and can the precursor relation to Han's immaterial labour survive it?
- Is the "oxymoronic structure of women-headed households" a transitional family form, or the durable kinship of multinational capital?
