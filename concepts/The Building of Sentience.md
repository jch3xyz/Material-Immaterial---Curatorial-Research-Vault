# The Building of Sentience

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for a *moist architecture* whose paradigmatic change is registered at the level of behaviour rather than form — a building that thinks for itself, perceives, learns, evolves, and self-organises, so that "engineering will embrace ontology" and "system more significant than structure."

## Longer Explanation

In the "Moist Manifesto" chapter ("Art @ the Edge of the Net," ch. 31), Ascott extends his lifelong object→behaviour argument from art into architecture. The shift he forecasts is not stylistic but ontological: the twentieth century's "exaggerated interest in what a building looks like, its mere appearance" gives way to "a concern with the quality of its gaze, how it sees us, how it perceives our needs." The building stops being a static form to be looked at and becomes a perceiving, responsive participant — its appearance subordinated to its conduct. (`Directly stated`)

This is condensed in two slogans. First, "the paradigmatic change in architecture will be registered at the level of behaviour rather than form." Second, the consequence: "Engineering will embrace ontology. Time will become more dominant than space, system more significant than structure." Form, space, and structure — the classical Euclidean coordinates of building — are demoted beneath behaviour, time, and system. Design becomes "a bottom-up process," and "seeding" matters as much as designing: the architect cultivates an emergent, self-organising process rather than fixing a finished object. (`Directly stated`)

The endpoint is sentience itself: "The goal will be the building of sentience, a moist architecture that has a life of its own, that thinks for itself, feeds itself, takes care of itself, repairs itself, plans its future, copes with adversity." Such a building is "as much emotional as instrumental, as intuitive as ordered"; its artificial neural networks are to be "synaptic" with our own. In the companion glossary (ch. 32), Ascott reduces this to the term *Smart architecture*: "To support the realities of cyborg living, the distributed self, and our technoetic ecology, architecture will have to become more conscious, anticipatory, and responsive." (`Directly stated`)

Structurally, this is Burnham's object→system thesis restated in an architectural register thirty-two years later. Where Burnham argued in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* that "a base-less sculpture ceases to be an object and becomes a physical system of forces" and that "for Object Art presence is everything, for Systems Art information is the key," Ascott applies the identical logic to the built environment: presence/appearance gives way to behaviour/information, the building dematerialised from object into living system. The concept therefore sits on the same `Object and System` spine that organises the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` tension, now pushed into Ascott's biological-and-spiritual ("moist") frame. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins "the building of sentience" / "moist architecture" (ch. 31) and the glossary entry "Smart architecture" (ch. 32). The building is to be sentient, self-organising, post-biological, and continuous with the planetary technoetic network ("our brain will invade the city and the city will enter our brain"). The shift is from form/appearance to behaviour/gaze, from structure to system.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** *(precursor, not cited by Ascott)* — *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) supplies the underlying move: sculpture's evolution from autonomous object to "physical system of forces," from presence to information. Ascott's architecture-of-behaviour is the same object→system logic in a new medium. See [[Object and System]], [[Systems Esthetics]].

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object vs System]] — the building of sentience is the *resolution* of this tension toward the system pole: the building ceases to be an object (form, appearance, structure) and becomes a behaving system (perception, time, information).

## Questions It Raises

- If a building's worth is its behaviour rather than its form, what becomes of architecture as a visual and material discipline — does "the quality of its gaze" replace the façade as the unit of judgment?
- Is "engineering will embrace ontology" a literal program (sentient, self-aware buildings) or a metaphor for responsiveness — and where, in `Artificial Life` / consciousness terms, would the line fall?
- A building "that thinks for itself… plans its future" and "gets into our minds" is also a building that surveils ("how it sees us"). Does Ascott's optimistic sentient architecture invite the very control that Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] diagnoses?
- Does the move from structure to system genuinely dematerialise architecture (as it did sculpture for Burnham), or does a self-feeding, self-repairing building simply re-materialise the digital as ever more matter — the [[Material vs Immaterial]] question in built form?
