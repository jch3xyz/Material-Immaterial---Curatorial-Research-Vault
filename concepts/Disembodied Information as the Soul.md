# Disembodied Information as the Soul

## Brief Definition

The theological reading of cybernetics' founding move: when information was decoupled from matter and treated as an immaterial, substrate-independent quantity, it became — in O'Gieblyn's phrase — "the materialist's substitute for the soul," supplying secular thought with a transcendent essence that survives the body.

## Longer Explanation

O'Gieblyn locates the origin of this idea in the historical decontextualization of information that [[N. Katherine Hayles]] traces in *How We Became Posthuman*. The earliest cyberneticists, O'Gieblyn notes, "understood that information processing had to be instantiated in a particular context and embodied in some kind of physical form." But over the [[The Three Waves of Cybernetics|Macy Conferences]] (1946–1953), as the ambition to make cybernetics a universal metaphor grew, "context began to seem like a hindrance." [[Claude Shannon]]'s information theory stripped information of meaning, and during the conferences information "became decoupled from matter," reconceived as "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates." The result was an understanding of information that was "practically immaterial, 'a mathematical quantity weightless as sunshine, moving in a rarefied realm of pure probability, not tied down to bodies or material instantiations.'" `Directly stated`

The decisive move, for O'Gieblyn, is the theological one. An idea engineered precisely to dispense with metaphysics circles back to it: a weightless, meaning-stripped, body-independent essence is structurally identical to the soul of the tradition that materialism claimed to have left behind. "It became, in other words, the materialist's substitute for the soul." `Directly stated` This is why the [[Mind as Machine|computer metaphor]] — "an analogy that was expressly designed to avoid the notion of a metaphysical soul" — "has returned to us these ancient religious ideas about physical transcendence and the disembodied spirit." `Directly stated`

The hinge to transhumanism follows directly. Transcendence, in the work of [[Ray Kurzweil]] and others, depends on the premise that the self is a [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern]] of information rather than the perishable hardware that instantiates it — and that such a pattern, "essentially computational," can be transferred onto another substrate. O'Gieblyn frames the stakes in Hayles's terms: "Within transhumanist thought, transcendence depends on the notion that information can be liberated from the material constraints of the physical world: it is an ideology, as the critic N. Katherine Hayles points out, in which 'disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form.'" `Directly stated` Here [[The Computational Universe|the universe-as-computation]] picture and the Platonic dream of an eternal Form converge: information is not merely abstracted from the body but exalted above it, the truest and most permanent layer of reality.

O'Gieblyn's own reflective testimony makes the soul-substitution concrete. The appeal of the pattern theory, she writes, was not superpowers or even immortality but "the notion that my interior life was somehow real — that the purely subjective experience that I had once believed to be my soul was not some ghostly illusion but a process that contained an essential and irreducible identity." Transhumanists, she observes, "do not believe in the soul, but they subscribe to a concept that is not so dissimilar." The ironic underside, which she names elsewhere in the chapter, is that this Platonic rhetoric "obscures what mind-uploading actually entails" — a possibly experience-less copy — so that "the soul is already so illusory that it will not be missed if it doesn't survive the leap into the great digital beyond." `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — Reads the disembodiment-of-information thesis theologically. The decoupling of information from matter at the Macy Conferences produced a "materialist's substitute for the soul"; transhumanist transcendence is its eschatology, and the resemblance to Christian resurrection is "historical and genetic," not coincidental.
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — Supplies the source diagnosis O'Gieblyn quotes: information was made to "flow unchanged between different material substrates," became "weightless as sunshine," and in transhumanist ideology "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form." For Hayles this is a contingent construction to be resisted — see [[Information Lost Its Body]] and [[The Materiality of Informatics]].
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — The "patternist" for whom consciousness is a pattern of information that "does not reside in the hardware of our brains" and can therefore, "at least in theory, be transferred onto a computer." The soul-substitute made operational as mind-uploading.
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** — Source of the founding image O'Gieblyn cites: "we are but whirlpools in a river of ever-flowing water. We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves" — pattern as the locus of identity over matter.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment]] — the thesis that selfhood is inseparable from a material, situated body; disembodied information as soul is precisely its negation.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — denies that information can be weightless or substrate-free; treats the "soul" of weightless information as a contingent fiction.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — shares the prior of a disembodied essence, yet Hayles argues both the liberal subject and the posthuman pattern-self erase the body; an uneasy ally rather than a clean opposite.

## Questions It Raises

- If information is "weightless as sunshine" only because the cyberneticists chose to abstract away context, is the disembodied soul of transhumanism an artifact of a methodological convenience rather than a discovery about the self? (the body as data)
- Can a materialism that needs an immaterial, body-independent essence to secure identity still call itself materialism — or has it quietly reinstated the very dualism it claimed to dissolve? (meaning, posthumanism)
- If Shannon's information is meaning-stripped, what exactly is being preserved when a "soul" is uploaded — pattern without significance? Does transcendence here smuggle meaning back in, or expose its absence? (meaning)
- When perception, memory, and attention are reconceived as a transferable algorithm, does the resulting self-model alter how we experience our own embodiment in advance of any actual upload? (technology and perception)
- O'Gieblyn warns the Platonic rhetoric "obscures what mind-uploading actually entails." If the uploaded copy may lack subjective experience, does the soul-substitute deliver the soul or only its outward behavior? (posthumanism)
