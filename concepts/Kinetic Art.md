# Kinetic Art

## Brief Definition

For Jack Burnham, Kinetic Art is "a gradual attempt throughout this century to produce nonrepresentational art using the parameters of real time and motion" — the first major movement of sculpture conceived as system rather than object.

## Longer Explanation

Kinetic Art is the keystone concept of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*'s sixth chapter and the first art form Burnham treats fully as a passage from object to system. Its minimal definition is deliberately spare: "Most specifically, Kinetic Art has been a gradual attempt throughout this century to produce nonrepresentational art using the parameters of real time and motion." It is distinguished from the older art of [[Automata]] precisely by its emphasis: "The word kinetic is important because it decidedly places the emphasis for this new art on motion and its effects-not intelligence, anthropomorphism, temporal sequence, or coordination, all aspects of the older art of automata." (`Directly stated`)

Burnham roots Kineticism in the rationalization of the machine that governed the first industrial revolution. Its prehistory is "the contemporary artistic recognition of the drive toward total mechanization; it is the celebration of what Franz Reuleaux, in defining the machine, called the 'closed kinematic chain'-the ability of man to control motion and power in a determinate fashion." He distinguishes *kinematics* (Ampère's "geometry of touching surfaces," motion unattached to forces or objects) from *kinetics* (motion resulting from forces directly connected to physical systems), and notes that the materially-oriented word *kinetic* attached itself to sculpture because of sculpture's tangible, three-dimensional properties. A second strand of the prehistory is physiological: the motion studies of E. J. Marey and E. Muybridge, who "succeeded in transforming the recording of motion phenomena from entertainment into scientifically useful information" — Muybridge's *The Human Figure in Motion* (1880) "inspired Duchamp to depict motion through a succession of overlapping forms." (`Directly stated`)

The chapter's polemical core is the diagnosis of failure-as-significance. Burnham concedes the movement's "relative aesthetic failure" and the "comparative mechanical incompetence of its champions," yet insists the failure is meaningful: "the desire to make art kinetic is one of the prime artistic urges of the present century; to misinterpret this is to reject the direction to which Western art has committed itself." This is why the chapter is titled "The Unrequited Art." Burnham's recurring difficulty is that Kinetic Art unites art with forces inherently hostile to it — the machine "could not allow" the autonomous psychic involvement art demands — so "Successful Kinetic Art until now has either defied or trivialized the principles of mechanical invention." He frames four pivotal pioneers — Marcel Duchamp, Naum Gabo, Alexander Calder, and Laszlo Moholy-Nagy — each of whom recognized the potential of an art of motion, made a few experiments, and then withdrew (Duchamp judging mechanics in art "unartistic"; Gabo declaring the technical solution "a task for future generations"; Calder abandoning motor-driven work for the wind-driven mobile; Moholy theorizing a "freely moving" sculpture "free from mechanical and technical movement"). (`Directly stated`)

The concept matters to the larger book because Kinetic sculpture is where Burnham first names the object→system migration in operation. It is the first art form, outside pure automata, "in which essential parts of its working order are purposely hidden from the observer," producing "effects without visible cause" — and on the question of maintenance and breakdown it becomes a system in the technical sense: "the Kinetic construction is not an objet d'art in the conventional sense, but a systeme d'art. It is a system in the sense that any series of interacting components may need repair and adjustment from time to time." The chapter's later half tracks the postwar revival — Fontana's "White Manifesto," Yves Klein's rallying cry of "immateriality," Tinguely's "joyous machine," and the New Tendency / field-kinetics groups (Group Zero, NUL, the G.R.A.V., Group N and Group T) — whose repetitive field structures "became the symbol of an artistic yearning for immateriality." This makes Kinetic Art the sculptural entry point to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis: the move from inert place-bound form toward motion, energy field, and information. (`Directly stated` for the quotations; `Interpretive synthesis` for the link to the vault's immateriality axis.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — Kinetic Art as nonrepresentational art using "the parameters of real time and motion," the first movement of [[Object and System|sculpture as system]]; he traces its machine-theory prehistory (Reuleaux's closed kinematic chain, Ampère's kinematics, Marey/Muybridge's motion studies), its four withdrawing pioneers (Duchamp, Gabo, Calder, Moholy-Nagy), and the postwar New Tendency / field-kinetics revival. Credits the art historian Willoughby Sharp with coining "Kineticism" and "Kineticist." (`Directly stated`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — Kinetic Art is the first art form Burnham treats as a *systeme d'art* rather than an *objet d'art*; the chapter is where the object→system migration is first named in operation.
- [[Automata]] — Kinetic Art's prehistory; Burnham distinguishes the two by emphasis (motion and its effects vs. intelligence, anthropomorphism, coordination).
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — the broader program prioritizing system over object that Kinetic Art's "systeme d'art" anticipates.
- [[Virtuality]] — the "virtual volumes" of Gabo, Duchamp, and Moholy-Nagy (a volume "described by the speeding trajectories of an object") prefigure the dematerialization of mass into motion.
- [[Dematerialization]] — Moholy's equation reducing "sculpture material mass relationships" to "sculpture volume relationships," and the New Tendency's "yearning for immateriality," parallel Lippard's later diagnosis of the dissolving art object.
- [[New Media Art]] — Burnham positions Kineticism, light, and field dynamics as forerunners of the technologically mediated, experiential art the vault tracks under this committed research-focus.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object vs System]] — Kinetic Art enacts the tension's resolution toward the system pole: the moving work is "a system in the sense that any series of interacting components may need repair and adjustment from time to time," not the "traditional inert painting or sculpture."

## Questions It Raises

- If "the desire to make art kinetic is one of the prime artistic urges of the present century" yet the movement is "with very few exceptions a failure," what does an aesthetics of the necessary-but-unrealizable claim for art?
- Why did each of the four pioneers — Duchamp, Gabo, Calder, Moholy-Nagy — advance to "the brink of raw technology" and then withdraw? Is the machine, as Burnham implies, "inherently at odds with artistic endeavor"?
- When the New Tendency makes the repetitive field "a symbol of infinity" and immateriality, has Kinetic Art left sculpture entirely for the energy field — and does field-kinetics borrow physics as metaphor or as method?
