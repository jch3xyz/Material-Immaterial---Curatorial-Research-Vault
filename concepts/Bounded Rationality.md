# Bounded Rationality

## Brief Definition

Herbert Simon's principle, carried by Meadows into systems thinking: people make quite reasonable decisions on the incomplete, delayed information available to them from their own position in a system — so separate rational actions can aggregate into outcomes no one wants.

## Longer Explanation

Meadows adopts `Bounded Rationality` from the Nobel-winning economist Herbert Simon to explain one of her named reasons that "systems surprise us." The claim is deflationary about human agency but not about human competence: within the bounds of what any actor can see and know from their place in the system, their behavior is reasonable. What they lack is not intelligence but information — especially about "more distant parts of the system." Fishermen do not know how many fish there are; businessmen do not know what other businessmen are planning to invest. Their information is "incomplete and delayed, and their own responses are delayed," so they "systematically under- and overinvest." The concept therefore folds `Delays` and imperfect `Mental Models` directly into the account of why individually sensible choices produce collectively perverse results. `Directly stated`

Against the neoclassical picture of the omniscient optimizer, Simon substitutes the *satisficer*: "We are not omniscient, rational optimizers, says Simon. Rather, we are blundering 'satisficers,' attempting to meet (satisfy) our needs well enough (sufficiently) before moving on to the next decision." We pursue our nearby interests rationally but can only act on what we know; we rarely foresee the impact of our actions on the whole system, so instead of a long-term optimum we find a choice we can live with for now and stick to it. This is why the same bounded rationality of every actor, each tuned to a different local goal, becomes the engine of `Policy Resistance` and of the tragedy of the commons — "Bounded rationality in a nutshell!" as Meadows glosses the herdsman overgrazing the shared pasture. `Directly stated`

The most consequential move Meadows makes with the concept is diagnostic and anti-heroic. Because the constraint lives in the structure and not the person, swapping actors changes nothing: "Taking out one individual from a position of bounded rationality and putting in another person is not likely to make much difference. Blaming the individual rarely helps create a more desirable outcome." The bounded rationality of each actor "may not lead to decisions that further the welfare of the system as a whole" — the exact opposition captured by the `Individual Rationality vs System Welfare` tension. `Directly stated`

The cure, then, is never replacement of the actor but redesign of what acts on the actor. "What makes a difference is redesigning the system to improve the information, incentives, disincentives, goals, stresses, and constraints that have an effect on specific actors." From a wider vantage, "information flows, goals, incentives, and disincentives can be restructured so that separate, bounded, rational actions do add up to results that everyone desires." For entrenched `Policy Resistance` specifically, the leverage is an *overarching* goal: "aligning the various goals of the subsystems, usually by providing an overarching goal that allows all actors to break out of their bounded rationality." Bounded rationality is thus one of Meadows's clearest demonstrations of `No Separate Systems` — the actor's rationality is real, but its horizon is set by the system it is embedded in, and only the system can enlarge it. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Herbert Simon]]** — the origin of the concept (forward-stub author). Simon's theory of bounded rationality, Meadows notes, "challenged two hundred years of economics based on" the assumption of the rational optimizing actor; the human being is a satisficer working with incomplete information and limited cognitive reach, not an omniscient maximizer.
- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — imports Simon's concept as a *systems* explanation: bounded rationality is a property of an actor's *position* in a structure (its information, incentives, delays, and constraints), so the intervention target is the structure, not the person. It underwrites her account of `Policy Resistance`, the tragedy of the commons, and her insistence that blaming individuals is a category error.

## Related Concepts

- [[Mental Models]] — bounded rationality is what a mental model looks like from inside a system: a locally accurate, globally partial picture of the world.
- [[Delays]] — the incomplete-and-delayed information that bounds an actor's rationality is largely a matter of information and response `Delays`.
- [[No Separate Systems]] — because no actor stands outside the system, each one's rationality is bounded by its own vantage; enlarging that vantage requires seeing the whole.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — Meadows's remedy is structural: "if all feedback loops are serving the same goal," bounded, separate actions can add up to desirable results.
- [[Systems Theory]] — bounded rationality is Meadows's systems-theoretic reframing of individual choice, locating explanation in structure rather than character.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Individual Rationality vs System Welfare]] — the tension bounded rationality names directly: decisions that are rational for each actor "may not lead to decisions that further the welfare of the system as a whole."

## Questions It Raises

- If blaming the individual "rarely helps," where does moral responsibility land in a system whose bad outcomes emerge from everyone's reasonable choices?
- Can an "overarching goal" that lets actors "break out of their bounded rationality" be supplied without concentrating the very power (or surveillance of the whole) that other authors in the vault treat as domination?
- How much can "even slight enlargement of bounded rationality" — better, more timely information — actually accomplish before the structural incentives themselves must change?
- Is the satisficer a permanent feature of cognition, or an artifact of an information environment that could, in principle, be redesigned to approach the optimizer?
