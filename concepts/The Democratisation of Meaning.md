# The Democratisation of Meaning

## Brief Definition

Ascott's claim that interactive, telematic art is political because it distributes the power to *make* meaning — not merely to receive or exchange messages — turning art into a shared participation in the creation and ownership of reality.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott the shift in art "from appearance to apparition, from object to process" is not only an aesthetic or technological development but a political one. When the observer is moved from the periphery to the centre of the creative process — invited to construct experience and meaning within a context the artist designs rather than to consume a finished, authored product — the monopoly on meaning held by the classical artist (and by the institutions that frame their work) is broken. The issue, he writes, "concerns the democratisation of meaning as much as the democratisation of communications—that is to say, a shared participation in the creation and ownership of reality." The phrase deliberately exceeds the more familiar demand for democratised *communications* (open access to the network, to bandwidth, to broadcast): Ascott wants the deeper democratisation of who gets to author the real itself. (`Directly stated`.)

This rests on Ascott's [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)|radical constructivism]] — the view, drawn from Watzlawick and von Foerster, that reality is constructed rather than represented. If perceiving is inventing, then participating in a work's transformation is participating in the construction of a world, not just interpreting a given one. Hence his keystone formulation: "In the cyberculture, to construct art is to construct reality, the networks of cyberspace underpinning our desire to amplify human cooperation and interaction in the constructive process." Art ceases to be "a window onto the world" and becomes "a doorway through which the observer is invited to enter into a world of interaction and transformation." The democratisation of meaning is the political name for that doorway. (`Directly stated`; the bridge to reality-construction is `Strongly implied` by Ascott's juxtaposition of the two passages within one chapter.)

The concept names the participatory and political *pole* of telematic art. Where [[Reciprocal Communication]] and [[Negotiated Meaning]] describe the formal and semantic mechanics of two-way, distributed authorship, the democratisation of meaning states the stakes: shared *ownership* of reality, an amplification of "human cooperation and interaction in the constructive process." It is also where Ascott's art-politics distinguishes itself from a merely technical egalitarianism — open communications channels can carry an undemocratic distribution of authorial power; only when meaning-making is itself shared is the network politically transformed. (`Interpretive synthesis`.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Negotiated Meaning]] — the semantic mechanism: meaning is co-produced by participants rather than transmitted from author to viewer; the democratisation of meaning is its political consequence.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — two-way, distributed authorship is the structural precondition for shared meaning-making.
- [[The Interface as Doorway]] — art as a doorway "through which the observer is invited to enter," not a window onto an authored world; the architectural figure of participation.
- [[Radical Constructivism (Ascott)]] — the epistemology underwriting the claim: to construct meaning is to construct reality.
- [[New Media Art]] — the broader art-historical category this politics belongs to (Burnham-anchored; Ascott extends it toward participatory network art).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — art as a technology of shared experience, the medium in which the democratisation of meaning is enacted.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Postmodernist Art (Ascott)]] — the art of "appearance" and representation, "the servant of ideologies of both church and state," in which meaning is authored and delivered top-down rather than shared — the order Ascott's participatory politics displaces.

## Questions It Raises

- Does distributing the *production* of meaning genuinely distribute power, or can a constructivist aesthetic coexist with an undemocratic ownership of the networks themselves?
- If "to construct art is to construct reality," who is accountable for the realities constructed — and what happens when the shared construction is captured or manipulated?
- How does Ascott's "democratisation of meaning" relate to, and differ from, the demand for democratised *access* (communications) that techno-utopian network discourse usually foregrounds?
