# Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves

## Brief Definition

The threshold condition — in [[Yuval Noah Harari|Harari's]] *[[Homo Deus]]* — at which authority migrates from the human individual to an external data-processing system: the moment "the system knows me better than I know myself," after which the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|liberal individual]] does not survive being crushed by an outside power but instead "disintegrate[s] from within."

## Longer Explanation

For Harari this is not a metaphor but a mechanism with a precise trigger. Liberalism, he argues, rests on three assumptions — that I am an *in-dividual* with a single authentic self, that this self is free, and that "I can know things about myself nobody else can discover." The third is the load-bearing one: liberalism "grants the individual so much authority" only because, historically, no external system could actually monitor the self well enough to overrule it. "During the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, the belief in individualism nevertheless made good practical sense, because there were no external algorithms that could actually monitor me effectively." The KGB and FBI lacked both the biochemical map and the computing power. So the voter, the customer and the beholder were sovereign by default — not by right, but for want of a rival.

The argument's force is that the threshold is **low**. Harari is emphatic that the system "will never know me perfectly, and will never be infallible. But there is no need for that." It is enough that the algorithm "know me better than I know myself, and will make fewer mistakes than me" — and, he adds drily, this "is less difficult than it may sound, given that most people don't really know themselves well." Once crossed, the migration of authority is not coerced but *rational*: "it would be madness not to follow their advice." This is why the collapse is from within. The individual is not abolished by a tyrant; it is out-competed at its own defining task — self-knowledge — and quietly hands over the keys. Compare Han's [[Smart Power]], which likewise rules not by prohibition but by seducing the subject into its own surveillance; Harari supplies the epistemic version of the same friendliness.

Harari makes the threshold concrete with two cases. **Angelina Jolie's 2013 double mastectomy**: facing a decision about her own body, "she did not climb a mountaintop… and attempt to connect to her innermost feelings. Instead, she preferred to listen to her genes, whose voice manifested not in feelings but in numbers." Her feelings said "everything is perfectly fine"; the [[Algorithmic Determinism|algorithm]] reading her BRCA mutation said "there is a time bomb ticking in your DNA" — an 87 per cent probability of breast cancer. The algorithm knew her body better than her sensations did. **The Facebook-Likes study**: an algorithm watching a person's Likes outperformed the personality predictions of work colleagues at 10 Likes, friends at 70, family at 150, and spouses at 300 — "if you happen to have clicked 300 Likes on your Facebook account, the Facebook algorithm can predict your opinions and desires better than your husband or wife." The intimate Other who "knew me best" is overtaken by a counting machine; this is [[Big Data]]'s claim staked on the terrain of the self.

The reason the algorithm can win is supplied by Harari's master premise that [[Organisms Are Algorithms|organisms are algorithms]]: if the self is "an assemblage of many different algorithms lacking a single inner voice," then "an external algorithm could theoretically know me much better than I can ever know myself." Self-knowledge stops being a privileged inner act and becomes a computational problem — "Self-knowledge through numbers," in the Quantified Self motto Harari quotes — at which silicon outperforms carbon. The note is thus the political payoff of his [[Dataism]]: when "the algorithms know how each person is going to vote," even democratic elections become "obsolete," not because they are unethical but because the [[The Body as Data|narrating self]] is now a worse data-processor than the network it reports to.

## The Oracle / Agent / Sovereign Escalation

Harari frames the migration of authority as a three-stage ratchet, not a single event: "Once Google, Facebook and other algorithms become all-knowing oracles, they may well evolve into agents and finally into sovereigns." The stages are escalating grants of delegated authority:

- **Oracle** — the algorithm answers. You retain the decision; it merely advises. ("'Listen, Google,' I will say, 'both John and Paul are courting me…'") This is the Jolie stage: the genetic test speaks, the human still chooses.
- **Agent** — the algorithm acts on your behalf. You set the goal and let it execute; Harari's example is authorising Google to vote for you, so it votes "according to the real feelings and interests of the collection of biochemical algorithms known as 'I'" rather than your momentary mood.
- **Sovereign** — the algorithm holds the authority. Decisions are no longer delegated but owned by the network; the human becomes "integral parts of a huge global network," an "obsolete algorithm." Harari's outer horizon is algorithms owning capital and property outright — "an algorithmic upper class owning most of our planet."

Each step is voluntary and each is locally reasonable; the escalation is the cumulative effect of many rational hand-overs. The point is continuous with [[The Algorithm as Providence]]: as in O'Gieblyn's reading of the algorithm as a stand-in for divine omniscience, the sovereign algorithm "watches every breath you take… It will definitely know you much better than you know yourself."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — originator of the formula in the vault. The mechanism is epistemic and political: liberalism collapses *the day* an external system out-knows the self, and the individual "disintegrate[s] from within." Harari's tone is diagnostic-neutral; he declines to defend the subject he describes losing.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** *(resonance, not the same claim)* — Han's [[Big Data]] and [[Smart Power]] describe the same friendly, non-coercive capture of the subject through self-exposure, but Han reads it as critique and "data totalitarianism," where Harari reports it with cool ambivalence. (Interpretive synthesis on the linkage.)
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** *(resonance)* — [[The Algorithm as Providence]] and [[Patternism]] supply the religious grammar (omniscience, providence) that Harari's "all-knowing oracle" secularises. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous self whose "free choices are the ultimate source of authority"; this note names exactly the condition that voids it.
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — the "Listen to your feelings!" command this concept overrides with "Listen to the algorithms — they know how you feel."

## Questions It Raises

- Is the threshold ("knows me better than I know myself") an empirical fact about prediction accuracy, or a self-fulfilling one — does deferring to the algorithm *produce* the worse self-knowledge it then exploits?
- If authority migrates because the algorithm "makes fewer mistakes," who defines "mistake," and against whose interests is accuracy measured (the customer's, or the network's)?
- Harari calls the collapse non-coercive ("disintegrate from within"). Does the absence of a Big Brother make the loss of the individual easier to resist, or impossible to even perceive?
- Does the oracle → agent → sovereign escalation have a natural stopping point, or is each rational hand-over the premise of the next?
- For an art/curatorial practice built on the privileged inner act of judgment ("beauty is in the eye of the beholder"), what survives when "beauty will be in the calculations of the algorithm"?
