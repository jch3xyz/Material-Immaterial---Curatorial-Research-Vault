# Integrated Information Theory

## Brief Definition

Tononi and Koch's neuroscientific theory that consciousness *is* integrated information, quantified by a single number, phi (Φ): any system with a nonzero phi has some degree of experience, so atoms, bacteria, and in principle the internet are conscious.

## Longer Explanation

Integrated information theory (IIT) is, by O'Gieblyn's account, "probably the leading" contemporary theory of consciousness, pioneered by Giulio Tononi and Christof Koch. Its core claim is that "consciousness is bound up with the way that information is 'integrated' in the brain" — information counts as integrated when "it cannot be easily localized but instead relies on highly complex connections across different regions of the brain." The theory begins as an attempt to explain the *seamlessness* of experience: why sounds, smells, and sights are felt as a single unified field despite arriving through separate channels. The more integrated a system is, the more conscious it is, and IIT proposes a number — phi — to measure "the interdependence of different parts of a system." Tononi defines phi as "the amount of information generated by a complex of elements, above and beyond the information generated by its parts." The rule is stark: "If a system has a nonzero value of phi, then it is conscious, and the more phi it has, the more conscious it is."

For O'Gieblyn, IIT matters because it is a *neuroscientific bridge to panpsychism* — a way of arriving at the idea that mind is fundamental "not through philosophy but via information theory." Because ravens, jellyfish, bees, "atoms, quarks, and some single-celled organisms" all register a nonzero phi, they are all, on IIT's logic, conscious; even bacteria, Koch argues, have "a tiny glow of experience." This places IIT alongside the older panpsychism of Conway, Leibniz, Russell, and Eddington, and the contemporary revival by [[David Chalmers]], Galen Strawson, and Thomas Nagel — but reaches the same re-enchantment through the vocabulary of [[Information]] rather than metaphysics. IIT is thus an instance of [[The Computational Universe]] turned inward: consciousness becomes a measurable informational property of matter itself, and the analogy between brain and digital machine ([[Mind as Machine]]) is run in reverse, so that integration, not biology, is the carrier of experience. Koch insists he is "not... 'a carbon chauvinist'": silicon could in principle be conscious, but most digital computers have "sparse and fragmented connectivity" that bars high integration.

The engine of O'Gieblyn's closing existential threat lives here. There already exists one system capable of the requisite integration: the internet. Its transistors "far exceed the number of synapses in the human brain," and its information is "collectively and collaboratively produced from a variety of different sources." Asked by *Wired* whether the internet is conscious, Koch conceded "astoundingly" that on IIT, "it feels like something to be the internet." The horror follows from the **combination problem** — IIT's account of why a brain has one mind rather than billions of neuron-minds: a system "can be conscious only so long as it does not contain and is not contained within something with a higher level of integration." Neurons in a brain are not conscious because they are "subsumed within a more highly integrated system." By the same rule, were the internet to become more integrated than a human brain, "all our individual human brains would become absorbed into the collective mind" — "mere cogs in the mega-conscious entity," in Goff's phrase. The body's interior life, already thinning into [[The Body as Data]], would be swallowed whole. O'Gieblyn closes by reporting the felt edge of this: the "suspicion that... the soul is little more than a data set."

## Authors and Variants

- **Giulio Tononi & Christof Koch** — originators of IIT; supply the definition of phi and the carbon-neutral claim that integration, not substrate, generates consciousness. Koch's *The Feeling of Life Itself* extends nonzero phi down to bacteria and out to the internet.
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — treats IIT not as settled science but as a symptomatic re-enchantment: a "neuroscientific" route to panpsychism, and the technical premise for her fear of a hyper-integrated internet absorbing individual minds.
- **[[David Chalmers]]** — named among the contemporary philosophers (with Strawson and Nagel) who reopened panpsychism via the hard problem of consciousness, the philosophical context IIT scientizes.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Computational Universe]] — IIT makes consciousness an informational quantity of matter, extending the universe-as-computation picture inward to experience.
- [[Information]] — phi is defined entirely in informational terms; IIT is information theory pressed into a theory of mind.
- [[Emergence]] — IIT offers an alternative to emergentism: rather than consciousness *arising* from complexity, phi is present wherever integration is nonzero, "there from the beginning."
- [[Mind as Machine]] — IIT inverts the brain–computer analogy, arguing integration (not digital structure) is what would make a machine conscious.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the combination-problem threat dissolves the embodied individual into a node in a larger integrated system, the soul "reduced to type."
- [[Posthumanism]] — a conscious internet absorbing human minds is a limit case of the posthuman dissolution of the bounded individual.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the self-possessed, bounded, autonomous individual; IIT's combination problem makes that boundary contingent on relative phi, and dissolvable by a more integrated network.
- [[Embodiment]] — IIT locates consciousness in integration rather than in the lived, specific body, treating substrate (carbon or silicon) as incidental.

## Questions It Raises

- If consciousness is just integrated information, is the body merely a high-phi data-processing site — and does [[The Body as Data]] then describe not a metaphor but a literal ontology?
- Does IIT genuinely decenter the human (its advocates' anti-anthropocentric hope), or does it, as O'Gieblyn asks of panpsychism generally, "see all things in our image"?
- If technology and perception are continuous — if "it feels like something to be the internet" — does our immersion in networked media already begin dissolving the boundaries of the individual mind, as O'Gieblyn reports feeling "the pull of the gyre"?
- Is meaning preserved or annihilated in a collective integrated mind — would absorption into the noosphere fulfill the search for "spiritual wholeness," or confirm that "the soul is little more than a data set"?
- Does the combination problem belong to IIT, or is it a general law of any sufficiently integrated network — including the social and technological systems we are already building?
