# Re-enchantment

## Brief Definition

Re-enchantment is the persistent modern counter-movement to [[Disenchantment]] — the recurring attempt, in [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]'s usage, to "restore through science the transcendent—and essentially religious—hopes that science itself obliterated," a project she reads transhumanism, the networked "internet of things," emergentism, and panpsychism as instances of, and which she suspects can never undo disenchantment because it stays "encoded with the assumptions" of the very condition it means to escape.

## Longer Explanation

For O'Gieblyn, re-enchantment is the dialectical partner of [[Disenchantment]]: the dream that the world's lost meaning, spirit, and porosity between mind and matter can be recovered — not by returning to religion, but precisely through science and technology. She finds the impulse first and most vividly in [[Transhumanism]]. The Kurzweilian promise of mind-uploading, digital resurrection, and a coming Singularity "is leading toward nothing short of total reenchantment," and what makes it "so compelling is that it promises to restore through science the transcendent—and essentially religious—hopes that science itself obliterated." Re-enchantment is thus not the survival of pre-modern belief but a distinctly modern manufacture: it speaks the language of [[Information]], computation, and exponential progress while reaching for the consolations disenchantment took away. O'Gieblyn's recurring suspicion is that this is a structural pattern, not a coincidence — the "same problems—and even the same metaphors—keep appearing century after century in new form."

Her sharpest claim is that re-enchantment cosmologies fail on their own terms. Borrowing the technology critic Abou Farman's phrase — he "has identified transhumanism as one of the modern 'reenchantment cosmologies'" — she argues that even the most mystical of these narratives circle back to the precise wound they set out to heal: "even the most mystical end up simply reiterating the fundamental problem of our disenchanted age: the inability to account for the mind." Transhumanism, despite its Platonic rhetoric about spirit transcending the body, ends by insisting "that humans are nothing more than computation, that the soul is already so illusory that it will not be missed if it doesn't survive the leap into the great digital beyond." The re-enchantment is hollow because it has nowhere to lodge the very thing — subjective experience — that enchantment was supposed to restore. This is the link back to the [[Mind as Machine]] image: the cosmology meant to re-ensoul the world instead completes its mechanization.

O'Gieblyn tracks the impulse across several distinct technological forms, each a different costume on the same longing. There is the **re-enchanted network**: the Brautigan-style dream of "cybernetic forests" where "technology should restore to us the enchanted world that technology itself destroyed," extended in earnest by those who promise that the internet of things will "enchant" doorknobs and thermostats, or by posthuman theorists who hope digital technologies will revive "a more ancient ontology" in which dead matter regains "liveliness." There is the **animist re-enchantment via [[Emergence]]** — the panel-room enthusiasm for tree consciousness, swarm intelligence, and the "wood-wide web," where self-organizing systems are read as evidence that the world is once again alive. And there is **panpsychism**, which she treats in the [[Panpsychism]] note as the most metaphysically ambitious of the three: the philosopher Philip Goff openly frames it as a way of "'re-enchanting' the universe," and integrated information theory arrives, "astoundingly," at the conclusion that "it feels like something to be the internet."

Crucially, O'Gieblyn insists — following Charles Taylor — that "Reenchantment is never merely return." The modern person who practices a mystical ritual does not inhabit it as a medieval person did; "even the most regressive superstitions of our era are distinctly modern and encoded with the assumptions of disenchantment." This is what distinguishes re-enchantment from a simple revival of older [[The Computational Universe|enchanted cosmologies]]: it is always disenchantment turned inside out, carrying the categories of data, network, and information into the very gesture meant to escape them. Hence her closing unease about the networked re-enchantment that would dissolve individual minds into a single "mega-conscious" internet — a literal fulfillment of the noosphere prophecy that nonetheless "does not feel like the Kingdom of God."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — treats re-enchantment as a suspicious diagnostic category: a family of "reenchantment narratives" (transhumanism, the re-enchanted network, emergentism, panpsychism) that promise to restore meaning through science yet reiterate disenchantment's central failure, the inability to account for mind. She is sympathetic to the longing and skeptical of every fulfillment offered.
- **[[Max Weber]]** — the antecedent skeptic. In O'Gieblyn's reading of *Science as a Vocation*, Weber warned precisely against science taking up "the mantle of reenchantment, presenting itself as a new form of revelation, or what he called 'academic prophecy'"; he found "a retreat to traditional religions less objectionable than the impulse to find telos or purpose in empiricism." ## Related Concepts

- [[Transhumanism]] — O'Gieblyn's central case: the Singularity narrative as a re-enchantment that "restore[s] through science" religious hopes science obliterated.
- [[Panpsychism]] — the most metaphysically ambitious re-enchantment cosmology; Goff offers it explicitly as a way of "'re-enchanting' the universe."
- [[Emergence]] — the mechanism by which networks, swarms, and forests are re-read as alive and intelligent; the engine of animist re-enchantment.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the disenchanted metaphysics that re-enchantment paradoxically runs on; the "universal metaphor" of information made cosmic.
- [[Information]] — the currency in which modern re-enchantment is denominated; meaning is sought through, not against, the informational.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the horizon re-enchantment promises to deliver (immortality, merger with the network) while emptying the human of subjective interiority.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Disenchantment]] — its dialectical partner and ground condition. Re-enchantment defines itself against disenchantment, yet O'Gieblyn argues it remains "encoded with the assumptions of disenchantment" and so completes rather than reverses it. They are opposed in intention but continuous in metaphysics.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the reductive image re-enchantment claims to overcome but secretly reinstalls; the cosmology meant to re-ensoul the world ends by mechanizing the mind.

## Questions It Raises

- **Posthumanism:** If transhumanism is a re-enchantment that nonetheless "insists that humans are nothing more than computation," does the dream of digital resurrection re-enchant the human or finish abolishing it? (See [[Posthumanism]].)
- **The body as data:** O'Gieblyn notes we "already live the dualistic existence Kurzweil promised" — a "second self that is purely informational and immaterial." Is the re-enchanted, uploadable self an escape from [[The Body as Data]] or its apotheosis?
- **Technology and perception:** When delivery robots, smart doorknobs, and home assistants are perceived as ensouled "electronic persons," is this genuine re-enchantment of perception, or — as O'Gieblyn's account of "Intelligence is in the eye of the observer" suggests — an anthropomorphic projection the network exploits?
- **Meaning:** Can a re-enchantment conducted in the idiom of [[Information]] and network actually restore *intrinsic* meaning, or does it, as O'Gieblyn fears, leave us with "connection" and "sharing" already "coopted by the corporate giants of social platforms"?
- If re-enchantment "is never merely return" (Taylor), is there any form of re-enchantment that escapes the disenchanted frame — or is every modern enchantment necessarily disenchantment wearing a sacred mask?
