# Primal Distance (Urdistanz)

## Brief Definition

Primal distance (*Urdistanz*) is Martin Buber's name — adopted by [[Byung-Chul Han]] — for the founding interval between self and Other that is "the very 'principle of being-human'" and "the transcendental condition for any alterity existing at all," the distance that keeps the Other a *Thou* rather than reifying it into an *it*.

## Longer Explanation

Han imports the term from Buber's essay "Distance and Relation" to explain what is destroyed when the Other is sexualized. In his account, "'primal distance' (Urdistanz)" is not the mere gap between two bodies but the transcendental precondition of otherness as such: Buber "claims that such distance serves as the very 'principle of being-human' and constitutes the transcendental condition for any alterity existing at all." Without it there is no Other to relate to at all — only objects. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The work primal distance performs is protective. "'Primal distantiation' prevents the Other from being reified into an object, an 'it.'" Its erosion is exactly what sexualization accomplishes: "The Other as sexual object is no longer a 'Thou.' It is impossible to have a relationship with it." For Han the distance is what confers dignity — it "brings forth the transcendental dignity and propriety that frees—that is, distances—the Other into his or her otherness," and this is "precisely … what makes it possible to address the Other properly." A sex object can be summoned but not addressed; it "has no 'countenance,' which is what constitutes alterity: the otherness of the Other commands distance." Distance, in other words, is not the opposite of relation but its ground. (Label: `Directly stated`.)

The diagnosis extends beyond sex to the whole regime of total proximity, and here Han turns Urdistanz against the media form of the present. "By means of social media, we seek to bring the Other as near as possible, to close any distance between ourselves and him or her, to create proximity. But this does not mean that we have more of the Other; instead, we are making the Other disappear." The de-distancing that promises nearness in fact abolishes it: "Nearness is negative insofar as remoteness is inscribed within it. But now, a total abolition of remoteness is underway. This does not produce nearness so much as it abolishes it. Instead of closeness, it entails crowding." Genuine nearness is animated by the negativity of the remoteness it holds within it; the flattened proximity of total visibility yields only *crowding*, a positivity that "lacks any such power to animate." Erosion of Urdistanz is thus continuous with the destruction of [[Eros]] and the sameness Han elsewhere calls the [[Hell of the Same]]. (Label: `Strongly implied`.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — adopts Buber's *Urdistanz* in *The Agony of Eros* (Ch. 3, "Being Able Not to Be Able") as the interval whose erosion, under sexualization and social media, annihilates the [[The Other|Other]] and destroys erotic experience; it is the ontological cognate of his own value-concept the [[Pathos of Distance]].
- **Martin Buber** *(original source, cited by Han; not yet a vault author)* — in "Distance and Relation" primal distantiation (*Urdistanz*) is the founding act by which a human being sets the world at a distance and can then enter into relation with it, making distance the "principle of being-human." Han quotes Buber directly; see [[Byung-Chul Han and Emmanuel Levinas]] for the Buber–Levinas pairing Han draws on.

## Related Concepts

- [[Pathos of Distance]] — Han's own cognate value-concept; where *Urdistanz* names the transcendental condition of alterity, the pathos of distance names the practical disposition of valuing and preserving that interval. The two are the ontological and the ethical faces of the same defense of distance.
- [[The Other]] — primal distance is what preserves the Other *as* Other (a *Thou*); its erosion reifies the Other into an *it* and, at the limit, makes the Other disappear.
- [[Eros]] — for Han eros requires the "atopia of the Other"; primal distance is the spatial-relational precondition eros depends on, so its collapse is a collapse of eros.
- [[Negativity]] — nearness "acts negatively" because remoteness is inscribed within it; primal distance is a figure of the negativity Han defends against the smooth positivity of the present.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Positivity]] — "crowding acts positively"; the de-distanced, totally proximate world is a world of mere positivity that abolishes the animating negativity distance carries.
- [[Hell of the Same]] — the abolition of remoteness produces not the Other but more of the Same; total proximity is a mode of the sameness Han diagnoses.
- [[Transparency]] — total visibility and consumption (the pornographic "face" with no secrets) dissolve the distance and "countenance" that constitute alterity.

## Questions It Raises

- If alterity is transcendentally conditioned on distance, is any technologically mediated intimacy — which by design closes distance — structurally incapable of encountering an Other?
- Han treats *Urdistanz* and the [[Pathos of Distance]] as nearly interchangeable; is the ontological claim (distance conditions otherness) separable from the ethical claim (we ought to preserve distance), or does the first smuggle in the second?
- Buber's Urdistanz is the ground of relation; Han deploys it almost entirely as a defense against proximity. Does Han inherit Buber's dialectic of *distance and relation*, or only the distance?
- Is "crowding" (total proximity without nearness) a distinct social form, or a symptom of the same de-distancing that transparency and sexualization each perform in their own register?
