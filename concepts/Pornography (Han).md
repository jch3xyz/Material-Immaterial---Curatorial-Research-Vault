# Pornography (Han)

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], "pornography" names not a genre of explicit imagery but a structural-formal condition in which the image, body, or society sheds veil, secret, mediation, and hermeneutic depth in favor of total exhibition and unmediated, infectious contact.

## Longer Explanation

Han's deployment of "pornographic" is analytic rather than moral: it designates a form, not a content. Across the four opening chapters of *The Transparency Society*, the term consistently marks the abolition of negativity — of distance, veil, ambiguity, and mediation — in the visual, erotic, and communicative orders. Already in the chapter on positivity, transparent images "freed from all dramaturgy, choreography, and scenography, from any hermeneutic depth, and indeed from any meaning at all . . . become pornographic," because "pornography is unmediated contact between the image and the eye" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/04 Ch1 - Positivity]]). The pornographic is thus first defined by a missing interval — the short-circuit between eye and image that forecloses contemplation, gaze, and reading. (Label: Directly stated.)

In the chapter on exhibition, this formal definition is generalized into a diagnosis of the social field. "The society of exhibition is a society of pornography. Everything has been turned outward, stripped, exposed, undressed, and put on show" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/05 Ch2 - Exhibition]]). Pornography is here the social form correlated with [[Exhibition Value]]: the commodity-mode in which "possessing 'no secret,'" the displayed thing "stands 'doomed . . . to immediate devouring.'" Hypervisibility, smoothness, and the elimination of the [[Aura]] are its symptoms; images "filled with exhibition value offer no complexity. They are unambiguous — that is, pornographic." The chapter on evidence then sharpens the contrast with [[Eros]] and [[Seduction]]: "Transparency or straightforwardness would be the end of eros — that is, pornography. Thus it is no accident that our contemporary society of transparency is at the same time a porno-society as well" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/06 Ch3 - Evidence]]). Pornography appears as the libidinal-economic ruin produced when the [[Secrecy|Secret]], the [[The Veil|Veil]], and ambiguity are dismantled. (Label: Directly stated.)

The dedicated chapter draws the consequences. Against [[Giorgio Agamben]]'s reading of nudity as profanatory, Han insists that "exposure per se is pornographic" and that "the positivity of exhibiting nudity without a veil is pornographic. It lacks erotic luster. The pornographic body is smooth. Nothing interrupts it." The defining formula is structural and post-hermeneutic: "Precisely where the secret vanishes in favor of total exhibition and bareness, pornography begins. It is characterized by penetrating, intrusive positivity" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/07 Ch4 - Pornography]]). Borrowing [[Roland Barthes]], Han argues that pornographic images are "unary" — without [[Punctum]], without rift — and operate "by direct, tactile, and infectious means. They are post-hermeneutic." The chapter closes by binding this category to Debord: "The society of pornography is a society of the spectacle." Pornography in Han's sense is therefore a name for a whole regime of visibility — smooth, total-exposure, post-hermeneutic, infectious — into which images, bodies, communication, and politics are absorbed. (Label: Directly stated, with Interpretive synthesis across chapters.)

## Psychopolitics (Han) — datasexuality and the pornographic traits of dataism

In *Psychopolitics*, Han carries the structural-formal sense of "pornographic" forward from the visual-erotic field of *The Transparency Society* into his diagnosis of [[Information|dataism]] and Big Data. The same formal logic recurs: where the secret, the veil, and narrative detour vanish in favor of total measurement and exhibition, the pornographic begins. Now, however, the object of the diagnosis is not the image or the body but the datum. Han writes that "numbers and data are not just being absolutized — they are becoming sexualized and fetishized," and that this libidinal investment in the [[The Body as Data|Quantified Self]] gives the new faith its erotic charge: "On the whole, dataism is displaying libidinous — indeed, pornographic — traits. Dataists mate with their data" ([[raw/Han - Psychopolitics/11 Big Data]]). The "datasexual," borrowed via Morozov, is "relentlessly digital" and considers data "sexy"; "the digitus is starling to play the part of the phallus" — the digit (and the act of counting/clicking) usurps the place of desire. (Label: Directly stated; "starling" preserves the raw-source spelling.)

This is the same pornography Han defined in *The Transparency Society* — a regime of total exposure stripped of [[Secrecy|Secret]], [[The Veil|Veil]], and narrative detour — relocated to the order of data. Just as the pornographic image is "additive," "unary," and "post-hermeneutic," so dataism is "purely additive," gives "up on any and all meaning," and substitutes counting for the recounting from which a sense of self derives: "Counting is not recounting." The pornographic trait of dataism is thus the libidinal face of the same elimination of [[Narrativity|narration]] in favor of [[Information|addition]] that drives Han's critique of Big Data; the datasexual's "mating" with data is, formally, the porno-society's "unmediated contact" displaced from eye and image onto the [[The Body as Data|self-measuring subject]] and its datastream. (Label: Interpretive synthesis, anchored to the verbatim quotations above and to the cross-text continuity of Han's definition.)

## Guy Debord (The Society of the Spectacle)

Han's closing move — "The society of pornography is a society of the spectacle" — routes his whole diagnosis back to Guy Debord, and the ground it descends from is laid in the opening chapter of *The Society of the Spectacle* (1967, "Separation Perfected," theses 1–34). Debord fixes the [[Spectacle]] not as a mass of pictures but as a social form: "The spectacle is not a collection of images; it is a social relation between people that is mediated by images" (th. 4). And considered in its own terms, the spectacle "is an affirmation of appearances and an identification of all human social life with appearances" (th. 10) — a total identification of the lived with the visible that is the structural parent of Han's porno-society. Where Han's pornographic image is "post-hermeneutic," offering "nothing to read," Debord's spectacle "is the opposite of dialogue" and "whatever escapes people's activity, whatever eludes their practical reconsideration and correction" (th. 18): both name a regime of appearance that admits no reply, no interval, no reading. (Label: Directly stated for Debord's theses; Interpretive synthesis for the structural descent of Han's pornography from Debord's spectacle.)

The two critics differ in register — Han's is libidinal-aesthetic (veil, eros, punctum, smoothness), Debord's is politico-economic (separation, alienation, "capital accumulated to the point that it becomes images," th. 34) — but they converge on a single mechanism: the eclipse of directly lived experience by its own exhibited representation. Han's "unmediated contact between the image and the eye" is the pornographic close-up of what Debord describes at the scale of a whole society, where "everything that was directly lived has receded into a representation" (th. 1). Han's structural pornography is thus best read as the transparency-society's inheritance of the spectacle: the same "affirmation of appearances," intensified into total exposure and stripped of the last veil. (Label: Interpretive synthesis.)

## Byung-Chul Han (The Agony of Eros)

In *The Agony of Eros* (2012), Han restages pornography no longer principally as an image-property but as the *antagonist of eros* — the display-form of "bare life" and the mechanism by which sexuality itself is annihilated. The book's dedicated chapter opens: "Porn is a matter of bare life on display. The antagonist of eros, it annihilates even sexuality. In this respect, it is more effective than morality" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/05 Porn]]). The paradox that follows is the chapter's signature formula — pornography is obscene not by surplus but by absence: "What is obscene about pornography is not an excess of sex, but the fact that it contains no sex at all." Following [[Jean Baudrillard]]'s *Fatal Strategies* (sexuality "fades much more surely into the more sexual than sex: porn"; porn is the "anticipation of dead sex in living sexuality"), Han argues that the threat to sex today is not the puritan's "pure reason" but pornography, and that "even real sex is turning into porn." This anchors pornography to the book's category of [[Bare Life]]: it is survival-reduced, secret-less sexuality put on display. (Label: Directly stated; the inner formulas "more sexual than sex" and "dead sex in living sexuality" are Baudrillard's, cited by Han.)

The chapter's philosophical core is a sustained polemic against [[Giorgio Agamben]]. Where Agamben's "In Praise of Profanation" celebrates profanation as the liberation of consecrated things into common use — and reads the exhibited nudity of models and porn-stars as opening onto "a new form of erotic communication" — Han inverts the verdict: "The pornographication of the world is unfolding as the profanation of the world. Porn profanes the erotic. Agamben's 'In Praise of Profanation' fails to recognize this social process" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/05 Porn]]). Agamben's "thesis of secularization blinds him," Han argues, because exhibition does not liberate: on the Benjaminian point the vault routes through [[Cult Value vs Exhibition Value (Han via Benjamin)|cult value and exhibition value]], "musealization and exhibition are precisely what destroy their cult value and replace it with exhibition value" — the museum profanes the temple, tourism's "non-sites" profane pilgrimage's places. So the "face loaded with exhibition-value to the point of bursting" (Agamben's phrase, quoted and rejected) "promises no 'new collective use of sexuality.' Indeed, exhibition destroys any and all possibilities for erotic communication." Han's verdict: "A naked face without mystery or expression — reduced simply to being on display — is obscene and pornographic. Capitalism is aggravating the pornographication of society by making everything a commodity and putting it on display. Knowing no other use for sexuality, it profanes eros — into porn." (Label: Directly stated. Caution: the cat-and-yarn example, "loaded until it bursts with exhibition-value," "a new form of erotic communication," and "one's own absolute mediality" are Agamben's own words, quoted by Han only to reject them.)

This profanation, Han continues, "is unfolding as deritualization and desacralization. Today, ritual spaces and actions are disappearing. The world is becoming more naked and more obscene" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/05 Porn]]). Against the reduction of love to "warmth, intimacy, and pleasant arousal," he sides with Bataille's ritualized "holy eroticism" and with [[Jean Baudrillard]]'s opposition of seduction to love ("Ritual is in the realm of seduction. Love is born from the destruction of ritual forms") to argue that ritual distance and scenic illusion — not their liberation — are the very condition of eros: "In contrast, the erotic is never free of secrecy." "Pornography completes the deritualization of love," and, in the polemic's sharpest turn, "Agamben's profanation even promotes the deritualization and pornographication of the world in that it suspects ritual spaces of constituting compulsive forms of sequestration." This *Agony of Eros* account is the second, fuller round of the Han-contra-Agamben argument the vault already registers from *The Transparency Society* (nudity without a veil), now generalized from nudity to profanation as such. (Label: Directly stated; "Love is born from the destruction of ritual forms" is Baudrillard's, cited by Han.)

The book's penultimate chapter, "The Politics of Eros," folds pornography into its master opposition between the "Two scene" (love as event, the wholly [[The Other|Other]], difference) and the "One scene" (sexuality's self-identical, consumable object that "never calls me into question"). Here pornography is diagnosed as the terminal case of narcissism: "Pornography reinforces habituation, for it erases otherness altogether. Its consumer does not even have a sexual counterpart. As such, it occupies the One scene" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/07 The Politics of Eros]]). Because "what is pornographic is precisely the lack of contact and encounter with the Other," and "autoerotic contact and auto-affection protect the ego from being touched or seized by the Other," the conclusion is that "pornography intensifies narcissification." This joins pornography directly to the [[Hell of the Same]]: it belongs "to the order of the habitual, which reproduces the Same," whereas love as event is "dehabitualizing and denarcissifying." Pornography is thus, in *The Agony of Eros*, both the profanation that destroys eros's ritual conditions and the auto-affective One scene that forecloses the [[The Other|Other]] on which eros depends. (Label: Directly stated, with Interpretive synthesis for the cross-chapter integration.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Pornography is a structural-formal condition of any image, body, or relation stripped of veil, secret, distance, and hermeneutic depth; it names the visibility-regime of the [[The Transparency Society|Transparency Society]] and is coextensive with [[Exhibition Value]], [[Smoothness]], and the [[Spectacle]]. In *The Agony of Eros* the same concept is refunctioned as the *antagonist of [[Eros]]*: porn is "[[Bare Life|bare life]] on display," obscene because "it contains no sex at all," the profanation that "completes the deritualization of love," and the narcissistic "One scene" that "erases otherness altogether" and so "intensifies narcissification." (Label: Directly stated.)
- **[[Giorgio Agamben]]** — Han reads *against* Agamben across two books. In *The Transparency Society* he rejects Agamben's claim that pornographic nudity holds "profanatory potential" and grounds a "new form of erotic communication," arguing that "Agamben fails to recognize that exposure per se is pornographic" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/07 Ch4 - Pornography]]). *The Agony of Eros* mounts the fuller, second round of the same polemic — now against Agamben's "In Praise of Profanation" and *Nudities*: "The pornographication of the world is unfolding as the profanation of the world . . . Agamben's 'In Praise of Profanation' fails to recognize this social process," and "Agamben's 'profanation' amounts to just so much profanity" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/05 Porn]]). The disagreement is registered in [[Byung-Chul Han and Giorgio Agamben]]. Attribution caution: the cat-and-yarn deactivation example, "loaded until it bursts with exhibition-value," "a new form of erotic communication," and "one's own absolute mediality" are Agamben's own words, quoted by Han only to reject them. (Label: Directly stated.)
- **[[Jean Baudrillard]]** — Han mobilizes Baudrillard's account of obscenity and seduction ("More generally things visible do not come to an end in obscurity and silence — instead they fade into the more visible than visible: obscenity"; the "total promiscuity of the look with what it sees") as a precursor diagnosis of the pornographic regime. In *The Agony of Eros* the debt is explicit: porn as "the more sexual than sex" and the "anticipation of dead sex in living sexuality" (*Fatal Strategies*), and the seduction-vs-love axiom "Ritual is in the realm of seduction. Love is born from the destruction of ritual forms" — all Baudrillard's, cited by Han. See [[Byung-Chul Han and Jean Baudrillard]]. (Label: Directly stated / Strongly implied.)
- **[[Roland Barthes]]** — Han uses Barthes's distinction between [[Punctum]] and [[Studium]] to define the pornographic image as "unary," "smooth and transparent," lacking rift, ambiguity, or wounding intensity. (Label: Directly stated.)
- **[[Guy Debord]]** in *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]* — Han ends the pornography chapter by binding his concept to Debord's: "Pornographic, deculturalized images … empty out into spectacle. The society of pornography is a society of the spectacle" ([[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Transparency Society/07 Ch4 - Pornography]]). Debord's Chapter 1 grounds that descent: the [[Spectacle]] is "a social relation between people that is mediated by images" (th. 4) and "an affirmation of appearances and an identification of all human social life with appearances" (th. 10) — the total-exposure, anti-hermeneutic regime of representation from which Han's structural pornography derives ([[raw/Debord - The Society of the Spectacle/04 Ch1 - Separation Perfected]]). They differ on register (Han's libidinal-aesthetic vs. Debord's politico-economic) but share the diagnosis of total mediation and the eclipse of lived experience by representation. (Label: Directly stated for Han's citation of Debord; Interpretive synthesis for the conceptual alignment.)
- **[[Walter Benjamin]]** — Speculative connection: Han's pornography is the inverse of Benjamin's beauty, in which "the beautiful is neither the veil nor the veiled object but rather the object in its veil." Benjamin himself does not use "pornography" as a category, but Han frames it as the structural negation of veiled beauty; the *Agony of Eros* rebuttal of Agamben leans on the Benjaminian [[Cult Value vs Exhibition Value (Han via Benjamin)|cult-value/exhibition-value]] distinction ("musealization and exhibition … destroy their cult value and replace it with exhibition value"). (Label: Interpretive synthesis.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Exhibition Value]] — the economic-aesthetic logic whose generalization Han names "the society of pornography."
- [[Smoothness]] — the surface-property of the pornographic image; "the pornographic body is smooth. Nothing interrupts it."
- [[Spectacle]] — Han's chapter closes by binding pornography to Debord's category; Debord defines the spectacle as "a social relation between people that is mediated by images" (th. 4).
- [[The Transparency Society]] — the broader diagnosis of which the porno-society is the libidinal-visual face.
- [[Positivity]] — pornography is characterized by "penetrating, intrusive positivity."
- [[Hypervisibility]] — "hypervisibility is obscene; it lacks the negativity of what is hidden, inaccessible, and secret."
- [[Obscenity]] — Han uses Baudrillard's "more visible than visible" to mark pornography's tipping into obscenity.
- [[Aura]] — pornography is the regime in which the aura, "the appearance of a distance," has vanished.
- [[Punctum]] — pornographic images are "unary" — they have no punctum, only studium.
- [[Bare Life]] — in *The Agony of Eros*, "porn is a matter of bare life on display"; pornography is the display-form of survival-reduced life.
- [[Hell of the Same]] — pornography "reproduces the Same," "erases otherness altogether," and "intensifies narcissification"; it is the auto-affective "One scene" opposed to love's "Two scene."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Eros]] — pornography is the structural ruin of eros; eros lives by veil, distance, and intermittence. In *The Agony of Eros* porn is named eros's "antagonist," and "porn profanes the erotic."
- [[The Other]] — "what is pornographic is precisely the lack of contact and encounter with the Other"; porn "erases otherness altogether," where love as event admits the wholly Other.
- [[Seduction]] — seduction operates through ambiguous codes and detour; pornography "neither allures nor alludes; instead, it infects and affects." In *The Agony of Eros*, "pornography is eliminating erotic seduction, which toys with scenic illusion and deceptive appearances."
- [[The Veil|Veil]] / [[Secrecy|Secret]] — Benjamin's "object in its veil" is, for Han, the formal opposite of the pornographic; "the erotic is never free of secrecy."
- [[Aura]] — the auratic distance is what compulsive exhibition annihilates.
- [[Hermeneutics]] — pornographic images "have no deep hermeneutic structure"; they are "post-hermeneutic."

## Questions It Raises

- If "pornographic" names a form rather than a content, does any image with [[Aura]], [[The Veil|Veil]], or [[Punctum]] escape the category — or is the regime totalizing within late capitalism?
- How does Han's structural pornography differ from, or supersede, Baudrillard's "obscenity" and Debord's "spectacle"? Is it a synthesis, a refinement, or a renaming?
- If exhibition is exploitation and "the society of exhibition is a society of pornography," is curatorial display itself caught in this diagnosis? What would a non-pornographic exhibition look like?
- Han rejects Agamben's claim that pornographic nudity can be "profaned" toward new erotic use, and in *The Agony of Eros* charges that Agamben's profanation actually *promotes* pornographication by suspecting ritual of "compulsive sequestration." Is there any operation of de-pornographication available within the [[The Transparency Society|Transparency Society]], or does the diagnosis foreclose it?
- If pornography "intensifies narcissification" and "occupies the One scene," is de-pornographication the same movement as the recovery of [[Eros]] and the [[The Other|Other]] — the passage from the One scene to the Two scene? What politics (Han's "politics of eros") would that entail?
- What is the relation between Han's pornography and his concept of [[Smoothness]] in later works (e.g., *Saving Beauty*)? Are they the same condition under different names? (Speculative.)

Raw file: [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/05 Porn]]
Relevant passage:

> Porn is a matter of bare life on display. The antagonist of eros, it annihilates even sexuality. In this respect, it is more effective than morality: "Sexuality does not fade into sublimation, repression and morality, but fades much more surely into the more sexual than sex: porn." Pornography derives its appeal from the "anticipation of dead sex in living sexuality." What is obscene about pornography is not an excess of sex, but the fact that it contains no sex at all.

Supports: The *Agony of Eros* definition of pornography as [[Bare Life|bare life]] on display and the antagonist of [[Eros]] — obscene by absence, not excess, of sex. Anchors the book-specific reframing. (The inner quotations "more sexual than sex" and "anticipation of dead sex in living sexuality" are [[Jean Baudrillard]]'s, cited by Han.) Label: `Directly stated`.

> A naked face without mystery or expression—reduced simply to being on display—is obscene and pornographic. Capitalism is aggravating the pornographication of society by making everything a commodity and putting it on display. Knowing no other use for sexuality, it profanes eros—into porn.

Supports: The pornographication-as-profanation thesis and its capitalist aggravation — the secret-less, exhibited face is obscene, and capitalism, "knowing no other use for sexuality," profanes eros into porn. Grounds the cross-links to [[Exhibition Value]], [[Cult Value vs Exhibition Value (Han via Benjamin)]], and the Han-contra-Agamben polemic in [[Byung-Chul Han and Giorgio Agamben]].
