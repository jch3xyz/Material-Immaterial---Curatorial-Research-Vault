# Psychopolitics

## Brief Definition

Psychopolitics is Byung-Chul Han's name for the form of government proper to the neoliberal regime: a technology of power that no longer disciplines the body but seizes, optimizes and steers the psyche itself, passing — in its digital phase — "from passive surveillance to active steering."

## Longer Explanation

Psychopolitics is the titular master concept of Han's book and the anchor that organizes its argument. Where earlier regimes of power addressed the body — sovereign power as the "might of the sword," disciplinary power as the "careful 'administration of bodies'" — the neoliberal regime "has discovered the psyche as a productive force." Han stages this as a succession of governmental technologies: sovereignty, then disciplinary [[Biopolitics]], then psychopolitics. Borrowing Deleuze's bestiary, he writes that "the disciplinary regime organizes itself as a 'body'. It is a biopolitical regime," whereas "the neoliberal regime, in contrast, seems like a 'soul'. As such, psychopolitics is its form of government." The shift from body to soul, from mole to snake, is a "mutation, indeed an intensification, of capitalism, which remains one and the same." (`Directly stated`)

The motor of this turn is the change in the mode of production. Neoliberalism implements "immaterial and non-physical forms of production"; "what gets produced are not material objects, but immaterial ones." Because the body "no longer represents a central force of production," productivity is enhanced "not... by overcoming physical resistance so much as by optimizing psychic or mental processes." Psychopolitics therefore operates not through external coercion but through the engineering of freedom: it "ensures that individuals act on themselves so that power relations are interiorized — and then interpreted as freedom," so that "self-optimization and submission, freedom and exploitation, fall into one." This is why psychopolitics is the regime of [[Auto-Exploitation]] and the [[Achievement Society]], and why its operative cell is the [[Exploitation of Freedom]] rather than the disciplinary command. (`Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis`)

Han reads psychopolitics as the concept Foucault failed to reach. Having turned in the late 1970s to neoliberal forms of government, Foucault "stuck to the concepts of population and biopolitics" and so "failed to do what the circumstances actually called for: to make the turn to psychopolitics." Biopolitics, tied to "the biological, the somatic, the corporal," is structurally incapable of reaching the psyche: "Biopolitics, which makes use of population statistics, has no access to the psychic realm. It can deliver no material for drawing up a psychogram of the population." Psychopolitics is thus defined by contrast — it is precisely the power that biopolitics cannot exercise. (`Directly stated`)

### Digital psychopolitics

The book's sharpest claim concerns the digital intensification of this power. "Today, we are entering the age of digital psychopolitics. It means passing from passive surveillance to active steering." Where biopolitical statistics could deliver only a demographic, "Big Data provides the means for establishing not just an individual but a collective psychogram — perhaps even the psychogram of the unconscious itself." Digital psychopolitics therefore "manages to intervene in psychic processes in a prospective fashion," predicting and pre-empting behaviour "on a pre-reflexive level," "perhaps even faster than free will." Its concrete technique is [[Big Data]]: in elections, "micro-targeting is data-driven psychopolitics," "the practical microphysics of power." Han frames the stakes as the end of the free, self-possessing subject — "Big Data has announced the end of the person who possesses free will." Digital psychopolitics is not minted as a separate concept here but housed as the contemporary, technologized phase of the single concept psychopolitics. (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — coins/adopts *psychopolitics* as the name for the neoliberal "soul"-regime that exploits the psyche as a productive force, and as the concept Foucault's biopolitics could not reach. Han pointedly rejects defining psychopolitics as a *sub-form* of biopolitics (he criticizes Alexandra Rau and Thomas Lemke for treating "psychopolitics" "as a biopolitical form of government"); for Han it is a successor regime, not a species of biopolitics. Its decisive contemporary form is *digital psychopolitics*, powered by [[Big Data]]. (`Directly stated`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Big Data]] — the instrument of digital psychopolitics; "Micro-targeting is becoming the standard practice of psychopolitics."
- [[Society of Control]] — the Deleuzian frame (mole/snake, body/soul) Han uses to oppose disciplinary biopolitics to the neoliberal psychopolitical regime.
- [[Achievement Society]] — the subjective correlate; the self-optimizing achievement-subject is the figure psychopolitics governs.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — psychopolitics' characteristic mode: interiorized power "interpreted as freedom," so that self-optimization and exploitation coincide.
- [[Exploitation of Freedom]] — the mechanism by which psychopolitics extracts value without external coercion.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — the surveillance architecture within which active steering becomes possible.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Biopolitics]] — the negative foil. Biopolitics is "a politics of the body in the fullest sense" and "is incapable of enabling subtle interventions in the psyche"; psychopolitics is the power that reaches the psyche where biopolitics cannot. The opposition is body vs. soul, demography vs. psychogram, passive surveillance vs. active steering.

## Questions It Raises

- If psychopolitics steers the psyche "faster than free will," what — if anything — remains of freedom as a category, and on what ground could resistance ("a third Enlightenment") be mounted?
- Is Han right that psychopolitics is a *successor* to biopolitics rather than a *form* of it (contra Rau and Lemke), and what hangs on that distinction?
- Does the body genuinely drop out under immaterial production, or does psychic optimization (fitness, "sexiness," neuro-enhancement) re-inscribe a governed body that the body/soul opposition obscures?
- If digital psychopolitics reads the "digital unconscious," does it merely exploit pre-existing desire or actively produce it?
