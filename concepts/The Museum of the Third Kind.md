# The Museum of the Third Kind

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's three-stage typology of the museum, culminating in a "museum of the third kind" — no longer a place to hang the art object nor a virtual reproduction of one, but a site of mind-to-mind, cortex-to-hypercortex contact that yields "interactivity at the level of the spirit."

## Longer Explanation

In "The Mind of the Museum" (1996), Ascott periodizes the museum into three kinds in order to argue that the institution must undergo "[r]adical revision, visionary restructuring" — for him "not just an option; it's the only survival strategy" in the face of "the realities of cyborg life and telematic consciousness." The **first kind** is the classical museum, "where those with the leisure to do so could hang out with the muse" — and which, Ascott declares flatly, "is past" (`Directly stated`).

The **second kind** is the museum's first, inadequate digital reflex: the attempt to take "the materiality of art, in all its glorious and discrete objecthood," and have it "transposed into its virtual other." Ascott's verdict is that this "won't work" — merely re-rendering the discrete art object in a virtual register does not change the museum's underlying relation to art as a stock of objects to be displayed (`Directly stated`). On this reading the second kind is a category error: it digitizes the object while leaving the object-centered paradigm intact, which is precisely why it fails. This is the point at which the concept turns the spine of the vault's `Material vs Immaterial` tension — the second kind treats immateriality as a new container for the same material objecthood, whereas the third kind abandons objecthood altogether (`Interpretive synthesis`).

The **third kind** is Ascott's positive proposal: a museum "where the close encounter of minds with minds, cortex to hypercortex, virtual with the actual, leads to interactivity at the level of the spirit." The unit of the museum is no longer the artwork-object but the encounter between minds, networked into what Ascott calls the hypercortex; the museum's value is reciprocal and participatory rather than custodial and exhibitionary (`Directly stated`). Ascott grounds the claim in concrete institutions of the moment — the Ars Electronica Center in Linz and the NTT InterCommunication Center in Tokyo, both of which he had a hand in — as prototypes of the digital museum "of the twenty-first century" (`Strongly implied`).

The typology is the institutional corollary of Ascott's broader program: it carries the logic of `Telematic Art` and `Reciprocal Communication` into the museum itself, recasting the institution as a node in a mind-to-mind network rather than a repository of objects. It is the concrete, architectural statement of the argument that `The museum of the future must be a fluid organism not a static institution`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the sole author of the typology. The "third kind" names the museum proper to telematic culture: a site of cortex-to-hypercortex contact and "interactivity at the level of the spirit," opposed to both the object-hanging first kind and the merely-virtualized-object second kind.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the second kind tries to keep material objecthood by giving it a "virtual other"; the third kind dissolves the object into mind-to-mind interaction, pushing the tension to its immaterial pole.

## Questions It Raises

- If the museum's unit becomes the encounter between minds rather than the artwork-object, what (if anything) does it conserve, and for whom?
- Can "interactivity at the level of the spirit" be distinguished, in practice, from the very "website design and postvideo production" Ascott says "is not enough"?
- Does abandoning objecthood emancipate the museum from "the ringing of its own cash registers," or merely relocate the market into the network?
