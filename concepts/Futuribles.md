# Futuribles

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's coinage for the artwork — and his [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — understood as an anticipatory, speculative *projection of possible (and alternative) futures*: "the delineation of futuribles," an art whose function is to dream up alternatives so that the present itself becomes more changeable.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, a *futurible* is the future-facing face of the art object. In "Behaviourables and Futuribles" (1967) he describes the work in its total process as "a behaviourable in its history, a futurible in its structure, a trigger in its effect" — three aspects of one process-object. The behaviourable looks back at what the work *has done* to behaviour; the futurible looks forward at what it *could bring about*. Against the mimetic tradition of depicting the world ("figuration"), Ascott sets "prefiguration: the delineation of futuribles" — an art that sketches what is not yet the case rather than re-presenting what already is. *(Directly stated.)*

The motive is explicitly transformative, not merely predictive. "We are very much concerned with generating futuribles," Ascott writes, "maybe that's because the more we can dream up alternative futures, the more changeable the present can become." *(Directly stated.)* Imagining alternatives is itself the lever of change; the futurible is a tool for loosening the grip of the given. This is why generating futuribles is, for Ascott, "the essence of behaviourist art" — see [[Behaviourist Art]] — and why his [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] is "essentially a futurible — anticipatory and speculative," offered as "an alternative" rather than a finished blueprint. *(Directly stated.)*

In "The Psibernetic Arch" (1970) Ascott sharpens the term against professional futurology. The economists, sociologists and astrologers who generate scenarios for paying clients aim at "surprise-free" predictions; but "for the artist, there is no client... and so, for us, the generating of futuribles is essentially a far less constrained affair and may become a creative act." *(Directly stated.)* The artist's freedom from the client's demand for certainty is precisely what lets the futurible be creative rather than actuarial. Ascott names this an "art of predictive or anticipatory structure, which creates alternative futures, a distillation in pure visual energy of what could be the case. The art of futuribles" — borrowing Herman Kahn's distinction that the task "is not to predict the future but to sketch alternative futures." *(Directly stated.)*

The futurible therefore reframes the art object's value as *information about possibility* rather than fixed material form, aligning it with Ascott's broader move from product to process and from structure to system. He restages the critic accordingly: "The critic as seer would be involved in seeing again, in an entirely fresh light, a visionary rather than a merely visual art." *(Directly stated.)* "Art has anticipated anticipation," he claims — "the ability to predict, to sketch for oneself all kinds of alternative futures" — making the capacity to imagine alternatives, not any particular image, the real subject of the work. *(Directly stated.)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins *futuribles* (1967–70) for the anticipatory, alternative-future projection that the artwork and the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] embody; the term is the future-facing pole of his behaviourable/futurible/trigger triad and the goal of [[Behaviourist Art]]. A durable term in his vocabulary, recurring across *Telematic Embrace*. It feeds his later realization of [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] as anticipatory, participatory practices. *(Directly stated.)*

## Related Concepts

- [[Behaviourist Art]] — the parent program: generating futuribles "is the essence of behaviourist art," whose aim is generating change.
- [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — Ascott's institutional proposal, itself described as "essentially a futurible" — anticipatory, speculative, an alternative.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the futurible is realized through two-way, participatory exchange (the "cool," feedback-admitting work), not one-way message delivery; cf. Ascott's hot/cool gloss after [[Media Hot and Cold]].
- [[New Media Art]] — the future-oriented, participatory art tradition Ascott helps found, of which the art of futuribles is an early formulation.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the artwork as an instrument for anticipating and inducing experience and behaviour, consonant with the futurible's transformative aim.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Systems Esthetics]] — interpretive contrast: Burnham's 1968 systems esthetics analyses the artwork-as-system in the present tense (information, process, real-time feedback), whereas Ascott's futurible foregrounds the *anticipatory/speculative* axis — the projection of alternative futures. They are closely allied (both move "beyond the object") but emphasize the actual versus the possible. *(Interpretive synthesis.)*
- Figuration / mimetic representation — Ascott explicitly opposes "figuration" with "prefiguration: the delineation of futuribles"; the futurible delineates what could be, not what is. *(Directly stated.)*

## Questions It Raises

- If the artwork's value lies in projecting alternative futures rather than depicting the present, does the futurible dissolve the distinction between art and futurology — or is the artist's freedom from a "client" what keeps it art?
- Can imagining alternative futures actually make the present "more changeable," or does Ascott overstate the political efficacy of speculative imagination?
- Does the futurible require the immaterial (information, telepathy, "instant communications with no media") to escape the constraints of physical media, linking it to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis?
- How does the futurible's anticipatory structure differ from mere prediction once it is institutionalized in the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] — does an "alternative" survive being scheduled and built?
