# The Computer as an Extra-Sensory Device

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's claim that the computer is "an extra-sensory device dealing invisibly with the invisible" — a machine that operates on the connections, systems, fields, and forces lying outside ordinary "material perception," simulating the non-linear, non-local, layered, and discontinuous patterns of the new physics in order to render what is invisible visible.

## Longer Explanation

In "Beyond Time-Based Art: ESP, PDP, and PU" (1990) Ascott pivots from the visible world of objects to the *invisible* substrate the new physics describes — "the dance of electrons, the discontinuous quantum jumps, tunnelling, and transformations of energy." Our natural senses, he argues, register only "the rather gross level of material perception"; the quantum events that actually constitute reality are "totally invisible to our everyday perception." The computer is the instrument that closes this gap. It is "an extra-sensory device dealing invisibly with the invisible" — it works on the order *behind* appearances rather than on appearances themselves. (`Directly stated`)

What makes the computer extra-sensory is not magic but *simulation*: "It is these patterns of events—non-linear, non-local, layered, and discontinuous—that the computer can simulate." Because the machine can model the topology of quantum and chaotic process, it can present to perception what perception cannot reach unaided. Hence Ascott's keystone formulation: "It is through the computer that we can hope to glimpse the unseeable, to grasp chaos and the secret order in the disordered." The device extends cognition past the threshold of the senses — an apparatus for the invisible. (`Directly stated`)

This is, for the vault, a clear *seed* of Ascott's later [[Cyberception (Ascott)|cyberception]], the "postbiological faculty" of technologically-extended, network-formed perception he names in the cyberception chapters. In 1990 the claim is still device-centred (the computer as prosthesis of the unseeable) rather than faculty-centred (a new sense the human acquires through connectivity); the move from the *device* that renders the invisible visible to the *faculty* of perceiving the invisible is the line that runs forward into cyberception. This note records only the 1990 device-claim and forward-links the later term; it does not collapse the two. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

The concept belongs to Ascott's broader argument that "telematic art rejects representation in favour of simulation" and, "moving beyond object art and time-based art, uses simulation to render what is invisible visible, to bring the virtual, the potential, the unseen, and unrealised into view." The extra-sensory computer is the technical condition of that rendering: it is how the immaterial pole — process, field, the virtual — is brought into view at all. It thus belongs to the immaterial side of the eight-author [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension, naming the instrument by which the invisible/immaterial is made perceptible. (`Strongly implied`)

## Questions It Raises

- Is the "extra-sensory" computer genuinely extending perception, or only producing a persuasive image (a *simulation*) of an invisible order it cannot verify? Ascott's "secret order in the disordered" is asserted, not demonstrated.
- If the device renders the invisible visible, does the resulting "visibility" still count as perception, or is it a new category between perception and representation?
- How far is the 1990 device-claim already the later faculty of [[Cyberception (Ascott)|cyberception]], and where does the device-vs-faculty distinction actually hold?
- Does extending perception to the non-local, non-linear quantum substrate dissolve the boundary of the perceiving body — and so feed the dispersed, out-of-body subject of [[Telematic Art]]?
