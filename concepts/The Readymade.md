# The Readymade

## Brief Definition

The Readymade is an ordinary, frequently utilitarian object raised to the status of fine art by the artist's "laying on of hands" — a paradoxical idealism, since it is all idea with no deliberate construction technique.

## Longer Explanation

In *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, Jack Burnham reads the Readymade as Marcel Duchamp's threat to the very idealism of the sculpted object. Where Cubist, Constructivist, and abstract sculptors worked to release "essential form" from mass, gravity, and the base, Duchamp moved in the opposite direction: he took already-existing, mundane manufactured things and, by the artist's nomination alone — "a 'laying on of hands'" — elevated them to art. Burnham locates this maneuver within his chapter on the vanishing sculptural base, where the pedestal that once guaranteed an object's status as art dissolves and "fewer things begin on a pedestal, but all receive wary respect." (Directly stated)

The irony Burnham draws out is that the Readymade is not anti-idealist but *hyper*-idealist. Precisely because it involves "no deliberate construction technique on the part of the artist," it is "all idea" — a pure conceptual gesture rather than a made thing. This makes the Readymade a paradoxical inversion: an attack on the idealism of sculpture that turns out to be the most idealist object of all, located entirely in the act of designation. (Directly stated)

Burnham's larger claim is that the Readymade did not exhaust itself as a one-off provocation. "Rather than dying out as an ontological caper," it "has become one of the pervasive influences in modern sculpture." He gives it a striking epistemological figure: "a sort of Goedel's Theorem of aesthetics, which has proved the provisional quality of art" — that is, the Readymade functions like an undecidability proof internal to art, demonstrating that no fixed definition of what counts as art can be complete or final. By severing art from craft and locating it in idea and context, the Readymade is a precursor to [[Conceptual Art]] and to the [[Dematerialization]] of the art object that Lippard later charts. (Directly stated for the quoted claims; the conceptual-art / dematerialization lineage is `Interpretive synthesis`.)
