# Object Art

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's name for the "New York Hard-Center, Minimal, or Object" school (Judd, Morris, Truitt, Nevelson) — a deliberately inert, massive, palpable three-dimensional form that rejects dynamic-geometric and vitalist theory and reasserts sculpture as "solid, palpable, and real."

## Longer Explanation

For Burnham, Object Art (also "Object sculpture," "Minimal," or "Hard-Center" sculpture) is the last alternative the avant-garde discovered once formalism and vitalism had reached exhaustion. In Chapter 1, "Sculpture's Vanishing Base," he locates its emergence at the moment the sculpture base finally withered: the "New York Hard-Center, Minimal, or Object sculptors (Don Judd, Robert Morris, Ann Truitt, and Mike Nevelson were some of the earliest) created a school which makes blanket rejection of all the older dynamic-geometric and vitalist theories." Against the long modern drive toward attenuated, gravity-defying, dematerialized form, their answer was emphatically material: "Their solution offers a three-dimensional form as inert as it is massive." To want sculpture that is again "solid, palpable, and real," Burnham notes, "may appear tautological, but in truth these characteristics reaffirm those qualities which have been methodically removed from modern sculpture" (Directly stated).

In the Introduction Burnham gives Object Art its sharpest theoretical formulation: it is "sculpture de-idealized." "In theory at least, Object sculpture is art which has been cut free of all previous iconic influences; it is sculpture de-idealized." This is the Part-I Object pole of his book's overall division of modern sculpture into object and system — but he immediately qualifies its purity. Object sculpture "is not free of the idiosyncrasies of human perception," and it is "quite likely not the last step in the process toward absolute 'thingification.'" Reification, the Marxian "thingification" (Verdinglichung) that drives Burnham's whole account, will continue to push sculpture beyond the static object toward life-simulating systems (Directly stated).

In Chapter 3, "Formalism: The Weary Vocabulary," Burnham reads Object Art as the terminus of the formalist-mathematical lineage rather than its renewal. Its bare Euclidean shapes confess that the mathematical model — the scientific model that had stocked the modern sculptor's form-vocabulary — can no longer generate icons: "The cones, pyramids, cubes, rectangular solids, regular polyhedrons, and spheres of Object sculpture are an implicit admission that the eternal verities of Pythagorean solids are the stuff from which sculpture is made-and always will be." It "offers tacit admission that the mathematical model can no longer solve the iconic problems of sculpture" (Directly stated).

In Chapter 4, "Form Exhaustion and the Rise of Phenomenalism," Object Art becomes the hinge from "transcendent idealism" to "transcendent phenomenalism." Stripped of associated meaning and imagery, "Object sculpture admits to being itself-nothing else." It does not abandon meaning so much as relocate it in the act of looking: "Conceived intellectually, Object sculpture depends upon its lack of associated meanings and trivialized format for an identity. It does attempt to present meaning as a series of perceptual experiences." Reading Judd and Morris through Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology, Burnham argues the inert object shifts the burden of "vitalizing" sculpture onto the viewer's own perception — its coldness and impenetrability "exerts the same raw presence as any other object; it makes no attempt to be understood or to come to us" (Directly stated).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — Object Art is the achievement of "Object or Minimal sculpture, along with a desire to preserve the static, material consistency of past sculpture": sculpture de-idealized, "as inert as it is massive," the Part-I Object pole that nonetheless cannot halt the larger drift of reification toward the system. Burnham treats "Object," "Minimal," and "New York Hard-Center" as interchangeable names for the same school and reads it both as the end of the weary formalist/mathematical vocabulary and as the entry point of phenomenalism into sculpture (Judd, Morris, Truitt, Nevelson; with Tony Smith, Dan Flavin, Sol LeWitt, Robert Murray, Lyman Kipp named in Chapter 4).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Object Art opposes the dematerializing, gravity-defying, "scribble in the air" tendency of modern sculpture by reaffirming the solid, palpable, real object; Burnham frames this as a dialectical tension internal to twentieth-century sculpture.
- Object Art also defines itself by negation against dynamic-geometric, vitalist, "open," and pseudo-mechanistic sculpture — "Object sculpture admits to being itself-nothing else" — rejecting the transcendent-image fixation of both the vitalists and the Constructivists.

## Questions It Raises

- If Object Art is "sculpture de-idealized" yet "not free of the idiosyncrasies of human perception," can any object ever reach Burnham's "absolute 'thingification'" — or does perception always re-idealize the inert thing?
- Burnham calls Object Art the static-object pole that reification will inevitably push toward the system. Is the inert Minimal object a genuine reassertion of sculpture-as-object, or merely a way-station on the road to life-simulating, cybernetic art?
- If the meaning of Object Art is relocated from the form to "a series of perceptual experiences" in the viewer, is it still sculpture in any traditional sense, or has it become a branch of Optical Art and phenomenology?
- When the base withers and Object sculpture's "severely geometrical shapes resemble bases more than sculpture," what, if anything, still distinguishes sculpture from the ordinary object?
