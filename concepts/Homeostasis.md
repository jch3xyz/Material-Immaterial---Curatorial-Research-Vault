# Homeostasis

## General Orientation

Homeostasis names the maintenance of steady states against shock and change — order held constant amid "the variations of outer shock and change." Across the corpus it is reached from two directions that share a single core. One line treats homeostasis as a *historical and physiological* fact carrying philosophical weight. For [[N. Katherine Hayles]] (and, through her account, [[Norbert Wiener]]) it is the technical, period-defining concept of *first-wave [[Cybernetics]]* — the maintenance of steady states via negative [[Feedback Loops|feedback]], the founding equivalence that lets the same stabilizing logic run through animals, humans, and machines, and the contingent point at which information was conceived as disembodied stable value. For Wiener that stability is existential and fragile: homeostasis is the "contrarian island" of order held against the entropic tide. The other line *operationalizes or generalizes* the same principle. For [[Jack Burnham]], homeostasis is the biological model — coined by **Walter B. Cannon** for the body's involuntary self-balancing — that he routes through negative [[Negative Feedback (Burnham)|feedback]] into a design goal for cybernetic art, the hinge by which sculpture moves from [[Material vs Immaterial|object]] to [[Systems Theory|system]]. For [[Marshall McLuhan]], homeostasis is an older biological-political figure tied back to Hippocrates' *ponos* ("toil," the fight of the body in disease) and applied to the **city as a form of the body politic**, which answers "irritations" with "counter-irritant" new extensions in pursuit of "staying power, constancy, equilibrium." Hayles historicizes homeostasis as a quantifiable wager about *information*; Wiener charges it with entropy and dread; Burnham makes it the technical premise of an art of systems; McLuhan generalizes it into a law of how social bodies extend themselves under pressure (the "City as Extension of the Body" material folds here, alongside [[Media as Extensions of Man]]). [[Donella Meadows]] supplies what the vault had only secondhand: the *native-discipline mechanism*. In system-dynamics terms the thing every prior account gropes toward is a [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing (goal-seeking) feedback loop]] — a stock held near a goal by a monitoring device and a corrective response (the thermostat-furnace), whose goal must be set to compensate for the continuous draining and filling that act on the stock. The shared core in every case is the same: order held against shock and change.

## Brief Definition

The maintenance of steady states via negative [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] in organisms and machines — the central concept of first-wave [[Cybernetics]] (1945–1960) and, in Hayles's account, the period-defining *constellation* that allied information-as-thing, the signal/noise distinction, and stability, and that won out over [[Reflexivity]] largely because it was the more quantifiable option. In its native systems-dynamics discipline ([[Donella Meadows|Meadows]]) the mechanism is a single [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing feedback loop]] returning a stock to its desired state — kept DISTINCT from [[Dynamic Equilibrium]], the balanced-flow *state* in which inflow equals outflow.

## Longer Explanation

The corpus approaches homeostasis along two broad lines that converge on its physiological origin and split over what to make of it.

**Homeostasis as a historical constellation and an existential wager (Hayles, Wiener).** For [[N. Katherine Hayles]], homeostasis is first of all an inherited biological idea: "Traditionally, homeostasis had been understood as the ability of living organisms to maintain steady states when they are buffeted by fickle environments." Her own gloss is concrete — "When the temperature soars, sweat pours out of the human body so that its internal temperature can remain relatively stable." The decisive cybernetic move of the Macy period was to extend this from organisms to machines: "Like animals, machines can maintain homeostasis using feedback loops." Steam-engine governors had long stabilized mechanical systems, but only in the 1930s and 1940s was the feedback loop "explicitly theorized as a flow of information." Homeostasis is thus the concept that let the same stabilizing logic run indifferently through animals, humans, and machines — the founding equivalence of [[Cybernetics]]. What makes it the *period-defining* concept, in Hayles's reading, is that it names not an isolated idea but a whole constellation of mutually reinforcing commitments. The first wave "took homeostasis as a central concept," and its load-bearing alliance is with information conceived as a *thing*: "Making information a thing allies it with homeostasis, for so defined, it can be transported into any medium and maintain a stable quantitative value, reinforcing the stability that homeostasis implies." On the same side fall the signal/noise distinction and instrumental language — "Thus the construction of information as a signal/noise distinction and the privileging of homeostasis produced and were produced by each other" — while ambiguity, allusion, and metaphor were sorted onto the side of [[Reflexivity]]. To privilege homeostasis is to imagine the goal of a system as "less a specific site than stability itself." Hayles's most pointed claim is that homeostasis prevailed not because it was truer but because it was *tractable*: "Homeostasis won in the first wave largely because it was more manageable quantitatively. Reflexivity lost because specifying and delimiting context quickly ballooned into an unmanageable project." She immediately denaturalizes the victory — "these outcomes are tied to the historical contingencies of the situation," and "Conceptualizing information as a disembodied entity was not an arbitrary decision, but neither was it inevitable" — noting that amid "the hysteria of McCarthyism, homeostasis implied a return to normalcy in more than one sense." Homeostasis is thus where Hayles locates the *contingent* installation of [[Embodiment vs Information|information as disembodied stability]] — the moment the body could begin to become [[The Body as Data|data]]. And it does not simply vanish when the second wave turns to reflexivity; it persists as a *skeuomorph*: "Homeostasis, a foundational concept during the first wave, functioned during the second wave as a skeuomorph" — no longer "an initiating premise" but a gesture used "to authenticate new elements," exerting "an inertial pull" that limited "how radically they could transform the constellation." For [[Norbert Wiener]], read through Hayles, the same principle carries an existential and probabilistic charge. Homeostasis is fragile order purchased against entropy: "Only if creative play is allowed, if the mechanism can adapt freely to changing messages, can homeostasis be maintained, even temporarily, in the face of constant entropic pressure toward degradation." In his cosmology life and homeostasis appear as "contrarian islands" holding out against the entropic tide, and its absence in "the body politic" is a source of cybernetic anxiety.

**Homeostasis as a physiological model generalized into art and politics (Burnham, McLuhan).** Both trace the word to the body and then carry it outward. For [[Jack Burnham]], homeostasis enters art theory through its biological origin — the term **Walter B. Cannon** coined in *The Wisdom of the Body* (1932) for the body's involuntary self-balancing, "the ability of the body to keep its quantities of liquids, solids, and gases in a state of equilibrium," maintained "by various strategies, which include feedback mechanisms." Burnham routes this into a theory of art through negative feedback, "a fractional return of the energy output of a system in the form of information guiding the system's future activities," the equilibrium-securing principle shared by "the chemical equilibrium of the body" and "a servo-controlled processing mechanism." Equilibrium maintained by [[Negative Feedback (Burnham)|negative feedback]] thus becomes the technical hinge of the move from sculpture-as-[[Material vs Immaterial|object]] to sculpture-as-[[Systems Theory|system]], and ultimately of "Cyborg Art." For [[Marshall McLuhan]], homeostasis is the name modern science gives to a very old principle of bodily and political self-maintenance, routed back through Hippocrates' *ponos*: "equilibrium as a strategy of the staying power of any body." He applies it to the **social body** as readily as the biological one — "the city, as a form of the body politic, responds to new pressures and irritations by resourceful new extensions always in the effort to exert staying power, constancy, equilibrium, and homeostasis" — making homeostasis the standing motive of [[Media as Extensions of Man|extension]] itself, scaling from the Greek city-state to Telstar, where the pursuit of equilibrium is also what drives disruptive new extensions. Where Hayles treats homeostasis as a *historically contingent* installation that won because it was quantifiable, and Wiener as a *fragile* order held against entropy, Burnham *operationalizes* it as an art principle and McLuhan generalizes it into a *standing law* of how extended bodies behave.

**Homeostasis as the balancing feedback loop, named in its native discipline (Meadows).** [[Donella Meadows]] is the only voice in the vault writing from *inside* system dynamics, and she gives homeostasis its mechanical grammar. The stabilizing behavior every other author invokes is, in her vocabulary, a [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing feedback loop]]: "goal-seeking structures in systems and are both sources of stability and sources of resistance to change." Its anatomy is exactly the thermostat: "Any balancing feedback loop needs a goal (the thermostat setting), a monitoring and signaling device to detect deviation from the goal (the thermostat), and a response mechanism (the furnace and/or air conditioner, fans, pumps, pipes, fuel, etc.)." A single such loop is precisely the homeostatic case — "A single balancing loop brings a system stock back to its desired state" — while the many-loop generalization is [[Resilience]]. Crucially, Meadows adds the operating condition the secondhand accounts leave implicit: a homeostatic loop cannot simply aim at its target, because the stock is always draining or filling; its goal must be set to *overcompensate*. "A stock-maintaining balancing feedback loop must have its goal set appropriately to compensate for draining or inflowing processes that affect that stock." This is the native-discipline mechanism behind Cannon's self-balancing body, Wiener's order-against-entropy, and McLuhan's body politic; it is kept DISTINCT from [[Dynamic Equilibrium]], which names the balanced-flow *state* (inflow equal to outflow) rather than the goal-seeking *mechanism* that pursues it.

## Marshall McLuhan (Understanding Media)

For [[Marshall McLuhan]], in the chapter "Roads and Paper Routes," homeostasis is the name modern science gives to a very old principle of bodily and political self-maintenance. He arrives at it through the city's history of internal conflict: the city, "formed as a kind of protective hide or shield for man," purchased that protection "at the cost of maximized struggle within the walls," and "it was amidst such irritations that man produced his greatest inventions as counter-irritants." These inventions were "extensions of himself by means of concentrated toil, by which he hoped to neutralize distress." McLuhan then routes the modern term back through antiquity: "The Greek word ponos, or 'toil,' was a term used by Hippocrates, the father of medicine, to describe the fight of the body in disease. Today this idea is called homeostasis, or equilibrium as a strategy of the staying power of any body." Homeostasis is thus, for McLuhan, the *deep continuity* between Hippocrates' diseased body fighting to recover and the modern systems-theoretic vocabulary of equilibrium — confidence: `Directly stated`.

What is distinctive in McLuhan is that he applies homeostasis to the **social body** as readily as the biological one: "All organizations, but especially biological ones, struggle to remain constant in their inner condition amidst the variations of outer shock and change. The man-made social environment as an extension of man's physical body is no exception." The decisive formulation is the city as body politic: "The city, as a form of the body politic, responds to new pressures and irritations by resourceful new extensions always in the effort to exert staying power, constancy, equilibrium, and homeostasis." Here homeostasis becomes the *motive* of [[Media as Extensions of Man|media as extensions]] — every new road, alphabet, or wheel is a "resourceful new extension" by which the body politic answers irritation in the effort to stay constant. McLuhan even folds the whole arc of the city — from the alarm of the village, through the resistance of the city, to the exhaustion and inertia of empire — into a "disease and irritation syndrome," felt "as normal physical expressions of counter-irritant recovery from disease" (confidence: `Directly stated`).

The figure scales up to electric media. McLuhan reads Telstar in the same homeostatic key: "By outering these satellites as extensions of our nervous system, there is an automatic response in all the organs of the body politic of mankind. Such new intensity of proximity imposed by Telstar calls for radical rearrangement of all organs in order to maintain staying power and equilibrium." Where [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] treats homeostasis as a *historically contingent* installation that "won" because it was quantifiable — and that survives only as a skeuomorph once reflexivity displaces it — McLuhan treats it as a *standing law* of how extended bodies behave: the body politic perpetually re-extends itself to restore equilibrium under the shocks of new media (confidence: `Interpretive synthesis` for the contrast; the McLuhan claim itself is `Directly stated`). Notably, McLuhan's "extensions in the effort to exert staying power" are themselves disruptive — "acceleration beyond a point in any system becomes disruption and breakdown" — so the pursuit of homeostasis is, in his account, also what unsettles it.

## Jack Burnham (Beyond Modern Sculpture)

For [[Jack Burnham]], in the final chapter of *Beyond Modern Sculpture* ("Robot and Cyborg Art"), homeostasis enters art theory through its biological origin: it is the term **Walter B. Cannon** coined in *The Wisdom of the Body* (1932) for the body's involuntary self-balancing. Burnham reports it directly — Cannon "called this system of automatic valves and thermostats homeostasis. Defined, this is the ability of the body to keep its quantities of liquids, solids, and gases in a state of equilibrium" — and immediately ties it to feedback: "Cannon found that the body is constantly forcing such a balance by various strategies, which include feedback mechanisms" (confidence: `Directly stated`). This is the [[Homeostasis (Cannon via Burnham)|Cannon-via-Burnham]] sense of the word: equilibrium maintained by automatic correction, the physiological model that cybernetics would generalize to machines.

What makes Burnham distinctive is that he routes homeostasis into a theory of art through **negative feedback**. He defines the mechanism for "both animals and machines" as "a fractional return of the energy output of a system in the form of information guiding the system's future activities," and then names the link to Cannon explicitly: "[[Negative Feedback (Burnham)|Negative feedback]] (as was mentioned with Cannon's homeostasis) is the process whereby any number of coupled systems are designed to maintain a relatively constant input-output relationship. This might be the chemical equilibrium of the body or the constant adjustments of a servo-controlled processing mechanism" (confidence: `Directly stated`). Homeostasis is thus, for Burnham, the equilibrium-seeking principle that negative [[Feedback Loops|feedback]] secures across organisms and servo-machines alike — the same indifference to substrate that lets the same stabilizing logic run through bodies and machines, here imported into the art object as a design goal.

Burnham then makes homeostasis an *art principle*. The self-stabilizing machines of [[Cybernetics]] — W. Ross Ashby's homeostat among them — supply the behavioral repertoire (goal-seeking, equilibrium, "ultrastability") that the first true cybernetic sculptures pursue; Burnham reads Nicolas Schöffer's CYSP I as carrying a "homeostatic device" that balances its sound tower. Where [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] historicizes homeostasis as the *contingent, quantifiable* wager of first-wave cybernetics and [[Marshall McLuhan|McLuhan]] generalizes it into a standing law of the body politic's self-extension, Burnham operationalizes it: equilibrium maintained by negative feedback becomes the technical hinge of the move from sculpture-as-[[Material vs Immaterial|object]] to sculpture-as-[[Systems Theory|system]], and ultimately of "Cyborg Art" — "the cybernetic organism as an art form" (confidence: `Interpretive synthesis` for the three-way contrast; the Burnham claims themselves are `Directly stated`).

## Donella Meadows (Thinking in Systems)

For [[Donella Meadows]], writing from within the discipline of system dynamics, homeostasis is not a special biological or historical phenomenon but a named structural type: the [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing feedback loop]]. Where [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] narrates homeostasis as a period concept of cybernetics, [[Jack Burnham|Burnham]] borrows Cannon's physiology for art, and [[Marshall McLuhan|McLuhan]] routes it through Hippocrates into the body politic, Meadows gives the vault its *native-discipline mechanism* — the primary-source grammar the other authors invoke secondhand. She defines the balancing loop as one of the two elementary feedback structures: they are "goal-seeking structures in systems and are both sources of stability and sources of resistance to change" (confidence: `Directly stated`). This is homeostasis in its most general form: a loop that opposes whatever direction of change is imposed on a stock, holding it at or within a goal value — and, by the same token, resisting deliberate correction, the exact ambivalence McLuhan glimpses when equilibrium-seeking extensions turn disruptive.

The mechanism's anatomy is the thermostat that recurs across the whole corpus, here given its parts explicitly: "Any balancing feedback loop needs a goal (the thermostat setting), a monitoring and signaling device to detect deviation from the goal (the thermostat), and a response mechanism (the furnace and/or air conditioner, fans, pumps, pipes, fuel, etc.)." A goal, a sensor, a corrective effector — the triad behind Cannon's "system of automatic valves and thermostats," Wiener's order held against entropy, and every servomechanism Burnham imports into art. The single-loop case is precisely homeostasis: "A single balancing loop brings a system stock back to its desired state" (confidence: `Directly stated`), with the many-loop generalization being [[Resilience]] — "a rich structure of many feedback loops that can work in different ways to restore a system even after a large perturbation."

Meadows also supplies the operating condition the secondhand accounts leave implicit. Because a real stock is continuously draining or filling, a homeostatic loop cannot simply aim at its target; it must set its goal to *compensate* for those flows or it will chronically fall short: "A stock-maintaining balancing feedback loop must have its goal set appropriately to compensate for draining or inflowing processes that affect that stock" (confidence: `Directly stated`). This is why one sets the thermostat above the desired room temperature, over-pays a debt to beat the interest, or over-hires to cover attrition — the goal is not the target but the target plus the leak. Meadows's balancing loop is therefore the general parent of homeostasis; it must be kept DISTINCT from [[Dynamic Equilibrium]], which names the *state* in which opposing flows are exactly equal so the stock stays level, rather than the goal-seeking *mechanism* that steers a stock toward a set point (confidence: `Interpretive synthesis` for the parent/state distinction; the individual Meadows claims are `Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — Treats homeostasis as a *constellation* and a period marker rather than a neutral biological fact: the central concept of first-wave cybernetics, allied with information-as-thing and signal/noise, that "won" by being quantifiable, and that survives into the second wave only as a skeuomorph. For her it is the historically contingent point at which information was abstracted from materiality.
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** — Carries homeostasis as both technical principle and existential drama. Hayles reads it into his biography (solving a math problem to "regain psychological homeostasis") and into his cosmology, where "life and homeostasis" appear as "contrarian islands" holding out against the entropic tide; "Only if creative play is allowed... can homeostasis be maintained, even temporarily, in the face of constant entropic pressure toward degradation." Homeostasis for Wiener is fragile order purchased against noise, entropy, and death — and its absence in "the body politic" is a source of cybernetic anxiety.
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — Names homeostasis explicitly and ties it to Hippocrates' *ponos*: "equilibrium as a strategy of the staying power of any body." Extends it from the biological body to the social body — "the city, as a form of the body politic," answering "irritations" with "counter-irritant" new extensions in pursuit of "staying power, constancy, equilibrium, and homeostasis." For McLuhan, homeostasis is the standing motive of [[Media as Extensions of Man|extension]] itself, scaling from the Greek city-state to Telstar; the pursuit of equilibrium is also what drives disruptive new extensions.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — Operationalizes homeostasis as an art principle. He takes the biological model from **Walter B. Cannon**'s *The Wisdom of the Body* — "the ability of the body to keep its quantities of liquids, solids, and gases in a state of equilibrium" via feedback — and routes it through [[Negative Feedback (Burnham)|negative feedback]] into a design goal: equilibrium maintained across "the chemical equilibrium of the body" and "a servo-controlled processing mechanism" alike. This is the [[Homeostasis (Cannon via Burnham)|Cannon-via-Burnham]] sense, the technical hinge of the move from sculpture-as-object to sculpture-as-system and of "Cyborg Art."
- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — Supplies the *native-discipline mechanism*. In system-dynamics terms homeostasis is a [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing (goal-seeking) feedback loop]] — "goal-seeking structures in systems and are both sources of stability and sources of resistance to change" — whose anatomy is a goal (the thermostat setting), a monitoring device, and a response mechanism, and which "brings a system stock back to its desired state." She adds the operating condition the others leave implicit: the loop "must have its goal set appropriately to compensate for draining or inflowing processes that affect that stock." Meadows's balancing loop is the general parent of homeostasis; kept DISTINCT from [[Dynamic Equilibrium]] (the balanced-flow state) and generalized by [[Resilience]] (the many-loop case).
- **W. Ross Ashby (homeostat)** — Mechanized the concept. Ashby's homeostat was built so that homeostasis "included the environment as part of the picture," posing the problem as: given some function of the environment, can the organism find an inverse "such that the product of the two will result in a steady state?" Hayles uses the homeostat to show how a "simple machine" smuggled in a "complex network of assumptions" about humans as mechanisms "trying to maintain homeostasis," and Burnham reads its goal-seeking "ultrastability" into the behavioral repertoire of the first cybernetic sculptures.

## Related Concepts

- [[Cybernetics]] — homeostasis is the organizing concept of its first wave; cybernetics is the field, homeostasis its period-defining constellation.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the *mechanism* by which homeostasis is maintained; homeostasis is the steady-state goal, the loop the means. (Distinct: this note is the constellation, not the device.)
- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — Meadows's native-discipline name for the goal-seeking loop that *is* homeostasis; the general parent of which homeostasis is the biological/physiological instance.
- [[Dynamic Equilibrium]] — the balanced-flow *state* (inflow equal to outflow); kept DISTINCT from homeostasis, which is the goal-seeking *mechanism* that pursues a set point.
- [[Resilience]] — the many-loop generalization: where a single balancing loop returns a stock to its desired state (homeostasis), resilience "arises from a rich structure of many feedback loops."
- [[Information]] — homeostasis is allied with information-as-thing, "a stable quantitative value" transportable across media.
- [[The Body as Data]] — homeostasis is where the stabilizing, quantifiable, medium-independent value of information is installed, enabling the body to be read as data.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the later waves (reflexivity, autopoiesis, virtuality) define themselves against and after the homeostasis constellation.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Reflexivity]] — the *rival constellation* and the organizing concept of the second wave; homeostasis stands with information-as-thing, signal/noise, and instrumental language, reflexivity with information-as-action, infinite regress, and ambiguity. Homeostasis "won," reflexivity "lost," in the first wave.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — homeostasis names the contingent moment at which information was conceived as a "disembodied entity" maintaining stable value apart from its material substrate; Hayles contests precisely this separation.
- **Entropy / noise** (Wiener's antagonist) — homeostasis is the temporary order held against entropic degradation and death; for Wiener it is the contrarian island in an entropic sea.

## Questions It Raises

- If homeostasis "won" because it was *quantifiable* rather than *true*, how much of the posthuman picture of information as disembodied, medium-independent value rests on an administrative convenience of the 1940s? (ties to [[Embodiment vs Information]], [[Material vs Immaterial]])
- When homeostasis allies information-as-thing with "a stable quantitative value" that "can be transported into any medium," what has to be done to the body for it to count as such a medium — and is this the origin of [[The Body as Data|the body as data]]?
- Does treating the steady state as "stability itself" rather than "a specific site" already abstract embodiment away, privileging pattern over the fickle, buffeted, sweating body Hayles invokes? (ties to [[Embodiment]])
- If homeostasis survives only as a *skeuomorph* — an authenticating gesture exerting "inertial pull" — does its vestige still shape how the posthuman imagines stability, even after reflexivity and virtuality displace it? (ties to [[Posthumanism]])
- If a homeostatic loop must set its goal to *overcompensate* for continuous draining and filling ([[Donella Meadows|Meadows]]), then the "steady state" is never the true target but the target-plus-the-leak — does this quiet fact undo the picture of homeostasis as simple return to a fixed norm? (ties to [[Balancing Feedback Loop]], [[Dynamic Equilibrium]])
