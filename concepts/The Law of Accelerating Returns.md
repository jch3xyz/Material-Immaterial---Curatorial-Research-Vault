# The Law of Accelerating Returns

## Brief Definition

[[Ray Kurzweil]]'s master mechanism: evolution — biological first, then technological — is an information process whose products and whose pace both grow at least exponentially, with the rate of exponential growth itself growing exponentially (a "double exponential"); it is the engine that makes the [[The Singularity Is Near|Singularity]] argument run.

## Longer Explanation

Kurzweil names the law in a single load-bearing sentence at the head of Chapter 2, "A Theory of Technology Evolution": "The ongoing acceleration of technology is the implication and inevitable result of what I call the law of accelerating returns, which describes the acceleration of the pace of and the exponential growth of the products of an evolutionary process" (`Directly stated`). Two quantities accelerate at once — the *products* of evolution (their power, speed, price-performance) and the *pace* at which new products arrive. The Singularity, for Kurzweil, is not an article of faith but, in his framing, "the inexorable result of the law of accelerating returns."

The mechanism is positive feedback across stages. Each epoch of evolution builds on the fruits of the last — biology builds DNA, DNA builds brains, brains build technology, technology builds better technology — "so the rate of progress of an evolutionary process increases at least exponentially over time" (`Directly stated`). Kurzweil grounds the law in a particular reading of evolution as an order-increasing, *open* (not closed) system: it "draws upon the chaos in the larger system in which it takes place for its options for diversity," and therefore "in an evolutionary process order increases exponentially" — the move that lets him claim the law does not violate the second law of thermodynamics. The "order" in question is not mere complexity but [[Order as Information That Fits a Purpose|information that fits a purpose]], which in biological evolution is survival.

The distinctive claim — what separates Kurzweil from a plain Moore's-Law extrapolation — is the *second-order* acceleration. Because the returns of a process feed back into the resources poured into that process, "this results in a second level of exponential growth; that is, the rate of exponential growth — the exponent — itself grows exponentially" (`Directly stated`). On a logarithmic chart of computing price-performance this shows up as an upward *curve* rather than a straight line: it took three years to double computing's price-performance at the start of the twentieth century, two years in the middle, and about one year by 2000. This is the "double exponential," and it is what underwrites the book's headline forecast (see below).

### The Intuitive-Linear vs. the Historical-Exponential Diagnosis

The argument that converts the reader is a diagnosis of perceptual error, made in Chapter 1, "The Six Epochs." Forecasts of the future "dramatically underestimate the power of future developments because they are based on what I call the 'intuitive linear' view of history rather than the 'historical exponential' view" (`Directly stated`). Kurzweil's mathematical gloss: "an exponential curve looks like a straight line when examined for only a brief duration," so even sophisticated observers extrapolate the *recent* rate of change forward and so under-shoot. The payoff of correcting the error is the book's most quoted figure: "we won't experience one hundred years of technological advance in the twenty-first century; we will witness on the order of twenty thousand years of progress (again, when measured by today's rate of progress), or about one thousand times greater than what was achieved in the twentieth century" (`Directly stated`). The linear/exponential contrast is, for Kurzweil, "the most important failure that prognosticators make."

### Moore's Law as the Fifth Paradigm

Chapter 3, "Achieving the Computational Capacity of the Human Brain," is the hardware demonstration, and its central move is to *subordinate* Moore's Law to the larger law. Shrinking transistor feature sizes "was not, in fact, the first paradigm to bring exponential growth to computation and communication. It was the fifth" (`Directly stated`). Kurzweil enumerates the preceding four: "We've already had five paradigms (electromechanical calculators, relay-based computing, vacuum tubes, discrete transistors, and integrated circuits) that have provided exponential growth to the price-performance and capabilities of computation. Each time a paradigm reached its limits, another paradigm took its place." The consequence is that the much-predicted "demise of Moore's Law" is not a ceiling on computation but the local end of one S-curve; the law of accelerating returns predicts a sixth paradigm ("computing into the molecular third dimension") already taking shape. This is where [[The Law of Accelerating Returns]] connects to [[Paradigm Shift and the S-Curve|paradigm shift and the S-curve]]: innovation "turns the S-curve of any specific paradigm into a continuing exponential."

### The Law as an Economic Theory

Kurzweil is explicit that the law is not merely a curve-fitting exercise: "The law of accelerating returns is fundamentally an economic theory" (`Directly stated`). The engine, he argues, is "the economic imperative of a competitive marketplace," which he treats as "the equivalent of survival in biological evolution" — myriad small advances, each "with its own particular economic justification," summing into the macro trend. He faults "contemporary economic theory and policy" for being "based on outdated models" that overlook computational capacity, bandwidth, intellectual property and knowledge as the "increasingly vital (and increasingly increasing) constituents that are driving the economy." This is the sense in which the law claims to be predictive about markets, education, and human knowledge, not only chips.

### Reach: From Chips to Brain Scanning

The Prologue plants the seed as a "meta-idea" — "that the power of ideas to transform the world is itself accelerating" — and reports that the theory grew out of empirical data gathered in the 1990s on "all information-related technologies." Chapter 4, "How to Reverse Engineer the Human Brain," then *applies* the law to the project the whole Singularity argument depends on: that we will scan and model the brain in time. Brain reverse engineering is, Kurzweil writes, "growing exponentially," and on the prospect of destructive high-resolution scanning he adds: "But again, in accordance with the law of accelerating returns, this potential is growing exponentially, as are all other facets of brain scanning" (`Directly stated`). The law thus does double duty: it predicts that the *hardware* will be cheap enough and that the *knowledge of the brain* will be deep enough, at roughly the same moment — the convergence that, in Kurzweil's telos, makes [[Mind Uploading|mind uploading]] and [[Patternism|patternism]] thinkable.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** (*[[The Singularity Is Near]]*) — the law in its native form: a quantified, optimistic, double-exponential law of evolution-as-information, Moore's Law demoted to its fifth paradigm, framed as an economic theory and used to date the Singularity. The valence is celebratory and inevitabilist ("inexorable result").
- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — receives Kurzweil's law critically (see [[Meghan O'Gieblyn and Ray Kurzweil]]), reading the exponential telos as a re-enchantment narrative in technological dress rather than a neutral empirical finding (`Interpretive synthesis`).

- [[Dataism]] — the worldview that treats reality as information flow; the law of accelerating returns is one of its most confident forecasting instruments.
- [[Transhumanism]] · [[Mind Uploading]] · [[Patternism]] — the projects the law's timeline is marshalled to make plausible.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Acceleration]] — **do not collapse.** Same lexical root, opposite valence. For [[Byung-Chul Han]], acceleration is speed stripped of sense and narrativity — a pathology, "the crisis is not acceleration but loss of narrativity." For Kurzweil, accelerating returns are the very signature of progress and order. Han diagnoses the experience of dis-synchrony and the [[Hell of the Same]]; Kurzweil celebrates the exponent. They name nearly inverse phenomena and must be cross-linked as a contrast, never merged (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Questions It Raises

- Is the "double exponential" an empirical regularity of *all* evolutionary processes, or a curve fitted retrospectively to a hand-picked set of information technologies?
- If the law is "fundamentally an economic theory," does it survive the absence of the competitive marketplace it names as its engine — and is "economic imperative as the equivalent of survival" an analogy or an identity?
- Does treating a *historical trend* as a *law* smuggle inevitability ("inexorable result") into what is at most an extrapolation?
- Can a measure of "order as information that fits a purpose" be defined non-circularly when the purpose (survival, then market value) is supplied after the fact?
- What does the intuitive-linear/historical-exponential framing share with — and how does it differ from — Han's claim that the real change is not speed but the loss of narrative time?
