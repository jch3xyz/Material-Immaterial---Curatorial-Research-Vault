## Brief Definition

Surveillance is the systematic, asymmetric observation, recording, and analysis of human conduct — in Zuboff's account, the *corporate*, unilateral, secret claiming of human experience as free raw material that "puts the 'surveillance' in surveillance capitalism."

## Longer Explanation

For Shoshana Zuboff, surveillance is not primarily the state watching its citizens (the Orwellian or panoptic image) but a new *economic* operation: surveillance capitalism "unilaterally claims human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioral data." The watching is performed by private firms — Google as "the pioneer, discoverer, elaborator, experimenter, lead practitioner, role model, and diffusion hub" — whose business is to extract `Behavioral Surplus` (data beyond what service improvement requires), refine it through machine intelligence into `Prediction Products`, and sell those forecasts in behavioral futures markets. Surveillance here is the input side of that supply chain.

What makes this *surveillance* rather than mere data collection is its character. In the rendition chapters Zuboff names the five adjectives that "put the 'surveillance' in surveillance capitalism": the operation is "typically unauthorized, unilateral, gluttonous, secret, and brazen." These traits encode a deep asymmetry of knowledge and power: surveillance capitalists "know everything about us, whereas their operations are designed to be unknowable to us." Knowledge flows *from* us but not *for* us — the privatization of `The Division of Learning in Society`. The concrete operations that cross the gap from lived experience to extractable data Zuboff calls `Rendition`: the rendering of bodies, locations, faces, emotions, and conduct into behavioral data, often outside awareness and under "the dictatorship of no alternatives."

Surveillance is therefore the perceptual organ of a new apparatus Zuboff names `Big Other` — "the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior." Big Other is staged explicitly against Orwell's Big Brother: there is "no brother here of any kind, big or little, evil or good." Where totalitarian surveillance sought to possess the soul through terror, Zuboff's surveillance is governed by *radical indifference* — "a form of observation without witness" that reduces experience to measurable behavior while staying steadfastly indifferent to its meaning. The watching does not care who we are; it cares only that we can be rendered, predicted, and tuned.

Crucially, Zuboff insists this surveillance is not technologically inevitable but "a specifically constructed human choice" — a *logic that commands technology*, the puppet master and not the puppet. The same ubiquitous computing that produced the sovereign, occupant-controlled "Aware Home" also produced the extractive Nest thermostat that ships its data upstream. Surveillance, on this view, is contingent and contestable: it can in principle be interrupted (it "rests on this original sin" of dispossession), which is why naming it precisely is the first move toward contesting it.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — surveillance as the *corporate, economic* operation at the heart of surveillance capitalism: unauthorized, unilateral, secret extraction of human experience as free raw material, asymmetric "knowledge from us but not for us," operationalized as `Rendition` and instrumented through `Big Other` under the governing disposition of radical indifference. Distinct from (and in her view more dangerous than) classical state/totalitarian surveillance because it does not need our conformity and is animated by `Instrumentarian Power` rather than terror.
- *Open to other authors.* This note is the vault's top-level `Surveillance` hub; it anticipates sections from Han (voluntary self-exposure / the `Digital Panopticon`), and from the Foucault/Deleuze disciplinary-and-control lineage, where surveillance is theorized from the watched subject's side rather than the watching capital's side. ## Opposing Concepts

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — Zuboff frames surveillance as a threat to "the right to the future tense," "an essential condition of free will"; instrumentarian surveillance treats autonomy as friction ("the sin of autonomy"), placing it on the determinist pole against the self-determining subject.
- The right to sanctuary / the inviolable refuge — surveillance's "no exit" world is the negation of the sanctuary (the home as refuge) that opens Zuboff's book; plain text until its dedicated note is anchored.

## Questions It Raises

- If surveillance is corporate and economic rather than primarily statist, do legal categories built for state surveillance (privacy, the Fourth Amendment, monopoly) even reach it?
- Zuboff argues surveillance does not *need* our conformity (unlike totalitarianism). What forms of resistance remain effective against a watcher that is indifferent to whether we comply or object?
- Is the asymmetry — "knowledge from us but not for us" — corrigible by transparency and consent, or is consent itself a "fig leaf" ("privacy policies are more aptly referred to as surveillance policies")?
- How does Zuboff's *unilateral, secret* surveillance relate to Han's *voluntary self-exposure*? Are these rival accounts of one apparatus, or two distinct mechanisms operating at once?
- If surveillance is a contingent "human choice" rather than a technological inevitability, what would it mean to interrupt rendition — the "original sin" on which the whole enterprise rests?
