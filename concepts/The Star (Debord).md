# The Star (Debord)

## Brief Definition

In Guy Debord's *The Society of the Spectacle*, the star is the celebrity as the human agent of the spectacle — a "spectacular representation of living human beings" who dramatizes permitted roles and, on entering the spectacle, "renounces all autonomous qualities," becoming the opposite of an individual (theses 60–61).

## Longer Explanation

The star is Debord's name for the point at which the [[Spectacle]] takes human form. Where the commodity had already colonized the world of things, the star extends that colonization into the person: living human beings become "superficial objects that people can identify with in order to compensate for the fragmented productive specializations that they actually live" (th. 60). The star is thus not a gifted individual but a *specialist of apparent life* — a screen onto which the spectator projects a wholeness that the spectator's own fragmented labor denies them. The celebrity's freedom, mobility, and "fullness" are precisely the "inaccessible results of social labor," the by-products of production "magically projected above it as its ultimate goals: power and vacations" (th. 60). `Directly stated`.

Debord splits the star into two archetypes that map the two poles of the spectacular economy. The **star of consumption** embodies lifestyle and the "entire realm of consumption," modeling equal happiness available through the commodity; the **star of decision-making** embodies power and "must possess the full range of admired human qualities," so that "official differences between them are thus canceled out by the official similarity implied by their supposed excellence in every field of endeavor" (th. 61). The two can even trade places — "a governmental power may personalize itself as a pseudo-star" while "a star of consumption may campaign for recognition as a pseudo power over life" (th. 60) — because both are functions of the same system: the decision-making and the consumption "that are at the beginning and the end of a process that is never questioned." `Directly stated`.

The star's defining move is a self-cancellation. "The agent of the spectacle who is put on stage as a star is the opposite of an individual; he is as clearly the enemy of his own individuality as of the individuality of others" (th. 61). Entering the spectacle "as a model to be identified with, he renounces all autonomous qualities in order to identify himself with the general law of obedience to the flow of things" (th. 61). This is a form of [[Reification]] applied to the person: the human being is emptied of interiority and autonomy and made to circulate as an image. Debord's examples — Khrushchev retroactively made a general, Kennedy "surviving as an orator" through a ghostwriter's continuity — show that "the admirable people who personify the system are well known for not being what they seem" (th. 61). `Directly stated`.

The star reaches its purest form under the [[Concentrated Spectacle]], where the good is "concentrated in a single individual, the guarantor of the system's totalitarian cohesion" — the "absolute star" with whom "everyone must magically identify... or disappear" (th. 64). Read forward, the star anticipates the later logic of visibility that this vault's Han cluster names [[Exhibition Value]]: the person who has value only insofar as it is put on display and identified-with, having "renounced all autonomous qualities" to exist as pure exhibition. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — the star as the human agent of the spectacle: a "spectacular representation of living human beings" who projects "general banality into images of permitted roles" (th. 60) and, in entering the spectacle, becomes "the opposite of an individual" by renouncing "all autonomous qualities" (th. 61). Splits into the star of consumption and the star of decision-making, converging in the "absolute star" of the [[Concentrated Spectacle]] (th. 64).

## Related Concepts

- [[Spectacle]] — the star is the spectacle personified; the general concept of which the star is the human agent.
- [[Concentrated Spectacle]] — its "absolute star," the single individual with whom the totalitarian order demands universal identification.
- [[Exhibition Value]] — the Han-cluster term the star prefigures: value that exists only in being displayed and identified-with.
- [[Reification]] — the star is reification turned on the human being, who is emptied of autonomy and made to circulate as an image.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-possessed individual; the star is expressly "the opposite of an individual," its enemy, having "renounced all autonomous qualities" (th. 61).

## Questions It Raises

- If the star "renounces all autonomous qualities," is the spectator who identifies with the star performing the same renunciation at one remove?
- Does the two-fold star (consumption / decision-making) collapse the distinction between market and state, or merely reveal that they were always one system?
- Is Debord's 1967 star continuous with the later "society of exhibition" and its [[Exhibition Value]], or does digital self-exhibition make everyone a star and so dissolve the category?
