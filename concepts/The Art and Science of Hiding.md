# The Art and Science of Hiding

## Brief Definition

[[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s name for the vanguard of activists, artists, and inventors who, under "the intolerable conditions of glass life," create wearables, masks, makeup, and software to make the human body and face invisible to computer vision and facial recognition — a brilliant artistic resistance Zuboff honors as "canaries in the coal mine" yet warns is finally a counter-declaration that "risks becomes an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage."

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 17 of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, Zuboff observes that just as "too many of the best and brightest of a new generation devote their genius to the intensification of the click-stream," an opposite vocation has emerged: "a new generation of activists, artists, and inventors feels itself called to create the art and science of hiding." The condition that compels them is what she elsewhere calls glass life — total exposure to [[Big Other]]'s ubiquitous sensate apparatus — and their aim is the negation of that exposure: "The intolerable conditions of glass life compel these young artists to dedicate their genius to the prospects of human invisibility, even as their creations demand that we aggressively seek and find our bearings." (`Directly stated`)

The work is, as the phrase insists, both art and engineering. Zuboff catalogs the provocations: "signal-blocking phone cases, false fingerprint prosthetics… LED privacy visors to impede facialrecognition cameras, a quilted coat that blocks radio waves and tracking devices… a clothing line called 'Glamouflage' featuring shirts covered with representations of celebrity faces to confuse facial-recognition software, anti-neuroimaging surveillance headgear… and an anti-surveillance coat that creates a shield to block invasive signals." Chicago artist Leo Selvaggio prints "3-D–printed resin prosthetic masks to confound facial recognition," calling his effort "an organized artistic intervention" (`Directly stated`). The exemplary figure is the Berlin-based artist Adam Harvey, whose practice "begins with 'reverse engineering… computer vision algorithms' in order to detect and exploit their vulnerabilities through camouflage and other forms of hiding." His "Stealth Wear" uses "silver-plated fabrics" that "reflect thermal radiation, 'enabling the wearer to avert overhead thermal surveillance,'" and is inspired by traditional Islamic dress, whose idea that "garments can provide a separation between man and God" Harvey "redirects… to create garments that separate human experience from the powers that surveil." His "Think Privacy Selfie Mirror" — a bestseller at Manhattan's New Museum, surfaced "Today's Selfie Is Tomorrow's Biometric Profile" — turns the vanity object into an indictment of the [[Quantified Self|rendition of the body into biometric data]] (`Directly stated`).

The vanguard extends from garments to software and installation. Benjamin Grosser's Facebook and Twitter "demetricators" delete the visible counts of likes, friends, and followers ("Remove the numbers and find out"), and his "Go Rando" extension "obfuscates your feelings on Facebook" by randomizing the emoji behind every "Like," "thus undermining the corporation's surplus analyses as they compute personality and emotional profiles." Trevor Paglen's performance art "combines music, photography, satellite imagery, and artificial intelligence to reveal Big Other's omnipresent knowing and doing" — "trying to look inside the software that is running an AI." Ai Weiwei's 2017 installation "Hansel & Gretel" stages an experience in which "participants viscerally confront the surveillance implications of their own innocent picture taking." Read through the vault's art/new-media cluster, these works are [[Art as Experiential Technology]] turned defensive — feedback-driven, participatory artifacts whose medium is the surveilling apparatus itself — and a contemporary, adversarial chapter of [[New Media Art]]. (`Directly stated` for the works; `Interpretive synthesis` for the cross-cluster placement)

Yet Zuboff's framing is double. The vanguard is a sign to be read: "Our artists, like our young people, are canaries in the coal mine. That the need to make ourselves invisible is the theme of a brilliant artistic vanguard is another kind of message in a bottle cast from the front lines of mourning and revulsion" (`Directly stated`). The honor she pays it is inseparable from the limit she names. The art and science of hiding is, in her schema, a *counter*-declaration — a defensive measure that leaves the offending facts intact — and so it shares the counter-declaration's structural weakness: "Glass life is intolerable, but so is fitting our faces with masks and draping our bodies in digitally resistant fabrics to thwart the ubiquitous lawless machines. Like every counter-declaration, hiding risks becomes an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage. These conditions are unacceptable. Tunnels under this wall are not enough. This wall must come down." (`Directly stated`)

This is why the concept is the keystone bridge between the vault's surveillance and art clusters: it is the precise point where anti-surveillance aesthetics meet Zuboff's political economy. The artist who hides is performing the [[The Hiding Strategy|hiding strategy]] in reverse — concealing the watched rather than the watcher — and thereby contesting the asymmetry of knowledge and power that the firm's own secrecy installs. But for Zuboff the symmetry is incomplete and self-defeating: individual invisibility, however inventive, "does not challenge or alter the offending practice." The art and science of hiding defends the [[The Right to Sanctuary|right to sanctuary]] privately where only a *synthetic* declaration — collective, institutional — could secure it. The vanguard's brilliance is thus a diagnosis of the wound, not its cure; its very necessity is the outrage. (`Strongly implied`; cross-read with [[The Counter-Declaration and the Synthetic Declaration]])

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** ([[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]], Ch.17) — coins the phrase and uses it normatively in two registers at once. As *celebration*: the anti-surveillance vanguard (Harvey, Selvaggio, Grosser, Paglen, Ai Weiwei) is a "brilliant artistic vanguard," a moral "canary in the coal mine" whose work makes the intolerability of glass life sensible and demands that we "find our bearings." As *critique*: because hiding is a counter-declaration, it "risks becomes an adaptation" — a way of making glass life survivable that lets the wall stand. The art and science of hiding is the aesthetic face of [[The Counter-Declaration and the Synthetic Declaration|the counter-declaration]].

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Knowledge vs Power]] — the axis the practice contests rather than reverses: it tries to shrink the asymmetry of what the firm can know, but as a counter-declaration it operates within, and so concedes, the declared order. Listed here because hiding fights the power-through-knowledge imbalance without dismantling it.

## Questions It Raises

- If anti-surveillance art is "a brilliant artistic vanguard" *and* a counter-declaration that "risks becomes an adaptation," what would a *synthetic* artistic or curatorial intervention look like — one that brings the wall down rather than tunnels under it?
- Zuboff reads these works through political economy; the Burnham/Ascott cluster reads art as system, feedback, and participation. Does the art and science of hiding belong to [[New Media Art]] as a genre, or is it better understood as a moral signal whose value is diagnostic rather than aesthetic?
- Is the body-and-face camouflage of Harvey, Selvaggio, and the Glamouflage line a defense of [[The Right to Sanctuary|sanctuary]], or — like the masks Zuboff finds "intolerable" to wear — itself a symptom of sanctuary already lost?
- Can a practice premised on individual invisibility ever scale into the collective "We will" of a synthetic declaration, or does its very form ("I opt out for me") confine it to adaptation?
