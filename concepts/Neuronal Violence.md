# Neuronal Violence

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], **neuronal violence** is the new, system-immanent **violence of positivity** — "a terror of immanence" that is "not viral because it does not derive from immunological negativity," and that "does not deprive, it saturates; it does not exclude, it exhausts."

## Longer Explanation

Han opens *The Burnout Society* by periodizing pathology: each age has its "signature afflictions," and the twenty-first century is determined "neither by bacteria nor by viruses, but by neurons." The neuronal illnesses he names — "depression, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), borderline personality disorder (BPD), and burnout syndrome" — "are not infections, but infarctions; they do not follow from the negativity of what is immunologically foreign, but from an excess of positivity." Neuronal violence is the name Han gives to whatever produces these infarctions: a violence that "does not proceed from system-foreign negativity," but is "systemic — that is, system-immanent — violence" (`Directly stated`).

The decisive move is to define this violence by what it does *not* do. "The violence of positivity does not deprive, it saturates; it does not exclude, it exhausts. That is why it proves inaccessible to unmediated perception." It is not a privation, an exclusion, or an attack — the grammar of immunological defence — but a surplus: it "derives from overproduction, overachievement, and overcommunication" and "is no longer 'viral.'" Where viral and immunological violence presuppose an Other, an Enemy, a singularity "hostile to the system," neuronal violence "does not presume or require hostility. It unfolds specifically in a permissive and pacified society. Consequently, it proves more invisible than viral violence" (`Directly stated`).

This invisibility is structural, not accidental. Neuronal violence "inhabits the negativity-free space of the Same, where no polarization between inside and outside, or proper and foreign, takes place." Because it is immanent — welling up from within the system rather than intruding from outside — "it does not involve immune defence." Han states the contrast at its sharpest through a mythic figure: "Medusa is surely the immunological Other in its extreme form… radical alterity that one cannot behold without perishing." Neuronal violence, by contrast, "escapes all immunological optics, for it possesses no negativity." It is "a terror of immanence" that "differs radically from horror that emanates from the foreign in the immunological sense" (`Directly stated`).

Much of the chapter is a critique of Baudrillard, whose theory of power Han judges "riddled with leaps of argument and vague definitions because it attempts to describe the violence of positivity — or, in other words, the violence of the Same when no Otherness is involved — in immunological terms." Baudrillard's "viral violence," even when it "operates hyperpositively, like cancerous cells," still "obeys the immunological scheme: the enemy virus intrudes into a system, which functions immunologically and fights off the invader." Han's correction is that "the genealogy of hostility does not coincide with the genealogy of violence": the violence of positivity has no hostility at its root at all. The terminal symptom is burnout itself — "Burnout syndrome occurs when the ego overheats, which follows from too much of the Same. The hyper in hyperactivity is not an immunological category. It represents the massification of the positive" (`Strongly implied` for the synthesis; constituent quotations `Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — *The Burnout Society* (ch. "Neuronal Power"): neuronal violence is the system-immanent violence of positivity that produces "psychic infarctions" (depression, ADHD, BPD, burnout). It saturates rather than deprives, exhausts rather than excludes; it is non-viral, non-immunological, and "more invisible than viral violence" because it unfolds in a "permissive and pacified society" with no Enemy to defend against. Han develops it against Baudrillard's immunological misreading of the violence of the Same.

## Related Concepts

- [[Positivity]] — neuronal violence *is* "the violence of positivity"; it is the harm proper to an excess of the positive, distinct from the broader concept of positivity as such.
- [[Immunological Age]] — the prior paradigm neuronal violence has left behind; the "past century was an immunological age," organized on inside/outside, friend/foe, self/other, which neuronal violence escapes entirely.
- [[Hell of the Same]] — the "negativity-free space of the Same" that neuronal violence inhabits; burnout "follows from too much of the Same."
- [[Achievement Society]] — the social form whose "overproduction, overachievement, and overcommunication" generate the surplus positivity from which neuronal violence derives.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — neuronal violence "possesses no negativity"; it is precisely the violence that is *not* the negation-of-negation structure of immune defence. The dialectic it follows is "not the dialectic of negativity, but that of positivity."

## Questions It Raises

- If neuronal violence "proves inaccessible to unmediated perception," how can a critique or a politics even locate its object — and does saturation foreclose the distance from which resistance was once possible?
- Han argues hostility and violence have separate genealogies. What forms of harm fall outside *both* the immunological and the neuronal — or does the violence of positivity now subsume the field?
- Is the move from "deprive/exclude" to "saturate/exhaust" a genuine historical break, or a redescription of capitalist overproduction in pathological-medical terms?
- How does neuronal violence relate to the self-inflicted character of [[Auto-Exploitation]] — is the violence of positivity ultimately a violence one does to oneself? (`Speculative connection`)
