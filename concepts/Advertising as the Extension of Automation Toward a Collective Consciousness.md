# Advertising as the Extension of Automation Toward a Collective Consciousness

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's reframing of advertising not as the persuasion of individual consumers but as "a crude attempt to extend the principles of automation to every aspect of society" — a handicraft groping toward "the ultimate electronic goal of a collective consciousness" and "a programmed harmony among all human impulses."

## Longer Explanation

The keystone idea of the Ads chapter is that advertising has stopped being about products. "The product matters less as the audience participation increases," McLuhan writes, and "The trend in ads, then, is away from the consumer picture of product to the producer image of process." Ads now present "compressed images that include producer and consumer, seller and society in a single image" — the corporate image of an "all-inclusive and never-ending process" rather than a private commodity. Persuasion of the individual is the wrong frame; the ad is a "vigorous dramatization of communal experience," built by teams who "have billions to spend annually on research and testing of reactions" and whose products are "magnificent accumulations of material about the shared experience and feelings of the entire community" (`Directly stated`).

From that observation McLuhan makes the chapter's largest claim, the one this note is built on: "To put the matter abruptly, the advertising industry is a crude attempt to extend the principles of automation to every aspect of society." Advertising is thus folded into the automation apparatus rather than treated as a separate marketing function. Its aim is total integration: "Ideally, advertising aims at the goal of a programmed harmony among all human impulses and aspirations and endeavors." And the endpoint is not a sale but a fusion of minds: "Using handicraft methods, it stretches out toward the ultimate electronic goal of a collective consciousness." The figure is at once technological and almost theological — a "pre-established harmony" of "all production and all consumption" with "all desire and all effort," at which point "advertising will have liquidated itself by its own success" (`Directly stated`).

This places advertising squarely inside the book's electric-CNS framework. Because electric technology has extended the central nervous system outside the body (`[[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]]`), the medium that addresses it does not reach an arm's-length audience but works on the sensorium directly: ads "are intended as subliminal pills for the subconscious in order to exercise an hypnotic spell," operating on "the very advanced principle that a small pellet or pattern in a noisy, redundant barrage of repetition will gradually assert itself." Advertising "push[es] the principle of noise all the way to the plateau of persuasion" — a "depth principle of onslaught on the unconscious." Read alongside `[[Media as Extensions of Man]]` and `[[The Global Village]]`, the "collective consciousness" advertising reaches toward is the social nervous system electrically pooled and made to move "in any tempo or pattern" the operator chooses — the same capture diagnosed in `[[Leasing the Central Nervous System]]`, now extended from the medium itself to the corporate management of every impulse (`Strongly implied`).

The interpretive payoff is a striking upstream bridge. Decades before behavioral data, McLuhan describes an apparatus whose goal is not the rational choice of an autonomous consumer but the engineering of "programmed harmony among all human impulses" — power exercised on the affective and unconscious layer of a collective, toward a state in which steering and being steered become indistinguishable. That is recognizably the terrain Han names `[[Psychopolitics]]` and `[[Emotional Capitalism]]` (power that mines and modulates feeling rather than disciplining bodies) and that Harari names `[[Society as Algorithm]]` and `[[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves]]` (the dream of total behavioral prediction and orchestration). McLuhan predates all of them and shares none of their vocabulary or data infrastructure, so the connection is a vault bridge, not his attribution; it is labeled `Speculative connection` and developed in `[[Marshall McLuhan and Byung-Chul Han]]` and `[[Marshall McLuhan and Yuval Noah Harari]]`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — in *Understanding Media* (Ch. 23) defines advertising as "a crude attempt to extend the principles of automation to every aspect of society," aiming at "a programmed harmony among all human impulses" and "the ultimate electronic goal of a collective consciousness." The frame is diagnostic and structural, not moralizing about deception: the scandal is not false copy but the automation of the social unconscious toward pre-established harmony.

> Cross-author resonances below are interpretive bridges, not McLuhan's own attributions. Han and Harari do not cite this passage; the convergence is the vault's, captured in `[[Marshall McLuhan and Byung-Chul Han]]` and `[[Marshall McLuhan and Yuval Noah Harari]]`.

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (resonance) — `[[Psychopolitics]]` and `[[Emotional Capitalism]]` describe a later, datafied stage of the same project: power that works on impulse and emotion rather than on the rational consumer, soliciting and steering affect toward consumption. McLuhan's "programmed harmony among all human impulses" reads as a forerunner of Han's affect-mining psychopower (`Speculative connection`).
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (resonance) — `[[Dataism]]`, `[[Society as Algorithm]]`, and `[[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves]]` give the "collective consciousness" a computational body: a society modeled as one data-processing system whose impulses can be predicted and orchestrated. McLuhan's "handicraft methods" stretching toward an "electronic goal" anticipate the apparatus Harari describes once the handicraft is automated by data (`Speculative connection`).

## Opposing Concepts

- The implied counter-position is McLuhan's hope for conscious recognition of media for what they are — the artist's "integral awareness" that turns subliminal onslaught into something "consciously attended to" and therefore "comical," draining its hypnotic power. He notes that "any ad consciously attended to is comical" because "ads are not meant for conscious consumption," so awareness is itself the resistance to programmed harmony (`Strongly implied`).

## Questions It Raises

- If advertising's success would mean it has "liquidated itself" in a "pre-established harmony" of all desire and effort, is the "collective consciousness" a utopia of frictionless integration or the abolition of the difference between wanting and being made to want?
- McLuhan calls the methods "handicraft" reaching toward an "electronic goal." When that goal is reached by data — when `[[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves]]` replace the ad team's "research and testing of reactions" — does the project change in kind, or only in efficiency?
- If ads work on "the shared experience and feelings of the entire community," is the unit of persuasion ever really the individual at all — and what becomes of the autonomous consumer that critiques of "false and misleading ad copy" presuppose?
- Can "programmed harmony among all human impulses" be distinguished from domination, given that McLuhan frames it as harmony rather than coercion?
