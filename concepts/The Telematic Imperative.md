# The Telematic Imperative

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's claim that once telematic networks dissolve geographical boundaries, the single ethical and existential mandate that remains is to connect — a condition under which territorial aggression and polarized politics become irrelevant and even the self turns permeable.

## Longer Explanation

The Telematic Imperative is Ascott's radicalisation of E. M. Forster's famous epigraph "only connect," extended from the interior moral life of the individual to the planetary scale of the networked world. In Ascott's 1996 lexicon *Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions for the Post-Biological Era*, the entry reads in full: "When there are no more geographical boundaries, territorial aggression is as irrelevant as polarized politics. The only imperative is to connect. Nowadays even the self is permeable." The imperative is not a moral exhortation laid over the network from outside; it is the condition the network produces. Where there is no territory, there is nothing to defend and nothing to conquer, and the political antagonisms organised around bounded ground lose their object [Directly stated].

The concept carries three linked moves. First, a dissolution thesis: telematic connectivity erases the geography on which territorial aggression and polarised politics depend, so those structures become "irrelevant" rather than merely opposed [Directly stated]. Second, a reduction to a single mandate: of all possible imperatives, only one survives the dissolution — *to connect* — which Ascott elsewhere in the same lexicon turns into the connective ethos of the technoetic artist [Strongly implied]. Third, a thesis about the self: connection does not leave the connector intact but renders the self "permeable," open and porous to the network rather than sealed and bounded — the same decentred, distributed, multiple self Ascott names elsewhere with "I connect, therefore I am multiple" [Directly stated].

The imperative is the ethical face of [[Telenoia]], Ascott's networked-consciousness opposite of paranoia: where the industrial-era self was "anxious, alienated, secretive, and neurotically private," the telematic self is open, connected, and planetary. It is also the operational principle of [[Connectivism]] (the way of the technoetic artist) and the first step of Ascott's fivefold path — "connectivity, immersion, interaction, transformation, and emergence." Connection is thus both the precondition (you must be on the net) and the value (the only thing the net asks of you) [Interpretive synthesis].

Because the imperative makes the self permeable rather than sovereign, it sits in productive tension with the bounded liberal subject. Ascott embraces exactly the porosity that other authors treat warily: the permeable, distributed self is the cyborg's [[Fractured Identities|fractured]], partial self affirmed rather than mourned, and the negation of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]]'s self-possessed autonomy. Whether permeability is liberation or dissolution is the question the imperative leaves open [Speculative connection].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — The Telematic Imperative is Ascott's coinage. It belongs to his late, third-phase "technoetic" vocabulary, alongside [[Network Consciousness]], [[Telenoia]], and [[Connectivism]]. The imperative is the compressed ethical statement of the larger argument of [[The Telematic Embrace]]: that the proper subject of art and life under the network is not appearance or matter but connection and consciousness. It generalises E. M. Forster's "only connect" from the individual to the planet, and ties the mandate to connect to a corresponding transformation of the self into something permeable [Directly stated].

## Related Concepts

- [[Connectivism]] — the imperative is the ethic; connectivism is "the way of the technoetic artist" that practices it.
- [[Telenoia]] — the affective/cultural state the imperative produces: networked consciousness replacing industrial-era paranoia.
- [[Network Consciousness]] — the planetary mind the imperative-to-connect builds toward.
- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the title concept; the imperative is its one-line ethical core.
- [[Connective Criticism]] — Ascott's networked, non-judgmental critical practice, an instance of the connect-first imperative applied to art discourse.
- [[The Post-Biological Era]] — the framing epoch in which "even the self is permeable."
- [[The Global Village]] — McLuhan's networked planet; the imperative is the ethical successor to global-village interdependence [Interpretive synthesis].
- [[Posthumanism]] — the permeable, distributed self is a posthuman subject.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Connection vs Narcissism]] — the tension the imperative sits inside: the mandate to connect can produce genuine telenoic openness, or it can collapse back into narcissistic self-display through the same channels. The imperative names connection as the value; the tension marks its failure mode.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the bounded, self-possessed, sovereign self that the imperative's "permeable" self negates.

## Questions It Raises

- If the only imperative is to connect, is *what* one connects to or *why* simply unaskable — does the imperative evacuate connection of content?
- Does the dissolution of geographical boundaries really make territorial aggression "irrelevant," or does it relocate antagonism to new, non-geographical fronts (informational, infrastructural, attentional)?
- Is a "permeable" self a liberation from the sealed liberal subject, or the loss of the very boundary that makes a self a self?
- Can the imperative to connect be distinguished, in practice, from a compulsion to be permanently available — and at what point does telenoic connection become its narcissistic double?
