# The Means of Behavioral Modification

## Brief Definition

The global apparatus through which surveillance capitalism does not merely *know* behavior but *shapes* it at scale toward guaranteed commercial outcomes — a "means of behavioral modification" that Zuboff names by deliberate echo and inversion of Marx's "means of production," and which it eclipses as the fountainhead of capitalist wealth and power in the twenty-first century.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], the means of behavioral modification names the second of surveillance capitalism's two great machineries. The first is *extraction*: the unilateral claiming of human experience as [[Behavioral Surplus]] (see [[The Extraction Imperative]]). But knowing is not enough. Driven by [[The Prediction Imperative]] — the competitive demand for ever-more-certain prediction products — surveillance capitalists discover that the most predictive data come from *intervening* in behavior itself. So extraction is twinned with *execution*: "The extraction architecture is combined with a new execution architecture, through which hidden economic objectives are imposed upon the vast and varied field of behavior." When these material infrastructures begin "to function as a coherent whole, they produce a twenty-first-century 'means of behavioral modification.'" Its aim, Zuboff is precise, "is not to impose behavioral norms, such as conformity or obedience, but rather to produce behavior that reliably, definitively, and certainly leads to desired commercial results." (`Directly stated`)

The name is a calculated provocation. Zuboff structures her whole diagnosis as a rewriting of Marx's grammar of capital. In surveillance capitalism the relationship inverts: "the production of goods and services is subordinated to a new global architecture of behavioral modification." The ostensible product — the search, the app, the device — is demoted to a means; behavioral modification becomes the real end. By the close of the book the formula is stated flatly: "Under surveillance capitalism, the 'means of production' serves the 'means of behavioral modification.' Machine processes replace human relationships so that certainty can replace trust." And the historic shift in the locus of power: "Power was once identified with the ownership of the means of production, but it is now identified with ownership of the means of behavioral modification that is [[Big Other]]." This is the conceptual hinge that makes the note an *opposing* development of [[Karl Marx]] rather than a borrowing — Zuboff keeps Marx's structural question (who owns the engine of wealth?) while replacing the engine. (`Directly stated`)

The apparatus is not a knowing machine but an *actuating* one. Its concrete techniques — tuning, herding, and conditioning — are the substance of [[Economies of Action]], the phase in which surveillance capitalists move from predicting behavior to making it happen ("make them dance"). "Ubiquitous computing is not just a knowing machine; it is an actuating machine designed to produce more certainty about us and for them." The trajectory Zuboff traces is a reorientation from knowledge to power: "this decade-and-a-half trajectory has taken us from automating information flows about you to automating you." This is also where the concept connects to the broader surveillance cluster: the means of behavioral modification is the *economic* engine beneath Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[Smart Power]], and the institutional, for-profit machinery that operationalizes [[Dataism]]. (`Directly stated` / `Interpretive synthesis` for the cross-author placement.)

Finally, the concept carries a political charge through its scale and lawlessness. "In its latest incarnation, behavioral modification comes to life as a global digital market architecture unfettered by geography, independent of constitutional constraints, and formally indifferent to the risks it poses to freedom, dignity, or the sustenance of the liberal order." Where mid-twentieth-century behavior modification was aimed at a walled-off "them" — prisoners, captives, military enemies — its surveillance-capitalist form is ambient and aimed at everyone. Its full theoretical exposition — B. F. Skinner's operant conditioning, radical behaviorism, and the metamorphosis into [[Instrumentarian Power]] and [[Big Other]] — unfolds in the later chapters; this note is the forward-anchor that names the concept and tracks its first appearances. (`Directly stated`; the Skinner/instrumentarian exposition is `Strongly implied` here, fully developed in chs. 12–13 and beyond.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins "the means of behavioral modification" as a deliberate echo and inversion of Marx's "means of production." It is the *execution* arm of surveillance capitalism (extraction being the first), realized through [[Economies of Action]] (tuning, herding, conditioning) and culminating in ownership of [[Big Other]]. It "eclipses ownership of the means of production as the fountainhead of capitalist wealth and power in the twenty-first century."
- **[[Karl Marx]]** (the structure being rewritten) — Zuboff retains Marx's framing question (power lies with whoever owns the engine of wealth) but substitutes a new engine; the term is an antithesis-by-quotation, not an extension. Marx remains a forward-stub author in the vault.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Karl Marx]]'s **means of production** — the term Zuboff explicitly inverts: production is now *subordinated* to behavioral modification, which displaces it as the source of capitalist wealth and power.
- [[Society of Control]] / [[Biopolitics]] — adjacent power-formations Zuboff positions surveillance capitalism's instrumentarianism as succeeding or differing from; behavioral modification works through actuation rather than discipline, norm, or the administration of life.

## Questions It Raises

- If the means of behavioral modification "eclipses" the means of production, does ownership of behavior-shaping infrastructure become the decisive axis of class power — and what would collective ownership or expropriation of it even mean?
- Zuboff insists the aim is commercial results, "not conformity or obedience" — but when behavior is reliably produced at scale, is the distinction between commercial actuation and political control stable?
- The apparatus is "formally indifferent" to freedom and dignity. Is indifference a more total form of domination than the active malice of twentieth-century behavior-modification regimes aimed at a captive "them"?
- How far is the Skinnerian inheritance load-bearing rather than rhetorical — i.e., does operant conditioning at scale actually work as advertised, or is "guaranteed outcomes" itself a marketing claim?
