# The Twelve Leverage Points

## Brief Definition

Donella Meadows's famous ranked list of twelve "places to intervene in a system," counting *up* in effectiveness from 12 (numbers and parameters, the weakest) to 1 (transcending paradigms, the strongest) — offered self-consciously as a provisional, "slithery" ordering rather than a formula.

## Longer Explanation

In the essay that became Chapter 6 of *Thinking in Systems*, Meadows crystallizes decades of systems analysis into a single ranked catalogue of *leverage points* — "places in the system where a small change could lead to a large shift in behavior" (`Directly stated`). The list is the book's central and, for this vault, most consequential move. Its twelve rungs, in Meadows's own numbering (the higher the leverage, the lower the number), are: **12. Numbers** — "Constants and parameters such as subsidies, taxes, standards"; **11. Buffers** — "The sizes of stabilizing stocks relative to their flows"; **10. [[Stocks and Flows|Stock-and-Flow Structures]]** — "Physical systems and their nodes of intersection"; **9. [[Delays]]** — "The lengths of time relative to the rates of system changes"; **8. [[Balancing Feedback Loop|Balancing Feedback Loops]]** — "The strength of the feedbacks relative to the impacts they are trying to correct"; **7. [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop|Reinforcing Feedback Loops]]** — "The strength of the gain of driving loops"; **6. [[Information Flows]]** — "The structure of who does and does not have access to information"; **5. [[Rules (Systems)|Rules]]** — "Incentives, punishments, constraints"; **4. [[Self-Organization]]** — "The power to add, change, or evolve system structure"; **3. [[System Goals|Goals]]** — "The purpose or function of the system"; **2. [[Paradigms]]** — "The mind-set out of which the system—its goals, structure, rules, delays, parameters—arises"; and **1. [[Transcending Paradigms]]** (`Directly stated`).

The ordering is not arbitrary. Roughly the first half of the list is *material* — numbers, buffers, physical stock-and-flow plumbing, delay lengths — the parts of a system that are, as Meadows repeatedly notes, "usually physical entities, not easy to change." The turn comes precisely at rung 8: "Now we're beginning to move from the physical part of the system to the information and control parts, where more leverage can be found" (`Directly stated`). From there the leverage rises as the intervention point becomes more *immaterial* — the flow of information, the rules, the system's capacity to reorganize itself, its goal, and finally the shared mind-set out of which all the rest arises. This ascent is the vault's [[Leverage rises as the intervention point becomes more immaterial|immaterial-leverage argument]] and one of the sharpest empirical statements of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension in the whole corpus (`Interpretive synthesis`).

Meadows insists the list is provisional. She offers it "with much humility and wanting to leave room for its evolution," warns that "What you read here is still a work in progress; it's not a recipe for finding leverage points," and later concedes: "It is a tentative list and its order is slithery. There are exceptions to every item that can move it up or down the order of leverage" (`Directly stated`). The two highest rungs strain the ranking's own logic. Paradigms "are harder to change than anything else about a system," yet paradigm change "can happen in a… millisecond," "a click in the mind, a falling of scales from the eyes"; whole societies, though, "resist challenges to their paradigms harder than they resist anything else." Above even paradigm change sits *transcending* paradigms — "to keep oneself unattached in the arena of paradigms," "to let go into not-knowing, into what the Buddhists call enlightenment."

The list's most political observation is its closing warning about resistance. "The higher the leverage point," Meadows writes, "the more the system will resist changing it—that's why societies often rub out truly enlightened beings" (`Directly stated`). Real leverage is thus inversely correlated with accessibility: the interventions that would change the most are the ones a system most fiercely defends. This is why, she notes, "Probably 90—no 95, no 99 percent—of our attention goes to parameters" at the bottom of the list, "but there's not a lot of leverage in them" — "Diddling with the details, arranging the deck chairs on the Titanic." Mastery, in the end, "has less to do with pushing leverage points than it does with strategically, profoundly, madly, letting go and [[Dancing with Systems|dancing with the system]]."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*, 2008) — the concept's sole and originating anchor. The twelve-point list first appeared in her widely circulated 1997 essay "Places to Intervene in a System" and is reproduced, with commentary, as Chapter 6. Meadows attributes the underlying idea of leverage to MIT's Jay Forrester (whose World and urban-dynamics models supply her running examples) but the ranked list itself is hers. She presents it not as doctrine but as "an invitation to think more broadly about system change," repeatedly flagging its tentativeness and the way its top rungs — paradigms and transcending paradigms — verge on the mystical.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — not an opposite so much as the tension the list *stages*: Meadows ranks the material interventions (numbers, buffers, physical structure) lowest and the immaterial ones (information, rules, goals, paradigms) highest, inverting the intuition that concrete, physical change is the powerful kind.

## Questions It Raises

- If leverage rises as the intervention becomes more immaterial, and "the higher the leverage point, the more the system will resist changing it," is effective change structurally rare — reserved for those a system is most disposed to "rub out"?
- Meadows admits "its order is slithery" and that "there are exceptions to every item." Does a ranked list whose author disavows its own ordering function as knowledge, or as a heuristic — and what is the difference?
- The top two rungs (paradigms, transcending paradigms) leave the technical vocabulary of stocks and flows entirely for Kuhn and Buddhist enlightenment. Is this the point at which systems thinking "leads us to the edge of what analysis can do," or the point at which it stops being systems thinking?
- If "90—no 95, no 99 percent—of our attention goes to parameters," what accounts for the systematic mis-allocation of intervention effort toward the least effective rung?
