# Paradigms

## Brief Definition

In Donella Meadows's system of [[Leverage Points]], a paradigm is the mind-set out of which a system arises — "the great big unstated assumptions," a society's deepest shared beliefs about how the world works — and intervening at this level is the second-highest leverage a person can wield, above goals and structure and rules, and short only of transcending paradigms altogether.

## Longer Explanation

Meadows ranks twelve "places to intervene in a system" from least to most powerful, and paradigms sit at number 2, above even the goals of the system. She takes the term in the Kuhnian sense: "The shared idea in the minds of society, the great big unstated assumptions, constitute that society's paradigm, or deepest set of beliefs about how the world works" (`Directly stated`). These beliefs go unspoken precisely because everyone already holds them — that money measures something real, that growth is good, that nature is a stock of resources to be converted to human purposes, that one can "own" land. Each such assumption looks self-evident from inside the paradigm and "utterly dumbfounded" the cultures that did not share it.

The reason a paradigm ranks so high is that it is upstream of everything else in the system. "Paradigms are the sources of systems. From them, from shared social agreements about the nature of reality, come system goals and information flows, feedbacks, stocks, flows, and everything else about systems" (`Directly stated`). This inverts the usual intuition that a system is built from its material parts: for Meadows the [[Feedback Loops|feedbacks]], stocks, and goals a modeler diagrams are downstream expressions of a prior worldview. She quotes Emerson to the same effect — that "every nation and every man instantly surround themselves with a material apparatus which exactly corresponds to... their state of thought" — and offers the pyramids (built out of a belief in the afterlife) and skyscrapers (built out of a belief that downtown space is enormously valuable) as the material residue of paradigms.

What makes the paradigm leverage point paradoxical is the mismatch between its difficulty and its cost. One could argue paradigms belong lowest on the list because they are "harder to change than anything else about a system," yet "there's nothing physical or expensive or even slow in the process of paradigm change. In a single individual it can happen in a millisecond. All it takes is a click in the mind, a falling of scales from the eyes, a new way of seeing" (`Directly stated`). Because it is purely mental, the change can be instantaneous in a person; because it is collective, "whole societies are another matter — they resist challenges to their paradigms harder than they resist anything else." Meadows's method for changing paradigms follows Thomas Kuhn — keep pointing at the anomalies and failures of the old paradigm, speak and act loudly from the new one, place people who hold it in positions of visibility and power, and work not with reactionaries but with active change agents and the open-minded middle ground. Above paradigms she names one further leverage point, **Transcending Paradigms** (leverage point #1): staying unattached in the arena of paradigms, realizing that no paradigm is "true," which she likens to what "the Buddhists call enlightenment" (`Directly stated`).

Meadows's Kuhnian, worldview-scale sense of "paradigm" must be held **distinct** from Ray Kurzweil's [[Paradigm Shift and the S-Curve]], where a "paradigm" is a narrowly technical unit — "a method or approach to solving a problem," such as shrinking transistors — that runs an exponential growth curve until exhausted, at which point the next paradigm takes over. Kurzweil's paradigm shift is a mechanical hand-off internal to the machinery of accelerating technology; Meadows's paradigm change is a shift in a culture's deepest metaphysical assumptions and the highest-leverage move an intervener can make. The two share only the word (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*) — Paradigms are leverage point #2 in her ranked list of interventions: the shared, unstated worldview that is "the source of systems," from which goals, feedbacks, stocks, and flows all descend. Changed in an individual "in a millisecond," fiercely resisted by whole societies; only Transcending Paradigms (staying unattached to any worldview) ranks higher.
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** (*The Singularity Is Near*) — uses "paradigm" in the narrower technical sense of a method or approach to a problem that follows an S-curve; see the distinct note [[Paradigm Shift and the S-Curve]]. Not the Kuhnian worldview sense Meadows means.

## Questions It Raises

- If paradigms are the true source of systems, does material intervention (changing stocks, flows, feedbacks) ever produce lasting change, or only surface adjustment?
- Meadows says paradigm change can happen "in a millisecond" in one mind yet is fiercely resisted by whole societies — what accounts for the gap between individual and collective conversion?
- Is Transcending Paradigms — holding no worldview as true — itself a paradigm, and if so, is any position truly "outside" the system it observes?
- Does Meadows's claim that a purely mental change is the second-highest leverage point cut against the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis, locating the deepest causal power in the immaterial mind-set rather than the material apparatus?
