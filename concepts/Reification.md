# Reification

## Brief Definition

Marx's term *Verdinglichung* ("thingification") — the transformation of all ideas into objects — redefined by [[Jack Burnham]] as a *productive* aesthetic mechanism: the constant re-synchronization of artistic sensibility with the disclosed form-world of scientific theory, the engine that has driven modern sculpture's century-long transformation.

## Longer Explanation

Burnham takes over the most dynamic concept in Marx's analysis of capitalism — reification, or "thingification" (*Verdinglichung*), the process by which "certain societies transformed all ideas into objects" so that "man himself became an exchangeable object, a commodity" — and explicitly redirects it away from Marx's diagnosis of alienation. He announces that he "will not consider if Marx's economic solutions could reverse the effects of reification" and that "reification will be described from another point of view" (`Directly stated`, Introduction). Where for Marx reification names a loss (the sundering of human involvement from the work process, the isolation of the spirit from sensual contact with the environment), Burnham recasts it as the affirmative motor of an art form: "reification is the means by which the world is made anew, through sculpture or other artifacts, in a scientific culture" (`Directly stated`).

The keystone formulation is that "the process of 'thingification' which has given birth to modern sculpture is the constant resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory" (`Directly stated`, Introduction). Reification is thus the mechanism by which sculpture keeps re-tuning itself to whatever picture of reality science has most recently disclosed — atomic and mathematical models, vitalism, cybernetic systems — abandoning its anthropomorphic inheritance in the process. By Chapter 3 the term has become so fused with science's own abstractions that "sculpture becomes 'thingified' by means which cannot be perceived except through the rules for ordering finite or infinite sets of abstract points in a given continuum" (`Directly stated`), and by Chapter 4 the whole stylistic apparatus of art history collapses: "style has become meaningless as the way of explaining what are essentially a series of technological reifications" (`Directly stated`).

Burnham makes the engine concrete with four detectable trends that "thingification" sets in motion as it "brings sculpture away from its traditional modes of existence" (`Directly stated`, Introduction): (a) the transition of sculpture from craft methodology to a reflection of the modern production of goods; (b) the passage from idealism (the traditional hieratic values of the sculpted object) to materialism; (c) the evolution from a psychically-impregnated totemic object toward a more literal adaptation of scientific reality via the model or technologically-inspired artifact; and (d) the replacement of inanimate sculpture with life-simulating systems through technology. These trends point sculpture toward the immaterial: sculpture "increasingly has forsaken its anthropomorphic ideal to become a continuum of steadily changing ideas about the world" (`Directly stated`, Chapter 4), a "steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality."

Burnham further structures the concept on a major/minor distinction modeled directly on Thomas Kuhn. Just as science alternates between revolutionary paradigm shifts and the elaborating work Kuhn calls "normal science," "there are 'major reifications' by which sculpture comes to entirely new modes of form consciousness, and what could be called 'minor reifications' where a given form consciousness is elaborated. Both, but particularly the former, are dependent on the evolving scientific world picture" (`Directly stated`, Introduction). Major reifications are the sculptural analogue of paradigm shifts (Copernican astronomy, Newtonian mechanics); minor reifications are the analogue of normal science (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — borrows *Verdinglichung* from [[Karl Marx]] but inverts its valence: not the alienating reduction of persons to commodities but the generative "means by which the world is made anew" in art, the "constant resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory." Four trends (craft→production, idealism→materialism, totem→scientific model, inanimate→life-simulating systems) and a major/minor distinction mapped onto Kuhn's revolutionary vs normal science.
- **[[Karl Marx]]** *(forward-stub author — no book in `raw/`)* — the originating sense Burnham cites: reification ("thingification," *Verdinglichung*) as the dialectical process by which societies "transformed all ideas into objects," whereby "man himself became an exchangeable object, a commodity," accomplishing "his own alienation from self." Burnham reports this sense accurately, then sets Marx's economic remedy aside to repurpose the concept.

## Related Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — Burnham's reification is the mechanism that *drives* the "steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality"; it produces the same loss of mass and object-status that [[Lucy Lippard]] later names in conceptual art, but from the opposite direction (science-tuned form-consciousness rather than idea-as-art).
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — reification is the process underlying sculpture's movement across this axis: from "inert materiality: weight, mass, and form" toward a "continuum of steadily changing ideas about the world."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — held here as an opposing pole as well as a related one: Burnham's "thingification" is etymologically and conceptually the *making-into-a-thing*, yet in his account the cumulative effect of successive reifications is sculpture's loss of its thingly substance — the concept names a process whose endpoint contradicts its name.

## Questions It Raises

- Can a concept Marx coined to diagnose *alienation* be cleanly repurposed as an affirmative aesthetic engine, or does Burnham's "another point of view" smuggle the critique back in (sculpture "surviving only through constant threats to its own origins")?
- If sculpture's task is the "constant resynchronization" with science's disclosed form-world, what happens once science abandons visual models for pure mathematics — does reification run out of perceptible forms to thingify?
- How firmly does the major/minor distinction actually map onto Kuhn's revolutionary/normal science, given that Burnham concedes sculpture has "not been all transitional"?
- Is reification's terminus the dissolution of the object into a system (Burnham's "object to system" thesis), and if so is "thingification" still the right name for it?
