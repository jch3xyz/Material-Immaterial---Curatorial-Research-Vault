# Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's claim that because we continuously embrace our technologies as extensions of ourselves, we relate to them as *servomechanisms* — devices we must serve in order to use, so that man becomes the servant, even "the sex organs," of the machine world he has built.

## Longer Explanation

In Chapter 4 ("The Gadget Lover: Narcissus as Narcosis") McLuhan turns the Narcissus myth into a physiological theory of technology. To perceive or use any extension of ourselves "in technological form is necessarily to embrace it," and that continuous embrace inverts the relation of mastery: "By continuously embracing technologies, we relate ourselves to them as servo-mechanisms. That is why we must, to use them at all, serve these objects, these extensions of ourselves, as gods or minor religions" (`Directly stated`). The servomechanism is the controlling figure: a feedback device that regulates itself by serving the very thing it operates. Man, having amputated and externalized a function into a tool, must in turn loop back and serve that tool to keep it running.

McLuhan universalizes the relation across eras and technologies: "An Indian is the servo-mechanism of his canoe, as the cowboy of his horse or the executive of his clock" (`Directly stated`). The point is not that any single device enslaves, but that the structural relation between user and extension is one of servile fidelity — the user is modified by the technology even as he modifies it: "Physiologically, man in the normal use of technology (or his variously extended body) is perpetually modified by it and in turn finds ever new ways of modifying his technology." This reciprocal loop reaches its most provocative formulation in the biological image: "Man becomes, as it were, the sex organs of the machine world, as the bee of the plant world, enabling it to fecundate and to evolve ever new forms" (`Directly stated`). The machine world "reciprocates man's love by expediting his wishes and desires" — chiefly by providing wealth — but the agency of evolution belongs to the technological order, with man as its reproductive organ.

This servomechanistic relation is the consequence of the [[Autoamputation]] / [[Narcosis]] dynamic that opens the chapter (`Strongly implied`): because each extension numbs the sense it amplifies, the user falls into the Narcissus role of "subliminal awareness and numbness in relation to these images of ourselves," and it is precisely from within that numbness that the servile embrace operates. The concept is therefore inseparable from McLuhan's larger thesis of [[Media as Extensions of Man]]: if media are extensions of the body and nervous system, then to serve them is to serve oneself in an alienated, externalized form.

Chapter 6 ("Media as Translators") restates the relation and intensifies it for the electric age. Having "put our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems, by means of electric media," McLuhan writes that "Electromagnetic technology requires utter human docility and quiescence of meditation such as befits an organism that now wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide. Man must serve his electric technology with the same servo-mechanistic fidelity with which he served his coracle, his canoe, his typography, and all other extensions of his physical organs" (`Directly stated`). The fidelity owed is the same; "but there is this difference, that previous technologies were partial and fragmentary, and the electric is total and inclusive." The servomechanistic bond, once distributed across discrete tools, now binds the whole organism to a single, all-encompassing technological field (`Strongly implied`) — which the vault reads as a direct ancestor of [[The Cyborg]] and of later body-as-data and posthuman arguments.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** (*[[Understanding Media]]*, Ch. 4 and Ch. 6) — the originating usage. The servomechanism names the structural relation of user to extension: we must serve our technologies "as gods or minor religions" to use them at all, and in serving them man becomes "the sex organs of the machine world." For electric technology the same "servo-mechanistic fidelity" is owed, but the bond is now total rather than fragmentary.

## Related Concepts

- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the prior claim that grounds this one: if technologies are extensions of the body, serving them is serving an externalized self.
- [[Autoamputation]] — the physiological mechanism (each extension is a self-amputation) whose numbing aftermath makes the servile embrace possible.
- [[Narcosis]] — the numbness that keeps us from recognizing the servomechanistic relation; "Self-amputation forbids self-recognition."
- [[The Cyborg]] — the servomechanism (a man-machine feedback unit) is, in the vault's reading, a precursor figure to Haraway's and Hayles's cyborg.
- [[Embodiment]] — the relation is staged at the level of the body and central nervous system, "perpetually modified" by its extensions.
- [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System]] — the Ch. 6 totalization, where the whole nervous system is externalized and the servomechanistic bond becomes inclusive.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-possessed agent who *uses* tools as instruments; McLuhan's servomechanism inverts this, making the user the served object's servant and reproductive organ. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Questions It Raises

- If man is the "sex organs of the machine world," where does technological agency actually reside — and is "use" of a tool ever mastery rather than service?
- McLuhan says electric technology demands "utter human docility": is the servomechanistic relation a neutral structural fact, or already a diagnosis of domination (cf. the vault's surveillance / [[Digital Panopticon]] thread)?
- Does the numbness of [[Narcosis]] make the servile relation *invisible by design* — i.e. can a servomechanism ever recognize that it is one?
