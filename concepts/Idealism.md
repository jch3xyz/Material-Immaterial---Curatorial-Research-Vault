# Idealism

## Brief Definition

Idealism names two positions the corpus keeps deliberately apart. In its philosophy-of-mind sense it is the metaphysical claim that consciousness, not matter, is the fundamental substance of reality: there is "little reason to believe in anything beyond consciousness," and "the physical world is wholly constituted by consciousness." In [[Jack Burnham]]'s art-historical sense it is a "scientific idealism" — the practice of seeking "essential reality through the theories and models put forth provisionally by scientists," treating the abstract scientific model as a window onto an invisible order rather than dissolving matter into mind. The two share a structure (a mental or theoretical construct taken as the route to essential reality) but point in opposite directions: one re-enchants matter as mind, the other apprehends reality *through* science's models and stays on the side of [[The Computational Universe|scientific materialism]].

## Longer Explanation

In its philosophy-of-mind sense, idealism is the last stop on a road map [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]] borrows from [[David Chalmers]]: "One starts as a materialist, then one becomes a dualist, then a panpsychist, and one ends up as an idealist." Each station fails on its own terms — materialism cannot explain consciousness, dualism is inelegant, [[Panpsychism]] only deepens the inscrutability of matter — until, "by taking each of these frameworks to their logical yet unsatisfying conclusions," one arrives at the conviction "that there is little reason to believe in anything beyond consciousness and that the physical world is wholly constituted by consciousness. This is idealism." It is the inverse of the [[Mind as Machine]] metaphor and of [[The Computational Universe]]: where those make information or mechanism the substrate, idealism makes mind the substrate and treats matter as derivative — an "explanatory abstraction." This places it under the [[The Mind-Body Problem]] umbrella while remaining distinct: rather than reconcile mind and matter, idealism dissolves matter into mind. `Directly stated`

O'Gieblyn's anchoring figure is Bernardo Kastrup, a computer scientist who "took a shortcut through this trajectory, converting directly from materialism to idealism" when the [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness|hard problem of consciousness]] hit him "like a brick" at CERN. His reasoning runs through Descartes's *cogito* — "all we can know for certain is thought and perception" — but stops short of Descartes's reconstruction of the external world: "unlike Descartes, Kastrup does not attempt to use this knowledge as a foundation for rebuilding a belief in the material world." The mind-matter dichotomy, for Kastrup, is "at root an error of language": naming first-person experience "mind" objectifies the subjective and lets us mistake consciousness for just another object, "and not in fact what it is: the underlying substrate of matter." "Reality is fundamentally experiential," he writes in *The Idea of the World*. `Directly stated`

The most consequential move is Kastrup's cosmic-mind cosmology, which O'Gieblyn frames through an article she encountered titled "Could Multiple Personality Disorder Explain Life, the Universe, and Everything?" Idealism faces an old objection: if all is one mind, why is the world shared and stable, and why are our minds bounded and separate? The first half is answered by a **universal mind** — "whether it goes by God, Brahman, or some other name" — the device that, in Bishop Berkeley's eighteenth-century version, keeps the world "in perpetual existence simply by looking at it," distinguishing idealism from solipsism. The second half — the **decomposition problem**, "how this universal consciousness broke itself into distinctive minds" — Kastrup answers by borrowing the **"disassociated alters"** of dissociative identity disorder (DID): "If something analogous to DID happens at a universal level, the one universal consciousness could, as a result, give rise to many alters with private inner lives like yours and ours." All sentient creatures are "broken-off parts of this conscious whole, while the inanimate world was a kind of vision dreamed up by the cosmic mind." `Directly stated`

O'Gieblyn's reading of idealism is finally reflexive and theological rather than evaluative. The cosmic mind is, she notes, "a common feature of idealism" and — under whatever name — a return of God. Her sharpest formulation makes idealism the photographic negative of Cartesian metaphysics: "Descartes needed God to prove that the external world exists. Kastrup does not believe in the material world and yet needs God to prove it doesn't." She reads this not as a refutation but as a symptom of [[Disenchantment]]'s instability — "the fact that God keeps barging into philosophy long after we declared him dead." Idealism's deepest lure, for someone who "grew up religious," is its promise to re-fuse meaning with the world: if "the very nature of the world is subjective," then "the meanings we think to discern in the world may not, after all, be more personal projections, but actual properties of the world." Whether this is recovered truth or "simple familiarity" dressed as logic is the question she refuses to settle. `Strongly implied`

[[Jack Burnham]] names a separate, art-historical sense of idealism that the corpus keeps distinct from the metaphysics-of-mind reading above. In *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) the formalist and geometric sculptor pursues "scientific idealism" — "essential reality through the theories and models put forth provisionally by scientists; their use as sculpture or icon transforms them into ideational verities." Here the route to essential reality is not consciousness but the provisional scientific model (atomic diagram, topological surface, mathematical function) taken as a window onto an invisible order — a position that stays resolutely on the side of [[The Computational Universe|scientific materialism]] rather than dissolving matter into mind. For Burnham this idealism is historically doomed: once physics abandoned visual models, sculpture moves "from millenniums of transcendent idealism" toward "transcendent phenomenalism" (see [[Idealism vs Phenomenalism]] and [[Scientific Idealism]]). The contact between the two senses is structural, not doctrinal: each names a position in which a mental or theoretical construct is taken as the route to essential reality, but where the philosophy-of-mind idealism re-enchants matter as mind, Burnham tracks sculpture's de-idealization and dematerialization toward perception, system, and eventually [[Artificial Life|life-simulating systems]]. His exposition is developed in his own section below. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — treats philosophy-of-mind idealism as the terminus of Chalmers's trajectory and as a reflexive case study in the return of God. Her signature reading: idealism inverts Descartes — God is now summoned not to prove the world exists but to prove it does not. She is skeptical ("I find all this highly implausible") yet unable to rule it more implausible than its rivals, and alert to her own religious susceptibility to its promise of cosmic meaning.
- **[[David Chalmers]]** *(forward-stub)* — supplies the materialist → dualist → panpsychist → idealist truism that O'Gieblyn organizes the position around, and judges "Kastrup's brand of idealism... among the most plausible new theories of mind."
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — names a distinct art-historical "scientific idealism": the formalist sculptor seeks "essential reality through the theories and models put forth provisionally by scientists," treating the abstract scientific model as a modern icon onto an invisible order. Unlike the philosophy-of-mind idealism, this stays on the side of scientific materialism, and Burnham reads it as historically doomed — superseded by the perceptual turn of "transcendent phenomenalism" (see [[Scientific Idealism]] and [[Idealism vs Phenomenalism]]).
- **Bernardo Kastrup (primary contemporary source)** — converts directly from materialism to idealism via the hard problem ("Quantities could not generate qualities"); matter is an "explanatory abstraction," consciousness "the underlying substrate of matter," and reality "fundamentally experiential." Supplies the cosmic-mind / disassociated-alters solution to the decomposition problem. Plain text — not yet a vault-worthy note.
- **Bishop George Berkeley (historical source)** — the eighteenth-century idealist whose infinite divine mind keeps the world in existence "simply by looking at it"; the prototype Kastrup updates with the DID analogy. Plain text.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Mind-Body Problem]] — the umbrella dilemma. Idealism is one resolution: it does not bridge mind and matter but abolishes matter as a separate substance, recasting the dichotomy as "an error of language."
- [[Panpsychism]] — the penultimate stop on Chalmers's ladder and idealism's nearest kin; both make mind fundamental, but panpsychism keeps matter (ensouled) while idealism dissolves it entirely into consciousness.
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — the engine of conversion: because "quantities could not generate qualities," consciousness "would never arise from electrical circuits," pushing Kastrup off materialism altogether.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the structural inverse O'Gieblyn keeps in view: Wheeler's "it from bit" makes information the substrate; idealism makes mind the substrate. Both relocate the world's ground away from inert matter.
- [[Disenchantment]] — the condition idealism tries to reverse; O'Gieblyn reads the recurrence of God in idealism as evidence that disenchantment never fully took.
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — the Hayles informatic frame; quantum "superposition" and the cosmos "as an enormous neural network" let Kastrup read physical pattern as "patterns of excitation of this universal mind."
- [[Posthumanism]] — idealism dissolves the boundary between mind and world, decentering the bounded human subject from a direction opposite to the machinic posthuman.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the foil: where data-reduction makes the soul "little more than a data set," idealism makes the data-world a dream of the cosmic mind, inverting which term is real.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Mind as Machine]] — the metaphor idealism most directly negates. "Mind as machine" holds consciousness to be computation arising from circuitry; idealism holds that "consciousness would never arise from electrical circuits, no matter how closely they resembled the structure of the human brain." The physicalist, in Kastrup's words, "dismisses its own primary, first-person point of view in favor of an abstract third-person perspective."
- Reductive materialism / physicalism — "a cathedral of physicalism" (CERN) is precisely what Kastrup defects from; idealism declares matter the abstraction and mind the only certainty.

## Questions It Raises

- **Meaning:** If "the very nature of the world is subjective," do the meanings we find in the world become "actual properties of the world" rather than projections — and can that claim ever be distinguished from wishful thinking, given that "wishful thinking" as a slur "comes from the disenchanted view that the mind is less real than the objective world"?
- **The body as data:** If the inanimate, measurable world is "a vision dreamed up by the cosmic mind," is the reduction of the self to [[The Body as Data|data]] a category error — mistaking an "explanatory abstraction" for the substrate — or is the dreamed body still binding?
- **Technology and perception:** Kastrup builds idealism out of neural networks and quantum field theory; if the universe "resembles an enormous neural network" and superposition signals a "cosmic mind... in a state of indecision," does our technology disclose mind in the world or only project our latest metaphor onto it (the radio, the transistor, the noumenal channel)?
- **Posthumanism / the return of God:** When God "keeps barging into philosophy long after we declared him dead," is idealism a genuine decentering of the human, or — like panpsychism — "merely another attempt to see all things in our image"? And does Kastrup needing God to prove the material world *does not* exist invert, or merely repeat, the Cartesian dependence on the divine?

## Jack Burnham (Beyond Modern Sculpture)

Burnham names a distinct, art-historical sense of idealism that should be kept apart from O'Gieblyn's metaphysics-of-mind idealism above. In *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) he argues that "Much of the most original formalist and geometric sculpture of the past half century owes its existence to what could be called 'scientific idealism.'" This is not the claim that consciousness is the fundamental substance, but that the sculptor seeks "essential reality through the theories and models put forth provisionally by scientists; their use as sculpture or icon transforms them into ideational verities." The scientific model — atomic diagram, topological surface, mathematical function — becomes the modern icon, and the abstract sculpture built from it is treated as a window onto an underlying, invisible order. See [[Scientific Idealism]] for the dedicated concept. `Directly stated`

For Burnham this idealism is historically doomed. Once physics abandoned visual models for "scientific symbolism and computation," the science-derived form-world ceased to be visualizable, and "The decline of formalism is the decline of a world fashioned to operate on a strictly visual-geometrical level." Object/Minimal art is the endpoint: "In theory at least, Object sculpture is art which has been cut free of all previous iconic influences; it is sculpture de-idealized." The succeeding program is not a deeper idealism but its opposite — an attention to the act of perception itself: "Static sculpture has emerged from millenniums of transcendent idealism and now moves toward transcendent phenomenalism." This is the axis captured by the tension [[Idealism vs Phenomenalism]]. `Directly stated`

The contact point with O'Gieblyn's idealism is structural rather than doctrinal: both name a position in which a mental or theoretical construct (consciousness for Kastrup; the provisional scientific model for the formalist sculptor) is taken as the route to "essential reality." But Burnham's scientific idealism stays resolutely on the side of [[The Computational Universe|scientific materialism]] — it is reality apprehended *through* science's models, not the dissolution of matter into mind. Burnham's interest is the reverse trajectory: where O'Gieblyn's idealism re-enchants matter as mind, Burnham tracks sculpture's de-idealization and dematerialization toward perception, system, and eventually [[Artificial Life|life-simulating systems]]. `Interpretive synthesis`
