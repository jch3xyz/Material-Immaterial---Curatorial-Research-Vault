# The Syndrome of the Horseless Carriage

## Brief Definition

Zuboff's name for the cognitive error of attaching our sense of a new peril to old, familiar facts — describing an unprecedented power in the borrowed vocabulary of a prior one — so that the conclusions we reach about it are necessarily incorrect.

## Longer Explanation

The phrase invokes the early automobile, named and pictured as a *carriage without a horse* because the only available frame for the new machine was the old one it displaced. Zuboff turns this into a diagnostic figure: when a genuinely unprecedented power appears, we lack a language for it and reach instinctively for the most recent comparable thing, dressing the new threat in the familiar terms of the old. In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* the chief instance is the reflexive use of Orwell's Big Brother and "digital totalitarianism" as the lens for the dangers posed by surveillance capitalism. Zuboff argues this is the syndrome at work — and that the borrowed frame does not merely mislead but actively disarms us, because "the conclusions to which they lead us are necessarily incorrect" (`Directly stated`).

The figure does double duty in the chapter. First, it is a warning: as scholars once appropriated "the language of imperialism" to name the still-unnamed power of totalitarianism, so today "it is we who now reach for familiar vernaculars of twentieth-century power like lifesaving driftwood." Second, it is a methodological imperative. Because an unprecedented power "defies human comprehension," the first act of resistance is the patient, original work of naming the thing on its own terms — which is exactly what Zuboff undertakes when she names this new species of power [[Instrumentarian Power]] (instrumentarianism) rather than a variant of totalitarianism (`Directly stated`).

The stakes are practical. Zuboff insists that "the equation of instrumentarian power with totalitarianism impedes our understanding as well as our ability to resist, neutralize, and ultimately vanquish its potency." The horseless carriage is therefore not a neutral metaphor but a polemic against analytic laziness: to fight a twenty-first-century power with twentieth-century categories is to misidentify the enemy and fight the wrong war (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — uses the figure as a recurring motif across the book and, in Chapter 12, as the hinge of her argument that surveillance capitalism wields [[Instrumentarian Power]], "the precise antithesis of Orwell's Big Brother," not a digital totalitarianism. The companion images are the "lifesaving driftwood" of borrowed twentieth-century vernaculars and the imperative to begin "the work of naming a strange form of power unprecedented in the human experience."

## Related Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the unprecedented power the syndrome obscures; naming it correctly is the cure for the syndrome.
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the economic logic whose novelty the borrowed-frame error fails to grasp.
- [[Friendly Big Brother]] — Han's data-friendly inversion of Orwell; a parallel insistence (from a different angle) that the Orwellian frame is the wrong fit for the new non-coercive power.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — the panvasive sensate apparatus that the totalitarian frame mischaracterizes.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Radical Behaviorism]] — the intellectual genealogy (Planck → Meyer → Skinner) that Zuboff names *as itself* the proper lineage of instrumentarian power, against the misleading Orwellian/totalitarian frame the syndrome supplies.

## Questions It Raises

- If the past "offers no adequate compass," by what method do we name a power that "defies human comprehension" without smuggling in the very analogies the syndrome warns against?
- Is the horseless-carriage error inevitable for any unprecedented phenomenon, or is it specific to powers that disguise themselves as continuous with the familiar?
- Does naming a power on its own terms genuinely enable resistance, or can a more precise name still leave us without the means to act?
