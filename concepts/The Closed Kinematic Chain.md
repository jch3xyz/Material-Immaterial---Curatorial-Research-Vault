# The Closed Kinematic Chain

## Brief Definition

Franz Reuleaux's definition of the machine as "a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies" — a determinate arrangement that gives man "the ability to control motion and power in a determinate fashion" — which Jack Burnham makes both the deep root and the fatal limit of Kinetic Art.

## Longer Explanation

The "closed kinematic chain" is the nineteenth-century kinematicist Franz Reuleaux's reduction of the machine to its essence: a system of paired, mutually constraining bodies whose every motion is fully determined. Burnham invokes it at the head of his chapter on Kineticism to locate the buried impulse of the entire kinetic enterprise. Kinetic Art, he argues, "represents the contemporary artistic recognition of the drive toward total mechanization; it is the celebration of what Franz Reuleaux, in defining the machine, called the 'closed kinematic chain'-the ability of man to control motion and power in a determinate fashion." The kinetic artist, knowingly or not, takes up the determinate machine — the perfected nineteenth-century object — as artistic material. `Directly stated`

Reuleaux is, for Burnham, the "pivotal intellect" who "completed the process of rationalization" in machine design: he reduced the engagement of machine elements to the principle of "pair closure" and observed "that machinery developed as designers reduced the freedom between pair-closure elements." The closed kinematic chain is thus the endpoint of a logic of *eliminating freedom* — progressively meshing components so that motion becomes ever more efficient, ever less arbitrary. This is precisely why it founds Kineticism and dooms it. The same determinism that lets man "control motion and power" forbids the indeterminacy, ambiguity, and "autonomous capitulation to a not always rational visual order" that art requires. As Burnham puts it, "the machine, as Reuleaux made clear, could not allow that to happen." The kinetic dream is "unrequited" because its chosen medium is constitutively hostile to the psychic involvement art demands. `Directly stated`

Burnham reads the closed kinematic chain as more than a technical fact; he reads it as a worldview, and a menacing one. "The fact, as Reuleaux has insisted, that the machine was 'a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies' bothered many nineteenth-century poets and thinkers" — Goethe, Thoreau, Baudelaire, Samuel Butler among them — because it raised the question of "what would happen to the more autonomous and ephemeral values of life if the unstoppable rationality of this new breed of objects were to proliferate into all areas of life." Buried in Reuleaux's enlightened reasoning, Burnham writes, "were the seeds of an implacable bourgeois totalitarianism," for Reuleaux "was capable of seeing the machine as the model for the well-run society." The determinism of the chain is therefore not only an aesthetic problem but a political and existential one — the machine as a template for a constrained life. `Directly stated`

Because the closed kinematic chain restricts rather than liberates, the kinetic artist is left with only three moves, all of them ways of *breaking* the chain. He may **humanize** the machine — Tinguely's "joyous machine," "one that is, in fact, free," or Duchamp's stipulation that mechanisms be "tamed or humanized, thus rendered incapable of upsetting the balance of cause and effect"; he may **randomize** it — using the machine "primarily as a power source and as a 'randomizing' device, a means for producing unstable and unsuspected results," diverting "the essential restrictive nature of the machine"; or he may **destroy** it, allowing "the machine new degrees of freedom—even, on occasion, by destroying it" (Tinguely's self-destroying machines, which Burnham deems "only a Luddite solution"). Each is an attempt "to allow the machine new degrees of freedom" — to undo the very pair-closure that defines the chain. `Directly stated`

The closed kinematic chain is, finally, the structural foil to the open, life-simulating systems Burnham celebrates later in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (the cybernetic and robot/cyborg art of Chapter 8). Where the closed chain is determinate, frictionless, and finite — a perfected nineteenth-century *object* — the open system is mutually dependent and may "manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life: self-organization, growth... input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death." Kineticism fails, in Burnham's account, because it tried to make life out of a closed, deterministic machine; the systems esthetic succeeds (or promises to) because it abandons the closed chain for open, self-organizing, feedback-governed [[Cybernetics|cybernetic]] organization. The closed kinematic chain marks the boundary the [[Object and System|object-to-system]] migration must cross. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — borrows Reuleaux's term to name the determinate machine that Kinetic Art both celebrates and is defeated by. The closed kinematic chain is the deep root of Kineticism (the "drive toward total mechanization") and its limit (the determinism that "could not allow" the irrationality art requires), forcing artists to humanize, randomize, or destroy the machine. It is the closed, determinate counter-pole to the open, self-organizing [[Object and System|system]] of his later chapters.
- **Franz Reuleaux (via Burnham)** — the nineteenth-century kinematicist whose definitions Burnham quotes verbatim: the machine as "a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies," and the "closed kinematic chain" as man's "ability to control motion and power in a determinate fashion," built up from the principle of "pair closure" through the progressive reduction of freedom between elements. Reuleaux remains a one-off figure within the vault (named only inside Burnham), so this concept carries his usage rather than a standalone author page.

## Related Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — the closed kinematic chain is the perfected *object*-machine; Burnham's whole argument tracks the migration from this closed, determinate object toward the open, life-bearing system.
- [[Mechanism (Burnham)]] — the broader Burnham concept of the machine/mechanism in art; the closed kinematic chain is its rigorous, Reuleaux-derived definition and its restrictive core.
- [[Systems Theory]] — supplies the vocabulary ("closed" vs open systems) against which Burnham measures Kineticism; the closed kinematic chain is a *closed* system, deliberately contrasted with the open systems of cybernetic art.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the later "new order of physical principles (automation made possible through electronics)" that, in Burnham's account, renders Reuleaux's "essentially Newtonian world of geometric relationships of secondary importance" — the open, feedback-governed alternative to the closed chain.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the closed kinematic chain is determinism on the material/object side of the vault's canonical tension; the systemic, information-governed, self-organizing art Burnham endorses sits on the immaterial side ("for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor").

## Questions It Raises

- If the machine is by definition "a faultlessly constrained and closed system of bodies," can a *kinetic* art ever be more than a parody, humanization, or destruction of the machine — or is the kinetic ambition self-contradictory at its root?
- Burnham reads Reuleaux's determinism as carrying "the seeds of an implacable bourgeois totalitarianism." Does the move from closed kinematic chains to open cybernetic systems escape that politics, or merely relocate determinism into feedback and program?
- Where exactly does the closed chain end and the open system begin — is it a difference of mechanism (feedback, self-organization), of life-properties, or only of the artist's willingness to introduce "new degrees of freedom"?
