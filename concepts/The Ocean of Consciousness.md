# The Ocean of Consciousness

## Brief Definition

Harari's title image for the claim that human science has mapped only two tiny regions of a possibly infinite spectrum of mental states — so that "we are living on a tiny island of consciousness within a giant ocean of alien mental states," a vastness that includes premodern shamans and monks, the bigger-brained Neanderthal, and (via Nagel's bat) the irreducibly alien minds of other animals.

## Longer Explanation

The metaphor governs the chapter of *Homo Deus* in which Harari diagnoses the central danger of techno-humanism: it "seeks to upgrade the human mind and give us access to unknown experiences and unfamiliar states of consciousness," yet "since we are unfamiliar with the full spectrum of mental states, we don't know what mental aims to set ourselves" ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/13 The Ocean of Consciousness]]). We are like islanders who have just built a boat and are "about to set sail without a map or even a destination" — worse, because the islanders at least know they occupy a small space within a large sea, whereas "we fail to appreciate that we are living on a tiny island of consciousness within a giant ocean of alien mental states." (`Directly stated`)

Harari grounds the image in an analogy to the electromagnetic and acoustic spectrums: "just as the spectrums of light and sound are far larger than what we humans can see and hear, so the spectrum of mental states is far larger than what the average human is aware of." Where human vision covers only wavelengths between 400 and 700 nanometres, "the spectrum of possible mental states may be infinite, but science has studied only two tiny sections of it: the sub-normative and the WEIRD." The first is the well-mapped territory of "psychiatric disorders and mental diseases"; the second is the normative mind as studied almost exclusively in *Western, educated, industrialised, rich and democratic* populations. The super-normative zone — the prosperous or extraordinary mind — remains, as of 2016, "largely terra incognita to science." (`Directly stated`)

The ocean's reaches extend far beyond present WEIRD humans. Before the global village "the planet was a galaxy of isolated human cultures, which might have fostered mental states that are now extinct"; "shamans, monks and ascetics systematically explored the mysterious lands of mind," reporting states of "supreme tranquillity, extreme sharpness and matchless sensitivity," of "the mind expanding to infinity or dissolving into emptiness" — states modern Western culture, having "lost faith and interest in superior mental states," dismisses as the province of "a drug addict, mental patient or charlatan." Beyond *Homo sapiens* lie our bigger-brained Neanderthal cousins, who "might well have had many mental states that no Sapiens had ever experienced," and beyond all human species lie the minds of animals. (`Directly stated`)

It is here that Harari recruits Thomas Nagel. Citing "one of the most important articles about the philosophy of mind," Nagel's 1974 "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?", Harari notes that "a Sapiens mind cannot fathom the subjective world of a bat": no quantity of algorithms "about the bat body, about bat echolocation systems and about bat neurons … will tell us how it feels to be a bat." The bat lives "in a world of echoes" we are "completely oblivious to"; the whale may have "musical experiences which even Bach and Mozart couldn't grasp." The chapter therefore turns the ocean into both a humility and an exhortation: drugs, genetic engineering, "electronic helmets and direct brain–computer interfaces may open passages to these places," so that "just as Columbus and Magellan sailed beyond the horizon … we may one day set sail towards the antipodes of the mind." The danger is that, lacking a map, we will instead upgrade only those faculties "the current economic and political system needs," and so end up "downgrading humans." (`Directly stated`; the synthesis of map-lessness and downgrade risk is `Strongly implied`)

### The WEIRD critique

Embedded in the ocean image is a methodological indictment that stands on its own. Harari draws on "a groundbreaking 2010 study" by Joseph Henrich, Steven J. Heine and Ara Norenzayan, which surveyed the leading psychology journals of 2003–2007 and "found that though the papers often make broad claims about the human mind, most of them base their findings on exclusively WEIRD samples." In the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, "96 per cent of the sampled individuals were WEIRD," "68 per cent were Americans," and "more than two-thirds of the individuals sampled … were psychology students in Western universities." Hence Harari's barb: "the study of the human mind has so far assumed that *Homo sapiens* is Homer Simpson." The WEIRD critique is the empirical floor of the metaphor — proof that even the small "island" we believe we have charted is itself only a coastal strip. (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — Coins the title image in *Homo Deus*. The ocean is not a thesis about consciousness as such but about the *poverty of our map* of it: an epistemic and political argument against upgrading minds we do not understand. It folds together (1) the spectrum analogy, (2) the sub-normative/WEIRD limitation, (3) extinct and non-WEIRD human mental states, (4) Neanderthal cognition, and (5) Nagel's animal minds.
- **Thomas Nagel** *(via Harari)* — Harari imports the bat from Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" to populate the ocean with non-human minds. In Harari's hands Nagel's point about the irreducibility of subjective experience becomes evidence for the *vastness* of the spectrum rather than (as in Nagel) an argument against reductive physicalism. (See [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] for the qualia/Nagel bridge proper.) (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Related Concepts

- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — Adjacent but distinct. The ocean is about the *extent* of the spectrum of minds; the hard problem is about *why there is experience at all*. Nagel's bat and the irreducibility of qualia are the bridge between them. (The Nagel/qualia material should be extended into the hard-problem note separately.)
- [[Embodiment]] — Each alien mental state is keyed to a different body and sensorium (bat echolocation, the whale's enlarged limbic system); the ocean is in part a claim that consciousness is body-relative.
- [[Transhumanism]] — The ocean is Harari's warning that techno-humanism would "set sail" to upgrade minds without a chart of the mental spectrum.
- [[Mind Uploading]] — A would-be voyage across the ocean that presumes the mind is mappable and portable; the chapter casts doubt on whether we even know the destination.
- [[Disenchantment]] — Harari notes "the humanist revolution caused modern Western culture to lose faith and interest in superior mental states"; the ocean's unexplored super-normative coast is a casualty of disenchantment.
- [[Dataism]] — The chapter ends by pivoting from techno-humanism to Dataism; the ocean is the last humanist image before meaning is handed to information flows.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — The ocean dissolves the assumption that the WEIRD, normative individual is the measure of mind; "Homo sapiens is Homer Simpson" is precisely a rebuke to taking that subject as universal.

## Questions It Raises

- If the spectrum of mental states "may be infinite," in what sense can any upgrade be called an *improvement* rather than merely a *relocation* within the ocean?
- Does the WEIRD critique undermine not only the upgrade project but the very normative baseline ("the healthy mind") from which psychology measures pathology?
- Is Nagel's bat evidence of an *empirical* frontier we might one day cross with brain–computer interfaces, or of an *in-principle* limit on what one kind of mind can know of another?
- If non-WEIRD, premodern, and animal minds occupy the ocean, who has the authority — and the map — to decide which of its regions are worth colonising?
