# The Refusal of Innocence

## Brief Definition

The cyborg's deliberate rejection of origin myths, original unity, organic wholeness, the Garden and the Fall, and of victimhood as the privileged ground for insight — a commitment to partiality and perversity in place of innocent wholeness.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Donna Haraway]], the cyborg is constitutively *without innocence*. It has no origin story in the Western sense, no Garden to be expelled from, no original unity to mourn or recover. Where humanist and revolutionary narratives ground politics in a lost wholeness — the myth of original unity, fullness, bliss, the phallic mother from whom all must separate — the cyborg "skips the step of original unity, of identification with nature in the Western sense." Innocence here names a structure of feeling and politics, not a moral purity: the longing for a prelapsarian totality, the dream of being made whole, the expectation that one's father (or one's theory) will save one through "a restoration of the garden." The cyborg refuses all of it (`Directly stated`, ch.01).

The refusal is also a refusal of a particular *epistemology of suffering*. In ch.02, Haraway attacks "the corollary insistence on victimhood as the only ground for insight," which, together with innocence, "has done enough damage." To ground knowledge and political authority in being the most violated, the closest to nature, the least implicated, is — on Haraway's account — to reinstate exactly the "natural matrix of unity" and "organic or natural standpoint" that the cyborg dissolves. "With the loss of innocence in our origin, there is no expulsion from the Garden either"; the cyborg's politics "lose the indulgence of guilt with the naïveté of innocence." White feminists discovering "the noninnocence of the category 'woman'" is offered as the paradigm case (`Directly stated`, ch.02).

In ch.06 the refusal becomes a positive method of *writing*. "Cyborg writing is about the power to survive, not on the basis of original innocence, but on the basis of seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other." It "must not be about the Fall, the imagination of a once-upon-a-time wholeness before language, before writing, before Man." The figure of Malinche — "not Eve before eating the forbidden fruit" — models a chimeric, spliced, illegitimate literacy that survives precisely because it has no claim to an original language before violation. To refuse innocence is to refuse "the ideological resources of victimization so as to have a real life," and to recognise oneself "as fully implicated in the world" (`Directly stated`, ch.06). The closing image confirms it: "A cyborg body is not innocent; it was not born in a garden."

This note folds the **Frankenstein** and **Eden / the Garden / the Fall** symbols in as *evidence* rather than treating them as separate motifs. The cyborg, "unlike the hopes of Frankenstein's monster," does not expect its father to save it through a restoration of the garden; "the cyborg would not recognize the Garden of Eden; it is not made of mud and cannot dream of returning to dust." These refused-origin symbols are the imagistic content of the disposition (`Strongly implied` that they function as a single argumentative cluster).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — The refusal of innocence is a foundational cyborg disposition running across the *Manifesto*: in ch.01 it is named ("completely without innocence"; no origin story; the refused Frankenstein/Eden figures), in ch.02 it is theorised as a critique of victimhood-as-ground-for-insight, and in ch.06 it becomes a writing practice ("not on the basis of original innocence"). It is paired throughout with the cyborg's positive commitments — partiality, irony, intimacy, perversity, affinity, and being "fully implicated in the world."

## Questions It Raises

- If neither innocence nor victimhood can ground political insight, what does — implication, affinity, "seizing the tools"? Is "being fully implicated in the world" a stable ground or a permanent unsettling?
- Does refusing victimhood-as-ground risk dissolving the standing of the actually violated, or does it (as Haraway intends) free their politics from a "latent position of moral superiority"?
- How far can the refused-origin symbols (Frankenstein, Eden, the Fall) be generalised beyond Haraway's feminist-science-fiction archive before they lose their critical edge?
- Is "regeneration, not rebirth" a coherent alternative to the wholeness it refuses, or another wholeness in disguise?
