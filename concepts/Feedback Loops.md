# Feedback Loops

## Brief Definition

The feedback loop is the cybernetic mechanism by which information circulates between a system and its environment (or among a system's components), and in Hayles's account it is the device that renders the boundaries of the autonomous liberal subject "up for grabs" by letting control and information flow across the skin rather than stopping at it.

## General Orientation

This note holds more than one author's sense of "feedback," and they pull in different directions:

- **The cybernetic-informational loop ([[Norbert Wiener]] via [[N. Katherine Hayles]]).** Feedback is "behavior controlled by negative feedback," the self-regulating circulation of *information* between a system and its environment. Read this way the loop is diagnostic of subjectivity: it makes the body's boundary porous and underwrites the deconstruction of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] (`Directly stated`). This is the dominant register of the Longer Explanation below.
- **The autopoietic dissent ([[Humberto Maturana]] / [[Francisco Varela]], via Hayles).** Second-order cybernetics turns the loop "inside out": if systems are informationally closed, "no information crosses the boundary," so the loop no longer connects system to environment (see [[Systems Theory]]).
- **The physiological / homeostatic register ([[Marshall McLuhan]]).** McLuhan reads media as *extensions* of the body and treats every extension as a homeostatic strategy — an "attempt to maintain equilibrium" for the overstressed central nervous system, with autoamputation, counter-irritation, and comfort as its tactics. Here "feedback" also names the historical break (in the Automation chapter) by which the electric "information loop or circuit" ends the linear, one-way sequence of mechanical and typographic culture. This is a physiological feedback-loop reading that sits alongside Wiener and Hayles, sharing their equilibrium-seeking vocabulary while keeping the loop emphatically embodied (see the McLuhan section below, and [[Homeostasis]]).

The senses overlap on the word *equilibrium* — Wiener's negative feedback and McLuhan's "strategies of equilibrium for the central nervous system" are cousins — but they are not identical: McLuhan's loop is a bodily, sensory homeostasis, not (primarily) a circuit of measurable information.

## Longer Explanation

For Hayles, the feedback loop is the load-bearing technical idea that converts [[Cybernetics]] from a control engineering into a theory of the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]]. She traces its history: feedback had long been "exploited to increase the stability of mechanical systems" — steam-engine governors and the like — but "it was not until the 1930s and 1940s, however, that the feedback loop was explicitly theorized as a flow of information." Once feedback is read as *informational* flow rather than mechanical regulation, the loop can be run indifferently through animals, machines, and humans, and cybernetics is "born when nineteenth-century control theory joined with the nascent theory of information."

The decisive consequence is ontological, not merely technical. Because "feedback loops can flow not only within the subject but also between the subject and the environment," the loop dissolves the bounded, self-possessing interior on which the liberal humanist subject depends. Hayles is explicit that "from Norbert Wiener on, the flow of information through feedback loops has been associated with the deconstruction of the liberal humanist subject." The loop is thus the concrete mechanism behind the posthuman claim that the human is continuous with its prosthetic and informational environment — see [[The Extrapolated Body]] and the cyborg, whose feedback couplings make "body boundaries are up for grabs."

In the discussion of virtual reality, the feedback loop becomes phenomenologically vivid. VR "puts the user's sensory system into a direct feedback loop with a computer," and Hayles reports that the user "learns, kinesthetically and proprioceptively, that the relevant boundaries for interaction are defined less by the skin than by the feedback loops connecting body and simulation in a technobio-integrated circuit." Here the loop is not an abstraction but something the lived body is trained into — a key bridge between [[Embodiment]] and information, where subjectivity feels "dispersed throughout the cybernetic circuit." Crucially, this is the point at which Hayles's argument resists pure dematerialization: the loop runs *through* a body that learns it proprioceptively, so the circuit is "technobio-integrated," not disembodied.

Hayles also extends the feedback loop beyond the body-machine couple to the relation between technology and discourse. Against a Foucauldian absorption of the body into discourse, she argues that "focusing on embodiment would help to clarify the mechanisms of change, for it links a changing technological landscape with the instantiated enactments that create feedback loops between materiality and discourse." Incorporation — habitual, sedimented bodily knowledge — is what couples the two: "in the feedback loop between technological innovations and discursive practices, incorporation is a crucial link." Even her historical method is loop-shaped: concepts and artifacts "engage each other in continuous feedback loops," a seriated pattern of "overlapping replication and innovation."

## Marshall McLuhan (Understanding Media)

McLuhan supplies a *physiological* feedback-loop reading that runs parallel to Wiener and Hayles, grounded not in information theory but in a medical theory of stress. In "The Gadget Lover," he reports that "Medical researchers like Hans Selye and Adolphe Jonas hold that all extensions of ourselves, in sickness or in health, are attempts to maintain equilibrium" (Source 6). Every medium — every extension of the body — is thus a homeostatic move: the central nervous system, faced with "the stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load," resorts to "autoamputation," cutting off or extending the "offending organ, sense, or function" to relieve strain. The extension is itself a "counter-irritant," and the numbness it produces is the price of bearable amplification. The whole apparatus is equilibrium-seeking, and McLuhan generalizes it into a tidy pair of tactics: "Whereas pleasure is a counter-irritant (e.g., sports, entertainment, and alcohol), comfort is the removal of irritants. Both pleasure and comfort are strategies of equilibrium for the central nervous system" (Source 6). This is feedback in the regulatory, homeostatic sense — a self-protective loop in which the body monitors stress and acts to restore balance — but pointedly *embodied*, which is why it cross-links cleanly to [[Homeostasis]] and reinforces Hayles's insistence (above) that the cybernetic loop runs *through* a material body rather than over its head (`Directly stated`).

In the "Automation" chapter McLuhan uses the word "feedback" in something much closer to the explicit cybernetic sense, and makes it epochal. Examining the pattern of automation, he writes, "Anybody who begins to examine the pattern of automation finds that perfecting the individual machine by making it automatic involves 'feedback.' That means introducing an information loop or circuit, where before there had been merely a one way flow or mechanical sequence" (Source 7). Feedback so understood is for McLuhan a historical rupture: "Feedback is the end of the lineality that came into the Western world with the alphabet and the continuous forms of Euclidean space" (Source 7). The "information loop or circuit" replaces "one-way flow" with "dialogue between the mechanism and its environment," weaving individual machines into "a galaxy of such machines" and, ultimately, the global "central nervous system" extended by electric media. The two registers meet in McLuhan's recurring figure of the servomechanism: in Narcissus he becomes "the servomechanism of his own extended or repeated image," and in Automation the feedback circuit is what marks the servomechanistic machine off "from the older mechanical principle of one-way movement." McLuhan thus joins the equilibrium-seeking vocabulary of cybernetics — extension as a "strategy of equilibrium" — to the historical claim that the closed information loop dissolves the linear, fragmented order of typographic and mechanical culture (`Strongly implied` for the synthesis of the two registers; the individual passages are `Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — Uses the feedback loop diagnostically: as the cybernetic mechanism that erodes liberal autonomy, defines the technobio-integrated circuit of VR, and links technology to discourse via incorporation. She insists the loop runs through material, embodied processes, so it is for her a hinge against the disembodiment of information rather than evidence for it.
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** — The originating figure. For Wiener "a flexible, self-regulating system of control based on feedback from the system itself" is required precisely because the universe is probabilistic; teleological behavior is redefined as "behavior controlled by negative feedback." Hayles reads Wiener as wanting to preserve liberal subjectivity even as his own feedback logic undermines it.
- **Second-order / autopoietic variant (dissent)** — In the second wave, Maturana and Varela turn the loop "inside out": on the premise that systems are informationally closed, "no information crosses the boundary separating the system from its environment," so "the loop no longer functions to connect a system to its environment." This denies that messages circulate in feedback loops at all (see [[Systems Theory]]) and shifts attention from the observed system to the observer.
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — A physiological / homeostatic register. All extensions of the body are "attempts to maintain equilibrium"; pleasure (counter-irritation) and comfort (removal of irritants) are "strategies of equilibrium for the central nervous system." In the Automation chapter McLuhan also uses "feedback" in its cybernetic sense — "an information loop or circuit" that ends the "one-way flow" of mechanical and typographic linearity. The loop here is equilibrium-seeking and emphatically embodied (see [[Homeostasis]]).

## Related Concepts

- [[Cybernetics]] — the field that the informational feedback loop constitutes; the loop is its founding mechanism.
- [[Embodiment]] — the loop, in VR and incorporation, is learned through and runs through the body, not over its head.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the loop is the technical ground for the dissolution of the autonomous subject into its environment.
- [[The Extrapolated Body]] — the cyborg/prosthetic extension realized by informational pathways and feedback couplings.
- [[Systems Theory]] — second-order cybernetics and autopoiesis reinterpret (and partly deny) the loop's function.
- [[The Body as Data]] — feedback presupposes a body legible as information flowing through the circuit.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the loop is the contested site of this tension: read one way it dematerializes the subject into information flow; read Hayles's way, the "technobio-integrated circuit" keeps the loop anchored in materiality.
- The **autopoietic closure** thesis opposes the classical feedback loop directly: if no information crosses the system boundary, the loop cannot do the connective work the first wave assigned it.

## Questions It Raises

- If the feedback loop makes the body's boundary "up for grabs," does posthuman embodiment survive, or does the loop quietly license the fantasy that information can leave its body behind? (ties to [[Embodiment vs Information]])
- When VR trains the user to feel boundaries "defined less by the skin than by the feedback loops," is the body being extended or erased — incorporated into the circuit, or reduced to [[The Body as Data]]?
- Can the feedback loop link technology to discourse without dissolving materiality into information, given Hayles's claim that incorporation is the "crucial link"?
- If autopoiesis is right that no information crosses the system boundary, is the entire posthuman picture of subject-environment feedback a category error — and what then becomes of the [[Material vs Immaterial]] distinction the loop was supposed to bridge?
