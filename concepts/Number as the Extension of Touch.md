# Number as the Extension of Touch

## Brief Definition

McLuhan's Chapter 11 thesis: number is unintelligible in isolation but becomes clear once read as a bodily extension — specifically an extension and separation of touch, the "haptic" sense — paired against writing as the extension of sight, so that the grasping, incorporating drive of crowds and of accumulated wealth is the tactile hand extended.

## Longer Explanation

For Marshall McLuhan, number belongs to the same explanatory frame as every other medium in *Understanding Media*: it is an extension of the body. He sets it directly beside writing in a sense-paired opposition. Writing extends and separates "our most neutral and objective sense, the sense of sight," whereas number "is an extension and separation of our most intimate and interrelating activity, our sense of touch." The two great technologies of literate civilization are thus a *visual* one and a *tactile* one — and McLuhan's whole reading of the West turns on which sense a medium amplifies. This is why the concept is anchored in [[Media as Extensions of Man]] and unfolds through [[Sense Ratios]]: number, like any medium, does its work by re-proportioning the senses, here by separating out and intensifying touch. [`Directly stated`]

The move that makes the chapter coherent is McLuhan's claim about intelligibility. "In isolation, number is as mysterious as writing. Seen as an extension of our physical bodies, it becomes quite intelligible." Number had been treated as an abstract, magical, or self-standing thing — "the ancient world associated number magically with the properties of physical things," and Spengler took it for "the essence of all things perceptible to the senses." McLuhan dissolves the mystery by relocating number in the sensorium: it is the haptic sense, externalized. The "faculty of touch, called the 'haptic' sense by the Greeks," he notes, "was popularized as such by the Bauhaus program of sensuous education, through the work of Paul Klee, Walter Gropius, and many others in the Germany of the 1920s" — the lineage that, for McLuhan, recovered touch as the integrating, nervous-system sense of art against the dominance of the visual. [`Directly stated`]

Touch, on McLuhan's account, is the *interrelating* and *grasping* sense, and this is what explains number's social pathologies of accumulation. The drive of crowds and of wealth to grow without limit is, he proposes, the tactile hand extended: "The mysterious need of crowds to grow and to reach out, equally characteristic of large accumulations of wealth, can be understood if money and numbers are, indeed, technologies that extend the power of touch and the grasp of the hand." Numbers — "whether of people or of digits, and units of money" — possess "the same factual magic for seizing and incorporating." The pleasure of the crowd is "delight in the multiplication of number," and McLuhan enlists Baudelaire's intuition that "number is within the individual. Intoxication is a number." Number is therefore "not only auditory and resonant, like the spoken word, but Originates in the sense of touch, of which it is an extension." It is "auditory and resonant" in expression but tactile in origin. [`Directly stated`]

Finally, number carries an *iconic* power that follows from its tactile, inclusive character. "It is the quality of number that explains its power to create the effect of an icon or an inclusive compressed image" — the compressed, all-at-once grasp of the headline figure ("Cyclist John Jameson, 12, Collides with Bus") or the statistical chart, the modern "cave-drawings or finger-paintings of the statisticians' charts." Where the visual/typographic regime fragments, sequences, and separates — producing privacy, point of view, and specialism — number's tactile resonance is integral and incorporating, closer to the tribal "heap" than to the literate series. This is the link to [[Detribalization and Retribalization]]: McLuhan reads the electric, statistical amassing of numbers as restoring "a new influx of primitive intuition," even as literacy had earlier given number "a separate life and intensity." [`Directly stated`]

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — *Understanding Media* (1964), Chapter 11 ("Number: Profile of the Crowd"). Number is the extension and separation of touch, set against writing as the extension of sight. Tactile, grasping, and incorporating, number explains the growth-drive of crowds and wealth and carries an iconic, compressed power; it is "auditory and resonant" in form but originates in touch. The haptic/Bauhaus lineage (Klee, Gropius, Moholy-Nagy) supplies the recovery of touch as the integrating sense.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Typographic Man]] — writing is the extension of *sight*; the typographic, visual regime fragments, sequences, and separates (privacy, point of view, specialism), whereas number is the extension of *touch* and is integral, resonant, and incorporating. The opposition is internal to McLuhan's sense-paired account, not a separate tension. [`Directly stated`]

## Questions It Raises

- If number is the extension of touch and writing the extension of sight, what does the digital computer — which "substitutes 'yes' and 'no' for numbers" and is "strong on contours, weak on digits" — extend? Does the binary machine return number toward the tactile/contour sense or further abstract it?
- Is the growth-drive of crowds and capital genuinely *tactile* — the hand's grasp externalized — or is "touch" here a diagnostic metaphor for incorporation and accumulation?
- McLuhan calls number "auditory and resonant" yet rooted in touch; how stable is the touch/sound/sight mapping when a single medium spans several senses?
- If literacy gave number "a separate life and intensity" while nonliterate societies "had small use for numbers," is quantification itself a symptom of "developing visual stress" — number recruited into the service of the visual, fragmenting regime it ostensibly opposes?
