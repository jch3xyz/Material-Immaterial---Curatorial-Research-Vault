# Decision Rights

## Brief Definition

The power to choose whether to keep something secret or to share it — Zuboff's reframing of privacy, which surveillance capitalism does not so much erode as redistribute, concentrating the rights over disclosure within surveillance capital.

## Longer Explanation

In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, Shoshana Zuboff distinguishes "decision rights" from "privacy" in order to recast the standard privacy complaint as a question of power. Decision rights, in her formulation, "confer the power to choose whether to keep something secret or to share it," and the individual who holds them "can choose the degree of privacy or transparency for each situation." She grounds this in US Supreme Court Justice William O. Douglas's 1967 articulation of privacy as "the choice of the individual to disclose or to reveal what he believes, what he thinks, what he possesses." Privacy, on this reading, is not a quantity that one has more or less of; it is the *self-determining authority to decide* who knows what. (`Directly stated`)

The central move of the chapter is that the conventional lament — that privacy is being *eroded* — misses what is actually happening. "In the larger societal pattern," Zuboff writes, "privacy is not eroded but redistributed, as decision rights over privacy are claimed for surveillance capital." The rights themselves do not disappear; they migrate. Instead of individuals deciding "how and what they will disclose, these rights are concentrated within the domain of surveillance capitalism." This is why Zuboff treats decision rights, rather than privacy, as the right unit of analysis: it makes visible a transfer of power, not a mere leakage of information. (`Directly stated`)

The 2003 Google patent ("Generating user information for use in targeted advertising") is Zuboff's documentary evidence that this expropriation of decision rights is a declared design goal, not an accident. The patent's inventors caution that user profile information (UPI) "can be determined (or updated or extended) even when no explicit information is given to the system," and that an "initial UPI may include some expressly entered UPI information, though it doesn't need to." Zuboff reads this as Google making "clear that they are willing — and that their inventions are able — to overcome the friction entailed in users' decision rights": the company's methods "enable it to surveil, capture, expand, construct, and claim behavioral surplus, including data that users intentionally choose not to share." Decision rights are not so much violated as rendered moot in advance — the patent anticipates the recalcitrant user who withholds, and deduces the data anyway. (`Directly stated`)

The deepest harm Zuboff names is temporal and epistemic: decision rights "vanish before one even knows that there is a decision to make." Because rendition is ubiquitous and obscure, the moment of choice is bypassed entirely; the user is never positioned as a chooser. This connects [[Decision Rights]] to her broader political diagnosis — surveillance is "the path to profit that overrides 'we the people,' taking our decision rights without permission and even when we say 'no.'" The redistribution of decision rights is thus the micro-mechanism beneath the macro-claims of [[Instrumentarian Power]] and [[Big Other]]: a population stripped of the authority to decide its own disclosure is a population available for prediction and modification. (`Strongly implied`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — surveillance capitalism is the economic logic that *lays claim to* decision rights; this concept names what it takes.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the asset extracted once decision rights over disclosure are overridden; the patent's UPI mechanisms are the apparatus of that override.
- [[The Right to Sanctuary]] and [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — Zuboff's positive counter-claims; both are attempts to re-anchor self-determining decision rights against their expropriation.
- [[Big Other]] / [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the architecture and the power form that presuppose the prior capture of individual decision rights.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] (Han) — Han's diagnosis of *voluntary* self-exposure is the obverse of Zuboff's claim that decision rights are taken "even when we say 'no'"; the two frame the same disclosure problem from the side of the subject (Han) and the side of capital (Zuboff). (`Speculative connection`)
- [[Surveillance]] / [[Quantified Self]] — the broader cluster within which the redistribution of decision rights operates.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Privacy vs Decision Rights]] — the tension this concept anchors: Zuboff explicitly argues that framing the problem as *privacy* (a possession that erodes) obscures the real issue, which is *decision rights* (an authority that is transferred). The pair is less an opposition of values than a contest over the correct description.

## Questions It Raises

- If privacy is decision rights, can the harm of surveillance capitalism be remedied by privacy law at all — or only by laws that restore the *authority to decide*?
- When decision rights "vanish before one even knows that there is a decision to make," what would meaningful consent even consist of?
- Is the redistribution of decision rights reversible, or does the concentration of disclosure-authority within surveillance capital constitute a one-way [[The Coup from Above|coup]]?
- How does Zuboff's account of taken decision rights relate to Han's account of subjects who exercise their decision rights *toward* total self-exposure?
