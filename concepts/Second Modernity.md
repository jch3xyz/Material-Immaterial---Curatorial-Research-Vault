# Second Modernity

## Brief Definition

The historical condition of psychological individuality — a society of people "born to a sense of psychological individuality, with its double-edged birthright of liberation and necessity," who experience both the right and the requirement to choose their own lives.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Shoshana Zuboff]], the *second modernity* is the latest phase of "individualization," the centuries-long process she calls "the human signature of the modern era." It must be sharply distinguished from two things it is routinely confused with. First, from the *first modernity* — the mass society of Ford's Model T consumers, the moment when "life became 'individualized' for great numbers of people as they separated from traditional norms, meanings, and rules." Second, and emphatically, from the neoliberal *ideology of "individualism"* that "shifts all responsibility for success or failure to a mythical, atomized, isolated individual, doomed to a life of perpetual competition." Individualization is a consequence of long-term modernization; individualism is an ideology weaponized against it. *(Directly stated)*

The second modernity is born of the first: "What began as a modern migration from traditional lifeways bloomed into a new society of people born to a sense of psychological individuality, with its double-edged birthright of liberation and necessity." Its members experience "both the right and the requirement to choose our own lives." No longer content to be anonymous members of the mass, they feel an entitlement to self-determination — "an extraordinary achievement of the human spirit, even as it can be a life sentence to uncertainty, anxiety, and stress." In Zuboff's account this "felt experience" of psychological sovereignty broke upon the world long before the internet appeared to amplify its claims; by the second modernity, "the self is all we have," and everything — "family, religion, sex, gender, morality, marriage, community, love, nature, social connections, political participation, career, food" — "must be reviewed, renegotiated, and reconstructed on the terms that make sense to us." *(Directly stated)*

The concept is structurally unstable, and that instability is the heart of Zuboff's argument. She names "the existential contradiction of the second modernity that defines our conditions of existence: we want to exercise control over our own lives, but everywhere that control is thwarted." Some of this instability is the intrinsic cost of authoring one's own life, but much of it is engineered: "second-modernity instability is also the result of institutionalized shifts in economic and social policies and practices associated with the neoliberal paradigm." Zuboff describes a "destructive collision of two opposing historical forces" — one vector being the long shift "from the mass to the individual," the other being the neoliberal project whose aim is "to reverse, subdue, impede, and even destroy the individual urge toward psychological self-determination and moral agency." *(Directly stated)*

This collision is what makes the second modernity the staging ground for [[Surveillance Capitalism]]. The same conditions that "summoned the emancipatory promise of the internet into our everyday lives as we sought remedies for inequality and exclusion" also "provide important shelter for surveillance capitalism's ability to root and flourish." The unmet second-modernity hunger for self-determination, colliding with neoliberal economic violence, leaves individuals exposed to "the draconian quid pro quo" in which "information and connection are ransomed for the lucrative behavioral data" — the second modernity furnishes both the need that platforms answer and the vulnerability they exploit. *(Strongly implied)* Zuboff leaves the contradiction open, asking whether "the instability of the second modernity" can "give way to a new synthesis: a third modernity that transcends the collision." *(Directly stated)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — The second modernity is the condition of "psychological individuality," the lived demand for self-determination produced by individualization and thwarted by the neoliberal habitat; its "existential contradiction" both motivates the embrace of digital tools and shelters surveillance capitalism. Zuboff borrows the periodization (first/second modernity) and the "existential contradiction" framing partly from the sociologist [[Zygmunt Bauman]], whom she quotes on "the yawning gap between the right of self-assertion and the capacity to control the social settings which render such self-assertion feasible." *(Directly stated)*

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the second modernity is its enabling "stage"; the unmet hunger for self-determination and the neoliberal shelter together let it root and flourish.
- [[Surveillance]] — the data-extractive apparatus that fastens onto second-modernity individuals through the "quid pro quo" of connection-for-behavioral-data.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — Zuboff's self-determining, sovereign individual is a sociological cousin of the autonomous liberal subject; the second modernity is where that subject's claims become felt mass experience.
- [[Achievement Society]] — Han's self-exploiting achievement-subject names, from another angle, the same compulsory freedom Zuboff calls the "requirement to choose our own lives" ("a life sentence to uncertainty, anxiety, and stress").
- [[Biopolitics]] — the second modernity is the terrain on which power shifts from governing populations to capturing individual self-determination.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the second modernity *is* this tension lived as a historical condition: the right to author one's life set against an "economics and politics from whose vantage point we are but ciphers."
- The neoliberal ideology of *individualism* — Zuboff explicitly opposes individualization (a modernizing process) to "individualism" (an atomizing ideology aimed at reversing it). *(Directly stated)*

## Questions It Raises

- If the second modernity's craving for self-determination is precisely what surveillance capitalism feeds on, can that craving be satisfied without enriching the apparatus that exploits it?
- Is the "third modernity" Zuboff gestures toward a genuine historical possibility, or only a rhetorical horizon?
- How does Zuboff's individualization-vs-individualism distinction map onto Han's achievement society, where "freedom itself is producing coercion"?
- Does the "double-edged birthright of liberation and necessity" make psychological individuality an achievement or a burden — and who decides?
