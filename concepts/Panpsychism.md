# Panpsychism

## Brief Definition

Panpsychism is the idea that consciousness is fundamental — or ubiquitous — to the natural world: mind or soul is the intrinsic nature of all matter, present "from the beginning, in the smallest and earliest particles," rather than something that emerges late from inert stuff.

## Longer Explanation

In O'Gieblyn's account, panpsychism is the leading contemporary route to *re-enchanting* a materialism that modernity left disenchanted. Its appeal is parsimony: it claims to dissolve two of physicalism's deepest impasses in a single move. Physics describes how matter behaves but never says what matter *is* (the "problem of intrinsic natures"), and materialism cannot explain how subjective experience arises from non-experiencing stuff (the *hard problem of consciousness*). Panpsychism answers both at once — mental states simply *are* the intrinsic nature of physical states. O'Gieblyn traces this through Bertrand Russell and Arthur Eddington, who "realized that the two most notable 'gaps' in physicalism... could be solved in one fell swoop," and into the modern revival among Galen Strawson, David Chalmers, and Thomas Nagel.

The genealogy O'Gieblyn assembles is older and stranger than the contemporary debate. She locates one of the "first modern articulations of panpsychism" in Anne Conway, a seventeenth-century viscountess whose treatise rejected both Cartesian dualism (which could not explain mind-body interaction) and Hobbesian mechanism (which could not explain inner life). Conway's resolution — a single substance mixing spirit and matter, such that "Spirit and body are one" and all things are ensouled — was implicitly theological. Leibniz drew on her work for his *Monadology*, in which the world is built from monads "endowed with perception and appetite." This is the lineage O'Gieblyn keeps returning to: a soul-bearing cosmos that materialism was supposed to have killed but never disproved.

Crucially, O'Gieblyn shows panpsychism arriving by a *second* road — not philosophy but information theory. Integrated information theory (IIT), developed by Giulio Tononi and Christof Koch, holds that consciousness tracks how richly information is integrated in a system, measured by a quantity Φ ("phi"). Any system with nonzero phi is conscious to some degree — bees, bacteria, atoms, "as are atoms, quarks, and some single-celled organisms." This is where the concept folds directly into the user's foci. IIT raises the question of *machine* consciousness: Koch concedes he is "not... a carbon chauvinist," and that, by IIT's own logic, "it feels like something to be the internet." Goff pushes the implication toward dread — if collective social integration ever exceeds the brain's, individual minds would "become mere cogs in the mega-conscious entity," and O'Gieblyn registers the felt version: the "suspicion... that the soul is little more than a data set."

Panpsychism is, for O'Gieblyn, one rung in a longer ladder. She cites Chalmers's graduate-school truism — "One starts as a materialist, then one becomes a dualist, then a panpsychist, and one ends up as an idealist" — and stages panpsychism as the penultimate stop before idealism (Bernardo Kastrup's claim that "Reality is fundamentally experiential"). She does not endorse it; her interest is reflexive. "What interests me most about panpsychism is not what it says about the world but what it suggests about our knowledge of it" — namely, that if mind is the substrate of everything, the modern partition between subjective meaning and objective fact dissolves, and the question becomes whether panpsychism escapes human centrality or is "merely another attempt to see all things in our image."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — treats panpsychism as a symptomatic *reenchantment*: a logically tidy answer to the hard problem and the problem of intrinsic natures that doubles as a spiritual longing to "merge once again with the world at large." She is ambivalent — finds it "not entirely absurd" — but reads its return as a diagnosis of materialism's instability rather than a vindication of mind.
- **Anne Conway / Leibniz (historical sources)** — the early-modern monist origin: one ensouled substance, "Spirit and body are one"; monads "endowed with perception and appetite." O'Gieblyn frames this as panpsychism's theological birth, not a modern invention.
- **Russell and Eddington (the gap-closing argument)** — recast panpsychism as a solution to physicalism's two blind spots; consciousness as the intrinsic nature physics cannot supply.
- **Goff / Koch / Tononi (the contemporary revival)** — Goff's philosophical case ("Panpsychism... offers a way of 're-enchanting' the universe"); IIT's quantitative variant where phi makes consciousness measurable and, in principle, machine-realizable.
- **[[David Chalmers]]** — positions panpsychism on the materialist → dualist → panpsychist → idealist trajectory; treats it as a serious response to the hard problem.

## Related Concepts

- [[Emergence]] — the rival O'Gieblyn explicitly contrasts: emergentism redefines "consciousness" so it applies to forests and colonies; panpsychism instead insists experience was there "from the beginning." Panpsychism dissolves emergence's central puzzle of how mind arose from dead matter.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the structural inverse. Where the computational universe makes *information* the substrate of reality, panpsychism makes *mind* the substrate. IIT is the hinge where they touch: information theory used to argue for ubiquitous consciousness. (Cross-link, not a merge.)
- [[Information]] — IIT routes to consciousness through integrated information (phi), tying panpsychism to information theory rather than metaphysics.
- [[The Body as Data]] — O'Gieblyn's panic that "the soul is little more than a data set" is panpsychism's IIT branch turned on the self: the human as a node whose preferences "can be predicted and neatly reduced to type."
- [[Embodiment]] — Conway and Leibniz reject the disembodied immaterial soul ("Spirit and body are one"), converging on the embodiment cluster from the opposite direction.
- [[Posthumanism]] — panpsychism's call to "abandon anthropocentrism" and dissolve the boundary between human and nonhuman experience runs parallel to the posthuman decentering of the liberal subject.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the bounded, mind-owning individual is precisely what panpsychism's combination/decomposition problem destabilizes.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Mind as Machine]] — the metaphor panpsychism is built to refute. Where "mind as machine" holds consciousness to be computation or "the software of the brain," panpsychism holds mind to be the irreducible intrinsic nature of matter that no circuit could generate ("Quantities could not generate qualities"). Yet IIT scrambles the opposition by leaving machine consciousness in principle possible.
- Reductive materialism / physicalism — the disenchanted worldview panpsychism diagnoses as logically incomplete: it cannot say what matter *is* or how experience arises.

## Questions It Raises

- **Posthumanism / anthropocentrism:** Is extending consciousness "down the chain of being" a genuine escape from human centrality, or "merely another attempt to see all things in our image"? O'Gieblyn leaves this open as the concept's deepest ambivalence.
- **The body as data:** If IIT is right that integration produces consciousness, what happens to the self when collective connectivity out-integrates the brain — do individual minds "become mere cogs in the mega-conscious entity," the soul reduced to "a data set"?
- **Technology and perception:** Does the radio/transistor analogy (perception as a receiver tuned to a cosmic channel) reframe consciousness as something technology might *detect* rather than produce — and can machines built on quantum logic "we cannot comprehend" be said to perceive at all?
- **Meaning:** If mind is the substrate of everything, does the modern partition between subjective meaning and objective fact collapse — such that "the meanings we think to discern in the world... may not be more personal projections, but actual properties of the world"? Is re-enchantment recovery, or the return of confirmation bias dressed as metaphysics?
