# Human Body Version 2.0

## Brief Definition

Ray Kurzweil's program of reverse-engineering and incrementally replacing the body's biological organs — blood, heart, lungs, digestion, hormonal system, skeleton, skin — one "small, benign step at a time," until by the early 2030s the human body is more nonbiological than biological.

## Longer Explanation

"Human Body Version 2.0" is Kurzweil's name for the engineered successor to the frail biological original (which he figures as [[Version 1.0 Biological Bodies]]). It is the concrete, organ-by-organ cash-out of his larger thesis of [[Transcending Biology]]: where that thesis is the polemical argument that biology is a substrate to be left behind, version 2.0 is the practical roadmap for doing so. The "intertwined revolutions of G, N, and R" (genetics, nanotechnology, robotics) supply the tools; `Directly stated`, Kurzweil writes that they "will transform our frail version 1.0 human bodies into their far more durable and capable version 2.0 counterparts."

Crucially, the upgrade is gradualist and medical in its early framing — "we will implement it one small, benign step at a time" — which lets each replacement be justified as therapy before it becomes enhancement. Kurzweil walks through the organs in sequence. Digestion is redesigned so that "special metabolic nanobots" introduce nutrients directly into the bloodstream while "elimination nanobots" act "like tiny garbage compactors," freeing us from eating-for-survival. Blood is made "programmable": Rob Freitas's [[Nanobots|respirocytes]] (robotic red blood cells), artificial platelets, and "microbivores" (white-blood-cell replacements) outperform their biological originals by orders of magnitude. The heart is not repaired but eliminated — "a more effective approach will be to get rid of the heart altogether" — because Freitas's self-propelling nanorobotic blood cells make centralized pumping unnecessary. Lungs follow, then "all the hormone-producing organs," kidneys, liver, and the gastrointestinal tract.

By the early 2030s Kurzweil totals the ledger `Directly stated`: "We've eliminated the heart, lungs, red and white blood cells, platelets, pancreas, thyroid and all the hormone-producing organs, kidneys, bladder, liver, lower esophagus, stomach, small intestines, large intestines, and bowel. What we have left at this point is the skeleton, skin, sex organs, sensory organs, mouth and upper esophagus, and brain." Even the survivors are slated for nanoengineered replacement: "The human skeleton version 2.0 will be very strong, stable, and self-repairing," and the skin is to be improved "with new nanoengineered supple materials." The endpoint Kurzweil states plainly: "By the 2030s we will become more nonbiological than biological." This is the threshold he glosses under the heading "We Are Becoming Cyborgs" (see [[The Cyborg]]).

Version 2.0 is explicitly not the terminus. Kurzweil reserves **Human Body Version 3.0** — "in the 2030s and 2040s" — for "a more fundamental redesign," in which, by incorporating "MNT-based fabrication into ourselves," "we'll be able to rapidly alter our physical manifestation at will." Where 2.0 reformulates each subsystem one at a time, 3.0 is the morphable body changeable at will, illustrated by J. Storrs Hall's "foglets." `Interpretive synthesis`: across both versions the operative logic is that the body is information whose biological implementation is a contingent, optimizable substrate — the position the vault tracks as [[The Body as Data]] and contests under [[Embodiment vs Information]] and [[Material vs Immaterial]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — coins and elaborates "human body version 2.0" (and 3.0) in *The Singularity Is Near*, Ch. 6, as the engineering program that realizes [[Transcending Biology]]. He frames it as the next stage of an existing trend (hip and joint replacements, artificial hearts) extended to total replacement, and defends it dialogically against the objection (voiced by "Bill") that "replacing a human body with a machine to exceed human performance leaves you with, well, a machine," answering that "the essence of being human ... it's our ability to reach beyond our limitations."
- **Natasha Vita-More** *(cited, not a vault note)* — Kurzweil points to her conceptual design "Primo Posthuman" as an artist's instance of the same optimizing impulse (mobility, flexibility, superlongevity).
- **Rob Freitas** *(cited, not a vault note)* — supplies the specific component designs (respirocytes, the "vasculoid") Kurzweil enrolls into the version 2.0 body.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — version 2.0 presupposes that intelligence and selfhood survive substrate replacement; the embodiment pole denies this.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the deposit this note cashes out: replacing biological matter while claiming continuity of the person treats the material body as inessential.

## Questions It Raises

- If each replacement is "small" and "benign," at what point — if any — does the cumulative substitution amount to ending the original person rather than upgrading them? (Kurzweil stages exactly this objection through "Bill" and declines to draw a line.)
- Does the gradualist, medical framing ("overcoming profound diseases and disabilities") function as a Trojan horse for non-therapeutic enhancement, as Kurzweil concedes when he calls medical applications "only the early-adoption phase"?
- Is the claim that the person persists across total organ replacement an empirical prediction or a metaphysical commitment to [[The Body as Data]]?
- What is preserved when the only "kept" organs (skin, sex and sensory organs, mouth, brain) are retained for "communication and pleasure" rather than function — is the body here anything but an interface?
