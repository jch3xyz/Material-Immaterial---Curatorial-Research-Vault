# The Robot

## Brief Definition

The robot is the modern, non-anthropomorphic mechanical man — a mechanical servant built "in the spirit of the modern, ultra-functional machine," whose very name (Czech for "forced labor," coined by Karel Capek) carries an implied lack of autonomy and free will.

## Longer Explanation

In Jack Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* the robot is the late, debased terminus of the long history of [[Automata]] — the "subsculptural" tradition of self-propelled images of men and animals that Burnham treats as the aesthetic core of [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]]. Where the eighteenth-century android (Vaucanson's flute player, his digesting duck) strove for organic fidelity, the robot abandons that ambition entirely. Burnham marks the difference precisely: "Its difference lies in its non-human characteristics, that is a lack of skin coloring, hair, clothing, and natural movements. Rather, the robot is an attempt to produce a mechanical servant in the spirit of the modern, ultra-functional machine." The robot is thus the android stripped of mimetic disguise — the form that lets the machine *look like* a machine. *Directly stated*.

The word itself encodes the concept's politics. "The term robot, first coined by Capek, comes from the Czech word meaning 'forced labor,' and implies a lack of autonomy and free will." Burnham is careful to note an irony of origins: in Capek's *R.U.R.* (1921) the robots "were not metallic imitations of men but androids, machines constructed to appear human" — synthetic flesh, not clanking steel. The familiar polished-steel figure is a later corruption: "Gradually Capek's original conception of the word robot has undergone debasement until it implies either a fearsome, cartoon-style, clanking mechanical man, or a person who insensitively follows orders with rigid efficiency regardless of their implications." Burnham splits the debased term into two fearsome meanings — the steel folklore monster, and the human reduced to mechanical obedience — both "hostile to some of the most valuable human qualities." *Directly stated*.

This second, sociological meaning is where the concept does its critical work. Burnham reads the decline of nineteenth-century automata against "rising signs of human alienation," and follows the robot into the postwar workplace: "in derision we may refer to someone with a strong sense of loyalty to his organization as a 'cheerful robot.'" The robot becomes the figure of the alienated organization man, of "an attitude of alienation where machines prevail, producing a decay of social, moral, and vocational ideals," and ultimately of a political danger — that habituation to mechanized life makes domination feel like convenience: "The danger is that as the public becomes bored and reconciled to the possibility of an automaton existence it acquiesces to government by technological expediency." *Directly stated*.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** (*Beyond Modern Sculpture*, ch. 5 "Sculpture and Automata") — the robot is the modern, ultra-functional, non-anthropomorphic mechanical man: the android's successor that drops skin/hair/clothing/natural movement; named for "forced labor," and so encoding the loss of autonomy and free will; debased into both a steel-folklore monster and, sociologically, the alienated "cheerful robot" of the organization man, whose acceptance threatens "government by technological expediency." Burnham distinguishes this clanking robot from the cybernetic "pseudo-organisms" (Grey Walter's tortoises, Ross Ashby's homeostat, Claude Shannon's maze-running mice) that "tenuously linked with life itself" — the robot is the Luddite-fear caricature, [[Cybernetics]] the genuinely lifelike successor. *Directly stated*.

## Related Concepts

- [[Automata]] — the parent category; the robot is automata's modern, mechanized, non-anthropomorphic phase.
- [[Subsculpture]] — the broader class of images "aesthetically ranked below sculpture"; the robot is a subsculptural form that re-entered the fine arts via Cubism (Archipenko, Léger, Picabia, Duchamp).
- [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] — the deep drive the robot serves, now stripped of religious/mimetic aspiration to bare functionalism.
- [[The Cyborg]] — Burnham's later (ch. 8) successor figure; the robot is the dead-end mechanical man, the cyborg the responsive man-machine system. Bridges to [[Donna Haraway]] and [[N. Katherine Hayles]].
- [[Cybernetics]] — the postwar science whose "electronic pseudo-organisms" displace the robot; what the robot only caricatured (the conscious machine), cybernetics approaches in fact.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Vitalism]] — the robot is the nonbiological re-creation of life, the antithesis of the living organism it imitates; "doomed to failure... by a final sense of inconviction."
- The eighteenth-century **android** (mimetic, skinned, naturally moving) — the robot is defined precisely *against* it: an "ultra-functional machine" that abandons anthropomorphic illusion. *Directly stated*.

## Questions It Raises

- If the robot's name means "forced labor," is the figure ever separable from a politics of subordination — of the machine, and of the human reduced to it?
- Does Burnham's "cheerful robot" (the loyal organization man) anticipate the vault's later diagnoses of self-mechanizing subjectivity — the [[Achievement Society]] subject, [[Auto-Exploitation]] — where coercion is internalized as cheerful compliance?
- Is "acquiescence to government by technological expediency" a precursor to the [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[Dataism]] — domination accepted because mechanized life is convenient?
- Why does the robot fail as art ("a final sense of inconviction") where the cyborg might succeed? What does responsiveness add that mere animation cannot?
