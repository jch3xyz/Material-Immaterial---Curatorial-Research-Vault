# Phenomenalism (Burnham)

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's name for the post-formalist aesthetic in which the subject of an artwork is the *experience of perception* rather than the form of the object — "transcendent phenomenalism" succeeding "transcendent idealism," with the sculpted form reduced to a means or vehicle for triggering perceptual experience in the viewer.

## Longer Explanation

Phenomenalism is the titular coinage of Chapter 4 of *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, "Form Exhaustion and the Rise of Phenomenalism" — the climax of Part I ("Sculpture as Object") and the hinge of the whole book. Burnham diagnoses the simultaneous exhaustion of the two governing programs of twentieth-century sculpture, [[Vitalism]] and "formal-reductivism," and announces their succession by an art whose concern shifts "away from a basis concern for form reduction and formal relationships and toward the varieties of experience which surround the perception of form." The decisive reversal is from making form to staging perception: "Through this approach the sculpted form is not an end in itself, but only the means-the vehicle-by which perceptual experiences are made possible" (`Directly stated`).

Burnham frames the historical transition in deliberately metaphysical terms, naming it as the succession of one transcendental regime by another: "Static sculpture has emerged from millenniums of transcendent idealism and now moves toward transcendent phenomenalism." Where classical sculpture sought "the creation of immutable ideals through objects" — the iconic, transcendent image craved by both the vitalists and the Constructivists — phenomenalist art relocates meaning into the act of looking. Burnham reads the new [[Object Art|Object / Minimal sculpture]] of Judd, Morris, Flavin, and Tony Smith through this lens: the inert object "does attempt to present meaning as a series of perceptual experiences," and "the obligation to 'vitalize' sculpture-make it come to life before the viewer-has been shifted to the viewer's capacity for analyzing his private methods of seeing" (`Directly stated`).

The philosophical engine Burnham imports is phenomenology — Husserl and especially Merleau-Ponty. From Husserl he takes the "crisis in European science" and *phenomenological reduction*, the distilling of "'pure' conscious experience... unhindered by all previous experiences, abstractions, or presuppositions." From Merleau-Ponty he takes perception as an *existential bodily act*: the viewer "not only is free to but must reconstruct the sculpture for himself," and "Perhaps a special crisis had to appear before sculptors would begin to consider seeing as essentially an existential act by the observer." This is why phenomenalism is, for the vault, the strongest primary-text anchor of art conceived as a perceptual apparatus — the body and its visual habits, not the form, become the site of the work (`Strongly implied`, parallel to Hayles's [[Embodiment]]). Merleau-Ponty's "carnal intersubjectivity" — "What arises from the first glance is what Merleau-Ponty called 'carnal intersubjectivity'" — names the relational perceptual field that "arises" between viewer and object(s) as the viewer moves among them.

Burnham attaches a sharp critical caveat that keeps phenomenalism from collapsing into mystification. The phenomenalist work cannot conjure content out of nothing; it can only sensitize us to the unreliability of our own seeing: "Yet in all candor, beyond the domain of scientific analysis, phenomenalism cannot imbue an object with attributes which are not there. It can only force us to be aware of the chaos and multi-layered nature of our visual habits." This is the disciplined limit Burnham draws around the new aesthetic — and his guard against the over-claiming he detected in Minimalism's "abbreviated notion of phenomenalism" (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — phenomenalism is Burnham's coinage for the experiential aesthetic that succeeds form-making once formalism and vitalism reach [[Form Exhaustion|exhaustion]]; "transcendent phenomenalism" succeeding "transcendent idealism," the sculpted form a "vehicle" for perceptual experience, theorized via Husserl's phenomenological reduction and Merleau-Ponty's "existential" perception and "carnal intersubjectivity." He treats it as an "abbreviated" or "idealistic" perceptualism in the Object sculptors' hands, and bounds it with the caveat that it "cannot imbue an object with attributes which are not there."

The philosophers Edmund Husserl, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, and (against them) G.W.F. Hegel and Albert Einstein, and the artists Donald Judd, Robert Morris, Dan Flavin, Tony Smith, and the painter Paul Cézanne, are named in Burnham's text; they are rendered here as plain text rather than wiki-links, as no canonical notes for them yet exist in the vault. The qualifier "(Burnham)" keeps this concept distinct from philosophical *sense-data phenomenalism* (the doctrine that objects are reducible to actual and possible sense impressions); Burnham's phenomenalism is an art-historical category for an experiential, perception-staging aesthetic, drawn from phenomenology rather than from the empiricist tradition.

## Related Concepts

- [[Object Art]] — phenomenalism's vehicle: Burnham reads de-idealized Object / Minimal sculpture as the form in which "transcendent idealism" gives way to "transcendent phenomenalism," meaning relocated from the object into "a series of perceptual experiences."
- [[Embodiment]] — Merleau-Ponty's "existential act" of seeing and "carnal intersubjectivity" ground perception in the perceiving body; phenomenalism is the art-historical correlate of the body-as-ground-of-perception that Hayles theorizes.
- [[Form Exhaustion]] — phenomenalism is the successor program that arises *because* formalism and vitalism have run out of new forms; the two are the paired halves of the chapter's title.
- [[Scientific Idealism]] — the iconic, model-borrowing impulse that phenomenalism leaves behind once a "world of invisible values" renders the "strictly visual-geometrical" picture of reality obsolete.
- [[Object and System]] — phenomenalism names the object-to-experience transition that Part II reframes as object-to-system; the burden of "vitalizing" passes first to the viewer's perception, then to the lifelike system.
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — phenomenalism is the primary-text root of art conceived as an instrument for triggering perceptual experience rather than presenting form (committed research-foci stub).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Idealism]] — Burnham frames the shift as the supersession of "transcendent idealism" (the immutable ideal, the transcendent icon) by "transcendent phenomenalism" (the perceptual situation); the image/icon gives way to the staged experience of seeing.

## Questions It Raises

- If the meaning of a phenomenalist work lies in "a series of perceptual experiences" staged in the viewer rather than in the object, is it still sculpture — or has art become a branch of phenomenology and Optical Art?
- Burnham warns that phenomenalism "cannot imbue an object with attributes which are not there." How much of Minimalism's claimed depth is in the object, and how much is the viewer's projection onto an inert thing?
- Does the turn from "transcendent idealism" to "transcendent phenomenalism" actually escape transcendence, or merely relocate it from the icon to the act of perception?
- If "seeing" is "essentially an existential act by the observer," does phenomenalist art presuppose a trained, perceptually self-aware viewer — and what becomes of it when "viewers will accept such a responsibility" only partially, if at all?
