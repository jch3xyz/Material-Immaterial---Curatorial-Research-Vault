# Evolutionary Humanism

## Brief Definition

One of the three humanist sects in Harari's taxonomy: the creed that conflict drives natural selection, that some humans are simply superior to others, and that the fittest should prevail — a century-old call to breed or build superhumans whose most extreme historical exhibit was Nazism, and whose dream [[Techno-Humanism]] now hopes to fulfil by peaceful technological means.

## Longer Explanation

In *Homo Deus*, Harari treats humanism not as a single world view but as a religion that "fragmented into several conflicting sects," all of which agree that human experience is the supreme source of authority and meaning yet "interpret human experience in different ways." Humanism's three branches are liberal (orthodox) humanism, socialist humanism, and **evolutionary humanism** — the last of which "sprouted" during the nineteenth and twentieth centuries and "whose most famous advocates were the Nazis" (Directly stated). What separates evolutionary humanism from its liberal and socialist siblings is its answer to the problem of conflicting human experiences. Where socialists subordinate the individual to the party and liberals "agonise about such contradictions," evolutionary humanism, "rooting itself in the firm ground of Darwinian evolutionary theory," holds that "conflict is something to applaud rather than lament." Conflict is "the raw material of natural selection, which pushes evolution forward. Some humans are simply superior to others, and when human experiences collide, the fittest humans should steamroll everyone else" (Directly stated).

From this evolutionary logic Harari draws the sect's program: if "in the name of human rights or human equality we emasculate the fittest humans," we prevent "the rise of the superman" and risk "the degeneration and extinction of *Homo sapiens*." The superior humans who "herald the coming of the superman" might be "entire races, particular tribes or exceptional individual geniuses," distinguished by "better abilities" in knowledge, technology, society, or art. The sect therefore valorizes war — for the character Harry Lime, and for Harari's reading of it, "the experience of war pushes humankind to new achievements," letting "natural selection free rein at last." Harari folds [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s "what does not kill me makes me stronger" and "war is 'the school of life'" into this lineage, and reads Hitler's *Mein Kampf* account of the trenches as the sect's autobiographical exhibit — the revelation that the world "is a jungle run by the remorseless laws of natural selection." (These figures are treated by Harari as illustrations of the sect, not as the concept itself.)

Crucially, Harari insists the concept must not be collapsed into its worst instance. "Hitler and the Nazis represent only one extreme version of evolutionary humanism," he writes; "just as Stalin's gulags do not automatically nullify every socialist idea and argument, so too the horrors of Nazism should not blind us to whatever insights evolutionary humanism might offer." Nazism, on his account, "was born from the pairing of evolutionary humanism with particular racial theories and ultra-nationalist emotions," and so "not all evolutionary humanists are racists, and not every belief in humankind's potential for further evolution necessarily calls for setting up police states and concentration camps" (Directly stated). "Auschwitz should serve as a blood-red warning sign rather than as a black curtain that hides entire sections of the human horizon" — a sect that "played an important part in the shaping of modern culture" and "is likely to play an even greater role in the shaping of the twenty-first century."

That greater future role is where the concept connects to Harari's later argument. In "The Ocean of Consciousness," the techno-religion of [[Techno-Humanism]] — which would use "genetic engineering, nanotechnology and brain–computer interfaces" to create *Homo deus*, "a much superior human model" — is described as "an updated variant on the old dreams of evolutionary humanism, which already a century ago called for the creation of superhumans" (Directly stated). The difference is means, not end: "whereas Hitler and his ilk planned to create superhumans by means of selective breeding and ethnic cleansing, twenty-first-century techno-humanism hopes to reach the goal far more peacefully." Evolutionary humanism is thus the historical hinge by which Harari links the eugenic past to the engineering present, and the ancestor concept beneath the contemporary projects gathered under [[Transhumanism]] and [[Posthumanism]] (Interpretive synthesis).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — names evolutionary humanism as one of three humanist sects (alongside liberal and socialist humanism), defines it by the applause of conflict and the doctrine that the fittest should prevail, marks Nazism as its most extreme exhibit while explicitly refusing to reduce the concept to that exhibit, and reads its century-old dream of superhumans as the lineage that [[Techno-Humanism]] updates by peaceful technological means.

## Related Concepts

- [[Techno-Humanism]] — the twenty-first-century techno-religion Harari calls an "updated variant" of evolutionary humanism's dream of superhumans, pursuing the same goal by genetic engineering, nanotechnology, and brain–computer interfaces rather than breeding and ethnic cleansing.
- [[Transhumanism]] — the broader contemporary movement to upgrade the human by technology; evolutionary humanism is its eugenic ancestor in Harari's genealogy (Interpretive synthesis).
- [[Posthumanism]] — the horizon beyond *Homo sapiens* toward which both the sect's "superman" and later upgrade projects point.
- [[Dataism]] — the rival techno-religion in *Homo Deus*; unlike evolutionary/techno-humanism it abandons the human will entirely, worshipping data rather than superhumans.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — liberal (orthodox) humanism treats every individual as a "unique" and "never-to-be-repeated" ray of light deserving equal freedom; evolutionary humanism explicitly attacks this equality, holding that emasculating the fittest in the name of human rights or equality endangers the species.

## Questions It Raises

- Can the concept's "insights" really be separated from the eugenic and genocidal program that was its most extreme exhibit, as Harari claims, or does the "blood-red warning sign" travel with every later variant?
- If techno-humanism is the same dream pursued "far more peacefully," does changing the means (engineering rather than ethnic cleansing) change the ethics of the end (the creation of a superior human model)?
- When superiority is defined by "better abilities" in technology and the production of new knowledge, who sets the yardstick — and does the rise of non-conscious algorithms invalidate or intensify the sect's hierarchy of human worth?
- Does Harari's own three-sect taxonomy, by treating evolutionary humanism as a permanent option rather than a defeated one, keep its logic alive into the twenty-first century?
