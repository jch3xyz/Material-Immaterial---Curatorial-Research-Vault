# Techno-Humanism

## Brief Definition

In *Homo Deus*, techno-humanism is the more conservative of the two emerging twenty-first-century techno-religions: it still "sees humans as the apex of creation and clings to many traditional humanist values," yet concedes that *Homo sapiens* has run its course and so turns to genetic engineering, nanotechnology, and brain–computer interfaces to upgrade us into [[Homo Deus]] — a creed that deifies the human will while building the very technology that would engineer the will itself.

## Longer Explanation

Harari sets techno-humanism inside a larger argument that "the new religions are unlikely to emerge from the caves of Afghanistan or from the madrasas of the Middle East" but "from research laboratories," and that the "most interesting place in the world from a religious perspective" is "not the Islamic State or the Bible Belt, but Silicon Valley." These "new techno-religions can be divided into two main types: techno-humanism and data religion." Where data religion ([[Dataism]]) holds that "humans have completed their cosmic task" and "should now pass the torch on to entirely new kinds of entities," techno-humanism is "the more conservative creed," one that "still sees humans as the apex of creation and clings to many traditional humanist values" (Directly stated). Its defining concession is stated in one sentence: "Techno-humanism agrees that Homo sapiens as we know it has run its historical course and will no longer be relevant in the future, but concludes that we should therefore use technology in order to create Homo deus – a much superior human model" (Directly stated).

Genealogically, Harari reads techno-humanism as "an updated variant on the old dreams of evolutionary humanism, which already a century ago called for the creation of superhumans" (see [[Evolutionary Humanism]]) — but with a crucial change of means: "whereas Hitler and his ilk planned to create superhumans by means of selective breeding and ethnic cleansing, twenty-first-century techno-humanism hopes to reach the goal far more peacefully, with the help of genetic engineering, nanotechnology and brain–computer interfaces" (Directly stated). The mechanism Harari proposes is a "second cognitive revolution": just as "a few small changes in the Sapiens DNA, and a slight rewiring of the Sapiens brain" once "turned us into the rulers of the planet," so "a few additional changes to our genome and another rewiring of our brain" might "give Homo deus access to unimaginable new realms and turn us into the lords of the galaxy." The upgrade is framed as a survival imperative: "since intelligence is decoupling from consciousness, and since non-conscious intelligence is developing at breakneck speed, humans must actively upgrade their minds if they want to stay in the game" (Directly stated).

This rests on the humanist inheritance Harari develops in "The Humanist Revolution," where humanism "worships humanity," makes "our free will" the "highest authority of all," and issues the "number one humanist commandment – listen to yourself!" Techno-humanism inherits this sanctification of the will: "Like all humanist sects, techno-humanism too sanctifies the human will, seeing it as the nail on which the entire universe hangs" (Directly stated). It "expects our desires to choose which mental abilities to develop, and to thereby determine the shape of future minds." But this is exactly where the creed turns against itself.

Harari names two threats. The first is a *downgrade*: when "a practical ability to engineer minds" is mixed "with our ignorance of the mental spectrum and with the narrow interests of governments, armies and corporations," the system may upgrade only those faculties it needs — focus, fast decision-making — while neglecting empathy, doubt, the capacities to "smell, dream and pay attention." "We may successfully upgrade our bodies and our brains, while losing our minds in the process. Indeed, techno-humanism may end up downgrading humans" (Directly stated). The second, deeper threat is the collapse of *authenticity*. Humanism's commandment to listen to one's authentic inner voice presupposes that there is one. But "Technological progress has a very different agenda. It doesn't want to listen to our inner voices. It wants to control them" — once we understand "the biochemical system producing all these voices, we can play with the switches, turn up the volume here, lower it there." The result: "As we learn to turn our inner volume up and down, we give up our belief in authenticity, because it is no longer clear whose hand is on the switch" (Directly stated). "Once people could design and redesign their will, we could no longer see it as the ultimate source of all meaning and authority. For no matter what our will says, we can always make it say something else."

This is the self-undermining dilemma at the heart of the concept (Interpretive synthesis, closely following Harari's own framing). Harari states it as "an impossible dilemma": techno-humanism "considers the human will to be the most important thing in the universe, hence it pushes humankind to develop technologies that can control and redesign our will… Yet once we have such control, techno-humanism would not know what to do with it, because the sacred human will would become just another designer product" (Directly stated). The creed that deifies the will builds the technology that turns the will into a product — and so dissolves the very ground it stands on. Harari's exit from the dilemma is to push past techno-humanism altogether: "a bolder techno-religion seeks to sever the humanist umbilical cord altogether," replacing "the desires and experiences of any humanlike beings" with [[Dataism]], which "worships data."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — coins/uses "techno-humanism" as one of two "new techno-religions" in *Homo Deus*. It is the *conservative* one: it preserves human supremacy and humanist values, concedes *Homo sapiens* is obsolete, and engineers [[Homo Deus]] via genetic engineering, nanotechnology, and brain–computer interfaces. Its genealogy is [[Evolutionary Humanism]] pursued peacefully; its fatal flaw is the will-engineering paradox.

## Related Concepts

- [[Evolutionary Humanism]] — techno-humanism is "an updated variant on the old dreams of evolutionary humanism," seeking superhumans by technology rather than by "selective breeding and ethnic cleansing." This is the direct lineage Harari draws.
- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — techno-humanism is a "humanist sect"; it inherits humanism's worship of the human and the commandment to "listen to yourself."
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — "liberal humanism makes way for techno-humanism" as medicine shifts from "healing the sick" to "upgrading the healthy"; techno-humanism is the successor creed once liberalism's sanctified will meets the technology to redesign it.
- [[Homo Deus]] — the product techno-humanism aims to create: "a much superior human model."
- [[Mind Uploading]] — adjacent upgrade-fantasy; techno-humanism's "second cognitive revolution" and mind-manufacturing share the premise that minds are engineerable substrates.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Dataism]] — the *other* techno-religion in Harari's pair and its radical successor: where techno-humanism keeps humans "the apex of creation," data religion concludes humans "should now pass the torch on to entirely new kinds of entities" and "worships data." Harari positions Dataism as the bolder creed that "sever[s] the humanist umbilical cord altogether."
- [[Transhumanism]] — **distinct concept, do not merge.** [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]'s transhumanism is the organized futurist movement read as secularized *Christian eschatology* (Huxley/Teilhard → Kurzweil's Singularity), a religious double of disembodiment. Harari's techno-humanism is genealogically other: it descends from *evolutionary humanism*, keeps the human as apex, and is defined by the will-engineering paradox rather than by resurrection/immortality eschatology. They name overlapping technologies (genetic engineering, brain–computer interfaces, upgrading the human) but have different sources and different internal logics. Cross-reference, do not collapse the names. (Interpretive synthesis)

## Questions It Raises

- If a creed must "sanctify the human will" to motivate building the technology that redesigns the will, is its self-undermining dilemma intrinsic — or could a techno-humanism survive that did not treat the will as "the nail on which the entire universe hangs"? (ties to [[Free Will vs Determinism]])
- When "it is no longer clear whose hand is on the switch," does authenticity name anything real, or was the "authentic self" always a humanist fiction that engineering merely exposes?
- Is techno-humanism's promised *upgrade* separable from the *downgrade* Harari warns of — can a system that "usually rewards us for the decisions we make rather than for our doubts" enhance the mind without amputating empathy, doubt, and attention?
- If techno-humanism and [[Dataism]] are the two exits from liberal humanism, is there any third path that keeps the human as a source of meaning without either deifying or dissolving the will?
- Does techno-humanism's shared toolkit with [[Transhumanism]] (genetic engineering, brain–computer interfaces) make the genealogical distinction (evolutionary humanism vs Christian eschatology) merely scholarly — or does the difference in *source* change what each movement will do with the same tools?
