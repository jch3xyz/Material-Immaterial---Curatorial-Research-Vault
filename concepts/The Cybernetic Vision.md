# The Cybernetic Vision

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's *l'esprit cybernétique* — cybernetics taken not as a method, an illustration, or robot art but as a "spirit," a "stance," a fundamental attitude that may inform art and in turn be enriched by it, grounded in "the perfectibility of systems" and "the perfectibility of man."

## Longer Explanation

"The Cybernetic Vision" is the title term of Ascott's foundational two-part manifesto "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" (*Cybernetica* 9 and 10, 1966–67). Ascott defines it first by what it is *not*: "By this term, I neither mean 'the art of cybernetics' nor refer to an art concerned to illustrate cybernetics, nor yet an art embodying cybernetic machines or robot art" (`Directly stated`). What he means instead is *l'esprit cybernétique* — "the spirit of cybernetics, which may inform art and in turn be enriched by it." The cybernetic vision is therefore prior to, and broader than, any technique: "the cybernetic vision in art, which will unify art with a cybernated society, is a matter of 'stance,' a fundamental attitude to events and human relationships, before it is in any sense a technical or procedural matter" (`Directly stated`). Art can still be examined cybernetically and can "derive technical and theoretical support from this science— as in the past it has done from optics or geometry," but for Ascott that descriptive use is secondary; the vision is an orientation of mind, not a toolkit.

The content of that orientation is a double perfectibility. "Fundamentally, cybernetics concerns the idea of the perfectibility of systems; it is concerned in practice with the procurement of effective action by means of self-organising systems. It recognises the idea of the perfectibility of man, of the possibility of further evolution in the biological and social sphere" (`Directly stated`). The cybernetic vision is thus optimistic and developmental: it reads both the artwork and the human being as open, self-organising, evolving systems rather than fixed objects or finished natures. This is why, for Ascott, cybernetics is "before it is a method or an applied science... a field of knowledge that shapes our philosophy, influences our behaviour, and extends our thought" — a worldview, not an instrument.

Because it is a stance, the cybernetic vision persists across Ascott's whole career even as its media change. In the table-top text he restates it as a settled disposition: "Our worldview is holistic and integrative. Our vision is cybernetic. We are no longer locked into the moment, we resist the partial view" (`Directly stated`) — the cybernetic vision now naming an integrative, anti-partial way of attending to "the rich interconnections of events." In "The Psibernetic Arch" (1970) he names his own art-historical contribution in the same register: "I have myself contributed the idea of a general behavioural quality in twentieth-century art that admits of cybernetic analysis" (`Directly stated`), and polices the boundary against the "stance"-less misreading — the "large-scale exhibitions of what is effectively cybernetic camp—creaking hardware and antique concepts—simply out to capture public attention at a trivial level." Cybernetic camp is precisely what the cybernetic vision is *not*: the hardware without the spirit. By the telematic essays of the 1980s the same vision underwrites the network: "Telematics has arisen from an ethos of cross-disciplinary science and is set within a cybernetic perspective of the world" (`Directly stated`). The cybernetic vision is the through-line from his 1960s behaviourist art to his telematic and technoetic programs.

The cybernetic vision is the *attitude* of which [[Behaviourist Art]] is the practice and the [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] the proposed institution: the participatory, feedback-driven art that completes itself in the spectator is what the vision looks like when it is made. It is kept DISTINCT from Jack Burnham's [[Systems Consciousness]] — Burnham (1968) names a *perceptual* refocusing from the art object's "skin" to energy-and-information flows, framed as a biological survival mechanism for sculpture; Ascott (1966–67) names an *attitudinal* stance, a spirit of double perfectibility that precedes any object or technique (`Interpretive synthesis`). The two are the complementary poles of 1960s cybernetic art — Burnham's object→system consciousness and Ascott's behaviour→system vision — and are cross-linked but not merged. It is likewise distinct from the broader cross-author [[Cybernetics]] note: that note tracks cybernetics as a science and field across Wiener, Hayles, Haraway, McLuhan, and Burnham, whereas "The Cybernetic Vision" is specifically Ascott's reframing of it as an artistic stance.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — coins the term in "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" (1966–67) as *l'esprit cybernétique*: not the art of cybernetics, not illustration, not robot art, but a "spirit" / "stance" / "fundamental attitude" informing art, grounded in "the perfectibility of systems" and "the perfectibility of man." Restated as a settled "holistic and integrative" worldview in the table-top text, re-anchored against "cybernetic camp" in "The Psibernetic Arch" (1970), and carried forward as the "cybernetic perspective of the world" underlying [[Telematic Art]] in the 1980s.

## Related Concepts

- [[Cybernetics]] — the science and field whose "spirit" the cybernetic vision distils; Ascott explicitly subordinates cybernetics-as-method to cybernetics-as-stance.
- [[Behaviourist Art]] — the practice the vision animates; behaviourist art is the cybernetic vision realized as participatory, feedback-driven work.
- [[Systems Esthetics]] — Burnham's object→system art program; Ascott's vision is its participatory twin, the behaviour→system pole of 1960s cybernetic art.
- [[New Media Art]] — the cybernetic vision is a foundational stance in the genealogy this note tracks; Ascott is the figure the Lippard ingest deferred this stub "until."
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the vision's commitment to the spectator-completed, feedback artifact is paradigmatically experiential.
- [[Reciprocal Communication]] — the two-way feedback the vision treats as "the content of art."

- [[Psibernetics]] — the 1970 extension of the vision across the cyb/psi "arch"; a later coinage in the same lineage.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — the cybernetic vision is defined against object-fixation: art "has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour," and aesthetic value migrates from the styling of objects to the design of systems.
- [[Systems Consciousness]] — NOT an opposition but a deliberately maintained distinction: Burnham's perceptual refocusing (the object's "skin" → energy/information flows, as survival mechanism) vs Ascott's attitudinal "stance" of double perfectibility. Cross-linked, not merged.

## Questions It Raises

- If the cybernetic vision is a "stance" before it is a technique, can an artwork be cybernetic in spirit while using no cybernetic hardware at all — and conversely, is "cybernetic camp" (hardware without the stance) simply not cybernetic art?
- Ascott grounds the vision in "the perfectibility of man." How does that mid-century optimism read beside the vault's later suspicion of perfectibility-talk — the surveillance and control anxieties of Han and Harari, or O'Gieblyn's transhumanist critique?
- Is Ascott's behaviour→system "vision" genuinely distinct from Burnham's object→system "consciousness," or are they two names for one 1960s reorientation that the vault keeps separate only by chronology and discipline?
- Does the cybernetic vision survive intact into the telematic and technoetic work, or does the "cybernetic perspective of the world" become something else once the network and consciousness, not feedback, are its subject?
