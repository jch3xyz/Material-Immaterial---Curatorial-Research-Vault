# Capitalism as Religion (Harari)

## Brief Definition

For [[Yuval Noah Harari]], free-market capitalism is a religion rather than a science: by issuing ethical verdicts — growth before family bonds, before social equality, before ecological harmony — under cover of factual, scientific-sounding statements, "free-market capitalism has crossed the border from the land of science to that of religion."

## Longer Explanation

Harari's claim turns on a category distinction between *factual statements* and *ethical judgements*. Economics presents itself as a positive science describing how production, credit and growth actually work. But the moment capitalism answers a question of value — *is economic growth more important than family bonds?* — it leaves the descriptive register entirely. He stages this with the case of the software engineer whose elderly father has a stroke: she can care for him herself (her income and the start-up's productivity suffer) or hire a low-wage carer from the other side of the world (business as usual). "Free-market capitalism has a firm answer. If economic growth demands that we loosen family bonds, encourage people to live away from their parents, and import carers from the other side of the world – so be it." Harari's diagnosis: "This answer, however, involves an ethical judgement rather than a factual statement... By daring to make such ethical judgements, free-market capitalism has crossed the border from the land of science to that of religion." (`Directly stated`)

The deeper object of the argument is the belief in *economic growth* itself, which Harari treats as the shared creed of otherwise opposed ideologies — Soviet five-year planners and American robber barons, Hindu revivalists and Chinese communists, all meeting at "the same bottleneck of economic growth." Growth has acquired "almost religious status... throughout the world," and Harari is explicit that the religion-label is warranted by what growth-belief *does*: "it may not be wrong to call the belief in economic growth a religion, because it now purports to solve many if not most of our ethical dilemmas." A religion, on this reading, is identified functionally — by its claim to adjudicate ethical dilemmas — and growth-capitalism qualifies because "since economic growth is allegedly the source of all good things, it encourages people to bury their ethical disagreements and adopt whichever course of action maximises long-term growth." (`Directly stated`)

Harari then renders the creed in openly theological vocabulary: capitalism has a "supreme value" (growth), a "number one commandment" ("thou shalt invest thy profits in increasing growth"), "devout capitalists," an "ardent capitalist faith in growth," and even an eschatology of "miracles here on earth" that competes with the old religions' "pie in the sky." The "credo of 'more stuff'" urges individuals, firms and governments "to discount anything that might hamper economic growth, such as preserving social equality, ensuring ecological harmony or honouring your parents." Modernity, for its part, "upholds growth as a supreme value for whose sake we should make every sacrifice and risk every danger," and teaches us "to fear equilibrium as if it were the Devil." The religious register is not decoration: it is the argument's claim that growth-capitalism functions as a value-system masquerading as a value-neutral science. (`Directly stated`)

Notably, Harari does not treat "religion" pejoratively here. "Most capitalists would probably dislike the title of religion, but as religions go, capitalism can at least hold its head high" — it has, on his account, actually overcome famine, plague and war, where "for thousands of years priests, rabbis and muftis explained that humans cannot overcome" them. The point is diagnostic, not dismissive: capitalism earns the title *religion* by what it does (adjudicate ethics, demand faith, exact sacrifice), and this is what makes it dangerous in the domain — ecology — where "growth true-believers do not just hope for miracles – they take it for granted that the miracles will happen." (`Strongly implied` for the non-pejorative reading; `Directly stated` for the quoted material.)

This concept is deliberately kept **distinct from [[Capital as Religion]]** (Han, via Walter Benjamin and Robert Kurz). The two share a thesis-name but make different arguments. Han's "Capital as Religion" is *Benjamin's cultic thesis*: capitalism as "the first case of a cult that creates guilt, not atonement," a religion of pure, irredeemable debt (*Schuld* = debt/guilt) in which "the state of unfreedom perpetuates itself." Harari's "Capitalism as Religion" is an *epistemological boundary-crossing*: capitalism is a religion because it smuggles ethical verdicts into the form of scientific fact — a claim about the is/ought confusion at the heart of economics, not about debt, guilt or atonement. The `(Harari)` suffix marks this register; the bare-titled Han note marks Benjamin's cult of debt. (`Interpretive synthesis` for the distinction.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (*Homo Deus*) — free-market capitalism, and more precisely the belief in economic growth, is a religion because it crosses "from the land of science to that of religion" by issuing ethical verdicts (growth over family bonds, equality, ecology) disguised as factual statements. Theological vocabulary throughout: supreme value, commandment, faith, devout capitalists, earthly miracles. The label is functional and not pejorative.
- **Distinct from [[Capital as Religion]]** ([[Byung-Chul Han]] via Walter Benjamin / Robert Kurz, *Psychopolitics*) — there, capitalism is a *cult that creates guilt, not atonement*: a religion of perpetual debt and guilt (*Schuld*) that perpetuates unfreedom. Same thesis-name, different argument — Han's is a theology of debt; Harari's is a critique of the is/ought boundary. Cross-linked by intent; the `(Harari)` suffix preserves the distinction.

## Related Concepts

- [[Capital as Religion]] — the Han/Benjamin namesake; shares the thesis-name "capitalism is a religion" but argues a religion *of debt and guilt*, not a *science-to-religion boundary crossing*. Cross-link and contrast.
- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — for Harari, the *other* modern religion that "sneaks meaning back" after capitalism delivers power; the modern deal is power (capitalism/growth) exchanged for meaning (humanism).
- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — the sect whose sanctified free individual ("the customer is always right") is the moral subject capitalism's growth-creed both serves and overrides.
- [[Dataism]] — the emerging techno-religion that, in Harari's arc, may succeed both growth-capitalism and humanism; another belief-system framed in explicitly religious terms.
- [[Disenchantment]] — capitalism-as-religion is a counter-case to a purely disenchanted modernity: science does not banish religion so much as modernity re-enchants the economy with a creed of growth.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Capital as Religion]] — listed under both related and opposing by design: it is the *contrast case* with the same name. The opposition is methodological (boundary-crossing of science/religion vs. cult of guilt and debt), not a clash of valuations.

## Questions It Raises

- If a religion is identified *functionally* — by its claim to "solve... our ethical dilemmas" — then is every value-laden science (not only economics) a crypto-religion? Where exactly does Harari draw the science/religion border, and is it stable?
- Harari and Han both call capitalism a religion but mean different things. Is "capitalism is a religion" a single insight refracted, or two unrelated arguments that happen to share a slogan — and does the curatorial split into two notes clarify or merely formalise the ambiguity?
- Harari frames the religion-label as non-pejorative ("capitalism can at least hold its head high"). Does this neutrality hold once the argument reaches ecology, where "growth true-believers" treat planetary survival as a matter of faith in coming miracles?
- If capitalism's verdicts are ethical, not factual, what would a *consciously religious* economics look like — one that owned its value-commitments instead of disguising them as science?
