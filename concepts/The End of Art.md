# The End of Art

## Brief Definition

Guy Debord's thesis that art's modern "declaration of independence is the beginning of its end" — that once art detaches from the common language of a myth-based community and becomes autonomous individual production, it enters a terminal decomposition (Baroque → Romanticism → Cubism → Dada → Surrealism) that issues either in art's self-transcendence within total history or in its preservation "as a dead object for spectacular contemplation" in "the age of museums."

## Longer Explanation

For Debord the end of art is not an event but a long internal law of separated culture working itself out. Culture, "the general sphere of knowledge and of representations of lived experiences," detached itself from "the unity of myth-based society" and "embarked on an imperialistic career of self-enrichment that ultimately led to the decline of that independence" (th. 180). Art, as the sensory branch of that culture, follows the same arc: "When art, which was the common language of social inaction, develops into independent art in the modern sense, emerging from its original religious universe and becoming individual production of separate works, it too becomes subject to the movement governing the history of all separate culture. Its declaration of independence is the beginning of its end" (th. 186). Autonomy is thus the very condition of dissolution — the moment art gains its own sphere it also begins to negate that sphere. `Directly stated`.

The end takes, crucially, **two opposing forms**. "The end of the history of culture manifests itself in two opposing forms: the project of culture's self-transcendence within total history, and its preservation as a dead object for spectacular contemplation. The first tendency has linked its fate to social critique, the second to the defense of class power" (th. 184). One end abolishes art by realizing it — carrying its lost "community of dialogue" over into lived praxis; the other end freezes art into an object of `Spectacle`, consumed rather than lived. This is why the concept is not simply nihilistic: for Debord "the abolition and realization of art are inseparable aspects of a single transcendence of art" (th. 191), so the "end" names both a death and a possible fulfillment. `Directly stated`.

Debord narrates the genealogy through art history proper. "The historical time that invaded art was manifested first of all in the sphere of art itself, beginning with the Baroque" — the art of "a world that had lost its center." "What eventually followed Baroque… was an ever more individualistic art of negation which, from Romanticism to Cubism, continually renewed its assaults until it had fragmented and destroyed the entire artistic sphere" (th. 189). Dada and Surrealism close the sequence: "Dadaism sought to abolish art without realizing it; Surrealism sought to realize art without abolishing it" (th. 191) — two one-sided halves of the single transcendence the Situationists claim to complete. The art of this dissolution is "at once an art of change and the purest expression of the impossibility of change… Its vanguard is its own disappearance" (th. 190). `Directly stated`.

The terminal condition is **the age of museums** — the concept's associated symbol/alias. When "all the art of the past comes to be recognized and appreciated historically, and is retrospectively reclassified as phases of a single 'world art,'" it forms "a sort of baroque structure at a higher level, a structure that absorbs Baroque art itself along with all its possible revivals" (th. 189). Past art is no longer produced but *consumed together*, wholesale: "In this age of museums in which artistic communication is no longer possible, all the previous ex pressions of art can be accepted equally" (th. 189) — accepted equally precisely because none of it communicates any longer. The museum is thus the spectacular form of art's end: culture preserved "in congealed form," the "communication of the incommunicable" (th. 192), where even art's negative gestures (Dada, modern poetry) are recuperated as "modernistic pseudo-innovations." Debord's melancholy formula gathers the whole diagnosis: "When art becomes independent and paints its world in dazzling colors, a moment of life has grown old… The greatness of art only emerges at the dusk of life" (th. 188). `Directly stated`.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — *The Society of the Spectacle* (theses 184–192). The end of art is the sensory sub-case of "the end of the history of culture." Art's autonomy is self-cancelling; its history is "a march toward culture's self abolition" (th. 180). The end bifurcates into self-transcendence (linked to social critique, `Recuperation`'s opposite) versus preservation as a dead object for the `Spectacle` (linked to the defense of class power). "The age of museums" is its consummated symbolic form — all past art consumed together as "a baroque structure at a higher level." `Directly stated`.
- **Ancestry to later art-abolition attempts** — Debord's "abolition and realization of art are inseparable" (th. 191) is the Situationist precursor to the postwar attempt to dissolve the art object itself: `Dematerialization` and `Conceptual Art` inherit the ambition to abolish art-as-object, though Lippard concludes dematerialization "failed to escape the market" — a fate Debord would read as `Recuperation` by the `Spectacle`. `Interpretive synthesis`.

## Related Concepts

- [[Spectacle]] — the second form of the end: art "preserved as a dead object for spectacular contemplation"; the museum age is the spectacle's cultural sector, "the communication of the incommunicable" (th. 192).
- [[Recuperation]] — the mechanism by which even art's negations ("co-opted rehashes of its negative manifestations," th. 192) are absorbed and neutralized as pseudo-innovation; the spectacular half of art's end.
- [[Reification]] — culture "congealed" / "in congealed form" (th. 192): past art turned from lived common language into a dead, contemplatable thing.
- [[Dematerialization]] — a later attempt to end art-as-object; Debord's genealogical ancestor via the "abolition of art" pole.
- [[Conceptual Art]] — the "neoliterature that baldly admits that it does nothing but contemplate the written word for its own sake" (th. 192) prefigures, and is arguably indicted in advance by, conceptualism's self-referential turn.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Recuperation]] — the two forms of the end **oppose** each other: self-transcendence within total history (art abolished-and-realized in lived praxis) versus recuperative preservation for spectacular contemplation. The concept's internal opposition, not an external one (th. 184).

## Questions It Raises

- If art's "declaration of independence is the beginning of its end," is aesthetic autonomy itself the disease, such that any avant-garde defense of art's separateness only accelerates the end it protests?
- Debord holds that abolition and realization of art are "inseparable aspects of a single transcendence" — but does any actual practice achieve both, or does every attempt collapse into one pole (Dada's abolition-without-realization, Surrealism's realization-without-abolition, dematerialization's re-absorption by the market)?
- Is the "age of museums" a terminal condition or a metastable one — can the wholesale historical consumption of all past art ("world art") ever be more than the spectacle's "official amnesia," or does it foreclose new artistic communication permanently?
- Does later conceptual and dematerialized art fulfill Debord's abolition of the object, or does it merely furnish the spectacle with "modernistic pseudo-innovations" — the very outcome th. 192 predicts?
