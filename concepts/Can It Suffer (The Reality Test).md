# Can It Suffer (The Reality Test)

## Brief Definition

Harari's one-question test for telling a real entity from a fiction: an entity is real if and only if it can suffer — "just ask yourself, 'Can it suffer?'"

## Longer Explanation

In "The Storytellers," Harari distinguishes a triple-layered Sapiens reality of objective things (trees, rivers), subjective experiences (fear, joy, desire), and a third layer of intersubjective fictions (money, gods, nations, corporations). Because that third layer can grow so powerful that it dominates the other two — "Jesus Christ, the French Republic and Apple Inc. have dammed and harnessed the rivers" — he proposes a portable criterion for keeping the layers straight. The reality test is that criterion: *Can it suffer?* When the temple of Zeus is burned, Zeus does not suffer; when the euro loses value, the euro does not suffer; when a bank goes bankrupt or a country loses a war, neither really suffers — these are metaphors. By contrast, the wounded soldier, the famished peasant, and the cow separated from her calf genuinely suffer. That capacity, for Harari, marks the line between fiction and reality. (`Directly stated`)

The test is an *ethical fulcrum*, not merely an ontological one. Harari is emphatic that fiction "isn't bad. It is vital" — no complex society can function without commonly accepted stories about money, states, and corporations, just as football requires shared made-up rules. The danger is category error: when "we forget that they are mere fiction, we lose touch with reality," and the tools become goals. Stories "are just tools. They should not become our goals or our yardsticks." The reality test is what allows one to "stop from time to time and look at things from the perspective of some real entity" — to refuse to judge a human network only by its own self-invented yardsticks (tax revenue, exam marks, divine commandments). (`Directly stated`)

Crucially, the suffering is real even when its cause is fictional. National and religious myths can trigger wars "in which millions lose their homes, their limbs and even their lives": "The cause of war is fictional, but the suffering is 100 per cent real." This is precisely why, for Harari, "we should strive to distinguish fiction from reality" — the suffering that fictions produce is the standing reason never to let a fiction become an end in itself. (`Directly stated`)

Read against the vault's organizing axis, the test maps the immaterial (fictions that cannot suffer) against the material register of embodied, suffering creatures (which can) — a parallel to the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension, where the immaterial story is real in its effects yet incapable of bearing the suffering it can cause. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — frames suffering as the single diagnostic of the real, deployed as a check on intersubjective fictions; the test is both a metaphysical criterion (what exists) and an ethical one (what must never be sacrificed to a story).

## Related Concepts

- [[Intersubjective Reality]] — the class of fictions (money, gods, nations, corporations) that the test is designed to detect; they are real *in their effects* but cannot themselves suffer.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the suffering body is the material pole; the fiction that cannot suffer is the immaterial pole.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Intersubjective Reality]] — the conceptual opposite of "real entity" in Harari's sense: an intersubjective fiction exists only in shared imagination and fails the suffering test, even as it shapes the material world.

## Questions It Raises

- If a fiction's effects are "100 per cent real," in what sense is the fiction itself unreal — and does the suffering test smuggle a moral hierarchy into an ontological claim?
- Where is the boundary of "can it suffer?" — does it track sentience, embodiment, or something else, and how does it adjudicate borderline cases (ecosystems, future persons, artificial agents)?
- For a curatorial practice working with immaterial and dematerialized objects, does the test imply that only embodied stakes carry ethical weight — or can an artwork's fiction be answerable to real suffering it touches?
- If human networks "judge themselves by yardsticks of their own invention," can the suffering test ever be applied from inside the fiction, or does it require a standpoint the fiction itself cannot supply?
