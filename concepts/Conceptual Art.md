# Conceptual Art

## Brief Definition

The art movement, chronicled most fully in Lucy Lippard's *Six Years*, in which the idea or concept supersedes the visual and material result — "idea art" predicated on the proposition that ideas can be art and that the material form is secondary, ephemeral, or "dematerialized." Its central inversion — idea paramount, object demoted — has parallels in the sculptural (object→system) and cybernetic (form→behaviour) turns charted elsewhere in the corpus.

> Note: this is the vault's first owner of the term "Conceptual Art," anchored entirely in Lippard's usage. If a broader cross-author treatment (e.g. via [[Joseph Kosuth]]'s own writings or later art-theoretical sources) later enters the vault, this note may need qualification.

## Longer Explanation

Conceptual art is defined by a single inversion of priority: the idea is paramount and the material object is demoted. Lippard's working definition is explicit — work "in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized'" (`Directly stated`, Lippard). The concept is not illustrated *by* an object so much as it *is* the work; the object, where it survives at all, is residue. This is why the movement is inseparable from [[Dematerialization]] — Lippard's name for the de-emphasis on the material aspects (uniqueness, permanence) that Conceptual art enacts. The two terms name the same phenomenon from different angles: Conceptual art is the genre, dematerialization the process.

The definitional core is sharpened by the artists Lippard quotes, who push the inversion from inside the practice. **[[Sol LeWitt]]** supplies its programmatic formula: in concept art "the idea or concept is the most important aspect of the work," and once the planning is done "the execution is a perfunctory affair. The idea becomes a machine that makes the art" (`Directly stated`, LeWitt) — the work relocated from execution to conception. **[[Joseph Kosuth]]** drives the inversion to its near-tautological limit: "All I make are models. The actual works of art are ideas" (`Directly stated`, Kosuth), the fabricated object reduced to a visual approximation of an art object held in the mind. Lippard also preserves LeWitt's own scrupulous distinction, which she adopts: conceptual art "with a small c" (his own work, where conventional material forms are generated by a paramount idea) versus Conceptual art "with a capital C" (the broader, more thoroughly dematerialized scene this book documents). The capital-C usage is the canonical referent of this note.

Around this core orbit a set of satellites that recur throughout *Six Years*: **the document** (the xerox sheet, photograph, or typed statement that stands in for an event "past or never directly perceived"); **art-as-information** (information "could be interesting in its own right and need not be visual," the genre's culminating institutional moment being Kynaston McShine's 1970 "Information" show at MoMA — see [[Information]]); **systems aesthetics** (**Hans Haacke**'s premise "to think in terms of systems: the production of systems, the interference with and the exposure of existing systems" — see [[Systems Theory]]); and **the catalogue-as-medium** (**Seth Siegelaub**'s strategy of publications "that were art rather than merely about art," and Lippard's own index-card catalogues). These are not incidental: they are the forms through which an art that refuses the object must instead circulate. Lippard's own verdict is deliberately dual. On one side, economic and political *failure*: the hope of escaping "the tyranny of a commodity status" was "for the most part unfounded," and "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries" (`Directly stated`, Lippard). On the other, a real *aesthetic contribution*: "An informational, documentary idiom has provided a vehicle for art ideas that were encumbered and obscured by formal considerations" (`Directly stated`, Lippard). The genre failed to decommodify art but succeeded in opening an informational, documentary register that continues to be useful.

The same inversion is reached, near-contemporaneously, from outside Lippard's circle. **[[Jack Burnham]]** tracks the sculptural counterpart: "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor" (`Directly stated`, Burnham), a relocation of the artwork from physical presence to information that pre-dates Lippard's vocabulary and names sculpture's "steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality." His object→system axis is a sibling of Lippard's idea→document axis (see [[Object and System]]). **[[Roy Ascott]]** reaches it from the cybernetic and participatory side, where the inversion is behaviour-over-form rather than idea-over-material: his "field theory that would replace the formalist modernist aesthetic" takes "as its focus not form but behaviour" (`Directly stated`, Ascott), making the artwork "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer)" where "the final responsibility for meaning lies with the viewer" (`Directly stated`, Ascott), and "when art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware… Process replaces product… system supersedes structure" (`Directly stated`, Ascott). Ascott's editor Edward A. Shanken reads telematic art as "an extension of conceptual art" and notes that Lippard quotes Ascott on the dedication page of *Six Years*. Across all four registers — idea, sculptural system, document, behaviour — the common move is the same: the burden of the work migrates off the durable object onto the relations, information, or awareness it organizes, a deliberate tilt toward the immaterial pole of [[Material vs Immaterial]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Lucy Lippard]]** — the historian-participant who names and bounds the movement. Her "for me" definition foregrounds idea over material and ties Conceptual art to dematerialization, decommodification, and an "alternative" distribution network; she insists the book documents "widely differing phenomena within a time span, not… a 'movement'," so her "Conceptual art" is a narrower aesthetic-intention category carved out of a chaotic field.
- **[[Sol LeWitt]]** — supplies the small-c / capital-C distinction and the foundational formula "The idea becomes a machine that makes the art," locating the work in conception rather than execution.
- **[[Joseph Kosuth]]** — the strong, near-tautological pole: "the actual works of art are ideas," the physical object reduced to a model held in the mind.
- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — the sculptural counterpart, reached through the object→system distinction: "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor." His diagnosis of sculpture's gravitation toward "seeming immateriality" anticipates Lippard's dematerialization from the systems side (see [[Object and System]]).
- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the cybernetic, participatory pole: his "field theory" demotes form in favour of behaviour and relocates the work into the idea-generating participator. Shanken positions telematic art as an extension of conceptual art (Lippard quotes Ascott on the dedication page of *Six Years*); his object→behaviour inversion converges with Lippard's idea-over-material one.

## Related Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — the process that defines the genre; Conceptual art is its art-historical instance.
- [[Information]] — the documentary/informational idiom is the medium Conceptual art adopts once it refuses the object; "Information" is also the name of its signal 1970 exhibition.
- [[Systems Theory]] — "systems aesthetics" (Haacke, Burnham) is a major satellite; systems replace formal concerns of composition and physical presence.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Conceptual art is a deliberate tilt toward the immaterial pole; the document and the idea displace the durable object.
- [[New Media Art]] — the "inexpensive, ephemeral, unintimidating" Conceptual mediums (video, performance, photography, narrative, text, actions) prefigure later media-based practice. (Research-foci stub.)
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — Lippard's interest in restructuring perception and in "remarking direct experience" connects the genre to art as an apparatus for producing experience. (Research-foci stub.)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — a Hayles-derived counter-pressure: where Conceptual art (and Lippard's "dematerialization") wagers that the idea can shed its body, this insists information is never disembodied. Lippard herself concedes the term is "inaccurate" — "a piece of paper or a photograph is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead" — making the opposition internal to her own usage.

## Questions It Raises

- If "the actual works of art are ideas," what exactly is dematerialized — the art object, or only our attention to it? Lippard's own concession (paper is as material as lead) suggests Conceptual art renames materiality rather than escaping it; how does this bear on [[Material vs Immaterial]]?
- Conceptual art's satellites — the document, art-as-information, the catalogue-as-medium — are the same forms later claimed by [[New Media Art]]. Is Conceptual art the prehistory of media art, or a distinct refusal of the medium altogether?
- Lippard frames Conceptual art as restructuring perception and "remarking direct experience" rather than producing objects. Does this make it an early instance of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — art whose product is a state of awareness in the viewer?
- The Postface splits the verdict: aesthetic success, economic/political failure. Can an "informational, documentary idiom" carry critical or political force once it is reabsorbed as a luxury commodity, or does commodification neutralize the idea?
- If the body of the artist becomes the work (Acconci, Piper, Ukeles, the "living sculptures"), is the dematerialized object simply relocated *into the body* — and what does that do to the idea/material hierarchy the genre is built on?

## Jack Burnham (Beyond Modern Sculpture)

Jack Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) supplies the sculptural counterpart to the conceptual-art turn Lippard charts: where Conceptual art demotes the object in favor of the idea, Burnham tracks an analogous migration in sculpture from the fixed *object* to the interacting *system*. His formula is exact — "It becomes clear that with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor" (`Directly stated`, Burnham). The two diagnoses run parallel: Lippard's "idea is paramount and the material form is secondary" and Burnham's relocation of the artwork from physical presence to information are the same de-emphasis of the durable object, reached from the sculptural and the conceptual side respectively. See [[Object and System]] for Burnham's master object→system distinction, and [[Jack Burnham and Lucy Lippard]] for the cross-book bridge.

Burnham also pre-dates Lippard's vocabulary of "dematerialization." Already in the closing chapter of his book's Part I he names sculpture's "steady gravitation toward seeming immateriality (through forms of attenuated and unstable materiality), while at the same time resisting this trend" (`Directly stated`, Burnham). This is a systems/information orientation that anticipates the conceptual movement's refusal of the object: the burden of the work shifts from the made form toward the relations, energy, and information it organizes (`Strongly implied`). Burnham's object→system axis is thus a near-contemporaneous sibling of Lippard's idea→document axis — distinct theses (sculpture's immateriality vs. the conceptual-art document) that converge on the same waning of physical presence.

## Roy Ascott (Telematic Embrace)

Roy Ascott reaches the conceptual-art turn from the cybernetic and participatory side, and his editor Edward A. Shanken places him squarely in that lineage: Lippard "prominently quotes [Ascott's 'The Construction of Change'] on the dedication page of her seminal *Six Years*," and Shanken reads telematic art as "an extension of conceptual art" (`Directly stated`, Shanken, editorial introduction). Where Lippard's inversion is idea-over-material, Ascott's is behaviour-over-form. In "Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art" (1980) he proposes "a field theory that would replace the formalist modernist aesthetic. It takes as its focus not form but behaviour" (`Directly stated`, Ascott). The artwork is no longer a discrete object holding a fixed message but "a matrix between two sets of behaviours (the artist and the observer), providing for a field of psychic interplay that can be generative of multiple meanings, where the final responsibility for meaning lies with the viewer" (`Directly stated`, Ascott) — a relocation of the work into the idea-generating participator that runs parallel to Lippard's relocation of the work into the concept.

The convergence is sharpened by Ascott's 1967 manifesto line, that once "art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware in the creative sphere. Process replaces product in importance, just as system supersedes structure" (`Directly stated`, Ascott, quoted by Shanken). This is the same de-emphasis of the made object that drives Conceptual art's idea→document axis, here reframed as an object→behaviour axis — a sibling, on the [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Object and System]] spines, of both Lippard's dematerialization and Burnham's object→system. (Provenance note: ch. 04 is Shanken's editor's introduction *about* Ascott; the field-theory passages are from Ascott's own 1980 essay in ch. 13.)
