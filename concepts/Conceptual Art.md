# Conceptual Art

## Brief Definition

The movement chronicled in Lucy Lippard's *Six Years* in which the idea or concept supersedes the visual and material result — "idea art" predicated on the proposition that ideas can be art and that the material form is secondary, ephemeral, or "dematerialized."

> Note: this is the vault's first owner of the term "Conceptual Art," anchored entirely in Lippard's usage. If a broader cross-author treatment (e.g. via [[Joseph Kosuth]]'s own writings or later art-theoretical sources) later enters the vault, this note may need qualification.

## Longer Explanation

For Lippard, Conceptual art is defined by a single inversion of priority: the idea is paramount and the material object is demoted. Her working definition is explicit — work "in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized'" (`Directly stated`). The concept is not illustrated *by* an object so much as it *is* the work; the object, where it survives at all, is residue. This is why the movement is inseparable from [[Dematerialization]] — Lippard's name for the de-emphasis on the material aspects (uniqueness, permanence) that Conceptual art enacts. The two terms name the same phenomenon from different angles: Conceptual art is the genre, dematerialization the process.

The definitional core is sharpened by the artists Lippard quotes. Sol LeWitt's "Paragraphs on Conceptual Art" (1967) states that "the idea or concept is the most important aspect of the work" and that, once the planning is done, "the execution is a perfunctory affair. The idea becomes a machine that makes the art" (`Directly stated`, LeWitt). Joseph Kosuth pushes the inversion to its limit: "All I make are models. The actual works of art are ideas" (`Directly stated`, Kosuth) — the fabricated object reduced to a "visual approximation" of an art object held in the mind. Lippard also preserves LeWitt's own scrupulous distinction, which she adopts: conceptual art "with a small c" (his own work, where conventional material forms are generated by a paramount idea) versus Conceptual art "with a capital C" (the broader, more thoroughly dematerialized scene this book documents). The capital-C usage is the canonical referent of this note.

Around this definitional core orbit a set of satellites that recur throughout *Six Years*: **the document** (the xerox sheet, photograph, or typed statement that stands in for an event "past or never directly perceived"); **art-as-information** (Lippard reports that "information could be interesting in its own right and need not be visual," and the genre's culminating institutional moment was Kynaston McShine's 1970 "Information" show at MoMA — see [[Information]]); **systems aesthetics** (Hans Haacke's premise "to think in terms of systems: the production of systems, the interference with and the exposure of existing systems" — see [[Systems Theory]]); and **the catalogue-as-medium** (Seth Siegelaub's strategy of publications "that were art rather than merely about art," and Lippard's own index-card catalogues for the "557,087"/"955,000" exhibitions). These are not incidental: they are the forms through which an art that refuses the object must instead circulate.

Lippard's verdict on the movement is deliberately dual, delivered in the Postface. On one side, economic and political *failure*: the hope that an idea art would escape "the tyranny of a commodity status" was "for the most part unfounded," and "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries" (`Directly stated`). On the other, a real *aesthetic contribution*: "An informational, documentary idiom has provided a vehicle for art ideas that were encumbered and obscured by formal considerations" (`Directly stated`). The genre failed to decommodify art but succeeded in opening an informational, documentary register that continues to be useful. Holding both halves at once is essential to the concept as Lippard frames it.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Lucy R. Lippard]]** — the historian-participant who names and bounds the movement. Her "for me" definition foregrounds idea over material and ties Conceptual art to dematerialization, decommodification, and an "alternative" distribution network; she insists the book documents "widely differing phenomena within a time span, not... a 'movement'," so her "Conceptual art" is a narrower aesthetic-intention category carved out of a chaotic field.
- **[[Sol LeWitt]]** — supplies the small-c / capital-C distinction and the foundational formula "The idea becomes a machine that makes the art," locating the work in conception rather than execution.
- **[[Joseph Kosuth]]** — the strong, near-tautological pole: "the actual works of art are ideas," with the physical object reduced to a model. (Forward-stub.)
- **[[Seth Siegelaub]]** — the dealer-as-distributor whose catalogue-as-medium and publication-as-exhibition strategies are the genre's circulatory system. (Forward-stub.)
- **[[Hans Haacke]]** — exemplar of the systems/information strand and of the movement's political edge (the censored 1971 Guggenheim real-estate piece). (Forward-stub.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — the process that defines the genre; Conceptual art is its art-historical instance.
- [[Information]] — the documentary/informational idiom is the medium Conceptual art adopts once it refuses the object; "Information" is also the name of its signal 1970 exhibition.
- [[Systems Theory]] — "systems aesthetics" (Haacke, Burnham) is a major satellite; systems replace formal concerns of composition and physical presence.
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — Conceptual art is a deliberate tilt toward the immaterial pole; the document and the idea displace the durable object.
- [[New Media Art]] — the "inexpensive, ephemeral, unintimidating" Conceptual mediums (video, performance, photography, narrative, text, actions) prefigure later media-based practice. (Research-foci stub.)
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — Lippard's interest in restructuring perception and in "remarking direct experience" connects the genre to art as an apparatus for producing experience. (Research-foci stub.)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — a Hayles-derived counter-pressure: where Conceptual art (and Lippard's "dematerialization") wagers that the idea can shed its body, this insists information is never disembodied. Lippard herself concedes the term is "inaccurate" — "a piece of paper or a photograph is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead" — making the opposition internal to her own usage.

## Questions It Raises

- If "the actual works of art are ideas," what exactly is dematerialized — the art object, or only our attention to it? Lippard's own concession (paper is as material as lead) suggests Conceptual art renames materiality rather than escaping it; how does this bear on [[Material vs Immaterial]]?
- Conceptual art's satellites — the document, art-as-information, the catalogue-as-medium — are the same forms later claimed by [[New Media Art]]. Is Conceptual art the prehistory of media art, or a distinct refusal of the medium altogether?
- Lippard frames Conceptual art as restructuring perception and "remarking direct experience" rather than producing objects. Does this make it an early instance of [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — art whose product is a state of awareness in the viewer?
- The Postface splits the verdict: aesthetic success, economic/political failure. Can an "informational, documentary idiom" carry critical or political force once it is reabsorbed as a luxury commodity, or does commodification neutralize the idea?
- If the body of the artist becomes the work (Acconci, Piper, Ukeles, the "living sculptures"), is the dematerialized object simply relocated *into the body* — and what does that do to the idea/material hierarchy the genre is built on?
