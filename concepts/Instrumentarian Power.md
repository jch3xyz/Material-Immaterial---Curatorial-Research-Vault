# Instrumentarian Power

## Brief Definition

Instrumentarian power is the "new species of power" born of surveillance capitalism that, instead of working through armies or violence, knows and shapes human behavior toward others' ends through a ubiquitous computational architecture of "smart" networked devices.

> **Forward-anchor.** This note is *named and introduced* here; Zuboff develops it fully in Part III of *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (Chapters 12-18). The sections below establish the term, its core opposition to totalitarianism, and its origin in radical behaviorism, and are written to be **extended** by later chunks of the Zuboff ingest (its materialization in [[Big Other]], its expansion into society as "the human hive," and its "utopia of certainty"). Do not over-build.

## Longer Explanation

For Shoshana Zuboff, surveillance capitalism's competitive drive for ever-more-predictive behavioral data produces a reorientation "from knowledge to power": it is no longer enough to *know* our behavior, the goal becomes to *shape* it — "to automate us." This shift gives rise to what she names instrumentarianism. Instrumentarian power "knows and shapes human behavior toward others' ends," and "instead of armaments and armies, it works its will through the automated medium of an increasingly ubiquitous computational architecture of 'smart' networked devices, things, and spaces" (Directly stated, Ch. 1).

In Chapter 12 Zuboff gives the formal definition: instrumentarianism is "the instrumentation and instrumentalization of behavior for the purposes of modification, prediction, monetization, and control." She distinguishes its two halves with a puppet figure: *instrumentation* is "the puppet" — "the ubiquitous connected material architecture of sensate computation that renders, interprets, and actuates human experience" — while *instrumentalization* names "the social relations that orient the puppet masters" who wield the machines to "transform us into means to others' market ends." This is the conceptual link to the material apparatus Zuboff later names [[Big Other]] (Directly stated, Ch. 12-13).

The keystone of the concept is a contrast with totalitarianism. Zuboff argues that interpreting today's threats through Orwell's Big Brother — the "horseless-carriage syndrome" of attaching new peril to old facts — "impedes our understanding." Totalitarianism operated through violence, genocide, and the "engineering of the soul," demanding "possession of each person from the inside out." Instrumentarian power "moves differently and toward an opposite horizon": it "has no interest in our souls," is "profoundly and infinitely indifferent to our meanings and motives," and "operates through the means of behavioral modification." She compresses the difference into a formula: "Totalitarianism was a transformation of the state into a project of total possession. Instrumentarianism and its materialization in Big Other signal the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty" (Directly stated, Ch. 1, Ch. 12).

Zuboff traces the concept's intellectual origins not to political theory but to early-twentieth-century theoretical physics (Max Planck → Max Meyer) and, most notably, to the "radical behaviorism" of B. F. Skinner and his "dream of a 'technology of behavior.'" Skinner's behaviorism reduced the human to observable, modifiable action from "the viewpoint of the Other-One," held that "freedom is ignorance" awaiting conquest by measurement, and anticipated the unobtrusive observation and "nudge" architectures that surveillance capitalism would later realize. For Zuboff, instrumentarian power is the operational fulfillment of Skinner's program by other (market) means — it replaces "the engineering of souls with the engineering of behavior" (Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis, Ch. 12-13).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins the term to name surveillance capitalism's distinctive power. It is a *market* project of total certainty, materialized in [[Big Other]], whose endpoint is an "instrumentarian society" patterned on machine learning — a "human hive" where "group pressure and computational certainty replace politics and democracy." (To be extended in Part III.)
- *Radical behaviorism (B. F. Skinner, via Zuboff)* — the named intellectual antecedent: a "technology of behavior" enacted from "the viewpoint of the Other-One." Skinner is the historical source, not a co-theorist of the term; rendered in plain text pending any dedicated ingest.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the parent economic order; instrumentarian power is the power it births and the power that sustains it.
- [[Big Other]] — the material/computational apparatus in which instrumentarian power is materialized; the "puppet" of Zuboff's instrumentation/instrumentalization pair.
- [[Surveillance]] — the broader practice this power systematizes and monetizes; realized in the vault by this ingest.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] (Han) — a strong resonance: voluntary self-exposure and pervasive observation. Zuboff's instrumentarianism, however, is explicitly the "antithesis of Orwell's Big Brother," not its fulfillment — a productive tension with the panoptic frame.
- [[Dataism]] (Han / Harari) — the worldview that treats behavior as extractable data; instrumentarian power is its operationalization toward modification and control.
- [[Society of Control]] (Deleuze / Han) — modulation of conduct via distributed apparatus rather than enclosure; a precursor logic.

## Opposing Concepts

- Totalitarianism — Zuboff's central foil: state-as-total-possession via violence and the engineering of the soul, versus market-as-total-certainty via behavioral modification. The two are "precise opposites," not variants.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — instrumentarian power presupposes Skinner's denial of an autonomous, free-willing self ("freedom is ignorance"), dissolving the liberal subject it observes from "the viewpoint of the Other-One."

## Questions It Raises

- If instrumentarian power is the "antithesis" of Big Brother, why does so much critique still reach for Orwell — and what does the totalitarian lens obscure or reveal?
- Is "indifference to our meanings and motives" a weaker or a more total form of domination than the soul-engineering it replaces?
- Does grounding the concept in Skinner make it a *fulfillment* of behaviorism, or does the market motive (certainty for profit) change its nature?
- How does instrumentarian power relate to the disciplinary and control-society lineage (Foucault, Deleuze, Han) — successor, hybrid, or break?
