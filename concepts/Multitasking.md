# Multitasking

## Brief Definition

For Byung-Chul Han, multitasking is not a sign of civilizational advance but a *regression* to the divided, scanning vigilance of wild animals — a broad but flat mode of attention incapable of the contemplative immersion on which culture depends.

## Longer Explanation

Han treats multitasking as a symptom of the late-modern overload of stimuli that he traces to excessive [[Positivity]]. As he puts it, "Excessive positivity also expresses itself as an excess of stimuli, information, and impulses. It radically changes the structure and economy of attention." The mounting burden of work forces "particular dispositions toward time and attention," and the resulting habit — the "attitude toward time and environment known as 'multitasking'" — does not, in his account, "represent civilizational progress." This is the note's central claim: multitasking is a downgrade, not an upgrade. *(Directly stated)*

The argument turns on a startling inversion. Far from being a uniquely human, late-modern achievement, multitasking is, Han insists, "commonplace among wild animals." It is "an attentive technique indispensable for survival in the wilderness": the animal busy eating must simultaneously hold off rivals, watch that it is not itself eaten, guard its young, and keep an eye on its sexual partner. Because it "is forced to divide its attention between various activities," it can never settle into contemplation. Han's pointed conclusion — "That is why animals are incapable of contemplative immersion—either they are eating or they are copulating" — supplies the wild-animal symbol that this note carries. The animal "cannot immerse itself contemplatively in what it is facing [Gegenüber] because it must also process background events." *(Directly stated)*

Han then closes the loop on his own age: "Recent social developments and the structural change of wakefulness are bringing human society deeper and deeper into the wilderness." Multitasking, and kindred activities like video games, "produce a broad but flat mode of attention, which is similar to the vigilance of a wild animal." The cost is civilizational, since "[w]e owe the cultural achievements of humanity—which include philosophy—to deep, contemplative attention," and "[c]ulture presumes an environment in which deep attention is possible." In this regression, "[c]oncern for the good life... is yielding more and more to the simple concern for survival." *(Directly stated)*

Multitasking should be distinguished from the attention-*regime* it issues into. Where multitasking names the specific practice — dividing one task into background-monitoring fragments — Han uses [[Hyperattention]] for the scattered mode of awareness as such ("[a] rash change of focus between different tasks, sources of information, and processes"). The two cross-link tightly: multitasking is the survival technique whose generalization in humans displaces deep, contemplative attention with hyperattention. *(Strongly implied)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — multitasking is regression, not progress: a return to the divided vigilance of wild animals that forecloses contemplative immersion and drags late-modern society "deeper and deeper into the wilderness." It is downstream of the excess [[Positivity]] of the [[Achievement Society]] and feeds [[Acceleration]] without producing anything new ("[a] purely hectic rush... reproduces and accelerates what is already available").

## Related Concepts

- [[Hyperattention]] — the scattered attention-regime that multitasking generalizes into; the practice (multitasking) versus the resulting mode of awareness (hyperattention).
- [[Achievement Society]] — the late-modern society of work and information whose burden forces the dispositions toward time and attention that produce multitasking.
- [[Acceleration]] — multitasking belongs to the "purely hectic rush" that merely reproduces and accelerates the already-available rather than yielding the new.
- [[Narcosis]] — McLuhan's term for the numbing self-amputation under media stimulus; a resonant precursor to Han's flattening of attention under excess stimuli. *(Speculative connection)*

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Positivity]] — multitasking is not opposed to positivity but its symptom; the contemplative *vita contemplativa* and "deep, contemplative attention" Han defends against multitasking are what genuinely oppose it. The opposition the note carries is therefore between the wild-animal vigilance of multitasking and contemplative immersion. *(Strongly implied)*

## Questions It Raises

- If multitasking is a survival technique of the wilderness, what does its spread reveal about late-modern society's relation to survival versus "the good life"?
- Is the practice of multitasking separable in principle from the attention-regime of hyperattention, or does any sustained multitasking necessarily install hyperattention?
- Can the cultural achievements Han ties to deep attention survive an environment structured for divided vigilance — and what, concretely, would restore "an environment in which deep attention is possible"?
- Does Han's wild-animal symbol risk naturalizing a historically specific labor regime as mere "regression," obscuring who profits from the multitasking subject? *(Speculative connection)*
