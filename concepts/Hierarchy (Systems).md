# Hierarchy (Systems)

## Brief Definition

In systems theory, a hierarchy is the arrangement of subsystems nested inside larger subsystems inside larger subsystems still — a self-organizing system's own invention that grants stability and resilience while reducing the amount of information any one part must track.

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows, hierarchy is one of the three characteristics — alongside [[Resilience]] and [[Self-Organization]] — that explain "why systems work so well." It is not a chart of who commands whom but a structural fact about how complex order is built: "The world, or at least the parts of it humans think they understand, is organized in subsystems aggregated into larger subsystems, aggregated into still larger subsystems. A cell in your liver is a subsystem of an organ, which is a subsystem of you as an organism, and you are a subsystem of a family... This arrangement of systems and subsystems is called a hierarchy." Meadows treats hierarchy as something a self-organizing system *generates* out of itself in the course of adding complexity, not something imposed from above (`Directly stated`). The title here is disambiguated as **Hierarchy (Systems)** precisely to hold this structural sense apart from the ordinary and political-power senses of the word.

Meadows makes the strong claim that hierarchy is not merely common but nearly inevitable for anything complex, borrowing Herbert Simon's watchmaker fable of Hora and Tempus: Hora prospers because he builds "stable subassemblies" that survive interruption, while Tempus's un-nested watch "fell to pieces" every time he set it down. Her generalization: "Complex systems can evolve from simple systems only if there are stable intermediate forms. The resulting complex forms will naturally be hierarchic." And more pointedly: "Among all possible complex forms, hierarchies are the only ones that have had the time to evolve" (`Directly stated`). Hierarchy is thus an evolutionary consequence of stable intermediate forms, which is why "corporate systems, military systems, ecological systems, economic systems, living organisms" all share the pattern.

Its two functional payoffs are stability and *information economy*. "Hierarchies are brilliant systems inventions, not only because they give a system stability and resilience, but also because they reduce the amount of information that any part of the system has to keep track of." This works because connection is uneven: "In hierarchical systems relationships within each subsystem are denser and stronger than relationships between subsystems. Everything is still connected to everything else, but not equally strongly." People in a department talk mostly to one another; liver cells communicate mostly with liver cells. When the differential information links are designed well, "No level is overwhelmed with information" and "feedback delays are minimized" — directly tying hierarchy's value to well-managed [[Feedback Loops]] (`Directly stated`). This partial decomposability is also why reductionist science can dissect a system level by level and still learn something.

Crucially, for Meadows the direction of service runs *upward from the bottom*, which inverts the everyday connotation of the word: "Hierarchies evolve from the bottom up. The purpose of the upper layers of the hierarchy is to serve the purposes of the lower layers." The upper levels exist to coordinate and enhance the subsystems that gave rise to them — "The original purpose of a hierarchy is always to help its originating subsystems do their jobs better." A hierarchy fails in two symmetrical ways when this purpose is forgotten. When a subsystem's own goals dominate at the expense of the whole, the result is [[Suboptimization]] — the team member chasing personal glory, the cell "multiplying wildly" that "we call... a cancer." When the top clamps down so hard that subsystems cannot self-maintain, the failure is *overcontrol* — "If the brain controlled each cell so tightly that the cell could not perform its self-maintenance functions, the whole organism could die." A functional hierarchy therefore has to balance the two: "there must be enough central control to achieve coordination toward the large-system goal, and enough autonomy to keep all subsystems flourishing, functioning, and self-organizing" — the balance the vault names [[Central Control vs Autonomy]] (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*) — hierarchy as one of three reasons dynamic systems work well; a bottom-up, self-generated nesting of subsystems whose upper layers exist to serve their originating lower layers, granting stability and resilience and reducing each part's information load; it fails through [[Suboptimization]] (a subsystem's goals dominate) or overcontrol (the top starves subsystems of autonomy).

## Related Concepts

- [[Self-Organization]] — hierarchy is one of the structures a self-organizing system generates as it grows more complex; the two are cause and product.
- [[Resilience]] — hierarchy contributes to resilience (well-designed information links minimize feedback delays), and both are properties that let systems work well.
- [[Suboptimization]] — the first characteristic failure mode of hierarchy, when a subsystem's goals dominate the whole.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — hierarchy's information economy works by keeping feedback delays short within and between densely-linked levels.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the discipline in which this structural, non-political sense of hierarchy is native.
- [[Emergence]] — the layered nesting of hierarchy is the scaffold on which higher-level properties emerge from lower-level parts.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Central Control vs Autonomy]] — hierarchy is not an unqualified good; it is functional only when it holds this tension in balance, and it fails toward overcontrol at one pole and toward [[Suboptimization]] at the other.

## Questions It Raises

- If a hierarchy's upper layers exist to serve the lower, why do they so reliably "forget" this purpose, and is that drift structural or contingent?
- Where is the line between healthy central coordination and pathological overcontrol, and can a system sense that it has crossed it before it "crashes"?
- Meadows's hierarchy is bottom-up and service-oriented; how does this structural sense relate to — or resist — the coercive, top-down hierarchies of power that dominate social and political life?
- If hierarchies are "the only" complex forms that have had time to evolve, does that make non-hierarchical complex order impossible, or merely historically absent?
