# Self-Regulation

## Brief Definition

The neoliberal regulatory doctrine under which firms set, monitor, and judge their own standards — enshrined across government and business by 2004 as the antidote to collectivism and the centralization of power, and a windfall for the surveillance capitalists who exploited the lawless space it opened.

## Longer Explanation

In Shoshana Zuboff's account, self-regulation is not a neutral administrative arrangement but the operative principle of the neoliberal "shelter" that allowed surveillance capitalism to root and grow. As an alternative to "coercive" governmental regulation, the doctrine holds — in Zuboff's verbatim formulation — that "firms set their own standards, monitor their own compliance with those standards, and even judge their own conduct in order to 'voluntarily report and remediate violations.'" By the time of Google's 2004 public offering, she writes, self-regulation "was fully enshrined within government and across the business community as the single most effective tool for regulation without coercion and the antidote to any inclination toward collectivism and the centralization of power." (`Directly stated`)

Zuboff places self-regulation inside the second of the three domains that enriched surveillance capitalism's habitat: "the shelter of specific historical circumstances, including the policies and juridical orientation of the neoliberal paradigm." Self-regulation is the regulatory face of [[Neoliberalism]] — a doctrine she traces back to the Gilded Age robber barons, whose industries were also "to be 'self-regulating': free to follow their own evolutionary laws," and which Google then "revived" and extended. She calls this inheritance plainly: "This neoliberal legacy was a windfall for the new surveillance capitalists." (`Directly stated`)

The doctrine converged with the legal architecture of the early internet. Section 230's "hands-off stance toward companies," Zuboff argues, "perfectly converged with the reigning ideology and practice of 'self-regulation,' leaving the internet companies, and eventually the surveillance capitalists among them, free to do what they pleased." Self-regulation is therefore one element of the "shelter" that joins with the active [[Fortifications]] — corporate control, claims to lawless space, lobbying, "free speech fundamentalism" — to keep democracy "at bay" and protect the flow of [[Behavioral Surplus]] from contest and constraint. (`Strongly implied`)

Notably, Zuboff records that self-regulation was tested and found wanting even by its own institutional champion before it hardened into doctrine: the Federal Trade Commission, having "favored self-regulation," concluded in 2000 that "such efforts alone cannot ensure that the online marketplace as a whole will follow the standards adopted by industry leaders," and recommended legislation — a path foreclosed when the post-9/11 turn to "surveillance exceptionalism" took comprehensive privacy law off the table. The legislative road not taken is part of why self-regulation operated, in effect, as no regulation at all. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Fortifications]] — the passive "shelter" of self-regulation pairs with the active fortifications Google built in politics and culture; together they defend lawless space.
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — self-regulation is one of the political-cultural circumstances that let the surveillance capitalist mutation root and flourish unchallenged.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the frictionless flow of behavioral surplus is what self-regulation (and the absence of binding law) protects.
- [[Surveillance]] — the broader regime whose unconstrained growth self-regulation enabled.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Right to the Future Tense]] — the democratic counter-claim that self-regulation forecloses; binding law and contestability over corporate self-judgment.

## Questions It Raises

- If a firm sets, monitors, and judges its own standards, in what sense is it "regulated" at all — and who, if anyone, is the regulated party?
- Why did self-regulation harden into doctrine by 2004 despite the FTC's own 2000 conclusion that it could not protect consumers?
- Is self-regulation separable from the active fortifications, or is "regulation without coercion" simply the polite name for the same project of keeping democracy at bay?
- Does the convergence of self-regulation with Section 230 represent an accident of legal history or a structural fit between neoliberal ideology and the extraction imperative?
