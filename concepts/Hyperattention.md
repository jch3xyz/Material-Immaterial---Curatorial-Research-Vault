# Hyperattention

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], hyperattention is the scattered, broad-but-flat mode of awareness — a "rash change of focus between different tasks, sources of information, and processes" — that is displacing the [[Deep Contemplative Attention|deep, contemplative attention]] on which culture, philosophy, and the creative process depend.

## Longer Explanation

Hyperattention is Han's name for the primary attention-regime of the [[Achievement Society]]. In *The Burnout Society* it arises from the same "excess of stimuli, information, and impulses" that defines the society of excessive [[Positivity]]: this excess "radically changes the structure and economy of attention," so that "perception becomes fragmented and scattered." Where [[Deep Contemplative Attention|immersive reflection]] dwells with a single object long enough for it to disclose itself, hyperattention skims rapidly across many — and, crucially, it cannot tolerate the very boredom out of which something new might emerge.

Han states the displacement directly: "Increasingly, such immersive reflection is being displaced by an entirely different form of attention: hyperattention. A rash change of focus between different tasks, sources of information, and processes characterizes this scattered mode of awareness" (ch. "Profound Boredom"). Two features distinguish it from mere busyness. First, its *breadth-without-depth*: it is "a broad but flat mode of attention," the structure of awareness produced by [[Multitasking]] and by activities such as video games. Second, its *intolerance of idleness*: "Since it also has a low tolerance for boredom, it does not admit the profound idleness that benefits the creative process." Han, following Walter Benjamin, calls this profound idleness "deep boredom" — the "dream bird that hatches the egg of experience" — and laments that the hyperattentive ego, the "hyperactive ego," has lost the "gift of listening" that deep boredom makes possible.

The stakes Han attaches to this are civilizational, not merely psychological. "We owe the cultural achievements of humanity—which include philosophy—to deep, contemplative attention. Culture presumes an environment in which deep attention is possible." Hyperattention therefore figures in his larger thesis that late-modern productivity erodes the conditions of meaning: a "purely hectic rush produces nothing new. It reproduces and accelerates what is already available." This ties hyperattention to [[Acceleration]] — it is the attentional correlate of a temporality that can only repeat and speed up what already exists, never tarry long enough for the genuinely new (`Directly stated` for the quotations; `Interpretive synthesis` for naming hyperattention the attention-regime of the Achievement Society and joining it to Acceleration).

Hyperattention is closely related to, but distinct from, [[Multitasking]]. Multitasking is Han's specific claim that the divided attention demanded by late-modern work is not "civilizational progress" but a *regression* to "the vigilance of a wild animal" — an attentive technique "indispensable for survival in the wilderness," where the animal "cannot immerse itself contemplatively in what it is facing... because it must also process background events." Hyperattention is the broader human attention-form that multitasking (and gaming) exemplifies and produces: the "broad but flat mode of attention, which is similar to the vigilance of a wild animal." One can read multitasking as the *practice* and the animal-vigilance argument that grounds it, and hyperattention as the resulting *mode of awareness* — Han uses the two in the same breath, but they are not interchangeable (`Strongly implied`; Han presents multitasking and gaming as producing the flat attention that he then names hyperattention, but does not formally rank the terms).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Hyperattention is the scattered, depth-less attention that excessive positivity produces and that displaces contemplative immersion. It is bound up with [[Multitasking]] (its animal-vigilance grounding), [[Acceleration]] (the hectic rush that "reproduces and accelerates what is already available"), and the loss of the "deep boredom" Han borrows from Benjamin. Its opposite pole is [[Deep Contemplative Attention]] — the *vita contemplativa* whose recovery Han, citing Nietzsche, treats as "one of the most necessary corrections to the character of mankind."

## Related Concepts

- [[Multitasking]] — The practice that exemplifies and produces hyperattention; Han's animal-vigilance argument grounds it as regression, not progress.
- [[Achievement Society]] — Hyperattention is the attention-regime of the achievement-subject; the convoluted movement of dancing, by contrast, "escapes the achievement-principle entirely."
- [[Acceleration]] — Hyperattention is the attentional face of a temporality that can only reproduce and speed up the already-available; "a purely hectic rush produces nothing new."
- [[Positivity]] — Hyperattention follows from "an excess of stimuli, information, and impulses" — the excess of positivity that "radically changes the structure and economy of attention."
- [[Hyperinformation]] — The informational excess that fragments and scatters perception is the medium in which hyperattention operates.
- [[Sense Ratios]] — *Cross-author bridge.* [[Marshall McLuhan|McLuhan]]'s claim that each medium re-proportions the senses offers a media-theoretic frame for Han's claim that an excess of stimuli "radically changes the structure and economy of attention"; the two converge on attention as historically reconfigured by media, though Han does not invoke McLuhan.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Deep Contemplative Attention]] — The directly opposed pole: the deep, immersive attention to which "we owe the cultural achievements of humanity—which include philosophy," and which hyperattention is "displacing."

## Questions It Raises

- Han treats hyperattention as a *displacement* of contemplative attention — but is it a wholesale replacement, or can the two coexist in the same subject? Does the "hyperactive ego" retain any access to deep attention?
- If hyperattention is "similar to the vigilance of a wild animal," is the boundary between [[Multitasking]] and hyperattention one of degree, of agency, or of scale (individual practice vs. epochal mode of awareness)?
- Han locates the cure in "profound idleness" and "deep boredom." But boredom is itself something the achievement-subject is trained to flee — what social conditions could restore an "environment in which deep attention is possible"?
- Does Han's account of hyperattention apply symmetrically to all media, or does it presuppose a particular regime ([[Hyperinformation]], the feed, the game)? How would it read against [[Marshall McLuhan|McLuhan]]'s [[Sense Ratios|sense ratios]] — as a loss of one sense-balance, or as a new one?
