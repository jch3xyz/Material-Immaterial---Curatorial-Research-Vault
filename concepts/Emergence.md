# Emergence

## Brief Definition

Emergence is the appearance of properties or behaviors "on their own" from a few simple local rules iterated through recursive feedback — complexity that arises spontaneously, "often developing in ways not anticipated by the person who created the simulation," and which in Hayles's account becomes the third wave's substitute for predictability and its bottom-up replacement for teleology.

## Longer Explanation

In *How We Became Posthuman*, emergence is the defining concept of the **third wave** of [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]] and the engine of bottom-up [[Artificial Life]]. Where the software branch of AL works by "building life from the 'bottom up'" — beginning "with a few simple local rules and then, through structures that are highly recursive, allow[ing] complexity to emerge spontaneously" — emergence names the moment when that synthesis pays off: "properties or programs appear on their own, often developing in ways not anticipated by the person who created the simulation." The designer sets the rules but does not author the result. Thomas Ray set the Tierra program running overnight expecting "a 1- or 2-byte variation" and found instead that "an entire ecology had evolved," parasites and hyperparasites included — the canonical illustration of behavior unanticipated by its creator.

Emergence depends on [[Feedback Loops]] as its underlying mechanism, and Hayles is careful to keep the two distinct: feedback is the recursive structure, emergence the spontaneous higher-order result that structure produces. "Structures that lead to emergence typically involve complex feedback loops in which the outputs of a system are repeatedly fed back in as input. As the recursive looping continues, small deviations can quickly become magnified, leading to the complex interactions and unpredictable evolutions associated with emergence." It is likewise distinct from [[Self-Organization]] and [[Autopoiesis]]: the third wave wants more than a system that reproduces its own internal organization. As Hayles puts it in the framing chapter, "The third wave swelled into existence when self-organization began to be understood not merely as the (re)production of internal organization but as the springboard to emergence." Self-organization (the second wave's circular closure) is the springboard; emergence is the leap "outward into the new."

The deeper move is what emergence *replaces*. Hayles reads the three waves through [[Seriation]] — overlapping replication and innovation rather than clean paradigm breaks — and within that frame "Reflexivity, the key concept of the second wave, is displaced in the third wave by emergence." Emergence inherits the conceptual slot once held by reflexivity. But its more consequential substitution is **epistemological**: AL has not relinquished reductionism so much as reversed its direction. "In place of predictability, which is traditionally the test of whether a theory works, they emphasize emergence." Rather than reasoning backward from a complex world to its fundamental elements, AL "start[s] with the elements... so that the complex phenomenal world appears on its own." Hayles crystallizes the wager in a chiasmus she draws from the vitalist debate: "If it is true that the analytical approach murders by dissection, by the same reasoning the synthetic approach of AL may be able to procreate by emergence." Emergence is thus offered as a *generative* method — procreation, not autopsy — and, in the benign-posthuman substitution list, it stands in for **teleology**: the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] view erases "no essential differences or absolute demarcations between... robot teleology and human goals," and emergence is precisely the mechanism that lets goals appear without a goal-setter, design without a designer's intention.

For the book's larger argument, this is double-edged. Emergence is what makes the [[The Computational Universe|computational universe]] persuasive — Luc Steels's "second-order emergence" bestows on a system "the capacity to evolve," and life and computation are claimed to converge "at the edge of chaos." But because emergence here operates on substrate-independent code ("the organism is the code, and the code is the organism"), it slides easily into the disembodied reading of [[Information]] that Hayles resists (see [[The Body as Data]], [[Embodiment vs Information]]). She does not reject emergence; she contests the framework that treats emergent informational pattern as more real than the [[Embodiment|embodied]] materiality it runs on.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — treats emergence as the third wave's keyword and its substitute for both reflexivity and predictability; reads "procreate by emergence" as the rhetorical hinge by which AL converts reductionism into a generative, bottom-up biology, while warning that emergence is too often imagined to operate on bodiless code.
- **[[Christopher Langton]]** — the AL theorist for whom emergence licenses the "strong claim": that "'aliveness' will be found to be a property of the [logical] form, not of the [material] basis." Emergence lets complexity be *synthesized* upward rather than *analyzed* downward, locating "life-as-we-know-it within the larger picture of life-as-it-could-be."
- **[[Francisco Varela]]** — bridges the second and third waves; reconceives autonomy so that "the system's organization, far from remaining unchanged, can transform itself through emergent behavior" — emergence as the transformation that pure [[Autopoiesis|autopoietic]] circularity could not supply.
- **Luc Steels (via Hayles)** — distinguishes *first-order emergence* (any property arising from interactions between components) from *second-order emergence*, which grants special privilege to emergent properties that add functionality, "particularly the ability to process information." Once second-order emergence is achieved, "the organism has in effect evolved the capacity to evolve."
- **Rodney Brooks (via Hayles)** — engineers emergence in embodied hardware: Genghis's gait "is not programmed in advance" but emerges as the legs take account of one another; consciousness itself is recast as "an emergent property" — "a 'cheap trick.'"

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — the underlying mechanism emergence depends on; "complex feedback loops" repeatedly fold outputs back as inputs until small deviations magnify into emergent complexity.
- [[Self-Organization]] — the second-wave springboard; emergence is what self-organization becomes when it leaps "outward into the new" rather than merely reproducing internal order.
- [[Autopoiesis]] — emergence supersedes autopoietic closure: the third wave wants the system to transform itself through emergent behavior, not just conserve its organization.
- [[Artificial Life]] — the field emergence powers; bottom-up AL exists to stage emergence (distinct from emergence the mechanism).
- [[The Three Waves of Cybernetics]] — emergence is the keyword of the third wave, displacing reflexivity per [[Seriation]].
- [[The Computational Universe]] — emergence is the bridge by which "higher-level computations" and "life" are claimed to arise spontaneously from cellular automata.

## Opposing Concepts

- **Teleology / predictability** — emergence is explicitly offered in their place: it substitutes generative, bottom-up appearance for goal-directed design and for prediction-as-test. (Hayles: "In place of predictability... they emphasize emergence.")
- [[The Body as Data]] — emergence as practiced on substrate-independent code reinforces the disembodiment of information; Hayles's counter-reading insists emergent pattern cannot be separated from its embodying medium.
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — emergence sits on the fault line: it can be claimed for bodiless informational pattern or re-grounded in [[Embodiment|embodied]] interaction (Brooks vs. Moravec), making it a contested rather than settled ally of disembodiment.

## Questions It Raises

- If emergent properties "appear on their own" in unanticipated ways, what is left of the designer's authorship — and does "procreate by emergence" describe genuine novelty or only the designer's intention deferred and rediscovered? (Hayles notes Ray's "biomorphic namings... function not so much as an overlay as an explication of an intention that was there at the beginning.")
- Emergence is celebrated for substituting for predictability. But if a theory's results "could not be predicted, even in theory," what counts as confirming or falsifying it — and does emergence quietly preserve reductionism rather than escape it (see [[Embodiment vs Information]])?
- When second-order emergence privileges "the ability to process information" above all else, does emergence become a covert vehicle for treating the body as substrate-independent data (see [[The Body as Data]])? Can there be an emergence that requires the resistance of materiality rather than abstracting it away?
- If emergence replaces teleology, does the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] lose the capacity to distinguish "robot teleology and human goals" — and is that erasure a liberation from intentionality or a loss of the embodied perspective from which goals are had?
- Brooks locates emergence in embodied gait while Moravec locates it in downloadable mind: is emergence intrinsically tied to [[Embodiment]], or can it be staged in pure informational pattern? The answer decides whether emergence underwrites or undermines the disembodied posthuman.
