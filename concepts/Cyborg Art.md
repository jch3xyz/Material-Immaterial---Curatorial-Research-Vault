# Cyborg Art

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's 1968 coinage for *the cybernetic organism as an art form* — sculpture that simulates the structure of life literally and is capable of sustained, two-way, reciprocal communication with a human being, which he names "the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture."

## Longer Explanation

Burnham introduces **Cyborg Art** in Chapter 8 of *Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) as the terminus of a long historical drive he traces from the paleolithic artist-magician forward: the yearning to make an image that does not merely "indicate, imitate, simulate" but "literally replace" the living thing it depicts, and then to go beyond replacement toward "images capable of intelligent intercourse with their creators." Where all prior sculpture — even contemporary Kinetic Art — could offer only "one-way stimulation for the human party involved," Cyborg Art is the first art capable of a "sustained two-way experience." Burnham defines it parenthetically as "the cybernetic organism as an art form," distinguishing it from the static **robot art** (mock robots, dolls, manikins) that occupies the bulk of the same chapter (`Strongly implied` that the two are sequential stages; `Directly stated` for the definition itself).

Crucially, Burnham deploys the **cyborg** in an art-world context in 1968 — seventeen years before Donna Haraway's *A Cyborg Manifesto* (1985). His source is technical, not feminist or political: he reports that "less than a decade ago the term cyborg (i.e., cybernetic organism) was created to mean a man-machine system in which the human partner functions unaware of his connection with the machine component," and generalizes it to "any artificial system connected through reciprocal feedback to an organism." Cyborg Art names the application of this feedback principle to sculpture — art that no longer merely "acts" the way "a ball of yarn" acts "when it is batted and chased by a kitten," but actually closes a feedback loop with its environment and its viewer (`Directly stated`).

Burnham anchors the concept in concrete works rather than treating it as pure speculation. He cites Nicolas Schöffer's **CYSP I** (1956) — a "cybernetic-spatiodynamic construction" sponsored by Phillips that reacts to changes in sound, noise, light, and darkness with "the unpredictability of an organism"; the **cybernetic games** of Enrique Castro-Cid; and, as "very likely the most technically sophisticated Cyborg Art produced to date," James Seawright's **Searcher** (1966), which uses a "closed-loop feedback" so that "the condition of its previous states determines the variety of its future states." For Burnham this represents sculpture's self-supersession: "sculpture seeks its own obliteration by moving toward integration with the intelligent life forms it has always imitated" (`Directly stated`).

The concept sits inside Burnham's larger **systems** thesis: cybernetics is "preeminently the science of organization," and as "the old attributes of the sculpted object are dropped one by one," art moves "toward a systems view." Cyborg Art is the point at which "the system itself will be made intelligent and sensitive to the human invading its territorial and sensorial domain" — the threshold beyond which the very term "sculpture" is dropped (`Interpretive synthesis`, grounded in the chapter's closing pages on responsive systems and E.A.T.).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — coins and owns the term. Cyborg Art (also "Post-Kinetic art") = the cybernetic organism as an art form, the next/ultimate stage of sculpture; grounded in **CYSP I**, **Searcher**, and cybernetic games; the precise definition note is **[[Cyborg (Burnham)]]**. The deployment of the cyborg figure in the art world predates Haraway by seventeen years and is purely technical/cybernetic in origin.
- **Variant within the chapter** — Burnham uses "Cyborg or Post-Kinetic art" interchangeably for the same category, and distinguishes both from "Mock Robots as Sculpture" (static anthropomorphic figures by Paolozzi, Trova, Segal, Kienholz, et al.), which are *not* Cyborg Art because they close no feedback loop.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — *not* opposed in substance but in register and provenance: Burnham's Cyborg Art is the cyborg as an aesthetic/sculptural form, technical and pre-political; Haraway's later cyborg is an ironic political myth. The note is cross-listed under both `related` and `opposes` to mark this same-figure / different-stakes split.
- Kinetic Art / the static art object — the foils Burnham defines Cyborg Art *against*: art that delivers only "one-way stimulation" and cannot communicate back.

## Questions It Raises

- If Cyborg Art is "the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture," does its arrival mean the *end* of sculpture as a category — and is the term Burnham predicts will be "dropped" already obsolete at the moment of coinage?
- How does Burnham's purely technical, cybernetic cyborg (1968) relate genealogically to Haraway's political cyborg (1985)? Did the art-world cyborg prepare the ground, run in parallel, or simply share a word?
- Burnham grounds Cyborg Art in works (CYSP I, Searcher) that are now historical curiosities. Is the concept best read as a failed avant-garde prediction, or as the accurate forecast of later responsive/interactive [[New Media Art]]?
- If the artwork closes a reciprocal feedback loop with its viewer, where does authorship lie — with the artist, the engineer ("there's a man behind it," per Seawright), the system, or the participant?
