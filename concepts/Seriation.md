# Seriation

## Brief Definition

Hayles's historiographic method-concept — appropriated from archaeological anthropology — that models conceptual change not as a clean rupture but as "a pattern of overlapping replication and innovation," in which some attributes of a constellation of ideas change from one period to the next while others persist.

## Longer Explanation

Seriation is the name Hayles gives to the *shape* of historical change in cybernetics, and by extension to the relation between the human and the posthuman. Against two influential models of intellectual history, she writes that "the development of cybernetics followed neither a Kuhnian model of incommensurable paradigms nor a Foucauldian model of sharp epistemic breaks. In the history of cybernetics, ideas were rarely made up out of whole cloth. Rather, they were fabricated in a pattern of overlapping replication and innovation, a pattern that I call 'seriation' (a term appropriated from archaeological anthropology)." The borrowing is literal: archaeologists chart how an artifact's attributes change over time on a seriation chart. Her worked example is the lamp, whose light-giving element shifts from wick to filament — a change that does not happen all at once but overlaps across decades.

The figure that emerges from such a chart is what Hayles calls the **tiger's iris**: each attribute is "narrow at the top when an attribute first begins to be introduced, with a bulge in the middle during the heyday of the attribute, and tapered off at the bottom as the shift to a new model is completed." Drawn together for a whole set of attributes, these overlapping irises "reveal patterns of overlapping innovation and replication. Some attributes change from one model to the next, but others remain the same." Hayles transfers this directly from material culture to conceptual history on the grounds that "concept and artifact engage each other in continuous feedback loops" — a glitch in an artifact prompts a new concept, which yields a new artifact, and so on (see [[Feedback Loops]]). Conceptual fields, she argues, therefore evolve like material culture.

In Hayles's architecture, seriation is the connective tissue of [[The Three Waves of Cybernetics]]. [[Homeostasis]] (first wave), reflexivity (second wave), and emergence/[[Autopoiesis]] (third wave) do not succeed one another by erasure; each older concept "lingers on as an allusion that authenticates new elements" and leaves "its imprint on the new constellation." This is why seriation is methodologically inseparable from her two companion devices: the *skeuomorph* (the transitional design feature, like simulated stitching on a vinyl dashboard, that "looks to past and future" and smooths the passage between constellations) and the *conceptual constellation* (the coherent cluster of ideas whose "formation marks the beginning of a period; its disassembly and reconstruction signal the transition to a different period"). Seriation names the overall *temporal pattern*; the skeuomorph names the *device* that does the overlapping; the constellation names the *unit* that overlaps.

The stakes of seriation are not merely descriptive. It grounds Hayles's most quoted claim. Echoing Bruno Latour's "we have never been modern," she writes that "the seriated history of cybernetics — emerging from networks at once materially real, socially regulated, and discursively constructed — suggests, for similar reasons, that we have always been posthuman." If change is seriated rather than apocalyptic, then the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] is not a coming event that will replace the human but an overlapping reinscription already underway: "human" and "posthuman" "coexist in shifting configurations that vary with historically specific contexts." This is also Hayles's defense against technological determinism — by recasting the disembodiment of information as a seriated, contingent negotiation rather than an inevitable break, she opens space to contest it and to keep [[Embodiment|embodiment]] in the picture.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — coins the term for *How We Became Posthuman*, appropriating it from archaeological anthropology and pairing it with the *skeuomorph* and the *conceptual constellation*. Seriation is her explicit alternative to both Thomas Kuhn (paradigm shift) and Michel Foucault (epistemic break), and the formal ground for "we have always been posthuman."
- **Archaeological anthropology (source domain)** — the seriation chart as a tool for mapping change in artifact attributes over time; the "tiger's iris" figure is the standard visual form Hayles carries over to conceptual history.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Three Waves of Cybernetics]] — the seriated sequence in action: homeostasis, reflexivity, emergence overlap rather than replace one another.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the human and posthuman are seriated ("we have always been posthuman"), not cleanly successive.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the history whose pattern of change seriation was coined to describe.
- [[Homeostasis]] / [[Autopoiesis]] — first- and second/third-wave anchor concepts whose persistence-as-allusion exemplifies seriation.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — concept and artifact "engage each other in continuous feedback loops," the mechanism by which conceptual fields evolve like material culture.
- [[Systems Theory]] — seriation is Hayles's narrative, embodied counter-method to "analytically driven systems theory" and its teleology of disembodiment.

## Opposing Concepts

- **Kuhnian paradigm shift** — Hayles explicitly rejects the "model of incommensurable paradigms"; seriation insists attributes persist across periods rather than being made incommensurable.
- **Foucauldian epistemic break** — likewise rejected; against the "sharp epistemic breaks," seriation foregrounds overlap and replication.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the apocalyptic "That was then, this is now" rupture narrative (Moravec's "postbiological" future) presumes a clean break that seriation dismantles; the posthuman is shown to reinscribe, not abolish, its predecessor.

## Questions It Raises

- If conceptual change is always seriated, can the shift from material to informational understandings of the body ever be a true break — or is "information lost its body" itself only an overlapping reinscription that embodiment never fully exits? (Ties to [[Embodiment vs Information]] and [[Material vs Immaterial]].)
- Does "we have always been posthuman" defuse the politics of the posthuman, or sharpen them — if there is no rupture to resist, is the work instead to contest which seriated strands get reinscribed?
- When [[The Body as Data|the body is reconstrued as data]], does seriation predict that the embodied, material body persists as a skeuomorphic "allusion" inside the new informational constellation rather than being erased?
- Is seriation itself a posthuman historiography — modeling concepts as distributed, overlapping, co-produced with artifacts — and thus performatively at odds with the [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|autonomous]] author of clean paradigm shifts?
