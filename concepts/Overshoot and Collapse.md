# Overshoot and Collapse

## Brief Definition

The characteristic behavior of a system whose exponential growth carries it past a limit it cannot perceive in time — the growing quantity overruns its constraint and then, depending on the speed and strength of the constraining loop, either settles smoothly, oscillates, or crashes.

## Longer Explanation

For Donella Meadows, overshoot-and-collapse is one member of a small family of behavior modes that a growing stock can exhibit when it presses against a limit. In *Thinking in Systems* she works it out through her "systems zoo" — minimal stock-and-flow models whose structure, not whose subject matter, dictates their behavior. The general warning behind the whole family is a matter of timing: "A quantity growing exponentially toward a constraint or limit reaches that limit in a surprisingly short time." Because growth is exponential, doubling or quadrupling the resource buys almost no extra time before the peak; "the higher and faster you grow, the farther and faster you fall."

The three outcomes are best seen in Meadows's fishery model — a renewable resource stock (fish) coupled to a growing capital stock (boats) through a [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]]. She lists the possibilities explicitly: "overshoot and adjustment to a sustainable equilibrium," "overshoot beyond that equilibrium followed by oscillation around it," and "overshoot followed by collapse of the resource and the industry dependent on the resource." Which one occurs "depends on two things. The first is the critical threshold beyond which the resource population's ability to regenerate itself is damaged. The second is the rapidity and effectiveness of the balancing feedback loop that slows capital growth as the resource becomes depleted." A fast balancing loop brings the system "smoothly into equilibrium"; a slower one makes it "oscillate"; a very weak one lets capital keep growing until "the resource and the industry both collapse" — the fish "turned, for all practical purposes, into a nonrenewable resource."

The mirror case is the nonrenewable, stock-limited resource — Meadows's oil-company model — which cannot adjust or oscillate at all but only follows "the classic dynamics of depletion": rapid growth of extraction capital fed by a resource that does not regenerate, so the capital stock rises "higher before it collapses." Here the only "real choice in the management of a nonrenewable resource is whether to get rich very fast or to get less rich but stay that way longer." This distinction — an adjustable overshoot for renewable, flow-limited stocks versus an inescapable depletion curve for nonrenewable, stock-limited ones — is what makes the concept the systems-theoretic engine beneath Meadows's *Limits to Growth* legacy: whether the human economy overshooting the planet's regenerative capacity recovers, oscillates, or crashes turns on the same two variables, the regeneration threshold and the speed of the constraining loop.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — the archetypal system dynamics behavior mode: a growing stock overruns a limit and then adjusts, oscillates, or collapses. Meadows insists the difference among the three is not the resource but the *structure* — specifically the critical regeneration threshold and the speed of the balancing loop. Cross-listed with her contrast between renewable (flow-limited) and nonrenewable (stock-limited) resources, where the latter admits only depletion, never adjustment.

## Related Concepts

- [[The Limits-to-Growth Archetype]] — the broader pattern this behavior mode instantiates; overshoot-and-collapse is the destructive tail of the limits-to-growth structure.
- [[Stock-Limited vs Flow-Limited Resources]] — the distinction that decides which outcome is possible: flow-limited (renewable) stocks can adjust or oscillate, stock-limited (nonrenewable) ones only deplete.
- [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] — the growth engine (capital, extraction, harvest) that produces the overshoot in the first place.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the balancing loop whose *speed* determines whether overshoot ends in equilibrium, oscillation, or collapse.
- [[The Growth Imperative]] — Harari's name for the economic compulsion to grow that, in Meadows's terms, drives systems toward their limits and into overshoot.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Homeostasis]] — the maintained equilibrium that a well-controlled balancing loop achieves; overshoot-and-collapse is precisely what happens when the homeostatic (balancing) feedback is too slow or too weak to hold the stock within its limit.

## Questions It Raises

- If exponential growth reaches its limit "in a surprisingly short time," can any system that perceives its constraint only through delayed feedback avoid overshoot at all?
- Where is the critical regeneration threshold for planet-scale renewable stocks, and can it be known before it is crossed?
- Does improving extraction or harvest efficiency ("high leverage, wrong direction") systematically convert renewable resources into effectively nonrenewable ones?
- What determines the speed of the balancing loop in a human economy — and can it be deliberately strengthened faster than the reinforcing growth loop?
