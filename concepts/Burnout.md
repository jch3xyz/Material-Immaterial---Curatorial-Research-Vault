# Burnout

## Brief Definition

For Byung-Chul Han, burnout is the title malady of the achievement society: the pathological endpoint of voluntary self-exploitation, in which the achievement-subject, free of any external master, drives and overheats itself until it collapses — a sickness not of repression but of excess positivity.

## Longer Explanation

Burnout names the moment when auto-exploitation reaches its terminal state. Because the [[Achievement Society]] has shed the negativity of prohibition and replaced the Freudian *Should* with an unlimited *Can*, the achievement-subject is "lord and master of itself," subject to no one but itself — and therefore exploits itself in the name of freedom. Han's central formulation, anchored in chapter 9, is that "Achievement society is the society of self-exploitation. The achievement-subject exploits itself until it burns out." The exploiter and the exploited are the same person; the violence is self-generated, and "more fatal than its counterpart inasmuch as the victim of such violence considers itself free." *(Directly stated)*

Crucially, burnout (with its frequent sequel, depression) is a malady of **[[Positivity]], not of [[Negativity]]**. In "Neuronal Power" (ch. 2) Han classes burnout among the neuronal illnesses — depression, ADHD, BPD, burnout syndrome — that "do not follow from the negativity of what is immunologically foreign, but from an excess of positivity." His image is thermal and self-referential: "Burnout syndrome occurs when the ego overheats, which follows from too much of the Same." The "hyper" of hyperactivity, he insists, "is not an immunological category. It represents the massification of the positive." Burnout is thus not an attack from an outside Other but a *terror of immanence* — the system, and the ego, exhausting itself from within. *(Directly stated)*

Because it arises from positivity, burnout (and its companion depression) **eludes psychoanalysis**. This is the polemical core of chapter 9, directed at Alain Ehrenberg, Richard Sennett, and Freud. Freud's psychic apparatus is "a repressive apparatus with commandments and prohibitions"; the unconscious, repression, and conflict are figures of negativity belonging to the now-departed disciplinary society. Depression, by contrast, "is not a consequence of repression"; it "follows from overexcited, overdriven, excessive self-reference that has assumed destructive traits." Hence Han's verdict: "In fact, depression defies the conflict model — that is, depression eludes psychoanalysis. Ehrenberg attempts to rescue psychoanalysis even though its conditions have disappeared." Han also rejects Ehrenberg's purely psychological framing (depression as the failure to "become himself") and Sennett's claim that the narcissist *intentionally avoids* closure — for Han, the achievement-subject is not unwilling but *incapable* of closure, scattering itself "into the open." *(Directly stated)*

Two terms fold into this concept. First, **No-Longer-Being-Able-to-Be-Able**: in chapter 3, Han writes that depression "erupts at the moment when the achievement-subject is no longer able to be able [*nicht mehr können kann*]," and is "first and foremost … creative fatigue and exhausted ability." The depressive's complaint, "Nothing is possible," can only arise in a society that thinks "Nothing is impossible." Second, the **melancholy-vs-depression distinction**: melancholy is a phenomenon of negativity — "preceded by the experience of loss," still standing in a (negative) relation to the absent thing — whereas depression "is cut off from all relation and attachment," objectless, undirected, "lack[ing] gravity." Burnout/depression is therefore *objectless* malady of excess and self-reference, categorically distinct from the loss-bound melancholy of the older order. *(Directly stated)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Burnout is the namesake malady of *The Burnout Society*. It is the pathological consequence of [[Auto-Exploitation]] in the [[Achievement Society]]: the achievement-subject, freed from the "commanding Other," overheats in a "rat race it runs against itself" until it burns out. Han frames it as a disease of [[Positivity]] (excess, the Same, the inability to say no), not of repression, which is why it escapes the Freudian/Ehrenbergian conflict model. He distinguishes it sharply from melancholy (negativity, loss) and folds in the figure of *no-longer-being-able-to-be-able* (creative fatigue, exhausted ability). *(Directly stated)*

## Related Concepts

- [[Achievement Society]] — burnout is its signature malady; "Achievement society is the society of self-exploitation."
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] — burnout is "the pathological consequence of voluntary self-exploitation"; the exploiter is the exploited.
- [[Positivity]] — burnout derives from "an excess of positivity," "too much of the Same," not from repression.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — burnout/depression lack all negativity (repression, prohibition, the Other, loss); melancholy, by contrast, *is* a phenomenon of negativity. The opposition is constitutive: psychoanalysis fails on burnout precisely because it presupposes negativity.

## Questions It Raises

- If burnout eludes psychoanalysis (which presupposes negativity), what therapeutic or critical practice could address a malady of pure positivity?
- Han insists depression is objectless and relationless, unlike melancholy's relation to loss — but is there a residual "object" (the unattainable ego ideal) against which the self measures and reproaches itself?
- If the achievement-subject experiences self-exploitation as freedom, how can burnout be recognized *as* exploitation rather than personal failure ("tired of having to become himself")?
- How does "no-longer-being-able-to-be-able" relate to the broader cultural prohibition on rest and slowness Han elsewhere mourns (e.g. the man of leisure he reads out of Nietzsche)?
