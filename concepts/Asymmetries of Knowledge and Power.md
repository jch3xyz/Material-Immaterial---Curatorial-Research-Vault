# Asymmetries of Knowledge and Power

## Brief Definition

The defining structural consequence of surveillance capitalism: a one-way division in which the firm knows a great deal about us while our access to its knowledge is sparse — so extreme, in Zuboff's reading of Durkheim, that they make "conflict itself impossible" by "refusing to admit the right of combat."

## Longer Explanation

For Shoshana Zuboff, "asymmetries of knowledge and power" name the load-bearing imbalance produced when surveillance capitalism takes command of what she calls the **division of learning in society** — the axial principle of social order in an information civilization, the successor to the industrial division of labor. The question is no longer only "Who knows?" but also "Who decides who knows?" and "Who decides who decides?" Surveillance capital, she argues, has gathered the power and asserted the authority to supply all the answers, producing "unprecedented asymmetries of knowledge and power" that operate "precisely as Durkheim had feared." `Directly stated`

The asymmetry is engineered through what Zuboff calls **the problem of the two texts**. Surveillance capitalism compels the production of two electronic texts, not one. The first, public-facing text we author and read — our posts, photos, searches, "likes." It "trails a shadow close behind": the **shadow text**, a "read only" accumulation of behavioral surplus that "says more about us than we can know about ourselves," yet is "hidden from our view." The asymmetry is therefore not incidental but constitutive: "They know a great deal about us, but our access to their knowledge is sparse: hidden in the shadow text and read only by the new priests, their bosses, and their machines." `Directly stated` A "narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists, their privately owned machines, and the economic interests for whose sake they learn" reverts the order of knowledge to a pre-Gutenberg condition. `Directly stated`

Zuboff borrows the pathology directly from Émile Durkheim. The young Durkheim, writing *The Division of Labor in Society*, recognized that the division could "take a dark turn" into an "abnormal" (sometimes translated "pathological") form, and singled out "what he viewed as the most dangerous form of inequality: extreme asymmetries of power that make 'conflict itself impossible' by 'refusing to admit the right of combat.'" `Directly stated` This is the sharpest sense of the concept: the asymmetry is so total that it forecloses contestation itself — there is no terrain on which to fight, because the action is illegible and the knowledge is one-directional. Such pathologies, Durkheim held and Zuboff repeats, "can be cured only by a politics that asserts the people's right to contest, confront, and prevail in the face of unequal and illegitimate power over society." `Directly stated`

Chapter 8 supplies the concept's most compressed formulation. Reviewing **rendition** — the operations that translate human experience into behavioral data — Zuboff lists five adjectives: "under surveillance capitalism, rendition is typically unauthorized, unilateral, gluttonous, secret, and brazen. These characteristics summarize the asymmetries of power that put the 'surveillance' in surveillance capitalism." `Directly stated` The five adjectives function as the operational signature of the asymmetry: it is taken without permission (unauthorized), decided by one side (unilateral), totalizing (gluttonous), concealed (secret), and indifferent to objection (brazen). Across the book the phrase recurs as the through-line linking the discovery of behavioral surplus, the dispossession cycle, and instrumentarian power into a single diagnosis of one-directional knowing. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — the originating use. The asymmetry of knowledge and power is the structural core of surveillance capitalism: the firm's "unprecedented concentrations of knowledge and power" command the division of learning, made operational through the two-text architecture (public text vs. read-only shadow text) and the five-adjective signature of rendition (unauthorized, unilateral, gluttonous, secret, brazen). Its limit case, via Durkheim, is the pathological asymmetry that makes "conflict itself impossible." `Directly stated`
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — a resonant but distinct diagnosis. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] also describes a regime in which the watched are maximally exposed while the watcher recedes from view, but he locates the engine in *voluntary self-exposure* and the dissolution of the master rather than in a corporate logic of accumulation. Zuboff's asymmetry is economic and structural where Han's is psychopolitical. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Transparency of the Watched vs Secrecy of the Watcher]] — the tension that names the asymmetry's two faces: total legibility of the watched against the illegibility of the watcher (the shadow text is "read only" for the firm, hidden from us)

## Questions It Raises

- If the asymmetry is "internally consistent" with the logic of accumulation rather than the product of "bad people," can it be regulated by frameworks (privacy, monopoly) aimed at intent or conduct?
- Durkheim's cure is a politics asserting "the right of combat" — but the asymmetry's defining trait is that it makes combat impossible. What counter-power could even contest an illegible, one-directional regime of knowing?
- Does Zuboff's economic-structural account of one-way knowing converge with or diverge from Han's account of voluntary self-exposure — and is the difference one of mechanism, of remedy, or both?
- The "new priesthood" reads a shadow text we cannot see. Is the asymmetry primarily epistemic (a division of *knowing*) or political (a division of *deciding who knows*)?
