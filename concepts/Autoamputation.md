# Autoamputation

## Brief Definition

For McLuhan, every technology or media extension of the human body is a defensive *self-amputation* by which the central nervous system isolates, offloads, and numbs an organ, sense, or function that has been over-stressed by acceleration and increased load.

## Longer Explanation

McLuhan adopts the term from the stress physiology of Hans Selye and Adolphe Jonas, for whom all extensions of the body, in sickness or health, are attempts to maintain equilibrium. "Any extension of ourselves they regard as 'autoamputation,' and they find that the autoamputative power or strategy is resorted to by the body when the perceptual power cannot locate or avoid the cause of irritation" (Ch. 4). McLuhan transplants this medical theory of disease and irritation into a general account of why humans build technology at all: "In the physical stress of superstimulation of various kinds, the central nervous system acts to protect itself by a strategy of amputation or isolation of the offending organ, sense, or function. Thus, the stimulus to new invention is the stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load" (Ch. 4). [Directly stated] The wheel amputates the foot; print and writing amputate and isolate the visual faculty; the phonograph extends and so diminishes the voice; the photograph and movie extend the eye and the gestural body. Each invention is therefore at once an extension outward and a severing of a function from the bodily whole: "Any invention or technology is an extension or self-amputation of our physical bodies, and such extension also demands new ratios or new equilibriums among the other organs and extensions of the body" (Ch. 4).

Autoamputation is inseparable from its twin, [[Narcosis]]. The amplification of the isolated function is, for McLuhan, unbearable to the nervous system except through numbness: "Such amplification is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception. This is the sense of the Narcissus myth" (Ch. 4). [Directly stated] The severed extension returns to us as an image we fail to recognize as our own — "Self-amputation forbids self-recognition" (Ch. 4) — which is why the gadget lover, like Narcissus, becomes the [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions|servomechanism]] of his own outered organ. The numbing is structural, not incidental: "An extension appears to be an amplification of an organ, a sense or a function, that inspires the central nervous system to a Self-protective gesture of numbing of the extended area, at least so far as direct inspection and awareness are concerned" (Ch. 18). [Directly stated] And in the radio chapter McLuhan generalizes the rule to all media: "All technological extensions of ourselves must be numb and subliminal, else we could not endure the leverage exerted upon us by such extension" (Ch. 30).

Because every amputation alters the equilibrium of the body, autoamputation is the mechanism by which a new medium resets the [[Sense Ratios]] — "such extension also demands new ratios or new equilibriums among the other organs and extensions of the body" (Ch. 4). The doctrine is the physiological underside of [[Media as Extensions of Man]]: an extension is never merely additive but always also a subtraction, a loss of the function it offloads. In the case studies of Part II, McLuhan literalizes the surgical metaphor. The phonograph "shrinks the national chest" by amputating vocal exercise; "amputation of such extensions of our physical being calls for as much knowledge and skill as are prerequisite to any other physical amputation" (Ch. 20); and the movie "is a mighty limb of the industrial giant. That it is being amputated by the TV image reflects a still greater revolution going on at the center of American life" (Ch. 29). [Directly stated]

In Chapter 7 McLuhan raises autoamputation from the bodily to the social scale. The introduction of any new technology is "huge collective surgery carried out on the social body with complete disregard for antiseptics" (Ch. 7); the incised area is numb, but "it is the entire system that is changed." [Directly stated] He names the underlying error the *Narcissus attitude*: "As long as we adopt the Narcissus attitude of regarding the extensions of our own bodies as really out there and really independent of us, we will meet all technological challenges with the same sort of banana-skin pirouette and collapse" (Ch. 7). The counter-irritant — the amputating technology — "usually proves a greater plague than the initial irritant, like a drug habit" (Ch. 7), so that the social surgery never reaches equilibrium but only generates the next round of stress and amputation.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** — coins the term as the keystone of his media theory by adopting Selye's and Jonas's stress physiology (Ch. 4). Autoamputation explains *why* humans extend themselves (relief of strain on the central nervous system under accelerating load), *what it costs* (the numbing of the amputated function, i.e. [[Narcosis]]), and *what it produces* (new [[Sense Ratios]] and the [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions|servomechanism]] relation to one's own extensions). He runs it through case study after case study (wheel/foot, speech/intellect, print/eye, phonograph/voice, photograph and movie/eye and gesture) and extends it to "collective surgery" on the social body (Ch. 7).
- **Hans Selye and Adolphe Jonas** *(named by McLuhan, no book in `raw/`)* — the source physiologists. For them autoamputation is a theory of disease and irritation, "an attempt to maintain equilibrium"; McLuhan notes it "was no part of the intention of Jonas and Selye to provide an explanation of human invention and technology" (Ch. 4). [Directly stated] McLuhan's move is to read technology as continuous with this physiological self-protection.

## Related Concepts

- [[Narcosis]] — autoamputation's inseparable twin; the numbness that makes the amplified extension bearable. McLuhan: amplification "is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness."
- [[Media as Extensions of Man]] — the positive face of the same act; autoamputation is the doctrine that every extension is also a subtraction.
- [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions]] — the consequence of self-amputation: numbed by the severed image, we serve our own outered organs "as gods or minor religions."
- [[Sense Ratios]] — every amputation "demands new ratios or new equilibriums" among the remaining senses and organs.
- [[Acceleration]] — McLuhan's named cause of autoamputation ("the stress of acceleration of pace and increase of load"); cross-link to Han's later, pathologized use of the term.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Sense Ratios]] — in tension with itself here: autoamputation simultaneously *unbalances* the existing sense ratio (by isolating and amplifying one function) and *forces* a new ratio or "closure." The opposition is between the prior bodily equilibrium and the violent re-equilibration the amputation imposes.

## Questions It Raises

- If every extension is also an amputation, is there any technology that is purely additive — or is loss the necessary price of every gain in reach? [Speculative connection]
- McLuhan locates the trigger in "acceleration of pace and increase of load." How does his physiological acceleration relate to Han's social-temporal [[Acceleration]] and to Kurzweil's celebratory law of accelerating returns? [Speculative connection]
- If "self-amputation forbids self-recognition," can the numbness ever be reversed — and is McLuhan's wager (the artist who "can correct the sense ratios before the blow of new technology has numbed conscious procedures," Ch. 7) an exit from autoamputation or merely another extension? [Interpretive synthesis]
- At the social scale, who performs the "collective surgery," and on whose body — given that "the area of impact and incision is numb" while "the entire system that is changed"? [Strongly implied]
