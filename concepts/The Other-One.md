# The Other-One

## Brief Definition

The "Other-One" is the behaviorist "viewpoint of observation" — traced by Zuboff from Planck through Max Meyer's 1921 textbook *Psychology of the Other-One* to B. F. Skinner — in which the human being is recast as an external object, an "organism among organisms," with all inner life ruled out of science as unobservable; for Zuboff it is the epistemological root of [[Instrumentarian Power|instrumentarian power]].

## Longer Explanation

In chapter 12, "Two Species of Power," Zuboff excavates the intellectual lineage of instrumentarianism and finds its founding move in a single epistemological decision: to study the human only from the outside, as an object among objects. Skinner credits the obscure German-trained psychologist Max Meyer, whose 1921 textbook bore "the ominous-sounding title *Psychology of the Other-One*," with establishing this foundation. Meyer in turn carried the teaching of his dissertation advisor, the physicist Max Planck, who held that "The outside world is something independent from man, something absolute" and that the discovery of its laws — including "the laws of human behavior" — was "the most sublime scientific pursuit in life." The Other-One is thus the importation of physics's stance toward dead matter into the science of human beings (`Directly stated`).

The decisive consequence is the eviction of the inner world from the field of knowledge. As Zuboff summarizes Meyer, "Human behavior would yield to scientific research only if psychologists learned to view humans as others," and under this "viewpoint of observation" the human is recast as "an 'it,' an 'other,' a 'they' of organisms: an 'organism among organisms,' distinguishable from a lettuce, a moose, or an inchworm only in degree of complexity." Only the socially visible, measurable behaviors count; "the world within the skin of the Other-One loses its preferred status." Skinner extended this into radical behaviorism, insisting that even self-observation must proceed "from the viewpoint of the Other-One" (`Directly stated`).

A built-in corollary is the forfeit of freedom for knowledge. Meyer, echoing Planck, held that "freedom of action in the animal world signifies the same that is meant by accidents in the world of physics" — apparent freedom is merely a gap in information awaiting closure by advancing science. From the viewpoint of the Other-One, the felt experience of free will is "but a bit of undigested denial," a "lacuna of ignorance" queued up for explanation. This is why Zuboff frames the whole tradition under the formula "freedom is ignorance": as the laws of behavior become known, the liberal idea of freedom contracts. Skinner's *Walden Two* is the utopian flower of this logic — a "superorganism" governed not by force or by the domination of souls but by the calm engineering of behavior in which "all the ideals of a liberal society — freedom, autonomy, privacy, a people's right to self-rule — must be forfeit" (`Directly stated`).

Chapter 13 completes the arc by making [[Big Other]] the global, computational realization of the Other-One's viewpoint. "From Big Other's point of view we are strictly Other-Ones: organisms that behave. Big Other encodes the viewpoint of the Other-One as a global presence." Where Meyer and Skinner could only theorize and stage the otherizing gaze, surveillance capitalism's ubiquitous digital apparatus operationalizes it at planetary scale, reducing human experience to "measurable observable behavior" while remaining indifferent to its meaning — what Zuboff calls *radical indifference*. The Other-One is therefore not a metaphor but the operating epistemology of instrumentarian power: a way of knowing that "severs our insides from our outsides," treating the abandoned interior as surplus to requirements (`Directly stated` for the encoding claim; `Interpretive synthesis` for the framing of the Other-One as the operating epistemology of the regime).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — uses "the Other-One" as the historical and epistemological key to instrumentarianism: a viewpoint of observation, inherited from Planck via Meyer and Skinner, that otherizes the human into an "organism among organisms," excludes inner life from science, and trades freedom for knowledge. [[Big Other]] is its global encoding; [[Instrumentarian Power]] is the power it licenses; [[Behavioral Surplus]] is what its gaze extracts.
- **Max Meyer (via Zuboff/Skinner)** — coined the phrase in his 1921 *Psychology of the Other-One*: "We are studying the Other-One in preference to Our-Selves," restricting science to the socially visible behavior of "the Other-One … in so far as it is of social significance rather than as it is of significance for himself."
- **B. F. Skinner (via Zuboff)** — radicalized the viewpoint so that *all* observation, even of one's own conduct, is enacted "from the viewpoint of the Other-One," and imagined the pervasive technologies that would "institutionalize the viewpoint of the Other-One" — a vision Zuboff reads as fulfilled, against his intentions, by surveillance capitalism. (`B. F. Skinner` and the figures Planck, Meyer, and Hannah Arendt are forward-stub authors, no book in `raw/`.)

## Related Concepts

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the species of power whose "way of knowing" *is* the viewpoint of the Other-One; the Other-One is its epistemological root.
- [[Big Other]] — the sensate computational apparatus that "encodes the viewpoint of the Other-One as a global presence."
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — what the otherizing gaze harvests once meaning and interiority are discarded.
- [[The Means of Behavioral Modification]] — Big Other as the instrument through which the Other-One's viewpoint acts on behavior, not just observes it.
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — Harari's parallel reduction of the human to a behaving/computable organism; converges with the "organism among organisms" of the Other-One.
- [[Dataism]] — the data-religion worldview that takes the externalized, behaving human as the only knowable human.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's diagnosis of pervasive observation; the Other-One names the prior epistemology that makes such observation count as knowledge.
- [[A Utopia of Certainty]] — the social order (Skinner's *Walden Two*, surveillance capitalism's guaranteed outcomes) that the viewpoint of the Other-One underwrites.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-knowing interior person the Other-One is designed to dissolve; for Meyer and Skinner, "freedom" and "will" are illusions awaiting scientific dispatch.
- The "Our-Selves" / first-person inner world — Meyer's explicit contrast term: science studies "the Other-One in preference to Our-Selves," and "the world within the skin of the Other-One loses its preferred status."

## Questions It Raises

- If knowledge of behavior advances only by otherizing the human, is there any science of persons that does not begin by abolishing the person?
- Zuboff frames the bargain as "freedom for knowledge" — but the knowledge is *proprietary*; who gains the knowledge, and who loses the freedom, when the Other-One's viewpoint is privatized in [[Big Other]]?
- Does the [[Quantified Self|quantified self]] — voluntary, intimate self-tracking — invite us to take up the viewpoint of the Other-One toward ourselves, completing the otherizing that Meyer reserved for the scientist?
- Is "radical indifference" a softer or a deeper form of domination than the soul-engineering totalitarianism it replaces?
