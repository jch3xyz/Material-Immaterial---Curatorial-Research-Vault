# The Simulation Hypothesis

## Brief Definition

The metaphysical claim, formalized by the Oxford philosopher [[Nick Bostrom]], that the universe is most likely a computer program — a simulation run on a "hard drive" in the basement-level reality of posthuman programmers — which O'Gieblyn reads not as naturalism but as a secularized argument from design.

## Longer Explanation

The simulation hypothesis is Bostrom's 2003 proposal, in the paper "Are You Living in a Computer Simulation?", that the cosmos itself is a digital environment. Its structure is statistical and transhumanist: assuming our descendants reach a "posthuman" stage of "almost godlike mastery over the world," they will run vast numbers of conscious historical simulations of their ancestors, so that the simulated minds will far outnumber the one "basement-level" reality — making it "far more likely than not that we are currently living in one of these simulated worlds." O'Gieblyn is careful to mark this as a *metaphysical* claim about the substrate of the whole universe, distinct from a simulation merely *of* a mind: the universe here "was software written by programmers in the binary language of code." (`Directly stated`)

O'Gieblyn's core reading is that "for all its supposed 'naturalism,' the simulation hypothesis was ultimately an argument from design" — a member of "a long lineage of creationist rhetoric that invoked human technologies to argue that the universe could not have come about without the conscious intention of a designer." She situates it within Jessica Riskin's "theological mechanism," the eighteenth-century move (Paley, Boyle) that evacuated purpose from a disenchanted, clockwork world and outsourced it to a distant Engineer. The simulation does the same work for the modern problems of fine-tuning and the "unreasonable effectiveness" of mathematics: if the cosmos is an intentionally programmed computer, its mind-bogglingly exact physical constants "suddenly made sense — they were programmed into the software." (`Directly stated`)

The deeper claim of the note is that the hypothesis does not merely *resemble* religion but *recapitulates specific Christian doctrines* — it is, in Bostrom's own phrase, a "naturalistic theology." O'Gieblyn traces three recapitulations, several of which she derived herself while turning over "the many axioms and theodicies" of her fundamentalist upbringing. **Theodicy:** evil becomes a debugging problem — "sin and suffering were simply errors in the code that could not be corrected without significantly disrupting the system" (echoed in the online "simulation theodicy" she catalogs). **Incarnation:** "What was the incarnation but an allegory about God entering the simulation through his human avatar, Christ?" — Christ as a programmer's avatar descending into the rendered world. **Resurrection:** worlds nested within worlds turn the afterlife into a sequence of Cartesian "false awakenings," "one false awakening after another, until we reached enlightenment." (`Directly stated` for the quoted recapitulations; `Strongly implied` that these three together constitute a recapitulated theology.)

The hypothesis also re-grounds an otherwise free-floating informational metaphysics. A standard objection to the [[The Computational Universe|informational universe]] — voiced via Claude Shannon, and via Bernardo Kastrup's image of "the Cheshire Cat's grin without the cat" — is that [[Information]] "cannot be 'ungrounded,' without a material instantiation." Bostrom answers this by relocating the substrate: "if the universe were an enormous computer, then this information would in fact be instantiated on something material, akin to a hard drive" — invisible to us because it sits in the programmers' reality. Finally, the hypothesis offers a "compelling explanation for the measurement problem" by analogy to video games, where the programmer's mantra is "only render that which is being observed": the quantum world stays in superposition until a conscious player looks, and the wave function collapses on render. O'Gieblyn's verdict is that the theory's true appeal is anthropocentric — it lets us "believe once again that we were at the center of things" — and that, pushed to its conclusion, it "didn't really explain anything about the universe or its origins," only "pushed back one degree" the question of where the basement-level world came from. (`Directly stated`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — treats the hypothesis as a modern creation myth and an argument from design in naturalist disguise; her contribution is the structural mapping onto theodicy, incarnation, and resurrection, and the diagnosis that informational metaphysics here re-grounds itself on a cosmic hard drive.
- **[[Nick Bostrom]]** (forward-stub) — the originator; frames it as a probabilistic, transhumanist outgrowth of his work on superintelligence and [[Posthumanism]], and himself conceded the "loose analogies" to omnipotent, omniscient, resurrecting gods that could "produce its own brand of naturalistic theology."
- **Variant lineages** O'Gieblyn names: John Wheeler's "it from bit" participatory universe, Seth Lloyd's claim that the universe *is* a computer, and the cluster of "informational realism," "cosmic computationalism," and "digital physics."

## Related Concepts

- [[The Computational Universe]] — the simulation hypothesis is the strongest literalization of the universe-as-computer; it supplies the missing material substrate (the programmers' hard drive) that informational metaphysics otherwise lacks.
- [[Information]] — the "third substance" Wheeler and the digital-physics tradition posit to dissolve matter; the simulation grounds it rather than leaving it free-floating.
- [[Posthumanism]] — Bostrom's premise is that our descendants become superintelligent posthumans capable of running conscious simulations.
- [[Mind as Machine]] — the same computer metaphor that models the mind is scaled up to model the cosmos; O'Gieblyn warns the metaphor "easily slides into literalism."
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — the perceived "discernible patterns and mathematical regularities" of the cosmos are what the hypothesis claims to explain as programmed code.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — O'Gieblyn's critique runs against treating information as an ungrounded, immaterial essence; she sides with Shannon's insistence that information must be physically instantiated, which is precisely the pressure the simulation answers by literalizing the substrate.
- Bohr's reflexive interpretation of physics ("Physics is not about how the world is... it is about what we can say about the world") — an anti-objectivist, subject-bound view that O'Gieblyn opposes to the simulation's totalizing, third-person, "Archimedean point" ambition. ## Questions It Raises
