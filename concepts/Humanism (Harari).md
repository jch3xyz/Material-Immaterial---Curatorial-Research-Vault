# Humanism (Harari)

## Brief Definition

For Yuval Noah Harari, humanism is the dominant modern *religion* — the worship of humankind, which relocates the source of meaning and authority from an external God or cosmic plan to the internal feelings and experiences of *Homo sapiens*.

## Longer Explanation

Harari treats humanism not as a philosophy or an ethical stance but as the reigning religion of the last three centuries, and he defines religion functionally: "Religion is anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures" (`The Odd Couple`). On this definition liberalism, communism and Nazism are all religions, and humanism is the family to which they belong. Its founding move is a reversal: "Whereas traditionally the great cosmic plan gave meaning to the life of humans, humanism reverses the roles, and expects the experiences of humans to give meaning to the great cosmos" (`The Humanist Revolution`). Where theists "worship theos (Greek for 'god')," "humanists worship humans" (`The Anthropocene`). [Directly stated]

The historical hinge, for Harari, is not the loss of faith in God but the gain of faith in humanity. He argues that "the central religious revolution of modernity was not losing faith in God; rather, it was gaining faith in humanity" (`The Humanist Revolution`). Once meaning is relocated, authority follows it, because "Meaning and authority always go hand in hand" (`The Humanist Revolution`). The medieval woman who consulted a priest about an affair becomes the modern woman who consults her own feelings and her therapist; the priest's hotline to God is replaced by the inner voice. Harari compresses the result into five domain-slogans: in ethics, "if it feels good — do it"; in politics, "the voter knows best"; in economics, "the customer is always right"; in aesthetics, "beauty is in the eye of the beholder"; and in education, "think for yourself." [Directly stated]

This worship of humankind is, for Harari, the engine of the book's whole agenda. "For 300 years the world has been dominated by humanism, which sanctifies the life, happiness and power of Homo sapiens. The attempt to gain immortality, bliss and divinity merely takes the long-standing humanist ideals to their logical conclusion" (`The New Human Agenda`). The same creed that sacralizes human life and feeling also licenses the unlimited exploitation of everything that lacks "the sanctity of human nature": "humanism has justified modern industrial farming in the name of Man" (`The Anthropocene`). [Directly stated]

Crucially, humanism does not stand alone. Harari frames modernity as a "covenant" or "deal" between humanism and science: it "would accordingly be far more correct to view modern history as the process of formulating a deal between science and one particular religion — namely, humanism" (`The Odd Couple`). Science supplies power, humanism supplies meaning and the values that direct it; the deal is detailed in [[The Modern Covenant]]. Harari also stresses that humanism is not a single coherent creed: "As it spread and evolved, it fragmented into several conflicting sects." It "split into three main branches" — the orthodox liberal branch ([[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]]), [[Socialist Humanism (Harari)|socialist humanism]], and [[Evolutionary Humanism (Harari)|evolutionary humanism]] — which agree that "human experience is the supreme source of authority and meaning, yet they interpret human experience in different ways" (`The Humanist Revolution`). [Directly stated]

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — humanism is the modern religion that worships *Homo sapiens*, relocating meaning and authority from an external God to inner human experience; it sanctifies the life, happiness and power of humankind, partners with science in [[The Modern Covenant]], and fragments into three sects (liberal, socialist, evolutionary).
- This concept note is the **umbrella religion**. Its orthodox liberal sect — "liberal humanism or simply liberalism," holding that "the voter knows best," "the customer is always right," and that each individual is "a single authentic inner self" — is carried in [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]]. Do not collapse the two.

## Related Concepts

- [[Liberal Humanism (Harari)]] — the orthodox sect of this religion; the individualist branch that became "liberalism"
- [[Socialist Humanism (Harari)]] — the sect redirecting authority from one's own feelings to the experiences of all others ("the party knows best")
- [[Evolutionary Humanism (Harari)]] — the sect (Nazism its extreme) that treats conflict between human experiences as the raw material of natural selection
- [[The Modern Covenant]] — the deal between science (power) and humanism (meaning) that Harari makes the spine of modernity
- [[The Sanctity of Human Life]] — the supreme humanist value, "the worth of human life," that drives the war against death
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — Hayles's (and O'Gieblyn's) constructed subject-position; the autonomous, self-possessing individual who is precisely the *subject* of Harari's religion. Complementary, not identical — cross-link, do not merge.
- [[Disenchantment]] — O'Gieblyn/Weber lineage; Harari's "God is dead" relocation of authority "from the sky to human feelings" is the same emptying of the external cosmos
- [[Capital as Religion]] — Han's parallel use of "religion" as a functional, deifying social form
- [[Posthumanism]] — the post-humanist "deal" Harari predicts will replace the covenant of science and humanism

## Opposing Concepts

- **Theism / belief in a great cosmic plan** — the order humanism inverts: meaning and authority descend from God rather than rising from human experience ("While theists worship theos … humanists worship humans," `The Anthropocene`).
- [[The Sanctity of Human Life]] — listed under both related and opposing because, while it is humanism's own supreme value, Harari frames the book's later post-humanism as the force that will overturn the very sanctity humanism enthrones.

## Questions It Raises

- If meaning and authority both flow from inner human experience, how does one adjudicate between contradictory experiences? (Harari makes this the fault line that splits humanism into its three sects.)
- If humanism is defined functionally as a religion, what distinguishes it from the theism it claims to have replaced — order, power, or truth?
- What happens to the worship of humankind when the same technologies it licensed begin to dissolve the autonomous human subject it sanctifies — i.e., when [[The Modern Covenant]] gives way to a post-humanist deal?
- How does Harari's "religion that worships *Homo sapiens*" relate to [[The Liberal Humanist Subject|the liberal humanist subject]] as critiqued by Hayles and O'Gieblyn — is the religion's worshipper the same constructed self that the posthuman dissolves?
