# Theatrum Mundi

## Brief Definition

The world-as-theater: a public sphere structured like a stage, where selves communicate through masks, roles, ritual forms, and scenic distance rather than by exposing psychic interiority — the dramaturgical order of selfhood that the tyranny of intimacy and the demand for authenticity dismantle.

## Longer Explanation

For Han, the *theatrum mundi* names a lost configuration of the public sphere, which he locates historically in the eighteenth century: "The world of the eighteenth century was a theatrum mundi. The public sphere resembled a stage. Scenic distance hindered immediate contact between bodies and souls" (`Directly stated`). On this stage, communication runs "through ritual forms and signs," and "this unburdens the soul." The decisive point is that the theatrical mode is not opposed to expression but to *exhibition*: theatrical acts "are objective feelings and not manifestations of psychic interiority. Therefore they are represented and not exhibited." Han draws this frame directly from Richard Sennett's *The Fall of Public Man*, citing Sennett's claim "that theatricality has a special, hostile relation to intimacy; theatricality has an equally special, friendly relation to a strong public life" (`Directly stated`).

The concept is anchored in Han's etymology of the person: "'Person' (in Latin, persona) originally means 'mask.' It gives the voice sounding through it (per-sonare) character; indeed, it lends the voice shape and form" (`Directly stated`). The *theatrum mundi* is therefore a world of personae — of masks that give the voice form. This reads as a defense of [[The Mask]] and of symbolic appearance [Schein] as the medium of public life rather than a falsification of it (`Strongly implied`). The dramaturgical self is sociable precisely because it keeps distance: "human beings become sociable when they preserve distance from one another. Intimacy, in contrast, destroys distance" (`Directly stated`). Sociability here is not mutual self-disclosure but submission to objective rules — "Whenever one plays with others, one subordinates oneself to the rules of the game. The sociability of play is not based on mutual self-disclosure" (`Directly stated`). The theatrum mundi is thus the home of [[Pathos of Distance]], the scenic interval that makes play, ritual, and role possible.

What displaces the theatrum mundi is the society of intimacy, which converts the stage into a market: "The world today is no theater where actions and feelings are represented and interpreted, but a market on which intimacies are exhibited, sold, and consumed" (`Directly stated`). The transparency society, "as a society of revealing and denuding, ... works against all forms of the mask, against symbolic appearance [Schein]." Han ties this to a double erosion: "The mounting deritualization and denarrativization of society also strip it of forms of symbolic appearance and render it naked" (`Directly stated`) — connecting the loss of the theatrical order to the loss of [[Narrativity]]. It is best read as a connected argument (`Interpretive synthesis`) that the theatrum mundi is sustained by [[Negativity]] — the distance, the outside, the Other that ritual and mask preserve — and that its collapse follows from intimacy's elimination of that negativity: in the digital "space of absolute closeness," "the outside has been eliminated" and "no negativity stands available to make change possible."

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Treats *theatrum mundi* as the diagnostic foil for the transparency society: a public sphere of ritual distance, mask (persona), and representation, opposed term-for-term to the intimate "market" of exhibition, denuding, and "pornographic lack of distance." The concept lets Han argue that authenticity and intimacy do not deepen public life but abolish it (`Directly stated`).
- **[[Richard Sennett]]** *(forward-stub — originating thinker; no book yet in `raw/`)* — Source of the theatricality/intimacy frame via *The Fall of Public Man*. Han borrows Sennett's thesis on theatricality's "hostile relation to intimacy" and "friendly relation to a strong public life," and Sennett's account of the ability "to 'play with and invest feeling in external images of the self'" whose loss Sennett reads as "a fateful development" (`Directly stated`).

## Related Concepts

- [[The Mask]] — the persona (per-sonare) that gives the voice form; the symbolic appearance the theatrum mundi requires and the transparency society denudes.
- [[Pathos of Distance]] — the scenic distance and self-distantiation on which theatrical sociability and ritual play depend.
- [[Narrativity]] — the theatrum mundi falls together with narrative; "deritualization and denarrativization" strip symbolic appearance simultaneously.
- [[Negativity]] — the outside, friction, and Other preserved by ritual and mask; its elimination in the "space of absolute closeness" closes the stage.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Intimacy as Psychological Formula of Transparency]] — the antithetical order: exhibition of psychic interiority, denuding, and the destruction of distance that dismantles the theatrum mundi. Where the theater represents, the market of intimacy exhibits.

## Questions It Raises

- Embodiment / the body as data: Han says scenic distance "hindered immediate contact between bodies and souls" — when the mask falls and the soul is "laid bare," does the body become legible as data precisely because no theatrical distance any longer veils it? (Tie to [[Negativity]].)
- Visibility vs understanding: If theater is "a site of representation" and the market "a site of exhibition," does the move from representation to exhibition trade interpretability (a role to be read and understood) for sheer visibility (an intimacy to be consumed)?
- Desire: Han claims intimacy yields not eros but "the pornographic lack of distance." Does desire require the scenic distance of the theatrum mundi — the mask, the role, the Schein — such that the demand for authenticity extinguishes the very desire it claims to liberate?
- Systems / feedback: In the "space of absolute closeness," personalized media ensure "the outside has been eliminated" and "no negativity stands available to make change possible." Is the loss of the theatrum mundi the precondition for a closed, self-confirming feedback loop in which the subject "encounters oneself everywhere"?
- Embodiment / selfhood: If "person" means "mask," does abolishing the mask abolish the person — leaving the narcissistic subject whose "borders ... grow hazy" and for whom "no stable self-image emerges"?
