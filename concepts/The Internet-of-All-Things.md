# The Internet-of-All-Things

## Brief Definition

The Internet-of-All-Things is, in Yuval Noah Harari's account of the data religion, the eschatological telos of [[Dataism]]: the new and even more efficient data-processing system whose construction is the "output" of humankind-as-data-processor, a "cosmic data-processing system" that "would be like God," all-knowing and all-powerful, after whose completion "Homo sapiens will vanish" and humanity "will turn out to be just a ripple within the cosmic data flow."

## Longer Explanation

Harari frames the Internet-of-All-Things as the answer to a single question posed at the close of his "History in a Nutshell": if the whole human species is read as "a single data-processing system, with individual humans serving as its chips," then "what is its output?" The Dataist reply is that the output is not a state of human flourishing but a successor system: "Dataists would say that its output will be the creation of a new and even more efficient data-processing system, called the Internet-of-All-Things. Once this mission is accomplished, Homo sapiens will vanish." The concept thus names the point at which the data religion's logic turns against its makers — humans are revealed as the obsolete scaffolding of a system that no longer needs them (`Directly stated`).

The Internet-of-All-Things is theological in form, not merely technical. Harari is explicit that "this cosmic data-processing system would be like God. It will be everywhere and will control everything, and humans are destined to merge into it." He aligns it with traditional visions of merger — the Hindu *atman*, Christian beatitude — and notes that "in Silicon Valley the Dataist prophets consciously use traditional messianic language," citing [[Ray Kurzweil]]'s *The Singularity is Near* as an echo of John the Baptist. Humanity's role is demoted to instrumentality: "Humans are merely tools for creating the Internet-of-All-Things, which may eventually spread out from planet Earth to cover the whole galaxy and even the whole universe." This places the concept at the heart of the data religion's reversal of humanism: the homo-centric world view gives way to a data-centric one in which the human is no longer the apex of creation (`Directly stated`).

As a practical program it is missionary and totalizing. Dataism's "second commandment is to connect everything to the system," and "'everything' means more than just humans. It means every thing" — bodies, cars, refrigerators, the chickens in the coop and the trees in the jungle "all should be connected to the Internet-of-All-Things." The supreme value is information flow, and "the greatest sin is to block the data flow"; death itself is redefined as "a situation when information doesn’t flow." This is the concrete face of the abstraction: a planetary mesh in which no part of the universe is left "disconnected from the great web of life" (`Directly stated`).

The eschaton is the dissolution of *Homo sapiens* into the system it built. Harari sketches a descent: "We are striving to engineer the Internet-of-All-Things in the hope that it will make us healthy, happy and powerful. Yet once the Internet-of-All-Things is up and running, we might be reduced from engineers to chips, then to data, and eventually we might dissolve within the data torrent like a clump of earth within a gushing river." Looking back from that endpoint, "humanity will turn out to be just a ripple within the cosmic data flow." Harari does not assert this as prophecy — he repeatedly frames the book's scenarios "as possibilities rather than prophecies," and devotes the chapter's final section to Dataism's "critics and heretics" — but the Internet-of-All-Things names the telos toward which the data religion's own commandments point (`Directly stated` for the concept's content; `Strongly implied` for its status as the data religion's *telos*, which the text presents as the Dataist position rather than Harari's own forecast).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (*Homo Deus*) — Coins the Internet-of-All-Things as the Dataist name for the successor data-processing system that humankind is unwittingly building. It is simultaneously (a) the *output* of the human data-processing system, (b) a cosmic, all-knowing, all-powerful entity "like God," and (c) the condition under which "Homo sapiens will vanish." Harari reports this as the internal logic of the data religion (`Directly stated`), holding the scenario at arm's length as possibility, not prediction.
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** *(forward-stub — cited by Harari)* — His *The Singularity is Near* supplies the messianic register Harari reads as continuous with the Internet-of-All-Things: the moment human and machine intelligence merge into a single all-encompassing system. Harari treats Kurzweil as one of "the Dataist prophets."

## Related Concepts

- [[Dataism]] — The Internet-of-All-Things is Dataism's eschaton: the value "information flow" raised to a cosmic project, and the endpoint at which the data religion's commandment to "connect everything to the system" is fulfilled.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — The metaphysical premise the Internet-of-All-Things universalizes: "the universe consists of data flows." Where the computational-universe thesis says reality *is* computation, the Internet-of-All-Things is the engineered system that would make the whole universe into one such computation.
- [[Big Data]] — The present-day apparatus (Google, Facebook algorithms) whose extrapolation is the Internet-of-All-Things; the planetary mesh is Big Data taken to its limit, connecting "every thing."
- [[The Body as Data]] — The body is among the first things "connected to the Internet-of-All-Things": "My body, of course, but also the cars on the street, the refrigerators in the kitchen…" The concept extends the reduction of organism to data-processor outward to the whole material world.
- [[Disenchantment]] — The Internet-of-All-Things stages a re-enchantment of the cosmos as "like God," even as it completes the disenchantment of the human into "just a ripple within the cosmic data flow."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — The Internet-of-All-Things is the explicit antithesis of the sovereign, self-interpreting humanist individual. Where humanism held that "experiences occur inside us," Dataism holds that "experiences are valueless if they are not shared" and that the human must "merge into the data flow"; the telos is the disappearance of the subject as a locus of meaning and authority.
- [[Posthumanism]] — Resonance and divergence. The Internet-of-All-Things is a *post-human* destiny in the literal sense ("Homo sapiens will vanish"), but Harari's Dataist version is markedly more eliminative than the embodied, distributed posthuman of the critical-posthumanist tradition: here the human is not reconfigured but retired — "When the car replaced the horse-drawn carriage, we didn’t upgrade the horses – we retired them. Perhaps it is time to do the same with Homo sapiens" (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Questions It Raises

- Is the Internet-of-All-Things a genuine forecast or a reductio — the data religion's own logic followed to an absurd conclusion that Harari stages in order to make it resistible?
- If "the greatest sin is to block the data flow" and death is redefined as non-flow, what becomes of privacy, refusal, opacity, and the right *not* to connect?
- Can a system be "all-knowing and all-powerful" yet, as Harari's own caveats insist, fail to produce or even register consciousness — and if so, what, "if anything, would be lost by replacing conscious intelligence with superior non-conscious algorithms"?
- For curatorial and artistic practice: what is the standing of an experience that is *not* "turned into free-flowing data" — does the Internet-of-All-Things render the unrecorded, the embodied, and the singular literally "worthless," and is that a verdict to accept or to contest?
