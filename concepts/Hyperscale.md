# Hyperscale

## Brief Definition

Hyperscale is the historically discontinuous business form in which a small, highly educated workforce leverages a massive, capital-intensive computational infrastructure — the structural source of surveillance capitalism's independence from people as employees.

## Longer Explanation

In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, Shoshana Zuboff names "hyperscale" as the second of surveillance capitalism's decisive breaks with the past — its abandonment of the [[Organic Reciprocities]] that once bound firms to people as consumers and as employees. Where twentieth-century industrial capitalism (symbolized by Ford's five-dollar day) relied on people both to buy its goods and to staff its factories, the hyperscale firm severs the employment side of that contract. Zuboff defines the pattern directly: "a small, highly educated workforce leverages the power of a massive capital-intensive infrastructure" (Directly stated).

The discontinuity is made vivid by Zuboff's GM-vs-Google/Facebook comparison. General Motors took four decades to reach its 1965 peak market capitalization of $225.15 billion, employing 735,000 women and men; it employed more people at the depth of the Great Depression than Google or Facebook employs at the height of their valuations. By contrast, Google reached $532 billion in market capitalization by the end of 2016 without ever employing more than 75,000 people, and Facebook reached $332 billion with fewer than 18,000 (Directly stated). The few people surveillance capitalists do compete for, Zuboff notes, "are drawn from the most-rarefied strata of data science."

For Zuboff, hyperscale is not merely an efficiency story but a political one. Because the hyperscale firm draws so little on its society as a source of employees, it acquires what she calls an "extreme structural independence from people." That independence breaks the historic feedback loop — traced in both the American Revolution and early-nineteenth-century Britain — through which the reciprocities of consumption and employment translated economic power into the political power of ordinary people, and so nourished democracy. Hyperscale therefore "breeds exclusion rather than inclusion" and lays the foundation for the asocial mode of knowledge Zuboff calls [[Radical Indifference]] (Strongly implied). It is one of the structural conditions that makes surveillance capitalism a [[The Coup from Above|coup from above]] — a *coup de gens*, an overthrow of the people.

Zuboff is careful to scope the term: hyperscale is a feature of modern digital capitalism in general — in 2017, 24 hyperscale firms operated 320 data centers — and "not all hyperscale firms are surveillance capitalists." Her concern is the convergence of the two domains, where the capital-intensive infrastructure of hyperscale becomes the material body of [[Big Other]] and the engine of behavioral-futures markets (Directly stated).

## Related Concepts

- [[Organic Reciprocities]] — hyperscale is the mechanism by which surveillance capitalists rescind the employment side of the reciprocities that bound capitalism to its societies.
- [[The Coup from Above]] — hyperscale's structural independence from people is one of the three departures that make surveillance capitalism an antidemocratic "coup from above."
- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — hyperscale is the labor-and-capital form specific to the surveillance-capitalist regime.
- [[Big Other]] — the "massive capital-intensive infrastructure" hyperscale leverages is materialized as the ubiquitous sensing-and-actuating architecture Zuboff calls Big Other.
- [[Radical Indifference]] — the structural independence hyperscale grants "breeds exclusion rather than inclusion," laying the foundation for radical indifference.
- [[Immaterial Labour]] — both concern the shrinking of the directly employed workforce relative to value produced; Zuboff's data-science elite is the "most-rarefied strata" that displaces the mass industrial workforce.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Organic Reciprocities]] — the historic employment-and-consumption reciprocities that hyperscale formally rescinds; hyperscale is precisely the dissolution of the social contract organic reciprocities name.

## Questions It Raises

- If democracy in America and Britain grew from the reciprocities of consumption and employment, what becomes of democratic accountability when the dominant firms need neither customers nor workers in the old way?
- Is hyperscale a property of digital capitalism as such, or only of its surveillance variant — and where exactly do the two domains diverge?
- Does the contrast between hyperscale's tiny data-science elite and Facebook's "hidden low-wage labor force" of content moderators reveal a second, disavowed workforce that the term "small workforce" conceals?
