# Technology as Metaphor

## Brief Definition

The thesis that human self-understanding is always cast in the idiom of the era's reigning technology, so that to seek truth one must work *within* the technological metaphors of one's own time rather than nostalgically outside them — and the corresponding danger that such metaphors, once ubiquitous, are forgotten as metaphors and taken literally.

## Longer Explanation

This is the central method and spine concept of [[Meghan O'Gieblyn|O'Gieblyn]]'s *[[God Human Animal Machine]]*. She states the principle directly: "To discover truth, it is necessary to work within the metaphors of our own time, which are for the most part technological." Rather than treating analogy as a decorative or escapable feature of thought, O'Gieblyn treats it as the unavoidable medium of human knowing — a position she shares (warily) with [[Ray Kurzweil]], who wrote to her that "the answers to existential questions are necessarily metaphoric" because "we could not get through our lives without having to choose metaphors for transcendent questions." The historicizing move is that the *same* perennial questions — the mind's relationship to the body, free will, immortality — recur in each era under whatever figure that era's technology makes available: "All the eternal questions have become engineering problems." Across Chapter 4 she frames the early Christians, the medieval alchemists, and the luminaries of Silicon Valley as engaged in "a singular historical quest, one that was expressed through analogies that were native to each era."

The figure most specific to our age is [[Mind as Machine]] — the computational theory of mind first proposed by [[Warren McCulloch]] and Walter Pitts and later hardened into "the computer metaphor." O'Gieblyn tracks how it built on the meaning-stripped concept of [[Information]] that [[Claude Shannon]] gave to [[Cybernetics]], and how, decontextualized at the Macy Conferences (she cites [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] directly here), it inflated into the claim that the universe itself is an information processor — the move underlying [[The Computational Universe]]. The deep structural claim is that **metaphors are two-way streets**: "It is not so easy to distinguish the source domain from the target... For centuries we said we were made in God's image, when in truth we made him in ours." The same reversibility runs through mind-as-machine, which flips into machine-as-mind: "as we increasingly come to speak of our minds as computers, computers are now granted the status of minds."

The hazard O'Gieblyn most fears is the **dead metaphor**. A metaphor dies not only when its referent goes obsolete but "when it becomes so common that we forget it is a metaphor... its meaning is taken to be literal." She quotes George Lakoff on neuroscientists who "commonly use the Neural Computation metaphor without noticing that it is a metaphor," and Robert Epstein's finding that researchers literally could not describe human behavior without it. In Chapter 8 she names the literalized endpoint "scientific fundamentalism": "we run into trouble when we forget that these are metaphors and take them at face value — when we insist, for example, that... the universe is in fact an enormous computer. This brand of scientific fundamentalism can lead, ironically, back to the premises of religion."

Crucially, O'Gieblyn's prescription is *not* to abandon metaphor. Pressed in Sweden on which metaphor she herself subscribes to, she clarifies "that we should not reject all metaphors, only that we should recognize them for what they are: crude attempts to elucidate concepts that are still beyond our understanding." The aim is reflexive metaphor-literacy: inhabiting the technological idiom while refusing its dead, literalized forms. In Chapter 13 she extends the analysis from metaphor to ideology, noting that "the most successful metaphors become invisible through ubiquity. The same is true of ideology, which, as it becomes thoroughly integrated into a culture... dissolves finally into pure atmosphere" — the viral metaphor (memes, "bots," the body as a data set) becomes the unexamined air of the information age.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]** — the originating use here. Technology as metaphor is both her method (historicize every figure for the mind back to its era's machine) and her warning (the dead metaphor / "scientific fundamentalism"). Her specific figure is [[Mind as Machine]].
- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — O'Gieblyn draws directly on Hayles's account of how, at the Macy Conferences, [[Information]] "came to take precedence over materiality" and was made to seem "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates." Hayles supplies the historical mechanism by which the metaphor became literalized — the disembodiment of information that underwrites both [[Posthumanism]] and [[The Computational Universe]].
- **[[Ray Kurzweil]]** — agrees with O'Gieblyn that existential answers are "necessarily metaphoric" and that atheism vs. theism is "a matter of the choice of metaphor," but to opposite effect: for Kurzweil the metaphor licenses transcendence, where for O'Gieblyn it marks the limit of what we can know.

## Related Concepts

- [[Mind as Machine]] — the specific technological figure of which this concept is the general method; the case study O'Gieblyn returns to.
- [[The Computational Universe]] — what mind-as-machine becomes when the metaphor is scaled to the cosmos and taken literally ("scientific fundamentalism").
- [[Information]] — the Shannon redefinition (meaning stripped, then decoupled from matter) that makes the computer metaphor portable across every domain.
- [[Cybernetics]] — the discipline in which the computational analogy was generalized into "a universal metaphor."
- [[Posthumanism]] and [[The Body as Data]] — downstream of the literalized metaphor: the self recast as a "purely informational and immaterial" second self.
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] / [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — Hayles's terms O'Gieblyn leans on to show what the metaphor elides (embodiment, instantiation).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, disembodied "I" that the metaphors both inherit and dissolve; O'Gieblyn's reflexive, located point of view ("a necessary limitation") is posed against the Archimedean, metaphor-blind subject.
- [[Embodiment]] — the residue the metaphor cannot capture (the "hard problem," "what it is like," McCulloch's admission that computation was "an idealization of the mind, not a mirror of its embodied reality").

## Questions It Raises

- If all perception is "see as" and all knowing is metaphor, is there any standpoint from which to judge a metaphor adequate — or only more or less self-aware ones? (ties to **meaning** and **technology and perception**)
- When mind-as-machine flips into machine-as-mind, what licenses our attribution of personhood to the Aibo, the chatbot, GPT-3 — anthropomorphism, or a genuine collapse of the distinction? (ties to **posthumanism**, **technology and perception**)
- If the self becomes "a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes" that big data treats as "most valuable and real," has the metaphor of the body as data already been taken literally? (ties to **the body as data**)
- What is the difference between a live metaphor that "elucidates" and a dead one that becomes ideology — "pure atmosphere" — and can that line be policed once a culture has internalized it? (ties to **meaning**)
- Is O'Gieblyn's prescription — inhabit the technological idiom while refusing its literalization — actually livable, or does ubiquity guarantee the slide into "scientific fundamentalism"?
