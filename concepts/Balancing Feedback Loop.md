# Balancing Feedback Loop

## Brief Definition

The stabilizing, goal-seeking kind of feedback loop: a closed chain of causation that opposes whatever change is imposed on a stock and homes it toward a goal or an acceptable range — the thermostat holding a room's temperature, a hot cup of coffee cooling to room temperature — and so is at once a system's chief source of stability and its chief source of resistance to change.

## Longer Explanation

For [[Donella Meadows]] the balancing feedback loop is one of the two elementary building blocks of every system (its counterpart being the [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]]). A [[Feedback Loops|feedback loop]] in general is "formed when changes in a stock affect the flows into or out of that same stock"; what makes a loop *balancing* is the *direction* of that correction. Where a reinforcing loop amplifies change, "A balancing feedback loop opposes whatever direction of change is imposed on the system. If you push a stock too far up, a balancing loop will try to pull it back down. If you shove it too far down, a balancing loop will try to bring it back up" (`Directly stated`). The loop carries a goal — a desired stock level or range — and a corrective process that reads the discrepancy between the stock and the goal and adjusts flows to shrink it. Meadows names three worked examples in Chapter 1: the coffee drinker who tops up (or lays off) caffeine to hold "a desired stock level (energy for work)"; a cup of coffee that cools — or, as iced coffee, warms — until "the discrepancy between coffee's temperature and room's temperature" reaches zero; and, in Chapter 2, the thermostat-furnace system that tracks heat inflow to heat outflow to hold a room at temperature.

The signature of a balancing loop is what Meadows calls *homing* behavior: "Whatever the initial value of the system stock … whether it is above or below the 'goal' … the feedback loop brings it toward" the goal, with "The change … faster at first, and then slower, as the discrepancy between the stock and the goal decreases." This same behavior-over-time shape recurs across radically different substrates — "when a radioactive element decays, when a missile finds its target, when an asset depreciates, when a reservoir is brought up or down to its desired level, when your body adjusts its blood-sugar concentration, when you pull your car to a stop at a stoplight." The world, Meadows writes, "is full of goal-seeking feedback loops" (`Directly stated`).

Because it resists imposed change, the balancing loop is double-edged. Meadows states the point as a unit: "Balancing feedback loops are equilibrating or goal-seeking structures in systems and are both sources of stability and sources of resistance to change" (`Directly stated`). The very mechanism that lets a system mend injuries, hold a range, and shrug off disturbances is also what makes a system hard to move when one *wants* it to move — a symmetry that carries forward into her later account of leverage points, where a stubborn balancing loop can be the thing frustrating an intended intervention.

Two qualifications matter for using the concept precisely. First, a balancing loop's goal must be set to account for competing flows or the stock will settle at the wrong level: "A stock-maintaining balancing feedback loop must have its goal set appropriately to compensate for draining or inflowing processes that affect that stock. Otherwise, the feedback process will fall short of or exceed the target for the stock" (`Directly stated`). Second, having a balancing loop is no guarantee of stability. "The presence of a feedback mechanism doesn't necessarily mean that the mechanism works well" — feedback can arrive too late, be unclear or incomplete, or trigger an action "too weak or delayed or resourceconstrained or simply ineffective," so "The goal of the feedback loop may never be reached by the actual stock" (`Directly stated`). And every balancing loop "has its breakdown point, where other loops pull the stock away from its goal more strongly than it can pull back" — the point at which dominance shifts to a competing loop (see [[Shifting Dominance]]).

This note grounds, in Meadows's native systems-dynamics vocabulary, the mechanism the vault otherwise meets secondhand as [[Homeostasis]] — the equilibrium-seeking regulation Walter Cannon named for the body and [[N. Katherine Hayles]] carries into cybernetics. Meadows is describing the same stabilizing circuit at the level of the general system: negative, goal-seeking feedback is what *does* the homeostatic work (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — the umbrella concept; the balancing loop is one of its two elementary kinds (the corrective, goal-seeking kind). Meadows's native-discipline treatment lives under that note's Meadows section.
- [[Homeostasis]] — the equilibrium-seeking regulation of a body or system; balancing (negative) feedback is the general-systems mechanism that produces homeostatic behavior. This concept is its native-discipline parent.
- [[Shifting Dominance]] — a balancing loop can be overpowered by a competing loop at its "breakdown point," at which point which loop dominates the system's behavior shifts.
- [[Systems Theory]] — Meadows's systems-dynamics lineage (via Jay Forrester), in which stocks, flows, and balancing/reinforcing loops are the primitives.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] — the amplifying, self-multiplying loop that *enhances* whatever change is imposed, driving exponential growth or runaway collapse. The balancing loop's structural opposite: correction versus amplification, homing versus snowballing.

## Questions It Raises

- If a balancing loop is the source of both stability and resistance to change, when is its resistance a system's health (buffering disturbance) and when is it a system's pathology (frustrating a needed intervention)?
- How does a system decide *which* loop governs its behavior when balancing and reinforcing loops of differing strengths pull the same stock in opposite directions — and what determines a balancing loop's "breakdown point"?
- Is the vault's secondhand [[Homeostasis]] (Cannon via Hayles) fully reducible to negative/balancing feedback, or does the biological concept carry something the general-systems mechanism leaves out?
