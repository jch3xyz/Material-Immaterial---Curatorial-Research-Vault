# The Post-Biological Body

## Brief Definition

For [[Roy Ascott]], the post-biological body is the body that emerges when the networked erasure of the bounded Cartesian self leads *not* to disembodiment or immateriality but to **re-materialisation** — the reinvention of the human being as a bionic, prosthetic, transformable corporeality whose interface to computer systems is set within the brain, dissolving the boundary between "natural" and "artificial" technologically, conceptually, and spiritually.

## Longer Explanation

Ascott's distinctive move is to refuse the obvious conclusion. The telematic network appears to abolish the individual — "the sense of the individual is giving way to the sense of the interface" — and the self appears to leak "out of body, out of mind" into data space. One would expect this to end in pure immateriality: the disappearance of the body into information. Ascott explicitly denies it. The "erasure" of the Cartesian, bounded, isolated subject "leads, however, not to a disembodiment of the person, nor to an immateriality, but to the rematerialisation, the redescription, reconstruction— in short, the reinvention of the human being" (`Directly stated`). The dissolution of the old body is the precondition for a new corporeality, not for incorporeality. This makes the concept the anchor of Ascott's **re-materialisation pole** in the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension: immaterialisation of the *art object* and re-materialisation of the *body* are, for him, two phases of a single reversible movement.

The defining figure is **"the post-biological body as interface."** Ascott wants the systems interface not worn on the body (data glove, head-mounted display, data suit — all destined "for the museum") but installed within it: "We want the systems interface set within our brain. We want the boundaries between 'natural' and 'artificial' to be as redundant technologically as they are becoming conceptually and spiritually" (`Directly stated`). The brain-set interface is the technological realisation of a boundary-dissolution that has *already happened* conceptually and spiritually; the [[Cyberception (Ascott)|cyberceptive]] faculty Ascott names is itself "the postbiological faculty." The natural/artificial distinction collapses from three sides at once.

This body is bionic, prosthetic, and cybernetic in its self-conception. "We are all becoming, to a greater or lesser extent, bionic," and "Our prostheses not only amplify and extend the body and its five senses but also augment cognition and memory and subtly transform the personality" (`Directly stated`) — prosthesis here reaches past the senses into cognition and character. The body therefore ceases to be given and becomes a project: "our frustration with the limitations of our own bodies will demand prostheses and genetic intervention of a high order. We realise that the body, like our own identity, can be transformed, indeed must become transformable" (`Directly stated`). The governing disposition is no longer biological but engineering-minded: "Increasingly, the attitude of the mind towards the body is post-biological. Its view is cybernetic, seeking always the perfectibility of systems" (`Directly stated`). The body is treated as a system to be optimised, at the meeting point of [[Cyberception (Ascott)|cyberspace]] and [[Moistmedia (Ascott)|moistmedia]].

Two clarifications matter. First, Ascott's "post-biological" is *not* an exit from matter — it is a *redescription* of matter (bits, atoms, neurons, genes recombined), which is precisely why it belongs to the re-materialisation pole and not to a flight into pattern. Second, it is bound up with the dissolution of the unitary self: "we are now each of us made up of many individuals, a set of selves," so that "We are all interface. We are computer-mediated and computer-enhanced" (`Directly stated`). The post-biological body is the corporeal correlate of [[Network Consciousness|network consciousness]] — many selves, set within the net, perfectible.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the post-biological body as interface: the networked erasure of the Cartesian body re-materialises into a bionic, prosthetic, transformable corporeality whose interface is set within the brain; the natural/artificial boundary dissolves technologically, conceptually, and spiritually. The body is a cybernetic system "seeking always the perfectibility of systems." Cross-linked to [[Cyberception (Ascott)]], [[Moistmedia (Ascott)]], and [[Technoetics]] as the faculty, substrate, and knowledge-form of this body.

## Related Concepts

- [[Cyberception (Ascott)]] — Ascott names this "the postbiological faculty"; the post-biological body is the corporeality that exercises it.
- [[Moistmedia (Ascott)]] — the substrate (bits, atoms, neurons, genes) in which the post-biological body is materially recombined; "post-biological life" meets cyberspace in the moist.
- [[Technoetics]] — the "new knowledge of the world" and "new definitions of life and human identity" that the post-biological body is built to inhabit.
- [[The Telematic Embrace]] — the converged field of computing and telecommunications whose erasure of the bounded self triggers the re-materialisation.
- [[The Cyborg]] — Ascott's bionic, prosthetic, machine-merged body is an artist's-eye cyborg (cross-link to Haraway's organism/machine breach and to [[Cyborg Art]]).
- [[Embodiment]] — Ascott insists this is a *new embodiment*, not disembodiment, converging with Hayles's claim that there is only ever different embodiment.
- [[Transcending Biology]] — Kurzweil's parallel imperative to surpass "version 1.0 biological bodies"; Ascott's prosthesis/genetic-intervention demand resonates with [[Human Body Version 2.0]] and [[Nanobots]].
- [[Posthumanism]] — the broader horizon; Ascott's "set of selves" set within the net is the artist's version of the posthuman subject.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Dematerialization]] — the obvious but, for Ascott, mistaken conclusion: that networked art and selfhood end in the vanishing of the object/body. Ascott's body re-materialises; the dematerialisation runs on the side of the *art object*, not the body.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — Hayles's reminder that information is always instantiated in a medium; Ascott shares the anti-immateriality instinct but pushes it the opposite way, toward an *augmented* corporeality rather than a critique of disembodiment.

## Questions It Raises

- If the post-biological body is re-materialisation rather than immateriality, in what sense is it "post-biological" at all — is "post-biological" a claim about substance (bits/atoms/neurons/genes) or only about the *attitude* the mind takes toward the body?
- Ascott's body "must become transformable." Who specifies the transformation, and does the demand for "perfectibility of systems" reinstate exactly the optimisation logic that the vault's [[Self-Optimization]] and [[Achievement Society]] notes diagnose as coercive?
- Does setting "the systems interface within our brain" dissolve the natural/artificial boundary, or does it relocate the surveillance/control surface of the [[Digital Panopticon]] inside the body?
- Is Ascott right that erasure of the Cartesian self leads to re-materialisation rather than disembodiment — or does the "set of selves" dispersed across the net describe a disembodiment he is unwilling to name?
