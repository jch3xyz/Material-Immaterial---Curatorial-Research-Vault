# Dataism

## Brief Definition

Dataism is the new faith — announced by David Brooks riding Chris Anderson's "End of Theory" — that everything measurable must be measured, that data is a transparent and reliable lens, and that with enough data theory itself becomes superfluous; Byung-Chul Han reframes it as a "second Enlightenment" that is in fact an ideology, a "data totalitarianism" that culminates in nihilism because data are additive, not narrative.

## Longer Explanation

Han locates dataism in a journalistic moment: "In the pages of the New York Times, David Brooks has announced a data revolution. His words are as prophetic as Chris Anderson's famous article 'The End of Theory'. 'Dataism' is the name of the new faith." The creed itself, in Brooks's words, carries "certain cultural assumptions - that everything that can be measured should be measured; that data is a transparent and reliable lens that allows us to filter out emotionalism and ideology; that data will help us do remarkable things - like foretell the future." Dataism thus presents itself as the dissolution of bias: a frictionless access to the real that needs neither intuition nor theory (`Directly stated`).

Han's central move is to expose dataism's self-image as itself ideological. "Dataism has taken the stage with the fervour of a second Enlightenment," whose buzzword is transparency and whose imperative "declares: everything must become data and information." But "although it announces that it is taking leave of all ideology, dataism itself is an ideology. It is leading to digital totalitarianism." By a "fatal dialectic," the second Enlightenment repeats the first: just as reason "switched over into barbarism," data-driven illumination is "leading to the barbarism of data." Echoing Adorno and Horkheimer, Han reads dataism's "transparency" as "another name for myth" — "false clarity." This places Dataism squarely inside Han's broader critique of [[Transparency]] and of [[Positivity]] (a positivism that admits only the measurable and additive) (`Directly stated`).

The deepest charge is nihilism. "Dataism, it turns out, is amounting to digital Dadaism," because, like Dada, it "takes leave of meaningful contexts of every kind." The diagnosis is categorical: "Dataism is nihilism. It gives up on any and all meaning. Data and numbers are not narrative; they are additive. Meaning, on the other hand, is based on narration. Data simply fills up the senseless void." This is where Dataism connects directly to [[Narrativity]] and to the tension [[Narration vs Addition]]: numbers can be counted but not recounted, and "counting is not recounting." Against Anderson's "end of theory," Han insists (via Hegel) that "theoretical thinking represents a narrative form of knowledge" and that totalized, purely additive data-knowledge "amounts to absolute ignorance: the absolute zero of Spirit" (`Directly stated`).

Because dataism only ever lays bare statistical correlations — "what is likely in statistical terms" — Han argues it is structurally "blind to the event" and therefore "blind to the future too": "not what is statistically likely, but what is unlikely - the singular, the event - will shape history." Against the pretension to absolute knowledge, Han therefore calls for a corrective: "a third Enlightenment is called for - in order to shine a light on how digital enlightenment has transformed into a new kind of servitude" (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Names dataism a "second Enlightenment" and unmasks it as ideology, "data totalitarianism," "the barbarism of data," "digital Dadaism," and ultimately "nihilism." For Han the decisive fault is formal: data are additive where meaning is narrative, so a totalized data-knowledge is a totalized ignorance — "the absolute zero of Spirit." He distinguishes dataism (the faith/ideology) from the apparatus that realizes it; the latter is treated under [[Digital Panopticon]] and Big Data as Big Business / micro-targeting.
- **David Brooks** *(forward-stub — coiner of the term as Han cites him)* — Announces dataism as "the rising philosophy of the day," whose creed is that "everything that can be measured should be measured" and that "data is a transparent and reliable lens."
- **Chris Anderson** *(forward-stub)* — His "The End of Theory" supplies dataism's strongest claim: "With enough data, the numbers speak for themselves." Han reads this "end of theory" as "taking leave of Spirit."

## Related Concepts

- [[Information]] — Dataism is the faith that the world dissolves without remainder into data and information ("everything must become data and information"); it is the ideology of data positivism.
- [[Transparency]] — "transparency is the buzzword of the second Enlightenment"; dataism's "transparent and reliable lens" is, for Han, a renamed myth.
- [[Positivity]] — Dataism's exclusive valuation of the measurable, the additive, the correlational is a positivism that purges negativity, intuition, and theory.
- [[Narrativity]] — The pole dataism cannot supply: "Meaning… is based on narration." Self-knowledge "derives from giving an account," not from counting.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — The surveillance apparatus through which dataism is operationalized (the aperspectival, total-memory Big Data regime); Dataism is its governing ideology.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Narrativity]] — Direct opposition. Data "are additive"; meaning "is based on narration." Dataism "gives up on any and all meaning," whereas narration confers sense, conclusion, and the event. See the tension [[Narration vs Addition]].

## Questions It Raises

- If dataism is "blind to the event," can any data-driven practice (curatorial, political, scientific) anticipate the singular — or only the average?
- Is Han's "third Enlightenment" a recoverable program, or only a negative gesture naming what dataism forecloses?
- Where exactly is the line between dataism-as-ideology and the Big Data apparatus that enacts it — and does critiquing the faith leave the infrastructure untouched?
- Does the claim that "data are additive, not narrative" hold for machine-learning models that generate narrative-like outputs, or does Han's distinction predate and elude them?
