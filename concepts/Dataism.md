# Dataism

## General Orientation

"Dataism" names a single emerging worldview held by two of the vault's authors in different registers. The **word** was coined journalistically — David Brooks in the *New York Times*, riding Chris Anderson's "The End of Theory" — and it is in that coinage that **Byung-Chul Han** picks it up to *critique* it: for Han dataism is the ideology of a "second Enlightenment" that culminates in additive nihilism (see the Han section below). **Yuval Noah Harari** supplies the *worldview*: he does not coin the term but builds it out into a full cosmology and an emerging religion — Dataism holds that "the universe consists of data flows" and that the value of any entity is its contribution to data processing. The two senses are complementary, not contradictory: Brooks coined the word, Han weaponizes it as a target of critique, and Harari elaborates it into the "data religion" whose supreme value is information flow. The productive contrast to hold in mind: **Han diagnoses Dataism as nihilism** (additive, anti-narrative, "digital Dadaism"), whereas **Harari narrates it from the inside** as the coming data-centric faith that may sideline humans altogether (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Brief Definition

Dataism is the new faith — announced by David Brooks riding Chris Anderson's "End of Theory" — that everything measurable must be measured, that data is a transparent and reliable lens, and that with enough data theory itself becomes superfluous; Byung-Chul Han reframes it as a "second Enlightenment" that is in fact an ideology, a "data totalitarianism" that culminates in nihilism because data are additive, not narrative.

## Longer Explanation

Han locates dataism in a journalistic moment: "In the pages of the New York Times, David Brooks has announced a data revolution. His words are as prophetic as Chris Anderson's famous article 'The End of Theory'. 'Dataism' is the name of the new faith." The creed itself, in Brooks's words, carries "certain cultural assumptions - that everything that can be measured should be measured; that data is a transparent and reliable lens that allows us to filter out emotionalism and ideology; that data will help us do remarkable things - like foretell the future." Dataism thus presents itself as the dissolution of bias: a frictionless access to the real that needs neither intuition nor theory (`Directly stated`).

Han's central move is to expose dataism's self-image as itself ideological. "Dataism has taken the stage with the fervour of a second Enlightenment," whose buzzword is transparency and whose imperative "declares: everything must become data and information." But "although it announces that it is taking leave of all ideology, dataism itself is an ideology. It is leading to digital totalitarianism." By a "fatal dialectic," the second Enlightenment repeats the first: just as reason "switched over into barbarism," data-driven illumination is "leading to the barbarism of data." Echoing Adorno and Horkheimer, Han reads dataism's "transparency" as "another name for myth" — "false clarity." This places Dataism squarely inside Han's broader critique of [[Transparency]] and of [[Positivity]] (a positivism that admits only the measurable and additive) (`Directly stated`).

The deepest charge is nihilism. "Dataism, it turns out, is amounting to digital Dadaism," because, like Dada, it "takes leave of meaningful contexts of every kind." The diagnosis is categorical: "Dataism is nihilism. It gives up on any and all meaning. Data and numbers are not narrative; they are additive. Meaning, on the other hand, is based on narration. Data simply fills up the senseless void." This is where Dataism connects directly to [[Narrativity]] and to the tension [[Narration vs Addition]]: numbers can be counted but not recounted, and "counting is not recounting." Against Anderson's "end of theory," Han insists (via Hegel) that "theoretical thinking represents a narrative form of knowledge" and that totalized, purely additive data-knowledge "amounts to absolute ignorance: the absolute zero of Spirit" (`Directly stated`).

Because dataism only ever lays bare statistical correlations — "what is likely in statistical terms" — Han argues it is structurally "blind to the event" and therefore "blind to the future too": "not what is statistically likely, but what is unlikely - the singular, the event - will shape history." Against the pretension to absolute knowledge, Han therefore calls for a corrective: "a third Enlightenment is called for - in order to shine a light on how digital enlightenment has transformed into a new kind of servitude" (`Directly stated`).

## Dataism as the Data Religion (Harari)

Where Han meets the term as critic, Yuval Noah Harari builds it into a full cosmology. His foundational statement is ontological: "Dataism says that the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing." For Harari this is no fringe notion but "current scientific dogma," "born from the explosive confluence of two scientific tidal waves" — the post-Darwinian life sciences that "have come to see organisms as biochemical algorithms," and the post-Turing computer sciences that "have learned to engineer increasingly sophisticated electronic algorithms." Their fusion is the decisive move: "Dataism puts the two together, pointing out that exactly the same mathematical laws apply to both biochemical and electronic algorithms," and it "thereby collapses the barrier between animals and machines, and expects electronic algorithms to eventually decipher and outperform biochemical algorithms." This is the animal/machine barrier collapse — the engine of the worldview (`Directly stated`).

Crucially, Harari narrates Dataism's mutation from theory into faith — the move that makes it a *religion* rather than a paradigm. He first frames the field of contenders in [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/13 The Ocean of Consciousness]]: searching for what might "replace desires and experiences as the source of all meaning and authority," he concludes that "only one candidate is sitting in history's reception room" — information — and that "the most interesting emerging religion is Dataism, which venerates neither gods nor man – it worships data." (His working definition of religion, from [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/07 The Odd Couple]], is functional: "Religion is anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures" — which is precisely what Dataism does for information flow.) The mutation itself is stated outright: "Like capitalism, Dataism too began as a neutral scientific theory, but is now mutating into a religion that claims to determine right and wrong. The supreme value of this new religion is 'information flow'." Dataism even has "its practical commandments" (maximise data flow; connect everything to the Internet-of-All-Things; "the greatest sin is to block the data flow") and "its first martyr" in Aaron Swartz (`Directly stated`).

The keystone inversion is Harari's reading of what Dataism does to humanism. Humanism's "number one commandment" was "listen to yourself!" — "Whereas humanism commanded: 'Listen to your feelings!' Dataism now commands: 'Listen to the algorithms! They know how you feel'." This is the precise hinge: feelings were "the best algorithms in the world" for millions of years, but "in the twenty-first century, feelings are no longer the best algorithms," so "you should now stop listening to your feelings, and start listening to these external algorithms instead." The consequence is a displacement of humans as such: "In the eighteenth century, humanism sidelined God by shifting from a deo-centric to a homo-centric world view. In the twenty-first century, Dataism may sideline humans by shifting from a homo-centric to a data-centric view." Harari pushes this to its terminus — humanity as a means to a successor system: Dataism's output "will be the creation of a new and even more efficient data-processing system, called the Internet-of-All-Things. Once this mission is accomplished, Homo sapiens will vanish." Humans, on this telling, "are merely tools for creating the Internet-of-All-Things," and "looking back, humanity will turn out to be just a ripple within the cosmic data flow" (`Directly stated`).

This is the productive contrast with Han. Both treat Dataism as a quasi-religion that subordinates the human, but their valences and registers differ. Han indicts it as **additive nihilism** — "Dataism is nihilism… Data and numbers are not narrative; they are additive" — and calls for a "third Enlightenment" against it. Harari, by contrast, *narrates the worldview from within*, mostly without polemic, as "a tremendous religious revolution" that may "sideline humans"; his closing critique is epistemic and tentative ("it's doubtful whether life can really be reduced to data flows"; "maybe we'll discover that organisms aren't algorithms after all"), not Han's charge that meaning is being voided. Han's [[Narrativity]] / [[Narration vs Addition]] objection is therefore the sharpest available counter to Harari's data-centric cosmology: where Harari measures a symphony by "the data it carries rather than… the experience it creates," Han would answer that "counting is not recounting" (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Names dataism a "second Enlightenment" and unmasks it as ideology, "data totalitarianism," "the barbarism of data," "digital Dadaism," and ultimately "nihilism." For Han the decisive fault is formal: data are additive where meaning is narrative, so a totalized data-knowledge is a totalized ignorance — "the absolute zero of Spirit." He distinguishes dataism (the faith/ideology) from the apparatus that realizes it; the latter is treated under [[Digital Panopticon]] and Big Data as Big Business / micro-targeting. Han takes the *word* from Brooks/Anderson and turns it into a target of critique.
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — The worldview-builder. Harari does not coin the term but elaborates it into a complete cosmology and "the data religion": "the universe consists of data flows," and value is contribution to data processing. Born of the post-Darwin (organisms-as-algorithms) and post-Turing (engineered algorithms) fusion, Dataism "collapses the barrier between animals and machines"; it then mutates "from a neutral scientific theory" into "a religion" whose "supreme value is 'information flow'." Its keystone is the inversion of humanism — "Listen to your feelings!" becomes "Listen to the algorithms!" — and its endpoint is the sidelining of humans: the Internet-of-All-Things, after which "Homo sapiens will vanish." Harari narrates from inside the worldview (mostly non-polemically); his own doubts are epistemic, not Han's charge of voided meaning.
- **David Brooks** *(forward-stub — coiner of the term as Han cites him)* — Coined the *word* "Dataism," announcing it as "the rising philosophy of the day," whose creed is that "everything that can be measured should be measured" and that "data is a transparent and reliable lens." (Brooks coined the term; Harari built the worldview around it.)
- **Chris Anderson** *(forward-stub)* — His "The End of Theory" supplies dataism's strongest claim: "With enough data, the numbers speak for themselves." Han reads this "end of theory" as "taking leave of Spirit."

## Related Concepts

- [[Information]] — Dataism is the faith that the world dissolves without remainder into data and information ("everything must become data and information"); it is the ideology of data positivism.
- [[Transparency]] — "transparency is the buzzword of the second Enlightenment"; dataism's "transparent and reliable lens" is, for Han, a renamed myth.
- [[Positivity]] — Dataism's exclusive valuation of the measurable, the additive, the correlational is a positivism that purges negativity, intuition, and theory.
- [[Narrativity]] — The pole dataism cannot supply: "Meaning… is based on narration." Self-knowledge "derives from giving an account," not from counting.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — The surveillance apparatus through which dataism is operationalized (the aperspectival, total-memory Big Data regime); Dataism is its governing ideology.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Narrativity]] — Direct opposition. Data "are additive"; meaning "is based on narration." Dataism "gives up on any and all meaning," whereas narration confers sense, conclusion, and the event. See the tension [[Narration vs Addition]].

## Questions It Raises

- If dataism is "blind to the event," can any data-driven practice (curatorial, political, scientific) anticipate the singular — or only the average?
- Is Han's "third Enlightenment" a recoverable program, or only a negative gesture naming what dataism forecloses?
- Where exactly is the line between dataism-as-ideology and the Big Data apparatus that enacts it — and does critiquing the faith leave the infrastructure untouched?
- Does the claim that "data are additive, not narrative" hold for machine-learning models that generate narrative-like outputs, or does Han's distinction predate and elude them?
