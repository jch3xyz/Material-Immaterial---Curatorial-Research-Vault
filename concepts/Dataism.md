# Dataism

## General Orientation

"Dataism" names a single emerging worldview that the vault's sources approach from three distinct registers — critique, cosmology, and political economy. The **word** was coined journalistically: David Brooks in the *New York Times*, riding Chris Anderson's "The End of Theory," announced "the new faith." From that coinage the vault's accounts fan out along their stances toward it.

One stance **diagnoses Dataism as ideology and nihilism**. [[Byung-Chul Han]] picks up the term to weaponize it: dataism is a "second Enlightenment" that, by a "fatal dialectic," repeats the first and culminates in "the barbarism of data" — "data totalitarianism," "digital Dadaism," and finally "nihilism," because data are additive where meaning is narrative. This is where Dataism connects to Han's broader critique of [[Transparency]] and [[Positivity]], and to the tension [[Narration vs Addition]]: "counting is not recounting."

A second stance **narrates Dataism from inside, as cosmology and religion**. [[Yuval Noah Harari]] does not coin the term but builds it into a full worldview: "the universe consists of data flows," and the value of any entity is its contribution to data processing. Born of the post-Darwin and post-Turing fusion, it "collapses the barrier between animals and machines" and mutates "from a neutral scientific theory" into "a religion" whose "supreme value is 'information flow'" — its keystone the inversion of humanism ("Listen to your feelings!" becomes "Listen to the algorithms!"), its terminus the sidelining of humans in the Internet-of-All-Things.

A third stance **exposes Dataism's political economy** — who owns the data flow and for whose ends. [[Shoshana Zuboff]] supplies the institutional machinery: the rendering of all experience into "behavioral surplus," captured by "a narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists" who command the division of learning in society. The data religion's dream of total prediction becomes, in her account, a "market project of total certainty" pursued through the apparatus of Big Other and the "radical indifference" of instrumentarian power.

The stances are complementary, not contradictory, and they converge on one charge: the voiding of meaning. Han indicts Dataism as **additive nihilism** and calls for a "third Enlightenment"; Harari **narrates the worldview from within**, his own doubts epistemic rather than moral ("it's doubtful whether life can really be reduced to data flows"); Zuboff adds the **ownership question** the others leave implicit. The productive contrast to hold in mind: where Harari measures a symphony by "the data it carries rather than… the experience it creates," Han would answer that meaning "is based on narration," and Zuboff would ask who profits from the worship of data (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Brief Definition

Dataism is the new faith — announced by David Brooks riding Chris Anderson's "End of Theory" — that everything measurable must be measured, that data is a transparent and reliable lens, and that with enough data theory itself becomes superfluous; Byung-Chul Han reframes it as a "second Enlightenment" that is in fact an ideology, a "data totalitarianism" that culminates in nihilism because data are additive, not narrative.

## Longer Explanation

Han locates dataism in a journalistic moment: "In the pages of the New York Times, David Brooks has announced a data revolution. His words are as prophetic as Chris Anderson's famous article 'The End of Theory'. 'Dataism' is the name of the new faith." The creed itself, in Brooks's words, carries "certain cultural assumptions - that everything that can be measured should be measured; that data is a transparent and reliable lens that allows us to filter out emotionalism and ideology; that data will help us do remarkable things - like foretell the future." Dataism thus presents itself as the dissolution of bias: a frictionless access to the real that needs neither intuition nor theory (`Directly stated`).

Han's central move is to expose dataism's self-image as itself ideological. "Dataism has taken the stage with the fervour of a second Enlightenment," whose buzzword is transparency and whose imperative "declares: everything must become data and information." But "although it announces that it is taking leave of all ideology, dataism itself is an ideology. It is leading to digital totalitarianism." By a "fatal dialectic," the second Enlightenment repeats the first: just as reason "switched over into barbarism," data-driven illumination is "leading to the barbarism of data." Echoing Adorno and Horkheimer, Han reads dataism's "transparency" as "another name for myth" — "false clarity." This places Dataism squarely inside Han's broader critique of [[Transparency]] and of [[Positivity]] (a positivism that admits only the measurable and additive) (`Directly stated`).

The deepest charge is nihilism. "Dataism, it turns out, is amounting to digital Dadaism," because, like Dada, it "takes leave of meaningful contexts of every kind." The diagnosis is categorical: "Dataism is nihilism. It gives up on any and all meaning. Data and numbers are not narrative; they are additive. Meaning, on the other hand, is based on narration. Data simply fills up the senseless void." This is where Dataism connects directly to [[Narrativity]] and to the tension [[Narration vs Addition]]: numbers can be counted but not recounted, and "counting is not recounting." Against Anderson's "end of theory," Han insists (via Hegel) that "theoretical thinking represents a narrative form of knowledge" and that totalized, purely additive data-knowledge "amounts to absolute ignorance: the absolute zero of Spirit" (`Directly stated`).

Because dataism only ever lays bare statistical correlations — "what is likely in statistical terms" — Han argues it is structurally "blind to the event" and therefore "blind to the future too": "not what is statistically likely, but what is unlikely - the singular, the event - will shape history." Against the pretension to absolute knowledge, Han therefore calls for a corrective: "a third Enlightenment is called for - in order to shine a light on how digital enlightenment has transformed into a new kind of servitude" (`Directly stated`).

## Dataism as the Data Religion (Harari)

Where Han meets the term as critic, Yuval Noah Harari builds it into a full cosmology. His foundational statement is ontological: "Dataism says that the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing." For Harari this is no fringe notion but "current scientific dogma," "born from the explosive confluence of two scientific tidal waves" — the post-Darwinian life sciences that "have come to see organisms as biochemical algorithms," and the post-Turing computer sciences that "have learned to engineer increasingly sophisticated electronic algorithms." Their fusion is the decisive move: "Dataism puts the two together, pointing out that exactly the same mathematical laws apply to both biochemical and electronic algorithms," and it "thereby collapses the barrier between animals and machines, and expects electronic algorithms to eventually decipher and outperform biochemical algorithms." This is the animal/machine barrier collapse — the engine of the worldview (`Directly stated`).

Crucially, Harari narrates Dataism's mutation from theory into faith — the move that makes it a *religion* rather than a paradigm. He first frames the field of contenders in [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/13 The Ocean of Consciousness]]: searching for what might "replace desires and experiences as the source of all meaning and authority," he concludes that "only one candidate is sitting in history's reception room" — information — and that "the most interesting emerging religion is Dataism, which venerates neither gods nor man – it worships data." (His working definition of religion, from [[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/07 The Odd Couple]], is functional: "Religion is anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures" — which is precisely what Dataism does for information flow.) The mutation itself is stated outright: "Like capitalism, Dataism too began as a neutral scientific theory, but is now mutating into a religion that claims to determine right and wrong. The supreme value of this new religion is 'information flow'." Dataism even has "its practical commandments" (maximise data flow; connect everything to the Internet-of-All-Things; "the greatest sin is to block the data flow") and "its first martyr" in Aaron Swartz (`Directly stated`).

The keystone inversion is Harari's reading of what Dataism does to humanism. Humanism's "number one commandment" was "listen to yourself!" — "Whereas humanism commanded: 'Listen to your feelings!' Dataism now commands: 'Listen to the algorithms! They know how you feel'." This is the precise hinge: feelings were "the best algorithms in the world" for millions of years, but "in the twenty-first century, feelings are no longer the best algorithms," so "you should now stop listening to your feelings, and start listening to these external algorithms instead." The consequence is a displacement of humans as such: "In the eighteenth century, humanism sidelined God by shifting from a deo-centric to a homo-centric world view. In the twenty-first century, Dataism may sideline humans by shifting from a homo-centric to a data-centric view." Harari pushes this to its terminus — humanity as a means to a successor system: Dataism's output "will be the creation of a new and even more efficient data-processing system, called the Internet-of-All-Things. Once this mission is accomplished, Homo sapiens will vanish." Humans, on this telling, "are merely tools for creating the Internet-of-All-Things," and "looking back, humanity will turn out to be just a ripple within the cosmic data flow" (`Directly stated`).

This is the productive contrast with Han. Both treat Dataism as a quasi-religion that subordinates the human, but their valences and registers differ. Han indicts it as **additive nihilism** — "Dataism is nihilism… Data and numbers are not narrative; they are additive" — and calls for a "third Enlightenment" against it. Harari, by contrast, *narrates the worldview from within*, mostly without polemic, as "a tremendous religious revolution" that may "sideline humans"; his closing critique is epistemic and tentative ("it's doubtful whether life can really be reduced to data flows"; "maybe we'll discover that organisms aren't algorithms after all"), not Han's charge that meaning is being voided. Han's [[Narrativity]] / [[Narration vs Addition]] objection is therefore the sharpest available counter to Harari's data-centric cosmology: where Harari measures a symphony by "the data it carries rather than… the experience it creates," Han would answer that "counting is not recounting" (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Shoshana Zuboff (The Age of Surveillance Capitalism)

If Han diagnoses Dataism as ideology and Harari narrates it as cosmology, **Shoshana Zuboff** supplies its *political economy* — the institutional machinery and the form of power that turn the data-flow worldview into a going concern. For Zuboff the rendering of all experience into data is not a free-floating faith but the founding operation of a new "logic of accumulation": surveillance capitalism's "shadow text" is "a burgeoning accumulation of behavioral surplus and its analyses, and it says more about us than we can know about ourselves." Where Harari's Dataists declare that "the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing," Zuboff names who profits from that valuation: the rendering of "both the world and our lives… pervasively rendered as information" is captured by "a narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists, their privately owned machines, and the economic interests for whose sake they learn." This "New Priesthood" is the concrete counterpart to Harari's "data religion" — a literal caste hoarding sacred knowledge, reverting "the pre-Gutenberg order" (`Directly stated`).

Zuboff also gives the data-flow imperative its endgame: not merely the worship of data but a "market project of total certainty." Through the ubiquitous apparatus she calls **Big Other** — "the sensate, computational, connected puppet that renders, monitors, computes, and modifies human behavior" — surveillance capital aims at "guaranteed outcomes," "the substitution of machines for social relations, which amounts to the substitution of certainty for society." This is the surveillance-economic face of what Harari calls the Internet-of-All-Things and of Han's warning that Big Data is "blind to the event": the dream of total prediction is here driven by competitive economic imperative rather than cosmic destiny. The productive contrast within this concept: Han indicts Dataism as additive nihilism, Harari narrates it as faith, and Zuboff exposes its *radical indifference* — instrumentarian power "reduces human experience to measurable observable behavior while remaining steadfastly indifferent to the meaning of that experience." All three converge on the voiding of meaning; Zuboff adds the ownership question — who commands the data flow, and for whose ends (`Directly stated`).

## Donella Meadows (Thinking in Systems)

Where Han, Harari, and Zuboff all approach Dataism as a *new* condition — an ideology, a cosmology, a logic of accumulation — [[Donella Meadows]] supplies the anti-quantification critique in its *native systems-discipline* form, and dates it not to Big Data but to a much older cultural habit. In her closing "systems wisdom" she names the measurement-worship that Dataism raises to a creed as a plain distortion of judgment: "Our culture, obsessed with numbers, has given us the idea that what we can measure is more important than what we can't measure." The systems consequence is precise and mechanical rather than merely moral: because the quantifiable is what gets built into the goals of feedback loops, a numbers-obsessed culture will "make quantity more important than quality," and the goals set around what can be counted will yield quantity at the expense of the values that cannot. This is, in effect, the [[What we can measure is not what is important]] argument stated from inside cybernetics — the discipline Dataism claims as its own — turned back against the reduction of value to measurable data (`Directly stated`).

Meadows's sharpest point is the fate of the unmeasurable. Against the "if you can't define it and measure it, I don't have to pay attention to it" ploy, she insists that the values that matter most are exactly the ones no metric captures — and that this immunity to measurement is not their weakness but the reason they need active defenders: "No one can define or measure any value. But if no one speaks up for them, if systems aren't designed to produce them, if we don't speak about them and point toward their presence or absence, they will cease to exist." Read beside Dataism, this is the systems-native counterpart to Han's charge that data "fills up the senseless void" and to Harari's account of a worldview that measures a symphony by "the data it carries" rather than the experience it creates: the very measurement-worship Dataism celebrates is, for Meadows, the mechanism by which a system quietly extinguishes what it cannot quantify. The bridge from her feedback-loop critique to the vault's Dataism / [[Quantified Self]] / [[Big Data]] / [[The Body as Data]] cluster is drawn here as `Interpretive synthesis`; Meadows's own claims are `Directly stated`.

## Byung-Chul Han (The Agony of Eros)

In the final chapter of *The Agony of Eros* — titled, after Chris Anderson's 2008 *Wired* essay, "The End of Theory" — Han extends the Dataism critique from *Psychopolitics* into an epistemology of thought itself, and supplies the sharpest formulation of its core distinction: data-driven positive science is *calculation*, not *thinking*. The pivot is a single sentence: "There is no such thing as data-driven thinking. Only calculation is data driven. The negativity of the incalculable is inscribed in thinking." Data — "which means 'things given'" — is a positivity, whereas thinking is grounded in a negativity that is "prior and superordinate to 'data'": theory "transcends the positivity of given facts and makes them suddenly appear in a new light." What Dataism structurally lacks, on this account, is not more data but the negativity of the incalculable — the resistance of what Han elsewhere in the book calls the atopic Other. This ties the *Agony of Eros* eros/Other vocabulary directly to the *Psychopolitics* Dataism/Big Data critique: where Big Data reproduces the Same, only the negativity of the Other breaks it (`Directly stated`).

Han's target is Anderson's claim that abundant data makes theory — even causality — superfluous ("With enough data, the numbers speak for themselves"; "correlation is more important than causality"). Han's answer is that this rests on "a weak and simplistic conception of theory," and that what Anderson celebrates as the end of theory is in fact the end of theory *of the emphatic sort*: "Data-based, positive science ('Google science'), which amounts to merely balancing out and comparing data, is putting an end to theory of the emphatic sort. It is additive or detective—not narrative or hermeneutic. No narrative tension animates it." This is the same charge that anchors the *Psychopolitics* section above — that data are additive where meaning is narrative — now grounded in a primary-text account of theory: prizing correlation over causality, Google science "falls apart into mere information." The claim connects Dataism to [[Narrativity]] ("not narrative or hermeneutic") and to [[Negativity]] and the [[Hell of the Same]] — calculation, "work on the positive," "always reproduces the Same," because "without negativity, only the Same exists" (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Names dataism a "second Enlightenment" and unmasks it as ideology, "data totalitarianism," "the barbarism of data," "digital Dadaism," and ultimately "nihilism." For Han the decisive fault is formal: data are additive where meaning is narrative, so a totalized data-knowledge is a totalized ignorance — "the absolute zero of Spirit." He distinguishes dataism (the faith/ideology) from the apparatus that realizes it; the latter is treated under [[Digital Panopticon]] and Big Data as Big Business / micro-targeting. Han takes the *word* from Brooks/Anderson and turns it into a target of critique, calling for a "third Enlightenment" against it. In *The Agony of Eros* (ch. 8, "The End of Theory") he sharpens the critique into an epistemology: "There is no such thing as data-driven thinking. Only calculation is data driven." Data-driven positive science — "Google science" — "merely balancing out and comparing data," prizing correlation over causality, is "putting an end to theory of the emphatic sort"; it is "additive or detective—not narrative or hermeneutic," and what it structurally lacks is the negativity of the incalculable / the atopic Other, so it "always reproduces the Same."
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — The worldview-builder. Harari does not coin the term but elaborates it into a complete cosmology and "the data religion": "the universe consists of data flows," and value is contribution to data processing. Born of the post-Darwin (organisms-as-algorithms) and post-Turing (engineered algorithms) fusion, Dataism "collapses the barrier between animals and machines"; it then mutates "from a neutral scientific theory" into "a religion" whose "supreme value is 'information flow'." Its keystone is the inversion of humanism — "Listen to your feelings!" becomes "Listen to the algorithms!" — and its endpoint is the sidelining of humans: the Internet-of-All-Things, after which "Homo sapiens will vanish." Harari narrates from inside the worldview (mostly non-polemically); his doubts are epistemic, not the charge of voided meaning.
- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — The political economist of Dataism. Zuboff supplies the institutional machinery behind the data-flow worldview: the rendering of all experience into "behavioral surplus," captured by a "narrow priesthood of privately employed computational specialists" who command the "division of learning in society." The data religion's dream of total prediction becomes, in her account, a "market project of total certainty" pursued through the apparatus of Big Other and the "radical indifference" of instrumentarian power — a "way of knowing" that registers measurable behavior while remaining "indifferent to the meaning of that experience." Zuboff's contribution to the concept is the *ownership* and *power* question Han and Harari leave implicit: who profits from the worship of data.
- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — The native-systems counterpoint. Meadows supplies the anti-quantification critique from inside cybernetics itself, the discipline Dataism claims: our numbers-obsessed culture "has given us the idea that what we can measure is more important than what we can't measure," so goals set around the quantifiable make "quantity more important than quality" and produce quantity at the expense of value. Her sharpest move concerns the unmeasurable — "No one can define or measure any value" — and its consequence: values not spoken for and not designed into systems "will cease to exist." Where Han, Harari, and Zuboff diagnose Dataism as new, Meadows shows the measurement-worship it celebrates to be a long-standing systems pathology.
- **David Brooks** *(forward-stub — coiner of the term as Han cites him)* — Coined the *word* "Dataism," announcing it as "the rising philosophy of the day," whose creed is that "everything that can be measured should be measured" and that "data is a transparent and reliable lens." (Brooks coined the term; Harari built the worldview.)
- **Chris Anderson** *(forward-stub)* — His "The End of Theory" supplies dataism's strongest claim: "With enough data, the numbers speak for themselves." Han reads this "end of theory" as "taking leave of Spirit," and in *The Agony of Eros* refutes it directly: Anderson's thesis "rests on a weak and simplistic conception of theory," and "Google science" ends only theory *of the emphatic sort*.

## Related Concepts

- [[Information]] — Dataism is the faith that the world dissolves without remainder into data and information ("everything must become data and information"); it is the ideology of data positivism. Zuboff's "both the world and our lives are pervasively rendered as information" is its surveillance-economic form.
- [[Transparency]] — "transparency is the buzzword of the second Enlightenment"; dataism's "transparent and reliable lens" is, for Han, a renamed myth.
- [[Positivity]] — Dataism's exclusive valuation of the measurable, the additive, the correlational is a positivism that purges negativity, intuition, and theory.
- [[Negativity]] — What Dataism structurally lacks: "the negativity of the incalculable is inscribed in thinking," and calculation, "work on the positive," "always reproduces the Same" because "without negativity, only the Same exists."
- [[Narrativity]] — The pole dataism cannot supply: "Meaning… is based on narration." Self-knowledge "derives from giving an account," not from counting. "Google science" is "additive or detective—not narrative or hermeneutic."
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — The surveillance apparatus through which dataism is operationalized (the aperspectival, total-memory Big Data regime); Dataism is its governing ideology. Zuboff's Big Other is the economic-logic account of the same apparatus.
- [[Hell of the Same]] — The noological fate of calculation: data-driven "work on the positive" "always reproduces the Same"; only the negativity of the atopic Other breaks it.
- [[What we can measure is not what is important]] — Meadows's systems-discipline statement of the anti-quantification critique: goals set around the quantifiable yield quantity at the expense of value, and unmeasured values "cease to exist." The native counter to Dataism's reduction of worth to data.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Narrativity]] — Direct opposition. Data "are additive"; meaning "is based on narration." Dataism "gives up on any and all meaning," whereas narration confers sense, conclusion, and the event. See the tension [[Narration vs Addition]].

## Questions It Raises

- If dataism is "blind to the event," can any data-driven practice (curatorial, political, scientific) anticipate the singular — or only the average?
- Is Han's "third Enlightenment" a recoverable program, or only a negative gesture naming what dataism forecloses?
- Where exactly is the line between dataism-as-ideology and the Big Data apparatus that enacts it — and does critiquing the faith leave the infrastructure untouched? (Zuboff's answer: the faith is downstream of ownership — to contest Dataism is to contest the political economy that profits from it.)
- Does the claim that "data are additive, not narrative" hold for machine-learning models that generate narrative-like outputs, or does Han's distinction predate and elude them?
- If Harari narrates Dataism as cosmic destiny and Zuboff exposes it as a "logic of accumulation," is the data religion inevitable or merely profitable?
- If, as Meadows warns, values not designed into systems "will cease to exist," does a culture that has made data its faith foreclose the quality it can no longer measure — and can a curatorial or political practice "speak up for" the unquantifiable inside a Dataist infrastructure?
- If, as Han argues in *The Agony of Eros*, "only calculation is data driven" and genuine thinking requires "the negativity of the incalculable," is a Dataist culture that mistakes calculation for thought structurally incapable of the theory it claims to have superseded?
