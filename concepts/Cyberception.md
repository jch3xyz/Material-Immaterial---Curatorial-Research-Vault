# Cyberception

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's name for a "post-biological faculty" — the convergence of perceptual and conceptual processes enabled by telematic connectivity, an all-at-once, pattern-and-system-seeing awareness that supersedes the linear, object-dividing perception of the age of reason and lets us perceive immaterial process as palpably as material objects.

## Longer Explanation

Cyberception is the keystone term of Ascott's 1990s "third phase," the move from the telematic art of the 1980s into [[Technoetics|technoetics]] and the post-biological body. He coins it as a *qualitative*, not quantitative, change in human being: not a faster or sharper version of the senses but "a whole new faculty," produced when the human sensorium fuses with [[The Cybernet|the cybernet]] — the planetary sum of interactive computer-mediated systems treated as part of our sensory apparatus. Where ordinary perception is "the awareness of the elements of environment through physical sensation," cyberception folds perception into *conception* (the originating and forming of ideas through the interaction of minds), so that the two processes become a single, telematically-amplified awareness. Ascott glosses it most economically in chapter 29 as "the technologically amplified faculties of perception and cognition," and in his "100 Terms" lexicon (chapter 32) as "the emergent human faculty of technologically augmented cognition and perception." *(Directly stated.)*

Its defining behaviour is whole-seeing: "an all-at-once perception of a multiplicity of viewpoints," "a bird's-eye view of events, the astronaut's view of the earth, the cybernaut's view of systems." This is "the antithesis of tunnel vision or linear thought" — the perceptual habit of the "age of reason, the age of certainty, determinism, and absolute values," which Ascott also calls "the age of appearance," in which objects and categories are ranged "behind one another" and divided. Crucially, Ascott frames cyberception "not so much a new faculty as a revived faculty," "us finding ourselves again" after the losses of mechanistic modernity — a return to a pattern- and process-seeing awareness the Tao and the Book of Changes once carried. *(Directly stated.)*

The faculty's most load-bearing claim for this vault is that it makes the immaterial perceptible: "We cyberceive transformative relationships and connectivity as immaterial process just as palpably and immediately as we commonly perceive material objects in material locations" — Ascott's strongest single statement on the [[Material vs Immaterial]] spine, anchoring his author-section in that tension. Cyberception thus lets us apprehend connectivity, system, [[Emergence|emergence]], and [[Apparition|apparition]] (the coming-into-being of the new) as directly as we apprehend things; it provides, he says, "X-ray vision and the optics of outer space," fulfilling the twentieth-century project of making the invisible visible. *(Directly stated for the immaterial-process claim; interpretive synthesis for the framing as the tension anchor.)*

Cyberception is also transpersonal and participatory, never merely receptive. It "heightens transpersonal experience and is the defining behaviour of a transpersonal art," giving "insight into the interconnectedness of all things, the permeability and instability of boundaries." Ascott insists it "is as much active and constructive as it is receptive and reflective" (chapter 30), and in his shamanic-web and "moist" essays he twins technologically-aided cyberception with a "rediscovered" psi-perception, the latent psychic capacity for out-of-body or mind-to-mind symbiosis. In the Planetary Collegium scheme (chapter 26) it is named as one of four "new ways of being and doing" alongside [[Telenoia|telenoia]], inter-reality, and cyber-estate — "Not natural deception!" *(Directly stated.)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the coiner and sole author of the term. Variants across the essays: in chapter 27 ("The Architecture of Cyberception") it is the full "post-biological faculty," the convergence of perception and conception via telematic connectivity, and the perceiver of immaterial process; in chapter 26 (the Planetary Collegium) it is compressed to "the emergent human faculty of technologically augmented perception/conception"; in chapter 29 ("The Mind of the Museum") to "the technologically amplified faculties of perception and cognition," the faculty whose *cultural consequences* the museum must learn; in chapter 32 ("Technoetic Aesthetics") to the lexicon entry "the emergent human faculty of technologically augmented cognition and perception." Chapters 30–31 add the "active and constructive" emphasis and the cyberception/psi-perception pairing. The definition note [[Cyberception (Ascott)]] holds the verbatim definitional core.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Sense Ratios]] — McLuhan's concept of the shifting proportion among the senses that each medium imposes is **distinct** from cyberception, not a synonym: McLuhan tracks the *reconfiguration of the existing biological senses* by media, whereas Ascott posits a *new, post-biological faculty* that supersedes the linear/visual sensorium of the age of appearance altogether. They are kin in lineage (both make perception a function of technology) but Ascott's all-at-once pattern-seeing is explicitly "the antithesis of tunnel vision or linear thought," opposing the fragmenting visual ratio that McLuhan attributes to the phonetic alphabet and print. *(Interpretive synthesis.)*
- Linear / tunnel-vision perception of the "age of reason / age of appearance" — the object-dividing, determinist mode cyberception revives us from. *(Directly stated.)*

## Questions It Raises

- Is cyberception genuinely a *new* faculty, a *revived* one, or simply a rhetorical reframing of attention shaped by new media? Ascott asserts all three at once.
- If cyberception perceives "immaterial process just as palpably" as material objects, what is the epistemic status of that perception — is it perception, inference, or interpretation given perceptual language?
- How does Ascott's "post-biological faculty" sit against Hayles's insistence that there is only different embodiment, not disembodiment? Cyberception celebrates leaving behind the biological sensorium even as it remains a bodily-experiential claim.
- Where does technologically-aided cyberception end and "rediscovered" psi-perception begin? Ascott twins them but does not bound them.
- Does naming an "amplified" faculty risk treating telematic networks as neutral extensions, eliding the surveillance and control dimensions those same networks carry (cf. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]])?
