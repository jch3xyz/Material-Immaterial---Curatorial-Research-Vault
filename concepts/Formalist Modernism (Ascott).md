# Formalist Modernism (Ascott)

## Brief Definition

Roy Ascott's antagonist concept: the dominant formalist modernist aesthetic understood as "a one-way street, stylistically irreversible, always rushing for the future" — a cultural form of "totally Newtonian structure" that reduces art to pure form and a fixed, one-way message, and which, despite claiming to overthrow the Newtonian worldview, is therefore only "faux modernist."

## Longer Explanation

In "Towards a Field Theory for Postmodernist Art" (1980, ch. 13) Ascott names the paradigm he wants to replace: "the formalist modernist aesthetic." Where his proposed [[A Field Theory of Art]] "takes as its focus not form but behaviour," formalist modernism takes form as everything — the artwork is treated as a discrete, self-sufficient object carrying "simple, clear, and direct messages," to be decoded by a passive spectator rather than completed by a participator. (`Directly stated`, ch. 13.) The concept is author-tagged `(Ascott)` to keep it distinct from [[Formalism (Burnham)]], Burnham's sculpture-specific account of formalist method and its [[Form Exhaustion|form exhaustion]]; Ascott's target is not a depleted sculptural vocabulary but a whole *cultural-temporal structure* of art. The two uses are cross-linked as distinct.

Ascott's central charge is temporal and structural. Formalist modernism "saw itself moving through time like an arrow, directed to the future with simple, clear, and direct messages on its shaft" — a linear, irreversible progress toward an ever-new future. (`Directly stated`, ch. 13.) This is the [[Linear vs Cyclical|linear, irreversible]] time-model of classical, Newtonian physics: "the simplest analysis of its cultural form shows a totally Newtonian structure." (`Directly stated`, ch. 13.) The irony Ascott presses is that modernism advertised itself as the overthrow of exactly that worldview. "The claim of formalist modernism to have dispensed with the Newtonian worldview was correct only to some extent within its own formal terms" — it broke with classical perspective and representation — "but the simplest analysis of its cultural form shows a totally Newtonian structure." (`Directly stated`, ch. 13.) Because it kept the deep grammar (linear time, one-way message, the closed self-sufficient object) of the very physics it claimed to leave behind, it was, "even in its own terms... 'faux modernist.'" (`Directly stated`, ch. 13.)

The new physics is the measure that condemns it. For Ascott a *truly* modern art would be organized like quantum indeterminacy and cybernetic feedback — reversible, probabilistic, participatory, with the observer's consciousness "a part of what he is observing" — not like Newtonian mechanics. Formalist modernism, still running on the discredited linear model, is therefore not modern enough; the genuinely contemporary move is to [[Postmodernist Art (Ascott)|postmodernist art]], which "recognizes time and periodicity, but, rather than being tied to one-way time series... can move back and forth in time." (`Strongly implied`, ch. 13 — Ascott directly states the two time-models; the verdict "not modern enough" is his explicit framing of "faux modernist.")

Ascott also defines formalist modernism by negation, as the absence of the connective qualities postmodernist art recovers. It "was not connective, inclusive, transactional, associative, referential, interactive, changeable, discontinuous, multilayered, impure, ambiguous; it ignored autobiographical data and questions of personality." (`Directly stated`, ch. 13.) This is the [[Form vs Behaviour]] split in its cultural form: an art of fixed form and one-way transmission versus an art of behaviour, transaction, and shared meaning-generation. Notably, Ascott does not simply discard modernism's aspirations; he reads the spiritual turn of postmodernist art as "closing the loop to the early aspirations of formalist modernism before it became trapped in a preoccupation with materialism" — locating the failure in modernism's drift into [[Material vs Immaterial|materialism]] rather than in its origins. (`Directly stated`, ch. 13.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** — the originating use. Formalist modernism is Ascott's named antagonist in ch. 13: a one-way, stylistically irreversible, future-rushing aesthetic with a "totally Newtonian structure" that, by retaining the linear/one-way grammar of the physics it claimed to overthrow, is "faux modernist." It is the foil against which he builds [[A Field Theory of Art]] and [[Postmodernist Art (Ascott)]].
- **Distinct from [[Formalism (Burnham)]]** — Burnham's `Formalism (Burnham)` is the sculpture-specific method (the logical examination of "material-optical-tactile features") whose decline he calls `Form Exhaustion`. Ascott's `Formalist Modernism (Ascott)` is a broader cultural-temporal structure (linear time, one-way message, the closed object). The two are cross-linked, not merged; the author tags mark the different scopes and uses.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[A Field Theory of Art]] — the direct paradigmatic opposite via [[Form vs Behaviour]]: form-as-message vs behaviour-as-field; the passive spectator decoding a fixed message vs the participator generating meaning.
- [[Postmodernist Art (Ascott)]] — opposed via [[Linear vs Cyclical]]: the arrow-of-progress, irreversible Newtonian time of formalist modernism vs the reversible, "back and forth" cyclical time of postmodernist art.

## Questions It Raises

- If keeping the deep grammar of Newtonian time makes an aesthetic "faux modernist," is *any* future-oriented avant-garde — including Ascott's own networked, accelerative vision — vulnerable to the same charge?
- Ascott reads modernism's failure as a late "preoccupation with materialism" rather than an original flaw; does this rescue or merely re-spiritualize the modernist project he claims to leave behind?
- Does the "one-way street / arrow to the future" diagnosis describe formalist modernism specifically, or the broader logic of [[Acceleration|acceleration]] and linear progress that the vault tracks across Han, Kurzweil, and McLuhan?
- Is "form vs behaviour" a genuine ontological break, or does a participatory, behavioural art still depend on formal artifacts (interfaces, systems, surfaces) that re-import the very formalism it opposes?
