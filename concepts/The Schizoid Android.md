# The Schizoid Android

## Brief Definition

Hayles's master figure for her reading of Philip K. Dick's mid-sixties novels: a "multiple pun" in which a person who acts like a machine, a literal machine, and the gendered (typically female) schizoid woman are fused into a single signifier that does not merely name but *enacts* the split between the human and the not-human subject positions.

## Longer Explanation

The schizoid android is not a character Dick names but a critical construct Hayles coins to organize the "extraordinarily complex traffic" running through Dick's fiction between the cultural, scientific, and psychological implications of [[Cybernetics]]. As she introduces it, the term "hints at the splittings, combinations, and recombinations through which Dick's writing performs these complexities." Its multivalence is the point. In one guise the schizoid android marks "the coming together of a person who acts like a machine with a literal interpretation of that person as a machine" — the cold, affectless "schizoid" who *is* an android by Dick's own definition. In another guise the android is placed *in opposition* to the schizoid: "some androids turn out to be more feeling than humans," so an android's capacity for empathy throws "into ironic relief the schizoid woman's incapacity for feeling." The figure therefore holds two mutually exclusive readings at once. (Directly stated.)

What makes the construct distinctive in Hayles's vocabulary is that it is a *performative* signifier, not a stable emblem. Her crucial formulation is that "the android is not so much a fixed symbol, then, as a signifier that enacts as well as connotes the schizoid, splitting into the two opposed and mutually exclusive subject positions of the human and the not-human." The schizoid android does boundary work: it is the textual operator by which the inside/outside distinction between the autonomous human and the incorporated machine-component is made unstable and re-decided over and over. This ties it directly to Hayles's larger thesis about what happens to the subject "when system boundaries are defined by information flows and feedback loops rather than epidermal surfaces" — the body ceases to guarantee organic wholeness and "the subject becomes a system to be assembled and disassembled." (Directly stated; Strongly implied for the systems-boundary framing.)

The figure is irreducibly gendered. The schizoid is "typically gendered female," represented as "a bright, cold, emotionally distant woman" with "a flattening of affect and an inability to feel empathy." Hayles braids this with Dick's biographical "dark-haired girl" — the recurring brunette who oscillates, the closer a male character draws to her, between the empathic dark-haired girl and the android-cold schizoid woman, destabilizing the boundaries that define him and his world. The schizoid android thus links "androidism in an attractive dark-haired woman with a radical confusion of boundaries between 'inside' and 'outside' for a male subject," folding gender into the boundary disputes alongside capitalism (the "politics of incorporation") and ontology. (Directly stated.)

Crucially for Hayles's project, Dick reaches these effects by *omitting* the body. Unlike [[Humberto Maturana]] and especially [[Francisco Varela]], whose [[Autopoiesis|autopoietic]] theory insists living systems can be artificially made, "he leaps over the importance of embodiment"; his fiction "shows almost no interest in how intelligent machines are constructed" and descriptions of bodies (except women's, as "sexual markers") "rarely appear." "Without embodiment to stabilize his epistemological skepticism, the cracks he opens in the perceiver's knowledge widen into rifts in the fabric of the world." For Hayles this is exactly why the schizoid android matters: it is a case where evacuating [[Embodiment]] in favor of perception, information, and predictable "programming" lets the inside/outside boundary collapse — a literary symptom of the same information/materiality split her book tracks. (Directly stated; Interpretive synthesis for the tie to her overarching argument.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — coins "the schizoid android" as the organizing figure of her Dick chapter; treats it as a performative signifier enacting the human/not-human split, and reads its un-embodiment as symptomatic of cybernetic boundary loss. This is her specific reading-construct in Dick, distinct from the cross-author [[The Cyborg|cyborg]].
- **[[Philip K. Dick]]** — supplies the raw materials Hayles fuses: the "schizoid" (in his essays, the cold, power-seeking person who "withdraws emotions from the world"), the android defined above all by "predictability," and the autobiographical "dark-haired girl." Dick himself writes that to fail to care about the real and the human is to be "schizoid and ... an android: that is, not human and hence not real."

## Related Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — the cross-author posthuman figure (Wiener to Haraway); the schizoid android is Hayles's narrower, Dick-specific construct, not a synonym for it.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the schizoid android entangles "cybernetics with the social, political, economic, and psychological formations that define the liberal subject"; Dick's checklist for the human (unique, unpredictable, emotional, self-defining) "reads like a compendium of qualities that the liberal humanist subject is supposed to have," every item of which his fiction puts in question.
- [[Autopoiesis]] — the boundary struggle to claim the "outside" autopoietic position while forcing the opponent into the "inside" allopoietic one is the systems-theoretic frame Hayles maps onto Dick's android/human disputes.
- [[The Observer]] — both Dick and the autopoietic theorists were "responding to the problem of incorporating the observer into the system," experimenting with radical epistemologies.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the figure's reduction of the human to perception, thought, and "programming," with the flesh treated as inessential, is a literary instance of the body rendered as informational pattern.
- [[Posthumanism]] — the dissolution of the bounded, organically whole subject into an assemblable/disassemblable system is the posthuman condition Hayles theorizes.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the schizoid android sits on the *information* side of Hayles's central opposition: Dick "leaps over the importance of embodiment," and it is precisely the absence of embodiment that lets the boundary between inside and outside, human and not-human, come apart.

## Questions It Raises

- If the schizoid android *enacts* rather than merely connotes the human/not-human split, can the boundary between human and machine ever be a stable property of a body — or is it always a performative effect of signification and information flow? (ties to [[Posthumanism]], [[Embodiment vs Information]])
- What is lost when a figure for the posthuman subject "leaps over" the body? Does Dick's un-embodiment make the schizoid android a cautionary case for Hayles's insistence that information is always instantiated in materiality? (ties to [[Embodiment]], [[Material vs Immaterial]])
- Why must the figure be gendered female? If "the body as data" is supposedly disembodied, why does the schizoid woman's body return as the one body Dick does describe — as a "sexual marker"? (ties to [[The Body as Data]], [[Embodiment]])
- When "system boundaries are defined by information flows and feedback loops rather than epidermal surfaces," who occupies the privileged "outside" position, and at whose expense? (ties to [[Autopoiesis]], [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]])
