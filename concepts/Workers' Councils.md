# Workers' Councils

## Brief Definition

For [[Guy Debord]], workers' councils are the affirmative, anti-spectacular form of social organization — a delegate-based, revocable, federated structure of proletarian self-management that "assume[s] all decision-making and executive powers" (th. 116) and, by abolishing hierarchy, specialization and [[Separation]], becomes the one terrain on which "the spectacle's negation of life [can] be negated in its turn" (th. 117).

## Longer Explanation

The councils are the positive term of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*. Where the book's first four chapters diagnose the [[Spectacle]] as "a social relation between people that is mediated by images" — a regime of [[Separation]] in which the proletariat is reduced to a contemplative spectator of its own alienated powers — Chapter 4 ("The Proletariat as Subject and Representation") reaches its practical conclusion in the council form. Debord takes up Marx's phrase for the aim of the movement — "the long-sought political form through which the working class could carry out its own economic liberation" — and answers that it "has taken on a clear shape in this century, in the form of revolutionary workers councils that assume all decision-making and executive powers and that federate with each other by means of delegates who are answerable to their base and revocable at any moment" (th. 116). Three structural features define the form: **delegation without representation** (delegates carry mandates and are recallable at any instant, so no separate governing stratum can crystallize), **federation** (councils coordinate horizontally rather than being subsumed under a party or state), and the **fusion of decision and execution** (the split between those who decide and those who carry out is abolished). Debord is careful, following Pannekoek, that the council "poses problems" rather than providing a ready solution — but "it is precisely within this form of social organization that the problems of proletarian revolution can find their real solution" (th. 116). *(Directly stated.)*

The councils matter to Debord's argument because they are the exact structural inverse of the spectacle. The spectacle is separation perfected; the council is "the terrain where active direct communication is realized, marking the end of specialization, hierarchy and separation" (th. 116). Where the spectator is fixed in a contemplative, passive relation to a life that appears to him as an alien order, in the councils "proletarian subjects can emerge from their struggle against their contemplative position" (th. 116) — the spectator becomes an agent. This is why Debord frames the councils not as a demand added to the world but as the negation of the negation: "the proletarian movement becomes its own product. This product is nothing other than the producers themselves, whose goal has become nothing other than their own fulfillment. Only in this way can the spectacle's negation of life be negated in its turn" (th. 117). The proletariat ceases to be [[Representation|represented]] — spoken for by parties, unions, ideologues — and becomes its own subject; the split between subject and representation that names the chapter is closed from the side of the subject. *(Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis for the inverse-of-spectacle framing.)*

Debord's assessment of the councils' historical record is sober but load-bearing. The councils that actually appeared (the German revolution, the soviets before their bureaucratic capture, Hungary 1956) "provided no more than a rough hint of their possibilities because they have immediately been opposed and defeated by class society's various defensive forces, among which their own false consciousness must often be included" (th. 116). Yet defeat does not falsify the form; it isolates it as the one thing worth recovering: "from the vantage point of the new stage of proletarian critique, the councils can be seen in their true light as the only undefeated aspect of a defeated movement" (th. 118). This is the Situationist program's practical hinge — the councils are "no longer at the periphery of a movement that is subsiding, but at the center of a movement that is rising" (th. 118). Correlatively, a revolutionary organization that predates council power "cannot claim to represent the working class"; its task "is to embody a radical separation from the world of separation" and to dissolve itself into the councils rather than rule through them (th. 119) — the source of the *councils-versus-party* opposition that runs through Situationist theory. *(Directly stated.)*

The councils return in the book's final chapter as the resolution of Debord's theory of ideology. Chapter 9 ("Ideology Materialized") argues that the spectacle is ideology made concrete — [[Reification|reified]] thought that has become the material organization of society — and that its overcoming must therefore be equally material and practical, not merely a corrected doctrine: "The self-emancipation of our time is an emancipation from the material bases of inverted truth" (th. 221). That emancipation "can be carried out neither by the isolated individual nor by atomized and manipulated masses, but only and always by the class that is able to dissolve all classes by reducing all power to the de-alienating form of realized democracy — to councils in which practical theory verifies itself and surveys its own actions" (th. 221). The council is thus where theory stops being contemplative (an image of the world held over against it) and becomes practice that tests itself in its own action — "there where dialogue has armed itself to impose its own conditions" (th. 221). The councils are Debord's name for the point at which critique and its object coincide: the abolition of the spectacle and the self-realization of the proletariat are the same event. *(Directly stated.)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Guy Debord]]** — the councils are the affirmative pole of *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*: delegate-based, revocable, federated self-organization (th. 116) in which the proletariat overcomes its spectator position and "becomes its own product" (th. 117), the "only undefeated aspect of a defeated movement" (th. 118) and the "de-alienating form of realized democracy" in which theory verifies itself (th. 221). Debord inherits the council idea from the anti-Leninist communist-left tradition (Pannekoek, whom he cites, and the broader council-communist current) and reworks it as the structural negation of the spectacle rather than merely a preferred institutional design. Marx supplies the framing phrase about the "political form" of liberation, which Debord identifies with the councils.

## Related Concepts

- [[Separation]] — the councils are defined precisely as the "end of specialization, hierarchy and separation" (th. 116); they are separation's practical undoing.
- [[Representation]] — the councils close the subject/representation split from the side of the subject: the proletariat ceases to be represented and "becomes its own product" (th. 117); delegates are mandated and revocable, not representatives.
- [[Reification]] — Chapter 9 makes the councils the material overcoming of reified/materialized ideology; they are where "practical theory verifies itself" (th. 221) rather than hardening into an alien order.
- [[Commodity as Spectacle]] — the councils supplant the regime in which "the commodity contemplates itself"; producers reclaim the powers the commodity-form had autonomized.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Spectacle]] — the direct, positive negation of the spectacle: the spectacle is contemplation, separation and passivity; the councils are active direct communication, unity and self-activity. Only in them can "the spectacle's negation of life be negated in its turn" (th. 117).

## Questions It Raises

- If delegates are revocable "at any moment" (th. 116), what holds a federation of councils together long enough to act at the scale of a modern economy, and does the mandate/federation mechanism actually prevent a new governing stratum from forming?
- Debord grants, via Pannekoek, that the council "poses problems" rather than solving them (th. 116). What are those problems, and does calling the councils "the only undefeated aspect of a defeated movement" (th. 118) protect the form from empirical refutation by insulating it from every historical defeat?
- What is the precise relation between a revolutionary organization and the councils, given that the organization "cannot claim to represent the working class" and must "embody a radical separation from the world of separation" (th. 119)? Is a non-representational, self-dissolving organization coherent, or does it reintroduce the party form it refuses?
- If overcoming the spectacle requires "an emancipation from the material bases of inverted truth" (th. 221), can the council form emerge under the very spectacular conditions it is meant to abolish, or does its possibility presuppose a rupture it cannot itself produce?
