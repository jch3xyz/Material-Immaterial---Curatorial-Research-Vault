# Affinity Not Identity

## Brief Definition

Donna Haraway's anti-essentialist basis for political unity: a coalition built through conscious choice, affinity, and political kinship rather than through natural identification or essential sameness — "related not by blood but by choice."

## Longer Explanation

"Affinity, not identity" names Haraway's answer to a crisis she takes as given: the collapse of any "essential" ground — gender, race, class — on which a feminist or socialist "us" might rest. In ch. 02 she states the crisis flatly: "gender, race, and class cannot provide the basis for belief in 'essential' unity. There is nothing about being 'female' that naturally binds women." The political problem, then, is how to act collectively *after* the loss of a natural matrix of unity. Her response is to relocate the ground of action from being to choosing — from who one essentially *is* to whom one consciously allies with. The recognition she tracks is "another response through coalition—affinity, not identity" (`Directly stated`).

The term is seeded definitionally in ch. 01, where Haraway glosses the word through the affinity group "Fission Impossible" in her own town: "(Affinity: related not by blood but by choice, the appeal of one chemical nuclear group for another, avidity.)" The chemistry pun is load-bearing — affinity is a *bond formed by elective attraction* between distinct elements, not a shared substance or descent. This is the cyborg's "united-front politics, but without the vanguard party": connection built across difference rather than fusion into an organic whole (`Directly stated`).

In ch. 02 the concept does its decisive work through Chela Sandoval's model of the "women of color" coalition, which Haraway reads as a self-consciously constructed political subject: "This identity marks out a self-consciously constructed space that cannot affirm the capacity to act on the basis of natural identification, but only on the basis of conscious coalition, of affinity, of political kinship." Identity here is an *achievement* — "consciousness is an achievement, not a natural fact" — and "women's culture" itself is "consciously created by mechanisms inducing affinity." The grounding argument is Haraway's twin critique of essentialist feminisms: Marxist/socialist-feminism that essentializes through the ontological category of labor, and Catharine MacKinnon's radical feminism whose totalizing "ontology" produces women as a "nonsubject, a nonbeing." Both, she argues, build unity through domination, incorporation, or taxonomy; affinity is the alternative that "can craft a poetic/political unity without relying on a logic of appropriation, incorporation, and taxonomic identification" (`Directly stated`, with `Interpretive synthesis` on the read of the essentialism critique as affinity's grounding).

The concept is materialized in ch. 05 and consummated in ch. 06. In ch. 05's catalogue of women's positions in the "informatics of domination," Haraway insists the list cannot be read "from a standpoint of 'identification,' of a unitary self. The issue is dispersion. The task is to survive in the diaspora" — affinity becomes the survival ethic of the scattered, who must "learn new couplings, new coalitions" by reading the webs of power rather than by claiming a common essence (`Directly stated`). Ch. 06 frees politics fully from the essentialist apparatus: recognizing "oneself" as fully implicated in the world "frees us of the need to root politics in identification, vanguard parties, purity, and mothering." Affinity is thus the political form proper to the cyborg — partial, ironic, without innocence — and Haraway closes by preferring it to the goddess: a coalition of the spliced and the monstrous over a return to organic wholeness (`Directly stated`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — coins "affinity, not identity" as the basis for cyborg/socialist-feminist coalition: unity by conscious choice and political kinship rather than natural identification, grounded in her critique of essentialist (Marxist-feminist and radical-feminist) theories of identity and consummated as the politics of the diaspora freed from "identification, vanguard parties, purity, and mothering."

## Related Concepts

- [[The Cyborg]] — the figure whose politics affinity *is*: the cyborg, "completely without innocence" and committed to partiality, has "a natural feel for united-front politics, but without the vanguard party."
- [[Embodiment]] — affinity presupposes "permanently partial identities and contradictory standpoints" lodged in "historically constituted" bodies, not in a shared natural essence.
- Informatics of Domination — the network of restructured "places" (Home, Market, State, Clinic, Church) across which the dispersed must build "new couplings, new coalitions" rather than read themselves as a unitary self.
- Situated Knowledges — Haraway's later development of partial, located, non-totalizing perspective; affinity is its political-organizational counterpart (`Speculative connection`).

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the autonomous, self-identical, bounded subject whose "natural identification" and "original wholeness" affinity explicitly refuses; "choice grounds a liberal politics... that imagine the reproduction of individuals before the wider replications of 'texts.'"
- Essentialist Feminist Identity — unity grounded in a natural matrix ("being 'female'"), in the ontology of labor, or in MacKinnon's "essential" nonexistence of women; affinity is precisely the non-essentialist alternative.
- Identity Politics (as natural identification) — coalition built on shared essence, descent, or a "logic of appropriation, incorporation, and taxonomic identification."

## Questions It Raises

- If unity is built by "conscious coalition" rather than essence, what holds an affinity together when interests diverge — and what prevents affinity from collapsing back into a new, softer essentialism?
- Haraway's affinity refuses "identification, vanguard parties, purity, and mothering": can a politics without a privileged revolutionary subject still "act potently," or does it trade efficacy for honesty about partiality?
- What is the relation between affinity and survival — why does Haraway repeatedly cast non-identitarian coalition ("the task is to survive in the diaspora") as a *survival* strategy rather than merely a corrective epistemology?
- Does the chemical metaphor ("the appeal of one chemical nuclear group for another, avidity") smuggle a kind of attraction-as-given back into a politics meant to be wholly constructed?
