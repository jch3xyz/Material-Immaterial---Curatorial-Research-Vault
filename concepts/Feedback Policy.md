# Feedback Policy

## Brief Definition

The systems wisdom that a dynamic, self-adjusting system cannot be governed by a static rule, so the best interventions are *feedback policies* — rules that change with the state of the system and, at their strongest, contain "meta-feedback loops" that design learning into the management process itself.

## Longer Explanation

*Feedback Policy* is Donella Meadows's normative extension of the descriptive [[Feedback Loops]] concept: once you accept that a system is a web of self-correcting loops, the practical question becomes what *kind* of policy can govern such a thing without fighting its own dynamics. Her answer, one of the fifteen "systems wisdoms" that close *Thinking in Systems*, is captured in the imperative heading **"Make Feedback Policies for Feedback Systems."** The core intuition is a mismatch of forms: "You can imagine why a dynamic, self-adjusting feedback system cannot be governed by a static, unbending policy" (`Directly stated`). A rule fixed in advance is brittle against a system that is continually reorganizing itself; it will be right for one system state and wrong for the next.

The alternative is a policy whose *strength scales with the state of the system*. Meadows's worked illustration is Jimmy Carter's proposed gasoline tax "proportional to the fraction of U.S. oil consumption that had to be imported" — a tax engineered to rise as imports rose and fall to zero if imports fell to zero, so the policy self-cancels once it has done its work. Such a rule is not merely cheaper but structurally correct: "It's easier, more effective, and usually much cheaper to design policies that change depending on the state of the system" (`Directly stated`). The policy is itself a [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing loop]], tuned to the very variable it aims to correct.

The concept's sharpest move is the second, recursive tier. "Especially where there are great uncertainties, the best policies not only contain feedback loops, but meta-feedback loops—loops that alter, correct, and expand loops. These are policies that design learning into the management process" (`Directly stated`). A meta-feedback loop does not just respond to the system; it revises the rule for responding — it builds in scheduled reconsideration, monitoring, and the authority to change course. Meadows's exemplar is the 1987 Montreal Protocol on the ozone layer, signed under deep scientific uncertainty: it fixed a phase-out schedule but "also required monitoring the situation and reconvening an international congress to change the phase-out schedule, if the damage to the ozone layer turned out to be more or less than expected." When, just three years later, the damage proved worse than foreseen, "the schedule had to be hurried forward and more chemicals added to it." That reconvening clause is the meta-loop in action; Meadows calls it "a feedback policy, structured for learning" (`Directly stated`).

Read against the leverage hierarchy, a Feedback Policy is the practical technique for intervening at rung 5, [[Rules (Systems)|Rules]] — but with a twist that reaches toward rung 4, [[Self-Organization]]. An ordinary rule is a static constraint; a *feedback* rule is a rule that adjusts; a *meta-feedback* rule is a rule that changes the rules, which is precisely the self-restructuring power Meadows locates higher up [[The Twelve Leverage Points|the leverage list]]. In this sense the concept is the normative counterpart to the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] observation that leverage rises as the intervention point moves from the physical parameters of a system to its information, control, and self-organizing capacities (`Interpretive synthesis`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** (*Thinking in Systems*, 2008) — the concept's sole and originating anchor. Meadows frames Feedback Policy as one of her closing systems wisdoms in Chapter 7, deriving it directly from the [[Feedback Loops]] structure that runs through the whole book. Her two canonical examples — Carter's proportional import tax (a first-order feedback policy) and the Montreal Protocol's reconvening schedule (a meta-feedback policy "structured for learning") — mark the two tiers of the idea. She attributes the underlying systems methodology to MIT's Jay Forrester, but the "feedback policy / meta-feedback loop" formulation is her own.

## Related Concepts

- [[Feedback Loops]] — the descriptive parent; Feedback Policy is its applied, normative extension (govern feedback systems *with* feedback, not against it).
- [[Systems Thinking]] — the broader disposition of which making feedback policies is a concrete practice.
- [[Leverage Points]] / [[The Twelve Leverage Points]] — a feedback policy operates at the Rules rung and, via meta-feedback, reaches toward Self-Organization.
- [[Self-Organization]] — a meta-feedback loop ("loops that alter, correct, and expand loops") is a policy that grants the management process the power to evolve its own structure.
- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — the loop-type a state-dependent policy like the proportional tax embodies.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Rules (Systems)]] — in its ordinary "static, unbending" form (a fixed constant, standard, or constraint), an ordinary rule is exactly what Feedback Policy is defined against; the concept converts a static rule into a self-adjusting one.

## Questions It Raises

- If the best policies "design learning into the management process," who holds the authority to reconvene and revise — and what happens when that meta-loop is captured or defunded?
- Carter's proportional tax and open-border development aid both failed to pass; is the obstacle to feedback policy technical, or is it that publics and legislatures "don't understand feedback" and demand fixed, legible rules?
- Where is the boundary between a healthy meta-feedback loop and endless renegotiation that never commits to a schedule at all?
- Does a meta-feedback policy blur the line between intervening at the level of [[Rules (Systems)|Rules]] and intervening at the level of [[Self-Organization]] — and does that mean feedback policy is quietly a higher-leverage move than its rung suggests?
