# Equivalence Without Equality

## Brief Definition

The operational flattening by which surveillance capitalism's "reality business" renders all people, things, relationships, and processes as interchangeable computational objects — formally equivalent as units of behavioral surplus, yet stripped of the distinct moral meaning, dignity, and human consequence that "equality" would honor.

## Longer Explanation

For Shoshana Zuboff, *equivalence without equality* names the moral price of treating the world as raw material for computation. In the drive toward total information that the prediction imperative demands, surveillance capitalism's reality business "renders all people, things, and processes as computational objects in an endless queue of equivalence without equality." Every datum is equivalent to every other as a candidate for extraction and prediction; nothing carries the standing — the unrepeatable situation, the claim to recognition — that equality among persons would entail. Equivalence is a measure of formal sameness for the machine; equality is a moral relation between persons. Zuboff's coinage marks the silent substitution of the first for the second. (Directly stated)

This flattening is the operational face of [[Radical Indifference]]: a "fundamentally asocial mode of knowledge" in which content "is judged by its volume, range, and depth of surplus" through the "'anonymous' equivalence of clicks, likes, and dwell times, despite the obvious fact that its profoundly dissimilar meanings originate in distinct human situations." Where radical indifference is the *posture* — the refusal to distinguish truth from falsehood, love from cruelty, so long as both yield surplus — equivalence without equality is the *result*: a world processed into "otherized," interchangeable things-among-things. The two are reciprocal; this note is the moral correlate of radical indifference, and Zuboff develops them together in the chapter on the surveillance-capitalist coup. (Directly stated)

Zuboff finds the worldview stated nakedly in a 2016 internal Facebook memo by executive Andrew Bosworth, where, she writes, "equivalence wins out over equality in the worldview of 'an organism among organisms.'" In Bosworth's logic, connecting people is "de facto good" whether it "finds love" or "costs a life" — the positives and negatives "must be viewed as equivalent, despite their distinct moral meanings and human consequences." This is the mechanism by which the public-facing "first text" becomes vulnerable to corruption: when Facebook standardized its News Feed so that "all news stories looked roughly the same as each other," investigative reporting, gossip, and "flat-out lies" became formally equal — an "expression of equivalence without equality" that "made Facebook's first text exceptionally vulnerable to corruption from what would come to be called 'fake news.'" Journalism, whose raison d'être is to "separate truth from falsehood," is precisely the rejection of equivalence; surveillance capitalism erases that reciprocity. (Directly stated)

The principle does not stop at content. Equivalence without equality, Zuboff argues, "also affects the high science of targeted advertising" — the ad-category machinery that automatically generated audiences for "'Jew hater'" and "'How to burn jews'" treats hate as one more equivalent signal of engagement. At its political limit the same logic underwrites tyranny, which Zuboff describes (via Hannah Arendt) as a "radical indifference in which every person, except the tyrant, is understood as an organism among organisms in an equivalency of Other-Ones" — an egalitarianism perverted into universal, equal *powerlessness*. (Directly stated / Strongly implied)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins *equivalence without equality* as the moral signature of the reality business and the operational expression of [[Radical Indifference]]: persons and relationships flattened into formally interchangeable computational objects, "an organism among organisms," processed for surplus without regard to their distinct human meanings.
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Han's [[Hell of the Same]] describes a parallel collapse of difference: the proliferation of the same that abolishes the negativity of the Other. Where Han diagnoses the *experiential* and erotic loss of alterity, Zuboff names its *economic-computational* mechanism — equivalence as a metric for extraction. (Interpretive synthesis — Zuboff does not cite Han.)
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — Harari's [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] dissolves the special standing of the human into data-processing functions; once organisms are algorithms, persons become commensurable with all other information processes. Bosworth's phrase "an organism among organisms," which Zuboff quotes, is the corporate enactment of exactly this leveling. (Interpretive synthesis — the resonance is the system's, not a citation.)

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Subject vs Object]] — equivalence without equality lands wholly on the *object* pole: the person is otherized into a thing-among-things. Equality is the moral demand of treating the other as a subject; equivalence denies it.

## Questions It Raises

- If equivalence is the native logic of computation, can any large-scale data system avoid producing equivalence *without* equality, or is the moral flattening intrinsic to rendering experience as data?
- Journalism's rejection of equivalence is named as its raison d'être — what other social institutions are constituted by an *inequality of meanings*, and are they all equally vulnerable to the reality business?
- Is Zuboff's equivalence without equality the same collapse Han names as the Hell of the Same and Harari as organisms-are-algorithms, or three distinct symptoms — economic, experiential, biological — of one underlying datafication?
- If tyranny is "an equivalency of Other-Ones," is equivalence without equality the specifically *democratic* injury of surveillance capitalism — the dissolution of the equal standing democracy presumes?
