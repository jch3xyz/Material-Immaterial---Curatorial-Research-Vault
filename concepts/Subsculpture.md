# Subsculpture

## Brief Definition

Jack Burnham's coinage for the vast substratum of anthropomorphic images — fetishes, idols, dolls, waxworks, manikins, puppets, and automata — historically "ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts."

## Longer Explanation

In *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, **subsculpture** is the organizing category of the chapter "Sculpture and Automata." Burnham gathers under it the "continuum of images" — "Fetishes, idols, amulets, funeral images, dolls, waxworks, manikins, puppets, and, most dramatically, automata" — that art historians "used to rank far below sculpture as a fine art." The term is explicitly defined by its place in a hierarchy: it names images "aesthetically ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts." Many of these artifacts, Burnham notes, are "rooted, not in any Western concept of beauty, but in some very practical purpose" — magical, religious, votive, or substitutive — so the category is defined by *function* and *low aesthetic standing* rather than by form. (Directly stated.)

Subsculpture matters to Burnham's larger argument because its boundary is porous and historically moving: "Already some of this subsculpture has crossed the boundary line of scholarship into the category of major art." He predicts that "the psychological motives behind the drive to create human images will assume greater importance than the supposed hierarchical levels of beauty which separate those images into minor and major art." The chapter follows "one particular branch of subsculpture: automata" — the "seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men" whose "progressive mechanization" carries "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge," Western man's drive "to re-create life nonbiologically." (Directly stated.)

The decisive event, for Burnham's narrative, is subsculpture's migration *into* fine art around 1910. "The theme of the pseudo-man, as manikin or robot, became a central issue of art after 1910." Archipenko's *Medrano I (Juggler)* (1912) "may be the first intimation of the modern robot in art"; Picabia and Duchamp brought "the machine overtly" into "the iconography of art"; de Chirico populated his cityscapes with faceless "manikins"; Schlemmer built a mechanized stage of "stylized robot and geometric costumes"; and Bellmer "built his life around the evocative qualities of the doll." Burnham's verdict on this convergence — "The doll, the manikin, and the robot precariously began to find their place in the fine arts" — registers exactly the breach of the old hierarchy his concept is built to name. (Directly stated.) This crossing is, in Burnham's scheme, one engine of the larger transition the book traces from the autonomous sculptural *object* toward sculpture conceived as *system*: a manikin or automaton is valued not as a static contemplative form but as a working, animated, behaving thing. (Interpretive synthesis.)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Jack Burnham]]** — coins *subsculpture* in *Beyond Modern Sculpture* to name the functional, lower-ranked field of human images (fetish, idol, doll, waxwork, manikin, puppet, automaton) that lies beneath sculpture-as-fine-art; uses its c.1910 migration into modern art (Archipenko, Duchamp, Picabia, de Chirico, Schlemmer, Bellmer) as a hinge in the object-to-system story.

## Related Concepts

- [[Automata]] — the "branch of subsculpture" the chapter actually follows; the self-animated image whose mechanization carries Burnham's Faustian/system thread.
- [[Object and System]] — subsculpture's crossing into art (the behaving manikin, the moving android) is a precursor of sculpture's shift from contemplative object toward animated system.
- [[The Readymade]] — Duchamp, named here among the artists who brought the pseudo-machine into art's iconography, is the figure who most radically dissolves the "demarcation line between sculpture and all other forms of anthropomorphic imagery."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Object and System]] — in one register subsculpture *opposes* the autonomous sculptural object: it is defined precisely as what sits *below* and *outside* sculpture-as-fine-art, the functional non-art against which the dignified, contemplative art object is measured. (The same note is therefore both related and opposing, by intent: subsculpture is the low term whose rise destabilizes the object hierarchy.)

## Questions It Raises

- If the line between "minor" and "major" art is set by "supposed hierarchical levels of beauty," what exactly licenses subsculpture's promotion — changed aesthetics, or changed attention to the "psychological motives" behind image-making?
- Does the migration of the manikin/robot into fine art c.1910 represent art absorbing subsculpture, or subsculpture's behaving, functional logic colonizing art and pushing it toward system?
- Is *automata* merely one branch of subsculpture, or — because it most fully expresses the "Faustian urge" to "re-create life nonbiologically" — the branch that reveals what all subsculpture was reaching toward?
