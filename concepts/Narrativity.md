# Narrativity

## Brief Definition

For [[Byung-Chul Han]], *narrativity* is the directional, scenic, selective quality that gives processes (ritual, pilgrimage, memory, the novel) tension, rhythm, and sense — and whose erosion is what the [[Transparency]] society calls progress.

## Longer Explanation

Han uses *narrativity* as the structural feature that distinguishes processes which *tell* from processes that merely *count*. In the chapter on [[Acceleration]] he draws an etymological wedge between the *procession* and the *processor*: both derive from *procedere*, "to step forward," but only the procession has "a strong sense of direction" and is "harnessed by narration, which lends it tension," while the processor "tells [erzählt] nothing. It simply counts [zählt]" (Source 2). Narrativity is therefore not ornament; it is what allows a sequence to have a *Wohin* — a "where-to" — and so to admit conclusion, transition, and meaning rather than mere break-off. Conclusion, Han writes, "is possible only within narration"; without it, "the ending only amounts to a breaking-off that gives pain and unsettles" (Source 2). This is why narrative is incompatible with acceleration: a [[Pilgrimage]] cannot be sped up because its itinerary is "not a passage to be traversed as quickly as possible, but a path rich in significance."

The key formal feature of narrativity is *selection*. "Narration practices selection. The narrative path is narrow; it admits only certain events. Thereby it prevents the positive from proliferating and de-individualizing" (Source 2). Narrativity is thus the antagonist of [[Positivity]]'s pile-up: where storage is additive and accumulates indifferently, narrative memory rearranges and reinscribes, so that traces have history rather than mere co-presence. Han uses Baudelaire's image of the "junkshop" — "used and worn-out symbols piled up any-old-how" — to mark what memory becomes once it has lost narrative gravity (Source 2). Selection is also why narration resists [[Transparency]]: an event with narrative tension is shaped by [[Negativity]] (obstacle, threshold, transition), and "transparent space is semantically impoverished" because it has dismantled "all borders and thresholds."

Narrativity is, finally, a temporal and ontological matter. In the chapter on [[Exhibition]], Han argues that contemporary photography "is determined by the present which lacks negativity and therefore destiny — it admits no narrative tension, nothing 'dramatic' in the sense of a novel [Roman]" (Source 1). The novel here names the form whose temporal substance has been hollowed out; without negativity there is no destiny, and without destiny no narration. In the chapter on [[Intimacy as Psychological Formula of Transparency|Intimacy]], denarrativization is paired with deritualization as the joint operation by which the [[Transparency|Society of Transparency]] strips life of "forms of symbolic appearance" and renders it "naked" (Source 3). The cure for the crisis Han diagnoses is therefore not deceleration — "deceleration alone produces no tact, no rhythm, no fragrance" — but the recovery of a "narrative gravity" that can "unite atoms into fragrant molecules" (Source 2).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — Narrativity is a normative-ontological feature of processes that have direction, scenography, selection, and tension. Its erosion (*denarrativization*) is one face of [[Transparency]], alongside deritualization, depersonalization, and the eclipse of [[Negativity]].
- **[[Guy Debord]]** *(Speculative connection)* — In *[[The Society of the Spectacle]]*, the spectacle's frozen, separated time may overlap with what Han calls the loss of narrative gravity; the resonance is plausible but not asserted by Han here.
- **[[Marshall McLuhan]]** *(Speculative connection)* — McLuhan's contrast between the linear/typographic and the mosaic/electric could be cross-read against Han's narration/addition pair, though the valences differ.
- **[[Donella Meadows]]** *(Speculative connection)* — A possible structural parallel: Meadows' systems thinking foregrounds direction, feedback, and selection over additive accumulation, but this connection is not made by Han.

## Related Concepts

- [[Negativity]] — Narrativity requires negativity (obstacle, threshold, destiny); without it there is no tension and no conclusion.
- [[Ritual]] — Rituals and ceremonies are "narrative processes" that "elude acceleration."
- [[Pilgrimage]] — Han's exemplary case of a narrative event whose itinerary cannot be accelerated.
- [[Memory]] — Distinguished from [[Storage]] precisely by narrativity: memory traces are rearranged and reinscribed, stored data is not.
- [[Scenic Distance]] — Narration requires "choreography or scenography"; intimacy collapses the scene.
- [[Threshold]] — Sites where narrative tension is constituted; "the topology of passion."
- [[The Novel]] — *Roman*, the form whose "narrative tension" current photography no longer admits.
- [[Fragrance of Time]] — What "narrative gravity" produces and what transparency annihilates.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Addition]] — "Addition is more transparent than narration. Only a process that is additive and not narrative admits acceleration."
- [[Storage]] — Memory's positivized double: accumulative, non-historical, "a junkshop."
- [[Transparency]] — The denarrativizing operation par excellence; transparency requires the smoothing-away of the obstacles narrative depends on.
- [[Positivity]] — Without narrative selection, the positive proliferates and de-individualizes.
- [[Acceleration]] — Narrative processes "have their own temporality, their own rhythm and tact" and cannot be sped up; their abolition is the precondition of acceleration.
- [[Pornography (Han)]] — The "stripping of narrativity, direction, and sense" is what makes the social body obscene (after Sartre).

## Questions It Raises

- If the cure for acceleration is "narrative gravity" rather than deceleration, what would a curatorial or institutional practice of *re-narrativization* look like in a digital context Han diagnoses as structurally additive?
- Is narrativity for Han always tied to a religious-ritual substrate (procession, pilgrimage, sacrifice), or can secular narrative forms — the novel, the museum sequence, the essay — carry it independently?
- How does narrativity relate to [[Aura]] and [[Cult Value]]? Both seem to depend on a withheld, non-exhibited dimension, but Han does not explicitly identify them.
- Is the opposition narration / addition stable, or does any sufficiently long act of addition (a chronicle, a database, an archive) eventually generate narrative effects against its own logic?
- What is the curatorial cost of denarrativization specifically — does an exhibition stripped of scenography become, in Han's terms, "obscene"?
