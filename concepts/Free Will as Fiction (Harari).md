# Free Will as Fiction (Harari)

## Brief Definition

Yuval Noah Harari's eliminativist claim that "free will" is an empty term on a par with "soul": every brain process is deterministic, random, or a probabilistic mix of the two, none of which is free, so freedom survives only as a story we tell ourselves.

## Longer Explanation

In *Homo Deus* Harari treats free will not as a contested philosophical thesis but as a factual error about the world. Liberalism, he argues, rests on the belief that the decisions of voters and customers are "neither deterministic nor random," that "each of us can wave the magic wand of freedom and decide things for ourselves." When scientists opened up the "Sapiens black box" they found "neither soul, nor free will, nor 'self' – but only genes, hormones and neurons that obey the same physical and chemical laws governing the rest of reality." The contradiction between free will and contemporary science is, for Harari, "the elephant in the laboratory" that researchers prefer not to see. (`Directly stated`)

The core of the argument is a dilemma with no third option. A neuron firing "may either be a deterministic reaction to external stimuli, or it might be the outcome of a random event such as the spontaneous decomposition of a radioactive atom," and "neither option leaves any room for free will." Decisions produced by a chain of biochemical causes are not free; decisions produced by random subatomic accidents are merely random; and "when random accidents combine with deterministic processes, we get probabilistic outcomes, but this too doesn't amount to freedom." From this Harari draws his sharpest formulation: "To the best of our scientific understanding, determinism and randomness have divided the entire cake between them, leaving not even a crumb for 'freedom'." The word "freedom," like "soul," becomes "an empty term that carries no discernible meaning," and "free will exists only in the imaginary stories we humans have invented." (`Directly stated`)

Harari adds what he calls "the last nail in freedom's coffin": evolution. If animals "freely" chose their food and mates, "natural selection is left with nothing to work on"; that all choices reflect a genetic code is precisely what lets selection operate. The decisive move, however, is not the metaphysics of neurons but a distinction about desire. Harari concedes that if "free will" means "the ability to act according to your desires," then humans have it — "and so do chimpanzees, dogs and parrots." But "the million-dollar question is not whether parrots and humans can act out their inner desires – the question is whether they can choose their desires in the first place." His answer is that we do not: "Yet people erroneously jump to the conclusion that if I want to press it, I choose to want to. This is of course false. I don't choose my desires. I only feel them, and act accordingly." Belief in free will, on this account, "results from faulty logic" — the slide from feeling a desire to authoring it. (`Directly stated`)

The corollary Harari builds on this is that desire, being authored by biochemistry rather than by a free self, can be engineered from outside. "If organisms indeed lack free will, it implies we could manipulate and even control their desires using drugs, genetic engineering or direct brain stimulation." Once such manipulation "become[s] routine, the supposedly free will of customers will become just another product we can buy" — the helmet that makes you "downright aching to play the piano" is the paradigm case. Free will is thus not only fictional but, in the same gesture, commodifiable: a purchasable product rather than an inner sovereignty. (`Directly stated`) Harari extends the same demolition to the unitary self — "humans aren't individuals. They are 'dividuals'" — but this concept note concerns the free-will claim specifically; the dissolution of the self is its companion argument, not its content.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** — pushes the free-will-vs-determinism debate to its eliminativist terminus: freedom is not weak or constrained but nonexistent, an "empty term" like "soul." His distinctive lever is the desire argument (we feel but do not choose desires) and its consequence — engineered, purchasable desire — which ties the metaphysical claim to a political-economic one.
- **[[Eliminativism]]** — the family of positions to which Harari's claim belongs; where O'Gieblyn's eliminativists deny that *consciousness* is real, Harari denies that *freedom* is real, applying the same "empty term" reduction to the will rather than to subjective experience. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Eliminativism]] — Harari's free-will claim is eliminativism applied to volition: "freedom," like "soul," names nothing in the world.
- [[Algorithmic Determinism]] — the determinist horn of Harari's dilemma; brain processes "obey the same physical and chemical laws governing the rest of reality."
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — supplies the premise that the "free individual is just a fictional tale concocted by an assembly of biochemical algorithms."
- [[Dataism]] — the worldview in which engineered, externally readable desire (predicting choices "before the person is aware of their own intention") supersedes the liberal free chooser.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — Harari does not occupy a pole of this tension so much as try to collapse it: he denies that *either* determinism *or* randomness leaves room for freedom, so the tension's "free will" pole is, on his account, empty. ## Questions It Raises

- If "I don't choose my desires" but "only feel them," what is left of moral and legal responsibility — Harari notes that "Because he chose to" no longer "cut[s] the mustard" in explaining a murder. What replaces it?
- Harari grants we have free will if it merely means "the ability to act according to your desires." Is the disagreement substantive, or a stipulation about which definition of "free will" counts?
- If desire is authored by biochemistry and can be installed by "drugs, genetic engineering or direct brain stimulation," is the difference between persuasion (advertisers, "priests, spin doctors") and a transcranial helmet one of kind or only of efficiency?
- When "the supposedly free will of customers" becomes "just another product we can buy," who owns the means of desire-production — and does the language of "free choice" survive as marketing for engineered choice?
- Harari concedes the heretical insight that "there are no free individuals" need not change anything until "translated into everyday technology." Is free will as fiction a claim about metaphysics, or a forecast about technology?
