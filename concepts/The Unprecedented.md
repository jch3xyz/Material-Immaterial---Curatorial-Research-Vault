# The Unprecedented

## Brief Definition

Shoshana Zuboff's central epistemological move: surveillance capitalism is *sui generis* and therefore unrecognizable — because we interpret the genuinely new through familiar categories (the "horseless carriage," monopoly, privacy, totalitarianism), we render invisible precisely what is new, so fresh naming becomes the prerequisite for effective contest.

## Longer Explanation

For Zuboff, "the unprecedented" is not merely an adjective describing surveillance capitalism but the keystone of her method. "One explanation for surveillance capitalism's many triumphs floats above them all: it is unprecedented." The decisive claim follows immediately: "The unprecedented is necessarily unrecognizable. When we encounter something unprecedented, we automatically interpret it through the lenses of familiar categories, thereby rendering invisible precisely that which is unprecedented." The familiar lens does not merely fail to illuminate the new — it actively obscures it, "turning the unprecedented into an extension of the past" and so contributing to "the normalization of the abnormal." `Directly stated`

The anchoring image is the "horseless carriage": the notion "to which people reverted when confronted with the unprecedented facts of the automobile." Naming the automobile by what it replaced — a carriage minus its horse — keeps perception tethered to the superseded category and blinds the observer to a genuinely new kind of object and a genuinely new social order. Zuboff doubles the image with a tragic historical illustration, the Taínos of the pre-Columbian Caribbean who, "unable to imagine their own destruction," took the armored Spanish soldiers for gods and "welcomed them with intricate rituals of hospitality." We, she argues, occupy the Taínos' position: "Like the Tainos, we faced something altogether new to our story: the unprecedented. And, like them, we risk catastrophe when we assess new threats through the lens of old experience." `Directly stated`

The political stakes are precise. "The unprecedented nature of surveillance capitalism has enabled it to elude systematic contest because it cannot be adequately grasped with our existing concepts." We reach for "categories such as 'monopoly' or 'privacy'" — and while those issues are "vital," the existing categories "fall short in identifying and contesting the most crucial and unprecedented facts of this new regime." The same syndrome afflicts the later analysis of power: [[Instrumentarian Power]] "has defied comprehension in part because it has been subjected to the 'horselesscarriage' syndrome," "viewed through the old lenses of totalitarianism, obscuring what is different and dangerous." Totalitarianism was "a transformation of the state into a project of total possession"; instrumentarianism and its materialization in [[Big Other]] are "the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty" — a different thing wearing a borrowed name. `Directly stated`

The argument is therefore self-justifying for the book as a whole: if the unprecedented is unrecognizable through inherited categories, then fresh naming is not ornament but the precondition of resistance. This is why *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* coins so much vocabulary ([[Behavioral Surplus]], the prediction imperative, [[Big Other]], instrumentarianism, [[Surveillance Capitalism]] itself): each neologism is an attempt to make the invisible visible so that it can be contested. Zuboff aligns herself with earlier theorists who "encountered the unprecedented in their time" — Durkheim, Marx, and Weber on industrial capitalism, and Arendt, Adorno, Polanyi, Sartre, and Milgram who "struggled to name the unprecedented" of totalitarianism — positioning her own act of naming as the same kind of intellectual labor. `Strongly implied`

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins and owns the move. "The unprecedented" is the epistemological hinge of the whole book: surveillance capitalism is genuinely new, hence unrecognizable through monopoly/privacy/totalitarianism, hence in need of new names before it can be fought. The "horseless carriage" and the Taínos are her twin emblems of the failure mode.
- **[[Yuval Noah Harari]]** (resonance, not citation) — Harari likewise warns that the data-driven future ([[Dataism]], [[Big Data]]) will outrun our inherited liberal-humanist categories; where Zuboff insists on *naming* the new economic logic, Harari frames the gap as one between obsolete philosophy and new technological reality. `Interpretive synthesis`
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** (resonance, not citation) — Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] is a deliberate *re-naming* against an old lens: he argues the digital regime of voluntary self-exposure cannot be grasped through Bentham's/Orwell's coercive panopticon and must be re-described from the side of the willing subject. The gesture parallels Zuboff's anti-"horseless-carriage" method, though Han brackets the economic motive Zuboff foregrounds. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — the unprecedented *thing* this concept says we cannot see; the master economic logic whose novelty motivates Zuboff's whole naming project.
- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — explicitly subjected to the "horselesscarriage syndrome," misread through the old lens of totalitarianism.
- [[Big Other]] — the materialization of instrumentarian power; "the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty," distinct from totalitarianism's "total possession."
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — one of the neologisms minted to render the unprecedented visible and contestable.
- [[Digital Panopticon]] · [[Dataism]] · [[Big Data]] — adjacent attempts (Han, Harari) to re-name digital-era power and knowledge against inherited categories.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the "horseless carriage" error is itself a category-mismatch about *what kind of thing* a new object is; surveillance capitalism is misperceived in part because its raw material (lived, material human experience converted into immaterial behavioral data) has no settled name on the vault's object→system / material→immaterial spine.

## Questions It Raises

- If the unprecedented is "necessarily unrecognizable," how does Zuboff herself escape the trap — what licenses her claim to have named it correctly rather than supplying another "horseless carriage"?
- Is fresh naming actually sufficient for "systematic contest," or can a regime absorb its own new names (as surveillance capitalism arguably absorbed "surveillance," "data," and even "privacy") without losing power?
- Where is the line between a genuinely unprecedented phenomenon and a familiar one (monopoly, dispossession, totalitarianism) that merely *looks* new — i.e., when is the "horseless carriage" diagnosis right and when does it overclaim novelty?
- Does the Taínos analogy frame the public as fated victims, and if so does it undercut the book's call to contest the unprecedented through understanding?
