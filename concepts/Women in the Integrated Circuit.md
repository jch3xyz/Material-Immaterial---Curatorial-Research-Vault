# Women in the Integrated Circuit

## Brief Definition

Haraway's title figure — borrowed from Rachel Grossman (1980) — for the dispersed, contradictory positions women occupy within a world "intimately restructured through the social relations of science and technology," where there is "no 'place' for women in these networks, only geometries of difference and contradiction crucial to women's cyborg identities."

## Longer Explanation

"Women in the integrated circuit" is the chapter (ch. 05) in which Haraway maps women's "historical locations in advanced industrial societies" onto the "scary new networks" she elsewhere calls the [[Informatics|informatics of domination]]. She opens by rejecting the older ideology that characterized "women's lives by the distinction of public and private domains" — factory/home, market/home, personal/political — as "now a totally misleading ideology." In its place she proposes "a network ideological image, suggesting the profusion of spaces and identities and the permeability of boundaries in the personal body and in the body politic." `Directly stated` The image deliberately doubles itself: "'Networking' is both a feminist practice and a multinational corporate strategy — weaving is for oppositional cyborgs." `Directly stated`

The figure is provenanced precisely. In ch. 03 Haraway writes: "I have used Rachel Grossman's (1980) image of women in the integrated circuit to name the situation of women in a world so intimately restructured through the social relations of science and technology." `Directly stated` The qualifier matters to her: she "used the odd circumlocution the social relations of science and technology to indicate that we are not dealing with a technological determinism, but with a historical system depending on structured relations among people." `Directly stated` The integrated-circuit figure thus names not a machine but a *position within a structured social field* — the gendered, raced, classed underside of the transition she charts from "white capitalist patriarchy" to the [[Informatics|informatics of domination]], one line of which reads "family/market/factory → women in the integrated circuit." `Directly stated`

To trace "one vision of women's 'place' in the integrated circuit" she walks "a few idealized social locations seen primarily from the point of view of advanced capitalist societies: Home, Market, Paid Workplace, State, School, Clinic-Hospital, and Church." `Directly stated` Crucially, these are not separable spheres: "Each of these idealized spaces is logically and practically implied in every other locus, perhaps analogous to a holographic photograph." `Directly stated` The seven loci mutually constitute one another, so that — as ch. 03 puts it — "the home, workplace, market, public arena, the body itself — all can be dispersed and interfaced in nearly infinite, polymorphous ways, with large consequences for women and others." `Directly stated`

The political punchline is a refusal of identity-as-ground. Across the seven loci "there is no 'place' for women in these networks, only geometries of difference and contradiction crucial to women's cyborg identities." `Directly stated` There is, Haraway insists, "no way to read the following list from a standpoint of 'identification,' of a unitary self. The issue is dispersion. The task is to survive in the diaspora." `Directly stated` Yet dispersion is not only defeat: "If we learn how to read these webs of power and social life, we might learn new couplings, new coalitions." `Directly stated` The figure therefore folds critique into possibility — the same gendered network that integrates and exploits women is the ground on which "oppositional cyborgs" might weave new alliances. `Strongly implied`

### The Network-Ideological Image

Against the public/private dichotomy, Haraway prefers "a network ideological image." `Directly stated` Its first virtue is descriptive accuracy — boundaries between "the personal body and ... the body politic" are now permeable — and its second is strategic ambivalence: "networking" belongs *at once* to feminist organizing and to "multinational corporate strategy." `Directly stated` Weaving the same web from below is what she calls the work of "oppositional cyborgs," tying this concept to [[The Cyborg]] as a figure of political identity rather than essence. `Strongly implied`

### The Holographic Photograph

The seven loci are mutually implied "perhaps analogous to a holographic photograph" — Haraway's figure for a totality in which every part already contains the whole. `Directly stated` It is the formal counterpart to her refusal of "a unitary self": because each locus implies every other, no single standpoint (Home, Workplace, etc.) yields the truth of women's situation, and the only adequate reading is one that holds the contradictions of the whole circuit at once. `Interpretive synthesis`

### Surveillance and Citizenship by Telematics

The "State" locus catalogues the political-technological stakes most directly relevant to the [[Digital Panopticon]] cluster: "Continued erosion of the welfare state; decentralizations with increased surveillance and control; citizenship by telematics; imperialism and political power broadly in the form of information-rich/information-poor differentiation." `Directly stated` The "Market" locus adds "surveillance systems through electronic funds transfer." `Directly stated` Haraway here names, in 1985, a regime of dispersed, networked control keyed to informational asymmetry — a striking precursor to later diagnoses of surveillance, control-by-information, and citizenship reconstituted through telematic infrastructure. `Speculative connection` Unlike Han's voluntarist [[Digital Panopticon]], Haraway frames the same apparatus through *gendered and raced distribution*: surveillance and control are not generic but fall unevenly across the seven loci of women's lives. `Interpretive synthesis`

## Questions It Raises

- If there is "no 'place' for women" in the networks, only "geometries of difference and contradiction," what kind of politics — coalition rather than identity — does survival "in the diaspora" require?
- Networking is "both a feminist practice and a multinational corporate strategy": how does an oppositional practice avoid being reabsorbed by the very corporate networks it borrows its form from?
- Haraway's 1985 "citizenship by telematics" and "surveillance systems through electronic funds transfer" anticipate the [[Digital Panopticon]]: what is gained by reading contemporary surveillance through *gendered and raced distribution* rather than as a generic apparatus?
- If the seven loci are "logically and practically implied in every other locus ... analogous to a holographic photograph," can any single standpoint ground critique, or only the contradictory whole?
