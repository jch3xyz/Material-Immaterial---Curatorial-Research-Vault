# The Materiality of Informatics

## Brief Definition

Hayles's constructive counter-thesis — and the title of chapter 8 — that the apparently immaterial realm of information is produced by, and crossed with, material and embodied conditions, so that the *illusion* of disembodiment, rather than disembodiment itself, becomes the proper object of inquiry.

## Longer Explanation

Where [[The Body as Data]] names the fantasy Hayles contests, *the materiality of informatics* names the method she puts in its place. The phrase is deliberately oxymoronic: "informatics" is precisely the network of relations that an ideology of dematerialization presents as weightless and placeless, yet Hayles insists it has a *materiality*. Her sharpest formulation comes at the end of chapter 2, where she warns against mistaking the cultural dominance of [[Pattern and Randomness|pattern and randomness]] for the disappearance of matter: "The pattern/randomness dialectic does not erase the material world; information in fact derives its efficacy from the material infrastructures it appears to obscure. This illusion of erasure should be the subject of inquiry, not a presupposition that inquiry takes for granted." This single sentence carries the whole program. The shift toward pattern is real and consequential, but it does not abolish presence; on the contrary, information becomes *effective* only by running through the very infrastructures — interfaces, media, bodies — whose disappearance it stages. The analyst's task is therefore not to celebrate or mourn disembodiment but to ask how the appearance of erasure is manufactured.

The deep reason information can never float free is stated in chapter 1 and threaded through the book: "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium." To conceive of information "as a thing separate from the medium instantiating it" is, for Hayles, a "prior imaginary act" that splits a single phenomenon into a spurious information/matter duality. The materiality of informatics is the refusal of that split. It applies equally to texts and to bodies: the book, like the human body, incorporates its encodings in a durable material substrate, so that "books and humans have something to lose if they are regarded solely as informational patterns, namely the resistant materiality" that marks both durable inscription and lived embodiment. To recover that materiality is to recover what "had to be elided, suppressed, and forgotten to make information lose its body."

In chapter 8 Hayles gives the thesis its title and its diagnostic edge. The "ecstatic pronouncements" of theorists who declare the body superfluous (Baudrillard, the Krokers, Hardison echoing Moravec) are read not as evidence that the body has vanished but as symptoms: "This subjectivity is constituted by the crossing of the materiality of informatics with the immateriality of information." The crucial move is reflexive — "The very theorists who most emphatically claim that the body is disappearing also operate within material and cultural circumstances that make the claim for the body’s disappearance seem plausible." Dematerialization "depends in complex and highly specific ways on the embodied circumstances that an ideology of dematerialization would obscure." Hayles even turns this analysis on Foucault: the Panopticon's power comes precisely from abstracting power "out of the bodies of disciplinarians into a universal, disembodied gaze," so that his account "participates in, as well as deconstructs, the Panoptic move of disembodiment." The lesson is general — it is "possible to deconstruct the content of the abstraction while still leaving the mechanism of abstraction intact." Hayles wants to dismantle the *mechanism*.

Methodologically, the materiality of informatics is operationalized through two interacting polarities Hayles introduces in chapter 8: body/embodiment and [[Inscription vs Incorporation|inscription/incorporation]]. The body and inscription are the normalized, abstract, transportable terms; [[Embodiment|embodiment]] and incorporation are the instantiated, contextual, "improvisational" terms that "can never be cut entirely free from its context." By reading texts (Burroughs's *The Ticket That Exploded*, where "the body materializes discourse") through this grid, she demonstrates that abstraction and embodiment, far from being separable layers, are in "constant and dynamic interplay." In the closing map of the posthuman (chapter 10), materiality reappears as the synthetic term that emerges "from the interplay between presence and absence" — meant "to refer both to the signifying power of materialities and to the materiality of signifying processes" — securing materiality a permanent place in the semiotics of [[Virtuality|virtuality]] rather than letting it be displaced by [[Information (Hayles)|information]].

## Authors and Variants

- **[[N. Katherine Hayles]]** — coins and develops the thesis as the constructive half of *How We Became Posthuman*. The materiality of informatics is her standing rebuttal to disembodiment ideology: information is always instantiated, its efficacy derives from the infrastructures it obscures, and the illusion of erasure is the object — not the premise — of analysis.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[The Body as Data]] — the fantasy this thesis contests: that the body is essentially a disposable instantiation of a code. The materiality of informatics is the methodological inversion — it takes the *erasure* of the body as a constructed illusion to be excavated, not as a fact to be assumed. Together they name the two sides of [[Embodiment vs Information]].

## Questions It Raises

- If "this illusion of erasure should be the subject of inquiry, not a presupposition," what analytic methods actually make material infrastructures visible again once they have been rendered transparent? (ties to information vs materiality)
- Does treating disembodiment as an *ideology* with material conditions risk reinstalling the body/embodiment distinction as just another abstraction — the very "mechanism of abstraction" Hayles warns against? (ties to embodiment; the body as data)
- If information "derives its efficacy from the material infrastructures it appears to obscure," is the posthuman privileging of pattern over instantiation simply false, or is it a productive cultural illusion that nonetheless does real work? (ties to posthumanism; information vs materiality)
- When "subjectivity is constituted by the crossing of the materiality of informatics with the immateriality of information," is the crossing a stable synthesis or a permanent tension — and what kind of subject is produced at the crossing? (ties to the body as data; posthumanism)
- How far does the reflexive critique generalize: if even Foucault "participates in, as well as deconstructs, the Panoptic move of disembodiment," can any theory of information escape complicity with the dematerialization it describes? (ties to embodiment)
