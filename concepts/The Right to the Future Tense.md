# The Right to the Future Tense

## Brief Definition

The elemental right, bearing directly on individual sovereignty and grounding free will, that names a person's capacity to imagine, intend, promise, and construct a future — a right Shoshana Zuboff argues is endangered by surveillance capitalism's architecture of behavior modification.

## Longer Explanation

For Zuboff the right to the future tense is not a constitutional nicety but a precondition of a fully human life. It is the assertion, implicit in every promise we make, that "through my will I can influence the future" — not total authority over what comes, but authority over "my piece of it." She grounds the right in the act of willing: the *will to will*, the capacity to commit oneself to a desired future "that is not yet real" and then to close "the gap between making promises and keeping them." To be able to say "I will" — and to mean it as a binding claim on tomorrow — is, on this account, the very form that freedom of will takes in time (Directly stated, Ch.1 / Ch.11).

The threat Zuboff diagnoses is structural rather than merely intrusive. Surveillance capitalism's economic imperatives drive it from the mere *extraction* of human experience toward *intervention* in that experience — the engineering of behavior at scale "as the means of production is subordinated to a new and more complex means of behavior modification." When a firm declares "the right to modify human action secretly and for profit," it does more than watch: it relocates authorship of the future. In Zuboff's formulation surveillance capitalism "effectively exiles us from our own behavior, shifting the locus of control over the future tense from 'I will' to 'You will'" (Directly stated, Ch.10). The result is what she calls a "panvasive digital architecture of behavior modification" that endangers the elemental right by guaranteeing outcomes — substituting certainty manufactured for profit in place of the open future a free will requires (Directly stated, Ch.11).

The right is therefore the hinge between this book's surveillance analysis and the older question of the will. The capacity to promise presupposes an indeterminate future that the self can help author; a system engineered for "guaranteed outcomes" forecloses exactly that indeterminacy, which is why Zuboff insists the right to the future tense is "an essential condition of free will." This places the concept squarely on the existing tension [[Free Will vs Determinism]]: behavioral futures markets do not refute free will philosophically so much as attempt to engineer it away economically (Interpretive synthesis). It also extends the vault's surveillance cluster — Han's [[Digital Panopticon]], the [[Big Data]] / [[Dataism]] complex of Han and Harari — by adding a *temporal* axis: not only the visibility of the self but the captured ownership of its tomorrow.

Zuboff instantiates the abstraction in a concrete struggle. The book's "stage-setting" episode is the Spanish right-to-be-forgotten case, where the Montes family and "millions of other Spaniards" were "compelled to claw back the future from the self-proclaimed inevitability of indifferent capital." It was there, she writes, that "the right to the future tense was on the move," asserting that surveillance capitalism and its digital architecture "are not, never were, and never would be inevitable" (Directly stated, Ch.2). The right to be forgotten thus appears as the right to the future tense in legal-political motion: a refusal of the claim that a person's data-rendered past must determine their available future.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins and develops the concept as the keystone of her account of surveillance capitalism. The right comprises, in her words, "our will to will, our autonomy, our decision rights, our privacy, and, indeed, our human natures." Its endangerment is the central harm the book names; "naming and taming are inextricable."

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the right to the future tense is "an essential condition of free will"; surveillance capitalism's "guaranteed outcomes" represent an *engineered* determinism that the right is invoked to resist.

## Questions It Raises

- If free will requires an open future, can a right framed around individual willing withstand a power that operates at population scale and "behind the screen"?
- Is the right to be forgotten a sufficient legal instrument for a structural harm, or only a single front in a larger contest over the future tense?
- How does Zuboff's "I will" / "You will" distinction relate to Han's account of voluntary self-exposure, where the subject seems to *will its own* exposure?
- Does naming behavior modification as a violation of an "elemental right" risk reasserting a liberal-humanist sovereign self that other authors in the vault (Hayles, Haraway) have placed under question?
