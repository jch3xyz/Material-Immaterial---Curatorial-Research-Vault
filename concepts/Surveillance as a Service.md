# Surveillance as a Service

## Brief Definition

Zuboff's coinage (abbreviated SVaaS): surveillance — the capture, analysis, and sale of behavioral surplus about individuals — offered as a standalone commercial product line, which she argues is a more accurate label than "software-as-a-service" (SaaS) for the firms that practice it.

## Longer Explanation

In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, Zuboff introduces "surveillance as a service" to name a trend in which behavioral-surplus extraction is no longer the hidden underside of an ostensibly free service (search, social networking) but has "become a service in its own right." She deliberately puns on the industry's own self-description: such firms call themselves "software-as-a-service," but, she insists, "they are more accurately termed 'surveillance as a service,' or 'SVaaS.'" The renaming strips away the euphemism and exposes the actual product being sold. *(Directly stated.)*

Her examples make the category concrete and show that it spans markets far from the born-digital giants. App-based lenders "instantly establish creditworthiness based on detailed mining of an individual's smartphone and other online behaviors" — down to intimate signals like how often you charge your battery or whether you return phone calls — to build predictive default models. Vetting firms sell "deep vetting of potential employees and tenants to employers and landlords," scraping a prospective tenant's social media to produce a report cataloguing "everything from your personality to your 'financial stress level,'" including protected-status information like pregnancy and age. Firms like hiQ assign "flight risk" scores to employees so employers can preemptively retain or terminate them, and location-data brokers like Safegraph amass trillions of device-location markers to sell. *(Directly stated.)*

The pattern is structurally significant for Zuboff's larger argument. SVaaS demonstrates that surveillance capitalism is a generalizable *logic of accumulation* and not merely a quirk of companies that happened to be born on the internet: "Surveillance capitalism was born digital, but... it is no longer confined to born-digital companies." The same firms that pioneered the [[The Extraction Imperative|extraction imperative]] established a template that any business can lease, license, or buy into. SVaaS is thus the economic proof that [[Behavioral Surplus|behavioral surplus]] has become a tradable raw material with its own dedicated supply markets. *(Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis.)*

Zuboff is pointed about who bears the cost. The same chapter ends the "myth of 'free'": "every consumer who pays his or her monthly telecom bill now also purchases the privilege of a remote and abstract but nevertheless rapacious digital strip search." And across her SVaaS examples it is "those who have less money and fewer options who are trapped in this Faustian bargain in which privacy is forfeit to social participation" — privacy becomes "luxuries that too many people cannot afford." SVaaS therefore distributes surveillance regressively, intensifying it precisely where economic vulnerability is greatest. *(Directly stated.)*

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** — coins SVaaS as a corrective re-naming of "software-as-a-service." It is one specialized expression of [[Surveillance Capitalism]]: the productization and resale of [[Behavioral Surplus]] by firms whose core offering *is* surveillance (app-based lenders, tenant/employee vetting services, flight-risk scorers, location-data brokers). Evidence that the surveillance logic is portable across industries, not native to any one platform.

## Related Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — SVaaS is the parent logic packaged and sold as a discrete product; the firms are surveillance capitalism's downstream wholesalers and retailers.
- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — the raw material that SVaaS firms capture, fabricate into prediction products, and trade.
- [[The Extraction Imperative]] — the drive for ever more behavioral data that SVaaS operationalizes as a standalone business model.
- [[Surveillance]] — the broad operation SVaaS commercializes; this concept names the moment surveillance itself becomes the marketed deliverable.
- [[Quantified Self]] — SVaaS turns the granular self-data of the quantified life (battery charges, GPS traces, message frequency) into externally owned prediction products without the subject's meaningful consent.
- [[The Transparent Customer]] — the individual rendered fully legible and "knowable" to lenders, landlords, and employers is the human end-product of SVaaS.
- [[Big Data]] — SVaaS is one industrial form Big Data takes once behavioral data become a freely supplied, salable commodity.
- [[Dataism]] — the worldview that all behavior reduces to extractable data underwrites the market SVaaS sells into.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — listed under both related and opposing because Zuboff's renaming is itself an act of opposition: SVaaS unmasks the benign industry self-label ("software-as-a-service") and re-describes it in the terms of the thing it actually does, which she critiques. The opposition is rhetorical and political, not categorical.

## Questions It Raises

- If surveillance can be packaged as a service for any industry, is there a meaningful boundary left between "tech companies" and the rest of the economy — or has the extraction logic become the default operating system of commerce?
- When privacy becomes a "luxury that too many people cannot afford," does consent ("opt in") retain any moral force, and what would non-regressive regulation of SVaaS markets look like?
- How does SVaaS differ, if at all, from older credit-scoring, tenant-screening, and background-check industries — is Zuboff naming a genuinely new kind of firm or re-describing an intensified continuation of one?
- Does naming the product "surveillance" rather than "software" change the legal and political accountability of these firms, and is the renaming itself a form of resistance?
