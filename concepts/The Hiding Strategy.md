# The Hiding Strategy

## Brief Definition

Eric Schmidt's deliberate, structural concealment of Google's surveillance operations — secrecy institutionalized in policy and "baked into the cake" of surveillance capitalism rather than improvised after the fact, designed to protect operations that were themselves designed to be undetectable.

## Longer Explanation

In *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, Shoshana Zuboff names the **hiding strategy** as the moment secrecy was converted from a contingent habit into a load-bearing institution. Once Google's leadership grasped the commercial power of [[Behavioral Surplus]], CEO Eric Schmidt formalized concealment as policy: employees were told not to speak about the firm's "novel methods, apparatus, message formats and/or data structures" or confirm rumors of its revenue. Zuboff's decisive claim is temporal and structural — hiding "was not a post hoc strategy; it was baked into the cake that would become surveillance capitalism." It is not a reaction to scrutiny but a precondition of accumulation: "Behavioral surplus was necessary for revenue, and secrecy would be necessary for the sustained accumulation of behavioral surplus." (`Directly stated`)

The hiding strategy is the institutional counterpart to two other figures in Zuboff's account: the **euphemism** that linguistically launders extraction, and the **one-way mirror**, the architecture that lets the firm see without being seen. Zuboff describes Google's automated apparatus as one that "operates as a one-way mirror irrespective of a person's awareness, knowledge, and consent, thus enabling privileged secret access to behavioral data," and she identifies the social form this serves: "A one-way mirror embodies the specific social relations of surveillance based on asymmetries of knowledge and power." The hiding strategy is the deliberate management of that asymmetry — the engine, not merely a side effect, of the knowledge-and-power imbalance between watcher and watched. The asymmetry is the [[The Transparent Customer|transparent customer]] made operational: the user rendered fully visible while the operation rendering them is held opaque. (`Directly stated` for the quotes; `Interpretive synthesis` for the euphemism/one-way-mirror triangulation)

Crucially, Zuboff distinguishes two registers of motive. There are "always sound business reasons for hiding the location of your gold mine," and the strategy did accrue competitive advantage — Schmidt also leaned on the "scientific and material complexity" of the operation as "an invisibility cloak over the whole operation" and a "very serious barrier to entry." But Zuboff insists the deeper reason is legitimacy and consent: "Google policies had to enforce secrecy in order to protect operations that were designed to be undetectable because they took things from users without asking and employed those unilaterally claimed resources to work in the service of others' purposes." Hiding protects undetectability; undetectability protects unilateral expropriation. (`Directly stated`)

The hiding strategy therefore underwrites the larger asymmetric order Zuboff calls the [[The Division of Learning in Society]] and the dominance she calls [[Instrumentarian Power]]: who knows, who decides, and who decides who decides are all settled in the firm's favor precisely because the operation cannot be seen. That Google "had the power to choose secrecy is itself testament to the success of its own claims" — concealment is both instrument and proof of the firm's accumulated power. (`Strongly implied`)

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Shoshana Zuboff]]** ([[The Age of Surveillance Capitalism]]) — coins the term, attributing it to Eric Schmidt, and treats it not as corporate PR discretion but as a *constitutive* mechanism of surveillance capitalism: secrecy structurally required by an accumulation logic premised on taking "things from users without asking." It is the institutional sibling of euphemism (linguistic concealment) and the one-way mirror (architectural concealment).
- **[[Byung-Chul Han]]** — provides the contrast case. Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] runs on *voluntary self-exposure*; the watched illuminate themselves. Zuboff's hiding strategy supplies the other half of the same asymmetry: while the watched are made transparent, the watcher is deliberately, institutionally concealed. The two accounts together name a regime of total visibility for the user and engineered opacity for the operator. (`Interpretive synthesis`)

## Related Concepts

- [[Behavioral Surplus]] — what the strategy exists to conceal; secrecy is the condition of its "sustained accumulation."

- [[Instrumentarian Power]] — the form of dominance secured by managing the asymmetry of knowledge that hiding produces.

- [[The Transparent Customer]] — the obverse: the user made fully legible while the operation is hidden.
- [[Surveillance]] — the practice the strategy renders invisible; "unilateral surveillance of online behavior."
- [[Digital Panopticon]] — Han's complementary diagnosis of voluntary visibility from below.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Knowledge vs Power]] — not an opposite so much as the axis the hiding strategy operates *along*: it deliberately widens the asymmetry of knowledge that converts into power. Listed here because the strategy is the mechanism by which the watcher's secrecy and the watched's transparency are kept structurally opposed.

## Questions It Raises

- If secrecy is "baked into the cake," can surveillance capitalism ever be made transparent without dismantling the accumulation logic itself, or is reform that demands disclosure a category error?
- How does the hiding strategy differ in kind from ordinary trade-secrecy ("hiding the location of your gold mine"), and does Zuboff's distinction between competitive-advantage motives and consent-evasion motives hold?
- Is the asymmetry of knowledge a contingent corporate choice — "the power to choose secrecy" — or an irreducible feature of any large-scale predictive apparatus?
- What is the relationship between concealment (hiding the operation), euphemism (renaming it), and undetectability (designing it not to be seen) — three layers of a single strategy, or distinct mechanisms?
