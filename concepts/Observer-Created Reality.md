# Observer-Created Reality

## Brief Definition

The position that reality is not found but constructed in consciousness through the act of observation — to look is to make a both/and situation collapse into a single either/or outcome — which Roy Ascott takes as the deep physical-philosophical model for an interactive art in which the observer constructs the work.

## Longer Explanation

For Ascott, "observer-created reality" is the quantum-measurement intuition (Schrödinger's Cat, the wave-function collapse) read as a general truth about consciousness and then turned into an aesthetic program. Before measurement the boxed cat is both dead and alive; the act of observation forces the system to "jump" from the superposed both/and to a determinate either/or. Ascott names the determinate result by its physics term — the **eigenstate** — and treats the jump itself as the moment reality is *made* rather than merely registered (`Directly stated`). His central move is to relocate the question from physics to art: if reality is created at the point of observation, then "human consciousness is seen as the instrument for creating reality," and art's task is no longer to depict a ready-made world but to design the contexts in which observers construct one.

Ascott assembles this model from four sources, all named in the chapter (`Directly stated`). **Erwin Schrödinger** supplies the cat and the paradox of measurement. The SF novelist **Greg Egan**, in *Quarantine* (1992), places the point of collapse "right in the brain" by imagining a brain "mod" that blocks the eigenstate effect and so prevents the wave function from collapsing — giving, in Ascott's phrase, "a post-biological context to the idea that reality is constructed." **Heinz von Foerster**, in his 1973 lecture "On Constructing a Reality," argues that "the environment, as we perceive it, is our invention." And **Paul Watzlawick**'s radical constructivism (1984) Ascott names as the philosophy "directly relevant to the interactive art aesthetic." The physics (Schrödinger/Egan) and the cybernetic epistemology (von Foerster/Watzlawick) converge on one claim: the observer is constitutive of what is observed.

This is why observer-created reality underwrites, rather than merely accompanies, Ascott's theory of interactive art. If the observer makes the world by looking, the artwork must become a system that the observer's behaviour completes — "responsive in important ways to the behaviour of its user," a "structural coupling between everyone and everything within the Net." The work is no longer a window onto a pre-existing reality but a doorway the observer enters; "to construct art is to construct reality." The concept thus binds tightly to `Radical Constructivism (Ascott)` (its explicit epistemological frame) and feeds his accounts of `Reciprocal Communication`, `New Media Art`, and `Art as Experiential Technology` (`Interpretive synthesis`).

Ascott also gives the idea a **post-biological** edge. Egan's fictional collapse-blocker locates the measurement event in neural tissue, and Ascott extrapolates: our interfaces are "moving closer and closer to the body and into the brain," so the technology that constructs reality is migrating inward. The construction of reality becomes, for him, a literal frontier of the nervous system, not only a philosophical claim about perception (`Strongly implied`).

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** (*Telematic Embrace*, ch. "From Appearance to Apparition," 1993) — "observer-created reality" is the deep model for interactive art: the quantum both/and→either/or jump (the eigenstate) generalised, via Schrödinger, Egan, von Foerster, and Watzlawick, into the thesis that consciousness is "the instrument for creating reality" and that therefore "to construct art is to construct reality."
- **Meghan O'Gieblyn** (cross-author parallel, `Interpretive synthesis`) — Ascott's wave-function-collapse-at-observation is the same physics O'Gieblyn treats under `The Measurement Problem`; his "reality is constructed in consciousness" is a constructivist cousin of the `Idealism` she canvasses. Ascott pushes it toward art-making rather than metaphysics of mind.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — observer-created reality belongs to the immaterial/apparition pole (reality as construction, "the invisible and immaterial") against the appearance/material pole of a ready-made world disclosed to a passive retinal gaze.

## Questions It Raises

- If the observer creates reality at the point of looking, is the artwork ever finished — or only ever "in flux," reconstituted by each act of observation?
- Where does the collapse actually happen? Ascott concedes "there is no agreement among physicists about precisely where… the measurement result is ultimately registered" — does Egan's brain-sited collapse smuggle a strong (idealist) reading into what is, in physics, an open question?
- Does generalising quantum measurement to all of consciousness equivocate between a technical sense of "observer" (any measuring apparatus) and a phenomenological one (a conscious viewer of art)?
- If "to construct art is to construct reality," what stabilises shared meaning — or is the price of observer-created reality a "celebration of semiotic instability"?
