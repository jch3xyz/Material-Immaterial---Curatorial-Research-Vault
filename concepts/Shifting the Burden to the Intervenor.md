# Shifting the Burden to the Intervenor

## Brief Definition

The addiction-or-dependence system trap: an intervenor takes over a system's own corrective load, and if the intervention lets the system's self-maintaining capacity atrophy, a destructive [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] is set in motion in which ever more intervention is required to hold the system in its desired state.

## Longer Explanation

In [[Donella Meadows]]'s *Thinking in Systems*, "shifting the burden to the intervenor" is one of the recurring system archetypes — what she also calls **system traps** — treated in Chapter Five. It is the structural name for addiction, and Meadows's point is that the same structure governs the chemical case and the systemic case alike: "This trap is known by many names: addiction, dependence, shifting the burden to the intervenor." Most people recognize "the addictive properties of alcohol, nicotine, caffeine, sugar, and heroin," she notes, but "not everyone recognizes that addiction can appear in larger systems and in other guises—such as the dependence of industry on government subsidy, the reliance of farmers on fertilizers, the addiction of Western economies to cheap oil or weapons manufacturers to government contracts." The trap does not care what the "drug" is; heroin, a subsidy, a fertilizer, and a price support are all the same move.

The mechanism begins with an ordinary [[Balancing Feedback Loop]]. A system has "a stock with in-flows and out-flows"; the stock "is maintained by an actor adjusting a balancing feedback loop," comparing a goal against "a perception of the actual state of the stock to determine what action to take." When that corrective process is "doing a poor (or even so-so) job," an intervenor may step in: "A well-meaning and efficient intervenor watches the struggle and steps in to take some of the load. The intervenor quickly brings the system to the state everybody wants it to be in." This is why Meadows is careful to say the move is not inherently bad — "Shifting a burden to an intervenor can be a good thing," even necessary; "Some systems really need an intervenor!" The trap is not the intervention but a *side effect* of it. Because "nothing has been done to solve it at its root cause," the original problem reappears, the intervenor applies more of the "solution," which "disguis[es] the real state of the system again," and more solution is then needed. Crucially, the states that interventions produce "don’t last. The intoxication wears off. The subsidy is spent. The fertilizer is consumed or washed away."

What converts this from a nuisance into a trap is atrophy. "The trap is formed if the intervention, whether by active destruction or simple neglect, undermines the original capacity of the system to maintain itself. If that capability atrophies, then more of the intervention is needed to achieve the desired effect. That weakens the capability of the original system still more." At that moment the governing balancing loop has been overwhelmed by a self-amplifying one: "If the intervention designed to correct the problem causes the self-maintaining capacity of the original system to atrophy or erode, then a destructive reinforcing feedback loop is set in motion. The system deteriorates; more and more of the solution is then required. The system will become more and more dependent on the intervention and less and less able to maintain its own desired state." This is the diagnostic signature Meadows attaches to addiction generally — "Addiction is finding a quick and dirty solution to the symptom of the problem, which prevents or distracts one from the harder and longer-term task of solving the real problem" — and it is why, as she puts it, "Addictive policies are insidious, because they are so easy to sell, so simple to fall for."

Because the trap is a structure and not a substance, Meadows illustrates it across registers that would otherwise seem unrelated: care of the aged shifting from families to Social Security and nursing homes; short-haul transport shifting from railroads and streetcars to government-built highways; arithmetic shifting from the head to the calculator; natural disease immunity shifting to vaccines and drugs; monoculture farming shifting from soil fertility to pesticides that "bring back the bugs in greater outbursts, requiring more pesticides in the future"; and the response to rising oil prices — "Rather than acknowledge the inevitable depletion of a nonrenewable resource and increase fuel efficiency or switch to other fuels, we can fix the price... making the depletion problem worse. When that policy breaks down, we can go to war for oil." The exit is the same in every case, and it is counterintuitive: not more skillful intervention but a *withdrawal* that first rebuilds the system's own capacity. "The problem can be avoided up front by intervening in such a way as to strengthen the ability of the system to shoulder its own burdens." Meadows's way-out is explicit about sequence: "If you are the intervenor, work in such a way as to restore or enhance the system’s own ability to solve its problems, then remove yourself. If you are the one with an unsupportable dependency, build your system’s own capabilities back up before removing the intervention. Do it right away. The longer you wait, the harder the withdrawal process will be." This makes the trap a mirror image of the vault's other systems structures: where [[Homeostasis]] is a healthy balancing loop that keeps a system near its goal, shifting-the-burden is what happens when that self-correcting capacity is quietly transferred out of the system and left to rot.

## Authors and Variants

- **[[Donella Meadows]]** — one of the system archetypes/traps of *Thinking in Systems* (Ch. 5), and her structural definition of addiction: an intervenor assumes a system's corrective load, and if the system's self-maintaining capacity is allowed to atrophy, a destructive [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] takes over so that "more and more of the solution is then required." Structurally identical whether the "drug" is heroin, a government subsidy, fertilizer monoculture, or an oil price support. The way out is to strengthen the system's own capacity first, then withdraw the intervention — the sooner the better, because "the longer you wait, the harder the withdrawal process will be."

## Related Concepts

- [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop]] — the destructive self-amplifying loop the trap sets in motion: as the system's own capacity atrophies, "more of the intervention is needed," which "weakens the capability of the original system still more."
- [[Balancing Feedback Loop]] — the original corrective loop the intervenor takes over; the trap is what happens when that loop's owner lets its own capacity to run the loop decay.
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the parent mechanism; the archetype is the moment a governing balancing loop is captured and overwhelmed by a reinforcing one.
- [[Shifting Dominance]] — the trap is a case of dominance shifting: from the system's own balancing loop to the reinforcing dependency loop between system and intervenor.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the archetype is one of the discipline's recurring structural templates, a "frequently found system structure that produces familiar behavior patterns."
- [[The Growth Imperative]] — Harari's mandate to keep growing is, structurally, a burden shifted onto perpetual intervention (subsidy, cheap oil, credit) rather than onto a system rebuilt to sustain itself.

## Opposing Concepts

- [[Homeostasis]] — the healthy end-state where a system's own balancing loop keeps it near its goal; shifting-the-burden is precisely the pathology in which that self-maintaining, homeostatic capacity is exported to an intervenor and allowed to atrophy.

## Questions It Raises

- Meadows insists "some systems really need an intervenor" — so is there any reliable way to tell, before the capacity atrophies, whether an intervention is building the system up or hollowing it out?
- If the same structure governs heroin, subsidy, fertilizer, and price supports, does the value-neutral systems account dissolve the moral distinction between them, or only relocate it into *how* the intervention is designed and withdrawn?
- When the intervenor is a technology we have "leased" the corrective load to (calculators for arithmetic, medicine for health), is the atrophy of the system's own capacity a bug of the trap or the very definition of an extension of man?
- If "the longer you wait, the harder the withdrawal process will be," what makes withdrawal so reliably deferred — is it the intervenor's self-congratulation, the dependent's fear of confronting "the real (and usually much deteriorated) state of the system," or both loops reinforcing each other?
