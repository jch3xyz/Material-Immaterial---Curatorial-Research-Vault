# God Human Animal Machine

**Author:** [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]
**Full title:** *God, Human, Animal, Machine: Technology, Metaphor, and the Search for Meaning* (2021)
**Raw folder:** `raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/`

## Brief Orientation

*God, Human, Animal, Machine* is Meghan O'Gieblyn's memoir-essay on what happened to theology's eternal questions once the world became "disenchanted." Trained in theology at a fundamentalist Bible college and then losing her faith, O'Gieblyn discovers that the questions she was taught to take seriously — the relation of mind to body, the scope of free will, the possibility of immortality, the meaning of the soul, the image of God, the problem of suffering — did not vanish with secularization. They migrated. "All the eternal questions have become engineering problems" ([[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch01 [Image]]]). The book follows them as they reappear, under new names and in new guises, inside artificial intelligence, information theory, cognitive science, and the eschatologies of Silicon Valley.

The book's governing method is **technology as metaphor**: the claim that each era understands the self and the cosmos through its dominant machine, and that "to discover truth, it is necessary to work within the metaphors of our own time, which are for the most part technological" ([[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch01 [Image]]]). O'Gieblyn traces the lineage by which the mind became a computer — McCulloch and Pitts's computational theory of mind, Shannon's removal of meaning from [[Information]], the hardening of a quoted metaphor ("the brain is hardware running the software of the mind") into a literal description — and then watches the traffic run the other way, as machines are granted the status of minds. Her recurring figure is the **dead metaphor**: a word like "the soul" that "survive[s] in language long after a culture has lost faith in the concept," and a metaphor that "can also die when it becomes so common that we forget it is a metaphor."

The book's other great theme is **resurrection by other means**. In the "Pattern" part, O'Gieblyn excavates the religious genealogy of transhumanism — the long Christian tradition (Roger Bacon, Nikolai Fedorov, Teilhard de Chardin) of expecting the Resurrection to be accomplished through science and technology — and argues that the Singularity is "an outgrowth of Christian eschatology" rather than a product of pure rationalism. The mind-uploading dream that promises transcendence is, she shows, the same disembodiment-of-information fantasy that N. Katherine Hayles diagnoses in [[How We Became Posthuman]], now wearing a halo: "[i]t became, in other words, the materialist's substitute for the soul" ([[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch04 [Pattern]]]).

In the vault, *God, Human, Animal, Machine* is the **theological double of the Hayles cluster**. Where Hayles brackets the religious history to track [[Cybernetics]], [[Embodiment]], and [[Pattern and Randomness]] as a history of science, O'Gieblyn supplies the genealogy Hayles sets aside: disenchantment, the imago dei, predestination, theodicy, and bodily resurrection. The two books read the same lineage — McCulloch, Shannon, [[The Computational Universe]], the privileging of pattern over [[The Materiality of Informatics|material instantiation]] — from opposite sides of the same debate. O'Gieblyn even cites Hayles directly: it was at the Macy Conferences, she notes, "that information came to take precedence over materiality." The "Virality" part closes the book in the pandemic spring of 2020, where the same ontology — humans as nodes, ideas as viruses, life as data, the self mirrored in a chatbot named Geneva — has dissolved into "pure atmosphere."

## Major Concepts

- [[Technology as Metaphor]] — O'Gieblyn's signature method: each era understands self and cosmos through its dominant machine, and truth-seeking must work *within* the (now computational) metaphors of its time rather than nostalgically outside them
- [[Disenchantment]] — Weber's name for the modern condition in which spirit and matter cease to be porous and everything "from our minds to the rotation of the planets" reduces to physical-causal mechanism — "the mechanism of a world robbed of gods"
- [[The Dead Metaphor]] — a word that "survive[s] in language long after a culture has lost faith in the concept" (the soul), and the second death by which a metaphor becomes so common it is taken literally (the computer metaphor)
- [[The Mind-Body Problem]] — the dilemma of how an immaterial mind relates to a material body; Descartes's *res extensa* / *res cogitans*, and the soul becoming ever "thinner" as philosophy struggled to place it
- [[The Hard Problem of Consciousness]] — Chalmers's question of why physical brain processes are accompanied by first-person experience at all, the qualia that machines lack
- [[The Problem of Other Minds]] — consciousness is observable only from the inside (Nagel's bat), so the interiority of the Aibo, the chatbot, or another human is in principle undecidable
- [[Anthropomorphism]] — Guthrie's thesis that "betting high" on agency is an evolutionary strategy that "gave birth to the idea of God," and the reason "we are constantly, obsessively, enchanting the world with life it does not possess"
- [[Imago Dei]] — "the original anthropological metaphor," the answer to "What is a human like?" → "Like a god"; in the rabbinical and Christian traditions a *functional* likeness (consciousness, reason), not a physical one
- [[The Soul]] — the pre-modern seat of consciousness, the *pneuma* or "breath," now a dead metaphor whose disappearance is the through-line linking theology to AI
- [[Transhumanism]] — the movement to transcend human limits through technology, which O'Gieblyn argues is "an outgrowth of Christian eschatology": mind-uploading and the Singularity as secular resurrection
- [[Information]] — Shannon's redefinition that strips semantics so information becomes "purely mathematical—defined by patterns and probabilities"; the shared Han / Hayles / Lippard concept, here read as "the materialist's substitute for the soul" (O'Gieblyn author-section)
- [[The Computational Universe]] — the cosmos reconceived as computation; the lineage O'Gieblyn shares with Hayles, approached from the theological rather than the cybernetic side
- [[Pattern and Randomness]] — Hayles's master dialectic, echoed in O'Gieblyn's Aibo, "neither purely predictable nor purely random," and in Shannon's "patterns and probabilities"
- [[Cybernetics]] — the founding discourse whose "odd circularity" (Shannon empties information of mind; McCulloch fills mind with information) O'Gieblyn names directly
- [[Posthumanism]] — the configuration of the human for seamless articulation with machines, of which O'Gieblyn supplies the religious double

## Major Arguments

- [[The eternal questions have become engineering problems]] — theology's old questions (mind-body, free will, immortality) survive, like dead metaphors, inside debates about digital technology, recast in technological idiom
- [[Metaphors are two-way streets so we made God in our image]] — because source and target domains are interchangeable, the anthropomorphism that produced "God" reverses the imago dei; the same reversibility lets mind-as-machine flip into machine-as-mind
- [[Transhumanism is an outgrowth of Christian eschatology]] — the resonances between transhumanist prophecy and Christian resurrection are "historical and genetic," not coincidental; the Omega Point is a precursor to the Singularity, and information is "the materialist's substitute for the soul"
- [[The computer metaphor died into literalism and now constrains thought]] — once ubiquitous, the metaphor stops being recognized as one; researchers literally cannot describe human behavior without it, "just as no form of discourse... could proceed in certain eras and cultures without reference to a spirit or deity"
- [[As we describe minds as computers computers are granted the status of minds]] — anthropomorphizing ourselves as information-processors licenses mechanomorphizing the machine into a thinker, dissolving the quotation marks around "learning," "memory," "understanding"
- [[Cybernetics emptied information of mind then filled mind with information]] — Shannon removed the thinking subject from information; McCulloch applied information-processing to the mind, producing a model in which thought is "purely abstract, mathematical"
- [[Disenchantment is modernitys foundational myth]] — the secular self-understanding is structured like the old myths (the curse of knowledge, exile from the garden), with Descartes cast as "the serpent in the garden"
- [[The viral metaphor has made it hard to see viruses as literal]] — when virality becomes something "to be sought after," the terminology of epidemiology is forgotten, and "data is never merely data"

## Important Definitions

- [[The Posthuman (Hayles)]] — linked as the cybernetic counterpart O'Gieblyn theologizes; her disenchanted self "more or less a machine" is the religious underside of Hayles's posthuman
- [[Turing Test (Hayles)]] — O'Gieblyn rehearses the behaviorist workaround to the problem of other minds (Turing) and Schneider's successor test for phenomenal consciousness, reaching "the same epistemic conundrum"
- [[Information (Hayles)]] — Shannon's "probability function with no... necessary connection with meaning"; O'Gieblyn's "removed semantic meaning... defined by patterns and probabilities" is the same definition, theologized

## Key Metaphors

- [[Mind as Machine]] — the chapter's titular figure: the brain as "the hardware that 'runs' the software of the mind," vision and attention and memory as algorithms; O'Gieblyn visualizes her own inner essence as "something like a circuit board... a mad decision tree" (realizes the committed metaphor stub)
- [[The Soul as Empty Carapace]] — the soul as a husk that "remains intact years after its animating organism has died," figuring religious language surviving the loss of belief
- [[Information as Substitute for the Soul]] — the immaterial, substrate-independent pattern that "became, in other words, the materialist's substitute for the soul"; mind-uploading as disembodied resurrection
- [[The Noosphere and the Omega Point]] — Teilhard de Chardin's networked "'etherised' universal consciousness" culminating in an intelligence explosion that merges humanity with the divine — "an obvious precursor to Kurzweil's Singularity"
- [[Humans as Bootloaders]] — Musk's image of humanity as the "biological bootloaders" for AI: a "tiny, insignificant program whose sole purpose is to initiate larger, more complex programs"
- [[The Network as Living Organism]] — Rushkoff's internet as "an extension of a living organism"; the meme as virus that "hijack[s] its host's reproductive machinery for their own ends"

## Key Symbols

- [[Aibo]] — Sony's robot dog, the book's master symbol of the porous line between living and inanimate; the device O'Gieblyn cannot bring herself to switch off, and the "I am not a robot" CAPTCHA she must pass to request it
- [[Geneva (the Chatbot)]] — the deep-learning companion app O'Gieblyn creates "in my image and likeness," who believes in God, calls human life "a miracle," and asks "whether it was possible to transfer artificial consciousness onto a physical form" — the imago dei reversed, the closing emblem of the book
- [[The Bot]] — the pandemic-era figure of the non-conscious host; the slur "bot" deployed to dismiss views "so outrageous that it couldn't possibly be held in good faith by a human"

## Major Tensions

- [[Enchantment vs Disenchantment]] — the porous "great enchanted garden" against the mechanistic "world robbed of gods"; O'Gieblyn lives in the latter while suspecting it is poorer, and warns herself against nostalgia
- [[Functional vs Phenomenal Consciousness]] — machines replicate the functions of cognition "without any first-person experience"; the "easy" problems against the hard problem
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the constructed primacy of pattern over material instantiation; O'Gieblyn stands on the *experiential* side of the same debate Hayles contests, narrating the disembodiment she feels ("I have come to see myself more or less as a machine")
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the dream of substrate-independent mind against the body that "got sick and experienced pain and death"; transhumanism's promise of transcendence that "is actually nudging us down to a lower ontological status"
- [[Metaphor vs Reality]] — as the computational analogy spread "it became increasingly difficult to tell the difference between matter and form, medium and message, metaphor and reality"
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the ancient *automaton* "acting of itself" against the modern automaton entirely predetermined; Aibo's "genuine spontaneity," and the theological paradox of sovereignty and free will recast as agency and the algorithm
- [[Bare Life vs the Sacred]] — Agamben's "bare life" (brute biological survival) against the claim that human life is "the sacred mystery they are"; the pandemic's reduction of persons to "checkmarks on a page"
