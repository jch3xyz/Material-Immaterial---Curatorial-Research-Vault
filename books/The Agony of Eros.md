# The Agony of Eros

**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw folder:** `raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/`
**Original:** *Agonie des Eros* (Matthes & Seitz Berlin, 2012), trans. Erik Butler, with a foreword by **Alain Badiou** (MIT Press, *Untimely Meditations 1*, 2017)

## Brief Orientation

*The Agony of Eros* is Byung-Chul Han's short, dense polemic that today's [[Achievement Society|achievement society]] — "the inferno of the same," in Badiou's rendering, "which contemporary society is increasingly becoming" — is abolishing [[Eros|eros]], the relation to the wholly [[The Other|Other]]. In the vault it is Han's **FOURTH book**, and it is the crucial one: it is the **primary source** for the *negative* concepts the vault has so far held only in fragments and cross-references — [[Eros|eros]], [[The Other|the Other]], [[Atopia|the atopos]], [[Negativity|negativity]], and the "power not-to." Where [[The Burnout Society]] gave the *positive* diagnosis (positivity, auto-exploitation, the achievement-subject), *Agony* supplies the *relational* counter-figure that diagnosis was always missing: the erotic relation to an alterity that "eludes all ability." As Badiou states it in the Foreword, Han's central argument is that "the negativity of Otherness—that is, the atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability—is constitutive of erotic experience" (`Directly stated`, Alain Badiou, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/01 Foreword - The Reinvention of Love|Foreword]]).

Han's diagnosis is that love is being killed by *positivity*, not by prohibition. "The crisis of love does not derive from too many others so much as from the erosion of the Other. This erosion is occurring in all spheres of life; its corollary is the mounting narcissification of the Self" (`Directly stated`, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/02 Melancholia|Melancholia]]). [[Eros|Eros]] "concerns the Other in the strong sense, namely, what cannot be encompassed by the regime of the ego"; it "presumes the asymmetry and exteriority of the Other," whose figure is the *atopos*, the placeless one: "It is not by chance that Socrates the lover is called atopos" (`Directly stated`, Melancholia). Against this, the [[Hell of the Same|inferno of the same]] "leaves no room for the negativity of what is atopos" — "Everything is being flattened out into an object of consumption" (`Directly stated`, Melancholia). [[Eros vs Depression|Eros and depression are opposites]]: "Eros pulls the subject out of itself, toward the Other. Depression, in contrast, plunges the subject into itself" (`Directly stated`, Melancholia).

The book's philosophical hinge is the chapter *Being Able Not to Be Able*, which turns Han's own [[Can vs Should|Can/Should]] diagnosis against itself. [[The freedom of Can is more coercive than the disciplinary Should|"You can exercises even greater constraint than You should"]]: "Auto-compulsion proves more fatal than allo-compulsion, because there is no way to resist oneself" (`Directly stated`, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/03 Being Able Not to Be Able|Being Able Not to Be Able]]). Eros is precisely what escapes this regime of ability — "a relationship to the Other situated beyond achievement, performance, and ability." Its structure is a productive *failure*: "Only by way of being able not to be able does the Other appear" (`Directly stated`, Being Able Not to Be Able). Here Han recovers, against [[Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault|Foucault]] (faulted for valorizing "power" and performance), the ethics of [[Byung-Chul Han and Emmanuel Levinas|Levinas]] and Buber — "primal distance" (*Urdistanz*), the "countenance" that "at once gives and conceals the Other," and the negativity of an absence "in a horizon of the future."

The middle chapters press the indictment across registers. *[[Bare Life|Bare Life]]* re-reads Hegel's master–slave dialectic to argue that the self-exploiting achievement-subject is "master and slave at once," so that [[The achievement-subject is master and slave at once so there is no end of history|there can be no "end of history"]] (`Directly stated`, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/04 Bare Life|Bare Life]]). *Porn* and *Fantasy* argue that the [[Pornography (Han)|pornographication]] of the world — the profanation of eros, contra [[Byung-Chul Han and Giorgio Agamben|Agamben]]'s praise of profanation — proceeds by destroying secrecy, distance, and imagination: "[[Fantasy vs Information|Information and fantasy are opposing forces]]" and "pornography—which maximizes visual information, as it were—destroys erotic fantasy" (`Directly stated`, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/06 Fantasy|Fantasy]]). *[[The Politics of Eros|The Politics of Eros]]* reads Plato and [[Byung-Chul Han and Alain Badiou|Badiou]] to argue that eros — love as a "Two scene" — is the energy of political revolt: "Eros manifests itself as the revolutionary yearning for an entirely different way of loving and another kind of society" (`Directly stated`, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/07 The Politics of Eros|The Politics of Eros]]). *The End of Theory* closes with the boldest claim: [[Theory in the emphatic sense is erotic|theory in the emphatic sense is erotic]] — "Without seduction by the atopic Other, which sparks erotic desire, thinking withers into mere work" (`Directly stated`, [[raw/Byung-Chul Han - The Agony of Eros/08 The End of Theory|The End of Theory]]), and "To be able to think, one must first have been a friend, a lover." This chapter's polemic against Chris Anderson's "The End of Theory" makes *Agony* the primary-source companion to Han's [[Dataism|Dataism]] critique: the [[Byung-Chul Han and Georges Bataille|Bataille]]-inflected defense of [[Negativity|negativity]] here is the same argument [[Big Data is blind to the event and so blind to the future|Big Data is blind to the event]] runs in [[Psychopolitics]].

*The Agony of Eros* extends the vault's Han cluster ([[Negative Potency]], [[Bare Life]], [[Pornography (Han)]], [[Hell of the Same]], [[Achievement Society]], among others), realizes the long-committed forward-stubs [[Eros]] and [[The Other]], and — through its account of the [[Pornography (Han)|pornographic]], the exhibited, and the transparent — connects Han's diagnosis to Debord's [[Spectacle]]: eros degraded "into a dull article for consumption," "put on show" rather than ritualized. The recurring interlocutors are Badiou (the "Two scene," fidelity to the event), Levinas and Buber (the atopic Other, primal distance, the caress), [[Byung-Chul Han and Hegel|Hegel]] (love as the Absolute, "the reconciled return out of another into self"), Bataille ("Eroticism … is assenting to life up to the point of death"), Agamben and [[Byung-Chul Han and Jean Baudrillard|Baudrillard]] (profanation and seduction, critiqued), [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz|Eva Illouz]] (the running sociological target — the "rationalization" of love and the "information-thin" imagination), [[Byung-Chul Han and Slavoj Žižek|Žižek]] (the mis-Platonized reading of Ballard), [[Byung-Chul Han and Martin Heidegger|Heidegger]] (Eros as "the oldest of the gods," the "clearing"), and [[Byung-Chul Han and Gilles Deleuze|Deleuze]] and Guattari, whose *What Is Philosophy?* supplies the book's closing "encomium of love."

## Major Concepts

New this ingest, or realized long-committed forward-stubs (`Eros`, `The Other`):

- [[Eros]] — the relation to the wholly Other "beyond achievement, performance, and ability"; realized here as the vault's primary Han-anchored eros note (open to a future Marcuse section). "Eros pulls the subject out of itself, toward the Other" (`Directly stated`, Melancholia).
- [[The Other]] — the *atopos*, the alterity "removed from the language of sameness"; realized here as the canonical cross-author note (NOT `The Other (Han)`). Its erosion, not its abundance, is the true crisis of love.
- [[Atopia]] — the "atopia of the Other, which eludes all ability" and "cannot be assimilated into the language of the Same"; the negativity constitutive of erotic experience, thought, and the event.
- [[Narcissism (Han)]] — "Narcissism is not the same as self-love"; the narcissistic subject "never manages to set any clear boundaries," so "the world appears only as adumbrations of the narcissist's self" (`Directly stated`, Melancholia). Depression as its malady.
- [[Melancholia (Han)]] — the reading of Lars von Trier's *Melancholia* (with Bruegel, Millais, Wagner's *Tristan*): the atopic Other arriving as apocalypse, "the atopia of the Other turn[ing] out to be the utopia of eros."
- [[Primal Distance (Urdistanz)]] — Buber's principle: distance that "prevents the Other from being reified into an object, an 'it'" and "frees—that is, distances—the Other into his or her otherness." Social media's abolition of remoteness "does not produce nearness so much as it abolishes it."
- [[Fantasy]] — imagination as a function of the *negativity* of withdrawal, not of information: "Only the negativity of withdrawal brings forth the Other in its atopic otherness." Hypervisibility "stifles fantasy."
- [[The Politics of Eros]] — eros as the "universal" energy of political action; love as Badiou's "Two scene" that ruptures the One and "remains faithful to what is yet to come."

## Major Arguments

- [[The freedom of Can is more coercive than the disciplinary Should]] — "You can exercises even greater constraint than You should. Auto-compulsion proves more fatal than allo-compulsion, because there is no way to resist oneself" (`Directly stated`, Being Able Not to Be Able).
- [[The achievement-subject is master and slave at once so there is no end of history]] — "As an entrepreneur of the self, he is master and slave at once… If we understand the dialectic of master and slave as the history of freedom, there can be no talk of the 'end of history,' for we are still far from being truly free" (`Directly stated`, Bare Life).
- [[Eros and depression are opposites the achievement-subject cannot love]] — "Eros and depression are opposites. Eros pulls the subject out of itself, toward the Other. Depression, in contrast, plunges the subject into itself"; the narcissistic achievement-subject can validate but not love (`Directly stated`, Melancholia).
- [[Theory in the emphatic sense is erotic]] — "Without seduction by the atopic Other, which sparks erotic desire, thinking withers into mere work"; "To be able to think, one must first have been a friend, a lover" (`Directly stated`, The End of Theory).

## Important Definitions

Existing definition notes this book deepens (grounded here in their primary Han source):

- [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)]] — the closing distinction of *The End of Theory*: "information society is a society of experience. Experiencing (Erlebnis) is also additive and cumulative. That is what distinguishes it from transformative experience (Erfahrung), which often occurs only once" (`Directly stated`, The End of Theory).
- [[Information (Han)]] — "Information is inherently positive"; "As positivity, information changes nothing and announces nothing. It is utterly inconsequential" — the definition *Agony* opposes to insight, event, and fantasy.
- [[Cult Value vs Exhibition Value (Han via Benjamin)]] — invoked in *Porn*: "musealization and exhibition are precisely what destroy their cult value and replace it with exhibition value"; the face "loaded until it bursts with exhibition-value."

## Major Tensions

- [[Eros vs Depression]] — the governing axis of the diagnosis: "Eros and depression are opposites"; eros as self-transcendence toward the Other, depression as the narcissistic subject drowning "in itself."
- [[Fantasy vs Information]] — "Information and fantasy are opposing forces"; "there is no such thing as an 'information-thick' imagination," because "only the negativity of withdrawal brings forth the Other." Contra [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz|Illouz]].
- [[Eros vs Sexuality]] — "Today, love is being positivized into sexuality"; the sexual object "always remains self-identical" and "never calls me into question," whereas eros requires "the negativity of otherness."
- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the master opposition inherited from the rest of the Han corpus, here trained on love, theory, and the event: a world of "mere positivity" that "lacks any such power to animate."

## References to Other Authors

New reference notes minted this ingest:

- [[Byung-Chul Han and Alain Badiou]] — the Foreword author and a running interlocutor: love as the "Two scene," "eternity … within the time span of life itself," "fidelity" to the event, and the closing "reinvention" of love (after Rimbaud).
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Emmanuel Levinas]] — the ethics of eros Han recovers against Foucault: the relationship of eros "impossible to translate into powers," the "countenance" that "at once gives and conceals the Other," the caress as "a game with something slipping away."
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Georges Bataille]] — eroticism as transgression and "the non-economy of eros and death": "Eroticism, it may be said, is assenting to life up to the point of death"; "unproductive expenditure" against restrictive economy.
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Slavoj Žižek]] — critiqued for a "Platonic" misreading of Ballard's "The Gioconda of the Twilight Noon": Maitland in fact "follow[s] an anti-Platonic dispositive," blinding himself to "retreat into the cave."

- [[Byung-Chul Han and Michel Foucault]] — the "severe critique": Foucault "valoriz[es] ability, 'power'… and therefore performance" and "is blind to the violence of the freedom that underlies auto-exploitation."
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Hegel]] — the "striking reading": love as the Absolute, "the reconciled return out of another into self," Spirit that "look[s] the negative in the face and [tarries] with it."
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Giorgio Agamben]] — "In Praise of Profanation" critiqued: "On this score, Agamben's 'profanation' amounts to just so much profanity."
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Jean Baudrillard]] — porn as "the anticipation of dead sex in living sexuality"; seduction (ritual) opposed to love.
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Martin Heidegger]] — the letter naming Eros "the oldest of the gods," thinking driven "onto untrodden paths"; faithfulness as a "clearing" in time.
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Gilles Deleuze]] — *What Is Philosophy?* (Deleuze and Guattari) as the book's closing authority: eros as "the transcendental condition for the possibility of thinking."
- [[Byung-Chul Han and Eva Illouz]] — the sustained sociological target across *Melancholia*, *Bare Life*, and *Fantasy*: the "rationalization" of love, its "feminization," and the "information-thin" imagination, all countered by Han's libido-economy of the negative.
