# Telematic Embrace

**Author:** [[Roy Ascott]]
**Full title:** *Telematic Embrace: Visionary Theories of Art, Technology, and Consciousness*
**Editor:** Edward A. Shanken (editor's introduction, glossary, and critical apparatus)
**Edition:** University of California Press, Berkeley, 2003 (collected writings, 1964–2003)
**Raw folder:** `raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/`

## Brief Orientation

*Telematic Embrace* (ed. Edward A. Shanken, 2003) is the first English-language collection of the writings of [[Roy Ascott]], "the outstanding artist in the field of telematics" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/04 From Cybernetics to Telematics]], quoting Frank Popper). It gathers more than three decades of essays — the earliest from 1964, the latest written for the volume — and so functions less as a single argument than as what Shanken calls "a unique archaeology of ideas" tracing "the intertwined cultural history of art, technology, and consciousness from the 1960s to the present" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/04 From Cybernetics to Telematics]]). Ascott is the founder of [[Telematic Art]] and, alongside [[Jack Burnham]], a pioneer of 1960s cybernetic art; this book is the primary source for realizing the vault's long-deferred [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] research-foci as a living, decades-long practice rather than a 1968 precedent. (Note: chapter 04 is **Shanken's editorial introduction** — all chapter-04 quotations are Shanken's, not Ascott's. The raw files are OCR transcriptions; quotations are transcribed character-for-character, preserving artifacts.)

The book's spine is a single, accelerating arc across three phases. The **cybernetic / behaviourist phase** (chs. 05–10, 1964–1972) opens with Ascott's first publication, in which, as Shanken puts it, "Ascott renounced the idea that the essence of art could be crystallized in material objects, arguing that art was, rather, characteristic of the behavioral processes by which such objects are generated" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/04 From Cybernetics to Telematics]]). Ascott's own thesis: "The vision of art has shifted from the field of objects to the field of behaviour, and its function has become less descriptive and more purposive" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/07 Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision]]). The spectator is drawn "into active participation in the act of creation" so that "a feedback loop is established," and Ascott proposes a [[Cybernetic Art Matrix]] (CAM) as a "technological and social framework" for an emergent "cybernetic vision in art." The **telematic phase** (chs. 11–24, 1978–1994) follows Ascott online (1978) into [[Network Consciousness]], [[Distributed Authorship|"dispersed authorship"]], and the title essay's central claim that telematic networking will constitute a planetary "telematic embrace that is greater than the sum of its parts" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/04 From Cybernetics to Telematics]]). The **technoetic / moist phase** (chs. 25–32, 1995–2003) carries the inquiry into [[Cyberception]], the [[The Post-Biological Body|post-biological body]], [[Technoetics]], and [[Moistmedia]] — "where dry pixels and wet molecules converge" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/31 Art @ the Edge of the NET: the Future Will Be Moist!]]).

Ascott's controlling reversal is the move from **observer to participator**. In the title essay he adopts the physicist John Wheeler's quantum dictum — "To describe what has happened one has to cross out that old word 'observer' and put in its place 'participator'" — and applies it to art: "In a telematic art, meaning is not created by the artist, distributed through the network, and received by the observer. Meaning is the product of interaction between the observer and the system, the content of which is in a state of flux, of endless change and transformation" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/20 Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?]]). The art object yields to the [[The Interface as Doorway|interface]]: "Instead of the artwork as a window onto a composed, resolved, and ordered reality, we have at the interface a doorway to undecidability, a data space of semantic and material potentiality." The computer itself "is not primarily a thing, an object, but a set of behaviours, a system" and "works best when it becomes invisible." Ascott tempers his utopianism with [[Connective Criticism]], warning (after Foucault) that "we can expect a growing . . . interest in telematics on the part of controlling institutions" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/04 From Cybernetics to Telematics]]).

Within the vault, *Telematic Embrace* sits at the crossroads of every major cluster. It is the practice-side complement to [[Jack Burnham|Burnham]]'s [[Systems Esthetics]] and [[Lucy Lippard|Lippard]]'s [[Dematerialization]]: where Burnham theorized the [[Object and System|object→system]] drift in 1968, Ascott had been *making* interactive, behavioural, networked art since 1959 and would extend it for forty years (Ascott's "behaviourist art" and "dispersed authorship" parallel and precede much of [[Conceptual Art]]'s rhetoric of [[Dematerialization|dematerialization]]). It is the most direct heir to [[Marshall McLuhan|McLuhan]] — Shanken places Ascott "in the tradition of futurologists like Marshall McLuhan and Buckminster Fuller" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/04 From Cybernetics to Telematics]]) — extending [[The Global Village]] into a literal [[Network Consciousness|network consciousness]] and the body's extension into [[Cyberception]]. Its post-biological body, [[The Distributed Self|distributed self]], and "many selves hypothesis" connect it to the cyborg/posthuman cluster of [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]] ([[Posthumanism]], [[Embodiment]], [[Virtuality]]) and [[Donna Haraway|Haraway]] ([[The Cyborg]]); its [[Telematic Time|omnipresence and simultaneity]], [[The Datapool|datapool]], and "we are all interface" furnish the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis its most enthusiastically immaterialist voice — Ascott embraces as liberation precisely the networked, distributed, surveillant condition that [[Byung-Chul Han|Han]] diagnoses as domination.

## Important Definitions

- [[Telematics (Ascott)]] — "computer-mediated communications networking . . . between geographically dispersed individuals and institutions . . . and between the human mind and artificial systems of intelligence and perception" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/20 Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Cyberspace (Ascott)]] · [[Cybernetics (Ascott)]] — "movement through 'cyberspace'"; cybernetics "a term reinvigorated and humanised by Norbert Wiener" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/20 Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Behaviourist Art (Ascott)]] — art of "the field of behaviour" rather than "the field of objects" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/07 Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Gesamtdatenwerk (Ascott)]] — "the Gesamtdatenwerk—the integrated data work"; after Wagner's *Gesamtkunstwerk* ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/20 Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Post-Photographic Practice (Ascott)]] — [[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/21 Photography at the Interface]]. *Strongly implied*.
- [[The Holomatic Principle (Ascott)]] — "to be in or at any one interface is to be in the virtual presence of all the other interfaces throughout the network" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/20 Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[A Physics of Information (Ascott)]] · [[Telenoia (Ascott)]] · [[Apparition (Ascott)]] — "the telenoia of universal connectivity" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/31 Art @ the Edge of the NET: the Future Will Be Moist!]]); apparition ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/24 From Appearance to Apparition: Communication and Cultur]]). *Directly stated / Strongly implied*.
- [[Cyberception (Ascott)]] · [[Technoetic (Ascott)]] · [[Hypercortex (Ascott)]] — "the hypercortex, mind in the Net"; "technoetic: techne and gnōsis combined" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/31 Art @ the Edge of the NET: the Future Will Be Moist!]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Connectivist Paradigm (Ascott)]] · [[Second-Order Cybernetics (Ascott)]] — "the (second-order) cybernetics of observing systems" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/20 Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Moistmedia (Ascott)]] — "Moistmedia (comprising bits, atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination)" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/31 Art @ the Edge of the NET: the Future Will Be Moist!]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Double Consciousness (Ascott)]] · [[Technoetic Aesthetics (Ascott)]] · [[Visionary Pragmatism (Ascott)]] · [[Telepresence (Ascott)]] — "Our double consciousness"; "this visionary pragmatism, as I shall call it" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/31 Art @ the Edge of the NET: the Future Will Be Moist!]]); [[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/32 Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions for th]]. *Directly stated*.
- [[Supervenience (Ascott)]] · [[Interactivity (Ascott)]] — the interface defined as the site where "content is created rather than received" ([[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/20 Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?]]); [[raw/Ascott - Telematic Embrace/32 Technoetic Aesthetics: 100 Terms and Definitions for th]]. *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis*.

## References to Other Authors

- [[Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham]] — the two pioneers of 1960s cybernetic art; Ascott's practice-side [[Telematic Art]] / [[Behaviourist Art]] beside Burnham's theory-side [[Systems Esthetics]]; both grounded in Wiener.
- [[Roy Ascott and Marshall McLuhan]] — Shanken places Ascott "in the tradition of futurologists like Marshall McLuhan"; [[The Global Village]] extended into a literal [[Network Consciousness]] and [[Cyberception]].

- [[Roy Ascott and Lucy Lippard]] — "dispersed authorship" and behaviourist art beside [[Dematerialization]] and [[Conceptual Art]]; the parallel 1960s–70s evacuation of the art object.
- [[Roy Ascott and Byung-Chul Han]] — Ascott's enthusiastic embrace of [[The Telematic Embrace|telematic connectivity, transparency, and the dissolved self]] against Han's diagnosis of the same as [[Digital Panopticon|digital domination]] (interpretive bridge).
- [[Roy Ascott and Meghan O'Gieblyn]] — "biotechnological theology," the noosphere, and transcendence beside O'Gieblyn's account of [[Re-enchantment]] and [[Transhumanism]] (interpretive bridge).
- [[Roy Ascott and Donna Haraway]] — "we are all interface," the many-selves self, and the breached human/machine boundary beside Haraway's [[The Cyborg|cyborg]].
- [[Roy Ascott and Hans Moravec]] · [[Roy Ascott and Ray Kurzweil]] — the post-biological body, [[Mind Uploading]], and the planetary mind beside Moravec/Kurzweil transhumanism (interpretive bridges).
