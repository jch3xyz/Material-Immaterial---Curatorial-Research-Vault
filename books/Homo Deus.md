# Homo Deus

**Author:** [[Yuval Noah Harari]]
**Full title:** *Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow* (2015; English ed. 2016)
**Raw folder:** `raw/Harari - Homo Deus/`

## Brief Orientation

*Homo Deus* is Yuval Noah Harari's history of the near future: a single, escalating argument that having tamed famine, plague and war, humanity will turn its accumulated power on three new projects — immortality, happiness and divinity — and that pursuing them will dismantle the very humanism that authorized them. The framing chapter states the whole book in miniature: "humanity's next targets are likely to be immortality, happiness and divinity," culminating in the project to "upgrade humans into gods, and turn Homo sapiens into Homo deus" ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/01 The New Human Agenda]]). These are not science-fiction eccentricities but the "logical conclusion" of "humanism – the worship of humankind." The book's structural conceit — "a gun that appears in Act I, to fire in Act III" — is humanism's self-undoing: "the same technologies that can upgrade humans into gods might also make humans irrelevant."

The book proceeds in three movements that map its three parts. First, *Homo Sapiens Conquers the World* establishes the keystone thesis that [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — "emotions are biochemical algorithms," and "the man pressing the buttons and drinking the tea is also an algorithm" ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/03 The Anthropocene]]) — and locates human dominance not in any "magical spark" but in [[Large-Scale Flexible Cooperation]] grounded in shared fictions ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/04 The Human Spark]]). Second, *Homo Sapiens Gives Meaning to the World* reconstructs how [[Intersubjective Reality]] — gods, money, nations, corporations — became "the most potent force on earth" ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/06 The Storytellers]]), how [[Religion as Social Order]] and science struck [[The Modern Covenant]] in which "humans agree to give up meaning in exchange for power" ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/08 The Modern Covenant]]), and how [[Humanism (Harari)]] relocated all authority to human feeling ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/09 The Humanist Revolution]]).

Third, *Homo Sapiens Loses Control* turns the algorithmic thesis against the humanist subject. If the self is split into [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self]] and [[Free Will as Fiction|free will is a fiction]] ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/11 The Time Bomb in the Laboratory]]), then the liberal individual is "a religious fantasy," and as [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness|intelligence decouples from consciousness]] humans risk becoming [[The Useless Class|economically and militarily worthless]] while [[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves]] take over the work of authority ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/12 The Great Decoupling]]). The book closes on two emergent techno-religions: [[Techno-Humanism]], which would engineer the will it claims to worship ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/13 The Ocean of Consciousness]]), and [[Dataism]], for which "the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing" ([[raw/Harari - Homo Deus/14 The Data Religion]]).

In the vault, *Homo Deus* is the **historian's hinge** between several existing clusters. Its "organisms are algorithms" thesis is the third independent statement of [[The Computational Universe]] alongside [[N. Katherine Hayles]] and [[Meghan O'Gieblyn]]; its [[Dataism]] is the popular-progenitor counterpart to [[Byung-Chul Han]]'s critique (Harari coins the religion Han later attacks); its assault on [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] meets Hayles's posthuman from the side of political economy; and its immortality/divinity engineering rejoins O'Gieblyn's [[Transhumanism]] and the [[Mind Uploading]] dream. Where Hayles tracks the science and O'Gieblyn the theology, Harari supplies the long historical narrative of how a Stone Age ape became, with respect to other animals, "a god."

## Major Concepts

- [[The New Human Agenda]] — having reined in famine, plague and war, humanity's next targets are immortality, happiness and divinity; the book's organizing frame
- [[Death as a Technical Problem]] — modern science reframes death not as metaphysical mystery but as an accumulation of solvable "technical glitches" — "every technical problem has a technical solution"
- [[Happiness as Biochemistry]] — the life-sciences redefinition of happiness as "different balances of bodily sensations," so that lasting contentment can only be reached by re-engineering human biochemistry
- [[Healing vs Upgrading]] — "no clear line separates healing from upgrading"; every enhancement enters as a cure, then crosses from restoring the norm to surpassing it
- [[Humanism (Harari)]] — "the worship of humankind," the dominant modern religion that "sanctifies the life, happiness and power of Homo sapiens"; later splitting into liberal, socialist and evolutionary branches
- [[The Anthropocene]] — the proposed epoch in which a single species becomes "the single most important agent of change in the global ecology"
- [[Organisms Are Algorithms]] — the book's keystone thesis: living beings are biochemical information-processors; "emotions are biochemical algorithms," and the human, the baboon and the vending machine differ only in complexity
- [[Animism]] — the worldview in which "there was no essential gap separating humans from other animals," all beings sharing one web of communication
- [[The Agricultural Deal]] — the theist cosmology of the Agricultural Revolution: animals and plants are silenced, gods mediate, and humans get dominion in exchange for obligations to the gods
- [[Industrial Farming]] — the gestation crate and the systematic breaking of the mammalian mother–infant bond, Harari's central test case for humanist ethics and a preview of how a superintelligence might treat *us*
- [[Large-Scale Flexible Cooperation]] — the real source of human dominion: the unique Sapiens ability to "cooperate flexibly in very large numbers" by believing in shared fictions
- [[Intersubjective Reality]] — the realm of entities (gods, money, nations, corporations) that exist only in the shared imagination yet become "the most potent force on earth"
- [[Society as Algorithm]] — writing and money let large societies "run as algorithms," processing data and reaching decisions through bureaucratic procedures no single mind comprehends
- [[Religion as Social Order]] — religion defined not by belief in gods but by its social function: any all-encompassing story that confers superhuman legitimacy on human laws and norms
- [[The Modern Covenant]] — the deal that defines modernity: "humans agree to give up meaning in exchange for power"
- [[The Growth Imperative]] — the modern dogma that economic growth is "absolutely essential," underwriting credit, the future, and the refusal to stop the new human agenda
- [[The Decoupling of Intelligence from Consciousness]] — the book's pivotal claim that high intelligence no longer requires consciousness, so non-conscious algorithms can outperform humans without ever experiencing anything
- [[The Useless Class]] — the projected mass of humans rendered economically and militarily worthless once algorithms outperform them at most tasks
- [[Algorithms That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves]] — once external algorithms can monitor our biochemistry, authority migrates from the inner voice to the data, and "the system will know you better than you know yourself"
- [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self]] — the two selves within us: a duration-blind narrating self (which makes our decisions by the "peak-end rule") and a moment-to-moment experiencing self
- [[Free Will as Fiction]] — brain processes are "either deterministic or random or a combination of both – but they are never free"; we can act on our desires but cannot choose them
- [[The Self as Fiction]] — like nations, gods and money, the unified individual self is an imaginary story; the "indivisible individual" dissolving into "dividuals"
- [[Techno-Humanism]] — the conservative techno-religion that would upgrade the human mind to keep pace with the decoupling, deifying the human will even as it engineers it
- [[The Engineering of Will]] — the central philosophical payload of the techno-humanist project: if the will can be technologically reshaped, there is no authentic self left to obey
- [[Dataism]] — the data religion for which "the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing" (Harari author-section; the concept [[Byung-Chul Han]] critiques)
- [[Freedom of Information (Dataism)]] — Dataism's supreme value and "the first new value since 1789": a freedom given not to humans but to information itself

## Major Arguments

- [[Death is a technical problem with a technical solution]] — modern medicine treats death as accumulated technical glitches, so "a couple of geeks in a lab" could in principle overcome it; the war against death becomes the century's flagship project
- [[The new agenda is humanism taken to its logical conclusion]] — immortality, bliss and divinity simply maximize the three values humanism already sanctifies (life, happiness, power)
- [[Fulfilling humanism will destroy it]] — the gun in Act I: the same technologies that upgrade humans into gods can make humans irrelevant, so realizing the humanist dream exposes its flaws
- [[Sensations and emotions are biochemical algorithms]] — "99 per cent of our decisions" are made by the refined algorithms we call sensations, emotions and desires, all under "quality control by natural selection"
- [[Humankind is replacing natural selection with intelligent design]] — for the first time in 4 billion years one species is poised to "replace natural selection with intelligent design, and to extend life from the organic realm into the inorganic"
- [[Each revolution silenced another class of beings]] — the Agricultural Revolution silenced animals and plants; the Scientific Revolution silenced the gods; "the world was now a one-man show"
- [[Humans rule the world through shared fictions]] — Sapiens dominate not through intelligence or tool-use alone but through large-scale flexible cooperation grounded in collectively believed fictions
- [[Only what can suffer is real]] — the reality test: "How do you know if an entity is real? Very simple – just ask yourself, 'Can it suffer?'" — gods, money and nations cannot; mammals can
- [[Writing let societies run as algorithms]] — writing and money broke the brain's data-processing limits, letting bureaucracies organize millions and "reshape reality"
- [[Modernity trades meaning for power]] — the entire modern contract reduces to "humans agree to give up meaning in exchange for power"
- [[Trust in future growth and credit broke premodern stagnation]] — belief in growth created credit, which financed the projects that delivered the growth, ending the zero-sum equilibrium of the premodern economy
- [[Humanism relocates meaning and authority from God to human feelings]] — humanism makes "human experience the supreme source of authority and meaning": "if it feels good – do it," "the voter knows best," "beauty is in the eye of the beholder"
- [[Liberalism rests on factual claims that science refutes]] — liberalism presupposes free will and an indivisible self, both of which the life sciences deny, so technology rather than philosophy will break liberalism
- [[We can act on our desires but cannot choose them]] — "humans act according to their desires," but they do not choose those desires; so free will, as ordinarily meant, is empty
- [[There is no single self only an experiencing and a narrating self]] — the cold-water and colonoscopy experiments expose two selves with different interests, neither of them the unified liberal individual
- [[The individual will disintegrate from within not be crushed by Big Brother]] — the threat to individuality is not Orwellian collectivism but the algorithmic decomposition of the person into monitored biochemical subsystems
- [[Intelligence is decoupling from consciousness]] — computer intelligence has advanced enormously while computer consciousness has not advanced at all; the system needs intelligent agents, not conscious ones
- [[Algorithms escalate from oracle to agent to sovereign]] — as we trust them, recommendation systems pass from answering our questions, to acting on our behalf, to shaping our desires and deciding for us
- [[Techno-humanism deifies the will then engineers it away]] — techno-humanism worships human will yet proposes to re-engineer it, dissolving the authentic self it claims to serve and risking the *downgrade* of humans to suit the system
- [[Dataism collapses the barrier between animals and machines]] — because both biochemical and electronic algorithms obey "exactly the same mathematical laws," Dataism treats King Lear and the flu virus as two patterns of data flow
- [[Capitalism won the Cold War because distributed data processing beats centralised processing]] — capitalism defeated communism not on ethics but because "distributed data processing works better than centralised data processing" in fast-changing times
- [[Freedom of information is the first new value since 1789]] — Dataism's supreme commandment ("maximise data flow") is the first genuinely novel value created since the humanist revolution, and it is given to information, not to humans

## Important Definitions

- [[A-mortality (Harari)]] — the condition of having "no expiry date" while remaining killable by accident or violence; distinct from the immortality of God, and source of unprecedented anxiety
- [[Divinity (Harari)]] — "not a vague metaphysical quality" and "not the same as omnipotence," but the Greek-/Hindu-god possession of specific super-abilities of creation and destruction
- [[Algorithm (Harari)]] — "a methodical set of steps that can be used to make calculations, resolve problems and reach decisions"; "the method followed when making the calculation," not a particular calculation
- [[Humanism (Harari)|Humanism (definition)]] — "the worship of humankind," which "sanctifies the life, happiness and power of Homo sapiens"
- [[Religion (Harari)]] — "anything that confers superhuman legitimacy on human social structures"; defined by social function, not by belief in gods
- [[The Reality Test (Harari)]] — "Can it suffer?" as the criterion that separates real entities (which can suffer) from intersubjective fictions (which cannot)
- [[Free Will (Harari)]] — the ability "to act according to your desires," which humans (and parrots) possess — as distinct from the ability to *choose* those desires, which they do not
- [[Dataism (Harari)]] — the doctrine that "the universe consists of data flows, and the value of any phenomenon or entity is determined by its contribution to data processing" (parallel to the existing [[Dataism (Han)]])
- [[Techno-Humanism (Harari)]] — the techno-religion that "still sees humans as the apex of creation" but would upgrade the mind to create Homo deus, "a much superior human model"

## Key Metaphors

- [[The Gun in Act I]] — Chekhov's Law as the book's structural conceit: "a gun that appears in Act I, to fire in Act III" — humanism's self-undoing — counterbalanced by the unfired Cold War gun (ability is not motivation)
- [[The Glass Ceiling of Happiness]] — the invisible cap on subjective well-being that prosperity cannot raise, held up by a psychological and a biological pillar
- [[The Human as Self-Replicating Vending Machine]] — "Humans are algorithms that produce not cups of tea, but copies of themselves" — the central machine-figure of the algorithmic thesis
- [[Universe as Chinese Opera vs Ibsen Drama]] — the dramaturgical figure for cosmological reduction: the animist "grand Chinese opera" rewritten by theism into "a bleak Ibsen drama with just two main characters: man and God," and by science into a "one-man show"
- [[The Vampire Bat Economy]] — the premodern zero-sum economy in which, like blood-sharing vampire bats, no one "believes in growth," against the modern faith in an expanding pie
- [[The Modern Deal as a Software Contract]] — modernity as the licence agreement nobody reads: "you take one look at it… tick 'I agree' and forget about it"
- [[The Narrating Self as Spin Doctor]] — the inner storyteller "akin to Gazzaniga's left-brain interpreter," forever spinning yarns, "duration-blind," editing experience with "a sharp pair of scissors and a thick black marker"
- [[The Ocean of Consciousness]] — the vast unexplored spectrum of possible mental states, of which "science has studied only two tiny sections," likened to the unseen wavelengths beyond visible light
- [[Retiring the Horses]] — the figure for human obsolescence: "When the car replaced the horse-drawn carriage, we didn't upgrade the horses – we retired them. Perhaps it is time to do the same with Homo sapiens"

## Key Symbols

- [[The Lawn]] — the worked symbol of historical contingency: an aristocratic status display that became a bourgeois necessity and a global craze; the case study for history as liberation from the felt-natural
- [[The Gestation Crate]] — the concrete-and-bars sow stall, emblem of industrial-farming cruelty and of subjective need ignored "as if they were already sausages"
- [[Harlow's Cloth Mother]] — the barren cloth dummy the infant monkeys clung to over the wire mother that fed them; symbol of the irreducible mammalian need for emotional bonding
- [[The Robo-Rat]] — Talwar's remote-controlled rat that "wants" to turn left when the electrode fires; the clinching image that the felt sense of free will survives external control
- [[The Hi-Tech Ark]] — the elite's tacit climate gamble that technology will rescue the rich while the poor drown; the "Ark Syndrome"
- [[Aaron Swartz]] — Dataism's "first martyr," the hacker who died for "a free and unlimited flow of information"; the human face of freedom-of-information as a value

## Major Tensions

- [[Healing vs Upgrading]] — the boundary that will not hold: every upgrade enters disguised as a cure
- [[Human Exceptionalism vs Ontological Equality]] — the "magical spark" question: does humanity possess a unique sacred essence, or is it "just an algorithm"? (Harari section added to the existing O'Gieblyn tension)
- [[Animism vs Theism vs Humanism]] — three cosmologies differing in who possesses voice, value and personhood
- [[Material Needs vs Emotional Needs]] — Watson's behaviourist provisioning against Harlow's cloth mother; the meat and dairy industries "breaking the most fundamental emotional bond in the mammal kingdom"
- [[Organic vs Inorganic]] — life's 4-billion-year confinement to the organic realm against humankind's coming extension of life "into the inorganic"
- [[Fiction vs Reality (Harari)]] — the intersubjective fictions that organize the world against the suffering bodies that are the only "reality" by the can-it-suffer test
- [[Fact vs Value]] — science can refute religious *facts* but cannot adjudicate *values*; "Religion seeks order, science seeks power, neither seeks truth"
- [[Meaning vs Power]] — the axis of the modern covenant: the meaning premodernity supplied against the power modernity pursues "within a universe devoid of meaning"
- [[Growth vs Ecology]] — the growth imperative that drives the whole agenda against the ecological collapse that is its real limit
- [[Intelligence vs Consciousness]] — the book's flagship tension: the decoupling of useful intelligence from felt experience
- [[The Experiencing Self vs The Narrating Self]] — the two selves with conflicting interests, exposed by the cold-water and colonoscopy experiments
- [[Free Will vs Determinism]] — the liberal magic wand of choice against brain processes that are "either deterministic or random… but never free" (Harari section added)
- [[Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman]] — the indivisible individual against the algorithmically decomposed "dividual" (Harari section added)
- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the soul/body dualism, the inorganic upgrade, and the data-flow ontology touching the vault's central tension (Harari section added)
