# How We Became Posthuman

**Author:** [[N. Katherine Hayles]]
**Full title:** *How We Became Posthuman: Virtual Bodies in Cybernetics, Literature, and Informatics* (1999)
**Raw folder:** `raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/`

## Brief Orientation

*How We Became Posthuman* is N. Katherine Hayles's history of how the human gave way to the posthuman in the half-century of cybernetics, and a sustained argument against the central fantasy that history produced: the belief that information is an immaterial pattern that "can circulate unchanged among different material substrates" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]]). The book began, Hayles writes, "with a roboticist's dream that struck me as a nightmare" — Hans Moravec's scenario of downloading human consciousness into a computer — and unfolds into a six-year study of cybernetics, information theory, autopoiesis, artificial life, and the literature contemporaneous with each.

The inquiry is organized around "three interrelated stories." The first "centers on how information lost its body, that is, how it came to be conceptualized as an entity separate from the material forms in which it is thought to be embedded" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]]). The second "concerns how the cyborg was created as a technological artifact and cultural icon in the years following World War II." The third "is the unfolding story of how a historically specific construction called the human is giving way to a different construction called the posthuman." These three braided narratives give the book its title and its arc.

Against the teleology of disembodiment, Hayles advances a single master counter-thesis: [[Embodiment]] matters. "[H]uman being is first of all embodied being" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/13 Conclusion - What Does It Mean to be Posthuman?]]), and "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]]). The posthuman, she insists, "need not be recuperated back into liberal humanism, nor need it be construed as antihuman" — "[w]hat is lethal is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/13 Conclusion - What Does It Mean to be Posthuman?]]). The book's stance is contestatory rather than elegiac: because the meaning of the posthuman is still open, "[t]he best possible time to contest for what the posthuman means is now."

Methodologically the book reads literature and science together as mutually constitutive discourses, charting conceptual change not as a Kuhnian paradigm shift or a Foucauldian epistemic break but through *seriation* — "a pattern of overlapping replication and innovation" in which old concepts linger as *skeuomorphs*. It tracks three waves of [[Cybernetics]]: the homeostasis era of the Macy Conferences (1945–1960), the reflexivity and self-organization of second-order cybernetics (1960–1980), and the virtuality and emergence of artificial life (1980–present). It is for readers who want both a history of the information age's founding assumptions and a vocabulary for resisting the dream of "disembodied immortality."

## The Three Braided Stories

Hayles describes the book's "unruly mass of material" as taking shape in "three interrelated stories" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]]). Each runs through every chapter; the literary readings show how each "circulate[d] more widely through the body politic."

- **How information lost its body** — the construction of [[Information]] as "a pattern, not a presence," abstracted from any material substrate. Traced from Shannon and Wiener's decontextualizing definition ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/05 Contesting for the Body of Information - The Macy Conferences on Cybernetics]]) to Moravec's and Minsky's dreams of downloading consciousness. Grounds [[Material vs Immaterial]] and [[Embodiment vs Information]]. *Directly stated.*
- **How the cyborg was created** — [[The Cyborg]] as "simultaneously entit[y] and metaphor," forged in postwar cybernetics through informational pathways linking organic body to prosthesis ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/07 From Hyphen to Splice - Cybernetic Syntax in Limbo]]). *Directly stated.*
- **How the human became the posthuman** — the displacement of [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] by [[Posthumanism|the posthuman]], "a point of view characterized by" the privileging of "informational pattern over material instantiation" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]]). *Directly stated.*

## The Three Waves of Cybernetics

Hayles charts [[Cybernetics]] through three *seriated* waves, each organized by a central concept that becomes a skeuomorph in the next.

- **First wave — homeostasis (1945–1960).** The Macy Conferences forge a paradigm in which humans, animals, and machines all maintain steady states through [[Feedback Loops]]. Anchored in [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/05 Contesting for the Body of Information - The Macy Conferences on Cybernetics]]; applied through Wiener in [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/06 Liberal Subjectivity Imperiled - Norbert Wiener and Cybernetic Anxiety]].
- **Second wave — reflexivity and self-organization (1960–1980).** Heinz von Foerster's second-order cybernetics folds the observer into the system; Maturana and Varela's autopoiesis "sees the world as a set of informationally closed systems." Anchored in [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/08 The Second Wave of Cybernetics - From Reflexivity to Self-Organization]].
- **Third wave — virtuality and emergence (1980–present).** Self-organization becomes "the springboard to emergence"; artificial-life programs "evolve the capacity to evolve." Anchored in [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/11 Narratives of Artificial Life]] and mapped in [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/12 The Semiotics of Virtuality - Mapping the Posthuman]].

## Major Concepts

- [[Posthumanism]] — the posthuman as "a point of view" that "privileges informational pattern over material instantiation" and configures the human "so that it can be seamlessly articulated with intelligent machines"
- [[Embodiment]] — the recovered ground of being; "human being is first of all embodied being," and embodiment is "always instantiated, local, and specific"
- [[Cybernetics]] — the science of "communication and control applying equally to animals, humans, and machines," read across three seriated waves
- [[Feedback Loops]] — the flow associated "with the deconstruction of the liberal humanist subject," dissolving the boundary between subject and environment
- [[Information]] — defined by Shannon as "a probability function with no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning"; "[i]t is a pattern, not a presence"
- [[Systems Theory]] — the "analytically driven" discourse Hayles sets against the more embodied resource of narrative
- [[The Cyborg]] — "technological artifact and cultural icon" of the postwar years, both literal prosthetic being and "metaphoric" condition of the keyboarder spliced into the screen
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — the proprietary, self-possessing "human" (Macpherson's possessive individualism) whose deconstruction the book documents
- [[The Body as Data]] — Gibson's "data made flesh," the body recoded as informational pattern
- The flickering signifier — language under informatics; signifiers that "flicker rather than float," succeeding Lacan's floating signifiers
- Virtuality — "the cultural perception that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns"
- Reflexivity — "the movement whereby that which has been used to generate a system is made, through a changed perspective, to become part of the system it generates"
- Autopoiesis — the self-making organization of "informationally closed systems" (Maturana and Varela)
- Emergence — properties that "appear on their own," the third wave's "springboard"
- Seriation and skeuomorphs — Hayles's historiographic tools for tracing "overlapping replication and innovation" without sharp breaks
- Distributed cognition — cognition residing not in the individual but "across a set of malleable media," human and nonhuman

## Major Arguments

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the separation of information from materiality is "a prior imaginary act," not a fact about the world; "[i]t is this materiality/information separation that I want to contest"
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — "the disembodiment of information was not inevitable"; "[e]mbodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated"
- That narrative, with its "located actions, and personified agents," is "a more embodied form of discourse than is analytically driven [[Systems Theory|systems theory]]" and so can resist "the teleology of disembodiment"
- That the disembodiment of information was an *erasure*: "how much had to be erased to arrive at such abstractions as bodiless information"
- That the posthuman is dangerous not in itself but in "the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self"
- That "posthumanity is already here," so "the question is what kind of posthumans we will be"
- That "[t]he best possible time to contest for what the posthuman means is now"

## Important Definitions

- The posthuman — "a point of view characterized by" privileging "informational pattern over material instantiation," treating consciousness "as an epiphenomenon," and configuring the human for "seamless" articulation with intelligent machines ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- [[Information]] — "a probability function with no dimensions, no materiality, and no necessary connection with meaning. It is a pattern, not a presence" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- Virtuality — "the cultural perception that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- Informatics (after [[Donna Haraway]]) — "the technologies of information as well as the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes that initiate, accompany, and complicate their development" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/04 Virtual Bodies and Flickering Signifiers]])
- Reflexivity — "the movement whereby that which has been used to generate a system is made, through a changed perspective, to become part of the system it generates" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])

## Key Metaphors

- The Platonic backhand and forehand — the paired moves by which "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- [[The Body as Data|Data made flesh]] — Gibson's *Neuromancer* phrase for the posthuman body recoded as informational pattern ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- The flickering signifier — the signifier under informatics, marked by "unexpected metamorphoses, attenuations, and dispersions" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/04 Virtual Bodies and Flickering Signifiers]])
- The Turing test as a magic trick — a test that "splices your will, desire, and perception into a distributed cognitive system" before you ever make a choice ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/02 Prologue]])
- [[The Extrapolated Body|The body as the original prosthesis]] — the posthuman view of "the body as the original prosthesis we all learn to manipulate," so that replacing it "becomes a continuation of a process" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- The seriation chart and the tiger's iris — conceptual change mapped like the waxing and waning of an artifact's attributes ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- The skeuomorph — the Camry's molded "stitching," the "Catholic Turing Test," a Janus figure looking "to past and future" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- The hyphen and the splice — *Limbo*'s anxious typographic "hyphen" versus the transformative cybernetic "splice" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/07 From Hyphen to Splice - Cybernetic Syntax in Limbo]])
- "Rememory" (after Toni Morrison's *Beloved*) — the book's project of "putting back together parts that have lost touch with one another" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])

## Key Symbols

- The empty cranial cavity — Moravec's robot surgeon reading and discarding the brain "as it is stripped away," the founding image of disembodiment ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- "Beam me up, Scotty" — "a cultural icon for the global informational society," the body dematerialized into pattern and rematerialized "without change" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- The Turing machine in its room — Hodges's "ideal" of a mind "left alone in a room of his own," "the embodiment of a perfect J. S. Mill liberal" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/02 Prologue]])
- The schizoid android — Philip K. Dick's "multiple pun," the figure in whom the human and the not-human split apart ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/09 Turning Reality Inside Out and Right Side Out - Boundary Work in the Mid-Sixties Novels of Philip K. Dick]])
- Tierra's creatures — the "parasites" and "hyperparasites" that evolve inside the computer, "natural forms of life" in an "artificial" medium ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/11 Narratives of Artificial Life]])
- The Chinese room — Searle's thought experiment, reread so that "it is not Searle but the entire room that knows Chinese" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/13 Conclusion - What Does It Mean to be Posthuman?]])

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the master opposition the book contests; the constructed "information/matter duality" in which "information is given the dominant position and materiality runs a distant second" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — embodied reality against "the leap from embodied reality to abstract information" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- Pattern/randomness vs. presence/absence — the epistemic shift, mapped on a semiotic square, that "affects human and textual bodies on two levels at once" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/04 Virtual Bodies and Flickering Signifiers]], [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/12 The Semiotics of Virtuality - Mapping the Posthuman]])
- The hyphen vs. the splice — metonymic separation that "maintain[s] the identity of each" against "a more reflexive and transformative union" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/07 From Hyphen to Splice - Cybernetic Syntax in Limbo]])
- The human vs. the posthuman — successor constructions of subjectivity that "coexist in shifting configurations" rather than replacing one another cleanly ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]])
- Body vs. embodiment / inscription vs. incorporation — "the abstraction of the body" against "a specific material experience," the framework of [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/10 The Materiality of Informatics]]
- Observer inside vs. outside the system — the reflexive problem that drives the second wave, where "feedback can also loop through the observers" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]], [[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/08 The Second Wave of Cybernetics - From Reflexivity to Self-Organization]])
- Mastery vs. distributed cognition — "the very illusion of control" against the view that "conscious agency has never been 'in control'" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/13 Conclusion - What Does It Mean to be Posthuman?]])
