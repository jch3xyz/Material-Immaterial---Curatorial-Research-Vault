# Thinking in Systems

**Author:** [[Donella Meadows]]
**Raw folder:** `raw/Meadows - Thinking in Systems/`

## Brief Orientation

*Thinking in Systems: A Primer* is the accessible primary source for systems theory. It was drafted in 1993 by the systems scientist Donella (Dana) H. Meadows — trained in chemistry and biophysics, a member of Jay Forrester's MIT team on the World3 global model, and lead author of the 1972 *The Limits to Growth* — and edited by Diana Wright of the Sustainability Institute. Meadows "died quite unexpectedly in 2001—before she completed this book" (*A Note from the Editor*, *Directly stated*); Wright published the manuscript posthumously in 2008, keeping "many of the examples… from her first draft in 1993" because "their teachings are as relevant now as they were then." The book is deliberately nontechnical: "I have kept the discussion nontechnical because I want to show what a long way you can go toward understanding systems without turning to mathematics or computers" (Introduction).

Its governing insight is that [[Structure Determines Behavior|structure determines behavior]]. Meadows opens with a Slinky: pulling the lower hand away lets it bounce, but "the answer clearly lies within the Slinky itself. The hands that manipulate it suppress or release some behavior that is latent within the structure of the spring" (Introduction, *Directly stated*). From this she draws the book's most heretical claim: "The system, to a large extent, causes its own behavior!" A [[A System|system]] is "a set of things… interconnected in such a way that they produce their own pattern of behavior over time," made of three "kinds of things: elements, interconnections, and a function or purpose" ([[Elements Interconnections and Purpose]], Ch. 1). Part One dissects that structure — [[Stocks and Flows|stocks and flows]], the [[Balancing Feedback Loop|balancing]] and [[Reinforcing Feedback Loop|reinforcing]] [[Feedback Loops|feedback loops]] that are a system's basic operating unit, [[Dynamic Equilibrium|dynamic equilibrium]], [[Delays|delays]], and the menagerie of behaviors in [[The Systems Zoo|the systems zoo]].

Part Two turns to why systems endure and why they confound us. Meadows argues that [[A system causes its own behavior through its structure|systems work so well]] because of three properties: "resilience, self-organization, or hierarchy" ([[Resilience]], [[Self-Organization]], [[Hierarchy (Systems)]], Ch. 3). They *surprise* us because of [[Bounded Rationality|bounded rationality]], [[Nonlinearity|nonlinearities]], and [[Delays|delays]] — and because "there are no separate systems. The world is a continuum. Where to draw a boundary around a system" is a choice of the observer ([[No Separate Systems]], Ch. 4). The recurring failure modes are the [[System Traps|system traps]] or [[Archetypes (Meadows)|archetypes]] — [[Policy Resistance]], [[The Tragedy of the Commons]], [[Drift to Low Performance]], [[Escalation]], [[Success to the Successful]], [[Shifting the Burden to the Intervenor]], [[Rule Beating]], and [[Seeking the Wrong Goal]] (Ch. 5) — "common structures that produce characteristic behaviors."

The book culminates in [[The Twelve Leverage Points|the twelve leverage points]] (Ch. 6), Meadows's ranked "list of places to intervene in a system," and in [[Dancing with Systems|dancing with systems]] (Ch. 7). The leverage hierarchy is the book's central and, for this vault, most consequential move: the lowest-leverage places to intervene are the *material* parameters — numbers, buffers, stocks, flows — while the highest are the *immaterial* ones — [[Information Flows|information flows]], [[Rules (Systems)|rules]], the power of [[Self-Organization|self-organization]], [[System Goals|goals]], and above all [[Paradigms|paradigms]], "the mind-set out of which the system… arises." "[[Paradigms are the sources of systems|Paradigms are the sources of systems]]," she writes; "there's nothing physical or expensive or even slow in the process of paradigm change. In a single individual it can happen in a… millisecond" (Ch. 6). Higher still is [[Transcending Paradigms|transcending paradigms]] — "to let go into not-knowing." Meadows closes by conceding the limits of the method itself: "We can't control systems or figure them out. But we can dance with them!" (Ch. 7). Systems thinking "can lead us to the edge of what analysis can do and then point beyond—to what can and must be done by the human spirit."

For this vault the book **grounds in its native discipline** the feedback / self-organization vocabulary that the art and posthumanist sources carry secondhand: [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s [[Cybernetics|cybernetics]], [[Jack Burnham|Burnham]]'s [[Systems Esthetics|systems esthetics]], and [[Roy Ascott|Ascott]]'s cybernetic and telematic art all inherit — from Wiener, Forrester, Bateson, Pask — the language of [[Feedback Loops|feedback]], [[Homeostasis|homeostasis]], and [[Emergence|emergence]] that Meadows here states from the discipline's own center. And her leverage-points hierarchy is a major new voice in the [[Material vs Immaterial]] tension: it is a systems-dynamics *argument* that the immaterial layers of a system — information, rules, goals, paradigms — hold more power over its behavior than any material stock or flow, an ordering that runs parallel to (and independent of) the vault's art-historical dematerialization thesis.

## References to Other Authors

- [[Donella Meadows and N. Katherine Hayles]] — the shared cybernetic vocabulary of feedback, homeostasis, and self-organization: Meadows states from within system dynamics the loop/self-organization machinery that Hayles narrates through the history of cybernetics (*interpretive synthesis*).
- [[Donella Meadows and Jack Burnham]] — Burnham's 1968 systems esthetics of the object→system carries secondhand the systems-theory vocabulary Meadows grounds in its native discipline; both make the immaterial system, not the material object, the locus of meaning (*interpretive synthesis*).
- [[Donella Meadows and Roy Ascott]] — Ascott's cybernetic and telematic art of participation, network consciousness, and behaviour-not-thing rhymes with Meadows's dance-with-the-system ethic and her leverage of information flows and paradigms over material parameters (*interpretive synthesis*).
