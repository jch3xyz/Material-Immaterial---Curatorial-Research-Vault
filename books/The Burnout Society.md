# The Burnout Society

**Author:** [[Byung-Chul Han]]
**Raw folder:** `raw/Han - The Burnout Society/`
**Original:** *Müdigkeitsgesellschaft* (2010), trans. Erik Butler (Stanford Briefs / Stanford University Press, 2015)

## Brief Orientation

*The Burnout Society* is Byung-Chul Han's foundational diagnosis of the present and, chronologically, the **earliest** of his three books in the vault — the primary source for much of the Han vocabulary first grounded here secondhand in [[The Transparency Society]] and [[Psychopolitics]]. In eight short essay-chapters Han argues that the twenty-first century has passed out of the [[Immunological Age|immunological age]] of the last century — the age of inside and outside, friend and foe, self and Other, whose pathologies were *infections* — into a [[Neuronal Violence|neuronal]] age whose signature afflictions (depression, ADHD, borderline personality disorder, burnout) are not infections but *infarctions*: not the work of a hostile, negating Other but the product of an [[Positivity|excess of positivity]]. "Harm does not come from negativity alone, but also from positivity—not just from the Other or the foreign, but also from the Same" (`Directly stated`, [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/02 Neuronal Power|Neuronal Power]]).

The book's central historical claim is that Foucault's [[Society of Control|disciplinary society]] — "of hospitals, madhouses, prisons, barracks, and factories" — has been superseded by an [[Achievement Society|achievement society (Leistungsgesellschaft)]] "of fitness studios, office towers, banks, airports, shopping malls, and genetic laboratories," whose inhabitants are no longer "obedience-subjects" but "achievement-subjects" who are "entrepreneurs of themselves." The governing modal verb shifts from the disciplinary, prohibitive *Should* to the limitless, affirmative *Can*: "Unlimited Can is the positive modal verb of achievement society" ([[Can vs Should]], [[raw/Han - The Burnout Society/03 Beyond Disciplinary Society|Beyond Disciplinary Society]]). The achievement-subject, "free from any external instance of domination," is "lord and master of itself" — and this is precisely the trap. The disappearance of domination "does not entail freedom"; it makes "freedom and constraint coincide," so that "the achievement-subject gives itself over to compulsive freedom." Excess performance "escalate[s] into [[Auto-Exploitation|auto-exploitation]]," which "is more efficient than [[Allo-Exploitation|allo-exploitation]], for the feeling of freedom attends it. The exploiter is simultaneously the exploited" (`Directly stated`). [[Burnout]] is the pathological consequence: "The achievement-subject exploits itself until it burns out."

Against this diagnosis Han mounts a defense of the negative. The achievement society is "poor in [[Negativity|negativity]]" — poor in interruption, in the pause, in the power to say no. So the middle chapters recover what positivity forecloses: [[Profound Boredom|profound boredom]], "a dream bird that hatches the egg of experience"; [[Deep Contemplative Attention|deep, contemplative attention]] against [[Multitasking|multitasking]] and [[Hyperattention|hyperattention]]; the [[Vita Contemplativa|vita contemplativa]] reaffirmed (contra Arendt's *vita activa*) as an active "mode of saying no"; the [[Negative Potency|negative potency]] of the "power not-to," read through Bartleby's "I would prefer not to." Han closes by reworking Agamben: the achievement-subject who fashions itself as *homo liber* "turns out to be [[Bare Life|homo sacer]]" — but a *homo sacer* under "the injunction to achieve [Bann der Leistung]" rather than a sovereign injunction, holy because its [[Bare Life|bare life]] "must be preserved at any cost." The closing horizon is an affirmative one: Handke's [[Fundamental Tiredness|fundamental, "we-tiredness"]], a "tiredness that trusts in the world," and a [[Community of Singularities|community that requires neither belonging nor relation]].

The recurring interlocutors are Foucault (disciplinary society), Alain Ehrenberg (depression — the running polemical target), Hannah Arendt (the *vita activa* and the *animal laborans*), Richard Sennett (narcissism and the gratification crisis), Giorgio Agamben (*homo sacer*, bare life, and the Bartleby reading — critiqued), Sigmund Freud (the psychic apparatus, the superego positivizing into the ego ideal), Nietzsche (the "last man" against the "sovereign man"), [[Byung-Chul Han and Walter Benjamin|Benjamin]] (deep boredom), [[Byung-Chul Han and Martin Heidegger|Heidegger]] (Gelassenheit, the Sabbath), [[Byung-Chul Han and Carl Schmitt|Schmitt]] (decision, the enemy, sovereignty), [[Byung-Chul Han and Jean Baudrillard|Baudrillard]] ("He who lives by the Same shall die by the Same"), and [[Byung-Chul Han and Hegel|Hegel]] (negativity keeps existence alive), alongside the literary cases of Melville's Bartleby, Kafka's Prometheus and Hunger Artist, and Handke's *Essay on Tiredness*.

## Major Concepts

This book is the **primary source** for much of the Han vocabulary the vault first grounded in his later books. Notes marked *(primary source)* are existing canonical notes that *The Burnout Society* originates or most fully argues; the rest are new this ingest.

- [[Achievement Society]] *(primary source)* — the society of self-exploiting "achievement-subjects" who are "entrepreneurs of themselves," superseding the disciplinary society.
- [[Auto-Exploitation]] *(primary source)* — voluntary self-exploitation "more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it."
- [[Allo-Exploitation]] — exploitation by an external master; the disciplinary mode the achievement society has surpassed.
- [[Positivity]] *(primary source)* / [[Negativity]] *(primary source)* — the master opposition; achievement society "is in the process of discarding negativity."
- [[Neuronal Violence]] — the system-immanent "violence of positivity," "a terror of immanence" that "does not deprive, it saturates; it does not exclude, it exhausts."
- [[Burnout]] — the title malady: the pathological endpoint of auto-exploitation, "when the ego overheats, which follows from too much of the Same."
- [[Can vs Should]] *(primary source)* — the modal shift from the disciplinary *Should* to the achievement society's "Unlimited Can."
- [[Hell of the Same]] *(primary source)* — "Burnout syndrome occurs when the ego overheats, which follows from too much of the Same"; Baudrillard's "He who lives by the Same shall die by the Same."
- [[Acceleration]] *(primary source)* — "Acceleration is abolishing all intervals"; the loss of the pause and the "between."
- [[Immunological Age]] *(primary source)* — the epoch of the foreign Other, friend/foe, the Cold War dispositive, now displaced by the neuronal age of positivity.
- [[Multitasking]] — not progress but "regression" to the scanning vigilance of wild animals.
- [[Hyperattention]] — the scattered, "broad but flat" awareness displacing deep contemplative attention.
- [[Deep Contemplative Attention]] — the "long and slow gaze" Han names as the precondition of all culture and philosophy.
- [[Profound Boredom]] — Benjamin's "dream bird that hatches the egg of experience"; the idleness that incubates the new.
- [[Vita Contemplativa]] — the contemplative life reaffirmed as an active "mode of saying no," contra Arendt.
- [[Animal Laborans]] — Arendt's "laboring animal," re-read: the late-modern *animal laborans* is "hyperactive and hyperneurotic," not serene.
- [[Negative Potency]] — "the power not to do—to adopt Nietzsche's phrasing, the power to say no."
- [[Hyperactivity as Hyperpassivity]] — "hyperactive intensification leads to an abrupt switch into hyperpassivity"; the maximally active subject is maximally passive.
- [[Bare Life]] — life "stripped of all transcendent value… reduced to the immanency of vital functions"; the achievement-subject as "homo sacer of his own self," "undead," holy.
- [[Doping Society]] — the achievement society's evolution into a regime of neuroenhancement: "Health is the new goddess."
- [[Fundamental Tiredness]] — the affirmative, healing "we-tiredness," a "tiredness that trusts in the world."
- [[Community of Singularities]] — the community founded by fundamental tiredness, held together by a "friendly *and*," needing "neither belonging nor relation."
- [[The Project (Han)]] *(primary source)* — "the change from subject to project"; "the project turns out to be a projectile that the achievement-subject is aiming at itself."
- [[Narrativity]] — denarrativization "makes life bare"; "the reduction of life to biological, vital processes… strips it of all narrativity."
- [[Society of Control]] — rejected as a category: it "still contains too much negativity" to name the achievement society.
- [[Self-Optimization]] — the performance-machine [Leistungsmaschine] to be optimized "without disturbance."
- [[Healing as Killing]] — the *Psychopolitics* extension of the same logic into the psyche; foreshadowed here in the doping society.
- [[Biopolitics]] — *homo sacer* / bare life / sovereignty territory Han re-reads against Agamben.

## Major Arguments

- [[The pathologies of the present are infarctions not infections]] — the present age is "determined neither by bacteria nor by viruses, but by neurons"; its illnesses follow "not from the negativity of what is immunologically foreign, but from an excess of positivity." `Directly stated`.
- [[Depression is caused by the imperative to achieve not the failure to become oneself]] — Han's central polemic against Ehrenberg: "It is not the imperative only to belong to oneself, but the pressure to achieve that causes exhaustive depression." `Directly stated`.

> Further arguments developed across the chapters but not yet minted as standalone notes this pass: that the disappearance of domination does not produce freedom but makes freedom and constraint coincide; that auto-exploitation outperforms allo-exploitation because it feels like freedom; that hyperactivity is an "extremely passive form of doing"; that depression eludes psychoanalysis because its conditions (repression, the negativity of prohibition) have disappeared; that the superego "positivizes itself into the ego ideal"; and that the achievement-subject is the *homo sacer* of his own self under the "injunction to achieve." These are flagged as realize-candidates for a future orphan-generation pass.

## Important Definitions

- [[Erfahrung vs Erlebnis (Han)]] — deep, altering experience (*Erfahrung*) vs. the ego-expanding event (*Erlebnis*); developed here via Sennett ("The narcissist is not hungry for experiences, he is hungry for Experience").

## Major Tensions

- [[Positivity vs Negativity]] — the governing axis of the whole diagnosis: a world "poor in negativity" and dominated by "excess positivity."
- [[Freedom vs Control]] — the "dialectic of freedom": "the disappearance of domination does not entail freedom," but a "compulsive freedom" that "abruptly switches over into violence."
- [[Discipline vs Control]] — disciplinary society (obedience-subjects, *Should*, prohibition) vs. achievement society (achievement-subjects, *Can*, the project).
- [[Subject vs Project]] *(primary source)* — "the change from subject to project"; the achievement-subject "liberates itself into a project."
- [[Narration vs Addition]] — denarrativization and the loss of "conclusive forms" against the additive openness that "favors growth."
