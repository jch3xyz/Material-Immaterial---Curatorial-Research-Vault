# Beyond Modern Sculpture

**Author:** [[Jack Burnham]]
**Full title:** *Beyond Modern Sculpture: The Effects of Science and Technology on the Sculpture of This Century*
**First edition:** George Braziller, New York, 1968
**Raw folder:** `raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/`

## Brief Orientation

*Beyond Modern Sculpture* (1968) is the foundational text of [[Systems Esthetics]] and the book in which [[Jack Burnham]] reframes the history of modern sculpture as a single, irreversible drift from the **object** to the **system**. Burnham's thesis is that sculpture is not dying of formal exhaustion but is "time extinction through the attainment of goals as old as sculpture itself" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]) — its oldest goal being to "bring inert matter to life." He argues that what has driven sculpture off its twenty-five-century course of the "free-standing human form and its life-emitting properties" is **technics**, the fusion of "science and technology (or technics—to link them into a single word)," which he, following Friedrich Jünger, treats as "the metaphysics of our century" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). Sculpture is recast "as an indication of man's changing conception of biology, as an indication of his biological role, and especially as a form of biological activity in itself." This is the book that supplies the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis its art-historical engine: the movement from sculpture as a palpable, based, anthropomorphic OBJECT toward sculpture as an immaterial, responsive, lifelike SYSTEM.

The book is organized in two parts mirroring that split, declared in the Introduction's closing section "Object and System": "The object denotes sculpture in its traditional physical form, whereas the system (an interacting assembly of varying complexity) is the means by which sculpture gradually departs from its object state and assumes some measure of lifelike activity" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). **Part I — Sculpture as Object** (Introduction + Chapters 1–4) traces [[Reification]] ("thingification," *Verdinglichung*, taken from Marx), the [[The Vanishing Base|vanishing base]], [[Vitalism]] and [[Organicism]] as the "biotic sources," [[Formalism (Burnham)|formalism]] as a "weary vocabulary" of proto-mechanical stylization, and finally [[Form Exhaustion]] and the rise of [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)|phenomenalism]] in Object/Minimal art. **Part II — Sculpture as System** (Chapters 5–8) follows the same drive into [[Automata]] and [[Subsculpture]], [[Kinetic Art|kineticism]] (the [[The Unrequited Art|"unrequited art"]]), [[Light as a Sculpture Medium|light]], and finally [[Cyborg Art]] — sculpture that, via [[Cybernetics]] and [[Reciprocal Communication|two-way communication]], would for the first time "respond to man in any intelligent fashion" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]).

Burnham's controlling claim is heterodox: abstraction is not, as Worringer held, psychic protection from cosmic anguish, but in an industrial society "nothing less than psychic preparation for the entire re-creation of society, including remaking the biological composition of its inhabitants" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). The sculptor "begins to imitate the machine maker and the creator of scientific models," and "the drive that finally prompted the sculptor to forsake naturalism and the human figure was not that of the desire to destroy the biological replica, but the realization that if he was serious in his quest to bring inert matter to life anthropomorphism would not accomplish it. The machine, he intuitively realized... remained the only means by which man would eventually reconstruct intelligent life" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). The icon of this age is not the totem but "the scientific model which interprets principles believed to be operative in the real world" — and the most original geometric sculpture owes itself not to materialism but to a "scientific idealism" that mistakes provisional scientific models for "ideational verities" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]).

Within the vault, *Beyond Modern Sculpture* is the keystone bridge between the **art** cluster and the **cyborg/posthuman/systems** cluster. It is the immediate theoretical context for [[Lucy Lippard|Lippard]]'s [[Dematerialization]] (Burnham's "Systems Esthetics," 1968, runs parallel to the same year's "Dematerialization of Art" article it is cited beside in *Six Years*); it anticipates [[Donna Haraway|Haraway]]'s [[The Cyborg|cyborg]] and [[N. Katherine Hayles|Hayles]]'s [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] by grounding "the cybernetic organism as an art form" in a Wienerian account of [[Cybernetics]], [[Feedback Loops|feedback]], and [[Homeostasis]]; and it shares with [[Marshall McLuhan|McLuhan]] a reading of technology as an environing system that extends and re-mediates the body. It is, finally, the primary source for realizing the vault's long-deferred [[New Media Art]] and [[Art as Experiential Technology]] research-foci. ## Major Concepts

- [[Systems Esthetics]] — the book's framework; sculpture read through the object→system transition; *Directly stated* (the Introduction's "Object and System" section).
- [[Object and System]] — the master division: "The object denotes sculpture in its traditional physical form, whereas the system... assumes some measure of lifelike activity" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Reification]] — Marx's "thingification" (*Verdinglichung*) repurposed as "the constant resynchronization of artistic sensibility with a disclosed form-world of scientific theory" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Technics]] — "science and technology (or technics—to link them into a single word)," responsible "both physically and spiritually" for "sculpture's impending rupture with the past" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Scientific Idealism]] — the impulse that "attempts to view essential reality through the theories and models put forth provisionally by scientists" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The Scientific Demiurge]] — "art becomes a psychical manifestation of the scientific demiurge, which moves ceaselessly in a single irreversible direction" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The Sculpture Base]] · [[The Vanishing Base]] — the base as the convention recognizing sculpture's "innate lack of biological autonomy and mobility," and its dissolution ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/03 Chapter 1 - Sculpture's Vanishing Base]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The Readymade]] · [[Object Art]] — sculpture "de-idealized," "cut free of all previous iconic influences," producing Object or Minimal sculpture ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Vitalism]] · [[Organicism]] — "vitalism is introduced both as a scientific doctrine and a sculpture aesthetic"; the biotic sources of modern sculpture ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]], [[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/04 Chapter 2 - The Biotic Sources of Modern Sculpture]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Form Exhaustion]] · [[Formalism (Burnham)]] — formalism as "The Weary Vocabulary"; "the essence of formalism in sculpture resided in its power for imbuing sculpture with varieties of proto-mechanical stylization" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The Scientific Model]] · [[Topology as the New Formalism]] — the model as "the contemporary icon par excellence"; topology as the formalism that succeeds the exhausted classical-machine vocabulary. *Directly stated / Strongly implied*.
- [[Light as a Sculpture Medium]] · [[Luminosity]] — "the most flexible visual art form yet devised"; luminosity as the visual impression of "internal energy in the source itself" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/09 Chapter 7 - Light as Sculpture Medium]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Phenomenalism (Burnham)]] — Object sculpture "seeks transcendence through the seeming rationality of materialism colored with phenomenological considerations" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Automata]] · [[Subsculpture]] — "Automata may be defined as all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men"; subsculpture as "types of images aesthetically ranked below sculpture" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The Robot]] · [[The Faustian Urge to Re-create Life Nonbiologically]] · [[The Clockwork Universe]] — "the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge... to re-create life nonbiologically" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The Unrequited Art]] · [[Kinetic Art]] · [[The New Tendency and Field Kinetics]] · [[The Closed Kinematic Chain]] — kineticism as "the celebration of what Franz Reuleaux... called the 'closed kinematic chain'" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/08 Chapter 6 - Kineticism - The Unrequited Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Systems Consciousness]] · [[Cyborg Art]] · [[Reciprocal Communication]] · [[The Mock Robot]] — Cyborg Art as "the cybernetic organism as an art form... the next, and perhaps ultimate stage of sculpture" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Anthropomorphism in Sculpture]] · [[Sculpture as Biological Signal]] — sculpture "as a form of biological activity in itself," and the abandonment of the human form ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis*.

## Major Arguments

- [[Modern sculpture is not dying of formal exhaustion but achieving its oldest goal the simulation of life]] — "not death through formal exhaustion as has appeared imminent, but time extinction through the attainment of goals as old as sculpture itself" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Only the machine not anthropomorphism can bring inert matter to life]] — "if he was serious in his quest to bring inert matter to life anthropomorphism would not accomplish it. The machine... remained the only means" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[In an industrial society abstraction is psychic preparation for the re-creation of life]] — "the role of artistic abstraction is nothing less than psychic preparation for the entire re-creation of society" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[A base-less sculpture ceases to be an object and becomes a physical system of forces]] — Ch. 1's reading of the vanishing base. *Strongly implied*.
- [[Constructivist sculpture dematerialized its support from the conceptual models of science]] — "Constructivism found its impetus in the evanescences of modern structural engineering, mathematics, and physics" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Vitalism is the transitional step from the sculptural object to the system]] — "vitalism, as a source of artistic reification, lasted as long as it remained tenable as a scientific philosophy" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis*.
- [[The mechanist-vitalist dispute was settled by sensibilities not facts]] — Ch. 2's account of vitalism's decline. *Strongly implied*.
- [[Biology machine theory and sculpture converge on understanding living matter through its creation]] — the Faustian re-creation thesis. *Strongly implied*.
- [[Technology's logic of duplication has dealt a death blow to the unique art object]] — "like the realistic portrait after the invention of photography, the object has been debased by its profusion" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The obsolescence of the visual scientific model drove the dematerialization of sculpture]] — Ch. 3/4 thesis. *Strongly implied*.
- [[Light not form determines our perception of sculpture]] — Ch. 7. *Strongly implied*.
- [[Object art shifts the obligation to vitalize sculpture onto the viewers perception]] — "not free of the idiosyncrasies of human perception... its shape is ambiguously interpreted through viewer ambience" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis*.
- [[Formalism and vitalism are exhausted because scientific reality has become invisible]] — "The very paradigms by which science expands... are increasingly difficult to approximate through the traditional means of sculpture" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The history of automata is the aesthetic core of a Faustian drive to re-create life nonbiologically]] — "Here the aesthetic core of the Faustian urge shows itself" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Future automata will abandon the human form because every replacement-machine first preserves then sheds the appearance of what it replaces]] — Ch. 8 argument. *Strongly implied*.
- [[A machine may be more responsive to human needs than another person]] — the cyborg-art horizon, "the kind of interaction of two men talking" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]). *Strongly implied*.
- [[The kinetic construction is not an object but a system]] — Ch. 6. *Directly stated*.
- [[The machine must be humanized with imprecision and fallibility to make art live]] — Tinguely's "joyous machine"; "Successful Kinetic Art until now has either defied or trivialized the principles of mechanical invention" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/08 Chapter 6 - Kineticism - The Unrequited Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[The true goal of the kinetic revival was immateriality not machine motion]] — Ch. 6/7 argument. *Strongly implied*.
- [[Art's correspondence with science is visual analogy not borrowed method]] — "What connects art to science is an a biding similarity between the artistic and scientific mind" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis*.
- [[Technics is self-augmenting and only madness escapes the machine]] — "madness lies in the indefinite possibilities of technology" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]). *Directly stated / Interpretive synthesis*.
- [[An art movement dies by realizing its goals not by self-destruction]] — the "time extinction through the attainment of goals" thesis applied to movements. *Strongly implied*.
- [[As art accepts technology it depends increasingly on the engineer]] — "What slight technical facility Kinetic artists have demonstrated has usually been the result of technical assistance contributed by sympathetic technicians" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/08 Chapter 6 - Kineticism - The Unrequited Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[All prior sculpture is a one-way message that does not respond to man]] — "these are all one-way messages... the result in every case was not communication but one-way stimulation" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Sculpture seeks its own obliteration by becoming the life it imitates]] — Ch. 8 culmination. *Strongly implied*.
- [[For Object Art presence is everything for Systems Art information is the key]] — the Part I / Part II contrast. *Interpretive synthesis*.
- [[There are no qualitative differences between living and nonliving matter]] — the physicochemical premise: "physicochemical conditions rather than spiritual energies are responsible for the origination of biological life" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[A biological society that builds superintelligent automata will be dominated by them]] — Ch. 8 speculation. *Speculative connection*.

## Important Definitions

- [[System (Burnham)]] — "an aggregate of components; first, its parts are mutually dependent; and second, it may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Object (Burnham)]] — "palpable, finite in dimensions... it has place, remaining inert and stationary" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Reification (Burnham)]] — "thingification" (*Verdinglichung*), "the way in which... societies transformed all ideas into objects" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Technique (Ellul via Burnham)]] — technics as "the metaphysics of our century." *Directly stated (Jünger named; Ellul tradition)*.
- [[Entropy (Burnham)]] — disorder/dissipation as the counter-tendency to system-building. *Interpretive synthesis*.
- [[Elan Vital (Bergson via Burnham)]] — the vitalist "life force" underwriting biomorphic sculpture. *Directly stated*.
- [[Mechanism (Burnham)]] — the machine as "a discrete, closed unintelligent system for producing work" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/08 Chapter 6 - Kineticism - The Unrequited Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Base Pedestal Plinth (Burnham)]] — the base as the convention recognizing sculpture's lack of biological autonomy ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/03 Chapter 1 - Sculpture's Vanishing Base]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Topology (Burnham)]] — the geometry of continuous transformation, the new formalism succeeding the classical machine. *Directly stated / Strongly implied*.
- [[Phenomenological Reduction (Husserl via Burnham)]] — the bracketing of the object to its appearance, underwriting Object-art perception. *Directly stated (Husserl tradition)*.
- [[Carnal Intersubjectivity (Merleau-Ponty via Burnham)]] — embodied perception as the ground of phenomenalist sculpture. *Directly stated (Merleau-Ponty named)*.
- [[Subsculpture (Burnham)]] — "types of images aesthetically ranked below sculpture and not included in the fine arts" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Automata (Burnham)]] — "all seemingly self-propelled or self-animated images of animals or men" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/07 Chapter 5 - Sculpture and Automata]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Robot (Capek via Burnham)]] — the mechanical worker/servant figure; Čapek's coinage. *Directly stated*.
- [[Cyborg (Burnham)]] — "the cybernetic organism as an art form" ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Cybernetics (Burnham)]] — after Wiener's 1948 *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine* ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/10 Chapter 8 - Robot and Cyborg Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Negative Feedback (Burnham)]] — error-correcting return of output as input; the basis of self-regulation. *Directly stated*.
- [[Homeostasis (Cannon via Burnham)]] — internally maintained equilibrium; "kinetically sustained equilibrium" as a characteristic of systems ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/02 Introduction]]). *Directly stated (Cannon tradition)*.
- [[Plasma (Burnham)]] — matter as energy-state; the dissolution of solid form toward field. *Interpretive synthesis*.
- [[Kinematics vs Kinetics (Burnham)]] — "kinematics has remained the geometry of touching surfaces"; kinetics as the study of motion and its effects ([[raw/Burnham - Beyond Modern Sculpture/08 Chapter 6 - Kineticism - The Unrequited Art]]). *Directly stated*.
- [[Objecthood (Fried via Burnham)]] — Michael Fried's category of the literal/theatrical object, in tension with sculptural presence. *Directly stated (Fried named)*.
