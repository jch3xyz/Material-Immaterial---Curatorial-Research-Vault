# Six Years: The Dematerialization of the Art Object from 1966 to 1972

**Author:** [[Lucy Lippard]]
**First edition:** Praeger, New York, 1973; reprinted University of California Press, 1996
**Raw folder:** `raw/Lippard - Six Years/`

## Brief Orientation

*Six Years* is a hybrid object: a chronological documentary anthology bracketed by essays in Lippard's own voice. Its core, organized year by year from 1966 to 1972, is "basically a bibliography and list of events, arranged chronologically" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/03 Author's Notes]]) — verbatim artist statements, exhibition records, catalogue texts, symposia transcripts, and book listings, set in a deliberately typographic code (bold for factual information, roman for anthological excerpts, italics for the editor's commentary). Around this chronicle Lippard wraps a 1995 retrospective introduction ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]), a 1973/1996 [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface|Preface]], and a 1973 [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/11 Postface|Postface]]. The book is therefore both a primary source for and a first historical account of what came to be called Conceptual art.

The book's organizing thesis is the **dematerialization of the art object**: a "process of dematerialization, or a deemphasis on material aspects (uniqueness, permanence, decorative attractive[ness])" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface]]) in favor of work "in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized'" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). This is the central art-historical instance of the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis — and it arrives already self-contested. Lippard concedes the term's inaccuracy ("a piece of paper or a photograph is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead"), and the Art-Language group's 1968 letter, reproduced in the chronicle, argues the point at length: "Matter is a specialized form of energy . . . they are nevertheless matter in one of its forms" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/07 1968]]). The book preserves the objection rather than resolving it.

Crucially, *Six Years* is a book whose **form is its argument**. Lippard wanted it "to expose the chaotic network of ideas in the air . . . the form of the book intentionally reflects chaos rather than imposing order" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface]]); its "anti-individualistic bias" makes it impossible to trace "any single artist's sequential development . . . without the help of the index." The chronicle-as-medium enacts the very dematerialization it documents: the publication becomes the artwork's distribution channel, paralleling [[Seth Siegelaub]]'s catalogues-as-exhibitions and the artists' books the book lists. (Note: the raw files are heavily damaged OCR; quotations here are transcribed character-for-character from the raw text, including its flaws, and many statements in the year-files are spoken by the *artists* whom Lippard quotes, not by Lippard.)

The book is finally a **self-critical verdict**. The hope that an idea-based art "would be able to avoid the general commercialization" was, the [[raw/Lippard - Six Years/11 Postface|Postface]] judges, "for the most part unfounded": "the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums . . . art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries." The 1995 introduction names the irony with terminal economy: "The escape was temporary. Art was recaptured and sent back to its white cell, but parole is always a possibility" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). The title's deepest irony is that the book *itself* became "a very expensive collector's item, paralleling the fate of the art it espoused" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/03 Author's Notes]]) — the recuperation argument staged on the book as object.

## Major Concepts

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the vault's master axis; *Six Years* is its art-historical case study, the "process of dematerialization, or a deemphasis on material aspects" of the art object ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface]]).
- **Dematerialization of the art object** — the book's title thesis; idea paramount, material form "secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). Forward-stub; the canonical concept note for this term.
- **Conceptual art** — "work in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary"; distinguished by [[Sol LeWitt]] into "conceptual art with a small c" versus "Conceptual art with a capital C" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). Forward-stub.
- [[Systems Theory]] — the period's pervasive frame; [[Hans Haacke]]: "the working premise is to think in terms of systems . . . Systems can be physical, biological, or social" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]); Jack Burnham's "Systems Aesthetics" (1968).
- [[Information]] — art reconceived as information rather than visual object; [[John Baldessari]]: "information could be interesting in its own right and need not be visual" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/05 1966]]).
- **Idea as machine** — [[Sol LeWitt]]: "The idea becomes a machine that makes the art" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]; LeWitt's line from his 1967 "Paragraphs on Conceptual Art"). Forward-stub.
- **Art as idea as idea** — [[Joseph Kosuth]]'s series title and program; "The actual works of art are ideas" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/06 1967]]). Forward-stub.
- **Distribution as form** — communication and distribution built into the work; [[Seth Siegelaub]]: "the catalog; the physical presence (of the work) is supplementary to the catalog" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/08 1969]]).
- **Anti-form / process** — [[Robert Morris]]: random piling and loose stacking that give "passing form to the material," refusing "preconceived enduring forms" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/07 1968]]). Forward-stub.
- [[New Media Art]] — the inexpensive ephemeral mediums (video, performance, photography, narrative, text) that the book inaugurates as art's distribution channels (research focus; forward-stub).
- [[Art as Experiential Technology]] — the period's "art-as-life, life-as-art" and behavioral works (Ukeles, Piper, Acconci) as a research focus (forward-stub).

## Major Arguments

- **The dematerialization of the art object** — the 1960s saw "a profound dematerialization of art . . . as more and more work is designed in a studio but executed elsewhere . . . the studio is again becoming a study" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/07 1968]], Lippard & Chandler). Directly stated. Forward-stub argument note.
- **The escape attempt failed to escape the market** — "Hopes that 'conceptual art' would be able to avoid the general commercialization . . . were for the most part unfounded"; the conceptualists now sell "for substantial sums" and "art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/11 Postface]]). Directly stated. The book's terminal self-critical verdict.
- **Art was recaptured but parole is possible** — the 1995 reframing of that verdict: "The escape was temporary. Art was recaptured and sent back to its white cell, but parole is always a possibility" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). Directly stated.
- **The market recuperated the critique, and recuperated this book** — the title's irony made literal: *Six Years* "went out of print for over a decade and became, ironically, a very expensive collector's item, paralleling the fate of the art it espoused" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/03 Author's Notes]]). Directly stated; the recuperation argument staged on the book-as-object.
- **It was the form, not the content, that carried the politics** — "it was usually the form rather than the content of Conceptual art that carried a political message. The frame was there to be broken out of" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). Directly stated.
- **"Dematerialization" is an imperfect but indispensable term** — Lippard concedes "dematerialization is an inaccurate term, that a piece of paper or a photograph is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead . . . But for lack of a better term I have continued to refer to a process of dematerialization" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface]]). Directly stated.
- **The chronicle's form is its argument** — "the form of the book intentionally reflects chaos rather than imposing order"; the point "is phenomenological rather than historical" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface]]). Strongly implied as method, directly stated as intent.

## Important Definitions

- **Conceptual art (Lippard)** — "work in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized'" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). Forward-stub definition note.
- **Dematerialization (Lippard)** — "a process of dematerialization, or a deemphasis on material aspects (uniqueness, permanence, decorative attractiveness)" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface]]). Forward-stub definition note.

## Key Metaphors

- **The white cell** — art as prisoner: "Art was recaptured and sent back to its white cell, but parole is always a possibility" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]); the white cube/prison cell pun condenses the recapture thesis. Forward-stub.
- **The crack in the wall** — escape imagery: the ephemeral mediums let women "move through this crack in the art world's walls" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]); "the frame was there to be broken out of." Forward-stub.
- **The studio becoming a study** — dematerialization figured as a change of workspace: "The studio is again becoming a study" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/07 1968]]). Forward-stub.

## Major Tensions

- [[Material vs Immaterial]] — the central axis: idea versus object, "dematerialized" work versus the Art-Language counter-claim that "they are nevertheless matter in one of its forms" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/07 1968]]).
- **Escape vs Recuperation** — the constitutive tension of the whole book: the artists' attempt to make work "free of art-world commodity status" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]) against the market that "absorb[s] conceptual art as another 'movement'" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface]]) and "recaptured" it. Forward-stub.
- **Idea vs Object** — the period's own framing; Lippard & Chandler saw "ultra-conceptual art emerging from two directions: art as idea and art as action" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). Forward-stub.
- **Communication vs Community** — Lippard's own caveat: "Communication (but not community) and distribution (but not accessibility) were inherent in Conceptual art . . . the forms pointed toward democratic outreach, the content did not" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). Forward-stub.
- **Acceptive vs Rejective** — [[Sol LeWitt]]'s and Lippard's distinction between an open, "acceptive" approach (Baxter, Nauman) and a closed, "rejective" one (Weiner, Kosuth) ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/04 Preface]]). Forward-stub.
