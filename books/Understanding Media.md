# Understanding Media

**Author:** [[Marshall McLuhan]]
**Raw folder:** `raw/McLuhan - Understanding Media/`

## Brief Orientation

*Understanding Media: The Extensions of Man* (1964) advances a single, recursive thesis and then tests it against thirty-three technologies. The thesis is [[The Medium Is the Message]]: "the personal and social consequences of any medium— that is, of any extension of ourselves — result from the new scale that is introduced into our affairs by each extension of ourselves, or by any new technology" (Ch. 1, *Directly stated*). What a medium carries — its "content" — is almost beside the point; "the 'content' of any medium is always another medium" (Ch. 1), and content is merely "the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind." Against the era's common sense — "the products of modern science are not in themselves good or bad; it is the way they are used that determines their value" — McLuhan flatly counters that this neutral-instrument view "is the numb stance of the technological idiot." The book is for anyone trying to read the form of a technology rather than its programming.

The structure is two-part. **Part I** (Introduction through Ch. 7) builds the general theory: media as [[Media as Extensions of Man|extensions of man]]; the division of [[Media Hot and Cold|hot and cool media]]; the [[Reversal of the Overheated Medium]] at the [[The Break Boundary|break boundary]]; the [[Autoamputation]]/[[Narcosis]] account of why every extension numbs the sense it amplifies ("This extension of himself by mirror numbed his perceptions until he became the servomechanism of his own extended or repeated image"); [[Hybrid Energy|hybrid energy]] released when media cross; [[Media as Translators|media as translators]] of experience; and [[Challenge and Collapse]], where speed-up of information drives a culture past a threshold of breakdown. **Part II** (Ch. 8 through Ch. 33) is thirty-three case studies — from the spoken and written word through roads, money, clocks, print, comics, the photograph, the press, the motorcar, games, telegraph, typewriter, telephone, phonograph, movies, radio, television, weapons, and finally automation — each applying the theory to one extension and tracing its effect on the [[Sense Ratios|sense ratios]] of its users.

McLuhan's historical arc is a reversal: "After three thousand years of explosion, by means of fragmentary and mechanical technologies, the Western world is imploding" (Introduction). The mechanical age extended the body in space and produced [[Typographic Man|typographic man]] — fragmented, specialist, detached, the literate subject who gained "the power to act without reacting." The electric age extends "our central nervous system itself in a global embrace, abolishing both space and time" — the [[The Electric Age as Extension of the Central Nervous System|central nervous system worn outside the skull]], collapsing the world into [[The Global Village|a global village]] and reversing [[Explosion vs Implosion|explosion into implosion]], specialism into wholeness, detachment into participation. The closing chapter brings this to [[Automation as Learning a Living|automation]]: "Automation is information," it "ends jobs" but restores [[The Restoration of the Role|roles]], and fuses work, learning, and leisure.

For this vault the book is a keystone upstream source. Its claim that "we have extended our central nervous system itself" and approach "the technological simulation of consciousness" prefigures the [[Posthumanism|posthuman]] and [[Distributed Cognition|distributed-cognition]] arguments grounded here in [[How We Became Posthuman|Hayles]], the [[The Body as Data|body-as-data]] and [[Dataism|Dataism]] of [[Homo Deus|Harari]], and the merger cosmology of [[The Singularity Is Near|Kurzweil]] — McLuhan's [[Man as Servomechanism of His Extensions|servomechanism]] is the ancestor of [[The Cyborg|the cyborg]]. His diagnosis that "we have leased our central nervous systems to private corporations" ([[Leasing the Central Nervous System]]) is a direct forerunner of Han's [[Digital Panopticon|digital panopticon]] and the vault's surveillance / [[Embodiment|embodiment]] / [[Material vs Immaterial|material-immaterial]] foci: the medium as the message is, at bottom, a claim that the immaterial form of a technology — not its material content — restructures the sensing body and the social field.
