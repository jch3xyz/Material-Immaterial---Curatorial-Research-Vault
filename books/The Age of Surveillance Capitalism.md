# The Age of Surveillance Capitalism

**Author:** [[Shoshana Zuboff]]
**Raw folder:** `raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/`

## Brief Orientation

*The Age of Surveillance Capitalism: The Fight for a Human Future at the New Frontier of Power* (2019) is the definitive critique of what Zuboff names in her opening mock-lexicon entry as "A new economic order that claims human experience as free raw material for hidden commercial practices of extraction, prediction, and sales" (`Directly stated` — [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/02 The Definition]]). Its central insistence is that this is not a technology but an economic logic: "Surveillance capitalism is not technology; it is a logic that imbues technology and commands it into action" (`Directly stated` — [[raw/Zuboff - The Age of Surveillance Capitalism/03 Chapter 1 - Home or Exile in the Digital Future]]). The book traces a pipeline — experience "unilaterally" claimed as [[Behavioral Surplus]], fed into "advanced manufacturing processes known as 'machine intelligence,'" and "fabricated into prediction products that anticipate what you will do now, soon, and later," which are then traded in "behavioral futures markets" (`Directly stated` — Ch. 1). We are not the customer and not the product: "We are the sources of surveillance capitalism's crucial surplus: the objects of a technologically advanced and increasingly inescapable raw-material-extraction operation" (`Directly stated` — Ch. 1).

The book's arc moves from discovery to domination. Zuboff narrates [[The Discovery of Behavioral Surplus]] at Google, the construction of [[Fortifications]] and [[Cornering]] strategies, the privatization of [[The Division of Learning in Society]], and the migration of extraction from the online world into the real one through [[Rendition]]. Its decisive turn is from knowledge to power: "With this reorientation from knowledge to power, it is no longer enough to automate information flows about us; the goal now is to automate us" (`Directly stated` — Ch. 1). This culminates in [[Instrumentarian Power]] — "a new species of power that I call instrumentarianism. Instrumentarian power knows and shapes human behavior toward others' ends" (`Directly stated` — Ch. 1) — materialized in [[Big Other]], "a ubiquitous sensate, networked, computational infrastructure." Zuboff is emphatic that this is not [[Big Brother vs Big Other|totalitarianism]]: "Totalitarianism was a transformation of the state into a project of total possession. Instrumentarianism and its materialization in Big Other signal the transformation of the market into a project of total certainty" (`Directly stated` — Ch. 1).

The political stakes are framed as constitutional, not merely commercial: surveillance capitalism is "best described as a coup from above, not an overthrow of the state but rather an overthrow of the people's sovereignty" (`Directly stated` — Ch. 1), threatening "the elemental right to the future tense" and "the right to sanctuary." Its closing wager is that nothing here is inevitable — "technological inevitability does not exist" — and that naming an unprecedented power is the first act of resistance.

For this vault, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* realizes the long-committed surveillance forward-stubs [[Surveillance]] and [[Quantified Self]], and supplies the for-profit institutional machinery beneath several existing diagnoses (`Interpretive synthesis`): the extraction of behavioral surplus from voluntary self-exposure is the economic engine under Han's [[Digital Panopticon]] and [[Psychopolitics]]; Big Other is the corporate counterpart to Han's [[Friendly Big Brother]]; the "raw material → behavioral data → prediction" pipeline is the for-profit form of Harari's and Han's [[Dataism]] and [[Big Data]]; and the ubiquitous sensate infrastructure is the very pervasive computing that Kurzweil embraced as liberation, here re-described as domination.
