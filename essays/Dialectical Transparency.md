# Dialectical Transparency

*The wish that power would turn transparent and the dread of being made transparent to power are one wish, run in opposite directions. From the translucent computer to the sealed black slab, from the data double to the regulated nervous system to the legible lover, transparency keeps arriving — and keeps dissolving the very thing it was meant to reveal.*

In 1998 the most desirable object in consumer technology let you see its own guts. The first iMac came sheathed in translucent Bondi-blue polycarbonate, and the point of the plastic was that it was clear: the curatorial record at the Victoria and Albert Museum credits Jonathan Ive with the innovation of housing the computer "in translucent coloured polycarbonate rather than giving it a standard grey box," a move that went on to influence "the shapes and colours of all types of domestic electrical products."[^1] Walter Isaacson reports Ive's own gloss on the gesture: the translucency "connected the engineering of the computer to the design," and the casing "would make visible the care that had gone into making all the components."[^2] A museum catalogue is blunter about the appeal: the see-through keyboard, mouse, and cord were "a marketing ploy to make consumers feel that the items were 'honest.'"[^3] Within two years the look had metastasised into a craze; as one design history records, the iMac "sparked a widespread industrial design revolution," and "a horde of translucent multicolored plastic goods flooded nearly every consumer market," from game consoles and telephones down to "staplers, paper towel dispensers, and kitchen appliances."[^4] For one strange moment around the millennium, the aspirational gesture of the machine was to show you what was inside it.

Pick up the most desirable object of the present and the gesture has reversed. The phone in your hand is a sealed black slab. Its software underwent, in 2013, the canonical pivot from skeuomorphism to flat design — Ive again, now stripping away every drawn seam and shadow in the name of what he called "a profound and enduring beauty in simplicity; in clarity, in efficiency."[^5] Its hardware is glued shut: the repair advocates at iFixit catalogue how "batteries get glued in with industrial adhesive," components are "soldered together into ungainly assemblies," and proprietary screws bar the way, so that a device can be, in their phrase, "designed never to be taken apart."[^6] Twenty years separate the two objects, and across that span the machine has become radically more complex, more capable, and more woven into every hour of the day — and radically more opaque. The guts are no longer the selling point; the absence of any visible guts is. We have traded the honest box for the smooth one.

That reversal is the small instance of a large law, and the law is the subject of this essay. The intuition that drives it is one almost everyone now shares in some inchoate form: a wish that the systems running our lives were *more* transparent, so that the power inside them could be seen and checked — paired with a dread that we ourselves are becoming *too* transparent, read and modelled and predicted with a fidelity we never consented to. The two feelings seem opposed. They are in fact the same wish travelling in opposite directions, and the direction is the whole of the matter. Transparency is never a property of a thing. It is the direction of a relation; it always runs *from* someone *to* someone. And the regime we live inside has quietly reversed the vector that the iMac's clear shell once seemed to promise — rendering us glass to the machine while the machine becomes a glass we cannot see through. That much is a politics. The harder half of the argument is older and stranger: where the wish for transparency does land on its proper object — the self, the other person — it does not reveal that object. It annihilates it. The thing seen through ceases to be there to be seen.

Hence the title. Byung-Chul Han, whose *The Transparency Society* is the spine of what follows, diagnoses our moment precisely as the abolition of the dialectic: "The society of positivity," he writes, "has taken leave of both dialectics and hermeneutics. The dialectic is based on negativity."[^7] To call transparency *dialectical* is therefore a provocation against the dogma Han anatomises — the dogma that more light is simply more good. It is to insist that opacity is not the enemy of transparency but its necessary other half; that the veil, the seam, the secret, the gap, the distance between two people are not failures of illumination but the conditions under which meaning, trust, eros, and selfhood are possible at all. Han borrows the figure from Hegel: Spirit has power, he quotes, "only by looking the negative in the face and tarrying with it," a lingering that yields "the 'magical power that converts it into being.'"[^8] The transparency regime cannot tarry. It races in the positive. This essay is an attempt to slow down in front of the negative long enough to see what the racing costs.

## The vanishing seam

Begin where the iMac began, with the surface, because the surface is already an argument. Han gives the surface a name — *smoothness* — and treats it not as a finish but as an ontology. Things become transparent, he writes in his first chapter, "when they are smoothed out and leveled, when they do not resist being integrated into smooth streams of capital, communication, and information."[^9] Smoothness is what is left when negativity has been planed away: no friction, no resistance, no interruption, no seam. The sealed slab is the perfected smooth object, and its smoothness is not innocent. In *Psychopolitics* Han makes the political consequence explicit, describing how "the neoliberal regime's technology of power takes on subtle, supple and smart forms; thereby, it escapes all visibility."[^10] This is the hinge the iMac story turns on. The translucent machine made its mechanism visible as a boast; the smooth machine makes its mechanism vanish — and a power that has gone smooth has, in the same gesture, gone invisible. You cannot see the seam because there is no longer a seam to see.

The disappearance is not only physical. It runs all the way down into the logic of the thing. The intelligence now folded inside the smooth slab is, by the admission of the people who build it, a black box. Meghan O'Gieblyn states the technical fact plainly: "Many forms of machine learning are considered 'black box' technologies. They are composed of many hidden layers of neural networks, and there's no way to ascertain what kind of model they are building from their experience."[^11] This is not a layperson's confusion but the consensus of the field. *MIT Technology Review* puts it without hedging — "No one really knows how the most advanced algorithms do what they do" — and *Nature*, surveying the same problem, concludes that even the engineers who train these networks "are no closer to understanding them than we are a human brain."[^12] O'Gieblyn draws the vertiginous corollary: these systems have "fulfilled Anderson's call for objective knowledge at the price of human comprehension," so that "to obtain the superior knowledge the machines possess, we must give up our desire to know 'why' and accept their outputs as pure revelation."[^13] The smoothing of the surface and the opacity of the depths are the same event. As the device shed its visible screws it also shed its legible reasons, until the most powerful object in the room became one that cannot, in principle, explain itself.

Here is the first form of the wish, and its first defeat. The wish is for the machine to be transparent so that the power operating through it would become visible — so that surveillance and control would no longer be hidden in plain sight. Han would call this the wish to dismantle the *arcanum*, the secret zone in which power has always preferred to work. But he warns, in the final chapter of *The Transparency Society*, that the warning runs the other way: "Transparency and power do not get along well. Power likes to cloak itself in secrecy."[^14] The smoothest, most pervasive power is precisely the least visible, and O'Gieblyn supplies the proof that the opacity is not even, in the end, technical. "The debate about technical explanations and their supposed impossibility," she writes, "is a sleight of hand meant to distract from the real obstacles to transparency, which are legal and economic." The deepest black boxes are sealed not by mathematics but by proprietary law, so that, in her perfect formulation of the closed loop, "not only are we not permitted to know the information these systems have about us; we are not permitted to know why we're not permitted to know."[^15] The clear shell promised an honest machine. The smooth shell delivers a machine whose dishonesty is structural, recursive, and protected. To wish it transparent is reasonable. It is also, on present evidence, to wish against the grain of everything the machine is built to be.

## The one-way mirror

If the machine has gone opaque, we have gone the opposite way, and the simultaneity is not a coincidence but a design. This is the second form of the wish, suffered rather than spoken: the dread of being made transparent. Shoshana Zuboff has given the architecture its exact name. Google's foundational invention, she writes, was the capacity to infer the inner life of individuals "with an automated architecture that operates as a one-way mirror irrespective of a person's awareness, knowledge, and consent, thus enabling privileged secret access to behavioral data." And she insists the mirror is not a metaphor but a structure: "A one-way mirror embodies the specific social relations of surveillance based on asymmetries of knowledge and power."[^16] That phrase — *asymmetries of knowledge and power* — is the thesis of this whole essay restated in another vocabulary. Transparency is a vector. The one-way mirror is what a vector looks like when it has been built into the world. On one side, total visibility; on the other, total opacity; and the glass between is engineered so that the light only ever travels one way.

The asymmetry is, for Zuboff, the entire point and not an abuse of the system. "They know a great deal about us," she writes of the surveillance capitalists, "but our access to their knowledge is sparse: hidden in the shadow text and read only by the new priests, their bosses, and their machines." The behavioural record that is harvested from us becomes a "second text" that is "about us, but it is not for us. Instead, it is created, maintained, and exploited outside our awareness for others' benefit."[^17] We are rendered legible in order to be acted upon; the legibility runs strictly outward. And lest the relation sound merely informational, Zuboff strips the consoling cliché away: "Forget the cliché that if it's free, 'You are the product.' You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass. The 'product' derives from the surplus that is ripped from your life."[^18] The smooth conveniences through which the extraction happens are themselves the trap — even the word *smart*, she notes, "is a euphemism for rendition: intelligence that is designed to render some tiny corner of lived experience as behavioral data."[^19] The smart home, the smart watch, the smart speaker: each is a panel of the one-way mirror, sold to us as a window and operating as a lens. We furnish our own surveillance and, in Zuboff's mordant accounting, "we now pay for our own domination."[^20]

It is worth pausing on the naïve version of the wish, because Han anticipated and refuted it forty years ago, and his refutation is the dialectical heart of this movement. The obvious response to the one-way mirror is to demand a two-way one: if they can watch us, let us watch them; democratise the surveillance until the asymmetry cancels out. Han quotes the futurist David Brin proposing exactly this — a "transparent society" of universal mutual scrutiny, announced in the form of a question: "Can we stand living exposed to scrutiny, our secrets laid open, if in return we get flashlights of our own that we can shine on anyone?" Han's verdict is swift and total. Brin's dream of complete mutual illumination, he writes, "degrades 'transparent society' into an inhuman society of control: everyone controls everyone."[^21] The flaw in the symmetric wish is that it accepts the premise — that the cure for being seen is to see — when the premise is the disease. A world of perfected mutual transparency is not a world freed from the panopticon; it is the panopticon completed, with every inmate promoted to guard. The vector cannot be neutralised by doubling it. It has to be refused at specific points and for specific things, which is a harder and less symmetrical demand than "transparency for all."

The data tradition pushes the asymmetry past the political and into the metaphysical. Yuval Harari names the threshold at which the one-way mirror stops being a matter of advertising and becomes a matter of authority: "Liberalism will collapse on the day the system knows me better than I know myself."[^22] He thinks the day is near, and his reasoning is the same rendition Zuboff describes, only carried to its end: "an external algorithm could theoretically know me much better than I can ever know myself. An algorithm that monitors each of the systems that comprise my body and my brain could know exactly who I am, how I feel and what I want."[^23] He has the empirical down payment ready — a study finding that with three hundred of your Facebook likes, "the Facebook algorithm can predict your opinions and desires better than your husband or wife."[^24] And he sees, more clearly than the privacy advocates, where the real danger lies. "The individual will not be crushed by Big Brother," he writes; "it will disintegrate from within."[^25] This is the point at which the two directions of the wish meet. The machine has become so opaque that we cannot see into it; we have become so transparent that it can see all the way into us, past the place where we can see ourselves. The mirror is not only one-way. It is one-way *and* it sees deeper into me than I do. That is the precise shape of the modern asymmetry, and it sets up the two interior movements that are the real heart of this essay — because the next question is not what the machine knows about me, but what happens to *me* when I begin to want that kind of knowledge for myself.

## Confession is the product

Before turning inward, one more outward turn, because the economy does not merely take our transparency — it teaches us to volunteer it, and to experience the volunteering as freedom. Han's sharpest and most uncomfortable claim is that the digital panopticon needs no guards, because the inmates illuminate themselves. "The inhabitants of the digital panopticon," he writes in the preface, "engage in lively communication and bare themselves of their own free will. In this way, they actively collaborate in the digital panopticon." The system "is only possible thanks to voluntary self-illumination and self exposure," and it "achieves perfection when its inhabitants do not communicate because of external constraint but out of inner need — that is, when the fear of giving up a private and intimate sphere yields to the need to put oneself on display shamelessly."[^26] Coercion has been replaced by appetite. We are not dragged into visibility; we run toward it, and the running feels like self-expression.

Capitalism organises this appetite and meters it. Han's term is *exhibition value*: the worth a thing acquires not from use and not from labour but purely from being seen. In the society of exhibition, he writes, "every subject is also its own advertising object," and the imperative is categorical: "Everything must become visible. The imperative of transparency suspects everything that does not submit to visibility. Therein lies its violence."[^27] The economy of attention then closes the trap by making exposure itself the productive act: "Exhibition is exploitation," Han writes; the imperative to display "destroys dwelling itself."[^28] One sees the mechanism with embarrassing clarity in the advice now given to anyone with something to sell. The marketing consensus is that a brand should post "behind-the-scenes content" to "humanize" itself, a tactic that, in the trade's own revealing language, "provides transparency into business practices."[^29] The same counsel descends to the individual maker: the artist is told no longer to show only finished work but to film the studio, the work in progress, the time-lapse of the hand — to "build a sense of intimacy" with an audience by exhibiting the process.[^30] The making, once the private antecedent of the work, becomes a second product; the self behind the work becomes a third. And the scholarship of the creator economy confirms that the "authenticity" thereby manufactured is exactly that — manufactured. As Emily Hund argues in her study of the influencer industry, authenticity online "is not just a social construction but an industrial one," produced and monetised on a schedule, so that "the people we think of as 'real' are merely those who have learned to exploit the industry's ever-shifting constructions of authenticity."[^31] The human element is a line item. The behind-the-scenes is the scene.

This is also where the older confessional impulse gets recruited and sold. Han traces it to Rousseau, whose *Confessions* inaugurate what Han calls truth-as-avowal: Rousseau wants his heart to be, in his own phrase, "transparent comme le cristal," and demands that his fellows "unveil" their hearts with the same crystalline sincerity. Han reads in this "dictatorship of the heart" the moment the morality of total transparency "necessarily switches to tyranny" — Rousseau's transparent man wants, like the Roman he admires, a house "built in such a way that whatever occurred within it could be seen."[^32] The glass house is the dream, and the data economy has built it, except that it has stripped the moral content away: "Digital transparency," Han writes, "is not cardiographic but pornographic. Moreover, it brings forth economic panoptica."[^33] What presents itself as the warm modern virtue of openness — share your feelings, show your process, be vulnerable, be real — is, in this reading, the affective engine of an extractive machine, the means by which we render ourselves and call the rendering liberation. And it points directly at the deeper question underneath all of this: not what we expose to the market, but what becomes of an inner life that has learned to treat exposure as the measure of the real.

## Dialing the levers

So turn inward, to the strangest precinct of the transparency regime — the wish to be transparent to oneself, and the new vocabulary in which that wish is now conducted. There is a way of talking about one's own mind that barely existed a generation ago and is now ambient: the language not of feelings but of *mechanisms*. People speak of regulating their nervous system, of a spiking cortisol, of a dopamine fast, of soothing the amygdala, of being dysregulated and needing to get back to baseline. Call it psychiatry-speak, to distinguish it from the more familiar therapy-speak; where therapy-speak is psychological and relational, psychiatry-speak is physiological and mechanical. It construes the self as an apparatus with levers, and proposes that the way to feel better is to reach in and adjust them. Much of its specific content is, by the verdict of the relevant clinicians, pseudoscientific: the "dopamine detox" misunderstands a neurotransmitter you can neither fast from nor deplete on demand;[^34] the vagus-nerve and "nervous-system regulation" wellness boom is, per a *Scientific American* survey, "largely unfounded";[^35] the viral panic over "cortisol face" medicalises ordinary stress into a syndrome.[^36] But the falseness of the particular claims is not the interesting thing about them. The interesting thing is the form of self-understanding they express, which would be just as significant if the neuroscience were exact — because much of it, at the level of mechanism, *is* true. We do have an amygdala; cortisol is real; behaviour does run on chemistry. The sociologist Nikolas Rose named the underlying mutation two decades before it went viral: we in the West, he wrote, have "come to understand our minds and selves in terms of our brains and bodies," producing a "neurochemical self" that can be inspected and "reconfigured."[^37] Psychiatry-speak is the folk dialect of the neurochemical self. It is the self made transparent to itself as a machine.

Why should this be a loss, if the mechanisms are real? O'Gieblyn supplies the missing premise: that the machine is a *metaphor*, and that we are at the stage of forgetting it is one. "To discover truth," she writes, "it is necessary to work within the metaphors of our own time, which are for the most part technological." Every age has read the mind through its most advanced device: "To be human was to be a mill, a clock, an organ, a telegraph machine," and now a computer, a network, an algorithm.[^38] The danger is specific and she states it exactly: "A metaphor can also die when it becomes so common that we forget it is a metaphor. It no longer functions as a figure of speech; its meaning is taken to be literal."[^39] This is the precise condition of psychiatry-speak. "The brain is the hardware, the mind the software" is a simile that has died into a creed — and, as O'Gieblyn notes, a confused one, since "the popular notion that the mind is software running on the brain's hardware is itself a form of dualism," smuggling back the very soul it claims to have abolished.[^40] At its limit the metaphor consumes its maker: the eliminativists conclude that since a computer "can operate convincingly without any internal life, so can we," and that interior experience is simply an illusion to be deleted.[^41] To narrate yourself as a mechanism is to enlist, often unknowingly, in a metaphysics that ends by denying you have an inside at all. McLuhan saw the mechanism beneath the mechanism. Quoting the Psalmist on idols — "they that make them shall be like unto them" — he observed that "the beholding of idols, or the use of technology, conforms men to them."[^42] We are becoming transparent to ourselves in the idiom of our machines because we have been conformed to our machines. The metaphor is not describing the self; it is remaking it.

And here the dialectic delivers its sharpest irony, because the science the levers-talk invokes does not, on close reading, support the levers-talk at all. Cybernetics — the science of self-regulating systems, the original source of the feedback loop the wellness industry has half-digested — teaches the opposite of manual control. N. Katherine Hayles states the cybernetic verdict on the sovereign self bluntly: "In the posthuman view, by contrast, conscious agency has never been 'in control.' In fact, the very illusion of control bespeaks a fundamental ignorance about the nature of the emergent processes through which consciousness, the organism, and the environment are constituted. Mastery through the exercise of autonomous will is merely the story consciousness tells itself."[^43] A homeostatic system maintains itself precisely *without* a central operator dialing its levers; the regulation is distributed, automatic, and — this is the point — opaque to the conscious mind, which is a late and partial guest in its own house. The psychiatry-speak project of consciously reaching in to adjust one's cortisol or one's dopamine is, by the lights of the very systems-science it borrows from, a category error: it installs a controller where cybernetics found none, and asks consciousness to operate levers that have always worked best precisely because consciousness could not reach them. Hayles adds the deeper twist, drawn from the autopoietic theorists: the levers may not even be there in the way the metaphor implies. Information, coding, and feedback, she writes, are "inferences drawn by an observer rather than qualities intrinsic" to a living system; "there are no messages circulating in feedback loops" inside the organism — these "are abstractions invented by the observer to explain what is seen."[^44] The mechanism is the description, not the thing. To dial it is to mistake the map for the territory and then try to edit the territory by drawing on the map.

This is what the disquiet has been reaching for, and it can now be stated as a thesis: there is a difference between *performing the actions that let the levers dial themselves* — under the veil of opacity, as they always have — and *trying to reach in and dial them directly*. Han names the loss with his usual economy. "Only machines are transparent," he writes; the human soul "requires realms where it can be at home without the gaze of the Other," and "total illumination would scorch it and cause a particular kind of spiritual burnout."[^45] To make oneself fully transparent to oneself is not to know oneself better; it is to convert oneself into the one kind of entity that *can* be fully transparent — a machine. And the conversion costs exactly what Han elsewhere says the dismantling of negativity always costs: the capacity for the slow, opaque, generative work of the negative. "Without a gap in knowledge," he writes, "thinking degenerates into calculation."[^46] The self that can be wholly calculated has, in the calculating, ceased to be a self in the old sense and become a problem in optimisation. Self-surveillance is self-rendition; the levers-talk is the language in which we learn to extract our own behavioural surplus and hand it to ourselves as a dashboard. We become, internally, the one-way mirror — watching ourselves from the outside, as data, with what Zuboff calls *radical indifference*: "a form of observation without witness."[^47]

And yet — the honest difficulty, which the dialectic forbids us to resolve too cleanly — the self really *is* opaque to itself, and not only contingently. Han, following Freud, grants the point at its root: "human existence is not transparent, even to itself," because "a rift runs through the human psyche and prevents the ego from agreeing even with itself. This fundamental rift renders self-transparency impossible."[^48] The unconscious is the name for the fact that the self is already other to itself. So the wish to be transparent to oneself is not the wish to clean a dirty window; it is the wish to abolish one's own otherness — to close the rift that the levers-talk promises to close and that Harari's algorithm promises to close from the outside, knowing our feelings, as he says, better than we do. Whether one would want that, granted, is genuinely unclear, and the unclarity is not a failure of nerve. It is the correct response to a wish whose fulfilment would erase the wisher. Which is exactly the shape the wish takes when it turns, last, toward another person.

## The unbearable wish to see inside the other

The deepest form of the wish is the oldest, and it has nothing to do with technology: it is the desire to see inside another person, to make the other transparent, to finally *know*. It is arguably the desire at the root of intimacy itself, and it is almost impossible to resist once transparency becomes technically or discursively available. The contemporary instrument for it is therapy-speak. Where once one simply found a lover baffling, one now has a diagnostic apparatus: he has an avoidant attachment style; she is trauma-bonded; that was love-bombing; this is a boundary. As Katy Waldman observed in her account of the dialect's rise, we now reach for clinical language to "name" the people around us — "we joke about our coping mechanisms, codependent relationships, and avoidant attachment styles," and the vocabulary has migrated wholesale into dating and friendship, dispensing gaslighting and trauma bonding as everyday currency.[^49] Therapy-speak is a transparency technology for persons. It promises to render the opaque other legible — to convert the unbearable not-knowing of another mind into a chart of mechanisms and types. It is the one-way mirror brought home and pointed at the beloved.

Han's argument is that this wish, uniquely, destroys precisely what it wants. The thing the lover seeks in the other is not information; it is the other *as* other, and transparency abolishes the other as such. "Transparent communication," he writes, has "a smoothing and leveling effect. It leads to synchronization and uniformity. It eliminates Otherness."[^50] When the other has been made fully legible — sorted into attachment style and trauma response — the other has been assimilated to the same, folded into a known type, and what Han calls the "inferno of the same" is the condition in which there is no longer anyone genuinely other to encounter. The fertile opacity is constitutive, and Han is explicit that interpersonal transparency is not a goal we are failing to reach but a goal we should not hold: "interpersonal transparency proves impossible to achieve. It is also not worth trying to do so. The other's very lack of transparency is what keeps the relationship alive."[^51] He calls Simmel as his witness — "the mere fact of absolute knowledge, of full psychological exploration, sobers us even without prior intoxication, paralyzes the vitality of relations" — and Sennett, for whom mature autonomy "means accepting in the other what you do not understand, an opaque equality."[^52] To know the other completely is to no longer have an other to know. The wish, granted, dissolves its object.

This is why Han can say, scandalously, that "there is no erotics of transparency." Eros, for him, lives entirely in the negative — in the veil, the secret, the withheld. "The erotic presumes the negativity of the secret and hiddenness," he writes; "precisely where the secret vanishes in favor of total exhibition and bareness, pornography begins."[^53] He quotes Baudrillard on seduction as the play with "something in the other that remains forever secret for him, something that I can never know directly about him but which nevertheless exercises a fascination upon me from behind its veil of secrecy."[^54] The pornographic — his name for the totally exposed — has no behind, no veil, no gap, and so no fascination; it can be liked but not loved. The therapeutic legibility of the other is, in this register, a kind of pornography of the soul: it turns the inexhaustible mystery of another person into a fully exhibited diagram, smooth and complete and dead. And the same logic governs the wish to be transparent to oneself, because the unconscious means the self *is* an other — so the demand for self-transparency is the demand to drag one's own veiled interior into total exposure, to become pornographic to oneself. The two interior wishes, for self-knowledge and for knowledge of the other, are one wish, and they fail in one way: each would, if granted, abolish the otherness — in the self, in the beloved — that made the wish worth having.

Zuboff, arriving from the wholly different direction of political economy, lands on the identical ground, and the convergence is the strongest bridge this vault knows how to build between the surveillance of markets and the metaphysics of the heart. Answering the surveillant's taunt — *what have you got to hide?* — she replies with an ontology: "the crucial developmental challenges of the self-other balance cannot be negotiated adequately without the sanctity of 'disconnected' time and space for the ripening of inward awareness." And then, flatly: "If you've got nothing to hide, you are nothing."[^55] The inviolable interior, the sanctuary, the place into which neither the market nor the lover nor one's own surveilling gaze is permitted to see, is not a privacy to be traded for convenience. It is the precondition of there being a self at all. "Both alternatives" — total transparency, or the paranoid flight from it — "rob us of the life-sustaining inwardness, born in sanctuary, that finally distinguishes us from the machines," Zuboff writes; "this is the well from which we draw the capacities to promise and to love."[^56] The opacity Han defends as the condition of eros, Zuboff defends as the condition of personhood, and they are describing the same gap. To love and to promise — the two acts by which a self binds itself to another across time — require an inside that cannot be seen into, by anyone, including oneself. The wish for transparency, pursued to its end, is the wish to drain that well.

## Coda: tarrying with the veil

What follows for a studio, a curator, a research programme — and for the person holding the smooth black slab? Not, certainly, a simple defence of secrecy, which would only be Brin's symmetric error wearing the opposite mask. The dialectical position is harder and more exact: transparency is neither to be maximised nor refused but *directed*, and its direction is a political and ethical question that has to be settled separately for each thing made transparent. The vector is everything. Toward power — the smooth machine, the shadow text, the proprietary black box, the one-way mirror of behavioural surplus — the demand for transparency is urgent and just; that is where the light should be forced to travel, against the grain of everything those systems are built to be. Toward the self and the other — the rift, the sanctuary, the veil, the opacity that keeps a relationship alive — the demand for transparency is a slow violence, and the right response is to protect the negative: to defend the gap, the secret, the distance, the unread interior, as the conditions of eros, trust, thought, and personhood. The wish that runs in both directions at once has to be split in two and aimed: more light on power, more shadow for the soul. Most of our suffering under the transparency regime comes from having let the regime aim it for us, which is to say exactly backwards — glass for the citizen, mirror-glass for the powerful.

For the maker this is unusually generative, because it converts a mood of helplessness into a brief. The translucent iMac can be reclaimed, not as nostalgia but as a recovered politics of the visible interior: an art that puts the machine's guts back on display, that re-introduces the seam the smooth slab erased, that makes the one-way mirror visible *from the wrong side* — that stages, for a viewer, the asymmetry they live inside but cannot normally see. An exhibition could hang the whole vector on the wall: the clear case beside the sealed slab; the shadow text beside the public profile; the data double beside the body it was ripped from; the dashboard of one's own "regulated" nervous system beside the opaque, self-dialing system that actually keeps one alive. And it could do the harder, quieter work too — make pieces that *withhold*, that refuse to be fully legible, that restore to the viewer the experience of an interior they cannot see into, and so return, against the pornographic completeness of the feed, what Han calls the punctum and the veil: the productive negativity that "plays the music" total transparency has silenced. The curatorial payoff of the whole argument is permission to treat opacity as a medium rather than a fault — to make work whose meaning is its reserve. Hegel's instruction, by way of Han, turns out to be a studio instruction: to look the negative in the face and tarry with it. The veil is not what hides the thing. The veil is the thing. The art worth making now is the art that knows the difference between the light we should be turning on the machine and the dark we should be keeping for ourselves.

## A note on method

This essay's overall confidence is **medium**, in the specific sense the vault gives the word: every individual quotation is `Directly stated` by its author and was verified character-for-character against the raw chapters, but the connective tissue — the claim that the political, economic, mechanistic, and intimate forms of transparency are one phenomenon running in different directions — is the essay's own `Interpretive synthesis`, and the reader should hold it as an argument, not a reported fact.

Three of the load-bearing bridges are constructions the authors did not make and would not all endorse. (1) The central thesis — that *transparency is the direction of a relation, and the modern regime has reversed its vector* — is the essay's, built by aligning Han's "society of transparency" with Zuboff's "asymmetries of knowledge and power"; the two never cite each other, and the vault's [[Glass Life]] note is careful that Han's glassy human (subject-side, self-imposed) and Zuboff's glass life (structural, extractive) are convergent in image but divergent in their locus of power. (2) The reading of *psychiatry-speak as a category error by the lights of cybernetics* — that Hayles's "conscious agency has never been 'in control'" refutes the levers-talk it superficially resembles — is the essay's interpretation; Hayles is writing about the posthuman subject, not about wellness culture, and the application is mine. (3) The pairing of Han's defence of erotic opacity with Zuboff's defence of sanctuary as "the well from which we draw the capacities to promise and to love" is an interpretive convergence across philosophy and political economy, flagged as such in the prose.

Two citation-backed links are firmer and are marked `high` on their citation layer: McLuhan's "they that make them shall be like unto them" is his own scriptural gloss, and O'Gieblyn explicitly names Shoshana Zuboff and her "behavioral futures," reproducing the reassurance "that the mirror is two-sided" — so the O'Gieblyn–Zuboff bridge rests on a real reading, not a resemblance. Where Harari is quoted forecasting that an algorithm will "know me much better than I can ever know myself," the essay treats this as his `Directly stated` claim about a near future, not as accomplished fact; he is the corpus's most extrapolative voice and is used here as a diagnosis of a tendency.

The outside material — the iMac G3 and the translucent-electronics craze; the skeuomorphism-to-flat pivot and the sealing of devices; the machine-learning black box; "dopamine fasting," the vagus-nerve and cortisol discourses, and the "neurochemical self"; therapy-speak; and the creator-economy norm of behind-the-scenes "authenticity" — is **not** vault provenance and carries no status/confidence label. It is cited in the footnotes in ordinary bibliographic form (author, title, publication, date, URL), located through web research and quoted only where a page's text was actually retrieved and confirmed. Several originals (the *New Yorker*, *New York Times*, and *Guardian* pieces) were paywalled or blocked to direct fetch and were confirmed through institutional reprints (UCL, the Kinsey Institute) or faithful reproductions; these are cited to their originals and flagged here. One widely reproduced Jony Ive line about flat design "getting design out of the way" could not be verified to an exact fetchable source and is therefore **not** quoted; the verified WWDC 2013 "beauty in simplicity" line is used instead. The artist-specific "document your process" advice is commonplace creator-economy counsel; it is footnoted to a representative source and to the marketing-industry norm it follows, not asserted as a single authority's doctrine.

Scope: the vault's [[Material vs Immaterial]] axis now spans ten authors, and several — Lippard, Haraway, Burnham, Ascott, Kurzweil — bear directly on transparency and were deliberately set aside to keep the argument's six-author weave legible. Two attribution conveniences should be named: Han's quotations of Simmel, Sennett, Baudrillard, Benjamin, Freud, and Hegel are quoted *as Han deploys them* (the vault's interest is Han's use, not the source texts), and the speakers are named in the prose; and Zuboff's "one-way mirror" is her coinage for Google's architecture in particular, here generalised to name the asymmetry as such, which is an extension she would likely accept but did not write. All Han quotations were checked against the raw chapters of *The Transparency Society* and *Psychopolitics*; all others against their named raw chapters. The only normalisation applied throughout is the vault convention: curly quotation marks and apostrophes rendered straight, and OCR line-wraps closed to single spaces (e.g. Hayles's raw "Inthe" → "In the"; Han's "self exposure," hyphen dropped in the scan, preserved as printed). In a small number of places a quotation is grafted into the essay's own syntax, and there a sentence-initial capital is silently lowered to fit (e.g. Han's "Total illumination" → "total illumination"; Simmel's "The mere fact" → "the mere fact") and a leading "And" is dropped — standard editorial practice, noted here for completeness; every such span was confirmed otherwise verbatim against the raw, and an adversarial per-chapter audit on 2026-06-18 re-checked all seventy-nine quoted spans against their cited chapters, the pass that caught two unmarked elisions and a chapter misattribution now corrected.
