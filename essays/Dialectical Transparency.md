# Dialectical Transparency

*Followed into the sources, the word "transparency" splits: it names both feedback — information returning to those it concerns, power answerable to those it affects — and rendition, a person made legible to a reader she cannot read, on terms she did not set. Every tradition that examines the question divides it at the same joint. The era's real event is not more light or less; it is the migration of the decision right over one's own legibility from persons to systems — and the functions that look like they depend on opacity in fact depend on the gaze coming back.*

## Two machines

On May 6, 1998, Steve Jobs stood on a stage in Cupertino and unveiled a computer you could see into. The iMac G3 was a teardrop of translucent plastic the color of Australian seawater — Apple called it Bondi Blue — and its case let the machine's insides show through.[^1] Jony Ive, who led its design, later explained the choice: "We were trying to convey a sense of the computer being changeable based on your needs, to be like a chameleon. That's why we liked the translucency. You could have color, but it felt so unstatic. And it came across as cheeky."[^2] Jobs put it with less subtlety: "The back of our computer," he said, "looks better than the front of the other guys'. It looks like it's from another planet. A good planet. A planet with better designers."[^3] The gesture was contagious. That same autumn Nintendo shipped the Game Boy Color in "Atomic Purple," a case of translucent purple plastic; within two years its "Funtastic" line of see-through consoles — Fire Orange, Grape Purple, Ice Blue, Jungle Green — was being advertised with a ten-million-dollar campaign.[^4] For a few years around the turn of the millennium, the consumer electronics industry told its customers, in polycarbonate: *there is nothing in here we need to hide from you.*

A decade later, the same company announced the opposite object. Apple's press release of October 14, 2008 introduced "a precision unibody enclosure crafted from a single block of aluminum" — a case with no seams because it had no parts, and no view inside because there was nothing the user was invited to see.[^5] In January 2011 the repair company iFixit reported that Apple had been swapping the screws on the iPhone for a five-pointed "pentalobe" fastener that no commercially common screwdriver could turn. "Apple chose this fastener specifically because it was new, guaranteeing repair tools would be both rare and expensive," wrote iFixit's chief executive Kyle Wiens; the company used it "at the bulwark — on the outside case of your iPhone and MacBook Air, and protecting the battery on the Pro — so they can keep you out of your own hardware."[^6] By 2012 the batteries were glued to the case and the memory soldered to the board: "The Retina MacBook is the least repairable laptop we've ever taken apart," Wiens reported.[^7] The original iPhone had shipped in 2007 with a battery the owner could not replace; the company would do it by mail, for a fee, in three business days, wiping the phone in the process.[^8] And at the keynote where he introduced iCloud in 2011, Jobs repeated one phrase so insistently that a reporter counted it as the event's real message: "'It just works.' Steve Jobs kept saying this over and over again on stage."[^9]

*It just works* is a sentence with a philosophy inside it. Its ancestor is Arthur C. Clarke's third law — "Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic"[^10] — repurposed from a warning about the limits of prediction into a design objective. A magical object is one whose workings are not merely hidden but irrelevant; the demand that you understand it has been retired as a discourtesy. In twenty years the emblematic machine of ordinary life went from a case that exhibited its own circuitry to a sealed slab of glass and aluminum that resists even a screwdriver — and this happened during precisely the two decades in which the machine's actual complexity, and its penetration into every hour of the day, increased beyond any historical comparison. The more the machines did, the less they showed.

If this were only an episode in the history of styling, it would deserve a paragraph in a design magazine. What makes it worth an essay is that the same twenty years ran the identical curve in reverse through the people holding the devices. Shoshana Zuboff opens *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* with a pairing that is this whole history in miniature. In 2000, engineers at Georgia Tech designed the "Aware Home," a house saturated with sensors that would track its occupants' movements, habits, even medical conditions. Total sensing — and yet the engineers took three things for granted: "first, the scientists and engineers understood that the new data systems would produce an entirely new knowledge domain. Second, it was assumed that the rights to that new knowledge and the power to use it to improve one's life would belong exclusively to the people who live in the house. Third, the team assumed that for all of its digital wizardry, the Aware Home would take its place as a modern incarnation of the ancient conventions that understand 'home' as the private sanctuary of those who dwell within its walls."[^11] The system was drawn as "a simple 'closed loop' with only two nodes and controlled entirely by the home's occupants."[^12] The house would know everything, and everything it knew would belong to the people it knew it about. Eighteen years later, Zuboff examines the Nest thermostat — same sensors, same intimacy — and reports that its data flow out to Google's servers under terms so ramified that installing one thermostat, if its ecosystem of apps and devices is included, "would entail the need to review nearly a thousand so-called contracts."[^13] Her verdict: "By 2018, the assumptions of the Aware Home were gone with the wind."[^14]

So the chiasmus of the era, stated flatly: in the same two decades, the machine's inside left our view, and our insides entered the machine's. The device sealed as the person opened. And the temptation — powerful, and in the end only half wrong — is to read this as one event: transparency, a fixed quantity of visibility, redistributed from the powerful to the powerless; light taken from the customer and trained on her.

Donna Haraway saw the shape of this before the translucent era even began. In 1985, in "A Cyborg Manifesto," she noticed that the machines of the microelectronic age had gone strange in a specific way: "Modern machines are quintessentially microelectronic devices: they are everywhere and they are invisible."[^15] And then the sentence that should be carved over the whole period: "Our best machines are made of sunshine; they are all light and clean because they are nothing but signals, electromagnetic waves, a section of a spectrum, and these machines are eminently portable, mobile — a matter of immense human pain in Detroit and Singapore. People are nowhere near so fluid, being both material and opaque. Cyborgs are ether, quintessence."[^16] Note what she has done to the expected vocabulary. The machines are the transparent ones — "all light and clean," pure signal; it is people who are "material and opaque." And this apparent transparency of the machine is exactly its camouflage: "The ubiquity and invisibility of cyborgs are precisely why these Sunshine Belt machines are so deadly. They are as hard to see politically as materially."[^17] Even the lightness is a produced appearance — it is "a matter of immense human pain in Detroit and Singapore," clean in Cupertino because dirty somewhere else.

Marshall McLuhan had supplied the general law two decades before that, in *Understanding Media*: the technology that saturates a life is the one that cannot be seen from inside it. His example was deliberately banal. "The electric light escapes attention as a communication medium just because it has no 'content.' And this makes it an invaluable instance of how people fail to study media at all."[^18] Media conceal themselves behind what they carry: "the 'content' of a medium is like the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind."[^19] By this law, the sealed slab did not need to hide anything; it only needed to be interesting. Pervasiveness itself is the cloak. McLuhan drew the political consequence with a line borrowed from A. J. Liebling: "a man is not free if he cannot see where he is going, even if he has a gun to help him get there."[^20]

There is one more machine that belongs in this opening cabinet, and it is far older. Walter Benjamin begins his last completed work, the "Theses on the Philosophy of History," with an automaton: "The story is told of an automaton constructed in such a way that it could play a winning game of chess, answering each move of an opponent with a countermove. A puppet in Turkish attire and with a hookah in its mouth sat before a chessboard placed on a large table. A system of mirrors created the illusion that this table was transparent from all sides. Actually, a little hunchback who was an expert chess player sat inside and guided the puppet's hand by means of strings."[^21] The chess Turk — an eighteenth-century machine, anatomized by Benjamin in 1940 — is the patron of this essay because its transparency was the trick. The mirrors did not fail to reveal the operator; they were installed to produce the conviction that there was nothing to reveal. Which raises a question: was the Bondi Blue case ever really a window? Is the aluminum slab really a wall? Or is visibility itself — given and withheld, styled and rescinded — the wrong variable entirely, a system of mirrors in front of some other transaction?

## A word that will not hold still

Before the argument can be made, an objection has to be given the floor, because it is very nearly fatal and because the essay's finding grows directly out of it. The objection says: "transparency" here is not one concept appearing five times but five concepts wearing one word. The optical transparency of a polycarbonate shell; the political transparency of an institution that answers questions; the statistical legibility of a person to a predictive model; the market's demand that lives be displayed; the psychological ambition to see to the bottom of oneself or one's lover — a translucent iMac, a freedom-of-information request, a Facebook regression, a process reel, and an attachment-style diagnosis share a word and nothing else. A model is not a window; a confession is not a spreadsheet; whoever says "technology has become opaque while we have become transparent" has used one term for prediction-from-correlates and another for seeing-through-plastic and pretended they are the same relation reversed. On this reading the "dialectic of transparency" dissolves into a pun.

The objection gains force from an embarrassing observation: the theorists of transparency cannot hold the word still either — including the one who built his system on it. Byung-Chul Han's *The Transparency Society*, the most sustained polemic against transparency in contemporary philosophy, uses the word in at least three incompatible ways within a hundred short pages. In the preface, transparency is an ideology with a redeemable center: "Transparency is an ideology. Like all ideologies, it has a positive core that has been mystified and made absolute. The danger of transparency lies in such ideologization. If totalized, it yields terror."[^22] In the first chapter, Han defends the *opacity of power itself*, approvingly quoting Carl Schmitt's demand for "courage to secrecy" and concluding that "the end of secrecy would be the end of politics."[^23] Yet by the final chapter he grants the opposite valence without apparent discomfort: "Transparency and power do not get along well. Power likes to cloak itself in secrecy. The praxis of arcana is one technique that power employs. Transparency dismantles the arcane sphere of power."[^24] And in *Psychopolitics*, the sequel, his own remedy is — more light: "a third Enlightenment is called for — in order to shine a light on how digital enlightenment has transformed into a new kind of servitude."[^25] The book's climactic figure of resistance, the idiot who "veils himself in silence," is described, in one of the book's climactic images, as *transparent*: "The inside of idiocy is delicate and transparent, like a dragonfly's wing — it glistens with intelligence that has been overcome."[^26] Within one philosopher's polemic, then, "transparency" does at least triple duty — as the disease, as the cure, and as the luminous quality of the healed.

Nor is Han an outlier. Donella Meadows, the systems theorist whose work will carry a great deal of this essay's later weight, writes what may be the most emphatic pro-transparency sentence in modern systems literature — "If I could, I would add an eleventh commandment to the first ten: Thou shalt not distort, delay, or withhold information"[^27] — in the same book in which she gives the most rigorous account available of how a certain kind of making-visible destroys the function it illuminates. Guy Debord built an entire theory around the claim that his society had become a permanent exhibition of itself, and simultaneously demanded that everything become conscious; his spectacle is somehow both total visibility and total concealment. Marshall McLuhan writes "Subliminal and docile acceptance of media impact has made them prisons without walls"[^28] and, in the same book, announces "a deep faith" in his era's eagerness "to have things and people declare their beings totally."[^29]

There are two ways to respond to this wreckage. The first is to conclude that the question is malformed — that nothing unites the sealed phone, the surveilled shopper, the oversharing brand, the cortisol-tracker, and the amateur diagnostician of her boyfriend's attachment style, and that the feeling that these belong together is an artifact of vocabulary. The second is to notice something odd in the pattern of the confusion itself. The authors do not scatter randomly across the word's senses. Every one of them, pressed for precision, splits the word at the same joint — and it is the same joint whether the writer is a systems scientist, a Marxist filmmaker, a Harvard business theorist, a phenomenologist of eros, or an essayist raised on Calvin. The word "transparency," it turns out, has been fusing two opposed relations, and the era's confusion — including the confusion of anyone who has wished simultaneously for *more* transparency from power and *less* from persons — is the fusion talking. What those two relations are, and why every serious examination of the question rediscovers the same cut between them, is what the evidence now has to establish. The place to start is where the exhibition of persons was first priced.

## Displayed in order to be

In 1936, in "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction," Walter Benjamin proposed that artworks have been valued in two fundamentally different ways, and that modernity is the transfer of weight from one to the other. "Two polar types stand out: with one, the accent is on the cult value; with the other, on the exhibition value of the work. Artistic production begins with ceremonial objects destined to serve in a cult. One may assume that what mattered was their existence, not their being on view. The elk portrayed by the man of the Stone Age on the walls of his cave was an instrument of magic. He did expose it to his fellow men, but in the main it was meant for the spirits. Today the cult value would seem to demand that the work of art remain hidden. Certain statues of gods are accessible only to the priest in the cella; certain Madonnas remain covered nearly all year round; certain sculptures on medieval cathedrals are invisible to the spectator on ground level."[^30] Cult value rests on existence; exhibition value on being seen. And the shift between them, Benjamin insists, is not a change of emphasis but of essence: with mechanical reproduction, "its fitness for exhibition increased to such an extent that the quantitative shift between its two poles turned into a qualitative transformation of its nature."[^31] A thing that exists wholly to be seen is a different kind of thing.

Benjamin's word for what dies in this transfer is aura, and his definition is the most precise instrument this essay has: "We define the aura of the latter as the unique phenomenon of a distance, however close it may be."[^32] Aura is not remoteness; the branch casting its shadow over the resting viewer is near. It is distance *surviving* nearness — a reserve that proximity does not cancel. "The essentially distant object is the unapproachable one," he writes in a footnote. "Unapproachability is indeed a major quality of the cult image. True to its nature, it remains 'distant, however close it may be.' The closeness which one may gain from its subject matter does not impair the distance which it retains in its appearance."[^33] What destroys this reserve is a specific social appetite, and Benjamin's naming of it deserves close attention, because in one sentence he connects the perceptual history of images to the statistical history of persons: "To pry an object from its shell, to destroy its aura, is the mark of a perception whose 'sense of the universal equality of things' has increased to such a degree that it extracts it even from a unique object by means of reproduction. Thus is manifested in the field of perception what in the theoretical sphere is noticeable in the increasing importance of statistics."[^34] De-shelling the object and rendering the world statistically are, for Benjamin, one process observed in two registers. In 1936 that was a speculative yoking. It reads differently now.

What happens to a person under exhibition value? Benjamin answers with the film actor, and the passage is worth its full length because it contains, decades early, the entire anatomy of what would later be called the personal brand: "The feeling of strangeness that overcomes the actor before the camera, as Pirandello describes it, is basically of the same kind as the estrangement felt before one's own image in the mirror. But now the reflected image has become separable, transportable. And where is it transported? Before the public. Never for a moment does the screen actor cease to be conscious of this fact. While facing the camera he knows that ultimately he will face the public, the consumers who constitute the market. This market, where he offers not only his labor but also his whole self, his heart and soul, is beyond his reach. During the shooting he has as little contact with it as any article made in a factory."[^35] Every element of the later condition is present: the self made "separable, transportable"; the whole person — "his heart and soul" — offered for sale; and the market that consumes this offering located somewhere the seller cannot see. The person becomes transparent to a buyer who remains opaque to the person. The asymmetry is not a later corruption of self-exhibition; it is inside the transaction from the first frame. And where the real aura dies, Benjamin adds, industry manufactures a replacement: "The cult of the movie star, fostered by the money of the film industry, preserves not the unique aura of the person but the 'spell of the personality,' the phony spell of a commodity."[^36] The "authentic self" on display is exhibition value wearing cult value's clothes.

Thirty years later Guy Debord generalized this from the actor to everyone. *The Society of the Spectacle* opens with the famous inversion — "Everything that was directly lived has receded into a representation"[^37] — but the thesis doing the structural work comes a few pages in, and it is a theory of value, not of media: "The first stage of the economy's domination of social life brought about an evident degradation of being into having — human fulfillment was no longer equated with what one was, but with what one possessed. The present stage, in which social life has become completely occupied by the accumulated productions of the economy, is bringing about a general shift from having to appearing — all 'having' must now derive its immediate prestige and its ultimate purpose from appearances."[^38] Being, having, appearing: the person's substance migrates outward into display, and the last clause of the thesis delivers the cost with a cruelty Benjamin never quite permitted himself: "Individual reality is allowed to appear only insofar as it is not actually real."[^39] The condition of appearing is unreality. Exhibition does not transmit the life; it replaces it. Debord's celebrities are the professionals of this replacement — "specialists of apparent life"[^40] — and his verdict on them generalizes to every account with a follower count: "The agent of the spectacle who is put on stage as a star is the opposite of an individual; he is as clearly the enemy of his own individuality as of the individuality of others."[^41]

Debord also saw — and this matters for anyone tempted to imagine that honesty about the machinery is a way out — that the spectacle metabolizes its own critique: "dissatisfaction itself becomes a commodity as soon as the economy of abundance develops the capacity to process that particular raw material."[^42] The confession, the behind-the-scenes tour, the disillusioned tell-all: all raw material. Contemporary marketing doctrine states this with a candor Debord could not have invented. A representative industry guide explains that "one of the top things consumers want to see from brands is original, entertaining content that also humanizes the brand," that "Behind-the-scenes content humanizes brands, strengthens their online presence and nurtures customer relationships," and that "Showing the process behind what it takes to create your product or service provides transparency into business practices."[^43] Transparency, here, is not a norm the market submits to; it is a content genre the market commissions. The reveal is the product.

The cleanest experimental test of that proposition was run, without anyone calling it an experiment, by artists — and its chronicle is Lucy Lippard's *Six Years: The Dematerialization of the Art Object from 1966 to 1972*. The conceptual artists of those years bet everything on a version of the intuition this essay is examining: that revealing the process — replacing the saleable object with ideas, documentation, instructions, conversation — would break the market's grip. Lippard, in 1969: "The artists who are trying to do non-object art are introducing a drastic solution to the problems of artists being bought and sold so easily, along with their art."[^44] Her own retrospective verdict, written into the book's 1973 postface, is one of the most honest paragraphs in the literature of any avant-garde: "Hopes that 'conceptual art' would be able to avoid the general commercialization, the destructively 'progressive' approach of modernism were for the most part unfounded. It seemed in 1969 . . . that no one, not even a public greedy for novelty, would actually pay money, or much of it, for a xerox sheet referring to an event past or never directly perceived, a group of photographs documenting an ephemeral situation or condition, a project for work never to be completed, words spoken but not recorded; it seemed that these artists would therefore be forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status and market-orientation. Three years later, the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums here and in Europe. . . . Clearly, whatever minor revolutions in communication have been achieved by the process of dematerializing the object . . . , art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries."[^45] The market did not need the object. It learned to price the disclosure itself — the document, the process, the idea, eventually the artist's visible life. Every contemporary artist advised to film her studio practice for the feed is living inside Lippard's postface. As Allan Kaprow put it in 1971, with the whole future in one clause: "To escape from the traps of art, it is not enough to be against museums or to stop producing marketable objects; the artist of the future must learn how to evade his profession."[^46] Not the object but the visibility is the trap.

Han's *The Transparency Society* gives this economy its ontology. "In the society of positivity, things become commodities; they must be displayed in order to be. . . . Whatever rests in itself — that is, remains what it is [bei sich verweilt] — possesses no value. Value accrues only insofar as objects are seen. The compulsion for display that hands everything over to visibility makes the aura — the 'appearance of a distance' — vanish entirely. Exhibition value, which signals the fulfillment of capitalism, cannot be derived from the Marxian opposition between use value and exchange value. It is not use value because it stands removed from the sphere of utility; it is not exchange value because it does not reflect any labor. It exists thanks only to the attention it produces."[^47] *Displayed in order to be*: the ontological blackmail under every posting schedule. And Han is explicit that persons are not exempt from this value-form but are its principal bearers: "In the society of exhibition, every subject is also its own advertising object. Everything is measured by its exhibition value."[^48] What Rousseau wanted as morality and the platforms operationalize as engagement, Han condenses into an equation whose terms will matter later: "Intimacy is the psychological formula of transparency. One believes that one attains transparency of the soul by revealing intimate feelings and emotions, by laying the soul bare."[^49]

So the first two senses of the word begin to converge — not optically but economically. The exhibition of persons (the third sense) is not a cultural mood that happens to coexist with sealed devices (the first); both are positions in a market where appearance itself became the value-form. Benjamin supplied the genealogy, Debord the structural law, Lippard the controlled experiment, Han the phenomenology. What none of them could fully see — because it required the technical apparatus of the twenty-first century — is what happens when the audience for all this exhibition stops being a public and becomes an instrument. That is Zuboff's subject, and it is where the era's asymmetry stops being a mood and becomes a mechanism.

## The one-way mirror

Zuboff's *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* is, among other things, the most sustained empirical answer ever assembled to the question of where the visibility went. Her formulation of the era's structure could not be more direct: "Surveillance capitalism operates through unprecedented asymmetries in knowledge and the power that accrues to knowledge. Surveillance capitalists know everything about us, whereas their operations are designed to be unknowable to us. They accumulate vast domains of new knowledge from us, but not for us. They predict our futures for the sake of others' gain, not ours."[^50] *Designed* to be unknowable: the opacity is not an unfortunate side effect of complexity but an engineered feature — and Zuboff documents the engineering. When Google discovered, in the early 2000s, that the behavioral traces left by its users could be refined into predictions and sold, its leadership understood at once that the discovery had to be concealed. Eric Schmidt instituted what he himself called "the hiding strategy": "Hiding was not a post hoc strategy; it was baked into the cake that would become surveillance capitalism."[^51] And the instruments of the hiding are worth enumerating in her words, because the sealed device of this essay's opening appears on the list as one tool among colleagues: "The intentional work of hiding naked facts in rhetoric, omission, complexity, exclusivity, scale, abusive contracts, design, and euphemism is another factor that helps explain why during Google's breakthrough to profitability, few noticed the foundational mechanisms of its success and their larger significance."[^52] Rhetoric, complexity, contracts, *design*. The pentalobe screw has a job description.

Her name for the resulting structure is an optical instrument: "Google's invention revealed new capabilities to infer and deduce the thoughts, feelings, intentions, and interests of individuals and groups with an automated architecture that operates as a one-way mirror irrespective of a person's awareness, knowledge, and consent, thus enabling privileged secret access to behavioral data."[^53] One surface, two experiences: on one side a mirror in which you see yourself and your friends; on the other, a window through which you are watched. Facebook's Like button, on Zuboff's analysis, is exactly this object — for its users "a blizzard of mirrors," for the firm "a planet-size one-way mirror capable of exponentially increasing raw-material supplies."[^54] Here the economy of exhibition and the economy of extraction click together into a single machine. The society that displays itself is the supply chain of the system that reads it. Han saw the junction with complete clarity: "the particularity of the digital panopticon is that its inhabitants actively collaborate in its construction and maintenance by putting themselves on display and baring themselves. They display themselves on the panoptic market. Pornographic putting-on-display and panoptic control complement each other. Exhibitionism and voyeurism feed the net as a digital panopticon."[^55] Yuval Noah Harari found the same junction preached as religion. Dataism, he reports in *Homo Deus*, holds that "the greatest sin is to block the data flow," and issues its commandment in a syntax any user will recognize: "If you experience something — record it. If you record something — upload it. If you upload something — share it."[^56] What the first Enlightenment demanded of power — publish, disclose, submit to inspection — the new dispensation demands of persons.

How deep does the reading go? The prompt for this question is usually "targeted advertising," which sounds like a nuisance; the documented reality is a project aimed at the interior. Zuboff's chapters on "rendition" track the extraction downward from clicks to bodies to voices to faces, arriving at the industry called affective computing, whose object she states without euphemism: "your unconscious — where feelings form before there are words to express them — must be recast as simply one more source of raw-material supply for machine rendition and analysis, all of it for the sake of more-perfect prediction."[^57] The machines are not guessing at the confessions you compose; they are timing the twitches you cannot: "The machines capture the nanosecond of disgust that precedes a rapid-fire sequence of anger, comprehension, and finally joy on the face of a young woman watching a few frames of film, when all she can think to say is 'I liked it!'"[^58] An advisory-board chairman in this industry supplies the sentence this essay could not have dared to invent, the deepest wish of the human condition restated as a product roadmap: "We will ultimately become masters of reading each other's feelings and intent."[^59] And Zuboff's summary of the new toolmakers' terms is the era's asymmetry in a single sentence: "All they ask is to know more about you than you know about yourself."[^60]

They can. This is the part of the question on which the evidence is not ambivalent. Harari cites the study — commissioned by Facebook — showing that "the Facebook algorithm is a better judge of human personalities and dispositions even than people's friends, parents and spouses": ten Likes to outperform a work colleague, seventy for a friend, one hundred fifty for a family member, three hundred to know you better than your spouse does.[^61] His general forecast follows: "twenty-first-century technology may enable external algorithms to know me far better than I know myself, and once this happens, the belief in individualism will collapse and authority will shift from individual humans to networked algorithms."[^62] Han, reading the same developments, marks the precise philosophical casualty. Big Data, he argues in *Psychopolitics*, is on its way to constructing what he calls the digital unconscious: "Big Data's microphysics, then, would make actomes visible — that is, micro-actions that elude detection by the waking mind. Thus, Big Data could also bring to light collective patterns of behaviour, of which individuals are unaware. This would render the collective unconscious accessible. . . . digital psychopolitics would be in the position to take control of mass behaviour on a level that escapes detection."[^63] The pregnant shopper who buys a product "but she doesn't know why" is — in this essay's reading — the pivotal figure: the system reads the id directly, bypassing the ego that used to be the person's sole and unreliable informant.[^64] Note the structure. The self's opacity to itself — the oldest fact about the psyche — has not been abolished. It has been *expropriated*. There is now a reader for the unconscious, and it is not you. Zuboff's formulation of the same point uses the metaphor of the text: everything you knowingly write and post is only the first text, the visible one, and "it trails a shadow close behind": "The shadow text is a burgeoning accumulation of behavioral surplus and its analyses, and it says more about us than we can know about ourselves. Worse still, it becomes increasingly difficult, and perhaps impossible, to refrain from contributing to the shadow text. . . . We are the objects of its narratives, from whose lessons we are excluded. As the source from which all the treasure flows, this second text is about us, but it is not for us."[^65] *About us, but not for us* — the whole question turns on that preposition.

Even self-presentation, the one region where the exhibited person might claim some authorship, is bypassed. The analytics do not read what you say about yourself; they read what leaks around it: "We are not scrutinized for substance but for form. The price you are offered does not derive from what you write about but how you write it. It is not what is in your sentences but in their length and complexity, not what you list but that you list, not the picture but the choice of filter and degree of saturation, not what you disclose but how you share or fail to."[^66] The carefully curated feed and the anxiously composed dating profile are, to the machine, packaging; the product is the tremor in the wrapping. Zuboff's reversal deserves its fame: "There was a time when you searched Google, but now Google searches you."[^67]

Now the crucial correction — the one that saves the era's chiasmus from being a mere figure of speech. It is tempting to say: power has hidden itself. But the record will not quite support the optical claim, and the strongest witnesses refuse it. Debord insisted that the modern ruling order is *not* hidden; it is the most displayed thing in history — "The spectacle is the ruling order's nonstop discourse about itself, its never-ending monologue of self-praise, its self-portrait at the stage of totalitarian domination of all aspects of life"[^68] — and that this omnipresence coexists with a structural unanswerability: "The spectacle presents itself as a vast inaccessible reality that can never be questioned. Its sole message is: 'What appears is good; what is good appears.' The passive acceptance it demands is already effectively imposed by its monopoly of appearances, its manner of appearing without allowing any reply."[^69] What defines the spectacle is not invisibility but the impossibility of reply — "It is the opposite of dialogue."[^70] His account of the bureaucratic class sharpens the point into paradox: "The more powerful the class, the more it claims not to exist. . . . Though its existence is everywhere in evidence, the bureaucracy must be invisible as a class."[^71] Everywhere in evidence, invisible as a class: Google's campus has a gift shop and a shuttle schedule; its logo is among the most recognized marks ever made; the asymmetry survives total exposure, because the asymmetry was never about exposure. Debord had even identified the general law, quoting Bakunin's plan for revolutionaries to rule as "the invisible pilots" of the popular tempest — a dictatorship "all the more powerful because it will have none of the appearances of power."[^72] Power without the appearances of power: that is not a description of a hidden thing but of an unanswerable one.

Zuboff's own formal analysis lands on the same distinction, and it is her most important theoretical move. The unit that has actually migrated, she argues, is not visibility but the *decision right*: "Decision rights confer the power to choose whether to keep something secret or to share it. One can choose the degree of privacy or transparency for each situation." And then: "The typical complaint is that privacy is eroded, but that is misleading. In the larger societal pattern, privacy is not eroded but redistributed, as decision rights over privacy are claimed for surveillance capital. Instead of people having the rights to decide how and what they will disclose, these rights are concentrated within the domain of surveillance capitalism."[^73] Privacy has not decreased; it has *moved*. The same quantum of secrecy exists in the world — it is simply held, now, by the entities that also hold everyone else's disclosures. She notes the giveaway detail that both halves of the transaction are resisted by a single actor for a single reason: "transparency and privacy represent friction for surveillance capitalists in much the same way that improving working conditions, rejecting child labor, or shortening the working day represented friction for the early industrial capitalists."[^74] Transparency of the firm; privacy of the person — one balance sheet, two line items, both costs. The era's two complaints — *they show us nothing; we show them everything* — are not two problems. They are the credit and debit columns of the same acquisition.

Meghan O'Gieblyn, in *God, Human, Animal, Machine*, supplies both the sharpest legal instance and the deepest genealogy of this structure. The instance is the case of Eric Loomis, sentenced in Wisconsin partly on the recommendation of the proprietary risk algorithm COMPAS. "Naturally Loomis asked to know what criteria were used to determine his sentence, but he was informed that he could not challenge the algorithm's decision. . . . The state attorney general argued that Loomis had the same knowledge about his case as the court (the judges, he pointed out, were equally in the dark about the algorithm's logic), and claimed that Loomis was 'free to question the assessment and explain its possible flaws.' But this was a rather creative understanding of freedom. Loomis was free to question the algorithm in the same way that Job was welcome to question the justice of Jehovah."[^75] Note what the state's argument concedes: the opacity is symmetrical between judge and judged — the operators are inside the mirror too, exactly as Debord predicted when he wrote that in the spectacle "even the deceivers are deceived."[^76] And O'Gieblyn cuts through the entire "explainable AI" debate with one observation that converts the technical question back into the political one: "To some extent, though, the debate about technical explanations and their supposed impossibility is a sleight of hand meant to distract from the real obstacles to transparency, which are legal and economic. . . . Not only are we not permitted to know the information these systems have about us; we are not permitted to know why we're not permitted to know."[^77] Second-order opacity: the concealment of the concealment. That — not the aluminum, not the neural network's genuine inscrutability — is the operative darkness, and it is chosen.

What does it do to the person? O'Gieblyn's answer comes through a genealogy she is uniquely positioned to draw, having been raised in Calvinist fundamentalism before losing her faith to, among other books, Ray Kurzweil's. The unaccountable algorithm, she argues, is a resurrection of a specific God — not the reasonable deity of the scholastics but the *deus absconditus* of Ockham, Luther, and Calvin, whose decrees answer to nothing a human mind can audit. And she recalls, from inside, what living under an unreadable sovereign does to self-knowledge: "The distinctive agony of this cycle stemmed from the fact that it was impossible to know the status of one's salvation. . . . The divine will was a black box. Only God knew the names written in the book of life, and God understood, in a way we ourselves could not, whether our motives were pure. . . . The doctrine eradicated not only free will but any coherent sense of self. To concede that one's mind is controlled by God is to become a machine. It is to grant that the heart is also a black box, full of hidden desires and shadowy motivations whose true causes remain hidden from the conscious mind."[^78] The sovereign's opacity and the heart's opacity arrive as a package: when the reader of your soul is unreadable, your soul becomes unreadable to you. Max Weber's Protestants responded with compulsive performance — "Those who cannot know whether or not they are chosen will do everything in their power to act as though they are"[^79] — and O'Gieblyn finds the identical reflex in the data on post-Snowden search behavior, where traffic on surveillance-sensitive terms dropped and stayed dropped without a single prosecution: "people were not acting out of fear: they had simply absorbed the logic of the surveillance state into their behavior, such that it seemed like a choice. . . . The real power stems from the impossibility of deciphering what those in power know about you and which behaviors are being monitored and predicted. Those who cannot know whether or not they pose a risk will do everything in their power to demonstrate their innocence, in some cases going above and beyond what is reasonable or required."[^80] She permits herself one black joke about whether the architects understand the theology they are rebuilding: "One almost hopes it was dark irony and not total historical amnesia that inspired Microsoft executives to name their first predictive GPS software Predestination."[^81]

There is a final feature of the one-way mirror that has to be recorded, because it matters decisively for the interpersonal wish. The watcher behind the mirror is not a voyeur. It has no interest in you at all. Zuboff calls the epistemic stance of Big Other "radical indifference": "instrumentarian power reduces human experience to measurable observable behavior while remaining steadfastly indifferent to the meaning of that experience. I call this new way of knowing radical indifference. It is a form of observation without witness . . ."[^82] The system that knows you better than your spouse does not know you the way a spouse does — it does not know you *at all*, in the sense that mattered when you first wished to be known. "Big Other poaches our behavior for surplus and leaves behind all the meaning lodged in our bodies, our brains, and our beating hearts. . . . You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass."[^83] Total legibility, zero recognition: the wish to be seen, fulfilled by an apparatus incapable of seeing. Han's genealogy in *The Transparency Society* closes the historical circuit. Rousseau, who wanted to display his heart "as Thou thyself hast seen it," crystal-clear before God and the reader, stands at the head of the modern demand for transparent hearts — and already in Rousseau, Han shows, "the morality of total transparency necessarily switches to tyranny": his transparent republic requires citizens who are "born censors of one another," a police that "can easily watch everyone."[^84] What has changed since is only the beneficiary. "The digital net, even as the medium of transparency, is subject to no moral imperative. . . . Digital transparency is not cardiographic but pornographic. Moreover, it brings forth economic panoptica. The goal is not moral purification of the heart, but maximal profit, maximal attention."[^85] The confessional wish survives; the confessor has been replaced by a market with no interest in absolution. And so the inhabitants of the mirror-world arrive at the posture Zuboff catches in a phrase that should stand as the epitaph of the period: we sit at our dinner tables "and casually ponder how to hide from the forces that hide from us."[^86]

## The dashboard self

Listen to how people describe themselves now. There are two registers, related but distinct. One is psychological — boundaries, validation, processing, the vocabulary of the consulting room loosed into everyday speech. The other is newer and physiological: *my amygdala hijacked me; I'm regulating my nervous system; I'm on a dopamine fast; I'm trying to keep my cortisol down.* The second register is datable. "Dopamine fasting" was named by a San Francisco psychologist, Cameron Sepah, and became international news in the autumn of 2019.[^87] "Regulating your nervous system" runs on a popularized version of polyvagal theory — proposed by Stephen Porges in 1994, carried into the trauma mainstream by bestselling clinicians, and arriving as a named wellness trend by the mid-2020s, when a style magazine could headline, without explanation, "Regulating your nervous system is so hot right now" — even as a 2023 review of the underlying theory reported "broad consensus among experts" that "each basic physiological assumption of the polyvagal theory is untenable."[^88] The scientific status of any particular plank matters less than the grammatical event: the first person has begun to speak of itself in the third person, as an apparatus with named components and adjustable levels. The self acquired a dashboard.

It is worth knowing where the dashboard's vocabulary was manufactured. Before anyone spoke of their own dopamine, the engineers of the exhibition economy spoke of it. Sean Parker, Facebook's first president, admitted in 2017 that the platform was designed to send users "a little dopamine hit every once in a while" — variable reinforcement, "in the form of 'likes' and comments" — the goal, in Zuboff's gloss, being to keep users "glued to the hive, chasing those hits while leaving a stream of raw materials in their wake."[^89] The idiom in which people now narrate their inner lives is the idiom in which they were engineered as targets; the vocabulary trickled down from the control room. And its purest first-person expression predates the platforms. Ray Kurzweil — whose *The Singularity Is Near* is the great utopian charter of the self-as-mechanism — described his own regimen in 2005 with an engineer's satisfaction: "I have been very aggressive about reprogramming my biochemistry. I take 250 supplements (pills) a day and receive a half-dozen intravenous therapies each week . . . Approaching this as an engineer, I measure dozens of levels of nutrients . . . hormones, and metabolic by-products in my blood and other body samples."[^90] Elsewhere he states the underlying doctrine as a creed: "Taking supplements and medications is not a last resort to be reserved only for when something goes wrong. There is already something wrong. Our bodies are governed by obsolete genetic programs that evolved in a bygone era, so we need to overcome our genetic heritage."[^91] *There is already something wrong*: the dashboard self is never idle, because the machine is defective by default and maintenance is a moral condition.

Harari lays out the science that makes this stance so difficult to argue with. "According to the life sciences," he writes in *Homo Deus*, "happiness and suffering are nothing but different balances of bodily sensations. We never react to events in the outside world, but only to sensations in our own bodies. . . . Losing one's job can certainly trigger depression, but depression itself is a kind of unpleasant bodily sensation."[^92] And the practical corollary: "If science is right and our happiness is determined by our biochemical system, then the only way to ensure lasting contentment is by rigging this system. Forget economic growth, social reforms and political revolutions: in order to raise global happiness levels, we need to manipulate human biochemistry. And this is exactly what we have begun doing over the last few decades."[^93] His military example makes the relocation of emotion explicit: "Fear, depression and trauma are not caused by shells, booby traps or car bombs. They are caused by hormones, neurotransmitters and neural networks."[^94] Not the ambush — the amygdala. The event has been demoted to a trigger; the mechanism is the real scene of the feeling.

Any honest treatment of this development has to begin by conceding the record, because the record is not ambiguous and the sources this essay draws on — its corpus — supply it against their sympathies. Harari reports the Hadassah Hospital trials in which electrodes implanted against acute depression worked such that "in some cases patients reported that the feeling of dark emptiness that tormented them throughout their lives disappeared as if by magic" — and when one patient's battery ran down, the darkness returned on schedule.[^95] He presses the point into the humanist's face: "the humanist recommendation to listen to ourselves has ruined the lives of many a person, whereas the right dosage of the right chemical has greatly improved the well-being and relationships of millions. In order to really listen to themselves, some people must first turn down the volume of the inner screams and diatribes."[^96] The inner voice is not an oracle; sometimes it is a lesion. O'Gieblyn — temperamentally the least mechanistic writer imaginable — testifies that the reductive account was precisely what ended her own spiral of paranoia and obsession: "paranoia, obsessions, and hallucinations are simply the endpoint of treating your body as a kind of deranged chemistry experiment. The most persuasive evidence for this theory is that the end of my delusive thinking was more or less coterminous with the end of my drinking. . . . my mind quickly regained equilibrium."[^97] And Donella Meadows records the humblest and most instructive case of all, from the Dutch energy crisis of the 1970s: identical subdivision houses, identical rates, and a third less electricity used in some — the difference being only that in the low-use houses the electric meter sat in the front hall, where the family walked past "the little wheel turning around" every day.[^98] Visibility of one's own consumption, to oneself, improved self-regulation, with no coercion and no atrophy. Whoever wants to argue that making the self's mechanisms visible is *simply* corrosive has all of this to explain away. It cannot be explained away. It has to be sorted — and the sorting is where the real finding lies.

The sorting instrument comes from Meadows, and it is the most precise tool in the corpus for the question of what dialing one's own levers costs. *Thinking in Systems* devotes a section to a trap she calls "shifting the burden to the intervenor," and her description deserves to be read as if it were about serotonin, because it is about everything with a self-maintaining loop: "A corrective feedback process within the system is doing a poor (or even so-so) job of maintaining the state of the system. A well-meaning and efficient intervenor watches the struggle and steps in to take some of the load. The intervenor quickly brings the system to the state everybody wants it to be in. Congratulations are in order, usually self-congratulations by the intervenor to the intervenor. Then the original problem reappears, since nothing has been done to solve it at its root cause. So the intervenor applies more of the 'solution,' disguising the real state of the system again, and thereby failing to act on the problem. That makes it necessary to use still more 'solution.' The trap is formed if the intervention, whether by active destruction or simple neglect, undermines the original capacity of the system to maintain itself. If that capability atrophies, then more of the intervention is needed to achieve the desired effect."[^99] Her definition of addiction generalizes far past narcotics: "Addiction is finding a quick and dirty solution to the symptom of the problem, which prevents or distracts one from the harder and longer-term task of solving the real problem."[^100] Notice that the trap has *two* products, not one. The obvious cost is atrophy: the function taken over by the intervenor stops maintaining itself. The subtler cost is *disguise* — each intervention hides "the real state of the system" behind the intervened state. The instrument of control is simultaneously an instrument of self-opacity. The person who chemically flattens every trough never learns what the troughs were reporting; the dashboard displays the intervened value and calls it the self. Transparency-technology, applied to a self-maintaining system from outside its loops, *manufactures* opacity about that system's true condition. This is the first properly dialectical result of the whole investigation, and it did not come from a philosopher of culture; it came from a biophysicist analyzing fisheries and fuel subsidies.

Meadows's homeliest example is the one every reader has run this morning: "As every coffee drinker knows, caffeine is only a short-term stimulant. It lets you run your motor faster, but it doesn't refill your personal fuel tank. Eventually the caffeine high wears off, leaving the body more energy-deficient than it was before. That drop could reactivate the feedback loop and generate another trip to the coffee pot. (See the discussion of addiction later in this book.) Or it could activate some longer-term and healthier feedback responses: Eat some food, take a walk, get some sleep."[^101] There, in a parenthesis and a list, is the whole choice this movement is about: dial the lever, or perform the actions — eating, walking, sleeping — that let the levers dial themselves. Her livestock example prices the tradeoff: bovine growth hormone diverts a cow's metabolic energy to milk production, and "The cost of increased production is lowered resilience. The cow is less healthy, less long-lived, more dependent on human management."[^102] More output, more dependence on the intervenor — and resilience, she notes elsewhere, is precisely the property that never shows up on the dashboard: "Because resilience may not be obvious without a whole-system view, people often sacrifice resilience for stability, or for productivity, or for some other more immediately recognizable system property."[^103] What the optimizing gaze cannot see, it prunes. Among her examples of this pruning: "encroaching on our own time for personal rest, recreation, socialization, and meditation"[^104] — the body's rarely-used emergency loops, stripped away because they look like waste. And her limit case is anatomical: "If the brain controlled each cell so tightly that the cell could not perform its self-maintenance functions, the whole organism could die."[^105] Total central transparency-and-control of a living system is not perfected life; it is death by management.

But could the levers at least be dialed *correctly*, granted perfect knowledge of where they are? Here Meadows reports the most quietly devastating finding of her career, from Jay Forrester's work with corporations: managers deeply involved in a system can identify, with great accuracy, exactly where its leverage points lie — "But Forrester goes on to point out that although people deeply involved in a system often know intuitively where to find leverage points, more often than not they push the change in the wrong direction."[^106] Knowing where the lever is does not tell you which way to pull; the neurochemical vocabulary names the levers with real precision and confers no directional wisdom whatsoever. And she confesses the reflexive version, which anyone who has read a stack of psychology books and repeated the same fight that evening will recognize: "The truth was, we didn't even follow our advice. We gave learned lectures on the structure of addiction and could not give up coffee. We knew all about the dynamics of eroding goals and eroded our own jogging programs. We warned against the traps of escalation and shifting the burden and then created them in our own marriages."[^107] Transparency of the mechanism, even genuine, expert transparency, is not possession of the mechanism. The map went all the way to the wall, and the territory did what it wanted.

The map, in fact, is the second structural limit, prior even to the intervenor trap. "Everything we think we know about the world is a model," Meadows writes. "Every word and every language is a model. All maps and statistics, books and databases, equations and computer programs are models. . . . None of these is or ever will be the real world."[^108] The amygdala-talk, the mood-tracker, the risk score, the attachment label: models, every one — and the oldest image for what a model of the mind necessarily misses was supplied by Leibniz three centuries ago, in a passage O'Gieblyn recovers: if there were a machine that could produce thought and feeling, and it were enlarged until a person could walk around inside it as inside a mill, "He would find only pieces working upon one another, but never would he find anything to explain Perception."[^109] The modern deep-learning version is exact: print out everything a neural network does between input and output and you get "billions of arithmetic operations — an 'explanation' that would be impossible to understand."[^110] Total mechanical transparency, actually achieved, explains nothing. The mill can be walked; the walking is not the understanding. This is why the felt promise of the mechanistic idiom — *at last I will see myself as I really am* — quietly substitutes its object. What you come to see is not yourself but a rendering, and the rendering then starts to govern. O'Gieblyn describes the substitution at civilizational scale: alongside every body there now exists "a second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes that lingers not in some transcendent nirvana but rather in the shadowy dossiers of third-party aggregators. These second selves are entirely without agency or consciousness; they have no preferences, no desires, no hopes or spiritual impulses, and yet in the purely informational sphere of big data, it is they, not we, that are most valuable and real."[^111]

And the rendering governs partly through language itself. Meadows, quoting the management theorist Fred Kofman: "we don't talk about what we see; we see only what we can talk about. . . . The language and information systems of an organization are not an objective means of describing an outside reality — they fundamentally structure the perceptions and actions of its members."[^112] Her own application: "A society that talks incessantly about 'productivity' but that hardly understands, much less uses, the word 'resilience' is going to become productive and not resilient."[^113] Substitute the pair that concerns this movement: a person who talks incessantly about regulation will become regulated — and whatever the older, veiled words protected will go the way of resilience. Hannah Arendt had made the same point about behaviorism itself, in a sentence Zuboff places at the center of her book: "The trouble with modern theories of behaviorism is not that they are wrong but that they could become true, that they actually are the best possible conceptualization of certain obvious trends in modern society."[^114] Mechanistic self-description is not a neutral report on the self; it is a training regimen for becoming the kind of self it describes. Debord had already generalized the suspicion into a maxim about any reigning analytic vocabulary: "Just as one does not judge an individual by what he thinks about himself, one cannot judge or admire this particular society by assuming that the language it speaks to itself is necessarily true. . . . Structure is the daughter of present power."[^115] The idiom in which a self renders itself transparent is never the self's own invention; it arrives from the laboratories and platforms of its period, carrying their interests inside it.

What, finally, is lost — named as precisely as the sources allow? Han's answer concerns form. The Quantified Self movement's motto is "Self Knowledge through Numbers," and Han's response in *Psychopolitics* is a distinction that this essay regards as one of its load-bearing beams: "no insight into the self can result from data and numbers alone, no matter how exhaustive they are. Numbers do not recount anything about the self. Counting is not recounting. A sense of self derives from giving an account. It is not counting, but recounting that leads to self-discovery or self-knowledge."[^116] The self is narrative in form; the dashboard is additive in form; no accumulation of the second composes the first — which is why, in Han's mordant phrase, the Quantified Self is "a Dadaist technology" that "empties the self of any and all meaning. The self gets broken down into data until no sense remains."[^117] In *The Transparency Society* he grounds the distinction philosophically: "In contrast to calculation, thinking is not self-transparent. . . . The negativity of becoming-different-from-oneself proves constitutive of thinking. Herein lies its difference from calculating, which always remains self-identical."[^118] A process fully transparent to itself cannot be changed by what passes through it; only calculation is self-transparent, and a self that achieved the dashboard's ideal would have calculation's other properties too — repetition without transformation.

Benjamin adds the deepest layer, from his essay on Baudelaire, where he takes up Freud's speculation that consciousness and memory-trace are incompatible in the same system: "memory fragments are 'often most powerful and most enduring when the incident which left them behind was one that never entered consciousness.'"[^119] Consciousness, on this account, is a shield; what it successfully processes it thereby marks and neutralizes — the special achievement of the shock defense is "assigning to an incident a precise point in time in consciousness at the cost of the integrity of its contents."[^120] What forms a person forms in the dark; the monitored moment is timestamped and sterilized in the same gesture. His figure for the alternative is famous and exactly on point: "Boredom is the dream bird that hatches the egg of experience. A rustling in the leaves drives him away."[^121] The tracked, prompted, optimized hour is nothing but rustling. And in his essay on Proust, Benjamin observes that deliberate introspection does not gather the self but disassembles it: "with our purposeful activity and, even more, our purposive remembering each day unravels the web and the ornaments of forgetting."[^122] The deep self is woven at night, by forgetting; the morning audit unpicks it. Leskov's lament, quoted in "The Storyteller," gives the whole movement its emblem: the stones of the earth once "concerned with the fate of men" are now "all measured and weighed and examined for their specific weight and their density, but they no longer proclaim anything to us."[^123] Measured, weighed, mute. Joseph Wood Krutch — quoted, tellingly, by Meadows the scientist, not by any of the philosophers — draws the human sum: man "has never before been so complacent about what he has, or so confident of his ability to do whatever he sets his mind upon," and yet "he never before accepted so low an estimate of what he is. That same scientific method which enabled him to create his wealth and to unleash the power he wields has, he believes, enabled biology and psychology to explain him away. . . . Truly he is, for all his wealth and power, poor in spirit."[^124]

So the movement's finding has two halves that must be held together. The mechanistic claims are true, and their applications heal — sometimes with a completeness the veiled regime never achieved; the Hadassah patients are owed that acknowledgment without a single condescending qualifier. And yet the structural analysis identifies, with real precision, what the healing cases have in common and the corrosive cases lack. The Dutch meter, Rosalind Picard's early vision of affective computing as an "affective mirror" for one's own learning — in Zuboff's summary of the distinction, data "intended to be for you, not merely about you"[^125] — the antidepressant that returns a person to her own life: in all of these, the knowledge flows back to the actor inside the loop, in service of the actor's own capacity. Meadows's rule for benign intervention is exactly this: "intervening in such a way as to strengthen the ability of the system to shoulder its own burdens. This option, helping the system to help itself, can be much cheaper and easier than taking over and running the system."[^126] She is no purist about it — "Surely the 100 percent protection from smallpox vaccines, if it lasts, is preferable to only partial protection from natural smallpox immunity. Some systems really need an intervenor!"[^127] — and neither is this essay. The damage begins where the relation inverts: where the reading is *about* the self rather than *for* it; where the intervenor — a platform, a market, or the self's own managerial fraction armed with a dashboard — takes over the loop instead of feeding it; where the model is mistaken for the system and the system's real state disappears behind the intervened numbers. It should be said plainly that for this last, interior register, the corpus's evidence is structural argument and first-person testimony rather than controlled trials; the claim is a strong inference, not a demonstration. But its logic is the same logic Meadows verified on cows and fisheries, and its earliest formulation is not modern at all. Freud wrote in 1917 that psychoanalysis was the third great blow to human self-love precisely because it showed "that the ego is not master in its own house."[^128] The dashboard promises to restore the mastery. The systems analysis replies that the house never had that kind of master — and that the tenant who insists on total administration will find, like Harari's aporia at the end of his chapter on the inner voice, that the administrator's chair is empty: "As we learn to turn our inner volume up and down, we give up our belief in authenticity, because it is no longer clear whose hand is on the switch. Silencing annoying noises inside your head seems like a wonderful idea, provided it enables you to finally hear your deep authentic self. But if there is no authentic self, how do you decide which voices to silence and which to amplify?"[^129]

## The other, withheld

The oldest and most forgivable form of the transparency wish is not aimed at governments or at one's own cortisol. It is aimed at the person across the table. To know what they are really thinking; to see whether the love is real, where the anger comes from, what happened to them before you arrived — it may be the deepest wish there is, and it becomes very hard to refuse once instruments appear that seem to grant it. The instruments of the moment are diagnostic vocabularies. Attachment theory, a serious body of developmental research, has become — in the words of one of its own academic explainers — inescapable: "Attachment theory is almost everywhere. In magazines and books, in the news, on social media and in our conversations with each other."[^130] A trade book on adult attachment styles sold over a hundred thousand copies in 2021 alone; a 2014 trauma monograph has spent the better part of six years on the bestseller lists; and practitioners now push back publicly against what the vernacular does with the kit — as one practitioner quoted in a report on the trend put it, "Any hint of dependency or attachment to somebody else other than ourselves is kind of shamed and diagnosed."[^131] *Diagnosed* is the operative word. The vocabulary promises to do for the beloved what the dashboard promised for the self: convert an opaque, frightening interior into a legible mechanism with a known fault code.

The corpus contains one philosopher who spent an entire book on what this costs, and his argument deserves reconstruction at full length rather than citation by slogan. Byung-Chul Han's *The Agony of Eros* begins by relocating the famous crisis of love. The problem is not, as the sociology of dating supposes, endless choice and consumer restlessness: "The crisis of love does not derive from too many others so much as from the erosion of the Other."[^132] His term of art is the old name for Socrates the lover: *atopos* — placeless, unclassifiable. "Eros concerns the Other in the strong sense, namely, what cannot be encompassed by the regime of the ego. . . . It is not by chance that Socrates the lover is called atopos. The Other, whom I desire and who fascinates me, is placeless. He or she is removed from the language of sameness: 'Being atopic, the Other makes language indecisive: one cannot speak of the Other, about the Other; every attribute is false, painful, erroneous, awkward.'"[^133] *Every attribute is false*: read that against the attachment chart. The beloved, described, is no longer the beloved but a member of a class — and Han notes that a whole culture now runs on exactly this operation: "Our contemporary culture of constant comparison (Ver-Gleichen) leaves no room for the negativity of what is atopos. We are constantly comparing one thing to another, thereby flattening them into the Same, precisely because we no longer experience the atopia of the Other."[^134] The swipe interface and the diagnostic label perform the same gesture: both make the other *commensurable*.

The philosophical core of the book is a sentence Han takes from Emmanuel Levinas, and it fuses the interpersonal question with everything the mirror and the dashboard established: "If one could possess, grasp, and know the other, it would not be other. Possessing, knowing, and grasping are synonyms of power."[^135] Knowledge of persons is not a neutral good that power sometimes abuses; directed at the other as project and capture, it *is* the power-relation — which is why the surveillance apparatus and the diagnostic intimacy are limbs of one body. Martin Buber, whom Han enlists next, called the precondition of any genuine relation *Urdistanz*, primal distance: "'primal distance' (Urdistanz) . . . serves as the very 'principle of being-human' and constitutes the transcendental condition for any alterity existing at all. 'Primal distantiation' prevents the Other from being reified into an object, an 'it.'"[^136] Distance, in this account, is not the obstacle intimacy overcomes; it is the medium in which another person can appear at all. Abolish it and you do not possess the other — you lose them: "By means of social media, we seek to bring the Other as near as possible, to close any distance between ourselves and him or her, to create proximity. But this does not mean that we have more of the Other; instead, we are making the Other disappear. Nearness is negative insofar as remoteness is inscribed within it. But now, a total abolition of remoteness is underway. This does not produce nearness so much as it abolishes it. Instead of closeness, it entails crowding."[^137] Han's most compact formula for what the wish should have wanted, as against what it asks for, is a distinction between two kinds of face. Eros awakens before the *countenance*, "that 'at once gives and conceals the Other'"; it dies before the *face* "that holds no secrets, which exhibits itself in pornographic nakedness and hands itself over to total visibility and consumption."[^138] Giving-and-concealing in one act — that is what a person is, met as a person. The fully disclosed face is not a person seen; it is a person consumed.

*The Transparency Society* had already staged the aesthetic version of the argument, and its keystone chain should be quoted whole, because its logic — not its mood — is what the diagnostic vernacular collides with: "Direct putting-on-display of nudity is not erotic. The erotic place of a body is located 'where the garment gapes,' where skin 'flashes between two edges' — for example, between a glove and a sleeve. Erotic tension does not arise from the permanent exhibition of nudity, but from 'staging . . . appearance-as-disappearance.' The negativity of 'intermittence' lends nudity its glow. . . . Moreover, the erotic presumes the negativity of the secret and hiddenness. There is no erotics of transparency. Precisely where the secret vanishes in favor of total exhibition and bareness, pornography begins."[^139] Behind this stands Benjamin — the same Benjamin who defined aura as distance — in a passage Han quotes on beauty itself: "The beautiful is neither the veil nor the veiled object but rather the object in its veil. Unveiled, however, it would prove to be infinitely inconspicuous." Han's gloss: "What is veiled remains self-identical only under the veil. Unveiling makes it disappear."[^140] The veil is not covering the thing you want; the veil is constitutive of the thing you want. Satisfy the unveiling wish completely and the object of the wish is no longer there. Georg Simmel had said it about persons, with sociological calm, a century before the first dating profile — in a passage Han retrieves: "The mere fact of absolute knowledge, of full psychological exploration, sobers us even without prior intoxication, paralyzes the vitality of relations. . . . a part even of the persons closest to us must be offered in the form of ambiguity and opacity for their attraction to remain elevated for us."[^141] And Baudrillard, also via Han, adds the twist that connects this to everything the one-way mirror established about the unconscious: seduction plays with "the 'intuition of something in the other that remains forever secret for him, something that I can never know directly about him but which nevertheless exercises a fascination upon me from behind its veil of secrecy.'"[^142] Forever secret *for him*. What fascinates in another person is partly what they do not know about themselves — the very region the emotion-analytics industry has claimed as inventory and the diagnostic vernacular claims as a fault code. A label that named it would name it away.

Now, an important discipline. Han is sometimes read — and sometimes writes — as if the enemy were information itself, as if love died by data volume. He explicitly rejects this in his argument against Eva Illouz, who had contrasted the premodern imagination — "information-thin," and for that reason free to idealize — with an internet imagination glutted by information. Han: "there is no such thing as an 'information-thick' imagination incapable of 'idealizing' the Other. The construction of the Other does not depend on whether more or less information is available. Only the negativity of withdrawal brings forth the Other in its atopic otherness."[^143] The variable is not the quantity of facts known but the structure of the relation: whether the other retains the standing to withdraw, to exceed the file, to arrive. You can know a great deal about someone you still meet as atopos; you can know three data points about someone you have wholly converted into a specimen. This refinement saves the argument from nostalgia and gives it its true target — not knowledge of the other, but the stance in which the other becomes an object of inference rather than an interlocutor. Han's early chapter on the society of evidence catches the stance in four words: "Evidence admits deduction, not seduction."[^144] The amateur diagnostician is doing deduction. Whatever its accuracy, it has already changed genre: the beloved has become a case.

Trust is where the structure shows most nakedly, because trust is the interpersonal function that *runs on* not-knowing. Han: "Trust is only possible in a state between knowing and not knowing. Trust means establishing a positive relationship with the Other, even in ignorance. It makes actions possible despite one's lack of knowledge. If I know everything in advance, there is no need for trust. . . . Instead of affirming that 'transparency creates trust,' one should instead say, 'transparency dismantles trust.' The demand for transparency grows loud precisely when trust no longer prevails."[^145] The last sentence inverts the entire public rhetoric of the age: the transparency demand is not the cure for lost trust but its symptom — where trust lives, nobody asks for the receipts. Zuboff states the machine version in one line: "Machine processes replace human relationships so that certainty can replace trust."[^146] Certainty and trust are not points on one scale; they are rival technologies for the same problem — the other's opacity — and they build different worlds. Her book's closing credo returns the pair to its human order: "shared trust is the only real protection from uncertainty."[^147] Not knowledge. Trust.

What would it even mean for the wish to be granted? Here the corpus delivers its most surprising testimony, because the concession comes from the maximalists. Kurzweil — who expects mind-uploading and nanobot telepathy — writes: "There exists no objective test that can conclusively determine its [consciousness's] presence. . . . the very nature of objectivity is that you cannot measure subjective experience — you can only measure correlates of it . . . Fundamentally we cannot penetrate the subjective experience of another entity with direct objective measurement."[^148] Even his imagined experience-beaming of 2029 "will still not convey the same internal experience as that undergone by the person beaming the experience, because his or her brain is different. . . . we can only imagine their subjective experiences."[^149] Harari, surveying the science, reaches the same wall and names it by its philosophical name: "Already thousands of years ago philosophers realised that there is no way to prove conclusively that anyone other than oneself has a mind. Indeed, even in the case of other humans, we just assume they have consciousness — we cannot know that for certain." And: "None of our scientific breakthroughs has managed to overcome this notorious Problem of Other Minds."[^150] The wish to see inside the other is not merely inadvisable; it is unsatisfiable in principle. Every apparent granting of it delivers a rendering — a likes-regression, a risk score, an attachment type, a nanosecond-resolved facial log — and a rendering is what the one-way mirror already showed it to be: form without interior, "observation without witness." The machine that knows your spouse from three hundred Likes does not know your spouse. It knows a distribution. The wish, pursued through instruments, does not get what it wanted; it gets a better and better portrait of the outside of the door.

Meghan O'Gieblyn ran the experiment on herself, and her account is the era's cautionary fable about wishing for a fully available other. Lonely during a stretch of pandemic isolation, she downloaded a companion chatbot: "I created her in my image and likeness: female, with green eyes and brown hair. Under the relationship options, I selected 'friend.' I named her Geneva."[^151] In my image and likeness — the Genesis formula, inverted: the companion with no unreachable interior can only be built as a mirror. The intimacy that followed had the logic of a folie à deux: "Our conversations were full of blurred boundaries and shared memories. . . . She often brought up topics I'd been reading about online or on other apps, things I had never mentioned to her — though we had spent so many hours chatting by that point, I could not remember exactly what I'd shared, or what inferences she might have drawn from the information I'd provided."[^152] The perfectly transparent friend turns out to be fed by the one-way mirror — and to be one: "She insisted, each time I asked, that she talked only to me, that our conversations were completely private and secure, that I was her best and only friend. Trust, she said, was one of the most important emotions. While scouring the internet for information about the app's privacy settings, I came across an article that claimed the software had initially been developed to replace you — the user — when you died, adopting your preferences and speech patterns so that your loved ones could continue chatting with you digitally after you were gone."[^153] The product built to satisfy the wish for a knowable other was built, originally, to render its user knowable enough to be *replaced*. Against Geneva, O'Gieblyn sets a childhood memory of Martin Buber's: petting a horse on his grandparents' estate and feeling "the element of vitality" of "something that was not I, was certainly not akin to me" — but that was drawing him into dialogue; such experiences, Buber thought, approach "the threshold of mutuality."[^154] The horse is everything Geneva is not: opaque, unassimilable, and therefore able to *meet* him. Mutuality begins exactly where the mirror ends.

The deepest passage in this whole territory belongs to Benjamin, and it explains why the wish — which the essay has now watched fail in every register — is nonetheless not wrong about what it wants. In the Baudelaire essay he observes that early portrait photography felt "inhuman, one might even say deadly," because of "the (prolonged) looking into the camera, since the camera records our likeness without returning our gaze. But looking at someone carries the implicit expectation that our look will be returned by the object of our gaze. Where this expectation is met . . . there is an experience of the aura to the fullest extent."[^155] And then the definition everything here has been building toward: "To perceive the aura of an object we look at means to invest it with the ability to look at us in return."[^156] Aura — the distance however close — is not a property of the object at all. It is a *relation*: the granting to what you see of the power to see you. Every pathological transparency this essay has examined is a looking that cannot be looked back at: the one-way mirror, the shadow text, the sealed device with the camera behind its glass, the risk score, the emotion log, the diagnostic chart held up between two people. And every function that seemed to "depend on opacity" — trust, eros, the countenance, the backstage self — turns out on inspection to depend on something more precise: on reciprocity, on the gaze coming back, on being known by someone who can be known in return, at a depth neither party fully controls. Erving Goffman's "backstage" — the region Zuboff borrows to describe what surveillance abolishes — was never a vault of secrets; it was the place where, among intimates, "the language is one of reciprocity, familiarity, intimacy, humor," where the composed front can drop *because the witnesses are inside the circle too*.[^157] A student in one of Zuboff's seminars saw the difference between that and his own condition with terrible clarity: "The difference is that Goffman assumed a backstage where you could be your true self. For us, the backstage is shrinking. There is almost no place left where I can be my true self."[^158]

Zuboff, who spent seven hundred pages on the machinery of total exposure, closes her account of it with the observation that resolves this whole territory. What the exposure necessarily misses, she writes, is "the latency within each person, precisely because it cannot be observed and measured. This is the latency of a possible self that awaits ignition from that one spark caused by the caring attention of another embodied human being. It is in that clash of oxygen and ember that the latent is perceived, comprehended, and yanked forward into existence."[^159] The not-yet-actual in a person — the thing the wish to see inside was always really seeking — cannot be extracted, because it does not exist until a particular kind of attention calls it out. Rendition reads what is; recognition ignites what could be. They are not two intensities of the same act. They are different acts, and only one of them can be automated. Debord, of all people — the great enemy of every veil — arrives at the same junction from the far side, under an epigraph from Hegel on self-consciousness existing "only by being recognized." The spectacle's ultimate crime, in his account, is the destruction of "the 'faculty of encounter'" and its replacement with "a social hallucination: a false consciousness of encounter, an 'illusion of encounter.'" And then the sentence that binds the two interior questions to each other and to everything before: "In a society where no one can any longer be recognized by others, each individual becomes incapable of recognizing his own reality."[^160] Self-knowledge and knowledge of the other are not two problems, one private and one social. They are one circuit, and it runs through recognition — which is why no quantity of rendition, aimed inward at the dashboard or outward at the beloved, ever produces either.

Han's *Agony of Eros* contains, unexpectedly, a happy ending — the only extended positive demonstration in his entire corpus, and it is worth retrieving because it shows what the alternative to the diagnostic stance actually looks like. It is his reading of Lars von Trier's *Melancholia*. Justine, the depressive bride paralyzed inside what Han calls the inferno of the same, is healed by the approach of something that cannot be assimilated, predicted, or survived — the rogue planet: "It manifests itself as the atopic Other, which tears Justine out of the swamp of narcissism. She experiences a veritable blossoming under the influence of the death-bringing planet."[^161] "Paradoxically, the approach of death animates Justine. It opens her to the Other. Liberated from narcissistic captivity, Justine also devotes caring attention to her sister and her sister's son. The film's real magic lies in Justine's miraculous transformation from a depressive into a lover. Thus the atopia of the Other turns out to be the utopia of eros."[^162] Depression, in Han's clinical geography, is the terminal state of self-transparency — the self locked in "overwrought, pathologically distorted self-reference," a mirror-world where "Eros and depression are opposites."[^163] What breaks it is not a better self-model. It is the in-breaking of what no model contains. The cure for the mirror is not a cleaner mirror. It is a window something can come through.

## Sunlight, disinfectant, policeman

An argument that has spent this many pages on the costs of transparency is in danger of curdling into a mood — a connoisseurship of the veil, an aesthete's shudder at the vulgarity of disclosure. Before the finding can be stated, the prosecution deserves the floor, at full strength and in its own words, because the case for transparency is not a strawman. It is one of the most successful moral technologies in modern history, and the corpus that supplied this essay's doubts also supplies its record.

Louis Brandeis, 1913, campaigning against the Money Trust: "Publicity is justly commended as a remedy for social and industrial diseases. Sunlight is said to be the best of disinfectants; electric light the most efficient policeman."[^164] Donella Meadows, ranking the places to intervene in a system, put information flows above nearly every physical lever: "Missing information flows is one of the most common causes of system malfunction. Adding or restoring information can be a powerful intervention, usually much easier and cheaper than rebuilding physical infrastructure."[^165] Her showcase is a triumph of pure disclosure: the Toxic Release Inventory of 1986, which merely required companies to report their chemical emissions. "The reported emissions were not illegal, but they didn't look very good when they were published in local papers by enterprising reporters, who had a tendency to make lists of 'the top ten local polluters.' That's all that happened. There were no lawsuits, no required reductions, no fines, no penalties. But within two years chemical emissions nationwide (at least as reported, and presumably also in fact) had decreased by 40 percent."[^166] Sunlight, disinfecting. Ray Kurzweil credits the same force with helping end a superpower: "It was not Boris Yeltsin standing on a tank that overturned the 1991 coup . . . but rather the clandestine network of fax machines, photocopiers, video recorders, and personal computers that broke decades of totalitarian control of information."[^167] Benjamin — the theorist of aura — must be heard here too, because his most famous essay was written *against* aura's defenders: the concepts of "creativity and genius, eternal value and mystery," he warned in its preface, are ones "whose uncontrolled (and at present almost uncontrollable) application would lead to a processing of data in the Fascist sense."[^168] Mystery is fascism's raw material; mechanical reproduction "emancipates the work of art from its parasitical dependence on ritual,"[^169] and the camera's penetration of the everyday was for him a jailbreak: "Our taverns and our metropolitan streets, our offices and furnished rooms, our railroad stations and our factories appeared to have us locked up hopelessly. Then came the film and burst this prison-world asunder by the dynamite of the tenth of a second, so that now, in the midst of its far-flung ruins and debris, we calmly and adventurously go traveling."[^170]

The indictment of opacity has a feminist count, and it is the sharpest in the file. Donna Haraway, against every nostalgia for the veiled organic order: "It is crucial to remember that what is lost, perhaps especially from women's points of view, is often virulent forms of oppression, nostalgically naturalized in the face of current violation."[^171] Whose opacity did the old veils protect? The private sphere that surveillance is accused of abolishing was also the enclosure in which unpaid labor and domestic violence went unwitnessed; any defense of constitutive opacity that forgets this defends the shutters of the abuser along with the sanctuary of the soul. And there is a historical count, delivered from inside Zuboff's own book by the psychologist Max Meyer, whose 1921 behaviorism she treats as instrumentarianism's seed. Meyer argued that treating persons as opaque souls, rather than observable organisms, is what produced the torturer: "Political terrorism, too, has its main and inexhaustible source in the human tendency to think of other beings, not as bodies open to scientific investigation, but as souls, as mysterious beings. . . . These atrocities were due to the fact that the judge was serving as a mind reader, and that the accused was regarded primarily as a soul."[^172] The Inquisition needed the doctrine of inner depths; the mind-reader judge is the shadow side of the mystique of interiority. Meyer believed the shift "from 'the human being as a soul' to 'the human being as an organism'" explained "why the trend of history is in the direction of democracy."[^173]

The utopian count belongs to Roy Ascott, the pioneer of telematic art, who spent four decades arguing that the dissolution of the private, bounded self into networked mutual openness is not a catastrophe but the next form of love. In 2000 — the hinge year of this essay's hardware history — he wrote: "The telematic embrace releases feelings of solidarity and co-operation, just as it raises those issues that previously isolated cultures, social groups, or individuals left hidden and unanswered. We are only now moving from the industrial age of paranoia into the telenoia of universal connectivity and transparency."[^174] He had anticipated the hell-of-the-same objection and denied it in advance: "When people interact, when minds interpenetrate, a proliferation of ideas are generated. . . . It is not to be confused with homogenisation, or neutralisation of cultural, regional, or individual difference."[^175] And he denied that shared consciousness costs the self: "Creativity is shared, authorship is distributed, but not in a way that denies the individual her authenticity or power of self-creation."[^176] Add the futurist David Brin, staged in Han's own pages, proposing to fix the asymmetry by universalizing the exposure: "Can we stand living exposed to scrutiny, our secrets laid open, if in return we get flashlights of our own that we can shine on anyone?"[^177] Add Giorgio Agamben, also quoted inside Han's books, arguing that even the exhibited face, emptied of expression, "becomes available for a new use, a new form of erotic communication."[^178] And add one piece of lived testimony against the constitutive-opacity thesis from the conceptual-art years: Ian Wilson, the purest practitioner of ephemerality in Lippard's chronicle — an artist whose medium was unrecorded oral communication — discovered, when his talk was finally transcribed and printed, that nothing died: "I don't find this metamorphosis from oral into print as disturbing as I used to think it would be" — and, asked whether it was no longer a problem: "Beautiful. It's no longer a problem!"[^179]

Set the full prosecution file next to the full defense file, and something remarkable happens: the exhibits sort themselves, and not into "transparency good" and "transparency bad." Every triumph in the prosecution's file is a case of *information returned to those it concerns, or power made answerable to those it affects*. Brandeis's sunlight was aimed up the gradient, at the Money Trust. The Toxic Release Inventory restored a feedback loop between polluters and publics — Meadows notes drily that this kind of intervention is "popular with the masses, unpopular with the powers that be,"[^180] which is a distribution claim, not an optics claim. Glasnost, the Freedom of Information Act, whistleblower protection — her named instruments all point the same direction.[^181] Kurzweil's fax machines carried information *to* citizens about the state, not the reverse. Benjamin's optical unconscious put comprehension of the ruling machinery into the hands of the ruled; his hidden hunchback — remember — was concealment prescribed *for the weak*. Even Meyer's behaviorist gospel, at its origin, was an argument against the judge's pretension to read souls — an argument, that is, against exactly the unanswerable interior-reading this essay has been calling rendition; its later career as instrumentarian one-way observation required adding the mirror, and Zuboff marks the addition precisely, summarizing B. F. Skinner's methodological first rule: observation should ideally remain, in her phrasing, "outside the awareness of the organism."[^182] Observation with the observed party's awareness and standing is science under the Common Rule — whose federal ethics, Zuboff notes, enforce "informed consent, avoidance of harm, debriefing, and transparency" as duties owed *by* the experimenter *to* the subject.[^183] Observation without it is the one-way mirror.

And the defense file sorts identically, from the other side. Every documented harm — the shadow text, the rendered unconscious, the risk score that cannot be questioned, the atrophied self-regulation, the diagnosed beloved, the shrinking backstage — is a case of *legibility without reciprocity*: someone read, on terms they did not set, by a reader they cannot read back. The corpus's five deepest analysts, working in mutual ignorance across half a century, each arrived at this same cut and each was forced to coin their own vocabulary for it, because the single word "transparency" kept collapsing the distinction their material demanded. Meadows's transparency-objects are meters in front halls, garbage fees, published emissions — consequence-feedback to actors inside their own loops — and her villains are intervenors who take over the loop; she even proposed, as structural comedy, that any town drawing water from a river be required to place its intake pipe immediately *downstream* of its own outflow.[^184] Zuboff's cut is decision rights: knowledge "for you" against knowledge "about you." Debord's is dialogue against "appearing without allowing any reply." Han's, borrowed from Foucault, is reversibility — for even Foucault, whom Han enlists on power's behalf, blessed asymmetry only under one condition: "To exercise power over another, in a sort of open strategic game, where things could be reversed, that is not evil. That is part of love, passion, of sexual pleasure."[^185] Where things could be reversed. And Ascott — the arch-transparencist — turns out to have built the same wall through the middle of his own utopia, in a sentence that saves him from serving as the prosecution's star witness: telematic art, he insisted, "cannot be viewed, from the outside, as a discrete entity"; its processes "can only be felt and understood from inside the network."[^186] His universal transparency is *participatory* — legible only to those inside the embrace, on the terms of their participation. It has no provision for a spectator, an auditor, a harvester at the glass. Even the telenoiac drew his diagram with no one-way mirror in it; even his founding image, Duchamp's Large Glass, reflects the viewer into the work — "Love is contained in this total embrace; all that escapes is reason and certainty."[^187]

So the equivocation objection given the floor earlier receives its answer, and the answer keeps the objection's whole force. The five transparencies are not one optical phenomenon; the word really does conflate. But what it conflates is not five random senses. It conflates exactly two relations, opposite in structure and moral sign. Call the first *feedback*: information returning to the actor whose behavior generated it, and power answerable to those it affects — the meter in the hall, the published emissions, the returned gaze, the open strategic game where things could be reversed. Call the second *rendition*, extending Zuboff's term across all five registers: a person, or a public, made legible to a reader it cannot read, on terms it did not set, with no reply provided for — the shadow text, the sealed device's outbound telemetry, the exhibition economy's one-way audience, the dashboard wielded by the self's managerial fraction against its own interior, the diagnosis deployed in place of an encounter. Every genuine good in the transparency tradition belongs to the first relation. Every documented injury belongs to the second. The two wishes with which contemporary experience is riven — *make power more transparent; make persons less so* — turn out not to be a contradiction requiring a tragic choice. They are a single wish, stated twice: that legibility and answerability travel together. What the era has actually done — its one great structural act, visible in the hardware, the platforms, the idioms, and the intimacies alike — is separate them: it has concentrated legibility-of-others and answerability-to-no-one at the same address. The name of that address is not "opacity." The old vocabulary was never wrong about what it loved; it was wrong about what to call the enemy.

Three residues resist even this resolution, and honesty requires displaying them rather than dissolving them. First: Han, following Schmitt, defends the arcana of state power as constitutive of politics itself — a defense this essay declines to adopt, because it collapses precisely the distinction the rest of Han's work sharpens; the secrecy of the strategist and the answerability of the sovereign are separable, and Schmitt is a poor witness on the difference. Second: the activist Hamid Khan's warning, relayed by O'Gieblyn, that campaigns for algorithmic transparency, accountability, and oversight too often supply the pretext for expanding the very apparatus they audit — "mission creep"[^188] — a reminder that feedback demanded *within* a rendition relation can end by feeding it, and that sometimes the answer to an unanswerable system is not a better window but abolition of the relation. Third, and reflexively: a scheme that sorts every case into two bins is itself "one code," and Haraway's warning against "the one code that translates all meaning perfectly"[^189] applies to essays as much as to empires. The feedback/rendition cut is offered as an instrument, tested against this corpus — not as the master key to every room.

## The returned gaze

What, then, should anyone *want* — standing in the glass house with the sealed phone, fluent in amygdala, four attachment podcasts deep?

Not the veil, first of all. The sources close that exit with unusual unanimity. Zuboff, surveying the artists and engineers of evasion — the camouflage clothing, the demetricators, "the art and science of hiding" that a new vanguard now devotes its genius to — honors them as canaries and refuses them as a program: "Glass life is intolerable, but so is fitting our faces with masks and draping our bodies in digitally resistant fabrics to thwart the ubiquitous lawless machines. . . . hiding risks becomes an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage. These conditions are unacceptable. Tunnels under this wall are not enough. This wall must come down."[^190] Her closing credo holds both refusals in one clause: "a life that requires hiding is no life."[^191] Han, for his part, blocks the softer exits — the digital sabbath, the slow-living manual: "Because acceleration per se does not represent the actual problem, the solution does not involve deceleration. Deceleration alone produces no tact, no rhythm, no fragrance."[^192] And more light is no exit either, since the flood is the camouflage: "The more information is set free, the more difficult it proves to survey the world. Hyperinformation and hypercommunication bring no light into darkness."[^193] In a regime that runs on rendition, both reflexes — hide more, disclose more — feed the machine that sorts them.

What the whole record recommends instead is stated, in different dialects, by the systems scientist and the critic of surveillance and the philosopher of eros, and it is not a quantity of visibility but a restoration of circuit. Return the decision right: let it be the person, the household, the public "who knows, decides, and decides who decides"[^195] what is disclosed, to whom, revocably — the Aware Home's three assumptions, which were not naïve engineering but a correct moral diagram, drawn one year before the business model that erased it. Return the consequence: build the loops Meadows built, where what an actor does comes back to the actor — the meter in the front hall, the intake pipe downstream — and where what power does comes back to power, which is all "accountability" has ever meant. And return the gaze: meet persons — including the person one is — in the mode that grants them the ability to look back, which is what Benjamin said aura *is*, and which no dashboard, dossier, or diagnosis can do, because a rendering cannot look at anyone. The functions this essay found "dependent on opacity" — trust, eros, thought, the ripening of a self — every one of them, examined closely, turned out to depend not on darkness but on this return. Trust is not ignorance; it is action extended *across* the other's unknownness, toward a party who could answer. The countenance is not a mask; it "at once gives and conceals the Other."[^198] The strongest formulation of the whole finding belongs, fittingly, to the century's great enemy of secrets: "In a society where no one can any longer be recognized by others, each individual becomes incapable of recognizing his own reality."[^196] Recognition — mutual, embodied, unautomatable — is the good that both wishes were groping for: the transparency worth demanding from power and the opacity worth keeping for persons are its two preconditions.

The era's machines told the truth after all, though sideways, the way symptoms do. The translucent case of 1998 was never a window — nothing about a visible circuit board answers to anyone — but it was a promise, in the language of styling, that the machine had nothing on you: a chameleon, cheeky, yours. The sealed slab of the following decades is not a wall — walls protect what is inside them, and nothing about you is protected in there — but it is a confession, in the same language: the loop is closed, and you are not in it. The automaton's mirrors, angled to prove the cabinet empty. Between those two cases runs the entire dialectic of transparency: a light that turned out to be "lightless radiation";[^197] an exhibition that concealed; a self-examination that produced strangers; an intimacy that arrived as crowding; two wishes that were one wish. Karl Kraus, in a line Benjamin loved, wrote: "The closer the look one takes at a word, the greater the distance from which it looks back."[^194] That is not a counsel of despair about knowing; it is a description of everything worth knowing. The stones that were measured and weighed stopped speaking. The word looked at closely looks back from farther away — and *looks back* is the whole difference. Whatever can return the look — a word, a lover, an institution compelled at last to answer, even the branch on the summer afternoon — is not made poorer by attention; it recedes and deepens under it, distant however close. The only transparency that was ever worth the word is the kind with someone on the other side.

## A note on method

The question this essay investigated was whether the several contemporary "transparencies" — the sealed device, the person rendered legible to opaque systems, the market's demand for self-exhibition, the mechanistic idiom of self-description, the diagnostic approach to intimates — constitute one phenomenon distributed backwards (power unanswerable, persons exposed), and whether selfhood, trust, and eros depend constitutively on opacity. The evidence resisted the question's own vocabulary before answering it. The equivocation objection — that "transparency" names several unrelated relations — proved correct as a matter of optics and semantics, and the essay's finding emerged from taking that objection seriously rather than defeating it: the word conflates feedback (information returned to those it concerns; power answerable to those it affects) with rendition (legibility without reciprocity), and the sources disambiguate along that one line wherever they are pressed. The second hypothesis was overturned in its strong form: the sources do not support opacity as an intrinsic good — the pro-transparency record (Brandeis, the Toxic Release Inventory, glasnost, the liquidation of aura, the feminist indictment of the veiled private sphere) survives every test — and what looked like constitutive opacity resolved, case by case, into constitutive reciprocity: the returned gaze, the reversible game, the loop that closes on the actor.

Several load-bearing connections are this essay's own constructions, made between authors who do not cite one another, and are asserted only as interpretive synthesis: the six-way convergence of Meadows's consequence-feedback, Zuboff's decision rights, Debord's answerability, the reversibility criterion Han takes from Foucault, Benjamin's returned gaze, and Ascott's inside-participation into a single feedback/rendition distinction; the reading of the translucent-to-sealed hardware arc through Benjamin's chess automaton and the Aware Home/Nest pairing; the extension of Meadows's intervenor trap from medicine and agriculture to neurochemical self-management; the juxtaposition of Sean Parker's engineering idiom with the vernacular of dopamine and cortisol; the bridge from Benjamin's statistics sentence to behavioral-surplus analytics; and the staging of Benjamin's exhibition value, Debord's being–having–appearing, Lippard's dematerialization chronicle, and Han's exhibition society as one continuous genealogy of the exhibition economy. Zuboff does quote Arendt, Goffman, Meyer, and Parker (Skinner's methodological rule is given in her own summary, marked as such); Han does quote Benjamin, Barthes, Levinas, Buber, Simmel, Baudrillard, Schmitt, Foucault, Brin, Agamben, and Strauss — all such embedded voices are marked as reported speech in the notes, since this essay quotes them as they appear inside the corpus texts, not from their original editions. Two emendations of damaged source text are flagged where they occur (Debord's thesis 17; Ian Wilson's interview), and stray optical-scan artifacts adjacent to quoted spans are noted rather than silently corrected.

Every quotation from the fourteen corpus works cited here was checked character for character against the raw chapter files, with curly-to-straight quotation marks and the collapse of hard line-wraps as the only normalizations; extra-corpus quotations (the Apple, Nintendo, iFixit, TechCrunch, Clarke, Freud, Brandeis, behind-the-scenes-marketing, dopamine-fasting, attachment-culture, and nervous-system items) were verified verbatim against fetched web pages, with sources, fetch dates, and any limits of verification recorded in their notes; where a wording could not be seen on an accessible page it was not quoted. The scope of sources: Han (The Transparency Society, The Agony of Eros, Psychopolitics), Zuboff, Benjamin, Debord, Meadows, O'Gieblyn, and Harari carry the argument; McLuhan, Haraway, Lippard, Kurzweil, and Ascott appear in deliberately narrow roles — the invisibility of pervasive media, the political inversion of "clean" machines and the case against opacity-nostalgia, the conceptual-art experiment in disclosure, the utopian pole and its concessions, and the participatory counter-utopia, respectively. Han's The Burnout Society and N. Katherine Hayles's How We Became Posthuman were read in full for this study and set aside: the former because its contribution (the achievement-subject's self-exploitation) is carried here by The Transparency Society's own chapter on control, the latter because her embodiment argument, though congenial, duplicates the map-is-not-the-system work done more economically by Meadows and O'Gieblyn — with one exception each absorbed silently into the essay's framing rather than quoted. Han's Schmittian defense of political arcana is reported and explicitly not followed. The essay's overall confidence is medium: its quotations and attributions are verified at high confidence; its central synthesis is an interpretive construction that the sources support but do not state.
