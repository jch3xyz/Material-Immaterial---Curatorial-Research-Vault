# We Have Always Been Cyborgs

*The cyborg is usually pictured as the body's first step out of itself and into the machine. Tracked through cybernetics and the work of Marshall McLuhan, Donna Haraway, and N. Katherine Hayles, it teaches the opposite lesson — that there was never a pure body to leave, and never a way to subtract the one we have.*

Begin with the word, because it does something strange before it does anything else. *Cyborg* is a contraction of *cybernetic organism*, and Donna Haraway's definition of it, written in 1985, is a single sentence that refuses to settle: "A cyborg is a cybernetic organism, a hybrid of machine and organism, a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction."[^1] Read it slowly and you notice it points two directions at once. On one side the cyborg is a literal thing — a body fused with a device, the pacemaker patient, the wired pilot, the diabetic and her insulin pump. On the other it is "a creature of fiction" — a being out of films and novels, an image we tell stories with. Most words ask you to choose between the real and the imagined; this one insists on both, and N. Katherine Hayles, who borrows the figure from Haraway, draws the consequence flatly: because the cyborg "partakes of the power of the imagination as well as of the actuality of technology," she writes, "Cyborgs actually exist."[^2]

That doubleness is the first thing to understand about the cyborg, and it is also a warning about how easily the figure is misread. The popular cyborg is a fantasy of escape: flesh upgraded into metal, the soft and mortal body shedding its limits and stepping into something faster, cleaner, and finally immortal. The cyborg, on this picture, is the body's first move *out* of itself — the opening installment of a story that ends with consciousness uploaded and the meat left behind. The three writers this essay reads — Marshall McLuhan, who supplies the figure's prehistory; Haraway, who named and politicized it; and Hayles, who turned it into a theory of the posthuman — were each, in different keys, arguing against exactly that picture. Tracked through their work, and through the science of cybernetics that gives the cyborg its first three letters, the figure delivers a lesson almost opposite to its reputation. It does not show that the human can leave the body. It shows that there was never a sealed, purely biological human standing apart from its tools to begin with — and that the one thing the cyborg cannot do is subtract the body it is made of.

## Cybernetic organism: where the word comes from

To understand the cyborg you first have to understand the *cyb*. It comes from cybernetics, the postwar science that, more than any technology, made the human-machine hybrid thinkable in the first place. Hayles's *How We Became Posthuman* (1999) is the vault's history of that science, and she begins, as the word itself does, with a sailor. "Coined from the Greek word for 'steersman,'" she writes, "cybernetics signaled that three powerful actors — information, control, and communication — were now operating jointly to bring about an unprecedented synthesis of the organic and the mechanical."[^3] The steersman is the founding image: a pilot adjusting the tiller against wind and current, correcting course by feeding the result of the last correction back into the next. Out of that homely loop the cyberneticians built a theory meant to cover everything that steers itself — the thermostat that reads the room and trims the furnace, the body that sweats to hold its temperature, the gunner tracking a plane. The field crystallized at a series of meetings in the 1940s and 1950s where, as Hayles records, Norbert Wiener, John von Neumann, Claude Shannon, Warren McCulloch "and dozens of other distinguished researchers met at annual conferences sponsored by the Josiah Macy Foundation to formulate the central concepts that … would coalesce into a theory of communication and control applying equally to animals, humans, and machines."[^4]

That last phrase is the hinge of the whole story, and it is worth pausing on. *Applying equally to animals, humans, and machines.* What cybernetics offered was a single vocabulary — feedback, signal, noise, control, information — in which a guided missile, a nervous system, and a corporation could be described as the same kind of thing: a system that processes information to regulate itself. To describe a person and a servomechanism in one language is already to have done something radical to the person. If what matters about you is the pattern of information flowing through your control loops, then the particular stuff you happen to be made of starts to look incidental — a substrate, swappable in principle for any other that carries the same pattern. This is the move Hayles spends her book tracking and contesting: the slow abstraction by which, in her phrase, information came to seem as though it could "lose its body."

The most consequential idea cybernetics produced, in Hayles's reading, is not technical but ontological — a claim about where a person ends. "Of all the implications that first-wave cybernetics conveyed," she writes, "perhaps none was more disturbing and potentially revolutionary than the idea that the boundaries of the human subject are constructed rather than given."[^5] Once a system is defined by its flows of information rather than by its skin, the skin stops being the obvious edge of the self. Hayles likes to invoke Gregory Bateson's deceptively simple question about a blind man with a cane: where does the man stop — at his hand, at the cane's tip, at the pavement his cane is reading? If the relevant system is the information loop by which he navigates, then the cane is inside it, and the man and his instrument are a single circuit. The boundary of the human is no longer a fact of biology but a decision about where to draw the line — and that decision can be drawn anywhere. This is the conceptual ground on which the cyborg becomes not a monster but a description: a name for what we look like once the edge of the body is admitted to be negotiable.

Hayles gives the cleanest available account of what the cyborg, born of this science, actually does to our categories. "Fusing cybernetic device and biological organism," she writes, "the cyborg violates the human/machine distinction; replacing cognition with neural feedback, it challenges the human-animal difference; explaining the behavior of thermostats and people through theories of feedback, hierarchical structure, and control, it erases the animate/inanimate distinction."[^6] Three boundaries fall at once — human from machine, human from animal, living from dead — and each falls because cybernetics has supplied one explanatory frame that straddles it. The cyborg is simply the figure in which those three erasures are gathered into a body.

It matters, finally, that none of this was anyone's intention. Hayles is insistent that the disembodiment cybernetics drifted toward was a contingent outcome, not a logical necessity, and her chief evidence is the founder himself. "For Wiener," she writes, "cybernetics was a means to extend liberal humanism, not subvert it."[^7] Wiener wanted to fortify the autonomous, self-directed individual by giving it a rigorous new science of self-regulation; the science then slipped its leash and began dissolving the very subject he meant to shore up. Hayles narrates this drift as three overlapping phases — she calls them the three waves of cybernetics, "the first, from 1945 to 1960, took homeostasis as a central concept; the second, going roughly from 1960 to 1980, revolved around reflexivity; and the third, stretching from 1980 to the present, highlights virtuality"[^8] — and the point of the periodization is to denaturalize the endpoint. Because the path from the steersman to "bodiless information" was historically contingent, it could have run otherwise, and it can still be redirected. Hold onto that: it is the seed of Hayles's whole quarrel, and the essay will return to it.

## Brains outside the skull

Twenty years before Haraway named the cyborg, Marshall McLuhan had already described the merger in almost every particular except the word. *Understanding Media* (1964) carries a subtitle that is also its entire thesis — *The Extensions of Man* — and the claim is that there is no such thing as a tool that leaves its user unchanged. Every medium, every technology, is a prosthesis: an organ, sense, or faculty pushed outside the body and amplified. The wheel extends the foot, the book extends the eye, clothing extends the skin, and "any extension," McLuhan writes, "whether of skin, hand, or foot, affects the whole psychic and social complex."[^9] An extension is never a neutral addition bolted onto a finished human; it reorganizes the whole sensory and social field around itself. Already the bounded body is leaking outward into its instruments.

McLuhan periodizes this leak into two great ages, and the second is the one that matters for the cyborg. "During the mechanical ages we had extended our bodies in space," he writes. "Today, after more than a century of electric technology, we have extended our central nervous system itself in a global embrace, abolishing both space and time as far as our planet is concerned."[^10] The mechanical extensions were partial — a lever here, a wheel there, single organs flung outward one at a time. Electric technology is different in kind, because the thing it externalizes is the nervous system, the organ that coordinates all the others. With the telegraph, the telephone, radio, and television, the network that runs the body's own signaling is turned inside out and laid over the surface of the planet. The image McLuhan reaches for to describe the result is as close to a literal cyborg as anyone got in 1964: electric technology, he writes, befits "an organism that now wears its brain outside its skull and its nerves outside its hide."[^11] The body has not been left behind; it has been turned inside out.

What McLuhan adds to the cybernetic picture — and what makes him indispensable to a real account of the cyborg — is that this merger has a felt cost, and the cost is numbness. He pairs every extension with an amputation. "Any invention or technology," he writes, "is an extension or self-amputation of our physical bodies, and such extension also demands new ratios or new equilibriums among the other organs and extensions of the body."[^12] To amplify one faculty is to offload it, and to offload it is to deaden it: the driver's legs forget how to run, the literate eye forgets how to listen. McLuhan reads this through the Greek myth of Narcissus, who failed to recognize his own reflection — the point of the story, McLuhan insists, is not vanity but anaesthesia. "Such amplification," he writes, "is bearable by the nervous system only through numbness or blocking of perception. This is the sense of the Narcissus myth," and its consequence is a kind of structural self-blindness: "Self-amputation forbids self-recognition."[^13] We cannot feel our extensions as ourselves, which is precisely why we mistake them for neutral tools "out there." And because we are numb to them, they govern us without our noticing. "By continuously embracing technologies," McLuhan writes, "we relate ourselves to them as servo-mechanisms" — we serve our own outered organs.[^14] The same word, *servomechanism*, that cybernetics used for the self-correcting machine, McLuhan turns back on us: in the electric age the human becomes the servomechanism of its own extensions, and "subliminal and docile acceptance of media impact has made them prisons without walls for their human users."[^15]

McLuhan even arrives, on his own, at the cybernetic premise that the living body is an information network. He notes approvingly that "it is also common now for biologists like Hans Selye to think of the physical organism as a communication network,"[^16] and he forecasts the translation of the whole human into information that Hayles would later anatomize: "In this electric age we see ourselves being translated more and more into the form of information, moving toward the technological extension of consciousness."[^17] His most precise statement of the merger describes the body folded back inside its own externalized nerves: "By putting our physical bodies inside our extended nervous systems, by means of electric media, we set up a dynamic by which all previous technologies that are mere extensions of hands and feet and teeth and bodily heat-controls — all such extensions of our bodies, including cities — will be translated into information systems."[^18] This is the cyborg condition described in advance — the body merged with its technologies, the world recoded as information — written two decades before the figure had a name.

And yet McLuhan stops, deliberately, one step short of the cyborg, and the place where he stops is the most instructive thing about him. Read his sentences again and notice the grammar: it is always *our* nervous system, *our* bodies, *our* hide, *our* brain worn outside the skull. The extensions belong to a "Man" who is doing the extending — a recognizable human subject who survives at the center of his prostheses, enlarged and numbed but never dissolved. Even at the electric extreme, the human is the owner and origin of the merger; the boundary is stretched to the horizon but the someone inside it remains. This is the line Haraway and Hayles will erase. For McLuhan the machine is *ours*; for Haraway, as we are about to see, "the machine is us." That single grammatical shift — from a Man who has extensions to a being who *is* the fusion, with no prior unextended self left over — is the difference between McLuhan's extended man and the cyborg proper.[^19] It is worth saying plainly that McLuhan never used the word *cyborg* and was not writing toward Haraway or Hayles; reading him as their precursor is a connection this vault constructs across the decades, not a debt anyone acknowledged. But the materials of the figure are all here in 1964: the prosthetic body, the externalized nervous system, the human as servomechanism of its machines. What is missing is only the willingness to let the owner go.

## The machine is us

Haraway lets the owner go, and turns the cyborg from a description into a politics. *A Cyborg Manifesto* is not a forecast of a future being; it is a claim about who we already are, written for the feminists and socialists of the 1980s as a deliberate provocation — "an effort to build an ironic political myth faithful to feminism, socialism, and materialism."[^20] Her wager is that the boundary-dissolving figure cybernetics produced, far from being something to fear, is a resource the left should seize. "By the late twentieth century, our time, a mythic time," she writes, "we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism — in short, cyborgs. The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics."[^21] *Our ontology*: not a prosthesis we might acquire but the kind of being we already are. The cyborg, she adds in the same breath, "is a condensed image of both imagination and material reality" — that doubleness again, now load-bearing, the figure where the fictional and the actual are welded together.[^21]

The cyborg becomes thinkable, for Haraway, because three boundaries have collapsed at once in late-twentieth-century science — what the vault tracks as the breached boundaries. She announces them precisely. The first: "By the late twentieth century in U.S. scientific culture, the boundary between human and animal is thoroughly breached," every supposedly unique human trait having failed to hold the line against biology and evolution.[^22] The second, which she calls "the second leaky distinction," is "between animal–human (organism) and machine," now that machines have grown self-regulating and lively while we have begun to seem mechanical: "Our machines are disturbingly lively, and we ourselves frighteningly inert."[^23] The third is the one that bears most directly on this vault's concern with the material and the immaterial. "The third distinction is a subset of the second: the boundary between physical and nonphysical is very imprecise for us," because the machines that now matter most are barely physical at all. "Our best machines are made of sunshine," Haraway writes; "they are all light and clean because they are nothing but signals, electromagnetic waves, a section of a spectrum… People are nowhere near so fluid, being both material and opaque. Cyborgs are ether, quintessence."[^24] Hold that contrast — machines made of sunshine, people material and opaque — because it contains the whole ambivalence of the cyborg. The figure points toward immateriality, toward the dream of the body dissolving into signal; and yet Haraway plants, right at the heart of it, the stubborn fact that *people* are not made of sunshine. We are material and opaque. The cyborg is where that tension lives, not where it is resolved.

Haraway is clear-eyed about the cyborg's ugly parentage. It is no innocent emancipator: it was born from the military and the market, from radar and missile guidance and the integrated circuit. "The main trouble with cyborgs, of course, is that they are the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism, not to mention state socialism," she writes — and then turns the indictment into the opening: "But illegitimate offspring are often exceedingly unfaithful to their origins. Their fathers, after all, are inessential."[^25] The same coding-of-the-world that produces what she calls the [[The Informatics of Domination|informatics of domination]] — a regime in which "communications sciences and modern biologies are constructed by a common move — the translation of the world into a problem of coding, a search for a common language in which all resistance to instrumental control disappears"[^26] — also produces a figure that can be turned against that regime, precisely because it owes its fathers nothing. This is why Haraway's cyborg is defined by what it refuses. "The cyborg is resolutely committed to partiality, irony, intimacy, and perversity," she writes; "it is oppositional, utopian, and completely without innocence."[^27] It has no Eden and no fall to mourn: "In a sense, the cyborg has no origin story in the Western sense."[^28] Her closing myth makes the refusal a manifesto in itself — "A cyborg body is not innocent; it was not born in a garden; it does not seek unitary identity… it takes irony for granted" — and resolves into the essay's famous last line: "Though both are bound in the spiral dance, I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess."[^29]

That preference — cyborg over goddess — is the exact point at which Haraway parts ways with the reading of the cyborg as flight from the body, and it is the heart of why she matters to this argument. The goddess is the figure of organic wholeness, of a return to nature and the unspoiled body; one might expect a thinker of machine-organism fusion to line up against the body and with the machine. Haraway does the opposite. She refuses the goddess not because she wants to escape embodiment but because the goddess's "wholeness" is a fiction that has historically been used to police women, and the cyborg's partiality is more honest. And when she states the cyborg's relation to its machinery, she states it as a relation *of* embodiment, not an escape *from* it. "The machine is not an it to be animated, worshipped, and dominated," she writes. "The machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment."[^30] Read that against McLuhan's grammar and the difference is total. McLuhan's machine is *ours* — owned by a Man standing behind it. Haraway's machine *is us* — there is no Man behind it to own anything, only the hybrid, and the hybrid is a mode of being a body, not of leaving one. The cyborg, for Haraway, is what survives "not on the basis of original innocence, but on the basis of seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other"[^31] — a figure of responsibility, not transcendence. "It is the simultaneity of breakdowns," she writes, "that cracks the matrices of domination and opens geometric possibilities":[^32] the dissolution of boundaries is an occasion for politics, and politics is something only embodied, situated, opaque beings can have.

## The cyborg and the posthuman are not the same thing

Hayles takes Haraway's cyborg and does two things with it: she gives it a history, and she carefully distinguishes it from a second concept it is constantly confused with — the posthuman. Hers is the most analytically precise treatment of the three, and the place to start is the debt, because Hayles is unusually scrupulous about it. The figure is not hers; she says so. "As Donna Haraway has pointed out," she writes, "cyborgs are simultaneously entities and metaphors, living beings and narrative constructions."[^33] She takes the term [[Informatics|informatics]] from Haraway too, by name — "Following Donna Haraway, I take informatics to mean the technologies of information as well as the biological, social, linguistic, and cultural changes that initiate, accompany, and complicate their development"[^34] — and she credits Haraway with the cyborg's erotic charge as well, "what Haraway calls its 'pleasurably tight coupling' between parts that are not supposed to touch."[^35] This is one of the rare places in the vault where the link between two authors rests on documented citation rather than the editor's construction: Hayles read Haraway and built on her, openly.

What Hayles adds is a birth certificate. Where Haraway's cyborg is mythic and timeless ("our ontology"), Hayles's is a specific postwar artifact, and she locates its conception at a scene she finds genuinely strange: the Turing test, the 1950 thought-experiment in which a human judge tries to tell a human from a machine by their typed answers alone. What that test quietly accomplishes, Hayles argues, is to split the human in two — into a body "present in the flesh on one side of the computer screen" and a set of informational markers reconstituted on the other. "This construction," she writes, "necessarily makes the subject into a cyborg, for the enacted and represented bodies are brought into conjunction through the technology that connects them."[^36] The cyborg, on this account, is not first a prosthetic body but a *split* one — the gap between the flesh that types and the pattern that appears on the far screen, stitched together by the machine between them. And she names the cost of that scene with a precision worth quoting: "Here, at the inaugural moment of the computer age, the erasure of embodiment is performed so that 'intelligence' becomes a property of the formal manipulation of symbols rather than enaction in the human lifeworld."[^37] The computer age opens, for Hayles, with a magic trick in which the body is made to disappear — and her whole book is an effort to put it back.

The cyborg's reality, for Hayles as for Haraway, lies in its refusal to be either purely material or purely figural. "Manifesting itself as both technological object and discursive formation," she writes, "it partakes of the power of the imagination as well as of the actuality of technology. Cyborgs actually exist."[^38] Were it only a technology it could be filed under bionics; were it only a metaphor it could be filed under science fiction. Because it is both, it cannot be filed away at all — it sits permanently astride the line between the material and the immaterial, which is exactly why it carries the weight of this vault's central argument.

But Hayles's most important pedagogical move is to pry the cyborg apart from the posthuman, two terms routinely used as synonyms. The cyborg is a *figure* — a hybrid body, an entity you could in principle point to. The posthuman is something else: not a being but a *point of view*. "What is the posthuman?" Hayles asks, and answers with deliberate understatement: "Think of it as a point of view characterized by the following assumptions."[^39] She lists four. The posthuman view "privileges informational pattern over material instantiation, so that embodiment in a biological substrate is seen as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life." It treats consciousness as a sideshow rather than the seat of identity. It "thinks of the body as the original prosthesis we all learn to manipulate, so that extending or replacing the body with other prostheses becomes a continuation of a process that began before we were born." And, "most important," it "configures human being so that it can be seamlessly articulated with intelligent machines," such that "there are no essential differences or absolute demarcations between bodily existence and computer simulation, cybernetic mechanism and biological organism, robot teleology and human goals."[^40]

The decisive consequence — and the line every reader of the cyborg should memorize — is that the posthuman is a matter of how subjectivity is constructed, not of what hardware you carry. "The construction of the posthuman," Hayles writes, "does not require the subject to be a literal cyborg… even a biologically unaltered Homo sapiens counts as posthuman. The defining characteristics involve the construction of subjectivity, not the presence of nonbiological components."[^41] You do not need an implant to be posthuman. You become posthuman the moment you start thinking of yourself as essentially a pattern of information that your body merely happens to carry — the moment you treat the flesh as a substrate. The cyborg is the figure; the posthuman is the *worldview that takes the figure literally and concludes the body is incidental*. One can have the figure without the worldview, and — more disturbingly — the worldview without the figure.

This distinction is what lets Hayles mount her critique, and her target is not the cyborg but the posthuman worldview's secret continuity with the very humanism it claims to overthrow. Her reference point for "the human," she explains, is the liberal humanist subject — the Enlightenment individual of autonomy, reason, and self-possession — and "the posthuman appears when computation rather than possessive individualism is taken as the ground of being."[^42] You would expect the posthuman to be that subject's opposite. Hayles's unsettling claim is that it is that subject's heir, because both share one fatal habit: contempt for the body. The liberal subject, she observes, was always located in the rational mind, never the flesh — "Identified with the rational mind, the liberal subject possessed a body but was not usually represented as being a body."[^43] Its prized universality depended on erasing bodily difference — sex, race, ethnicity — so that the "human" could be everyone by being, concretely, no one. And the posthuman, by recoding the body as replaceable information, simply finishes that erasure under a new sign. "The erasure of embodiment," Hayles writes, "is a feature common to both the liberal humanist subject and the cybernetic posthuman."[^44] The dream of uploading consciousness and leaving the meat behind is not a break from liberal humanism; it is liberal humanism's oldest wish — the self as disembodied mind — granted at last by a computer.

This is why Hayles's verdict is so carefully worded. She does not reject the posthuman. She is, in a sense, a posthuman thinker who wants a better posthuman. "What is lethal," she writes, "is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self."[^45] The danger is the splice: the posthuman's dissolution of the bounded subject fused to the liberal subject's fantasy of autonomous, immortal, disembodied mastery. Cut that graft and the posthuman becomes something she can affirm. "The posthuman does not really mean the end of humanity," she writes; "it signals instead the end of a certain conception of the human" — the autonomous liberal one, which "may have applied, at best, to that fraction of humanity who had the wealth, power, and leisure to conceptualize themselves as autonomous beings."[^46] Far from a rupture, the posthuman turns out to have a long, materially embedded history. "Bruno Latour has argued that we have never been modern," Hayles writes; "the seriated history of cybernetics… suggests, for similar reasons, that we have always been posthuman."[^47] We did not become hybrids of organism and machine in some recent science-fiction rupture. We were always already inside the loops — always extended, always coupled, always more system than sealed self.

## Different embodiment

Put the three writers in a line and a single argument runs through them, against the grain of the cyborg's pop-cultural reputation. McLuhan's extended human is not a body discarded but a body turned inside out and, crucially, numbed — still flesh, still a nervous system, only no longer able to feel its own edges. Haraway's cyborg is not flight from the body but a way of being one: "the machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment," and she would still rather be a cyborg, material and opaque, than a goddess of disembodied wholeness. Hayles's whole book is a protest against the reading of the cyborg as escape — against the "erasure of embodiment" performed at the computer's inaugural moment, against the graft that makes the posthuman lethal. The cyborg, read through all three, is not the body's exit. It is the discovery that the body has no exit — and, more than that, that there was never a sealed, self-owning, purely biological human standing outside its tools from which an exit could be made.

The deepest version of this lesson is Hayles's, and it is the note on which to rest the argument. The fantasy the posthuman smuggles in is that information is the essence and the body the accident — that you are a pattern, and the flesh is just this run's hardware. Hayles's counter-claim, the spine of *How We Became Posthuman*, is that information never actually floats free of matter; the disembodiment is an illusion produced by a particular history and a particular interface, and the body always returns to collect. Even her account of language under digital conditions — the [[Flickering Signifier|flickering signifier]], the on-screen mark that "flickers rather than floats" because the image is constantly refreshed — turns on the insistence that "the pattern/randomness dialectic does not erase the material world; information in fact derives its efficacy from the material infrastructures it appears to obscure."[^48] The pattern is always running *on* something, and the something is never incidental. She states the stakes most movingly as a choice between two futures, and the passage is the closest thing the essay has to a creed: "If my nightmare is a culture inhabited by posthumans who regard their bodies as fashion accessories rather than the ground of being, my dream is a version of the posthuman that embraces the possibilities of information technologies without being seduced by fantasies of unlimited power and disembodied immortality, that recognizes and celebrates finitude as a condition of human being, and that understands human life is embedded in a material world of great complexity, one on which we depend for our continued survival."[^49]

This is why the cyborg is, in the end, an *educational* figure rather than a frightening one. It teaches that the line between the human and its machines was always negotiable; that to extend a capacity is to be changed by it; that there is no innocent, pre-technological body to return to and no disembodied pattern to ascend into. Hayles's most quoted sentence makes the lesson a task rather than a prophecy: "Increasingly the question is not whether we will become posthuman, for posthumanity is already here. Rather, the question is what kind of posthumans we will be."[^50] The cyborg does not announce a future we are heading toward. It describes a present we are already inside — and the only thing still open is which version of it we choose. A posthuman that treats the body as a fashion accessory, or one that "celebrates finitude as a condition of human being." The figure that looks like the end of embodiment turns out to be the strongest argument for it.

## Coda: the cyborg as a curatorial lens

For a research vault built on the tension between the material and the immaterial, the cyborg is less a topic than an instrument — the single figure where that tension is staged rather than resolved, and it can be put to work. Haraway gives the exhibition its terms: machines "made of sunshine," "nothing but signals," set against human beings who are "both material and opaque." Stand those two phrases at the entrance of any show about technology and the body and you have a sorting rule. On one wall, the work that takes the cyborg as escape — the upload fantasy, the body as obsolete hardware, the dream of pattern lifted clean off its substrate; the lineage Hayles calls lethal. On the other, the work that takes the cyborg as Haraway and Hayles do — the machine as "an aspect of our embodiment," the extension that changes the one who extends, the pattern that always returns to its flesh. The curatorial question the three authors hand over is not *is this art about technology* — nearly everything now is — but *which cyborg is it: the one that leaves the body, or the one that proves the body cannot be left?*

The same rule writes as easily as it curates. An essay, a wall text, or a course built on this material would do well to begin where this one began — by refusing the reflexive picture of the cyborg as the body's first step into the machine, and replacing it with the harder, truer one the texts support: that we are the steersman's children, always already inside the loops, and that the cyborg's real lesson is not transcendence but the impossibility of subtraction. McLuhan saw the body turned inside out and worried we had gone numb to it. Haraway saw the hybrid and asked what politics an embodied, opaque, unfaithful creature might make from it. Hayles saw the magic trick of disappearance and spent a book putting the body back. To explain the cyborg honestly is to teach all three lessons at once — and to leave the reader, as Hayles does, not with a prophecy but with a choice about what kind of posthumans, and what kind of bodies, we mean to be.

## A note on method

This essay's individual quotations are all `Directly stated` and verbatim; its connective tissue is mixed, and the seams should be visible. The overall footing is **medium**: the quotes are secure and load-bearing, but several of the bridges between the three authors are the vault's own constructions rather than citations anyone made.

One link is documented and runs the helpful direction. N. Katherine Hayles read Donna Haraway and built on her openly — she takes the cyborg "as Donna Haraway has pointed out," adopts the term *informatics* "Following Donna Haraway," and credits Haraway with the cyborg's "pleasurably tight coupling." The Hayles → Haraway debt is therefore `Directly stated`, **high** confidence, attested in Hayles's own text. The Marshall McLuhan links are different and weaker by nature: McLuhan never used the word *cyborg*, predates both other authors, and was not writing toward them. Reading his "extensions of man" as the cyborg's prehistory — and reading the grammatical difference between his *Man who owns his extensions* and Haraway's *machine that is us* as the precise threshold the cyborg crosses — is `Interpretive synthesis`, **medium** confidence, a connection this vault draws and not a debt McLuhan acknowledged. I have flagged this in the prose where it bears on the argument.

The essay's controlling thesis — that the cyborg, against its popular reputation, teaches the impossibility of leaving the body rather than the means of doing so — is likewise the vault's own `Interpretive synthesis` (medium). It is built from `Directly stated` materials (Haraway's "an aspect of our embodiment" and "I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess"; Hayles's "erasure of embodiment," her "lethal graft," and her dream of a posthuman that "celebrates finitude"; McLuhan's numbness and his retained "Man"), but the synthesis that aligns all three into a single lesson about embodiment is mine, not a claim any one author makes about the other two. The title, *We Have Always Been Cyborgs*, is a deliberate blend of Hayles's "we have always been posthuman" and Haraway's "we are all… cyborgs" — a vault coinage, not a quotation from either.

Two scope notes. First, this is a three-author essay by the user's design: it draws only on McLuhan, Haraway, and Hayles, and on no sources outside the vault. The cyborg's wider company in the vault — Jack Burnham's 1968 "Cyborg Art" and his cybernetic definition of the cyborg, Roy Ascott's telematic and "post-biological" body, and the transhumanist poles (Kurzweil, Harari, O'Gieblyn) that read the posthuman as upload, obsolescence, or eschatology — is deliberately set aside; each would widen the frame past the three authors in play. Second, Hayles's cybernetics history is compressed here to what the cyborg requires (the steersman, the Macy synthesis, the constructed boundary, the three waves); her fuller account of homeostasis, reflexivity, autopoiesis, and the flickering signifier is only gestured at.

Every quotation was verified character-for-character against the raw chapters. The only normalizations applied are the vault-standard ones: curly quotation marks and apostrophes to straight; closure of OCR line-wrap artifacts and scanner glitches within a verbatim span (for example the source's "ofa certain conception" and "Wiencr" are rendered "of a certain conception" and "Wiener"); marked elisions shown with an ellipsis; sentence-initial case folded where a quotation is grafted into the essay's own syntax; and, where a quotation is truncated at a clause boundary, its terminal em-dash or comma closed with a period (as at Haraway's "no origin story in the Western sense," which the source continues with an em-dash). No word inside any quotation was changed, added, or reordered. An adversarial verification pass — one reader per raw chapter, checking every quoted span against the source — confirmed all fifty-five quotations verbatim under these normalizations, with no substantive failures.
