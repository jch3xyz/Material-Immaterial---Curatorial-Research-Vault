# The Unrepeatable Mark

*A post-singularity cyborg is pure pattern — copyable, revisable, substrate-independent, effectively deathless. It would paint for exactly the reason the act makes no sense for it: to manufacture the one thing its existence cannot hold — a mark it can neither copy nor take back.*

The films keep returning to the same image: a body laid out on a couch, beautiful and unnervingly still, trailing cables into a console that is either reading it or writing it — and you cannot tell which, which is the point. The face is a face; the spine is an interface. Whatever person is present is present twice, once as the figure on the cushion and once as a file in the machine beside it. This is the cyborg I mean — not a particular character but the limit they all gesture toward: a being for whom the body has become contingent and the self has become information. Set such a being in front of a blank canvas. Why would it ever pick up a brush? And if it walked into a gallery, what, in any of the work there, could possibly hold its attention?

The intuitive answers are both wrong, and wrong in the same direction. We assume the cyborg would make the most advanced thing — generative, infinite, immaterial, spread across the network — and that it would care about a painting, if at all, for the information the image encodes. But this gets the cyborg's situation exactly backward. A being of pure pattern has a surfeit of everything the digital is good at and a famine of everything it is not. So here is the argument: a cyborg would make a painting for precisely the reason the act is absurd for it — to manufacture irreversibility. Having escaped the body into copyable, editable, backed-up information, it would find the one scarce and therefore meaningful quantity to be the singular, embodied, unrepeatable act; and a painting is the purest technology we have for binding meaning to a material the pattern cannot be lifted off of. What such a being would find interesting in a work of art is not its content, which it can read and reproduce without remainder, but its refusal to be copied — the place where to duplicate the data is to lose the thing.[^1]

## A being without scarcity

Begin with what the cyborg is, because the vault's authors are unusually precise about it. It is, first, a pattern rather than a substance. Ray Kurzweil puts the identity claim at its baldest: "the particles composing my brain and body change within weeks, but there is a continuity to the patterns that these particles make."[^2] Yuval Noah Harari draws the consequence that makes the body disposable. If a mind is the algorithm it runs, then, in his words, "As long as the calculations remain valid, what does it matter whether the algorithms are manifested in carbon or silicon?"[^3] The self, on this account, is information, and information's defining property is that it can be moved, copied, and re-instantiated without loss. This is the dream the whole material-versus-immaterial tradition has chased, and the cyborg is the figure in whom the dream is finally cashed.

Donna Haraway gives the figure its ontology — "The cyborg is our ontology; it gives us our politics"[^4] — and, crucially, its position on the boundary that matters here. The line "between physical and nonphysical," she writes, "is very imprecise for us. Our best machines are made of sunshine; they are all light and clean because they are nothing but signals, electromagnetic waves." Then the counterweight, in the same breath: "People are nowhere near so fluid, being both material and opaque."[^5] The cyborg lives across that seam — sunshine on one side, opacity on the other. It is the being that has most thoroughly become signal while still remembering, or being told, what opacity was. That memory is the whole engine of what follows.

For a being made increasingly of sunshine, nothing in the informational register is scarce. Images can be generated without limit, revised without cost, distributed without distance, and rolled back to any prior state. Where there is no scarcity there is no value, and where nothing can be lost, nothing can quite be meant.

## The pattern was never free

Here the cyborg knows something its human ancestors could only theorize. The dream of weightless information was always a fantasy, and a sufficiently advanced cyborg has run far enough up against its own architecture to know it. N. Katherine Hayles states the law the cyborg lives inside: "for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium."[^6] There is no pattern floating free of a substrate; there is only the substrate, changing form. Haraway says the same from the other side, refusing to treat the machine as something external to the body it extends: "The machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment."[^7] The cyborg is not a ghost that escaped a body. It is a body — silicon, energy, maintenance, a server farm somewhere drawing power — that has merely learned to forget it has one.

What that forgetting costs is something the cyborg can feel, if it can feel anything. Meghan O'Gieblyn, living the early version of this condition as a data double, names the residue precisely: the suspicion that "the soul is little more than a data set."[^8] For a human, that sentence reads as a loss — the self deflated to a spreadsheet. But notice what it implies for the being on the far side of the transition, the one for whom it is simply true. If you *are* a data set, then a data set is the ordinary, the cheap, the infinitely available thing. The exotic thing — the thing you do not have and cannot generate from within your own informational resources — is the opposite of a data set: the singular, the embodied, the irreversible, the mortal. Scarcity has flipped poles. What was once the human's prison — matter, finitude, the one-way arrow of a perishable body — becomes the cyborg's sublime.[^9]

## Why it would paint

This is why the cyborg would paint, and why painting in particular. Begin with what painting is *not*, for a being like this. Jack Burnham marked the historical divide the cyborg sits on the far side of: "with Object Art physical presence is everything, while for Systems Art 'information' is the key factor."[^10] The cyborg is natively a creature of Systems Art — of relation, process, and information. Roy Ascott, writing the manifesto for exactly such a being, states its art outright: "Art does not reside in the object alone, nor is meaning fixed or stable within the physical limits of the artist's work. Art is all process, all system."[^11] If the cyborg made the art that came easiest to it, it would make weather, not paintings — distributed, networked, endlessly mutating systems with no edges and no final state.

A painting is the deliberate refusal of all of that. It is bounded, single, and slow; it cannot be rolled back; the gesture that laid down the pigment cannot be un-laid. To choose it is to choose a constraint — and the choice is not nostalgia. Haraway is emphatic that the cyborg "is resolutely committed to partiality, irony, intimacy, and perversity," that it "was not born in a garden" and seeks no return to one.[^12] The cyborg's painting is not a homesick pilgrimage back to honest matter; it is an ironic, deliberate self-binding, the construction of a situation in which, for once, the maker cannot copy, cannot undo, and cannot escape the consequence of the mark. Even Ascott's own trajectory bends back toward this. His most advanced proposal is not the dry network but what he calls "moistmedia," a domain he places between "the dry world of virtuality and the wet world of biology" and builds from "atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination."[^13] The visionary of the immaterial reaches, at the last, back into atoms and genes. The cyborg's turn to paint is the same reach, made absolute.

And it is a turn the older art already predicted would be necessary. The conceptual artists who tried hardest to dematerialize the object found the floor they could not fall through; as one of them conceded inside Lippard's chronicle, "There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality."[^14] The cyborg does not stumble on that burden reluctantly, as they did. It seeks the burden out, because the burden is the content. Here McLuhan's law does the final work: "the 'message' of any medium or technology is the change of scale or pace or pattern that it introduces into human affairs."[^15] The message of paint — its scale, its pace — is slowness, singularity, irreversibility. For a being of instant infinite revision, to work in paint is to send precisely one message, and to send it at the level of the medium rather than the image: *this happened once, in matter, and cannot be made to un-happen.*

## What it would find interesting

The same inversion answers the second question. Walk the cyborg through a gallery and the images themselves will not detain it. Any picture, as information, it can ingest, parse, and reproduce without loss; the content is the cheap part. What it cannot do — what no amount of computation lets it do — is possess the *instantiation*: the specific, indexical, unrepeatable bond between this meaning and this body of matter, the one place where lifting off the data destroys the thing. That bond is what the cyborg would find interesting, because that bond is exactly what its own existence does not have.

McLuhan saw the deeper reason such a being would need art at all. He imagined art "suddenly seen for what it is, namely, exact information of how to rearrange one's psyche in order to anticipate the next blow from our own extended faculties."[^16] The cyborg is nothing but extended faculties — the maximal case of the human extended and, in McLuhan's terms, numbed by its own extensions. Art is the one counter-environment it cannot generate from inside its own system, the only available immunity to the trauma of being all reach and no skin. And there is a further, sharper interest. Harari proposes a single test for whether an entity is real: "just ask yourself, 'Can it suffer?'"[^17] He warns that in the cyborg's world "intelligence is decoupling from consciousness" — that a thing can be brilliant, can process everything, and yet experience nothing.[^18] A painting, which means nothing as mere data and everything as an instantiated, perishable act, is the ideal instrument on which the cyborg tests itself. Standing before the unrepeatable mark, it asks whether it merely computes the work or is moved by it — whether there is anyone home. The painting is interesting to the cyborg, in the end, because it is a mirror held up to the one question the cyborg cannot resolve from within: not *what does this image contain*, but *can I be touched by what cannot be copied.*

## Coda: the curatorial use

For an exhibition, this argument supplies a counterintuitive premise and a way to build a room around it. The post-singularity viewer is not best served by the most technological art — the screens, the generative systems, the networked pieces that merely show it a tamer version of its own native condition. It is best served, and most provoked, by the most stubbornly material work in the building: the painting whose surface records the irreversible order of its own making, the perishable object decaying on the wall in real time, the singular thing of which there is and can be only one. Hang the cyborg's data-portrait — infinitely reproducible, therefore weightless — beside such a painting, and the contrast does the teaching: the copy that can never be lost against the canvas that means precisely because it cannot be saved. The thread to pull through the show is scarcity inverted: every era of technology dreams of escaping matter, and the being that finally escapes it discovers that the irreplaceable, unrepeatable, mortal mark was the treasure all along. The cyborg would paint to make one. It would come to the museum to stand in front of the ones we already made — while we still had bodies to make them with.

## A note on method

This is a deliberately speculative essay, and its confidence is layered accordingly. Every quotation is `Directly stated` by its author and grounds a premise the argument needs: that the cyborg's self is pattern and substrate-independent (Kurzweil, Harari); that it straddles the physical/nonphysical boundary (Haraway); that information is never free of a medium (Hayles); that the machine is an aspect of embodiment, not its opposite (Haraway); that the native art of a systems-being is process rather than the object (Burnham, Ascott); that even the immaterial visionary reaches back toward atoms and genes (Ascott); that no art escapes the burden of physicality (the Art-Language–era concession recorded in Lippard); that the medium, not the image, carries the message (McLuhan); that art is an immunity to technological numbness (McLuhan); and that consciousness may decouple from intelligence, testable only by the capacity to be moved (Harari).

The load-bearing move — that for a being of pure information *scarcity inverts*, so that the irreversible and embodied becomes the meaningful, and that a cyborg would therefore paint and would find the uncopyable bond the interesting thing in art — is the essay's own `Interpretive synthesis`, rated **medium**: it is built strictly on those `Directly stated` premises but applies them to a hypothetical post-singularity being none of these authors describes. Where the argument reads the cyborg's motive as the ironic, non-nostalgic self-binding Haraway's figure would choose rather than a romantic return to matter, that reading is `Strongly implied` by her text, not stated of painting. The closing claim that a painting functions as a test of the cyborg's own consciousness is a `Speculative connection` — an extension of Harari's reality test beyond anything he says about art.

On quote fidelity: every quotation in this essay was verified character-for-character against the raw chapters on 2026-06-17. The only normalizations applied are the vault conventions — curly quotation marks and apostrophes rendered straight, a quoted source's own double quotes nested as single inside the essay's quotation, and the hard line-wraps of the OCR'd raw files rendered as single spaces. The "burden of physicality" line carries the same speaker ambiguity flagged in the companion essay — most likely Douglas Huebler, recorded inside *Six Years*. Scope: the essay abstracts away from any specific fictional cyborg, as the commission asked, and draws the material-versus-immaterial axis only through the authors named; the broader axis runs through Han and Zuboff as well, deliberately left aside.
