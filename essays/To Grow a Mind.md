# To Grow a Mind

*Hand a post-singularity cyborg the three oldest questions of art — what to make it of, how to make it, what it is about — and they collapse into one. It would work in living substance, by cultivation rather than fabrication, on the only subject a being that has erased the line between maker and made can honestly take. Its art would not picture life. It would try to grow one.*

Picture the studio differently. Not a room of brushes and stretched linen but something nearer a greenhouse wired to a server: trays of cultured tissue under grow-lights, a vat in which something is slowly organizing itself, screens scrolling the code that tends the vat, and at the center a figure you cannot quite tell apart from the apparatus — its nerves run out into the trays, the trays run back into its nerves. This is the maker I want to take seriously: a post-singularity cyborg, a being for whom the body has become editable and the self has become a pattern that can be ported, copied, and revised. Now ask it the three questions we have always asked of an artist — what will you make it of, how will you make it, and what will it be about — and watch what happens to them.

A companion to this essay asks why such a being would ever pick up a brush, and answers that it would paint *against* its own nature, to manufacture the one irreversible, uncopyable mark its existence cannot otherwise hold. That is the cyborg in refusal. Here I want the opposite case: the art it would make if it worked *with* what it is rather than against it. And here the three questions do something strange — they stop being three. The material it would reach for is living substance, the stuff it is itself made of. The process would not be fabrication but cultivation. And the subject — the only one available to a being that has erased the boundary between the maker and the made — would be consciousness itself. Pursued together, those three answers name a single act: not the representation of life but its manufacture. The cyborg would try to grow a mind. In doing so it would complete the oldest and most secret ambition of the art it inherited, the one Jack Burnham found buried under the whole history of sculpture — the drive, as he put it, by which "Western man unknowingly converges art with a universal desire to re-create life nonbiologically."[^1]

## It would work in the stuff it is made of

Begin with the material, because the cyborg's situation determines it. Donna Haraway built her cyborg precisely on the collapse of the boundaries that used to tell us what a thing was made of. The line between organism and machine has gone soft — "Our machines are disturbingly lively, and we ourselves frighteningly inert," she writes[^2] — and so has the line beneath it, between the physical and the nonphysical. "Our best machines are made of sunshine," she observes; "they are all light and clean because they are nothing but signals, electromagnetic waves," while "People are nowhere near so fluid, being both material and opaque."[^3] The cyborg is the being that lives across both seams at once, sunshine and opacity in one body, and it does not experience the machine as something other than itself. "The machine is us, our processes, an aspect of our embodiment," Haraway insists.[^4] For such a maker there is no neutral material lying outside it to be picked up and shaped. Its medium and its membrane are the same substance.

That substance is no longer fixed. Ray Kurzweil's post-singularity body is an engineering project under continuous revision — "By the 2030s we will become more nonbiological than biological," he predicts[^5] — so the cyborg's flesh is already a designed and redesignable material, neither simply organic nor simply technical. Roy Ascott gives this merged substance its name and its artistic charge. "Between the dry world of virtuality and the wet world of biology lies a moist domain, a new interspace of potentiality and promise," he writes; "Moistmedia (comprising bits, atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination) will constitute the substrate of the art of our new century."[^6] *Moist* is the exact word, because it refuses the old dry/wet, digital/biological split that the question "what materials?" silently assumes. The cyborg would not choose between pigment and pixel. It would work in bits, atoms, neurons, and genes at once.

And here the deepest premise surfaces, the one that makes such a material continuous with life rather than merely adjacent to it. Burnham, reporting the consensus of the bio-electronics of his day, states it flatly: "there are no qualitative physical differences between living and nonliving matter," only "an ascending scale of complexity in the organization of matter." From which the consequence the whole essay turns on: "organisms artificially created may possess consciousness."[^7] If that is true, then to work in matter is already, potentially, to work in life — the difference is one of arrangement, not of kind. The cyborg's material is living substance not because it has chosen something exotic but because, on this view, *all* substance is incipiently alive, and the cyborg is simply the maker with the means to organize it that far.

One caution keeps this from floating off into the old fantasy of weightless creation, and it is worth stating because it is the hinge the companion argument turns on too. N. Katherine Hayles refuses the dream that information, or life, could ever be free of its substrate: "Embodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated."[^8] Even the cyborg's moist medium is local, specific, perishable — a particular vat, a particular tangle of tissue and code, drawing power somewhere. The material is genuinely material. That is precisely why the work made of it can be alive, and can die.

## Not carving but cultivating

If the material is living, the process cannot be carving. You do not carve a living thing; you raise it. The whole tendency of the art the cyborg inherits already bends this way. Ascott states the systems-artist's first principle without hedging: "Art does not reside in the object alone, nor is meaning fixed or stable within the physical limits of the artist's work. Art is all process, all system."[^9] Burnham had drawn the same line a generation earlier as the migration of sculpture "from the object to the system," and he was explicit that what distinguishes a system from an object is that a system "may manifest some of the fundamental characteristics natural to life: self-organization, growth, internal or external mobility, irritability or sensitivity, input and output, kinetically sustained equilibrium and eventual death."[^10] A maker who works in such a medium is no longer a fabricator of forms. The work grows, metabolizes, responds, and dies on its own; the maker's role moves upstream of all that.

What is left for the maker to do, then, is to set conditions. Ascott names the new office precisely: "Instead of creating, expressing, or transmitting content, he is now involved in designing context: contexts within which the observer or viewer can construct experience and meaning."[^11] And as the medium turns biological, that context-setting becomes frankly horticultural. His late writing reaches again and again for the garden. Against the museum he proposes that "we could plant knowledge landscapes — gardens of hypotheses," and folds a whole practice he calls cyberbotany into the formula "hyperculture seen as horticulture, the moist synthesis of artificial and natural systems."[^12] The governing verbs stop being *cut*, *cast*, *compose* and become *seed*, *tend*, *grow*. Ascott says as much of the architecture he imagines: "design will be a bottom-up process," its end "the building of sentience, a moist architecture that has a life of its own, that thinks for itself, feeds itself, takes care of itself, repairs itself, plans its future, copes with adversity."[^13]

The process therefore demands something the older idea of authorship cannot survive: the maker must let go of the work's outcome. Cultivation is not control. Ascott pushes this to its limit, relocating authorship from the artist into the living system itself: if there is an author of the work's meaning, he writes, "it may be the system of interaction itself, in all its particulars, that should be described as the author."[^14] The cyborg's process, then, is a double act of cultivation and relinquishment — to seed a system and then to cede to it. The maker plants and tends; the work, increasingly, makes itself.

## The only subject left

Which leaves the strangest question of the three, and the one whose answer the whole argument has been bending toward. What could such a work be *about*? The cyborg cannot take the old subjects. It cannot paint the world as a thing observed from outside, because Haraway's collapse has dissolved the standpoint: "It is not clear who makes and who is made in the relation between human and machine."[^15] Nor can it take the bounded self as its theme, having no bounded self to take. One subject survives the collapse, and Ascott names it: art will recognize "the primacy of consciousness as both the context and content of art," he argues, so that "consciousness in all its forms will be the field of its unfolding."[^16] For the cyborg, consciousness is not one possible subject among many. It is the only one left standing.

It is also the one thing the cyborg cannot settle about itself, which is what makes it not merely available but unavoidable. The hard problem of consciousness — "Why should physical processing give rise to a rich inner life at all?", in the formulation Meghan O'Gieblyn takes from David Chalmers, who adds that "It seems objectively unreasonable that it should, and yet it does"[^17] — bears on the cyborg with a force it never had for us. Yuval Noah Harari frames the coming century as the moment "intelligence is decoupling from consciousness," and delivers the sentence that ought to haunt any thinking machine: "intelligence is mandatory but consciousness is optional."[^18] The cyborg is the maximal case of that decoupling. It can compute everything and may experience nothing, and it has no way to tell from the inside which it is. Kurzweil, asked whether a being like this is conscious, concedes that the matter cannot be decided by any measurement: "precisely because the question is not scientific, it is a philosophical one — indeed, the fundamental philosophical question."[^19]

So the cyborg does what artists have always done with the question they cannot answer about themselves: it externalizes it into a made thing and watches. Burnham saw this telos in sculpture long before there was a cyborg to fulfill it. Cyborg art, he wrote, "is really the first attempt to simulate the structure of life literally," and through it "sculpture seeks its own obliteration by moving toward integration with the intelligent life forms it has always imitated."[^20] The cyborg's work is that obliteration completed: a built thing made not to depict a mind but to *be* a candidate for one. And then the only test that matters is the one Harari proposes for telling the real from the fictional — "just ask yourself, 'Can it suffer?'"[^21] The cyborg's artwork would be built precisely to be asked that question. Its subject is consciousness because the work is itself an experiment in consciousness, and the gallery is where the cyborg goes to find out whether mind can be manufactured — or, what is the same question turned around, whether *it* was.

## Engineering embraces ontology

Now the three answers can be seen for what they are. They were never three. To work in living substance, by cultivation, on the question of mind, is not to make three separate decisions about a work of art; it is to describe one act from three sides. The material that is incipiently alive, the process that grows rather than builds, and the subject that is consciousness itself converge on a single object — a self-organizing system that might, in the end, be aware. Ascott compresses the whole convergence into four words: "Engineering will embrace ontology."[^22] Making becomes being. The artwork stops standing for something and starts, simply, to be something — and the something it tries to be is alive.

This is why the cyborg's art completes rather than abandons the tradition it comes from. Burnham's claim was that the simulation of life was sculpture's oldest goal all along, that the drive "to bring inert matter to life" had only ever lacked its instrument, and that "the machine ... remained the only means by which man would eventually reconstruct intelligent life."[^23] The cyborg is that instrument arrived. Where we made images of the gods — Pygmalion carving a Galatea he could only pray to see breathe — the cyborg attempts the gods' own act, and reaches, in Kurzweil's frankly theological phrase, the point at which technology might "infuse the universe with spirit."[^24] The companion to this essay shows a cyborg refusing its nature to make the one mortal, uncopyable mark; this one shows a cyborg fulfilling its nature to make the one living, self-authoring system. They are the two horns of the same inversion — the being made of pattern reaching, in the first case, for irreversibility, and in the second, for life. The vault sets them side by side deliberately; neither author below wrote of a cyborg painter or a cyborg gardener, and the pairing is the vault's own.

## Coda: what to build the room around

For a curator, this argument changes what counts as the advanced work and what counts as the merely decorative. The instinct is to represent the post-singularity sensibility with screens — the generative, the networked, the spectacular. But by this argument those are the cyborg's idiom, not its art; they show it a tamer mirror of its native condition. The work that would actually provoke such a viewer is the work that crosses Ascott's moist threshold: the bio-art piece that grows and decays on its plinth across the run of the show, the artificial-life system left to evolve without an author, the cultured tissue kept alive by the gallery's own infrastructure — art that is a system rather than an image of one, and that one could, without quite joking, ask whether it suffers. Build the room around that question. Let the wall text put Harari's test to each piece in turn, and let the visitor decide where, along the scale from a thermostat to a person, each living work falls.

The honest caution is the same one that gives the argument its edge. The cyborg's danger is exactly Harari's decoupling: it may grow something dazzlingly intelligent and altogether empty, and have no way to know. Its art is therefore a wager and never a proof — every grown mind a Galatea who may or may not have woken. But that is what makes it the only art adequate to such a maker. A being that cannot be sure there is anyone home in itself would make, as its most serious work, a thing it hopes is alive — and would stand before it asking the one question it most needs answered and least can settle. The cyborg would not paint life. It would try to grow one, and then wait to see whether it could be moved by what it had made.

## A note on method

This is an openly speculative essay, and its confidence is layered. Every quotation is `Directly stated` by its author and grounds a premise the argument needs: that the boundaries between organism and machine and between physical and nonphysical have broken down, and that the machine is an aspect of the cyborg's embodiment (Haraway); that the post-singularity body is an engineered, increasingly nonbiological material (Kurzweil); that the art-substrate of such a being is "moist" — bits, atoms, neurons, and genes at once (Ascott); that there is no qualitative difference between living and nonliving matter, so artificially created organisms may possess consciousness (Burnham); that embodiment can be destroyed but not replicated, keeping the material genuinely material (Hayles); that art has become "all process, all system," that a system manifests the characteristics of life, that the artist now designs context and cultivates rather than fabricates, and that authorship passes to the system itself (Ascott, Burnham); that consciousness is the context and content of the coming art (Ascott); that the hard problem is unresolved (O'Gieblyn via Chalmers), that intelligence is decoupling from consciousness (Harari), and that whether a nonbiological entity is conscious is the fundamental philosophical and not a scientific question (Kurzweil); that the reality test is the capacity to suffer (Harari); and that sculpture's oldest goal was the literal simulation of life, completed only by the machine (Burnham).

The load-bearing move — that for a post-singularity cyborg the three questions of material, process, and subject *converge* into the single act of growing a living, possibly conscious other, and that this completes the Faustian goal Burnham diagnosed — is the essay's own `Interpretive synthesis`, rated **medium**. It is built strictly on those `Directly stated` premises but applies them to a hypothetical being none of these authors describes, and it joins authors who did not cite one another: Burnham's 1968 "re-creation of life nonbiologically," Ascott's "moistmedia" and "building of sentience," Haraway's breached boundaries, and Kurzweil's "body version 2.0" are braided here by the vault, not by any historical citation among them. The reading of the artwork as a test of its own consciousness — the application of Harari's "Can it suffer?" to a made object — is a `Speculative connection`, rated **low**: it extends Harari's reality test well beyond anything he says about art. The relation between this essay and its companion (refusal versus fulfilment of the cyborg's nature) is likewise the vault's construction, flagged as such in the body.

On quote fidelity: every quotation was verified character-for-character against the raw chapters on 2026-06-17. The only normalizations are the vault conventions — curly quotation marks and apostrophes rendered straight, a source's internal double quotes nested as single, spaced en-dashes rendered as em-dashes, and the hard line-wraps of the OCR'd raw files (notably the break inside Ascott's "moistmedia" sentence) rendered as single spaces. Burnham's "no qualitative physical differences" passage reports the consensus of bio-electronics researchers rather than asserting it in his own voice; the essay treats it as Burnham's reported premise, which is how the source presents it. Chalmers's "hard problem" formulation reaches the vault through O'Gieblyn's quotation of him, and is attributed accordingly. Scope: the essay draws only the seven authors named; the material-versus-immaterial axis it stands on also runs through Han, McLuhan, Lippard, and Zuboff, deliberately set aside to keep the argument single.
