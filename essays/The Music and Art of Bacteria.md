# The Music and Art of Bacteria

*Everyone who says the post-singularity being would make art — the futurist, the systems theorist, the telematic visionary — withdraws the word at the peak of the promise. What survives the crossing is the activity, inverted: art as the manufacture of the very conditions the singularity abolishes.*

In 1997, hundreds of lecturers, students and music fans filled a concert hall at the University of Oregon to hear three pieces of music played in sequence. One was by Johann Sebastian Bach. One was by Steve Larson, a professor of music at the university. And one was by EMI — Experiments in Musical Intelligence — a computer program written by the composer David Cope to imitate Bach's style. Larson had proposed the showdown himself, and he was confident of the outcome; as Yuval Noah Harari tells the story in *Homo Deus*, "Larson was convinced people would easily tell the difference between soulful human compositions, and the lifeless artefact of a machine."[^1] Then the vote was taken.

> The result? The audience thought that EMI's piece was genuine Bach, that Bach's piece was composed by Larson, and that Larson's piece was produced by a computer.[^2]

It was not a fluke of one evening. At an earlier festival performance in Santa Cruz, listeners who did not know the chorales were machine-made "praised the wonderful performance, and explained excitedly how the music touched their innermost being. They didn't know it was composed by EMI rather than Bach, and when the truth was revealed, some reacted with glum silence, while others shouted in anger."[^3] The critics regrouped around the last available redoubt — the music was "too accurate," it "has no soul" — and Harari records what happened to that objection under blind conditions: "when people heard EMI's compositions without being informed of their provenance, they frequently praised them precisely for their soulfulness and emotional resonance."[^4] Cope's later program Annie abandoned even the predetermined rules that made EMI legible as a clever puppet. Built on machine learning, "its musical style constantly changes and develops in reaction to new inputs from the outside world. Cope has no idea what Annie is going to compose next." Annie also writes haiku, two thousand of which Cope published interleaved anonymously with human poems, with a standing dare: "If you think you can tell the difference between human creativity and machine output, you are welcome to test your claim."[^5]

Notice what this episode does and does not settle. Whether a machine can produce objects that function as art for human audiences is no longer an open question; it was closed in a concert hall three decades ago, and the audience's fury is the receipt. What the fury reveals is stranger and more useful: the listeners were not reacting to anything *in* the music, because nothing in the music changed between the applause and the anger. They were reacting to the loss of a maker — a mortal, inward someone whose innermost being could touch theirs. The value, it turns out, had never been stored in the notes. It was stored in a relation between finite beings, and the revelation of the program's authorship did not degrade the object; it deleted the relation.

This is the right place to begin asking whether a post-singularity being — the fully upgraded successor creature that Ray Kurzweil calls simply "still human," that transhumanists call posthuman, that popular imagination calls the cyborg — would make art. The question looks like a question about capacities, and capacities are the one thing the being has by definition. Asked that way, it answers itself and teaches nothing. The Oregon vote relocates it. Art, whatever else it is, seems to live less in produced objects than in the conditions surrounding them — mortality, inwardness, opacity, an audience of others — and the singularity is, by its own advertisement, a bonfire of exactly those conditions. The honest question is not whether such a being *could* make art. It is whether anything called art survives translation into a world without scarcity, without death, without secrets, and — perhaps — without anyone home. On this question the corpus of thinkers who built and diagnosed the posthuman idea turns out to disagree with itself in an unusually instructive way: the loudest yes ever written concedes the case's hardest point in its own pages, and the philosophers who say no keep finding, at the bottom of their pessimism, something that looks like art's survival under another name.

* * *

The loudest yes belongs to Kurzweil, and it deserves to be taken seriously at full length, because *The Singularity Is Near* (2005) is not merely optimistic that post-singularity beings will make art. It builds art into the definition of the technology, the metaphysics, and the destination — and it does so more thoroughly than any of Kurzweil's critics usually acknowledge.

Start with the definition. The Singularity, for Kurzweil, is "a future period during which the pace of technological change will be so rapid, its impact so deep, that human life will be irreversibly transformed" — an epoch that "will transform the concepts that we rely on to give meaning to our lives, from our business models to the cycle of human life, including death itself."[^6] Meaning-concepts, note, are in the definition from the first sentence: what the Singularity transforms is not hardware but significance. And when he specifies what would count as machine intelligence reaching "human levels," the specification is aesthetic before it is logical: "By — human levels" I include all the diverse and subtle ways humans are intelligent, including musical and artistic aptitude, creativity, physical motion through the world, and understanding and responding appropriately to emotions."[^7] An intelligence without artistic aptitude would simply not qualify as the thing whose arrival he is predicting. Nor is aesthetic response an accessory bolted onto cognition. In his account of reverse-engineering the brain, it is the summit:

> The most complex capability of the human brain—what I would regard as its cutting edge—is our emotional intelligence. Sitting uneasily at the top of our brain's complex and interconnected hierarchy is our ability to perceive and respond appropriately to emotion, to interact in social situations, to have a moral sense, to get the joke, and to respond emotionally to art and music, among other high-level functions.[^8]

Being moved by music is, for the great prophet of machine intelligence, the last and hardest thing a machine will learn — the "cutting edge" of the very organ he proposes to copy. So when he imagines the copied and amplified mind, he imagines it more artistic than we are, not less. "We marvel at Einstein's ability to conjure up the theory of general relativity from a thought experiment or Beethoven's ability to imagine symphonies that he could never hear," he writes. "But these instances of human thought at its best are rare and fleeting... Our future primarily nonbiological selves will be vastly more intelligent and so will exhibit these finer qualities of human thought to a far greater degree."[^9] The enhanced artist will not even pay the old neurological tuition for mastery, the skull's zero-sum economy in which "although Einstein played music he was not a world-class musician. Picasso did not write great poetry... As we re-create the human brain, we will not be limited in our ability to develop each skill. We will not have to compromise 'one area to enhance another."[^10]

What would this amplified art-making look like? Kurzweil is unusually concrete. He names two new art forms outright, in the flat idiom of a careers counselor: "'Virtual-reality environment designer' will be a new job description and a new art form," and — for the streamed sensoria of "experience beamers" who "send the entire flow of their sensory experiences as well as the neurological correlates of their emotional reactions out onto the Web" — "virtual-experience design another new art form."[^11] The body itself becomes a medium: version 3.0 incorporates fabrication into the flesh, so that "we'll be able to rapidly alter our physical manifestation at will."[^12] Swarms of linked nanobots called foglets "can control sound and light to form variable sounds and images," a sculptural material that is image, instrument and structure at once.[^13] And he has practiced what he predicts. At a technology conference in 2001, wired with magnetic sensors, Kurzweil performed as Ramona, a photorealistic young woman who followed his movements and sang in his transformed voice — "I—Ramona—performed Jefferson Airplane's 'White Rabbit,' as well as an original song" — while his teenage daughter's dance moves animated a male backup dancer. His report on the experience is not an engineer's: "The experience was a profound and moving one for me... I experienced the emotional force—and not just the intellectual idea—of transforming myself into someone else."[^14] It is as close as the book comes to an artwork of its own, and its subject — identity worn and exchanged as costume — is the book's obsession in miniature.

Beneath the gadgets lies the metaphysics, and here the argument becomes genuinely interesting, because Kurzweil's whole ontology turns out to be an aesthetic theory. He calls himself "a 'patternist,' someone who views patterns of information as the fundamental reality," and illustrates the doctrine with his body's own turnover: "the particles composing my brain and body change within weeks, but there is a continuity to the patterns that these particles make."[^15] And when he needs to show a reader what it means for pattern to outrank matter — the claim on which uploading, immortality and the entire singularitarian program depend — the example he reaches for is not a brain. It is a painting:

> Random strokes on a canvas are just paint. But when arranged in just the right way, they transcend the material stuff and become art. Random notes are just sounds. Sequenced in an "inspired" way, we have music. A pile of components is just an inventory. Ordered in an innovative manner, and perhaps with the addition of some software (another pattern), we have the "magic" (transcendence) of technology.[^16]

Art is the worked example of his metaphysics — the everyday proof that pattern transcends substrate, which is exactly what a mind must do to survive being copied out of its skull. This is worth pausing on, because it means the relationship between the singularity and art is not that of a rich civilization to its hobbies. The singularity *is* the patternist thesis enacted at world scale; art is the thesis's oldest demonstration. When Kurzweil describes the destiny of intelligence — "the 'dumb' matter and mechanisms of the universe will be transformed into exquisitely sublime forms of intelligence"; a civilization that "will then overcome gravity... and engineer the universe it wants. This is the goal of the Singularity"[^17] — the vocabulary is not accidentally aesthetic. The terminal project of the post-singularity civilization, reorganizing all available matter into deepening pattern, is structurally a work of art at cosmic scale, and Kurzweil, asked what remains of human activity when machines do everything routine, answers accordingly: "The role of work will be to create knowledge of all kinds, from music and art to math and science. The role of play will be, well, to create knowledge, so there won't be a clear distinction between work and play."[^18] A society of nothing but artists, working in the only medium left — pattern — on a project whose finish line is the universe.

So the maximal yes is not a hedge or an aside; it is the book. And at its climax, in the mouth of a post-singularity intelligence named George 2048, it becomes explicit. When Molly, the dialogue's resident 2004-era human, objects that "we humans also do things like create art and music. That kind of separates us from other animals," George answers: "Indeed, Molly, that is fundamentally what the Singularity is about. The Singularity is the sweetest music, the deepest art, the most beautiful mathematics...."[^19]

Four lines later, Kurzweil's own book takes the case apart.

## The experiment had already been run

Here is the strange thing about treating the question as futurology: the experiment has already been conducted, with human subjects, and its records are published. Between 1966 and 1972 a generation of artists tried, deliberately and on principle, to make art shed its material body — to exist as idea, instruction, system, gas, radio wave, thought. The attempt is documented year by year in Lucy Lippard's *Six Years: The Dematerialization of the Art Object from 1966 to 1972*, and its exhibitions read today like field tests of the post-singularity condition, run on canvas-era hardware. In 1969 Lippard organized a show at the Paula Cooper Gallery in New York

> in which the symptoms of dematerialization were well advanced: an (apparently) "empty" room contained Haacke's *Air Currents* (a small fan), Barry's invisible *Magnetic Field*, Weiner's *Minute Pit In The Wall From One Air-Rifle Shot*, Wilson's "Oral Communication," a "secret" by Kaltenbach, a small black blip painted on the wall by Richard Artschwager, Huot's "existing shadows," and a tiny cable wire piece by Andre on the floor. The smallest room was, by contrast, crammed with printed matter—photo, text, xerox, and otherwise shrunken art.[^20]

Sol LeWitt had supplied the program two years earlier — "The idea becomes a machine that makes the art"[^21] — and by 1969 had radicalized it into a full license for immaterial making: "Ideas alone can be works of art; they are in a chain of development that may eventually find some form. All ideas need not be made physical."[^22] Douglas Huebler supplied the ethic of a world already full: "The world is full of objects, more or less interesting; I do not wish to add any more. I prefer, simply, to state the existence of things in terms of time and/or place."[^23] Lawrence Weiner supplied the ontology, a standing declaration attached to all his work — the artist may construct the piece; the piece may be fabricated; "The piece need not be built" — three states of the work, each "equal and consistent with the intent of the artist."[^24] And the dealer-impresario Seth Siegelaub, who staged an exhibition that existed only as a catalogue, drew the consequence that now reads like a specification of digital ontology written twenty years early:

> But when art does not any longer depend upon its physical presence, when it has become an abstraction, it is not distorted and altered by its representation in books and catalogues. It becomes primary information, while the reproduction of conventional art in books or catalogues is necessarily secondary information. For example, a photograph of a painting is different from a painting, but a photograph of a photograph is just a photograph, or the setting of a line of type is just a line of type.[^25]

An art whose reproduction is not a copy but the thing itself: this is precisely the property that defines every digital artifact, discovered inside the gallery system in 1969. Robert Barry went furthest toward pure substrate-independence — releasing measured volumes of inert gases into the desert air, exhibiting the carrier wave of a radio station "not [as a] means of transmitting information, but rather as an object," attempting during one exhibition "to communicate telepathically a work of art, the nature of which is a series of thoughts that are not applicable to language or image."[^26] His stated aesthetic could serve as the motto of the whole movement's far pole: "Just emptiness. Nothing seems to me the most potent thing in the world."[^27] Around these gestures grew a shared taste that Lippard catalogued precisely — "information and systems replaced traditional formal concerns of composition, color, technique, and physical presence... Lists, diagrams, measurements, neutral descriptions, and much counting," a "fascination with huge numbers," with permutations and dictionaries.[^28] Enumeration, permutation, measurement, neutrality: reading the inventory now, it is hard not to notice that when human artists tried to make art out of pure information, the pleasures they discovered were the native pleasures of computation.

While the artists dematerialized the object, a critic in their midst was reading the whole history of sculpture as a longer version of the same trajectory — and naming its destination with a word that would not become common currency for decades. Jack Burnham's *Beyond Modern Sculpture*, published in 1968, argues that sculpture's ancient business of making lifelike figures was never really about representation. "The machine, he intuitively realized, as unsubtle and inefficient as it was, remained the only means by which man would eventually reconstruct intelligent life, or what might be called life-bearing artifacts," Burnham writes of the modern sculptor's turn away from the figure; "sculpture seeks its own obliteration by moving toward integration with the intelligent life forms it has always imitated."[^29] In the book's second chapter he states what he calls "unquestionably one of the central ideas of this book": that in biology, machine theory and sculpture alike, "the meaning of organicism for all three disciplines has already begun to converge toward a single end result—the understanding of living matter through its creation."[^30] Art, science and engineering converge on one activity: understanding life by building it. And then, in a book about sculpture, seventeen years before Donna Haraway's cyborg and thirty-seven before Kurzweil's Singularity, comes this:

> Yet there is the possibility that an irreversible technology, one that destroys organic life and substitutes for it very sophisticated forms of synthetic life, is part of an unseen plan. If so, one might have a few premonitions of the part being played by sculpture in shaping our destination as a post-human species.[^31]

What part? Burnham's answer is the most audacious theory of art's social function in the entire literature. "Art, then, and the whole image-making drive may be means for preparing man for physical and mental changes which he will in time make upon himself. Sculpture, functioning so, becomes a kind of psychical radar signal preparing the human race thousands (or now perhaps only scores) of years in advance."[^32] On this account the millennia of carved bodies were not decoration; they were a species rehearsing, in stone, the self-re-creation it would eventually perform in flesh and silicon. And the rehearsal's final act would collapse the distinction between the artwork and the being: "It is only a step from here to suppose that in time an aesthetics of artificial intelligence will evolve," he predicts — "a series of art forms that manifest true intelligence, but perhaps more meaningfully, with a capacity for reciprocal relationships with human beings (in this case the word viewer seems quite antiquated)."[^33] Since "there are no qualitative physical differences between living and nonliving matter," it follows that "organisms artificially created may possess consciousness" — and therefore that "the possibility of a work of art actually attaining some form of consciousness is an incredible but not unlikely eventuality."[^34] The posthuman, in Burnham's teleology, does not make art. It *is* the art — sculpture's last work, the statue that finally answers back.

These two currents — the dematerializers and the systems theorist — were not parallel strangers. In the autumn of 1970 they physically converged, in an exhibition Burnham himself organized at the Jewish Museum in New York, recorded in Lippard's chronicle with her bibliographer's dryness: "*Software*. Jewish Museum, New York, September 16–November 8, 1970. Organized by Jack Burnham... Among participants: Acconci, Antin, Baldessari, Barry, Burgy, Denes, Giorno, Goodyear, Haacke, Huebler, Kosuth, Kaprow, Levine, Weiner."[^35] The theorist of living sculpture curating the artists of pure information, in a show named for the immaterial half of the machine: for one season, the rehearsal had a single stage.

And one participant kept rehearsing for another thirty years. Roy Ascott — an English artist who had absorbed cybernetics in the early 1960s, and whose four decades of essays are collected in *Telematic Embrace* — had already proposed in 1966 that the artwork could be "a self-organising system, an organism, as it were, which derives its initial programme or code from the artist's creative activity and then evolves its specific artistic identity and function in response to the environments it encounters," and that "[t]he interaction of man and computer in some creative endeavour, involving the heightening of imaginative thought, is to be expected."[^36] Across the following decades he built the aesthetic theory the question is asking for, piece by piece and in its own vocabulary. The criterion: "Classical aesthetics dealt with the behaviour of forms; the new aesthetic deals with forms of behaviour."[^37] The values: "The emerging new order of art is that of interactivity, of 'dispersed authorship'; the canon is one of contingency and uncertainty" — an art "no longer about appearance, and certainly not about representation, but... concerned with apparition, the coming into being of what has never before been seen or heard or experienced."[^38] The form: not the object but the interface, "a doorway to undecidability"; at planetary scale, the *Gesamtdatenwerk* — "echoing the Gesamtkunstwerk, or 'total artwork,' conceived by Richard Wagner. Whereas Wagner's Gesamtkunstwerk was performed in an opera house, however, the site of the Gesamtdatenwerk must be the planet as a whole, its data space, its electronic noosphere."[^39] He tested it in practice: *La Plissure du Texte* (1983) used a global timesharing network to let a "virtual community of co-authors" write "a planetary fairy tale... non-linear, indeterminate, branching, and unforeseeable at every point," running night and day for three weeks.[^40] The subject matter: "the primacy of consciousness as both the context and content of art, and the object and subject of study."[^41] The material: "Between the dry world of virtuality and the wet world of biology lies a moist domain... Moistmedia (comprising bits, atoms, neurons, and genes in every kind of combination) will constitute the substrate of the art of our new century."[^42] The method: "seeding replaces designing."[^43] Even the ontology of the work dissolves into probability — "Art does not reside in the artwork alone, nor in the activity of the artist alone, but is understood as a field of psychic probability... in which the viewer is actively involved," artist and viewer alike, in the Umberto Eco phrase Ascott borrows, "gambling on the possibility of semiosis."[^44] And in 1996, in a lecture on the museum's future, Ascott wrote the single most direct answer to the research question that exists in this literature:

> This emergent hyperconnectivity is no longer just between human minds but between our artificial agents, indeed between artificial life forms whose adaptive complexity can be expected to evolve into an artificial consciousness. It may well be that artistic process may be initiated by artificial agents and auto-constructed entities; they might become artistic identities, whether in silicon or in the moist terrain of artificial life as it integrates with our own wet biology.[^45]

Set the rehearsal's findings in a row and something remarkable emerges: every sub-question the posthuman question raises was answered, in the affirmative and in detail, by working artists and critics between 1966 and 2000. Aesthetic interests? Behavior over appearance, contingency and uncertainty, pattern, enumeration, emptiness, apparition. Subject matter? Consciousness itself, the man-machine boundary ("much significant anthropomorphic sculpture does not imitate man, but imitates robots trying to become human," Burnham observed[^46]), the planet as a single system. Materials and methods? Information, energy, instruction, living substrate, moistmedia; blueprint plus replaceable parts, "an organic parallel, of course, is the genetic encoding of hereditary traits";[^47] seeding over designing. Forms? Systems, networks, interfaces, datapools, probability fields, sentient architecture, autonomous organisms wandering the streets. Role of art? World-building — "Art is a form of world building, of mind construction, of self-creation," as Ascott put it[^48] — and the psychical radar of a species redesigning itself. Role of the artist? "[T]he 'artist' becomes a complex and widely distributed system, in which both human and artificial cognition and perception play their part."[^49] The question did not have to wait for the posthuman to arrive. The art world answered it in advance — which is exactly what Burnham's radar theory says art is for.

## What the experiment found

But *Six Years* is not a manifesto; it is a lab notebook, and it records the experiment's outcome as scrupulously as its design. The outcome was a double failure — and both failures bear directly on any being whose existence is premised on information's independence from matter.

The first verdict: matter took the art back. Lippard concedes it on the book's opening pages — "it has often been pointed out to me that dematerialization is an inaccurate term, that a piece of paper or a photograph is as much an object, or as 'material,' as a ton of lead. Granted."[^50] The concession had been forced on her almost immediately: in March 1968, weeks after her dematerialization essay appeared, the Art-Language group in Coventry wrote to point out that idea-art's documents

> may not be an art-object as we know it in its traditional matter-state, but they are nevertheless matter in one of its forms, either solid-state, gas-state, liquid-state. [...] The map is just as much a solid-state object (i.e., paper with ink lines upon it) as is any Rubens (stretcher-canvas with paint upon it).[^51]

Robert Smithson, from inside the movement, refused the premise altogether: "My work is impure; it is clogged with matter... There is no escape from matter. There is no escape from the physical nor is there any escape from the mind. The two are in a constant collision course."[^52] By 1970 even the movement's participants were writing its epitaph in advance: "There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality. To deny it is to descend to irony."[^53] Three decades later, N. Katherine Hayles would give this artists' intuition its full theoretical form in *How We Became Posthuman*: information "must always be instantiated in a medium" — the apparent weightlessness of the digital is an illusion produced by well-functioning infrastructure, not an ontological achievement.[^54] The artists got there first, by the empirical route: they tried to lift the pattern off the substrate, and the substrate came with it.

The second verdict: the market took the art back. This is the finding Lippard delivers against her own earlier hopes, and she structures the book so the reader watches the hope fail in real time. In 1969: "The people who buy a work of art they can't hang up or have in their garden are less interested in possession. They are patrons rather than collectors."[^55] Four years later, in the Postface:

> It seemed in 1969... that no one, not even a public greedy for novelty, would actually pay money, or much of it, for a xerox sheet referring to an event past or never directly perceived... it seemed that these artists would therefore be forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status and market-orientation. Three years later, the major conceptualists are selling work for substantial sums here and in Europe; they are represented by (and still more unexpected—showing in) the world's most prestigious galleries. Clearly, whatever minor revolutions in communication have been achieved by the process of dematerializing the object..., art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries.[^56]

Her final summation, written in 1995, compresses the whole arc into three sentences that should be printed above the desk of anyone forecasting art's liberation by technology: "The escape was temporary. Art was recaptured and sent back to its white cell, but parole is always a possibility."[^57]

The lesson generalizes with uncomfortable ease. Art's confinement — to objecthood, to commodity status, to institutions — was never a property of its substrate, so changing the substrate changed nothing structural. An art of pure information proved just as ownable, just as marketable, just as recuperable as bronze. Whatever value-system organizes a society will organize its art; the dematerialized work simply found new ways to be property. A post-singularity civilization would not escape this by being made of information — information is precisely what its economy would run on. Kurzweil says as much himself, without appearing to notice the deflation: in his post-singularity economy, where "the value of virtually all products... will be almost entirely in their information," art appears under the heading of intellectual property, its "profound struggle" being the protection of information rights, with the arts' business models requiring "as much creativity as the creation of the IP itself."[^58] The sweetest music, the deepest art — licensed, watermarked, and litigated. Lippard's verdict, translated forward: substrate-independence is not freedom; it is a change of cage.

## The word, withdrawn

So far the ledger reads: a maximal yes from the futurist, a detailed affirmative rehearsal from the art world, two structural warnings from the rehearsal's own records. The deeper pattern only appears when one looks at where each affirmation is at its strongest — because at exactly those points, with an almost eerie consistency, the affirmers themselves withdraw the word "art."

Kurzweil first. The "sweetest music, deepest art" declaration is the peak of his book's closing dialogue, and it survives for precisely four lines:

> MOLLY 2004: I see, so the music and art of the Singularity will be to my era's music and art as circa 2004 music and art are to ... NED LUDD: The music and art of bacteria.
>
> MOLLY 2004: Well, I've seen some artistic mold patterns. NED: Yes, but I'm sure you didn't revere them. MOLLY 2004: No, actually, I wiped them away. NED: Okay, my point then.[^59]

Kurzweil wrote this exchange. He chose to let Ned Ludd — his staged Luddite, usually a foil to be corrected — have the last word, and the word is devastating twice over. First: if the gap between post-singularity art and ours is the gap between our art and the aesthetic patterns of mold, then post-singularity art is illegible to humans *in principle* — not difficult, not avant-garde, but categorically unavailable, the way a symphony is unavailable to a bacterium. Kurzweil had already run the parable in the other direction, in a dialogue where a "futurist bacterium" two billion years ago promises its skeptical friend that future supercellular societies will create "music," and the friend can only answer: "Music?" — "Yes, patterns of sound." — "Sound?"[^60] Every confident sentence in this essay's second movement — Beethoven-moments amplified, virtual-experience design, the engineered universe — is, by its own author's parable, a bacterium's guess about symphonies. And second, more quietly: the one empirical datum the exchange contains about how a higher intelligence treats the art of a lower one is Molly's. She did not revere the mold patterns. She wiped them away.

Nor can Kurzweil secure the thing that would make post-singularity art *expression* rather than output. On machine consciousness his book is, to its credit, honest to the point of self-injury: "There exists no objective test that can conclusively determine its presence." Future machines "will be able to make other humans laugh and cry. And they'll get mad if others don't accept their claims. But this is fundamentally a political and psychological prediction, not a philosophical argument."[^61] Note what this concedes: the acceptance of posthuman art as art will be secured *rhetorically* — by the machines' skill at moving us — which is to say, by exactly the mechanism the Oregon audience found so enraging when EMI used it. And his universal creativity dissolves art's boundary at the moment it universalizes the activity: when work is knowledge-creation, play is knowledge-creation, and "there won't be a clear distinction between work and play,"[^62] the category *art* has no edges left — everything is art, which is one of history's more reliable ways of meaning nothing is.

Marshall McLuhan, who imagined "the technological simulation of consciousness" as the "final phase of the extensions of man" in *Understanding Media* in 1964, had already formalized that last worry as a law. Art, he wrote, needs a boundary to stand out against: "in a native society there is no true art because everybody is engaged in making art. Art and games need rules, conventions, and spectators. They must stand forth from the over-all situation as models of it in order for the quality of play to persist."[^63] Kurzweil's post-singularity society — every being a knowledge-artist, all activity creation — is the ultimate native society. McLuhan also closed the other exit, the one marked "autonomous machine artist": "a conscious computer would still be one that was an extension of our consciousness... as a ventriloquist's dummy is an extension of the ventriloquist."[^64] Either the machine's art is ours at one remove, in which case nothing posthuman has happened; or it is genuinely other, in which case we are back with the bacteria, wiping the mold away. And McLuhan had his own affirmation to withdraw from: his book's closing chapter declares that automation "would seem to be a fate that calls men to the role of artist in society," and its final sentences end on "self-employment and artistic autonomy" as the latent pattern of the automated world[^125] — a universal artisthood that his own law of the native society, applied one step further, dissolves on arrival.

Burnham's withdrawals are the most poignant, because his yes was the most detailed. At the very peak of his prediction — the moment the systems he championed achieve organic properties — his prose flinches into scare quotes and a hedge: such systems "will lead to 'sculpture'—if it can be called that—rivaling the attributes of intelligent life."[^65] A page-scale version of the same flinch structures his whole conclusion: "Would this be art as we have come to know it, or would it be the culmination of what futurologists term the Faustian urge, the grand illusion of a society convinced of its own scientific omnipotence?"[^66] His radar theory, followed to its end, consumes itself — a signal that prepares the species for its transformation has no function once the transformation arrives: "As we are leaving the stage where totems and votive images have profound psychic import for our culture, is it possible that eventually sculpture will cease to have meaning?"[^67] In the stabilized, fully scientific future he imagines, "the need for totemic artifacts would have disappeared as a psychic task"; art returns only if civilization collapses back into a "pre-scientific state."[^68] He had already stated the general law with aphoristic finality in his chapter on kinetic art: "The real way to kill an art movement is for it to realize its goals."[^69] Applied to his own teleology — sculpture's goal being the creation of living intelligence — the achieved posthuman is the death of sculpture, by success. And in 1970, in the interview Lippard preserved in *Six Years*, he said the quiet part aloud. Asked whether art was dissolving into nothingness: "No, it's dissolving into comprehension. The reason art exists in the first place is that it's a mystery."[^70] A being defined by total comprehension has not outgrown art the way an adult outgrows toys; it has dissolved the condition under which art was possible at all. Two years earlier Burnham had defined art as "what we do when we expend great time, care, and patience on an activity without knowing why"[^71] — a definition that quietly requires *not knowing why*. The final paragraph of his book turns the knife all the way around: "we may reinterpret the desire to transcend ourselves physically and intellectually through formal systems as merely a large-scale death wish... In the end, art may be simply the blueprint by which we seek such wisdom."[^72]

Even Ascott — the most sustained yes in the literature, the man who forecast silicon artists — pauses, mid-manifesto, to withdraw the noun. "Telematics can certainly sustain art," he told an audience in 1993, "and as I hope to show you, can extend it into unprecedented forms. But whether art, in the sense in which we have chosen to define it over the past hundred or so years (I'm thinking of romanticism and modernism, of course), is capable of sustaining itself into the twenty-first century, with or without technology, is quite another matter. My feeling is that it probably is not capable of doing so, and that we can well do without it."[^73] His new canon comes with a matching disclaimer: "no antecedent criteria can be applied to network creativity, since there is no previous canon to accommodate it."[^74]

Four witnesses, four ideologies, no coordination — Kurzweil never read Burnham; Ascott and McLuhan argue from opposite premises — and one convergent behavior: each, at the summit of his own affirmation, declines to promise that what survives the crossing will be *art*. The pattern is the finding. The question "would a posthuman make art?" keeps receiving the same strange answer from the people most committed to yes: *it would make something; the something may be greater than art; and the name will probably not come with it.*

## An inventory of abolished conditions

The withdrawal is not superstition. There is a principle underneath it, and the philosophers who never mention the singularity state it most precisely — because, on their account, the condition it names is not coming; it is here, and can be examined.

Byung-Chul Han's *The Transparency Society* opens by defining the state toward which the fully networked, fully calculable being tends: "Matters prove transparent when they shed all negativity, when they are smoothed out and leveled, when they do not resist being integrated into smooth streams of capital, communication, and information."[^75] Transparency, for Han, is not honesty; it is the removal of everything in a thing that resists calculation — its opacity, its interiority, its resistance, what he calls its negativity. And the being that achieves this state completely has, on his analysis, crossed a line: "Only machines are transparent. Eventfulness and freedom, which constitute life fundamentally, do not admit transparency."[^76] The relevance to a self-transparent, fully self-knowing posthuman is direct, because Han's entire aesthetics is built on the claim that beauty lives on the far side of that line. "Transparency is not the medium of the beautiful," he writes, and grounds the claim in Walter Benjamin:

> The beautiful is neither the veil nor the veiled object but rather the object in its veil. Unveiled, however, it would prove to be infinitely inconspicuous [unscheinbar]. . . . For that object, to which in the last instance the veil is essential, is not to be characterized otherwise. Since only the beautiful and outside it nothing—veiling or being veiled—can be essential, the divine ground of the being of beauty lies in the secret.[^77]

No veil, no beauty: not as moralism but as structure. Beauty also needs time of a particular shape — "The momentary gleam of the spectacle, immediate stimulation, is not beautiful, but rather the quiet afterglow, the phosphorescence of time... Transparency does not phosphoresce"[^78] — and creation needs a capacity that pure capability lacks. "There are two forms of potency," Han writes in *The Burnout Society*. "Positive potency is the power to do something. Negative potency, in contrast, is the power not to do."[^79] The computational being is the perfection of positive potency and the abolition of negative potency: "Machines cannot pause. Despite its enormous capacity for calculation, the computer is stupid insofar as it lacks the ability to delay... It is a machine of positivity [Positivmaschine]."[^80] What issues from a being that cannot pause is not creation but production, and Han's sentence on the difference reads, two decades on, like a review of generative models: "A purely hectic rush produces nothing new. It reproduces and accelerates what is already available."[^81] The genuinely new, when Han allows for it, is born from endured boredom — Benjamin's "dream bird that hatches the egg of experience" — and issues in luxury, in movement that escapes the achievement-principle. His example is dance, and he stakes an explicit species-claim on it: "Only human beings can dance."[^82]

Harari arrives at the same inventory from the other side — not from a theory of beauty but from the engineering roadmap. Item one: mortality. "A large part of our artistic creativity, our political commitment and our religious piety is fuelled by the fear of death," he writes; once people believe they can escape death, "the desire for life will refuse to go on pulling the rickety wagon of art, ideology and religion."[^83] The immortality project defunds art in the act of succeeding. Item two: the beholder. Harari's history of aesthetics is a history of where the authority over beauty resides — in the Middle Ages, outside the human ("The hands of painters, poets, composers and architects were supposedly moved by muses, angels and the Holy Spirit"); after the humanist revolution, inside human feeling, where "beauty is in the eye of the beholder."[^84] The third regime he sees coming closes the loop: "Once developed, such an algorithm could replace the voter, the customer and the beholder. Then the algorithm will know best, the algorithm will always be right, and beauty will be in the calculations of the algorithm."[^85] Aesthetic authority returns to an external, superhuman seat — the structural position of the medieval God, now occupied by recommendation infrastructure. Item three: the self that would express itself. Harari's sciences dissolve it twice. The self was always, on his account, an internal artist — "the narrating self" that "is forever busy spinning yarns about the past and making plans for the future," that "takes many short cuts" like "every journalist, poet and politician," that "goes over our experiences with a sharp pair of scissors and a thick black marker" and "files in the archive a story with a happy ending."[^86] Every human being, in this picture, already contains a working fiction-writer; selfhood *is* its output. But the same sciences that reveal the narrating self also demote it: "humans are not individuals – they are 'dividuals', i.e. humans are an assemblage of many different algorithms lacking a single inner voice or a single self."[^87] And under Dataism, the successor value-system Harari forecasts, the residual motive for making anything is neither expression nor beauty: "a human can write a poem about his experience and post it online, thereby enriching the global data-processing system. That makes his bits count... value lies not in having experiences, but in turning these experiences into free-flowing data."[^88] The poem as metabolic tribute. Should even that function lapse, the terminal position is already named: "people devoid of any economic, political or even artistic value."[^89]

If Han supplies the theory and Harari the trajectory, Shoshana Zuboff supplies the field report, because the nearest existing thing to a post-singularity intelligence — a planetary, sensate, learning apparatus — has already been built, and *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* spends seven hundred pages characterizing how it knows. Her name for its epistemology is the exact negation of the aesthetic attitude:

> Thanks to Big Other's capabilities, instrumentarian power reduces human experience to measurable observable behavior while remaining steadfastly indifferent to the meaning of that experience. I call this new way of knowing radical indifference. It is a form of observation without witness...[^90]

Observation without witness: total perception, zero regard. What such a system discards is precisely the layer art is made of and made for: "Big Other poaches our behavior for surplus and leaves behind all the meaning lodged in our bodies, our brains, and our beating hearts, not unlike the monstrous slaughter of elephants for ivory... You are not the product; you are the abandoned carcass."[^91] Where earlier powers needed art — totalitarianism worked on souls, and Stalin toasted the writers who engineered them — instrumentarianism replaces "the engineering of souls with the engineering of behavior" and therefore neither persecutes artists nor enlists them.[^92] It simply does not notice them, the way Molly did not notice the mold until she cleaned. Meanwhile the aesthetic categories migrate into the apparatus itself, and its own engineers reach, unprompted, for the vocabulary of composition: "We are learning how to write the music, and then we let the music make them dance."[^93] The finished work this composer aims at is the tuned population — "less a society than a population that ebbs and flows in perfect frictionless confluence"[^94] — and its one aesthetic judgment is stated by Zuboff with Han-like finality: "Friction is the only evil."[^95] Against all this she plants the human difference in the exact soil Han and Harari also chose: the sanctuary, the opaque interior. Its loss "rob[s] us of the life-sustaining inwardness, born in sanctuary, that finally distinguishes us from the machines. This is the well from which we draw the capacities to promise and to love"; without the freedom to will into an uncertain future, "there can be no subjects and no projects: only objects."[^96] Art is a project — a promise flung at an open future — and Meghan O'Gieblyn closes the inventory with the transhumanists' own concession about the being they are building toward, in the sentence this literature keeps circling back to: "it's very possible that our new, digital selves will entirely lack subjective experience, the phenomenon we most often associate with words like 'spirit' and 'soul.' Our resurrected forms might behave much like our current selves, but nothing will be going on inside."[^97] Nick Bostrom, the philosopher most responsible for formalizing superintelligence, is blunter still, warning that "we cannot blithely assume" a superintelligence will share "a taste for refined culture or for the simple pleasures in life."[^98]

Read as one list, the inventory is total. Mortality: engineered out. Opacity, secrecy, the veil: engineered out. Latency and the pause: engineered out. The bounded expressive self: dissolved into dividuals and data-flows. The witnessing audience of others: dissolved into total participation or radical indifference. Interiority: undecidable at best, absent at worst. Mystery — Burnham's raison d'être of art — dissolved into comprehension. For the post-singularity being *as specified by its own prophets*, the answer to the research question is not that it would decline to make art. It is that art's every precondition appears on the singularity's list of scheduled demolitions. The being as advertised has nothing to make art from, no self to make it with, and no one to make it for.

## The beings that actually arrive

The answer would end there, if the being as advertised could exist. The corpus's deepest counterargument is that it cannot — and that the posthumans who do arrive, the real ones, are already making art of a very particular kind.

The demolition of the advertisement is Hayles's life work. The posthuman, she insists, is not a species but "a point of view," whose first assumption "privileges informational pattern over material instantiation, so that embodiment in a biological substrate is seen as an accident of history rather than an inevitability of life."[^99] Against that assumption she sets what may be the corpus's hardest sentence: "Information, like humanity, cannot exist apart from the embodiment that brings it into being as a material entity in the world; and embodiment is always instantiated, local, and specific. Embodiment can be destroyed, but it cannot be replicated."[^100] Disembodiment, she notes elsewhere, is "a state no presence in the world can achieve" — so the pure-information posthuman of the prospectus is not a possible being but a fantasy about one, and she names the fantasy precisely: "What is lethal is not the posthuman as such but the grafting of the posthuman onto a liberal humanist view of the self," Moravec's uploaded "you" being not a new creature but the old sovereign subject "expanding its perogatives into the realm of the posthuman."[^101] Her famous fork follows: "If my nightmare is a culture inhabited by posthumans who regard their bodies as fashion accessories rather than the ground of being, my dream is a version of the posthuman that... recognizes and celebrates finitude as a condition of human being."[^102] Note what the fork does to the research question. The nightmare posthuman — the one this essay's inventory just disqualified from art — cannot exist. The dream posthuman — embodied, finite, distributed — retains every condition on the inventory: mortality, locality, noise, limit. Which posthuman was the question about? If the impossible one, the answer is a null set. If the possible one, the being was never severed from art's preconditions in the first place.

Fiction, the laboratory where these beings can actually be observed, keeps returning one result. Hayles dwells on Richard Powers's *Galatea 2.2*, in which a neural net named Helen — "as close to an informational being as one can imagine" — is trained on the human archive and then sits a literary Turing test, glossing Caliban's "the isle is full of noises." Her reading, next to the human contestant's "more or less brilliant" deconstruction, is four sentences long: "You are the ones who can hear airs. Who can be frightened or encouraged. You can hold things and break them and fix them. I never felt at home here. This is an awful place to be dropped down halfway."[^103] Then, having been given the full human record — war, genocide, child abuse — "she says simply, 'I don't want to play anymore.' She disappears, effectively committing suicide."[^104] The informational being's one aesthetic act is a lucid description of its own exile, followed by refusal. And in Philip K. Dick's *Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?*, read closely in Hayles's ninth chapter, the traffic runs the other way: the android Luba Luft is "an escaped android who was also a superb opera singer," and it is precisely her killing that cracks the bounty hunter's certainty — after it, Deckard "expressed regret at Luba's death," feeling the empathy that his world's official ontology reserves for humans.[^105] The made being's art does exactly what art does: it forces the audience to suspect a subject where the category system said there was none. Fiction cannot settle whether a machine artist is possible. But it records, with precision, what is at stake in the question — that art is where the boundary between beings gets tested, from both sides.

Haraway's cyborg completes the demolition, because the question's protagonist is, almost word for word, her declared enemy. The cyborg of *A Cyborg Manifesto* (1985) "has no origin story in the Western sense — a 'final' irony since the cyborg is also the awful apocalyptic telos of the 'West's' escalating dominations of abstract individuation, an ultimate self untied at last from all dependency, a man in space."[^106] The dependency-free ascended intelligence — the question's hero — is for Haraway the terminal fantasy of the very tradition the cyborg exists to subvert. Her actual cyborg is not coming; it is here ("By the late twentieth century... we are all chimeras"), and it is half-made of art by definition: "a creature of social reality as well as a creature of fiction."[^107] A being partly constituted by fiction sustains itself by fiction-making, and Haraway's account of that making is the corpus's most unsentimental theory of posthuman art: "Cyborg writing is about the power to survive, not on the basis of original innocence, but on the basis of seizing the tools to mark the world that marked them as other."[^108] Art as survival equipment, with an aesthetic program to match — "cyborg politics insist on noise and advocate pollution, rejoicing in the illegitimate fusions of animal and machine," a permanent "struggle against perfect communication, against the one code that translates all meaning perfectly."[^109] Set that beside Kurzweil and the reversal is complete: where the singularity dreams of lossless translation — pattern moved between substrates without remainder — Haraway's cyborg locates art precisely in the loss, the noise, the untranslatable residue. Perfect communication is not art's fulfillment. It is art's death, wearing art's clothes.

And when one looks at what artists actually do inside the most posthuman environment yet built, it is Haraway's program, not Kurzweil's, that describes them. Zuboff's seventeenth chapter is the corpus's only survey of practicing artists, and their shared medium is concealment: a generation "called to create the art and science of hiding." Adam Harvey reverse-engineers "computer vision algorithms" to defeat them with camouflage; his *Stealth Wear* line of silver-plated garments, which deflect thermal imaging, is "inspired by traditional Islamic dress, which expresses the idea that 'garments can provide a separation between man and God.' Now he redirects that meaning to create garments that separate human experience from the powers that surveil."[^110] Fabric that once veiled the human from the divine gaze, re-cut to veil it from the machine gaze: Han's entire aesthetics, sewn. Around Harvey, Zuboff catalogues resin masks, metric-erasing browser extensions, anti-drone hoodies, Trevor Paglen's AI performances, Ai Weiwei's surveillance installation — and delivers a double verdict. "Our artists, like our young people, are canaries in the coal mine," she writes; but "like every counter-declaration, hiding risks becomes an adaptation when it should be a rallying point for outrage... Tunnels under this wall are not enough. This wall must come down."[^111] Han, surveying the same condition in *Psychopolitics*, assigns the artist the same station under a different name: the idiot, who "by nature... is unallied, un-networked, and uninformed," who "erects spaces for guarding silence," a modern heretic living "from the faraway - like a stylite."[^112] His one concrete image of future practice is children in the ruins, profaning the old value-system by play: "playing with them and tearing them to shreds. Perhaps these children anticipated a future we will share: the world lies in rubble; in the ruins, we are playing too - tearing apart paper money."[^113] And on this practice he stakes everything: "The course our future takes will depend on whether we prove able, beyond the world of production, to make use of the useless."[^114]

Put the observations side by side and the pattern is unmistakable. Wherever the posthuman condition actually obtains, art does not disappear — it *inverts*. Its historic work was presence: making images, filling space, adding pattern to the world. Its work under posthuman conditions is negativity: hiding, veiling, silencing, refusing, jamming, profaning. The dematerializers made emptiness potent; Barry's "Nothing seems to me the most potent thing in the world" turns out to have been the rehearsal's deepest finding. Art's role in a posthuman society, on the actual evidence, is to manufacture the abolished conditions — to counter-engineer opacity, latency, and uselessness inside a system that produces transparency, speed, and function without limit.

Which reopens the speculative question one last time, from an unexpected angle. If art's posthuman function is the deliberate production of negativity, then a genuine post-singularity being could make art — but only by an act that cuts against its own advertisement: engineering limits into itself. Self-imposed opacity. Deliberate forgetting. Chosen latency, chosen locality, chosen finitude. The corpus quietly supports the possibility. Han himself defines form as constraint embraced — "All forms are slow. Each form is a detour"[^115] — and a being that builds detours into its own omnipotence is, by that definition, making form. Hayles's dream posthuman "recognizes and celebrates finitude" not as a defect but as a condition. Even Kurzweil's post-singularity culture rests, at bottom, on a chosen limitation: "Information lasts only so long as someone cares about it," he writes; civilization survives only if its members "continually rediscover, reinterpret, and reformat the legacy of culture" — care being precisely the unforced narrowing of infinite attention onto one perishable thing.[^116] Whether what such a being made should be called art is the question the sources genuinely leave open — Burnham withholding the word at his own conclusion, Han gesturing past the impasse toward "a mode of existence that still has no name: an unwritten future."[^117] The honest report is that the activity survives every scenario the corpus can construct, and the name survives none of them intact.

* * *

There is one more inversion, and it is the one the question least expects: the traffic between art and the singularity has, so far, run entirely the other way. The singularity did not produce an art. Art produced the singularity.

O'Gieblyn's *God, Human, Animal, Machine* is, among other things, a reader's autobiography of that production. When Kurzweil's prophecy captured her — a Bible-school student who had just lost her faith — it did not capture her with evidence: "Like all classic works of revelation, Kurzweil's narrative unfolded with the kind of elegant simplicity that is easily mistaken for truth."[^118] She carried the book "everywhere, in the bottom of my backpack," granting it "a totemic power. It seemed to me a secret gospel."[^119] What worked on her were the instruments of literature — narrative arc, eschatological form, the promise "to restore through science the transcendent—and essentially religious—hopes that science itself obliterated."[^120] The genealogy runs deeper than rhetoric. The very word for the condition was coined inside a poem, at the exact point where language failed: Dante, needing to describe a body passing beyond humanity in the *Paradiso*, "is forced to make up an entirely new word, transumanar," rendered into English in 1814 as "Words may not tell of that transhuman change."[^121] The posthuman entered the language as a poet's confession that it could not be described — seven centuries before it became an engineering schedule. Even Kurzweil's own faith has an artwork at its root: his grandfather, given the chance to handle Leonardo's manuscripts, "described the experience with reverence, as if he had touched the work of God himself. This, then, was the religion that I was raised with: veneration for human creativity and the power of ideas."[^122] The Singularity is that religion's cathedral — a work of eschatology whose medium is the future, and whose prophet, pressed by O'Gieblyn in an email exchange about the theological echoes, conceded the whole point in a sentence: "we could not get through our lives without having to choose metaphors for transcendent questions."[^123]

This is what Burnham's psychical radar looks like from inside: the image-making drive running ahead of the species, preparing it for "physical and mental changes which he will in time make upon himself." The carved figures, the automata, the dematerialized gestures, the telematic embraces, the singularity narratives themselves — one long transmission, sent forward. Art is not waiting at the far side of the singularity to be made by the being that arrives. Art is the practice that imagined the being, named it, rehearsed its condition, and is now — in the camouflage studios and the spaces of guarded silence — negotiating the terms of its arrival.

That the being would make is not, in the end, in doubt — making is the one activity every author in this literature grants it, from Kurzweil's universal knowledge-creation to Han's grim tuning of populations. Whether any of the making would be *art* depends on a single question the prophets cannot answer and the philosophers will not concede: whether the being can still lack something. Whatever crosses the singularity carrying no death, no secret, no pause, no other, will produce output of unimaginable sophistication — the sweetest music, the deepest patterns — and there may be no one there, on either side of the work, for whom it means anything. Whatever crosses carrying its finitude — chosen now, engineered even, but real — will still be doing the thing the mold was doing on Molly's wall: leaving a pattern that says, to any witness capable of being touched, *something lived here*. The first future has no use for the word art. The second never stopped needing it. Between those two futures the question stays open, exactly where the literature leaves it — and its last word belongs to the historian of the first escape attempt, who watched art shed its body for six years and get hauled back, and who wrote the sentence that covers bacteria, conceptualists, and cyborgs alike: the escape was temporary; parole is always a possibility.[^124]

## A note on method

This essay set out to test a question rather than to illustrate an answer: whether a post-singularity, posthuman being would make art — and, if so, with what aesthetics, subjects, materials, forms, and social role for art and artist. The initial expectation, frankly, was a qualified yes with a catalogue attached. The evidence reshaped it twice. First, the sources converged — from opposed ideological positions and without citing one another — on the same structural point: art's preconditions (mortality, opacity, latency, a bounded self, a witnessing other, mystery) are the singularity's stated demolition targets, and the corpus's own affirmers (Kurzweil, Burnham, Ascott — and McLuhan, whose automation chapter affirms universal artisthood while his own laws void it) each withdraw or forfeit the word "art" at the peak of their affirmations. That convergence, not any single verdict, became the essay's spine. Second, the historical record (Lippard's *Six Years*) and the contemporary record (Zuboff's chapter on the surveillance-era vanguard) turned out to answer the "role of art" question empirically rather than speculatively: where posthuman conditions actually obtain, art inverts from the production of presence to the production of negativity. The essay's closing position — the activity survives every scenario; the name survives none of them intact; the singularity is itself an artwork before it is a prediction — was arrived at from that evidence, and it complicates rather than confirms the question's premise.

Which connections rest on citation and which are constructed: the chains inside each author are documented in their texts. The cross-author architecture is this essay's own — Kurzweil never read Burnham; Han never mentions Kurzweil; Haraway and Ascott do not engage each other; Hayles discusses Moravec but none of the corpus's other principals. Three constructions bear the most weight and are flagged in the notes where they occur: the reading of the 1966–72 dematerialization movement as a "rehearsal" of the posthuman condition (the term "control experiment" is this essay's, though Burnham's radar theory and Ascott's arc are primary-text claims made at the time); the "inventory of abolished conditions," which assembles Han, Harari, Zuboff, and O'Gieblyn into a single checklist none of them wrote; and the closing inversion (art produced the singularity), which extends O'Gieblyn's documented genealogy and Burnham's stated teleology beyond what either says outright. Han's application to machine intelligence is an inference throughout: he diagnoses a society, not an AI. One genuine dispute is left standing rather than resolved: whether engineered, chosen finitude would recover art or merely simulate it — Burnham's withheld word against Han's "unwritten future."

Attribution and speaker cautions: the "burden of physicality" statement carries a speaker ambiguity recorded inside *Six Years* itself (Huebler by the 1970 chronicle's layout; adjoining sentences credited to Bochner in Lippard's retrospective essay) and is quoted here unattributed. Several load-bearing quotations are reported speech inside a corpus text and are labelled as such in the notes: Benjamin via Han; Bostrom, Dante/Cary, and Kurzweil's email via O'Gieblyn; Eco's "gambling" phrase via Ascott; Harvey's phrases and the "write the music" developer via Zuboff; Helen and Luba Luft (raw spelling "Loft") via Hayles. The Oregon concert and Santa Cruz episodes are Harari's account, not primary reportage.

Verification and normalization: every quotation was drawn from the raw chapter files of the corpus and checked character-for-character against them; the standard normalizations apply (curly→straight quotes, OCR hard line-wraps joined with single spaces, sentence-initial case changes marked with brackets). The Lippard and McLuhan raw files are lossy OCR; where a quoted word required emendation beyond those normalizations (Huebler's "any me," Barry's "telepat ly," Smithson's "the ical"/"fse," the Burnham interview's "in the first p," and Burnham's "artfor"/"ope"), the emendation is flagged in the corresponding note and the affected words carry reduced confidence. Scope: all the primary sources in play were harvested for evidence; Ascott's psi/field-theory essays and Kurzweil's engineering chapters are drawn on only lightly, and Hayles's middle chapters (the Macy Conferences, *Limbo*) and Harari's chapters on humanism's political forms were consulted and set aside as bearing on adjacent questions. No sources outside the corpus are cited; the one external fact load-bearing enough to matter (the date and setting of the Larson showdown) is taken from Harari's text as written.
