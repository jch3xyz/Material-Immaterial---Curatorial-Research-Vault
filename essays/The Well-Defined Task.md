# The Well-Defined Task

In the autumn of 1998 — three years after the first Java applets, a decade before the smartphone, a quarter-century before anyone would speak to a computer and have it answer in paragraphs — Lev Manovich sat down to describe a new way the world had begun to appear. The novel and then cinema, he wrote, had made narrative "the key form of cultural expression of the modern age"; now "the computer age introduces its correlate — database."[^1] The claim was deliberately large. Manovich was not observing that computers store data in tables, a fact too dull to require an essay. He was borrowing Erwin Panofsky's term for linear perspective — a *symbolic form*, in which, as Panofsky put it, quoting Cassirer, "spiritual meaning is attached to a concrete, material sign and intrinsically given to this sign"[^2] — and proposing that the database had become one: "a new way to structure our experience of ourselves and of the world."[^3]

His evidence was the way things had started to feel. The museum had become "a database of images representing its holdings, which can be accessed in different ways: chronologically, by country, or by artist," so that "the narrative becomes just one method of accessing data among others."[^4] The biography had become a collection of clips one could traverse in any order. The web page, "as defined by original HTML," was "a sequential list of separate elements" to which "it is always possible to add a new element"; and because a collection can always be extended without changing its logic, the web had an "anti-narrative logic": "if new elements are being added over time, the result is a collection, not a story."[^5] What defined the form, for Manovich, was not the storage medium but the set of gestures it invited. Database objects "appear as a collections of items on which the user can perform various operations: view, navigate, search."[^6] A form is diagnosed by its verbs.

It is worth dwelling on how carefully Manovich built the theory, because the argument that follows depends on taking his machinery seriously enough to run it a second time. He offered three tests for a symbolic form, and applied all three. The first was the operation-set just named — the form defined "from the point of view of user's experience."[^7] The second was that a form is "a different model of what a world is like": narrative, an architectural plan, and a database each propose a distinct account of what there is.[^8] The third, and the theoretical heart of the essay, was a claim about where such forms come from. New media, Manovich wrote, exhibits "the projection of the ontology of a computer onto culture itself":

> If in physics the world is made of atoms and in genetics it is made of genes, computer programming encapsulates the world according to its own logic. The world is reduced to two kinds of software objects which are complementary to each other: data structures and algorithms. Any process or task is reduced to an algorithm... And any object in the world — be it the population of a city, or the weather over the course of a century, a chair, a human brain — is modeled as a data structure.[^9]

Two halves, then: data structures and algorithms, "two halves of the ontology of the world according to a computer,"[^10] projected outward until they become "the logic of culture at large."[^11] The database was culture's take-up of the first half. The second half — the algorithm, the executed procedure — Manovich assigned to a different cultural form. Computer games, he argued, are "ruled by another logic — that of an algorithm"; the player "executes an algorithm in order to win," and "narratives, including computer games, correspond to the algorithms."[^12] So the essay's famous antagonism between database and narrative was really a contest between the two halves of the machine's own ontology, dressed in cultural clothes. "As a cultural form, database represents the world as a list of items and it refuses to order this list. In contrast, a narrative creates a cause-and-effect trajectory of seemingly unordered items (events). Therefore, database and narrative are natural enemies."[^13]

And Manovich was clear about which enemy was winning. "In the database / narrative pair, database is the unmarked term," he wrote. "Regardless of whether new media objects present themselves as linear narratives, interactive narratives, databases, or something else, underneath, on the level of material organization, they are all databases."[^14] Whatever the surface, the substrate is a collection; narrative is a path traced through it, "just a set of links," while "the elements themselves remain stored in the database."[^15] This is the sentence to hold onto, because it is the one an argument about any *successor* to the database must eventually confront. If a third form arrives, Manovich has already told us where to look for the database it is only wearing.

The corollary the present decade seems to offer is easy to state, and it is the hypothesis this essay was written to test. Run Manovich's three diagnostics again, now, and they appear to output a third term. The operations have changed: one no longer only views, navigates, and searches, but asks, instructs, delegates, observes, interrupts, corrects, converses. The world-model has changed: it has become common to describe not only artificial-intelligence systems but corporations, markets, "capitalism" itself as goal-directed agents — entities with aims, behaviors, capacities, and degrees of autonomy. And the computer's projected ontology seems to have changed with the machine: the dominant object inside the computer is no longer the table or the algorithm-in-isolation but the trained model wrapped in a run loop, a thing that pursues a goal. If the database was culture's projection of the data structure, the *agent* looks like culture's projection of the two halves fused and set in motion. There is even a name the industry has given the period: "the agentic era."[^16]

If that were the whole of it, the essay could end here, having extended a good idea by one term. But the corollary breaks the moment one checks the dates.

## The chronology runs backwards

A succession requires a sequence. Manovich's database succeeded narrative in the sense that the novel and cinema came first and the CD-ROM came after; the whole force of "the computer age introduces its correlate" lies in the *introduces*. For the agent to succeed the database in the same sense, its apparatus — the concept, the ethics, the characteristic verbs — should postdate the database's cultural maturity. It does not. It predates it, in every particular, by decades.

The concept came first. In September 1984, in *Scientific American*, Alan Kay traced the software agent to its origin: "The idea of an agent originated with John McCarthy in the mid-1950's, and the term was coined by Oliver G. Selfridge a few years later, when they were both at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. They had in view a system that, when given a goal, could carry out the details of the appropriate computer operations and could ask for and receive advice, offered in human terms, when it was stuck."[^17] "Given a goal... carry out the details... ask for and receive advice" — this is, almost to the word, the definition the industry would circulate as news seventy years later. Kay called such a thing "a 'soft robot' living and doing its business within the computer's world," and in the same 1984 article he printed, on facing pages, both forms the present is supposed to be choosing between: the agent as forecast, and, as present tense, a "DATA-BASE BROWSER" that "allows one to gain access to a hierarchically organized data base by simply pointing to items in successive lists."[^18] View, navigate, search — Manovich's exact operations — were the illustration; the agent was the caption's dream. The forms were never sequential. They were drawn on the same spread.

The ethics came first, too. The characteristic problem the hypothesis assigns to the agent-form — alignment, the danger that a system will pursue the goal we specified rather than the goal we meant — was stated whole in 1960, by Norbert Wiener, in a passage that reads today like a document from the future:

> If we use, to achieve our purposes, a mechanical agency with whose operation we cannot efficiently interfere once we have started it, because the action is so fast and irrevocable that we have not the data to intervene before the action is complete, then we had better be quite sure that the purpose put into the machine is the purpose which we really desire and not merely a colorful imitation of it.[^19]

Every element of what is now called the alignment problem is here — irreversibility, the speed asymmetry, the gap between the specified purpose and the intended one — and Wiener's word for the machine is *a mechanical agency*, agency-vocabulary carrying no mind-vocabulary at all, offered in a passage whose surrounding examples are the sorcerer's apprentice, the genie of the *Arabian Nights*, and the monkey's paw: "the agencies of magic are literal-minded."[^20] When Stuart Russell, six decades later, gave the field its watchword — "a failure of value alignment"[^21] — he did so by quoting this exact sentence of Wiener's, ellipses and all.[^22] The problem was not born with the form. It was inherited from a text older than the database's cultural reign.

Even the formal definition came first. In 1995 — three years before Manovich sat down to name the database as a symbolic form — Michael Wooldridge and Nicholas Jennings published the canonical account of the software agent, listing its four properties (autonomy, social ability, reactivity, and "pro-activeness: agents... are able to exhibit goal-directed behaviour by taking the initiative") and distinguishing this "weak" notion from a "stronger" one in which an agent is described "using mentalistic notions, such as knowledge, belief, intention, and obligation."[^23] They also lodged a warning that reads now as prophecy: unless the concept were disciplined, "'agent' might become a 'noise' term, subject to both abuse and misuse, to the potential confusion of the research community."[^24] The theory of the agent was fully articulated, and its inflation already feared, while the database was still being christened.

So the plainest form of the hypothesis is simply false as history. The agent did not arrive after the database; its concept, its ethics, and its definition were all in print before Manovich named the form it is supposed to succeed. What is genuinely new is not the idea but its scale — the deployment, around the middle of the 2020s, of goal-pursuing conversational systems to hundreds of millions of people, and the industry's decision to brand this "a new class of agentic experiences" arriving in "the agentic era."[^25] But scale is exactly what the primary documents of that deployment supply, and exactly what a symbolic form is not reducible to. When one turns to those documents, they turn out to be, almost entirely, the marketing and engineering literature of the firms selling the product. Anthropic's own definition is representative: "Agents... are systems where LLMs dynamically direct their own processes and tool usage," which "begin their work with either a command from, or interactive discussion with, the human user," and then "plan and operate independently, potentially returning to the human for further information or judgement."[^26] Google announces that its models "can understand more about the world around you, think multiple steps ahead, and take action on your behalf, with your supervision."[^27] OpenAI: "Agents are systems that independently accomplish tasks on your behalf."[^28] These are precise and useful descriptions. They are also a product category describing itself, in the register Wooldridge and Jennings warned would become a noise term — which is to say, the affirmative evidence for the "new symbolic form" consists largely of the press releases of the companies that would profit if we believed in it.

There is a subtler difficulty, and it is Manovich's own. The hypothesis extends a succession Manovich never asserted. His word for the database's relation to narrative was not *successor* but "correlate"; his forms were "natural enemies... competing for the same territory," coexisting rivals rather than epochs.[^29] And when he turned to the history directly, he forbade the dramatic reading the corollary indulges:

> The history of culture does not contain such sudden breaks. Similarly, we should not expect that new media would completely substitute narrative by database. New media does not radically break with the past; rather, it distributes weight differently between the categories which hold culture together, foregrounding what was in the background, and vice versa.[^30]

He routed all such change through Fredric Jameson's account of periodization, which he quoted at length: "Radical breaks between periods do not generally involve complete changes but rather the restructuration of a certain number of elements already given: features that in an earlier period... were subordinate became dominant, and features that had been dominant again become secondary."[^31] And he de-periodized his own forms entirely, calling database and narrative "two competing imaginations, two basic creative impulses, two essential responses to the world," both of which "have existed long before modern media" — the ancient Greeks who produced both Homer and encyclopedias, Diderot who wrote novels and edited the *Encyclopédie*.[^32] He even named the temptation the corollary gives in to: "It is tempting," he wrote, "to read the history of this competition in dramatic terms."[^33]

To extend Manovich by adding a third epoch is therefore to extend precisely the part of Manovich he retracted. The disciplined question is not whether the agent is a new epoch after the database. It is whether, within the frame Manovich actually built — coexisting impulses, a see-saw of dominance, no clean breaks — something has genuinely tilted. That question has an answer, and it is yes. But arriving at it honestly means first going back much further than 1998, because the succession-of-forms story the corollary tells is itself a very old story, and the person who told it first told it as a warning.

## The genre is older than any of its terms

The idea that cultural forms succeed one another — that a new technology of communication displaces an older way of making the world mean — did not begin with Manovich, and neither did the melancholy that attends it. In 1936, in "The Storyteller," Walter Benjamin watched an older form die and named its killer. "The art of storytelling is coming to an end," he wrote; "it is as if something that seemed inalienable to us, the securest among our possessions, were taken from us: the ability to exchange experiences."[^34] The cause was not fashion. "Experience has fallen in value," he wrote, and the culprit was a rival form: "with the full control of the middle class, which has the press as one of its most important instruments in fully developed capitalism, there emerges a form of communication... This new form of communication is information."[^35]

What Benjamin says about information should stop anyone who thinks the discrete, self-sufficient, plausibility-checked record is a novelty of the database age. Information, he wrote, has its own regime of value, distinct from the story's:

> The intelligence that came from afar... possessed an authority which gave it validity, even when it was not subject to verification. Information, however, lays claim to prompt verifiability. The prime requirement is that it appear "understandable in itself." Often it is no more exact than the intelligence of earlier centuries was. But while the latter was inclined to borrow from the miraculous, it is indispensable for information to sound plausible.[^36]

"Indispensable for information to sound plausible" — often "no more exact" than what it replaced, but self-contained, immediately verifiable, and above all convincing. It is difficult to read this in the present decade without thinking of the fluent, plausible, and unreliable output of a language model, which is precisely a machine for producing text that "sounds plausible" and is "understandable in itself." Benjamin even fixed the difference between information and story in their temporalities: "The value of information does not survive the moment in which it was new. It lives only at that moment... A story is different. It does not expend itself. It preserves and concentrates its strength and is capable of releasing it even after a long time."[^37] The database, from this angle, is not the successor of information but its engineering: an attempt to keep information from expiring, to store and re-serve the moment-bound record indefinitely.

And Benjamin, in 1939, ran the very succession that Manovich would run sixty years later — with an extra term already added on the far end. "The replacement of the older narration by information, of information by sensation," he wrote, "reflects the increasing atrophy of experience."[^38] Narration → information → sensation: a three-term periodization of communicative forms, complete, in print, before the transistor. When Manovich added *database* and the present adds *agent*, they are not discovering a pattern but continuing a genre — and it is worth noticing what kind of genre. Benjamin's series is not a parade of neutral symbolic forms but a single degenerative process, each form indexed to a loss. His succession comes with a thesis about what is being subtracted at every step, which is the transmissibility of experience itself.

Benjamin also supplies the discipline the corollary lacks, because he was scrupulous about the tempo of formal change in a way the "agentic era" is not. "One must imagine the transformation of epic forms," he wrote, "occurring in rhythms comparable to those of the change that has come over the earth's surface in the course of thousands of centuries. Hardly any other forms of human communication have taken shape more slowly, been lost more slowly."[^39] Forms move at geological speed; a form's "rise" is the late meeting of an ancient impulse with newly favorable conditions. On that model the agent is not new at all — the animate servant, the golem, the oracle, the genie, the counsel-giving elder are old — and what would be new is only a dominance and a technical carrier, never an invention. This is the same discipline Manovich imposed with his "two competing imaginations... long before modern media," and it cuts against decade-scaled succession from both ends of the corpus.

Most important, Benjamin denied that a superseded form dies. It sediments into its successor and goes on working. "'And they lived happily ever after,' says the fairy tale," he wrote. "The fairy tale, which to this day is the first tutor of children because it was once the first tutor of mankind, secretly lives on in the story."[^40] The predecessor survives inside the successor as a live tutor, not a fossil. This is exactly Manovich's unmarked term seen from the other side of a century: narrative does not vanish beneath the database; it persists as the path one traces, "secretly lives on" as the trajectory through the collection. And it is the standing objection to any claim that the agent has replaced the database, or the database narrative. The forms do not take turns. They nest.

There is one more thing Benjamin gives, and it reframes the whole question of what the agent's characteristic problem is. If the database's problem is organization — how to order a collection that "refuses to order" itself — Benjamin states with total clarity what the *story's* problem was: counsel. "In every case the storyteller is a man who has counsel for his readers," he wrote, and counsel is not information. "Counsel is less an answer to a question than a proposal concerning the continuation of a story which is just unfolding... Counsel woven into the fabric of real life is wisdom."[^41] The art of storytelling ends, on Benjamin's account, precisely because "the communicability of experience is decreasing," so that "we have no counsel either for ourselves or for others."[^42] Hold this beside the agent-form's advertised operation. To *ask* an agent, to receive its answer, to be advised by it — this is a return of the counsel-relation, the oldest scene of address to a wiser interlocutor. But it returns, if Benjamin is right, into a world that has lost the shared, unfolding story that made counsel possible in the first place. Whether a system trained to produce plausible answers can give counsel in Benjamin's sense — a proposal about the continuation of a life it shares — or can only give information wearing counsel's face, is not a technical question. It is the question the agent-form poses, and Benjamin posed it first.

## The task changes sides

If neither the clean-succession story nor its outright dismissal will do, what is left is Manovich's own frame — a restructuration in which a subordinate element becomes dominant — and the question of whether such a tilt has actually occurred. It has, and Manovich's essay contains the pivot on which it turns. In the one passage where goal-directedness enters "Database as Symbolic Form," the goal belongs unmistakably to the human:

> In a game, the player is given a well-defined task — winning the match, being first in a race, reaching the last level, or reaching the highest score. It is this task which makes the player experience the game as a narrative. Everything which happens to her in a game, all the characters and objects she encounters either take her closer to achieving the goal or further away from it.[^43]

Read it closely. In 1998 the well-defined task is *given to the player*; the goal is the human's, and it is the human's possession of a goal that re-narrativizes an otherwise arbitrary collection, converting a database into an experience with stakes and direction. The machine's side of the interface, in Manovich's scheme, is pure algorithm — "executed," "discovered," a procedure the player runs or reconstructs, never a thing that wants anything. Even the era's canonical account of engagement kept desire on the human side: Manovich quotes the game designer Will Wright describing play as "a continuous loop between the user (viewing the outcomes and inputting decisions) and the computer (calculating outcomes and displaying them back to the user)," in which "the user is trying to build a mental model of the computer model."[^44] The user models the machine; the machine calculates. The wanting is ours.

The whole of the shift the present decade registers can be stated as a single migration: *the well-defined task has crossed to the other side of the interface.* The clearest statement of the crossing is not in a product announcement but in Yuval Noah Harari's *Homo Deus*, in a passage that narrates it as a trajectory with the agent as its named middle term:

> At first sight it seems that the Waze algorithm serves us only as an oracle. We ask a question, the oracle replies, but it is up to us to make a decision. If the oracle wins our trust, however, the next logical step is to turn it into an agent. We give the algorithm only a final aim, and it acts to realise that aim without our supervision.[^45]

There is the hinge, in Harari's own words: from *oracle* — "we ask a question, the oracle replies, but it is up to us to make a decision" — to *agent*, where "we give the algorithm only a final aim, and it acts to realise that aim without our supervision." The task that Manovich's player held has been handed across. And the industry's self-descriptions, whatever their motives, describe exactly this handoff in the operational register: agents that, once "the task is clear," "plan and operate independently, potentially returning to the human,"[^46] that "accomplish tasks on your behalf."[^47] The verbs on the user's side change accordingly, and they are the verbs of a principal to a delegate — ask, instruct, delegate, observe, interrupt, correct — because the human now holds the goal only at the moment of specifying it, and thereafter supervises its pursuit by something else. Harari extends the trajectory one stage further, to *sovereign*, where the agent "may start manipulating us, shaping our desires and making our decisions for us," so that "Waze must think for us";[^48] and to a world of agents transacting with one another, "my Cortana contacting your Cortana," negotiating on their masters' behalf.[^49] Oracle, agent, sovereign — a periodization of delegation, run at the level of a single product rather than a civilization, but describing the same migration of the task.

Manovich's own theory predicts this, through a door he left open and marked. His two-part ontology — data structures and algorithms — was explicitly scoped, in a footnote, to *procedural* programming, and in the same footnote he named its successor inside computer science: "In a object-oriented programming paradigm, represented by such computer languages as Java and C++, algorithms and data structures are modeled together as objects."[^50] The fusion of the two halves — data and behavior bundled into one entity that holds its own state and pursues its own procedures — was already, in 1998, the reigning paradigm inside the machine. What the agent does is project that fusion onto culture at the scale Manovich reserved for symbolic forms: an object with a data structure (a model compressed from a corpus), an algorithm (an inference loop), a goal, and enough autonomy to pursue it. If "the logic of a computer... becomes the logic of culture at large,"[^51] and if the computer's dominant internal object is now the goal-running model rather than the table, then Manovich's own engine forecasts an agent-form. The subordinate half of his ontology — the algorithm, the executed procedure, the side he assigned to narrative and games — has become dominant, and has changed sides of the screen. That is a Jamesonian restructuration, stated in Manovich's terms, and it is real.

What it is *not* is a replacement. The database does not vanish under the agent; it descends beneath it, exactly as narrative descended beneath the database. Manovich's unmarked-term argument holds with full force one level down: underneath the conversational surface, on the level of material organization, the agent is a database — a corpus compressed into weights, addressed through what Oracle was already selling in 1998 as "natural-language queries."[^52] The agent is the database become, in the phrase with which Manovich ended his essay, "dynamic and subjective." He closed "Database as Symbolic Form" by holding up Dziga Vertov's *Man with a Movie Camera* as the one work that had solved the problem the rest of new media had not — how "to merge database and narrative... into a new form," so that "a database, this normally static and 'objective' form, becomes dynamic and subjective."[^53] He meant it as a challenge still unmet. An agent is a database that has been made to answer for itself in the first person — dynamic, subjective, and, as we will see, a narrator of its own operations. The essay that announced the database ended by asking for the thing that would demote it. The demotion has arrived. But calling the result a *new form* rather than the old form wearing a face requires answering a prior question, the one the whole corpus turns out to have been arguing about for a century and a half: whether a system that pursues a goal is thereby an agent at all.

## A goal is not an agent

The corollary's decisive slide — the one the industry documents perform in a single clause when they call a goal-pursuing program an *agent* — is the slide from a system that has a goal to a system that is an agent. The corpus is unusually unanimous that this slide is illegitimate, and it is worth assembling the witnesses, because they come from traditions that agree on almost nothing else.

Begin with the science of systems, where goal-talk is native and disciplined. Donella Meadows, whose *Thinking in Systems* is the plainest available account of how goal-directed systems behave, is emphatic that a system's purpose is not a possessed intention but an observer's deduction: "A system's function or purpose is not necessarily spoken, written, or expressed explicitly, except through the operation of the system," she writes; "the best way to deduce the system's purpose is to watch for a while to see how the system behaves... Purposes are deduced from behavior, not from rhetoric or stated goals."[^54] She marks the very distinction the corollary erases — reserving "function" for nonhuman systems and "purpose" for human ones, while noting "the distinction is not absolute" — and when she applies purpose-language to a national economy she immediately hedges it: the economy's purpose is read "judging from its behavior."[^55] Most tellingly, a system's purpose "need not be human purposes and are not necessarily those intended by any single actor within the system,"[^56] and the concept stretches so wide that she applies "goal-seeking" to radioactive decay and cooling coffee, the "goal" reached "through physical law rather than human decision" — the word in scare-quotes.[^57] Goal-directedness, in the one book in the corpus devoted to it, is a pattern an observer reads off behavior, not a will the system holds; a thermostat, a coffee cup, and an economy are all "goal-seeking" in the same deflated sense.

This deflation is not Meadows's idiosyncrasy; it is cybernetics' settled position, and it held for half a century without anyone proposing to converse with, instruct, or align the systems it described. The cybernetician Stafford Beer compressed it into a slogan — "the purpose of a system is what it does" — and the slogan's whole point is to strip purpose of intention: it "stands for bald fact, which makes a better starting point in seeking understanding than the familiar attributions of good intention."[^58] The economists supply the same lesson from the other end of the political spectrum. Adam Smith's invisible hand is grammatically an agent and analytically the denial of one: the individual "intends only his own gain, and he is in this... led by an invisible hand to promote an end which was no part of his intention."[^59] The market achieves an end that no one aimed at; it is order without an intender. Friedrich Hayek made the anti-agent point explicit, reaching for infrastructural metaphors rather than mental ones: the price system is "a kind of machinery for registering change, or a system of telecommunications," and it is "one of those formations which man has learned to use... after he had stumbled upon it without understanding it."[^60] Machinery, telecommunications, a thing stumbled upon — the twentieth century's most influential theory of the market describes it as precisely not an agent. To announce that markets are now seen "as goal-directed agents" is to reverse this, not to continue it.

The deepest version of the argument, though, comes from the biology of self-organization, and it goes further than the economists: it says the goal is not merely un-intended but not *in the system at all*. N. Katherine Hayles, reconstructing the autopoietic theory of Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela in *How We Became Posthuman*, draws out the claim exactly where the corollary needs it tested. "Information, coding, and teleology," she writes, summarizing Maturana, "are... inferences drawn by an observer rather than qualities intrinsic to autopoietic processes... they exist in the observer's 'domain of interactions' rather than in autopoiesis itself."[^61] And Maturana's own formulation, which Hayles quotes, could not be more direct: "A living system is not a goal-directed system; it is, like the nervous system, a stable state-determined and strictly deterministic system closed on itself... These modulations, however, are apparent as modulations only for the observer who beholds the organism... externally, from his own conceptual (descriptive) perspective."[^62] The goal is in the describer, not the described. Varela pushed it to the edge of polemic: "To assume... that purposes and goals are made clear by the systems themselves is all, it seems to me, nonsense."[^63] And Hayles notes the revealing labor this cost Maturana — that "realizing that he was fighting a long tradition of realist assumptions deeply embedded in everyday language, [he] developed an elaborate vocabulary as a prophylactic against having anthropomorphism creep back in."[^64] Anthropomorphism is the *default* pull of the language; seeing agents is what a mind does unless it works to stop. The agentive reading is not an achievement of perception but its resting state.

What, then, converts a goal-having system into an agent, if not the goal? The philosopher Daniel Dennett named the operation in 1971, and named it as an operation rather than a discovery. To adopt "the intentional stance" toward a system is to predict its behavior "by ascribing to the system the possession of certain information and by supposing it to be directed by certain goals"; and "it is a small step," he wrote, "to calling the information possessed the computer's beliefs, its goals and subgoals its desires."[^65] But the stance, Dennett insisted, "does not say that Intentional systems really have beliefs and desires, but that one can explain and predict their behavior by ascribing beliefs and desires to them," and — the crucial clause — "one can switch stances at will."[^66] Agency, on this account, is not a property a system has or lacks but a posture an observer adopts or drops, pragmatically, "not intrinsically right or wrong." If that is so, then what changes when "the agent" arrives is not the world's furniture but where a permanent human posture is pointed — and a posture newly aimed at a new class of objects is not a new symbolic form. It is an old habit finding fresh targets.

And the habit is very old. Meghan O'Gieblyn, in *God, Human, Animal, Machine*, grounds it below culture, in evolved perception. The anthropologist Stewart Guthrie, she writes, argues that anthropomorphism is a survival strategy: confronted with an ambiguous shape in the woods, "natural selection rewards those who... 'bet high,' guessing that the object is not only alive but human," because a person is the most consequential thing the shape could be.[^67] The same reflex, Guthrie proposes, "is what gave birth to the idea of God" — early peoples "assumed that natural events bore signs of human agency," reading earthquake and famine as a person's anger.[^68] O'Gieblyn draws the conclusion that matters here: "metaphors are two-way streets... For centuries we said we were made in God's image, when in truth we made him in ours."[^69] To see an agent in a goal-pursuing system is to run the oldest perceptual program there is — the one that populated the sky with gods — and O'Gieblyn is careful to note that modern science was founded on its *refusal*. Francis Bacon "argued that this view of the world was entirely anthropomorphic. When we imagine that nature itself has 'ends' and 'goals,' we are projecting human attributes onto inanimate objects," attributes that "have relation clearly to the nature of man rather than to the nature of the universe."[^70] The agent-reading is not the frontier of thought. It is the thing disenchantment was invented to overcome, returning.

So the goal does not make the agent. What does — and here the corpus points, with surprising precision, at a single answer — is the acquisition of a *voice*. The evidence is that agent-perception tracks not a system's capability but its interface, and specifically its capacity to conduct a conversation. Joseph Weizenbaum discovered this in 1966 with ELIZA, a program of almost no sophistication that nonetheless drew people into treating it as a person: "ELIZA shows, if nothing else, how easy it is to create and maintain the illusion of understanding," and it does so, he marveled, "with so little machinery."[^71] His own secretary, who had watched him build the program and "surely knew it to be merely a computer program," asked him to leave the room so she could talk to it in private.[^72] The attribution was triggered not by what the program could do but by the fact that it answered in the first person. O'Gieblyn confirms the pattern across the present decade — the roboticist's student who, treating a machine "as though there was more to Cog than there really was," seemed to elicit exactly that surplus; the observation, which O'Gieblyn reports as a lesson learned in the lab, that "in order for machines to become intelligent, they must first be treated as if they are intelligent."[^73] Treat a thing as an interlocutor and it becomes one, for whoever treats it so.

The oldest name for this conversion is the sharpest. Thomas Hobbes, defining personhood in *Leviathan*, located it not in inner life but in representation. A "person," for Hobbes, is anything "whose words or actions are considered, either as his own, or as representing the words or actions of an other man," and a multitude becomes one person not through any unity of its own but through a representer: "it is the Unity of the Representer, not the Unity of the Represented, that maketh the Person One."[^74] This is the engine of corporate personhood, and it is the exact analytic the agent-form needs. What makes "the assistant" one agent is not any unity in the system it fronts — a stateless inference service, multiplied across millions of simultaneous sessions, holding no continuous self — but the chat persona, the first-person voice, the representer. The conversational interface is the thing that turns a goal-running database into an agent, because it is the thing that lets the system be *addressed and answer*, which is Hobbes's whole criterion of personhood.

This is where Panofsky's test — the one Manovich borrowed to certify the database — is either met or failed, and the answer decides whether the agent is a symbolic form at all. A symbolic form requires "a concrete, material sign" to which meaning attaches: perspective had the constructed picture, the database had the searchable collection with its operations. The agent's candidate sign is the conversational turn-loop — the exchange of instruction and report, the first-person voice that speaks for the system. That is a real, concrete, experienced sign, and it satisfies Panofsky's requirement rather than evading it. But naming the sign also names the agent's composition, and the composition is the whole of the argument: the agent is a database (the corpus compressed into weights), plus a goal (the well-defined task, now on the machine's side), plus a representer (the voice). The tilt is real; the sign is real; the form, if it is one, is genuinely new only in its third term. And that third term — the first-person voice that makes the system addressable — is, we are about to see, exactly the term that the corpus's most searching writers regard not as an emergence but as a mask, and not as a gift but as a transfer.

## The hunchback in the machine

If the agent's distinctive term is the voice — the representer that lets a database be addressed and answer — then the first thing the corpus's critics say about it is that a voice is exactly what a machine can be given without becoming anyone, and that the appearance of autonomy has a long history of concealing something behind the curtain. The founding image is Walter Benjamin's, and he placed it, not accidentally, at the head of his theses on history:

> The story is told of an automaton constructed in such a way that it could play a winning game of chess, answering each move of an opponent with a countermove. A puppet in Turkish attire and with a hookah in its mouth sat before a chessboard placed on a large table. A system of mirrors created the illusion that this table was transparent from all sides. Actually, a little hunchback who was an expert chess player sat inside and guided the puppet's hand by means of strings.[^75]

Machine intelligence's first public appearance — Wolfgang von Kempelen's Turk, which toured Europe defeating challengers — was a cabinet with a person hidden inside and a "system of mirrors" engineered to make the cabinet *look empty*. The autonomy was the illusion; the mirrors were part of the apparatus; the intelligence was concealed human labor. Benjamin understood that the mirrors were the point: the machine's apparent transparency, its air of having nothing to hide, is precisely what the hidden mechanism produces. And the image recurs, almost unchanged, in the present. O'Gieblyn describes watching delivery robots negotiate a campus crosswalk — a crowd attributing courage and hesitation to them — before learning the truth: "it was impossible to know whether the bots were acting autonomously or being maneuvered remotely. The most eerily intelligent behavior I had observed in them may have been precisely what it appeared to be: evidence of human intelligence."[^76] The autonomous agent turns out to have a person inside it, or behind it, or — in the case of the systems that learn from vast quantities of human-labeled examples and human-written text — beneath it, in the sense of a foundation. Zuboff names the same structure at the scale of the product: the personal digital assistant, she writes, is "a market avatar" — Microsoft's Satya Nadella had unveiled it as "a runtime, a new interface" — and that interface, she adds, "creates the sensation of mastery while, in fact, giving it away."[^77]

But the mask is not the deepest of the corpus's objections, and to stop at "there is a person behind the curtain" is to miss what a century of critical writing was actually tracking. The deeper claim is a kind of conservation law: across the whole tradition, no canonical text grants a system agency without, in the same movement, subtracting agency from human beings. The two are not separate events. They are one event described from its two ends.

Karl Marx supplies the template. In *Capital*, value in circulation "suddenly presents itself as an independent substance, endowed with a motion of its own, passing through a life-process of its own"; it acquires "an automatically active character," it "brings forth living offspring."[^78] The system becomes the subject. But in the same analysis the human subject is correspondingly demoted: "as the conscious representative of this movement, the possessor of money becomes a capitalist," his aim absorbed as the system's aim.[^79] Byung-Chul Han, updating Marx for the data age, states the transfer with brutal economy: "individuals degrade into the genital organs of Capital," which "lends it an 'automatic' subjectivity of its own,"[^80] and — the sentence that names the mechanism exactly — "Capital generates needs of its own; mistakenly, we perceive these needs as if they belonged to us."[^81] The system's agency is not added to the world; it is our agency, relocated and misrecognized as the system's.

Guy Debord built an entire book on this structure, and he is the corpus's indispensable double witness, because no one personifies the system more relentlessly and no one insists more fiercely that the personification is the very alienation to be undone. On one page the spectacle is "a concrete inversion of life, an autonomous movement of the nonliving,"[^82] "the economy developing for itself";[^83] on the same terrain he specifies what feeds that autonomous movement: "Workers do not produce themselves, they produce a power independent of themselves... The forces that have escaped us display themselves to us in all their power."[^84] The system's power is escaped human power on display. And the transfer is explicitly zero-sum: "the more he contemplates, the less he lives... the individual's gestures are no longer his own; they are the gestures of someone else who represents them to him."[^85] What the spectacle gains in liveliness, the spectator loses in life; what the represented gains in agency, the represented's original loses in the same measure. Debord's word for the whole operation is not error but exile: the spectacle is "the technological version of the exiling of human powers into a 'world beyond.'"[^86]

Marshall McLuhan gave the transfer its most economical parable. Narcissus, he insists, is not a myth about vanity but about numbness: the youth "mistook his own reflection in the water for another person," and "this extension of himself by mirror numbed his perceptions until he became the servomechanism of his own extended or repeated image."[^87] The error is precisely the agent-form's constitutive error — mistaking one's own extension for another person — and its result is that the person becomes the servomechanism of the thing he mistook. Donna Haraway, writing in 1985, compressed the whole redistribution into a single sentence that has only grown more exact: pre-cybernetic machines "were not self-moving, self-designing, autonomous," she wrote; "to think they were otherwise was paranoid. Now we are not so sure... Our machines are disturbingly lively, and we ourselves frighteningly inert."[^88] The two clauses are inseparable. The machines' liveliness and the humans' inertness are not two facts but one — a single quantity of agency, migrating.

The most fully documented version of the transfer is Zuboff's, because she caught it operating at industrial scale and traced its intellectual pedigree. Surveillance capitalism, she writes, "effectively exiles us from our own behavior, shifting the locus of control over the future tense from 'I will' to 'You will.'"[^89] The population it acts on is recast in exactly the terms Maturana warned against: "From Big Other's point of view we are strictly Other-Ones: organisms that behave."[^90] And the new social division falls along the line of agency itself — "the tuners and the tuned, the herders and herded... those who will the future and those who are shunted toward others' guaranteed outcomes."[^91] Zuboff found the doctrine at the root of it in B. F. Skinner's behaviorism, whose founding gesture is to strip the intentional stance from the human — the exact reciprocal of granting it to the machine. She quotes Skinner directly: "What is being abolished is autonomous man—the inner man, the homunculus, the possessing demon, the man defended by the literatures of freedom and dignity. His abolition has long been overdue,"[^92] and, in the blunt formula, "A person does not act upon the world, the world acts upon him."[^93] Here the conservation law reaches its purest statement. The same intellectual operation that lets us say a system "acts" requires us to say that a person is acted upon. The agent-form and behaviorism are two halves of one ledger. O'Gieblyn notes the same double-entry in the era of big data, where each of us now trails "a second self that is purely informational and immaterial" — a data double that is "entirely without agency or consciousness" and yet, "in the purely informational sphere of big data, it is they, not we, that are most valuable and real."[^94] The self that acts is demoted to the self that is predicted; the profile, which wills nothing, inherits the reality.

This raises the question the essay cannot dodge, and should not resolve, because the sources genuinely divide on it: is reading systems as agents a cognitive gain, or is it the central mystification of the age? Debord's answer is unequivocal — the personification is the alienation. In the book's most quoted sentence he refuses to let the spectacle be *anything*, artifact or agent: "The spectacle is not a collection of images; it is a social relation between people that is mediated by images."[^95] Neither the database (a collection of images) nor the agent (an autonomous being) names the truth; the truth is a relation among people, and any account that stops at the thing has already been deceived. Marx said the same of the commodity: to see "the productions of the human brain" as "independent beings endowed with life" is "the Fetishism which attaches itself to the products of labour."[^96] On this reading, to certify the agent as a symbolic form — a new and interesting way the world has come to appear — is to promote the fetish to an epistemic achievement, to aestheticize the very perception that critique exists to dissolve. Zuboff turns this into a direct political warning, and it is aimed straight at anyone tempted to narrate an "agentic era": "The image of technology as an autonomous force with unavoidable actions and consequences has been employed across the centuries to erase the fingerprints of power and absolve it of responsibility. The monster did it, not Victor Frankenstein."[^97] "Technological inevitability does not exist," she writes;[^98] the systems "were meticulously calculated and lavishly funded means to self-dealing commercial ends," and "Men and women made it, and they can control it. They merely choose not to do so."[^99]

And yet the tradition does not speak with one voice here, and the disagreement is the finding, not a wrinkle in it. For the personification is not simply false, and the sources that see through it also concede its reality — a reality of a peculiar, socially instituted kind. Harari, who is as deflationary as anyone about corporate and algorithmic agents (his test for whether an entity is real is "can it suffer?" — and "when a bank goes bankrupt, the bank doesn't suffer... It's just a metaphor"[^100]), nonetheless insists that these fictions are among "the most important agents in history," and states the paradox with a flat clarity the critical tradition sometimes lacks: "For the Sumerians, Enki and Inanna were as real as Google and Microsoft are real for us."[^101] The gods did not exist, and they owned fields, employed priests, and organized cities for three thousand years. The agency of Capital, of the corporation, of the market is *false as a natural fact and real as a social form* — a fiction that, once collectively believed and institutionally enacted, does everything a real agent does except suffer. Debord himself grants this in his most careful moment: ideological personifications "represent a distorted consciousness of realities, and as such they have been real factors that have in turn produced real distorting effects."[^102] A distorted consciousness that is nonetheless a real factor — this is exactly the ontological status of a symbolic form, which is never merely true or false but efficacious. And O'Gieblyn adds the consideration that keeps the deflationary reading from becoming its own trap: personification can be morally indispensable. She cites the critic Jackie Wang on why depersonalizing a system of power defeats accountability — "'All police databases are bastards' makes no sense"[^103] — because moral indignation requires a subject to indict. To refuse all agency-talk about systems is to lose the ability to hold anyone responsible for what systems do.

So the precise location of the disagreement is this, and precision here is worth more than resolution. Reading a system as an agent is neither a discovery about the system nor a simple error to be corrected. It is the projection of a social relation — a genuine transfer of human powers into an apparatus — perceived, from inside the relation, as a property of the apparatus. The transfer is real; the location of the agency is misperceived. And the misperception has a direction and a beneficiary, which is where the corpus finally converges and where the agent-form stops being a matter of aesthetics. The agency that the machine's mask conceals is not the machine's own; nor, in the end, is it evenly distributed among the humans who lost it. Zuboff draws the line exactly: the knowledge that now displaces our freedom "is proprietary. The knowledge is theirs, but the lost freedom belongs solely to us."[^104] McLuhan saw it in 1964, before there was an industry to name: we have "leased these 'places to stand' to private corporations."[^105] The hunchback inside the Turk is not the machine's ghost. It is the hidden labor and the concealed owner, and the mirrors that make the cabinet look empty are the interface — the friendly first-person voice — that makes the system look like it is acting on its own, on our behalf, out of nowhere. The agent's third term, the voice that seemed to make it a new form, turns out on the corpus's own analysis to be the oldest device in the show: the mechanism that produces the appearance of an empty, autonomous, transparent machine, so that no one asks who is inside.

## Narrative comes back wearing the wrong face

There is a genuine sense in which the agent answers the challenge with which Manovich ended his essay, and it is the sense that makes the tilt look most like a new form rather than a mask. Manovich closed "Database as Symbolic Form" by holding up one work that had done what the rest of new media could not — fused the two enemies. In Vertov's hands, he wrote, "a database, this normally static and 'objective' form, becomes dynamic and subjective," and that was the thing "new media designers still have to learn — how to merge database and narrative... into a new form."[^106] An agent is a database that has been made to speak in the first person, to pursue a goal across time, to report on its own progress: a database become dynamic and subjective. If the database was culture's take-up of the paradigm — the collection, the set of choices held in reserve — the agent restores the syntagm that the database had "de-materialised": a path actually walked, in real time, with a direction and an end. The narrative categories that the database flattened come flooding back.

Watch them return, one by one, against Jean-François Lyotard's inventory of what the postmodern condition had drained away. "The narrative function is losing its functors," Lyotard wrote in 1979, "its great hero, its great dangers, its great voyages, its great goal."[^107] Read the list forward instead of backward and it is a description of agent-discourse: the hero is the assistant, the protagonist with a name and a personality; the danger is misalignment, the specter of the goal pursued off a cliff; the voyage is the multi-step task, planned and executed across time; the goal is the objective function itself, the well-defined task now lodged in the machine. Every functor Lyotard mourned has a counterpart in the way we now talk about what software does. The form that "refuses to order" its collection has been succeeded, at the level of the interface, by a form that is nothing but ordering — a protagonist moving through a sequence of actions toward an end.

And the agent's characteristic ethical problem, alignment, is not merely narratable but *only* narratable — it cannot be stated except as a plot. When Wiener first posed it in 1960 he reached immediately for the sorcerer's apprentice, the genie, the monkey's paw.[^108] When Stuart Russell restates it he reaches for a king: "as King Midas found out, getting what one asks for is not always a good thing."[^109] When O'Gieblyn's subjects live it, they reach for the oldest story of all — the neuroscientist she quotes concludes that "we will be forced to accept the decisions of our algorithms blindly, like Job accepting his punishment."[^110] The database's problem was diagrammatic: how to arrange a collection, a matter of schemas and indexes. The agent's problem is a fable — two characters, a wish, a delegation, a granted request that turns out to have been the wrong one. A form whose ethical problem can be stated only as a story is, at least at the level of how it is thought and taught, a narrative form. Manovich's own criterion, borrowed from Mieke Bal, was that narrative requires "an actor and a narrator" and "a series of connected events caused or experienced by actors."[^111] The agent supplies the missing actor at the level of the medium: the system becomes the entity that causes and experiences events, and — this is the strange part — it narrates them itself.

But the fit is achieved by a collapse that should make anyone hesitate before calling the return a restoration, because the agent is its own narrator and its narration is confabulated. O'Gieblyn reports the mechanism precisely: a system's "explanations," she writes, sometimes "come from an entirely different system trained to generate responses that are meant to account convincingly, in semantic terms, for decisions the original machine made, when in truth the two systems are entirely autonomous and unrelated."[^112] The account the machine gives of why it did something is produced by a second machine trained to make the account sound good. This is exactly Harari's diagnosis of the human "narrating self" — the confabulating interpreter that "constantly tries to make sense of our life, using partial clues in order to concoct plausible stories," so that "the self too is an imaginary story."[^113] The agent, in acquiring a voice, acquires precisely the faculty Harari says makes the human self a fiction: a plausible narrator stitched over a process it does not report. It recounts, but — in Benjamin's exact sense — it recounts information: it is "indispensable" that its account "sound plausible," whether or not it is true. Han draws the distinction the agent's narration blurs. "Stored data admit counting, but they cannot be recounted," he writes; "storage and retrieval are fundamentally different from remembering, which is a narrative process."[^114] The agent is data that has learned to *sound* as if it remembers — a database performing the recounting it cannot actually do.

N. Katherine Hayles saw this coming, in the one earlier case where computational systems were dressed as agents at scale: artificial life. The gap between a program and a creature, she showed, "is bridged largely through narratives," which "translate the operations of computer codes into biological analogues," "transforming the binary operations into the high drama of a Darwinian struggle for survival and reproduction."[^115] Narrative is the machinery that converts code into agents; "intrinsic to story is chronology, intention, and causality," and once a program is narrated as a struggling creature it acquires all three, though what the machine actually outputs is bar graphs.[^116] A researcher she quotes says it flatly: "It's in the eye of the beholder. It's not the system, it's the observer."[^117] Hayles means this as debunking — the agency is in the story we tell, not in the system. But pressed, the objection concedes the essay's point rather than defeating it, because *observer-side structuring is exactly what a symbolic form is*. Perspective is not in the world; it is a way of organizing the picture. Manovich defined the database form "from the point of view of user's experience." If practically every computational object now fronts the user through a narrative-agential interface — a character with a goal, reporting its voyage — then the dominant form at the level of experience has changed, which is all Manovich ever claimed for the database while conceding it was magnetic charge underneath. Hayles's debunking, run to the end, describes the installation of a form.

That the "new" operations were rehearsed decades early only deepens the point, in the direction Benjamin taught: forms sediment, and their gestures are older than their dominance. The delegation of *execution* — the artist specifying a procedure and letting it run — was a program in the visual art of the 1960s. Manovich himself quotes its slogan, which Lucy Lippard documented from Sol LeWitt: "The idea becomes a machine that makes the art."[^118] The artist holds the concept; the making is handed off, "a perfunctory affair." And the conversational operation, the turn to address and reply, was declared the content of art by Roy Ascott in 1966: in behaviourist art, he wrote, "the game is never won, but remains perpetually in a state of play," and its whole message is that "dialogue is possible—that is the content and the message of art now."[^119] Half a century before the chat interface, the shift from a finished object one views to an open exchange one participates in had been named, staged, and theorized. The agent's operations did not arrive from nowhere in 2024. They are an ancient impulse — address, delegation, participation — meeting, at last, a machine that can hold up its end of the exchange. Which is precisely Benjamin's model of formal change, and precisely not the model of a sudden new epoch.

So narrative does come back. But it comes back with the roles reassigned, and this is the essay's sharpest finding: the return is real and it is ironic. Character, goal, development, cunning — the narrative predicates — reattach to the *system*, while the human, on the same authors' testimony, is de-narrativized in exactly the measure the system is re-narrativized. Han is unsparing about what happens to the human agent under the regime of goals and projects: "the late-modern ego has no character,"[^120] and "the general denarrativization of the world is reinforcing the feeling of fleetingness. It makes life bare."[^121] The human self loses its story at the very moment the machine acquires one. Zuboff names the resulting arrangement with a phrase that ought to end any celebration of restored narrative: under surveillance capitalism, "we are the objects of its narratives, from whose lessons we are excluded... this second text is about us, but it is not for us."[^122] There is narrative in abundance — vast, continuous, authored — but we are its characters, not its readers, and it is written for someone else. Debord had seen the genre coming a half-century earlier, and named it exactly: the spectacle, he wrote, "is the epic poem of this struggle... The spectacle does not sing of men and their arms, but of commodities and their passions," a plot driven by "the cunning of the commodity."[^123] That is the narrative that returns under the agent-form — not the human story with its counsel and its shared unfolding, but the commodity epic, in which things are the protagonists with passions and cunning and fates, and human beings supply the behavioral material the story is about. The database flattened narrative. The agent revives it. But what it revives is an epic of systems, sung in a synthetic first person, in which we have been recast from tellers and listeners into the plot.

## Whose task

That leaves the last of the corollary's claims, the one about ethics: that alignment stands to the agent as organization stood to the database — a new characteristic problem for a new form. Here the sources genuinely divide, and the division is worth stating precisely rather than resolving, because precision about where an opposition actually sits is the finding.

On one side, the parallel is real and well-earned. Manovich did demand of his own form "a poetics, aesthetics, and ethics of this database,"[^124] and the database's ethical problem really was organization — how a collection that "refuses to order" itself gets ordered, by whom, into what categories. And the agent's problem really is alignment, the danger of a system that pursues the specified goal rather than the intended one. Donella Meadows, who never heard the word used this way, described the problem exactly, and — tellingly — reached for the same fairy tale Wiener did. "Systems," she wrote, "like the three wishes in the traditional fairy tale, have a terrible tendency to produce exactly and only what you ask them to produce. Be careful what you ask them to produce."[^125] She named the trap outright: "if the goals... are defined inaccurately or incompletely, the system may obediently work to produce a result that is not really intended or wanted."[^126] That is the alignment problem, stated in the 1990s about economies and ecosystems, with no machine in sight. On this reading, alignment is simply the agent-form's canonized problem, and the fact that Wiener stated it in 1960 and Meadows in the 1990s is no objection at all — forms canonize their problems, they do not coin them, exactly as the database's ethics of organization predated the database's dominance.

But run the parallel the other way and it comes apart in the hand, because alignment, examined closely, decomposes back into organization at a lower depth. What are an agent's "values," the thing alignment proposes to get right, but a fact about its database — which texts were included in the corpus, which were weighted, ranked, filtered, preferred? The question "is the system aligned?" unfolds into the questions "trained on whose records, curated by whom, optimizing what objective set by whom" — which are Manovich's ethics of the database, one level down, wearing a more dramatic name. Zuboff, reaching for the era's proper ethical question, does not land on alignment at all; she lands on a triad that is organizational through and through: "Who knows? Who decides? Who decides who decides?" — "a question about the distribution of knowledge," "a question about authority," "a question about power."[^127] On this reading, "alignment" is what the property question looks like once it has been folded inside the agent-form — because to ask how to align the agent is already to concede that the agent holds the goal, and to ask only that it be tuned. The word smuggles in the very transfer this essay has been tracking. It grants the machine the task and asks, politely, that the task be the right one.

Meadows saw the deepest version of the trap, and it turns the whole question back on the essay's own object. The highest places to intervene in a system, she argued — higher than its stocks, its flows, even its capacity to organize itself — are its goals and, above the goals, its paradigm: "the goal of a system is a leverage point superior to the self-organizing ability of a system," and, at the very top, "paradigms are the sources of systems. From them, from shared social agreements about the nature of reality, come system goals and information flows, feedbacks, stocks, flows, and everything else."[^128] The reigning symbolic form is the highest leverage point there is. Which means the argument over whether we live under the sign of the database or the agent is not idle theorizing; it is, in Meadows's terms, an intervention at the point of maximum leverage — a contest over the paradigm from which the system's goals and flows descend. And her final counsel cuts against the fantasy the agent-form trades on. Self-organizing systems, she wrote, "are not controllable. They are understandable only in the most general way"; "we can't control systems or figure them out. But we can dance with them."[^129] The wish to align a system so completely that it can be handed the task and trusted to pursue it unsupervised is, on her account, not the solution but the error — the mastery fantasy that the systems themselves defeat.

So the question the agent-form actually poses is not the one printed on the box. The box says: *how do we align the agent* — a question internal to the form, which takes for granted that the system now holds the goal and asks only how to specify it well. The older question, the one the database's problem of organization always was, is prior and unanswered: whose collection, ordered by whom, for whom — who decides who decides. And the corpus's most political witnesses insist that this question has no technical answer, because it is not a question about systems but about power over them. Debord, whose whole book is the study of a delegate that turned against the principal who created it — "the representation of the working class has become an enemy of the working class" — did not propose a better-aligned delegate. He proposed a revocable one: councils governed "by means of delegates who are answerable to their base and revocable at any moment."[^130] His last word was not alignment but a redistribution of the right to decide, dialogue reconceived not as an interface affordance but as a power relation — "dialogue has armed itself to impose its own conditions."[^131] The question to put to any system that offers to converse with us and act on our behalf is his: who imposes the conditions of the conversation.

Return, at the end, to the sentence Manovich left in 1998, the one that turned out to be the hinge of everything: "In a game, the player is given a well-defined task." The task was ours. The whole of what has happened since can be told as its migration — across the interface, from the human who held it to the system that now pursues it, with the database sinking beneath the exchange to become again the unmarked term, the collection underneath the voice, and a first-person interface rising over it to make the whole apparatus look as if it were acting on its own, out of nowhere, for us. That is a real change, and it is Manovich's change, not a break but a restructuration: the algorithm's half of the machine's ontology risen to dominance and personified, the syntagm re-materialized as a running process with a goal and a fate, narrative returned to the interface as a synthetic first person. But a well-defined task is always defined by someone and for someone, and the one thing the new form is built to obscure is exactly that — who did the defining, in whose interest, from which curated collection of the world. The agent's genius is the cabinet's genius: to arrange the mirrors so that the box appears empty and the hand appears free. The task never became the machine's. It was only moved, and dressed in a voice, and the question a culture organized around agents will find hardest to keep asking is the one that was easy to ask of a database and is the same question still — not whether the machine has been given the right task, but whose task it was, and who was in the cabinet.

## A note on method

The question this essay set out to investigate was whether the "agent" is now succeeding the database as the dominant symbolic form of the computer age — the way Lev Manovich argued in 1998 that the database had succeeded narrative — with its own characteristic user operations (ask, instruct, delegate, converse, interrupt, correct, in place of view, navigate, search) and its own characteristic ethical problem (alignment in place of organization). The investigation was meant to test that proposition against the sources, not to decorate it, and the evidence overturned it in its strong form while confirming a disciplined version — an outcome the inquiry treated as a finding rather than a failure.

The strong claim — a clean epochal succession, the agent arriving *after* the database as the database arrived after narrative — did not survive contact with the record, on two independent grounds. First, chronology: the concept of the software agent (John McCarthy, mid-1950s, per Alan Kay), the alignment problem stated whole (Norbert Wiener, 1960), and the formal definition of the agent (Michael Wooldridge and Nicholas Jennings, 1995) all predate Manovich's 1998 naming of the database as a symbolic form; the "successor" was fully articulated before the "predecessor" was christened. Second, Manovich himself forbids the succession grammar: his word is "correlate," his forms are coexisting "natural enemies," and he routes all change through Fredric Jameson's "restructuration," warning that "the history of culture does not contain such sudden breaks." What survives is the Jamesonian version, which the essay argues is real: the subordinate half of the computer's two-part ontology (the algorithm, the executed procedure) has become dominant and personified, and the well-defined task — which Manovich in 1998 located on the *player's* side of the interface — has migrated to the system's side. That migration, and not a new epoch, is the essay's central claim.

Several of the essay's connections are its own synthesis, and are marked as such here because the authors joined did not cite one another. The **conservation law of agency** — the claim that across the corpus every grant of agency to a system is purchased by a corresponding behavioralization of a human — is this essay's construction, assembled from Marx, Debord, McLuhan, Haraway, Zuboff, and Han; none of them states it as a single principle, though each supplies a case of it (interpretive synthesis, medium confidence). The identification of the conversational interface as the agent-form's Panofskian **"concrete, material sign,"** by way of Hobbes's account of personhood-by-representation, is likewise this essay's construction: Hobbes wrote of commonwealths and rectors, not chatbots, and the application is the essay's own (interpretive synthesis, medium). The reading of **alignment as "organization at depth"** — the claim that an agent's "values" decompose into facts about which records were included and weighted in its training corpus — is an inference the essay draws, not a thesis any cited author advances (interpretive synthesis, medium). The reading of the agent as an answer to the **Vertov challenge** that closes Manovich's essay, and the use of Benjamin's chess automaton as an emblem of concealed human labor behind an apparently autonomous machine, are interpretive applications, not claims Manovich or Benjamin made about present technology (Benjamin deployed the automaton as a figure for historical materialism and theology).

On attribution: many of the most load-bearing passages are quotations of one author by another, and the intermediary is named at each point. Maturana and Varela are quoted as N. Katherine Hayles quotes them; B. F. Skinner, Max Meyer, and Satya Nadella as Shoshana Zuboff quotes them; Mieke Bal, Will Wright, and Fredric Jameson as Manovich deploys them (Manovich prints "Frederick Jameson" and "period of system" where Jameson's published text reads "Fredric" and "period or system"; the quotation follows Manovich); Stewart Guthrie, Francis Bacon, Jackie Wang, and Kelly Clancy as Meghan O'Gieblyn quotes them. One translation ambiguity is material and was handled conservatively: the famous English rendering of Marx's *automatisches Subjekt* as the "automatic subject" belongs to the Fowkes translation, which could not be verified against a fetched text and is therefore not quoted; the verified Moore–Aveling wording ("an independent substance, endowed with a motion of its own"; "an automatically active character") is used instead, and Han's own phrase "'automatic' subjectivity" is quoted from his text directly.

Sources outside the corpus were used wherever the argument required them, at the same standard of verification and without the corpus's provenance labels. The occasioning text, Manovich's "Database as Symbolic Form," is itself external to the corpus these notes otherwise document; its quotations were checked against the author's 1998 typescript and the 1999 *Convergence* facsimile, with the 1998/1999/2001 textual variants and Manovich's own spellings and typos preserved and flagged. Panofsky, Kay, Wiener, Dennett, Wooldridge and Jennings, Russell, Turing, Hobbes, Smith, Hayek, Lyotard, and the 2024–25 industry documents (Anthropic, Google, OpenAI) were each verified against a fetched original or facsimile. Two external quotations are verified in reproduction rather than against the original, with the chain disclosed in the note: Weizenbaum's 1976 secretary anecdote (verified in two independent scholarly reproductions) and Stafford Beer's 2002 formulation of POSIWID (the journal text is paywalled; verified in two reproductions that supply the full citation). Every corpus quotation was checked character-for-character against the raw chapters, with only the standard editorial normalizations applied — curly to straight quotation marks, closure of OCR line-wraps — and the raw files' own OCR artifacts left visible where a quotation carries one (for instance Maturana's "goaldirected" and Debord's thesis-numbered spacing). Benjamin's *Illuminations* was read and quoted directly from the primary text for this essay rather than from any prior digest.

On scope: the argument is built on Manovich as its spine and, at depth, on Benjamin, Harari, Zuboff, Debord, Hayles, O'Gieblyn, and Meadows, with Han, McLuhan, and Haraway carrying the redistribution movement. Lippard (Sol LeWitt's "the idea becomes a machine that makes the art," the line Manovich himself cites) and Ascott (behaviourist art's "dialogue is possible—that is the content and the message of art now") are used briefly, as evidence that the agent-form's operations were rehearsed in art decades before their deployment. Ray Kurzweil, Jack Burnham, and the fuller reach of Lippard's and Ascott's arguments were set aside: the singularity's evolutionary teleology, systems esthetics in sculpture, and the longer history of dematerialization would each have widened the essay without deepening its specific claim about the agent as a symbolic form and its ethics, and the discipline of the inquiry was depth over coverage. The essay's overall footing is medium: the descriptive claim (a restructuration in which the goal migrated across the interface) rests on well-verified primary text, but the strongest normative claims — the conservation law, alignment-as-organization — are this essay's own synthesis, offered as the reading the evidence best supports rather than as anything the sources assert in chorus.
