# The dematerialized object and the disembodied soul

## Central Question

Conceptual art tried to send the art object away — into the idea, the mailed catalogue, the typed sheet. Cybernetics and its heirs tried to send the self away — into the pattern, the upload, the data set. Are these two flights from matter the *same gesture* performed in different domains? And if they are, do they fail in the same way?

## Thesis

Lucy Lippard's "dematerialization of the art object" and N. Katherine Hayles's "disembodiment of information" are structural twins: each names a postwar dream of escaping a material substrate — the gallery commodity in one case, the mortal body in the other — and each, on its own author's testimony, fails to clear the substrate it tried to leave. Meghan O'Gieblyn completes the figure a generation later by reporting the flight from the body as an *accomplished, lived* condition — the "second self" of big data — and, citing Hayles directly, reading it as the old dream of the soul in technical dress. The parallel is real and load-bearing, but it is a parallel this vault *constructs*: Lippard and Hayles never cite each other, and "dematerialization" (an economic-documentary claim about the object) is not identical to "disembodiment" (an ontological claim about information). The "same gesture" is a structural rhyme, not a historical identity — and saying so precisely is what keeps it useful.

## 1. Two flights, one hierarchy

Both flights begin by ranking the immaterial above the material. Hayles names the conviction directly: to live "in a condition of virtuality" is to share "the cultural perception that information and materiality are conceptually distinct and that information is in some sense more essential, more important, and more fundamental than materiality" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]]). `Directly stated`. Lippard's definition of conceptual art is the same hierarchy transposed into the studio: "work in which the idea is paramount and the material form is secondary, lightweight, ephemeral, cheap, unpretentious and/or 'dematerialized'" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). `Directly stated`. Idea over object, pattern over flesh: in both cases the material term is demoted to a vehicle the essential term merely happens to occupy.

The demotion has the same grammar in each domain — the inversion of origin and manifestation. Hayles finds it crystallized in William Gibson's phrase "data made flesh," which she reads with care: "Information is the putative origin, physicality the derivative manifestation" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/04 Virtual Bodies and Flickering Signifiers]]). `Directly stated`. Lippard's object suffers exactly this demotion to "derivative manifestation": the work migrates from the saleable thing toward "the thinking process almost exclusively," so that, in the 1968 Lippard–Chandler thesis, "such a trend appears to be provoking a profound dematerialization of art" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/07 1968]]). `Directly stated`. The object becomes a residue the idea leaves behind, just as the body becomes a residue the information leaves behind.

## 2. The dream and its motive

In both fields the flight is *motivated* — it promises an escape from a cage. For Lippard the cage is the market. She frames the whole episode as an "escape attempt": "In a decommodified 'idea-art,' some of us (or was it just me?) thought we had in our hands the weapon that would transform the art world into a democratic institution" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/02 Escape Attempts]]). `Directly stated`. Dematerialize the object and you might free art from "commodity status." For the cybernetic tradition the cage is the body's frailty and finitude, and the prize is the "dream or nightmare of the body as information" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/04 Virtual Bodies and Flickering Signifiers]]) — disembody the self and you might outlive the flesh. `Directly stated`. The two prizes differ (a democratic art world; a deathless mind), but the move is identical: subtract the material substrate and you are promised liberation from what that substrate costs you.

## 3. The internal rebuttal: the substrate will not subtract

The deepest convergence is that each flight is refuted *from inside its own movement*, and refuted on the same ground: there is no immaterial remainder; there is only a change of state.

Inside conceptual art the rebuttal was immediate. The Art-Language group wrote to Lippard and Chandler contesting her own term: the works "may not be an art-object as we know it in its traditional matter-state, but they are nevertheless matter in one of its forms, either solid-state, gas-state, liquid-state" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/07 1968]]). `Directly stated`. A statement recorded in the 1970 chronicle puts it flatly: "There is no art which does not bear some burden of physicality. To deny it is to descend to irony" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/09 1970]]). `Directly stated` *(speaker uncertain — see Provenance Flags)*. The "burden of physicality" is never shed, only displaced: from canvas to paper, from paper to photograph, from photograph to mailed documentation.

Hayles's whole book is the same rebuttal raised to a thesis. Against "such a powerful dream," she insists "that for information to exist, it must always be instantiated in a medium" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]]). `Directly stated`. And she pushes one step further than the artists, locating the error not merely in the forgetting of the medium but in the prior cut that produced the two terms: "conceiving of information as a thing separate from the medium instantiating it is a prior imaginary act that constructs a holistic phenomenon as an information/matter duality" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/03 Toward Embodied Virtuality]]). `Directly stated`. Read across the two fields, the Art-Language "matter-state" and Hayles's "must always be instantiated" are the same correction, arrived at independently, decades apart: the immaterial pole was never available; matter only changed its form. *(Cross-author mapping: `Interpretive synthesis` — see Provenance Flags.)*

## 4. The same defeat, in two registers

Because the substrate cannot be subtracted, both flights end in recapture — and here the parallel is at its tightest, even as the *register* of the defeat differs.

Lippard's defeat is economic and documentary. The hope that an object-free art "would therefore be forcibly freed from the tyranny of a commodity status" did not survive contact with the market; by the Postface she records that "whatever minor revolutions in communication have been achieved by the process of dematerializing the object (easily mailed work, catalogues and magazine pieces, primarily art that can be shown inexpensively and unobtrusively in infinite locations at one time), art and artist in a capitalist society remain luxuries" ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/11 Postface]]). `Directly stated`. Note the parenthesis: the very inventory of the "dematerialized" work — *mailed work, catalogues, magazine pieces* — is a list of media. The documentary residue is the material substrate, quietly reasserting itself as the thing that gets bought.

Hayles's defeat is ontological and ideological, and she states it in a sentence that could double as a gloss on Lippard's Postface: "The body's dematerialization depends in complex and highly specific ways on the embodied circumstances that an ideology of dematerialization would obscure" ([[raw/Hayles - How We Became Posthuman/10 The Materiality of Informatics]]). `Directly stated`. The flight from the body is itself staged by, and dependent on, a dense material apparatus (laboratories, journals, screens, capital) that the dream of weightlessness must hide in order to seem plausible. In both authors, then, the becoming-immaterial is *real as a strategy and illusory as an escape*: the ground each tried to leave behind is exactly what reabsorbs it. That structural rhyme — strategy succeeds, escape fails, substrate returns — is the spine of the "same gesture" thesis. *(The rhyme is `Interpretive synthesis`; each author's own claim is `Directly stated`.)*

## 5. O'Gieblyn: the flight completed and lived

If Lippard and Hayles write in the future or conditional tense of a *dream*, O'Gieblyn writes in the present tense of an *accomplished fact* — and she does so explicitly downstream of Hayles, which makes this the one bridge in the argument that rests on a real citation rather than a constructed parallel. In *God, Human, Animal, Machine* she names Hayles twice. She imports Hayles's history — that at the Macy Conferences "information came to take precedence over materiality" and came to seem "an entity that can flow unchanged between different material substrates" ([[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch04 [Pattern]]]) `Directly stated` — and she imports Hayles's critique, citing "the critic N. Katherine Hayles" for the formula that "disembodied information becomes the ultimate Platonic Form" ([[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch03 [Pattern]]]). `Directly stated`.

Then she collapses the conditional into the indicative. The disembodied self is no longer a roboticist's someday-upload; it already exists as the data double: "In addition to our physical bodies, there exists—somewhere in the ether—a second self that is purely informational and immaterial, a data set of our clicks, purchases, and likes." And the old hierarchy holds, now monetized: these second selves "are entirely without agency or consciousness ... and yet in the purely informational sphere of big data, it is they, not we, that are most valuable and real" ([[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch04 [Pattern]]]). `Directly stated`. This is precisely Hayles's inversion — "Information is the putative origin, physicality the derivative manifestation" — except realized as market fact rather than science-fiction figure. Lippard's object demoted beneath the idea, Hayles's flesh demoted beneath the pattern, O'Gieblyn's body demoted beneath the dossier: one gesture, three domains, the last of them already operational.

O'Gieblyn adds the term the other two leave implicit. Glossing Hayles's history, she writes that information "became, in other words, the materialist's substitute for the soul" ([[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch04 [Pattern]]]) — her own inference built on Hayles's account. `Strongly implied`. This names what the whole flight has been after all along: not merely a lighter object or a portable mind but an *incorruptible* one, the dematerialized thing imagined as the saved thing. Her closing testimony registers the cost from inside, as the felt erasure Hayles set out to reverse — "the suspicion that ... the soul is little more than a data set" ([[raw/OGieblyn - God Human Animal Machine/Ch09 [Metonymy]]]). `Directly stated`. The dematerialized object and the disembodied soul converge here on a single sentence: when the material term is demoted far enough, what is left is a pattern that feels, to the one living it, like a loss.

## 6. What the parallel is — and is not

The argument should not be overstated, and the vault's own apparatus is built to prevent that. Three honest limits:

- **No historical link binds Lippard and Hayles.** *Six Years* (1973) precedes *How We Became Posthuman* (1999) by a quarter century; the two work in different fields and neither cites the other. The "same gesture" is a structural isomorphism *this vault constructs*, rated `medium` confidence in [[Lucy Lippard and N. Katherine Hayles]], not an influence either author claims.
- **The two claims are not identical.** Lippard's "dematerialization" is an economic and documentary claim about the *art object* ("inaccurate term," she concedes — paper is as material as lead); Hayles's "disembodiment" is an ontological claim about *information*. They rhyme; they are not the same proposition. Calling them "the same gesture" is a deliberate compression, true at the level of *structure* (becoming-immaterial → non-optional substrate → internal rebuttal → recapture) and false if pressed to the level of *content*.
- **Only the O'Gieblyn–Hayles bridge is a citation.** That link is `high` confidence on its citation layer (O'Gieblyn quotes Hayles by name, twice); O'Gieblyn's theological extension — information as the soul's "substitute" — is her own `Interpretive synthesis`, not a claim Hayles makes.

What survives these limits is exactly what makes the essay worth writing: across art history, information theory, and the lived present of big data, the *form* of the flight from matter is constant — rank the immaterial above the material, promise an escape, discover the substrate was never optional, watch it return as market, as medium, as the felt loss of a self reduced to its pattern. The three authors are not making one argument; they are documenting one recurring fantasy and its one recurring defeat.

## Coda: the curatorial use

For an exhibition, the payoff is a single thread that runs from the 1968 mailed catalogue to the 2021 data double without anachronism: every era's most advanced art and most advanced technology has dreamed of leaving the body and the object behind, and every era has been handed back the bill — the residue, the medium, the flesh, the dossier. The most powerful objects to hang on this thread are the ones that *are* the residue: the typed sheet that "is nevertheless matter," the catalogue that turned out to be the commodity, the screen on which the weightless self is rendered and sold. The argument's gift to a curator is permission to treat a conceptual-art document and a data-portrait as the same kind of object — two souvenirs of the same failed escape.

## Provenance Flags

- **Speaker of "no art which does not bear some burden of physicality" is uncertain in the source.** The raw 1970 chronicle ([[raw/Lippard - Six Years/09 1970]]) runs three entries together: a clearly labelled **Mel Bochner** excerpt ("Elements from Speculation (1967–1970)," *Artforum*, May 1970), then a "Douglas Huebler. 1970." line, then the passage containing the quote. The quote falls *after* the Huebler attribution, so **Huebler is the most likely speaker**, but the OCR'd run-together layout leaves it genuinely ambiguous. Note that the vault's own three notes disagree: [[Dematerialization]] attributes it to **Bochner**, [[Material vs Immaterial]] to **Huebler**, and [[Lucy Lippard and N. Katherine Hayles]] to **Lippard's own concession**. This essay quotes it without a firm attribution and flags the conflict; the three notes should be reconciled in a future audit. The quote's *function* in the argument (an artist-statement of the material-pole rebuttal recorded inside *Six Years*) does not depend on the speaker.
- **The central "same gesture" thesis is the vault's `Interpretive synthesis`, not a historical claim.** Lippard and Hayles never cite each other; "dematerialization" (economic/documentary) and "disembodiment" (ontological) are non-identical claims. Confidence on the bridge is `medium` per [[Lucy Lippard and N. Katherine Hayles]].
- **The O'Gieblyn–Hayles bridge is the one citation-backed link** (`high` on the citation layer per [[Meghan O'Gieblyn and N. Katherine Hayles]]); O'Gieblyn's "substitute for the soul" gloss and her theological reading are her own extension, not Hayles's.
