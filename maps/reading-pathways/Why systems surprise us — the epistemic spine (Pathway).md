# Why systems surprise us — the epistemic spine

Meadows's account of why systems defeat our expectations: our knowledge is always a model, events explain nothing, boundaries are drawn by the observer, and cause is not proportional to effect. This is the pathway to read when the interest is epistemic — the gap between our mental models and the systems they try to grasp — and it lands, via the resilience/self-organization/hierarchy triad, on why systems work so well and yet so unpredictably.

- [[Mental Models]] — everything we know is a model, congruent with yet never the real world
- [[Event Behavior Structure]] — events explain nothing; structure is the source
- [[No Separate Systems]] — boundaries are observer-drawn; the world is a continuum
- [[Nonlinearity]] — cause not proportional to effect; flips behavior modes
- [[Bounded Rationality]] — reasonable decisions on incomplete information yield outcomes no one wants
- [[Resilience]] — the invisible capacity to persist and bounce back; distinct from static stability
- [[Self-Organization]] — the capacity to make one's own structure more complex
- [[Hierarchy (Systems)]] — nested subsystems that grant stability and reduce information load
- [[Suboptimization]] — a subsystem's goals dominating at the expense of the whole
- [[Events explain nothing only structure explains behavior]] — the epistemic keystone argument
