# The cyborg across the vault

The cyborg is the vault's most-traveled figure: a "cybernetic organism" that recurs across four authors and four decades, each reframing what it means to fuse body and machine. This pathway tracks that mutation — from Burnham's 1968 declaration that the cybernetic organism is *an art form*, through Haraway's 1985 ironic political myth of breached boundaries, into Hayles's recovery of embodiment against the posthuman fantasy of leaving the body behind, and out to Ascott's post-biological body dissolved into the network. Curatorially it is useful for staging the cyborg as neither a single icon nor a fixed prophecy but a contested figure — by turns art object, feminist weapon, cautionary diagnosis, and utopian telos — and for grounding any cyborg-themed exhibition in the precise place each thinker stands on the Material/Immaterial axis.

- [[The Cyborg]] — the master figure and entry point: the cybernetic organism as it migrates across the corpus, from art object to political myth to body of the future.
- [[Object and System]] — Burnham's structural axis; the art-historical ground from which the cyborg first emerges, as sculpture moves from object toward living system.
- [[Cyborg Art]] — Burnham's 1968 keystone: the "cybernetic organism as an art form," the vault's earliest cyborg, where the figure begins as an esthetic proposition rather than a body.
- [[Jack Burnham and Donna Haraway]] — the bridge from 1968 to 1985: Burnham's systems-cyborg read forward into Haraway's political one, the cybernetic organism crossing from gallery to manifesto.
- [[The Breached Boundaries]] — Haraway's three boundary breakdowns (human/animal, organism/machine, physical/non-physical) that make the cyborg thinkable as a condition we already inhabit.
- [[The Cyborg]] is established; here [[Fractured Identities]] — Haraway's refusal of a unified self, the cyborg as a creature of partial, contradictory, stitched-together identity.
- [[Affinity Not Identity]] — Haraway's political wager: coalition built on affinity rather than essence, the cyborg as a model for solidarity without origin myth.
- [[Cyborg vs Goddess]] — Haraway's defining tension ("I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess"), the choice of the impure, constructed, technological self over a return to nature.
- [[The Informatics of Domination]] — Haraway's dark counter-figure: the same coding logic that frees the cyborg also builds the command-control grid it lives inside, the hinge toward Hayles.
- [[N. Katherine Hayles and Donna Haraway]] — the cross-book bridge on informatics and the cyborg, carrying the figure from feminist myth into Hayles's account of how information lost its body.
- [[The Liberal Humanist Subject]] — Hayles's foil: the bounded, autonomous self the cyborg dissolves, and whose dissolution the posthuman either mourns or celebrates.
- [[Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman]] — the tension at the heart of Hayles's project, where the cyborg becomes the threshold figure between the old subject and the posthuman.
- [[Embodiment]] — Hayles's crucial corrective: the instantiated, non-replicable particular body the cyborg must not be allowed to shed, her resistance to disembodied information.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the fantasy Hayles warns against — the body recoded as downloadable pattern — which Ascott will instead embrace.
- [[Roy Ascott and N. Katherine Hayles]] — the pairing that turns Hayles's caution into Ascott's affirmation: the same merger of body and information read once as loss, once as promise.
- [[The Post-Biological Body]] — Ascott's telos: the body transformed beyond its biological limits, the cyborg arriving at its most dematerialized, network-dissolved form.
- [[Roy Ascott and Jack Burnham]] — the closing synthesis that loops the arc shut, the two cybernetic-art theorists framing the cyborg's full journey from 1968 object to networked post-biological body.
