# The posthuman body

This pathway traces embodiment through its successive disavowals and recoveries: it begins where Hayles re-grounds the posthuman in the lived, instantiated body and contests the dream that information can leave its flesh behind; passes through Haraway's cyborg, where the body's boundaries are breached but never abolished; confronts the Moravec/Kurzweil uploading fantasy, which would discard the body as mere substrate; and ends with Ascott's moistmedia and post-biological body, where the networked dissolution of the self re-materialises as an augmented, transformable corporeality rather than pure information. Curatorially it is the vault's spine for any project on the body, the digital, and the limits of disembodiment — useful for staging "the body" as a contested object that artists and theorists alternately defend, breach, abandon, and reinvent.

- [[Embodiment vs Information]] — the framing tension: the lived, instantiated body set against information imagined as substrate-independent; the axis the whole journey runs along.
- [[Embodiment]] — Hayles's anchor: embodiment as the local, particular, performative enactment that the ideology of immateriality keeps trying to abolish or extend; the body the pathway sets out to recover.
- [[The Body as Data]] — the dream-and-nightmare against which Hayles writes: the body recoded as an informational pattern that could be read off, ported, or made to survive its own flesh.
- [[Inscription vs Incorporation]] — Hayles's distinction between the body as a surface of inscribed signs and the incorporated, enacted body that no code fully captures; why embodiment resists download.
- [[The Materiality of Informatics]] — the counter-thesis that the immaterial is always materially produced; information must be instantiated in a medium, blocking the leap to pure pattern.
- [[Liberal Humanist Subject vs Posthuman]] — Hayles's pivot: the posthuman need not mean disembodiment; the danger is grafting it onto the autonomous, mastering liberal subject.
- [[The Breached Boundaries]] — Haraway's cyborg move: the organism/machine, human/animal, physical/non-physical boundaries are transgressed — not to escape the body but to reimagine it.
- [[The Cyborg]] — the figure itself: a hybrid of machine and organism that refuses the choice between flesh and code; embodiment pluralised rather than disavowed.
- [[Affinity Not Identity]] — Haraway's politics of the fractured, coalitional self; the cyborg body as partial and multiple, a bridge toward the dispersed networked self Ascott will name.
- [[Mind Uploading]] — the disavowal proper: consciousness imagined as downloadable from the brain, the body left behind as inessential meat.
- [[N. Katherine Hayles and Hans Moravec]] — the keystone confrontation: Moravec's download fantasy as the founding "nightmare" of Hayles's project — "the theory decontextualizes information; Moravec decontextualizes the theory."
- [[Human Body Version 2.0]] — Kurzweil's engineering version of the same wish: the biological body as version 1.0 hardware to be redesigned, optimised, and ultimately transcended.
- [[Transcending Biology]] — Kurzweil's imperative to surpass "version 1.0 biological bodies"; the affirmative pole that embraces the merger Hayles diagnoses.
- [[The Post-Biological Body]] — Ascott's reversal: the networked erasure of the Cartesian self leads not to disembodiment but to re-materialisation — a bionic, prosthetic, transformable corporeality with its interface set within the brain.
- [[Moistmedia]] — Ascott's substrate for that body: the "moist" interspace of bits, atoms, neurons, and genes, where the dematerialised digital resolves back into augmented matter — the dematerialisation/rematerialisation arc closing.
- [[Roy Ascott and Hans Moravec]] — the synthesis pairing: Ascott's affirmative post-biological body read beside Moravec's uploading dream, marking where the artist embraces the post-biological telos the theorist refuses.
